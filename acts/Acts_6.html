Acts 6

The Seven Chosen; Stephen Arrested

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and close companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author. His medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart, though debated), his literary polish, and his Hellenistic historiographical style are consistent with the educated Gentile physician of Colossians 4.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul under house arrest awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the trial's outcome was known. The silence about the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) and about Paul's death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date. Liberal scholarship often dates Acts c. AD 80–90, largely on the assumption that Luke used Mark; the early date remains defensible and is preferred by a significant minority of critical scholars.

What prompted it

Luke states his purpose in the prologue: to provide an “orderly account” (Luke 1:3) of the origins and expansion of the Christian movement. Acts traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome — geographically fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Theologically, Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel's advance is providentially directed despite (and through) opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

The Appointment of the Seven Deacons
1In those days when the number of disciples was increasing, the Hellenistic Jews among them complained against the Hebraic Jews because their widows were being overlooked in the daily distribution of food.2So the Twelve gathered all the disciples together and said, "It would not be right for us to neglect the ministry of the word of God in order to wait on tables.3Brothers and sisters, choose seven men from among you who are known to be full of the Spirit and wisdom. We will turn this responsibility over to them4and will give our attention to prayer and the ministry of the word."5This proposal pleased the whole group. They chose Stephen, a man full of faith and of the Holy Spirit; also Philip, Procorus, Nicanor, Timon, Parmenas, and Nicolas from Antioch, a convert to Judaism.6They presented these men to the apostles, who prayed and laid their hands on them.7So the word of God spread. The number of disciples in Jerusalem increased rapidly, and a large number of priests became obedient to the faith.

Greek Word Study

Ἑλληνιστάς / Ἑβραίους (Hellēnistas / Hebraious)Hellenists / Hebrews — the Hellenists were Greek-speaking Jews of the Diaspora who had settled in Jerusalem; the Hebrews were Aramaic-speaking Palestinian Jews. This cultural-linguistic divide within the Jewish Christian community is the first internal tension in Acts. It is resolved not by favouritism but by structural wisdom.
διακονίᾳ (diakonia)ministry/service — the root of "deacon." The word is used both for the daily distribution of food (v1: diakonia daily) and for the apostles' ministry of the word (v4: diakonia tou logou). Luke deliberately uses the same word for both, showing that both are genuine ministries — neither is inferior. The distinction is one of calling, not dignity.

Places

Jerusalem Synagogues
The "Synagogue of the Freedmen" (v9) was one of hundreds of synagogues in Jerusalem — Talmudic tradition counted 480. This particular synagogue drew Diaspora Jews from Cyrene, Alexandria, Cilicia, and Asia who had returned to the holy city. Stephen's opponents came from his own cultural background, making the debate both theological and personal.
The Sanhedrin
For the third time in Acts (after chs 4 and 5), the Sanhedrin is the venue for confrontation. Luke's structure shows the establishment's escalating hostility: first warning, then flogging, now a capital hearing. The progress toward Stephen's death is narratively inevitable.

Context

The Seven as Proto-Deacons

The Seven of Acts 6 are not formally called "deacons" (the noun form diakonos is never applied to them), but the narrative is the functional origin of the later diaconal office (1 Tim 3:8-13). Their qualifications — Spirit-filled, full of wisdom, of good reputation — set the standard for servant leadership in the church. Notably, all seven have Greek names, suggesting the appointment was sensitive to the Hellenistic community's representation.

Word and Table — v2-4

The apostles' distinction between "wait on tables" and "ministry of the word" is not a hierarchy of worth but a recognition of calling. The same word (diakonia) covers both — there is no "sacred/secular" divide. The structural innovation of the Seven enables both ministries to be done well simultaneously.

Cross-References

Numbers 11:16-17

Moses appoints seventy elders to share the burden of leadership — the OT precedent for delegated ministry. The Spirit given to Moses is distributed to the seventy. In Acts 6, the Spirit given to the apostles is expressed in the Spirit-filled Seven.

1 Timothy 3:8-13

The qualifications for deacons — dignified, not double-tongued, tested — build on the Acts 6 precedent. The office is formally named and structured by the time of the Pastoral Epistles.

Acts 6:7

"The word of God spread" — Luke's growth summary immediately after the structural innovation, signaling that the organizational response was Spirit-blessed.

Literary Highlights

Five-Fold Growth Summary — v7Acts 6:7 is the third of Luke's periodic growth summaries (after 2:47 and 5:14). Each summary marks the end of a narrative unit and signals Spirit-blessed expansion. This one adds a significant detail: "a large number of priests became obedient to the faith" — the establishment itself is beginning to fracture.
Stephen's Face — v15The Sanhedrin seeing Stephen's face "like the face of an angel" is Luke's anticipatory Christophany. Stephen is about to become the first martyr; his face shines as Moses's did at Sinai (Exod 34:29-35). The detail signals divine approval before Stephen speaks a word — he is vindicated by appearance before being vindicated by argument.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
6:2

"It would not be right for us to neglect the ministry of the word in order to wait on tables" — Robertson notes that this is not a dismissal of table service but a recognition of calling. The Twelve were specifically called to be witnesses (1:8, 22); this calling constrains what they can give time to. Robertson uses this to argue for specialized callings in the body of Christ.

6:6

The laying on of hands (epitithentes tas cheiras) — Robertson traces this practice from Numbers 27:18-23 (Moses investing Joshua) through the NT. It is a gesture of identification and authorization, not the mechanical transfer of power. The Seven receive their ministry through prayer and the community's recognition, with the laying on of hands as the public seal.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1

Keener notes that the Hellenistic/Hebrew division reflects real socio-cultural tensions. Hellenistic Jews were often viewed with some suspicion by Palestinian Jews as overly accommodated to Greek culture. The widows being overlooked may reflect unconscious bias or simple administrative failure — either way, Luke records it without minimizing the tension. The church's response is the model: structural change rather than denial.

6:5

The appointment of seven men all with Greek names (Stephen, Philip, Prochorus, Nicanor, Timon, Parmenas, Nicolas) is noted by Keener as almost certainly intentional: the Hellenistic community that raised the complaint receives exclusively Hellenistic representatives to address it. This is cultural sensitivity embedded in the church's structural response.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:2-4

MacArthur uses this passage to establish the NT pattern of ministry specialization. The apostles' commitment to "prayer and the ministry of the word" defines the primary calling of elders/pastors: not program administration but prayer and preaching. The failure to guard this priority is, for MacArthur, the besetting sin of contemporary church leadership.

6:7

MacArthur highlights the significance of "priests" becoming believers. The Jerusalem priestly families were Sadducean, the theological enemies of resurrection. Their conversion signals that the resurrection evidence was compelling even to those with the strongest institutional and theological interest in denying it.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
6:2-4

Calvin uses this passage as the foundation for the distinction between the offices of elder/pastor (ministry of the word) and deacon (ministry of mercy). Both are permanent offices of the church; neither is superior in dignity. The distinction is functional, not hierarchical. Calvin's church order at Geneva institutionalized exactly this dual structure.

6:10

"They could not stand up against the wisdom he spoke or the Spirit by whom he spoke" — Calvin notes that Stephen's opponents are defeated not by natural eloquence (though he was clearly gifted) but by the Spirit's work through him. The fulfillment of Luke 21:15 ("I will give you words and wisdom that none of your adversaries will be able to resist") is already happening.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1

The NET Bible notes the Greek Ἑλληνιστάς — "Hellenists." While sometimes interpreted as Greek-speaking Gentiles, the context (v1: "among them"; v2: the Twelve addressing "the disciples") makes clear these are Greek-speaking Jewish Christians, not Gentiles. The Gentile mission has not yet begun; this is an intra-Jewish-Christian tension.

6:7

"A large number of priests" — the NET notes this is a significant historical claim. The Jerusalem Temple employed a large number of lower-ranking priests (distinct from the high-priestly aristocracy) who lived in the city and served in rotation. Their conversion would represent a significant inroad into the religious establishment.

Stephen Seized — Full of Grace and Power
8Now Stephen, a man full of God's grace and power, performed great wonders and signs among the people.9Opposition arose, however, from members of the Synagogue of the Freedmen (as it was called) — Jews of Cyrene and Alexandria as well as the provinces of Cilicia and Asia — who began to argue with Stephen.10But they could not stand up against the wisdom he spoke or the Spirit by whom he spoke.11Then they secretly persuaded some men to say, "We have heard Stephen speak blasphemous words against Moses and against God."12So they stirred up the people and the elders and the teachers of the law. They seized Stephen and brought him before the Sanhedrin.13They produced false witnesses, who testified, "This fellow never stops speaking against this holy place and against the law.14For we have heard him say that this Jesus of Nazareth will destroy this place and change the customs Moses handed down to us."15All who were sitting in the Sanhedrin looked intently at Stephen, and they saw that his face was like the face of an angel.

Greek Word Study

ἀντέστησαν (antestēsan)could not withstand/resist — the aorist of anthistēmi. The same word Paul uses in Gal 2:11 ("I opposed him to his face") and in Eph 6:13 ("so that you may be able to stand your ground"). Stephen's opponents are not merely disagreeing; they are making a full counter-attack that fails.
ἄγγελου (angelou)of an angel — the simile "like the face of an angel" deliberately echoes Moses's shining face (Exod 34:29-35) and anticipates Stephen's vision of heaven open (7:55-56). His face reflects the glory of the one he is about to proclaim.

Places

The Synagogue of the Freedmen
Located in Jerusalem, this synagogue represented Diaspora Jews who had returned to the holy city — from Cyrene (North Africa), Alexandria (Egypt), Cilicia (Asia Minor, Stephen's probable home region), and Asia. Stephen would have worshipped here before his conversion. The dispute is between Stephen and his own former community.
Before the Sanhedrin
The Sanhedrin's Hall of Hewn Stone on the Temple Mount. The third apostolic hearing in Acts — but the first that will end not in flogging and release but in death. Luke marks the escalation by the unprecedented detail of Stephen's angelic face.

Context

The Synagogue of the Freedmen

The "Freedmen" (libertinoi — Latin loanword) were Jews or their descendants who had been enslaved and freed, probably from the Roman wars of 63 BC. Returning to Jerusalem as freedmen was a mark of piety; their synagogue represented a community with special pride in reclaimed Jewish identity — and therefore special sensitivity to perceived threats to the law and temple.

False Witnesses

The use of false witnesses (v13) echoes the trial of Jesus (Mark 14:55-59). Luke's parallel is deliberate: Stephen's passion narrative mirrors his Lord's. The charges — speaking against the holy place and the law — are the same charges brought against Jesus. The structural parallel signals that Stephen's martyrdom is patterned on and participates in the suffering of Christ.

Cross-References

Mark 14:55-59

False witnesses at Jesus's trial charged him with threatening the Temple — the same charge brought against Stephen. Luke frames Stephen's death as a repetition of Christ's passion in the life of the first martyr.

Exodus 34:29-35

Moses's shining face after meeting God on Sinai — the OT precedent for Stephen's angelic appearance. Both Moses and Stephen are mediators of divine revelation being rejected by their own people.

Acts 7:55-56

Stephen's face shines before his speech; his vision of the open heaven confirms God's approval after it. The framing brackets his entire defence with divine endorsement.

Literary Highlights

The Angel-Face as Divine VerdictStephen's face "like the face of an angel" (v15) is Luke's literary verdict placed before Stephen's speech: whatever the Sanhedrin decides, God has already declared his approval. The detail functions as dramatic irony — the judges are judging a man who is already vindicated by the heavenly court.
Mirror Structure — Acts 6-7 and Luke 22-23Luke constructs a deliberate parallel between Stephen's passion and Jesus's: false witnesses, charges about the Temple, the Sanhedrin, the crowd's rage, and a dying prayer for forgiveness (7:60 / Luke 23:34). The martyr walks the same road as the Master. This structural parallel is one of Luke's most sophisticated literary achievements in Acts.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
6:9

Robertson identifies the Synagogue of the Freedmen as a single synagogue with multiple congregational affiliations, not five separate synagogues. The grammar (tēs synagōgēs tēs legomenēs Libertinōn — the synagogue called of the Freedmen) suggests one body with diverse constituency. Robertson notes that Stephen's engagement with this particular synagogue community shows the Gospel's penetration of every sector of Jerusalem Jewish life.

6:15

"They saw that his face was like the face of an angel" — Robertson notes the imperfect "saw" (eidon — actually aorist, but Robertson treats the visual experience as continuous). He connects the shining face to Moses (Exod 34) and to the Transfiguration (Luke 9:29). The divine radiance is a sign of revelation about to be given.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
6:11-14

Keener gives careful attention to the charges against Stephen. The accusation that Jesus would destroy the Temple and change Mosaic customs (v14) reflects real tension in early Christianity about the Temple's future role. Jesus had spoken about the Temple's destruction (Luke 21:6) and about obsolete Jewish customs (Mark 7:15). Stephen's crime was theological consistency applied to their implications — conclusions the establishment found intolerable.

6:15

Keener notes that "like the face of an angel" was a recognized idiom in Jewish literature for extraordinary radiance or divine approval (cf. 1 Enoch; Josephus's description of holy men). The Sanhedrin members seeing this would have recognized it as a sign of prophetic authority — which makes their subsequent rejection of Stephen's speech all the more culpable.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:13-14

MacArthur defends the accuracy of the charge against Stephen as a distortion rather than a fabrication: Stephen probably did teach that Jesus had fulfilled and in some sense superseded both Temple worship (cf. John 4:21-24) and aspects of the Mosaic covenant. The witnesses' sin was not that they invented the idea but that they deliberately misrepresented its nuance and intention.

6:15

MacArthur connects Stephen's shining face to the principle of 2 Corinthians 3:18 — "being transformed into his image with ever-increasing glory." Stephen has spent himself in the ministry of the word and the Spirit; his face reflects the glory he has been contemplating. This is the natural result of Spirit-filled service, not a one-time miraculous exception.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
6:10

Calvin notes the sequence: the opponents could not refute Stephen's arguments → they secretly recruited false witnesses → they stirred up the crowd. This is the pattern of all bad-faith opposition to truth: when argument fails, manipulation follows. Calvin draws the lesson that the church should not be surprised when theological defeat of opponents is followed by personal attack.

6:15

Calvin interprets the angelic face as God's answer to the court's false proceedings: before the human verdict is reached, the divine verdict is displayed. Stephen is not a condemned blasphemer but a prophet in the succession of Moses — as his shining face declares. The detail is pastoral encouragement: those who suffer for the Gospel are not abandoned.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
6:9

The NET Bible notes the textual question of whether "Freedmen, Cyrenians, and Alexandrians" refers to one synagogue or two or three. The NET follows the interpretation of a single synagogue with multiple ethnic constituencies, noting that a single definite article (tēs) governs the whole phrase. This reading makes Stephen's opponents a single organized community rather than a spontaneous coalition.

6:14

"Change the customs Moses handed down to us" — the NET notes that "customs" (ethē) refers to the Jewish way of life derived from Moses, including but not limited to the written Torah. The charge anticipates the Jerusalem Council debate (Acts 15) about which Mosaic customs are binding on believers. Stephen is being killed for conclusions the church will eventually reach officially.
