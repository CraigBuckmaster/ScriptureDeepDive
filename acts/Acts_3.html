Acts 3

The Lame Beggar Healed

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and close companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author. His medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart, though debated), his literary polish, and his Hellenistic historiographical style are consistent with the educated Gentile physician of Colossians 4.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul under house arrest awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the trial's outcome was known. The silence about the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) and about Paul's death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date. Liberal scholarship often dates Acts c. AD 80–90, largely on the assumption that Luke used Mark; the early date remains defensible and is preferred by a significant minority of critical scholars.

What prompted it

Luke states his purpose in the prologue: to provide an “orderly account” (Luke 1:3) of the origins and expansion of the Christian movement. Acts traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome — geographically fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Theologically, Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel's advance is providentially directed despite (and through) opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Peter Heals the Lame Man at the Gate Beautiful
1One day Peter and John were going up to the temple at the time of prayer — at three in the afternoon.2Now a man who was lame from birth was being carried to the temple gate called Beautiful, where he was put every day to beg from those going into the temple courts.3When he saw Peter and John about to enter, he asked them for money.4Peter looked straight at him, as did John. Then Peter said, "Look at us!"5So the man gave them his attention, expecting to get something from them.6Then Peter said, "Silver or gold I do not have, but what I do have I give you. In the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, walk."7Taking him by the right hand, he helped him up, and instantly the man's feet and ankles became strong.8He jumped to his feet and began to walk. Then he went with them into the temple courts, walking and jumping, and praising God.9When all the people saw him walking and praising God,10they recognized him as the same man who used to sit begging at the temple gate called Beautiful, and they were filled with wonder and amazement at what had happened to him.11While the man held on to Peter and John, all the people were astonished and came running to them in the place called Solomon's Colonnade.12When Peter saw this, he said to them: "Fellow Israelites, why does this surprise you? Why do you stare at us as if by our own power or godliness we had made this man walk?13The God of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob, the God of our fathers, has glorified his servant Jesus. You handed him over to be killed, and you disowned him before Pilate, though he had decided to let him go.14You disowned the Holy and Righteous One and asked that a murderer be released to you.15You killed the author of life, but God raised him from the dead. We are witnesses of this.16By faith in the name of Jesus, this man whom you see and know was made strong. It is Jesus' name and the faith that comes through him that has completely healed him, as you can all see.

Greek Word Study

ἐπὶ τὸ αὐτό (epi to auto)together — the same phrase used in 2:1 and 2:44-47 for the gathered community. Peter and John's going to the Temple is not individual piety but communal practice; the early church maintained Jewish prayer rhythms.
ὥραν τῆς προσευχῆς (hōran tēs proseuchēs)hour of prayer — the ninth hour (3 PM) was the time of the evening sacrifice and a designated prayer time (Ps 55:17; Dan 9:21). The early believers did not immediately abandon Jewish liturgical structures.

Places

Gate Beautiful (Nicanor Gate)
The grand entrance between the Court of Women and the Court of Israel, reportedly made of Corinthian bronze and requiring twenty men to open (Josephus, Jewish War 5.5.3). The most trafficked gate of the Temple complex — the public location maximises the healing's witness value.
Solomon's Colonnade
The covered eastern portico of the Temple Mount where Peter delivers his second sermon. A recognized public meeting space, used by Jesus himself (John 10:23) and the early church (Acts 5:12). It provided shelter and visibility simultaneously.

Context

Gate Beautiful

The location of the "Beautiful Gate" is debated — possibly the Nicanor Gate (the large bronze gate between the Court of Women and the Court of Israel), which Josephus describes as far surpassing the other gates in beauty. Its public location means the healing was witnessed by large numbers.

The Name

Peter's command "in the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth" (v6) is the first of many "name" declarations in Acts (4:10, 12; 5:28, 40). The "name" in Jewish thought represents the person and their authority. Healing, baptism, and proclamation all occur "in the name" — the risen Jesus acts through his name as if personally present.

Cross-References

Isaiah 35:6

"Then will the lame leap like a deer" — the healing is a fulfilment of Isaiah's vision of eschatological restoration. Luke frames the miracle as a sign of the messianic age.

Acts 4:12

The "name" theme continues — "there is no other name under heaven given to mankind by which we must be saved." The healing at the Gate Beautiful is the enacted version of this declaration.

John 5:8

Jesus's healing of a paralytic with the command to "Get up! Pick up your mat and walk" is closely paralleled here — Peter acts in the name of the same Jesus, extending his healing ministry.

Timeline

c. AD 30
First Public Healing
Peter heals a man lame from birth at the Temple gate — the first public miracle of the apostolic church. The healed man's age (over 40, Acts 4:22) makes the miracle undeniable and verifiable.
c. 20 BC – AD 70
The Jerusalem Temple (Herod's)
Herod the Great's expansion of the Temple mount, begun c.20 BC, created the massive platform and courts of the first-century Temple. The Gate Beautiful and Solomon's Colonnade were features of this structure, destroyed by Rome in AD 70.
c. AD 30-33
Temple-Centred Worship
The Jerusalem church continues to use the Temple as a gathering and proclamation space for several years (Acts 2:46; 3:1; 5:12, 20, 21). The temple-relationship is strained by Stephen's speech (7:48-50) and severed by the AD 70 destruction.

Literary Highlights

Healing as Sign of the KingdomThe three-beat response to the healing (v8: he jumped to his feet, began to walk, went into the temple courts walking and jumping and praising God) is the physical enactment of the messianic promise. The man does not just walk — he leaps and praises. Luke's vivid verbs present the body's restoration as a visible doxology.
Silver and Gold — v6 ReversalPeter's "Silver or gold I do not have, but what I do have I give you" contrasts sharply with the wealth of the religious establishment (priests, Temple treasury). The church's power is not economic but spiritual. This irony will be pressed further in 4:13 — unschooled men with authority surpassing the authorities.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
3:6

Robertson notes the exact Greek: "In the name of Jesus Christ the Nazarene, walk" (peripatei — present imperative: keep on walking). The command is not a formula but an exercise of authority in Jesus's name. Robertson observes that Peter does not pray for healing but commands it — consistent with the authority delegated in the commission of Acts 1:8.

3:8

Three verbs in rapid succession: exallomenos (leaping up), periepātei (was walking), ēlthen (entered). Robertson savours Luke's vivid style: the imperfects capture the continuous action of a man who could not stop walking because it was so new to him.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
3:2

Keener provides background on beggars at temple gates — a well-attested practice in both Jewish and Greco-Roman sources. The socially marginal were drawn to temple precincts where charity was expected. The man's daily position at the Gate Beautiful made him a known fixture, meaning the healing would be recognized and verifiable by the entire Jerusalem community (4:16: "a notable sign, and we cannot deny it").

3:6

Keener notes the economic contrast embedded in Peter's words: he has no silver or gold (the currency of the Temple economy) but exercises the authority of the name. The healing demonstrates that the church's power is not the power of wealth or religious institution but the power of the risen Christ.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:6

MacArthur notes that the ability to heal in the apostolic period was a specific, temporary gift given to authenticate the apostles' message before the NT canon was complete. He distinguishes the apostolic sign-gifts from the ongoing spiritual gifts — not to diminish the miracle but to explain why identical miracles are not normative expectations for all believers today.

3:12-13

MacArthur emphasizes Peter's immediate deflection: "Why do you stare at us as if by our own power or godliness we had made this man walk?" The apostolic pattern is consistent throughout Acts — miracles point beyond the apostle to Christ. This is the anti-model of religious celebrity; the healer refuses the crowd's admiration.

3:19

"Times of refreshing" (kairoi anapsyxeōs) — MacArthur connects this to the OT concept of the messianic rest. The refreshing is a foretaste of the final restoration; repentance now participates in eschatological renewal.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
3:13-15

Calvin notes the bold contrast Peter draws: "You disowned the Holy and Righteous One" — "God raised him from the dead." The contrast is between human verdict and divine verdict. The resurrection is the reversal of the unjust condemnation, not merely a miracle of power.

3:17-19

Calvin's treatment of the "ignorance" excuse (v17: "I know that you acted in ignorance") is careful: ignorance does not remove guilt (they had Scripture and prophets) but it does affect the nature of the appeal. Peter does not excuse; he explains — and then calls for repentance. The acknowledgment of ignorance is pastoral, not exculpatory.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
3:15

"Author of life" (archēgos tēs zōēs) — the NET notes archēgos can mean originator, founder, or champion/pioneer. In Hebrews 2:10 and 12:2 it is used for Christ as the "pioneer" of salvation. Here the irony is maximised: the one who is the source and sustainer of all life was killed — but God raised him. The contradiction is the Gospel's scandal.

3:21

"Until the time comes for God to restore everything" (apokatastaseōs pantōn) — the NET notes this is the only NT use of apokatastasis. Some church fathers (Origen) used this verse for universal salvation; the context limits it to the eschatological restoration of "everything he promised long ago through his holy prophets" — i.e., the covenant promises, not necessarily universal salvation.

Peter's Second Sermon in Solomon's Colonnade
17"Now, fellow Israelites, I know that you acted in ignorance, as did your leaders.18But this is how God fulfilled what he had foretold through all the prophets, saying that his Messiah would suffer.19Repent, then, and turn to God, so that your sins may be wiped out, that times of refreshing may come from the Lord,20and that he may send the Messiah, who has been appointed for you — even Jesus.21Heaven must receive him until the time comes for God to restore everything, as he promised long ago through his holy prophets.22For Moses said, 'The Lord your God will raise up for you a prophet like me from among your own people; you must listen to everything he tells you.23Anyone who does not listen to him will be completely cut off from their people.'24"Indeed, beginning with Samuel, all the prophets who have spoken have foretold these days.25And you are heirs of the prophets and of the covenant God made with your fathers. He said to Abraham, 'Through your offspring all peoples on earth will be blessed.'26When God raised up his servant, he sent him first to you to bless you by turning each of you from your wicked ways."

Greek Word Study

μετανοήσατε (metanoēsate)repent — aorist imperative: an urgent, decisive call to turn. Not emotional remorse alone but a fundamental reorientation of mind and life. Peter's use of this word echoes Jesus's inaugural proclamation (Mark 1:15) and John's baptism.
ἀναψύξεως (anapsyxeōs)refreshing — from anapsychō, to cool, to revive. Found only here in the NT. The image is of relief from heat (the desert traveller finding shade and water) — a vivid metaphor for the spiritual relief that accompanies genuine repentance and the Spirit's work.

Places

Jerusalem — Temple Mount
The Temple Mount in the first century was a massive complex — the outer court alone was the size of twelve soccer fields. The southern steps (where crowds gathered) and Solomon's Colonnade provided ideal public-speaking venues. Peter's second sermon takes place in this same public space.
Bethlehem of Judea — Symbolic Geography
The Gospel has moved outward from Bethlehem (birth) to Galilee (ministry) to Jerusalem (death/resurrection) to the Temple (apostolic proclamation). Luke's geography is theological: the story always moves toward the centre before exploding outward.

Context

Prophet Like Moses — Deuteronomy 18

Peter's citation of Deuteronomy 18:15-19 (vv.22-23) applies the "prophet like me" promise to Jesus. Moses promised Israel a prophet who would speak God's words; failure to listen would mean being "cut off from the people." Peter's application is bold: Jesus is that prophet, and Israel's rejection of him was the most consequential listening failure in their history.

Covenant Continuity

Peter's closing argument (vv.24-26) places Jesus in the line of Abraham's covenant promise — "Through your offspring all peoples on earth will be blessed" (Gen 22:18). The Gentile mission is not an afterthought; it was promised to Abraham. Peter addresses Israel first ("first to you") but the blessing flows to all nations.

Cross-References

Deuteronomy 18:15-19

The foundational prophet-like-Moses text, applied to Jesus throughout the NT (John 1:21; 6:14; 7:40; Heb 3:1-6).

Genesis 22:18

"Through your offspring all peoples on earth will be blessed" — the Abrahamic promise that Paul develops in Galatians 3:16, 29.

Acts 7:37

Stephen repeats the Deuteronomy 18 application to Jesus, showing it was a standard piece of early Christian apologetic.

Literary Highlights

Prophet Typology — Moses to JesusPeter's sermon constructs a deliberate typological argument: Moses → prophets → Jesus. Each element of Moses's profile (liberator from bondage, lawgiver, mediator of God's word) finds its ultimate expression in Jesus. The "listen to him or be cut off" warning makes the sermon a call of covenant urgency.
Servant ChristologyPeter calls Jesus "his servant" (pais, vv.13, 26) — the Greek word used in LXX Isaiah for the Suffering Servant. This is one of the earliest NT applications of the Servant Songs (Isaiah 42-53) to Jesus, predating Paul's letters. The servant Christology grounds the soteriological understanding of the crucifixion.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
3:19

Robertson notes that "times of refreshing" (kairoi anapsyxeōs) is a phrase not found elsewhere in biblical Greek. He connects it to the Jewish expectation of the messianic era as a time of rest and renewal. The call to repent is therefore not merely moral but eschatological — turn now while the times of refreshing are being offered.

3:22-23

Robertson on the Deuteronomy 18 citation: the "must listen" (akousesthe) is an imperative of obligation. The "cutting off" language echoes covenantal excommunication (karet in Hebrew — being cut off from the people of God). Peter applies this warning with full seriousness: rejecting Jesus is covenant-breaking.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
3:17-18

Keener discusses the "acting in ignorance" appeal in light of Jewish penitential theology. Ignorance (shegagah) was a recognized category in Jewish law that affected the nature of atonement required. Peter's pastoral move — acknowledging ignorance before calling for repentance — is consistent with Jewish homiletic practice: soften the accusation in order to open the door to repentance.

3:25-26

Keener notes that Peter's identification of his audience as "heirs of the prophets and of the covenant" gives them covenantal standing that makes their rejection of Jesus all the more urgent to address — but also makes their repentance all the more possible. The covenant membership that should have led them to welcome Jesus can now lead them to receive the blessings he brings.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:17-18

MacArthur notes that Peter's acknowledgment of ignorance does not diminish responsibility; rather it explains why repentance is still available. Had Israel committed a deliberate, fully informed sin with no possibility of excuse, the situation would be hopeless. The ignorance means the door remains open.

3:21

MacArthur takes "restore everything" (apokatastasis pantōn) as referring specifically to the prophesied kingdom restoration for Israel — the fulfilment of OT covenant promises. He connects this to the disciples' question of 1:6 (restoring the kingdom to Israel): Jesus there said the timing was the Father's; here Peter says it awaits the national repentance of Israel.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
3:19-21

Calvin is characteristically cautious about the "restore everything" language. He interprets it not as universal salvation or national Israel's political restoration but as the completion of the redemptive plan — when all God's elect are gathered and his promises to the church are fully consummated. The "sending" of Christ (v20) refers to the second coming.

3:25-26

Calvin uses this passage to press the priority of the Jewish mission: "first to you" (v26) shows that the Gospel is not the abandonment of Israel but the fulfilment of Israel's covenant destiny. The Gentile inclusion does not mean Jewish exclusion; it means the Abrahamic promise has reached its universal scope.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
3:20

The NET Bible notes that "who has been appointed for you — even Jesus" (ton prokecheirismenon hymin Christon Iēsoun) is a significant phrase. Prokecheirismenon means "designated beforehand" — the appointment was before history, not merely after the resurrection. The NET translation committee debate: does "appointed" refer to the incarnation, the resurrection, or the second coming? Most likely: Jesus was divinely designated for this role from eternity.

3:26

"Raised up his servant" (anastēsas) — the NET notes this verb can mean either to raise from the dead or to raise up/appoint. In v26 the context (sending him "to you") suggests appointment/commissioning rather than resurrection per se, though Luke uses the same verb for the resurrection in v15.
