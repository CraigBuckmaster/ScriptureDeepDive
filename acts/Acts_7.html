Acts 7

Stephen's Defence and Martyrdom

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and close companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author. His medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart, though debated), his literary polish, and his Hellenistic historiographical style are consistent with the educated Gentile physician of Colossians 4.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul under house arrest awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the trial's outcome was known. The silence about the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) and about Paul's death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date. Liberal scholarship often dates Acts c. AD 80–90, largely on the assumption that Luke used Mark; the early date remains defensible and is preferred by a significant minority of critical scholars.

What prompted it

Luke states his purpose in the prologue: to provide an “orderly account” (Luke 1:3) of the origins and expansion of the Christian movement. Acts traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome — geographically fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Theologically, Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel's advance is providentially directed despite (and through) opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Greek Word Study

δόξης (doxēs)of glory — "The God of glory appeared to our father Abraham." The title connects the Sinai theophany language to the patriarchal call — God's glory is not confined to the Temple but preceded it by centuries. Stephen's argument begins with geography-transcending divine appearance.
κατοικεῖν (katoikein)to dwell/settle — Stephen traces Abraham's journey through Mesopotamia, Harran, and Canaan, noting at each stage that God was present and active. The God of the Bible is the God of the journey, not merely the God of the destination — a key plank of Stephen's Temple argument.

Places

Mesopotamia (Ur)
Abraham's origin — modern southern Iraq. God appeared to Abraham in Mesopotamia, before he left for Harran (Gen 11:31-12:1). The first divine appearance in Israel's covenant history occurs outside the promised land entirely — Stephen's geographic opening argument.
Harran
The city in northern Mesopotamia where Terah settled with Abraham and family. God's call to leave was given in Mesopotamia; Abraham completes the departure after Terah's death. Stephen tracks the movement precisely to show God's initiative operating across multiple geographies.

Context

Stephen's Speech — Structure

Acts 7 is the longest speech in Acts (60 verses). It is organized as a survey of salvation history in three phases: the patriarchs (Abraham, Joseph, vv.2-16), Moses (vv.17-43), and the Tabernacle/Temple (vv.44-50), followed by a direct indictment (vv.51-53). The argument is: God has always worked outside Jerusalem and the Temple; Israel has always rejected his agents; you are doing it again.

Abraham — Called Outside the Land

Stephen begins with God's call of Abraham in Mesopotamia — before circumcision, before Canaan, before the Temple. The point is theological: God's covenantal initiative precedes every Jewish institution. The covenant is older than the land, the law, and the Temple.

Cross-References

Genesis 12:1-3

God's call of Abraham — the foundation of Stephen's argument. The covenant begins outside Canaan, with a God who is not confined to any geography.

Genesis 15:13-14

God's prediction of the Egyptian sojourn — cited in v6. Stephen shows that even the Exodus was pre-planned, woven into covenant history from the beginning.

Deuteronomy 18:15

The prophet-like-Moses prophecy — cited in v37 as fulfilled in Jesus. Stephen's survey culminates in this claim: the whole of Israel's history pointed to Jesus, whom they rejected.

Literary Highlights

The Rejection-Sending PatternStephen's survey constructs a recurring pattern: God sends a deliverer → the people reject him → God's purpose advances anyway. Joseph rejected → becomes ruler; Moses rejected (twice) → becomes deliverer. The pattern builds relentlessly toward the climax: you rejected the Righteous One (v52) — the ultimate rejection.
Three Acts of Salvation HistoryThe speech is structured in three escalating movements: (1) Abraham and Joseph — God works outside the land; (2) Moses — the people repeatedly reject their deliverer; (3) Temple critique — God never needed the building you're defending. Each section answers a different aspect of the charges against Stephen.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
7:2

"The God of glory" — Robertson notes this unusual title appears here and in Psalm 29:3 (LXX: ho theos tēs doxēs). It frames the entire speech: God's glory is not temple-bound. The theophanic glory that appeared to Abraham in Mesopotamia is the same glory Stephen sees in heaven at the speech's end (7:55). Glory bookends the speech.

7:5

"He gave him no inheritance here, not even enough ground to set his foot on" — Robertson notes the pointed irony: the father of the Jewish nation did not own the land he lived in. The covenant was about promise and relationship, not property. Stephen's argument is that his accusers have confused the sign (the land and Temple) with the substance (the covenant relationship).

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
7:2-8

Keener situates Stephen's speech in the tradition of Hellenistic Jewish apologetics — particularly the tradition of retelling Israel's history to show its universal scope. Philo and Josephus both retell patriarchal history for Greek-speaking audiences, emphasizing the universality of God's working. Stephen uses the same tradition but arrives at a radical conclusion: the institutions of Jewish worship were always provisional, pointing beyond themselves.

7:6

The "400 years" of sojourn (citing Gen 15:13) versus the 430 years of Exod 12:40 is a longstanding exegetical puzzle. Keener follows the view that Stephen is using round numbers from Genesis; the Exodus figure is more precise. The theological point — God knew and planned the Egyptian sojourn — matters more than the arithmetic precision.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:2

MacArthur notes that Stephen's opening — "the God of glory appeared to our father Abraham while he was still in Mesopotamia" — directly refutes the premise of the temple charges. If God appeared to Abraham before the Temple existed, and if Israel was constituted as a people before they ever entered Canaan, then the Temple cannot be the prerequisite for God's presence. The Temple is the response to the covenant, not its foundation.

7:9-10

"God was with him" in Egypt — MacArthur uses this as a paradigm: the presence of God with Joseph in slavery and prison is the guarantee that God's covenant faithfulness is not geography-dependent. The same principle applies to the scattered, persecuted Jerusalem church: God is with them wherever they go.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
7:2-3

Calvin notes that Stephen begins with the word "glory" (doxa) to establish his thesis at the outset: the God of Israel is the God of majesty who cannot be confined. Every institution God establishes — law, tabernacle, temple — is a condescension to human weakness, not a limitation on divine freedom. Stephen will show that the institutions themselves pointed beyond themselves.

7:5

Calvin on "no inheritance, not even ground to set his foot on" — this detail demonstrates that the physical possession of Canaan was never the substance of the Abrahamic covenant but only its pledge. The patriarchs lived as strangers in the promised land because they understood that the true inheritance was spiritual and eschatological (Heb 11:9-16).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
7:4

The NET notes a textual issue: Genesis 11:26 says Terah was 70 when he fathered Abraham; Gen 11:32 says Terah died at 205; Acts 7:4 says Abraham left Harran after his father's death. If Abraham was born when Terah was 70, he would have left at 75 (Gen 12:4) — before Terah died at 205. Samaritan Pentateuch reads Terah's death at 145, resolving the chronology. Luke may follow a textual tradition different from the Masoretic Text, or Stephen is presenting a simplified narrative.

7:14

"Seventy-five in all" — the NET notes this follows the LXX (Gen 46:27; Exod 1:5 LXX) rather than the Hebrew text (which reads seventy). Luke uses the LXX, as do most NT authors when citing the OT. The difference reflects a variant tradition about how to count Jacob's descendants, not an error.

The Temple Critique and Stephen's Death

When he had said this, he fell asleep.

Greek Word Study

σκληροτράχηλοι (sklērotracheloi)stiff-necked — literally "hard-necked." A LXX term for Israel's stubbornness (Exod 33:3, 5; 34:9; Deut 9:6, 13). Stephen uses the very words God used to Israel in the wilderness — and applies them directly to the Sanhedrin. The accusation is devastating precisely because it uses their own scriptures.
ἀπερίτμητοι (aperitmētoi)uncircumcised — used of hearts and ears (v51). Circumcision of the heart is the Deuteronomic ideal (Deut 10:16; 30:6; Jer 4:4). Stephen accuses the circumcised defenders of the law of being uncircumcised where it actually matters — in heart and ear.

Places

Outside the City — Stoning Site
Stoning required taking the condemned outside the city walls (Lev 24:14; cf. Heb 13:12). Stephen was dragged "out of the city" (v58). The location is traditionally identified with an area north of the Damascus Gate. The detail that "witnesses" laid their coats at Saul's feet (v58) has legal significance: witnesses were required to throw the first stones.
Heaven Open — v56
"I see heaven open and the Son of Man standing at the right hand of God" (v56). Stephen's vision is the most explicit post-resurrection appearance of Jesus in Acts. The "standing" rather than sitting is often noted: the risen Lord stands in honor of his first martyr — a detail that marks the moment's solemnity.

Context

The Temple Critique — vv.44-50

Stephen's direct argument is from Isaiah 66:1-2: heaven is God's throne, earth his footstool — what house could contain him? This is not anti-worship but anti-idolatry of the institution. The Temple was David's idea and Solomon's achievement (vv.46-47); God graciously occupied it, but never needed it. Stephen is making a point from within the tradition, not outside it.

Always Resisting the Spirit

The accusation of v51 — "You always resist the Holy Spirit; as your fathers did, so do you" — is the thesis statement of the entire speech. Every example from Abraham through Moses was building to this: the pattern of rejection is not a recent aberration but the consistent posture of unfaithful Israel throughout its history.

Cross-References

Isaiah 66:1-2

The direct quotation (vv.49-50) — the prophetic undermining of Temple theology from within the prophetic tradition. Stephen is using Israel's own prophets to make his case.

Deuteronomy 10:16

"Circumcise your hearts, therefore" — the OT basis for Stephen's accusation. He applies the Deuteronomic standard the Sanhedrin would claim to uphold.

Luke 23:34

"Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing" — Jesus's dying prayer. Stephen's dying prayer (7:60: "Lord, do not hold this sin against them") deliberately echoes it. The first martyr follows the pattern of the first and only perfect martyr.

Timeline

c. 959 BC
Solomon's Temple Built
Solomon completes the Temple in Jerusalem — the culmination of David's desire and God's conditional permission (2 Sam 7:12-13). Stephen notes the progression: tabernacle (Moses, portable, functional) → Temple (Solomon, fixed, magnificent). God accommodated the permanence; he never required it.
c. AD 33-34
Stephen Stoned
Stephen becomes the first Christian martyr, stoned outside Jerusalem's walls. His death triggers the first great persecution and the scattering that fulfils the 1:8 commission. Paul (Saul) is present as a consenting witness (8:1).
c. AD 35
Paul's Conversion
Saul's persecution of the church in the aftermath of Stephen's death leads him to Damascus — where the risen Jesus appears to him. The martyrdom Stephen underwent, and the forgiveness he prayed for his killers, may have been seeds in Saul's eventual conversion.
c. 2000 BCc. AD 100

Literary Highlights

Dying Prayers — Mirror of ChristStephen's two dying prayers (v59: "Lord Jesus, receive my spirit" / v60: "Lord, do not hold this sin against them") deliberately echo Jesus's two cross-words from Luke 23 (23:46: "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit" / 23:34: "Father, forgive them"). Luke constructed the parallel across his two volumes. The martyr's death is the disciple's greatest imitation of the Master.
The Son of Man StandingThe only post-resurrection vision of Jesus as "Son of Man" in the NT. Daniel 7:13-14 had the Son of Man coming to the Ancient of Days; at the right hand means the session of authority (Ps 110:1) is established. That Jesus stands (not sits) at this moment is a detail Luke preserves as a mark of recognition — the heavenly court rises for its first martyr.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
7:48-50

Robertson notes that Stephen quotes Isaiah 66:1-2 to establish that the Temple was never God's ultimate dwelling — even Solomon's prayer at its dedication acknowledged this (1 Kings 8:27). Stephen is not rejecting worship but exposing the idolatry of treating the institution as ends rather than means. Robertson connects this to Jesus's conversation with the Samaritan woman (John 4:21-24).

7:55-56

"Jesus standing at the right hand of God" — Robertson notes that the only other NT text using this precise language is Hebrews 12:2 (seated at the right hand). The standing here may indicate that Jesus has risen from his throne in honor of his servant. Robertson accepts this interpretation as the most moving in the passage.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
7:51-53

Keener notes the rhetorical shift in vv.51-53: after fifty verses of historical narration (defensive, epideictic), Stephen suddenly shifts to direct denunciation (prosecutorial). This reversal is deliberate — he has been building to this accusation throughout. The Sanhedrin expected to judge him; he turns the court around and judges them from their own history.

7:58

Saul holding the coats is not a minor detail for Keener. Ancient stoning procedure required the witnesses (who threw the first stones) to divest themselves of outer garments for ease of movement. Saul's function as coat-keeper indicates that he was present in an official or semi-official supervisory capacity — a young man of standing, not a mere bystander.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:55-56

MacArthur treats Stephen's vision as an actual, physical sight of the glorified Christ — not a metaphor or a psychological experience. He notes that this is one of the few post-Pentecost appearances of the risen Jesus recorded in Acts (alongside Paul's Damascus road encounter). The vision grounds Stephen's boldness: he is not facing death alone but is witnessing the living Lord's vindication of him.

7:60

MacArthur on Stephen's prayer for his killers: "Lord, do not hold this sin against them." The aorist (stēsēs — literally "do not set up against them") implies a legal accounting that Stephen prays will not be made. MacArthur notes that this prayer, coming from a dying man with every reason for resentment, is the most dramatic expression of the forgiveness ethic in the NT outside the cross itself.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
7:48-50

Calvin on the Temple critique: Stephen is not abolishing worship but reforming its theology. The error of the Sanhedrin was not in valuing the Temple but in failing to understand what the Temple was for — to point beyond itself to the living God who inhabits eternity. Calvin uses this to warn against the same error in all ages: treating the means of grace as ends in themselves.

7:60

Calvin's commentary on Stephen's dying prayer is among his most moving: "This is the true mark of the children of God, that they pray for those who injure them." Calvin connects this directly to the Sermon on the Mount (Matt 5:44) and to the character of Christ: Stephen's prayer is possible because he is dying in Christ's pattern and by Christ's Spirit.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
7:55

The NET notes that "full of the Holy Spirit" (plērēs pneumatos hagiou) at v55 uses the adjective plērēs rather than the verbal form used elsewhere. The adjectival form describes a stable, ongoing state of being Spirit-filled — not a fresh infilling for this moment but the culmination of the Spirit-filled life described throughout the chapter (6:3, 5, 8, 10).

7:60

The NET notes "fell asleep" (ekoimetē) — Luke's euphemism for death that became the standard NT metaphor for a believer's death (1 Cor 15:6, 18, 20; 1 Thess 4:13-15). The metaphor carries resurrection hope: sleep ends in waking. Luke uses it for Stephen while using more direct death language (apethanen) elsewhere.
