Acts 1

The Ascension and Matthias

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and close companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author. His medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart, though debated), his literary polish, and his Hellenistic historiographical style are consistent with the educated Gentile physician of Colossians 4.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul under house arrest awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the trial's outcome was known. The silence about the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) and about Paul's death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date. Liberal scholarship often dates Acts c. AD 80–90, largely on the assumption that Luke used Mark; the early date remains defensible and is preferred by a significant minority of critical scholars.

What prompted it

Luke states his purpose in the prologue: to provide an “orderly account” (Luke 1:3) of the origins and expansion of the Christian movement. Acts traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome — geographically fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Theologically, Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel's advance is providentially directed despite (and through) opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Greek Word Study

ἤρξατο (ērxato)began — the imperfect of archomai used with an infinitive. Luke opens Acts by calling his Gospel the account of what Jesus "began" to do and teach, implying Acts records what Jesus continued to do through the Spirit and the church.
Θεόφιλε (Theophile)Theophilus — "lover of God" or "dear to God." Whether a specific Roman patron or a symbolic address to all believers, the dedication establishes Acts as part one of a deliberate two-volume historical work.

Places

Jerusalem
The geographic centre of Acts 1-7. The disciples are commanded to stay in Jerusalem (v4) until Pentecost. The city will witness both the birth of the church and its first persecution.
Mount of Olives
The site of the Ascension (v12) — a Sabbath day's walk (c.1 km) east of Jerusalem. The same hill from which Jesus descended into Jerusalem on Palm Sunday. A site of both royal entry and heavenly departure.

Context

The Two-Volume Work

Acts is the second volume of Luke-Acts, the longest single work in the NT. Luke's prologue (Luke 1:1-4) promises an "orderly account" for Theophilus. Acts 1:1 resumes that project. Ancient historians commonly opened their second volume by summarizing the first, which Luke does here in one clause. The forty days of post-resurrection appearances (v3) tie Acts directly to the end of Luke 24 and ground the apostolic witness in verifiable events.

The Kingdom of God

The phrase "speaking about the kingdom of God" (v3) bookends Acts: it opens (1:3) and closes (28:31) the entire narrative. Luke's concern is not merely with church growth but with the arrival and spread of the reign of God — a theological program that unites his two volumes.

Cross-References

Luke 1:1-4

The prologue to the Gospel, addressed to the same Theophilus, establishing the two-volume literary project.

Luke 24:44-49

The resurrection appearances and the commission to wait in Jerusalem — Acts 1 resumes exactly here.

Acts 28:30-31

The closing scene of Acts has Paul "proclaiming the kingdom of God" — the same phrase as 1:3, forming the book's theological inclusio.

Literary Highlights

Two-Volume InclusioActs opens with "what Jesus began to do and teach" (1:1) and closes with Paul "teaching about the Lord Jesus Christ" (28:31). The symmetry is deliberate: Jesus's ministry continues through the church by the same Spirit that empowered him.
Geographical Thesis Statement — v8The commission of 1:8 ("Jerusalem... Judea and Samaria... ends of the earth") is the book's own structural outline. Acts 1-7 = Jerusalem; 8-12 = Judea and Samaria; 13-28 = ends of the earth. Luke announces his plot in the mouth of Jesus himself.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1-2

Robertson notes that the Greek pros ("former") in "my former book" is pros logos, the technical term in ancient epistolary style for the first of a series. The perfect tense of the compound verb "I wrote" (epoiēsamen) implies the Gospel remains in Theophilus's possession and is still valid — not superseded but supplemented by Acts.

1:5

"You will be baptized with the Holy Spirit" — Robertson notes this is a passive verb with the Holy Spirit as the one doing the baptizing: believers are the subjects acted upon, not the agents. The distinction is important for understanding Acts 2.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
1:3-4

Craig Keener (Acts commentary) notes that the forty days of resurrection appearances is Luke's answer to ancient Greco-Roman accusations that the disciples invented the resurrection: Luke specifies the duration, the multiple witnesses, and the "convincing proofs" (tekmēriois — the strongest Greek word for evidence, used in legal and philosophical contexts). This is apologetic historiography.

1:8

Keener identifies 1:8 as Luke's structural programme for the entire book. "Ends of the earth" (heos eschatou tēs gēs) is drawn from Isaiah 49:6, where it describes the reach of the Servant of the Lord. By placing this phrase in Jesus's commission, Luke ties the church's mission to Israel's prophetic vocation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:4-5

MacArthur notes that the command "Do not leave Jerusalem" is strategically important: the Spirit was to come publicly, in the city of the pilgrimage festivals, where the greatest gathering of Jews from across the Diaspora would witness the event and carry the news home. The geographic concentration was temporary and deliberate.

1:8

The "power" (dynamis) of the Spirit is not primarily spiritual gifts but effective witness-bearing. MacArthur emphasizes that the apostolic commission is not optional or limited to the Twelve — it is the charter of the entire church. The progression Jerusalem-Judea-Samaria-ends of the earth is sequential but overlapping, not strictly chronological.

1:9-11

The cloud that receives Jesus is, for MacArthur, the Shekinah glory cloud — the visible form of God's presence in Exodus and the Transfiguration. The two angels' promise "will come back in the same way" specifies: visibly, bodily, in clouds — guaranteeing a literal, physical Second Coming, not a spiritual or metaphorical return.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
1:4-5

Calvin notes that Christ does not immediately depart after the resurrection but remains forty days to confirm the disciples' faith through repeated, undeniable appearances. The command to wait is, for Calvin, a test of obedience: they must resist the impulse to begin their mission before they are equipped. The gift is promised; patience must precede possession.

1:8

Calvin observes that the disciples' question about restoring the kingdom to Israel (v6) reveals how earthly their expectations still were. Jesus does not directly deny the expectation but redirects it: the kingdom they should be concerned about is not national restoration but the advance of the gospel. The Spirit's coming redefines what "restoration" means.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
1:6

The NET Bible notes that the question "are you at this time going to restore the kingdom to Israel?" uses the Greek apokathistanō — the standard LXX word for eschatological restoration (Jer 16:15; Ezek 16:55). The disciples are not simply confused; they are asking a genuine OT-grounded question. Jesus' answer reframes but does not dismiss the expectation.

1:11

"In the same way" (hon tropon) — the NET notes this is a careful phrase guaranteeing the mode of return matches the mode of departure: bodily, visibly, from the sky. It rules out purely spiritual or ecclesiastical interpretations of the Second Coming.

Matthias Chosen to Replace Judas

Those present were Peter, John, James and Andrew; Philip and Thomas, Bartholomew and Matthew; James son of Alphaeus and Simon the Zealot, and Judas son of James.14They all joined together constantly in prayer, along with the women and Mary the mother of Jesus, and with his brothers.15In those days Peter stood up among the believers (a group numbering about a hundred and twenty)16and said, "Brothers and sisters, the Scripture had to be fulfilled in which the Holy Spirit spoke long ago through David concerning Judas, who served as guide for those who arrested Jesus.17He was one of our number and shared in our ministry."18(With the payment he received for his wickedness, Judas bought a field; there he fell headlong, his body burst open and all his intestines spilled out.19Everyone in Jerusalem heard about this, so they called that field in their language Akeldama, that is, Field of Blood.)20"For," said Peter, "it is written in the Book of Psalms: ''May his place be deserted; let there be no one to dwell in it,' and, 'May another take his place of leadership.'21Therefore it is necessary to choose one of the men who have been with us the whole time the Lord Jesus was living among us,22beginning from John's baptism to the time when Jesus was taken up from us. Show us which of these two you have chosen25to take over this apostolic ministry, which Judas left to go where he belongs."26Then they cast lots, and the lot fell to Matthias; so he was added to the eleven apostles.

Greek Word Study

ἐλήμφθη (elēmphthē)was taken up — the divine passive (God is the implied agent). Luke uses the same verb (analambanō) used in LXX for Elijah's translation (2 Kings 2:9-11). The Ascension is not Jesus escaping the world but being exalted by God to the place of cosmic authority.
ἐντειλάμενος (enteilamenos)having given instructions — an aorist participle indicating completed action prior to the Ascension. The apostolic commission was fully given before Jesus left; the disciples carry forward a complete mandate, not a partial one.

Places

Upper Room
The room (hyperōon — upper room) where the disciples gathered (v13) is most likely the same large upper room used for the Last Supper (Luke 22:12). Located in Jerusalem, it became the first gathering place of the Christian community.
Field of Blood
Akeldama (v19) — from Aramaic ḥaqal dema, "field of blood." Located south of Jerusalem in the Hinnom Valley. Still identifiable today; a traditional site has been venerated since the Byzantine period.

Context

The Matthias Election

The election of Matthias (vv.15-26) is sometimes seen as premature (before the Spirit came). But Peter's argument from Psalms 69 and 109 is careful: the Scripture had to be fulfilled. The use of lots (v26) is the final OT-era method of discerning God's will — after Pentecost, the Spirit guides without lots.

The Apostolic Qualification

The requirement that the replacement apostle be a witness of the resurrection from the baptism of John onward (vv.21-22) defines the Twelve's unique function: they are the eyewitness foundation of the church's testimony (cf. Eph 2:20). This category cannot be replicated; it closed with the Twelve.

Cross-References

Psalm 69:25

Cited in v20 for the vacant place — "May his place be deserted." Peter's application to Judas is midrashic: the psalm describes the righteous sufferer's enemies, applied to the one who betrayed the righteous one.

Psalm 109:8

Cited for the replacement — "May another take his place of leadership." Peter reads both psalms as prophecies requiring historical fulfilment, showing Luke's high view of scriptural authority.

Literary Highlights

The Psalms as Prophecy — Midrash StructurePeter's use of two psalms (69 and 109) to interpret Judas's fate models the apostolic hermeneutic throughout Acts: events are interpreted through scripture, and scripture is seen as fulfilled in events. The word "had to be fulfilled" (edei plērōthēnai, v16) is the key — scriptural necessity governs history.
One Hundred Twenty — Symbolic NumberA group of 120 (v15) is exactly ten times twelve — the minimum required for a Jewish community to have its own council (Sanhedrin). Luke may signal that the church is constituted as a legitimate community from its founding moment.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
1:15

Robertson notes that "brothers and sisters" (adelphoi) in v15 is generic — Luke uses it for the mixed group of men and women mentioned in v14. The 120 represents a cross-section of the early community: apostles, women, and Jesus's brothers (who had been unbelievers during his ministry, John 7:5).

1:26

The lot (klēros) in v26 is the last recorded use of this OT divination method in the NT. After Pentecost, the Spirit guides through spiritual discernment (Acts 13:2; 15:28), not by lot. Robertson sees this as a transitional moment — the old and new eras overlap in Acts 1.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
1:14

Keener notes the significance of Mary and Jesus's brothers being present in the upper room. The brothers had been skeptical during Jesus's ministry (John 7:5; Mark 3:21); their presence here shows the resurrection had converted them. James becomes the leader of the Jerusalem church (Acts 15:13; Gal 1:19) — a remarkable trajectory from unbelief to martyrdom.

1:23-26

The casting of lots is not superstition but a well-attested Jewish method of determining divine will (Lev 16:8-10; Prov 16:33; 1 QS — the Qumran community also used lots for decision-making). Keener notes that the prayer preceding the lot (vv.24-25) distinguishes it from mere chance: God is asked to guide the lot's outcome.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:14

MacArthur emphasizes that the 120 devoted themselves to prayer before the Spirit came — not to program or strategy. The order matters: prayer precedes power. The church's first corporate act is not outreach but waiting before God.

1:20-22

MacArthur notes the careful apostolic qualifications: from John's baptism through the resurrection. This is not about spiritual maturity or gifting but about eyewitness standing. The apostolic office requires direct encounter with the risen Christ — which is why Paul's apostleship required the Damascus road appearance (1 Cor 9:1; 15:8-9).

1:26

MacArthur is cautious about the Matthias election: some argue Paul, not Matthias, was God's choice for the twelfth apostle. But the lot was preceded by prayer, and Luke presents it positively. The stronger case is that Matthias was legitimately chosen, and Paul's apostleship was of a different, unique order.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
1:15-17

Calvin observes that Peter's address — citing the Holy Spirit's authorship of the scriptures — demonstrates that the apostles' authority was always derivative, not independent. Peter does not make a decision on his own authority but shows that Scripture has already made it. This is the pattern of all true church governance: obedience to the written word.

1:26

Calvin sees the casting of lots as appropriate for this unique transitional situation and not as a general model for church decision-making. After Pentecost, the Spirit provides sufficient guidance through the word and prayer; mechanical divination is superseded. The point is not the method but the prayerful submission to God's sovereign choice.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
1:18

The NET Bible notes the apparent discrepancy between Matthew's account (Judas returned the money; the priests bought the field) and Luke's account (Judas himself bought it). The NET harmonizes: the field was legally associated with Judas because his money purchased it, even if the priests were the direct purchasers. Ancient practice allowed the donor's name to be attached to property funded by their money.

1:26

The verb "was counted" (synkatepsēphisthē) in v26 is a rare compound used only here in the NT — it means to vote/reckon together with. Luke uses precise legal and civic vocabulary throughout Acts, reflecting both his literary education and his awareness that he is recording events with historical and legal significance.
