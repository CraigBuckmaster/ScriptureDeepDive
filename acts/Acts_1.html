Acts 1

The Ascension and Matthias

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Greek Word Study

ἤρξατο ērxato — began: the imperfect of archomai — "what Jesus began to do." Acts presents itself as the continuation of Luke's Gospel: the risen Lord's work through the Spirit and the church is the direct sequel to his earthly ministry. The word frames the entire book as unfinished action.

ἐντειλάμενος enteilamenos — having given instructions: an aorist participle indicating the commission was fully given before the Ascension. The disciples carry a complete mandate forward, not a partial one.

Context

Acts is the second volume of Luke-Acts — the longest single work in the NT. Luke's prologue (Luke 1:1-4) promises an 'orderly account' for Theophilus; Acts 1:1 resumes that project. The commission of v8 ('Jerusalem... Judea and Samaria... ends of the earth') is the book's own structural outline: Acts 1–7 = Jerusalem; 8–12 = Judea and Samaria; 13–28 = ends of the earth. Luke places the entire plot summary in the mouth of Jesus himself.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 24:44–49The resurrection commission and the promise to wait in Jerusalem — Acts 1 resumes exactly at Luke's conclusion.
  • Acts 28:30–31The closing scene of Acts has Paul proclaiming the kingdom of God — the same phrase as 1:3, forming the book's theological inclusio.
  • Daniel 7:13–14The cloud that receives Jesus at the Ascension (v9) connects to the Son of Man coming with the clouds — Jesus's exaltation to the Ancient of Days.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:4–5

The command "Do not leave Jerusalem" is strategically important: the Spirit was to come publicly, in the city of the pilgrimage festivals, where Diaspora Jews would witness the event and carry the news home. The geographic concentration was temporary and deliberate.

1:8

The "power" of the Spirit is not primarily spiritual gifts but effective witness-bearing. The commission is not optional or limited to the Twelve — it is the charter of the entire church.

1:9–11

The cloud that receives Jesus is the Shekinah glory cloud. The angels' promise that he "will come back in the same way" guarantees a literal, physical, visible return — not a spiritual or metaphorical one.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:4–5

The command to wait is a test of obedience: the disciples must resist the impulse to begin their mission before they are equipped. The gift is promised; patience must precede possession.

1:8

The disciples' question about restoring the kingdom to Israel reveals how earthly their expectations still were. Jesus redirects: the kingdom they should concern themselves with is not national restoration but the advance of the Gospel worldwide.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:6

The question uses apokathistanō — the standard LXX word for eschatological restoration (Jer 16:15; Ezek 16:55). The disciples are not simply confused; they are asking a genuine OT-grounded question. Jesus's answer reframes but does not dismiss it.

1:11

"In the same way" (hon tropon) guarantees the mode of return matches the mode of departure: bodily, visibly, from the sky. It rules out purely spiritual interpretations of the Second Coming.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
1:1

Luke opens with "my former book" (protos logos) — the technical term in ancient epistolary style for the first of a series. The perfect tense implies the Gospel remains valid in Theophilus's possession and is supplemented, not superseded, by Acts.

1:8

"You will receive power when the Holy Spirit comes on you" — the passive construction with the Spirit as agent is deliberate: believers are acted upon, not the initiators. The "power" (dynamis) is specifically for witness-bearing, not primarily for sign-gifts.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:3

Luke's phrase "many convincing proofs" (tekmēriois) is the strongest Greek term for evidence, used in legal and philosophical contexts for irrefutable demonstration. This is apologetic historiography: Luke establishes the resurrection's factual basis before narrating its consequences.

1:8

"To the ends of the earth" (heos eschatou tēs gēs) draws from Isaiah 49:6, where it describes the reach of the Servant of the Lord. By placing this in Jesus's commission, Luke ties the church's mission to Israel's own prophetic vocation.

Verses 12–26 — Matthias Chosen to Replace Judas

For one of these must become a witness with us of his resurrection."23So they nominated two men: Joseph called Barsabbas (also known as Justus) and Matthias.24Then they prayed, "Lord, you know everyone's heart. Show us which of these two you have chosen25to take over this apostolic ministry, which Judas left to go where he belongs."26Then they cast lots, and the lot fell to Matthias; so he was added to the eleven apostles.

Greek Word Study

Ἀκελδαμά Akeldama — Field of Blood: Aramaic ḥaqal dema. Luke preserves the transliteration as historical evidence — a verifiable Jerusalem place-name still known to the community when Acts was written.

κλῆρον klēron — lot: the last use of the OT lot-casting method in the NT. After Pentecost, the Spirit guides through spiritual discernment (Acts 13:2; 15:28). This is a transitional moment: the old and new eras overlap in Acts 1.

Context

The Matthias election (vv.15–26) is the last OT-era decision of the church: lots cast after prayer, using Psalms 69 and 109 as interpretive keys. The apostolic qualification — eyewitness of the resurrection from John's baptism onward — defines the unique, unrepeatable foundation of the church's testimony (cf. Eph 2:20). The presence of Mary and Jesus's brothers in the upper room (v14) is significant: the brothers had been unbelievers during Jesus's ministry (John 7:5). The resurrection has converted them. James will become leader of the Jerusalem church (Acts 15:13).

Cross-Reference

  • Psalm 69:25Cited in v20 for the vacant place — "May his place be deserted." Peter reads the psalm's language about the righteous sufferer's enemies as prophetically applicable to the one who betrayed the Righteous One.
  • Psalm 109:8Cited for the replacement — "May another take his place of leadership." Both psalms are read as requiring historical fulfilment, demonstrating the apostolic hermeneutic of prophetic necessity.
  • Ephesians 2:20The church is "built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets." The restoration of the Twelve preserves the foundation's completeness before the Spirit comes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:14

The 120 devoted themselves to prayer before the Spirit came — not to program or strategy. The church's first corporate act is not outreach but waiting before God. Order matters: prayer precedes power.

1:20–22

The apostolic qualifications — from John's baptism through the resurrection — are about eyewitness standing, not spiritual maturity. The apostolic office requires direct encounter with the risen Christ, which is why Paul's apostleship required the Damascus road appearance (1 Cor 9:1; 15:8–9).

1:26

The casting of lots was preceded by prayer and is presented positively. Paul's apostleship was of a different, unique order — the stronger case is that Matthias was legitimately chosen.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:15–17

Peter's address citing the Holy Spirit's authorship of Scripture demonstrates that apostolic authority is always derivative — Peter shows that Scripture has already made the decision. This is the pattern of all true church governance: obedience to the written word.

1:26

The casting of lots was appropriate for this unique transitional situation and is not a general model. After Pentecost, the Spirit provides sufficient guidance through the word and prayer. The point is not the method but the prayerful submission to God's sovereign choice.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:18

The apparent discrepancy between Matthew's account (priests bought the field) and Luke's (Judas bought it) is harmonised: the field was legally associated with Judas because his money funded the purchase, even if the priests were the direct buyers.

1:26

The verb "was counted" (synkatepsēphisthē) is a rare compound used only here in the NT — meaning to vote or reckon together with. Luke uses precise civic vocabulary, reflecting his awareness that he is recording events with historical and legal significance.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
1:15

"Brothers and sisters" (adelphoi) in v15 is generic — the mixed group of men and women of v14. The 120 represents a cross-section: apostles, women, and Jesus's formerly skeptical brothers.

1:26

The lot (klēros) is the last recorded use of this OT method in the NT. After Pentecost, the Spirit provides guidance through the word and prayer. Robertson sees Acts 1 as a deliberate transitional moment between eras.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:14

The brothers' presence confirms the resurrection converted them. James becomes the leader of the Jerusalem church (Acts 15:13; Gal 1:19) — a remarkable trajectory from unbelief to martyrdom.

1:23–26

Casting lots was a well-attested Jewish method for discerning divine will (Lev 16:8; Prov 16:33; also used at Qumran). The preceding prayer (vv.24–25) distinguishes it from mere chance: God is asked to guide the outcome.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

Literary Structure

Two-Volume Inclusio — vv.1–2Acts opens with "what Jesus began to do and teach" (1:1) and closes with Paul "teaching about the Lord Jesus Christ" (28:31). The symmetry is deliberate: Jesus's ministry continues through the church by the same Spirit that empowered him. The word "began" frames Acts as the continuation, not the conclusion.
Geographical Thesis — v8 — v.8The commission of 1:8 is the book's own structural outline: Jerusalem (chs 1–7), Judea and Samaria (chs 8–12), ends of the earth (chs 13–28). Luke announces his entire plot in the mouth of Jesus — the reader knows the destination before the journey begins.
Acts 1 establishes both the theological foundation (Spirit-empowered witness) and the geographic program (Jerusalem to Rome) that the entire book will unfold.
