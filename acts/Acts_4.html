Acts 4

The Apostles Before the Sanhedrin

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and close companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author. His medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart, though debated), his literary polish, and his Hellenistic historiographical style are consistent with the educated Gentile physician of Colossians 4.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul under house arrest awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the trial's outcome was known. The silence about the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) and about Paul's death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date. Liberal scholarship often dates Acts c. AD 80–90, largely on the assumption that Luke used Mark; the early date remains defensible and is preferred by a significant minority of critical scholars.

What prompted it

Luke states his purpose in the prologue: to provide an “orderly account” (Luke 1:3) of the origins and expansion of the Christian movement. Acts traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome — geographically fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Theologically, Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel's advance is providentially directed despite (and through) opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Peter and John Arrested — Boldness Before the Council
1The priests and the captain of the temple guard and the Sadducees came up to Peter and John while they were speaking to the people.2They were greatly disturbed because the apostles were teaching the people, proclaiming in Jesus the resurrection of the dead.3They seized Peter and John and, because it was evening, they put them in jail until the next day.4But many who heard the message believed; so the number of men who believed grew to about five thousand.5The next day the rulers, the elders and the teachers of the law met in Jerusalem.6Annas the high priest was there, and so were Caiaphas, John, Alexander and others of the high priest's family.7They had Peter and John brought before them and began to question them: "By what power or what name did you do this?"8Then Peter, filled with the Holy Spirit, said to them: "Rulers and elders of the people!9If we are being called to account today for an act of kindness shown to a man who was lame and are being asked how he was healed,10then know this, you and all the people of Israel: It is by the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, whom you crucified but whom God raised from the dead, that this man stands before you healed.11Jesus is "'the stone you builders rejected, which has become the cornerstone.'12Salvation is found in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given to mankind by which we must be saved."13When they saw the courage of Peter and John and realized that they were unschooled, ordinary men, they were astonished and they took note that these men had been with Jesus.14But since they could see the man who had been healed standing there with them, there was nothing they could say.15So they ordered them to withdraw from the Sanhedrin and then conferred together.16"What are we going to do with these men?" they asked. "Everyone living in Jerusalem knows they have performed a notable sign, and we cannot deny it.17But to stop this thing from spreading any further among the people, we must warn them to speak no longer to anyone in this name."18Then they called them in again and commanded them not to speak or teach at all in the name of Jesus.19But Peter and John replied, "Which is right in God's eyes: to listen to you, or to him? You be the judges!20As for us, we cannot help speaking about what we have seen and heard."21After further threats they let them go. They could not decide how to punish them, because all the people were praising God for what had happened.22For the man who was miraculously healed was over forty years old.

Greek Word Study

ἀρχιερεύς (archiereus)high priest — the Sadducean high-priestly family. The captain of the Temple guard (stratēgos tou hierou) was second only to the high priest in authority. The Sadducees' disturbance at the preaching of the resurrection is ideological, not merely political: Sadducees denied bodily resurrection (Acts 23:8).
διαπονούμενοι (diaponoumenoi)greatly disturbed/annoyed — a strong word suggesting deep vexation. The resurrection preaching was not merely seditious but theologically intolerable to the Sadducean establishment. Luke uses the same verb in 16:18 for Paul's exorcism.

Places

Jerusalem — Sanhedrin
The Sanhedrin (synedrion — "sitting together") was the supreme Jewish council of 71 members: high priests, elders, and scribes. It met in the Hall of Hewn Stone on the Temple Mount. Peter and John's examination before the full council marks the church's formal confrontation with Israel's highest authority.
Annas's House
Annas (Hanan ben Seth, high priest AD 6-15) remained the power behind successive high priests after his deposition by Rome. His compound in Jerusalem was likely the venue for informal examinations before the formal Sanhedrin session. Jesus had been brought here first (John 18:13).

Context

Sadducees and Resurrection

The Sadducees rejected bodily resurrection as a Pharisaic innovation without Torah basis (they accepted only the five books of Moses as authoritative). The apostolic preaching of resurrection therefore struck at their theological distinctives, not merely their political interests.

Five Thousand Men

The growth from 120 (1:15) to 3,000 (2:41) to 5,000 men (4:4) in a matter of weeks represents extraordinary expansion. Luke's numbers may be symbolic (ten times the foundational Twelve, fiftyfold from the original 120) as well as historical — both dimensions were important in ancient historiography.

Cross-References

Psalm 118:22

"The stone the builders rejected has become the cornerstone" — cited in v11. This is one of the most widely applied OT texts in the NT (Matt 21:42; Mark 12:10; Luke 20:17; 1 Pet 2:7). Its use here places Jesus's rejection and vindication on the trajectory of divine irony running through the Psalms.

Isaiah 28:16

The "cornerstone" image drawn from Isaiah and Psalm 118 together. Acts 4:11 uses the rejected-stone/cornerstone combination as the crystallized summary of the Gospel.

Acts 4:12

The unique-salvation claim — no other name under heaven — is the doctrinal climax of the healing narrative and Peter's most exclusive christological statement.

Timeline

c. AD 30
First Arrest
Peter and John are arrested by Temple authorities after the healing and sermon — the first arrest of apostles in Acts. They are held overnight and examined by the Sanhedrin the next day.
c. AD 30-66
High-Priestly Families
The Annas dynasty controlled the high priesthood for much of the first century. Annas himself served AD 6-15; his sons and son-in-law Caiaphas (AD 18-36) held it afterward. The family's wealth and Sadducean theology made them natural opponents of resurrection-preaching.
c. AD 70
Destruction of the Sanhedrin
With the fall of Jerusalem in AD 70, the Sanhedrin as a political-judicial body ceased to exist. The rabbinic council at Yavneh replaced it as a purely religious body. Acts captures the Sanhedrin at the height of its power and the beginning of its crisis.

Literary Highlights

Cornerstone IronyThe quotation of Psalm 118:22 in v11 ("the stone you builders rejected has become the cornerstone") is addressed directly to the builders — the Sanhedrin, Israel's religious leadership. The irony is perfect: the experts in building God's people have rejected the very stone God chose. Their expertise is their condemnation.
Unschooled Men with AuthorityThe Sanhedrin's recognition (v13) that Peter and John were "unschooled, ordinary men" (agrammatoi kai idiōtai — literally "unlettered and lay-persons") who nonetheless spoke with extraordinary authority is Luke's rhetorical counter to every credential-based objection to the Gospel. The authority comes from "having been with Jesus," not from Torah training.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
4:8

"Filled with the Holy Spirit" precedes Peter's speech — Robertson notes this is a fresh filling (plēstheis — aorist participle) distinct from the Pentecost filling. The Spirit is not dispensed once but repeatedly given for specific acts of witness. This fits the pattern throughout Acts of Spirit-filling accompanying bold proclamation.

4:11

Robertson on the rejected stone quotation: Jesus had used this very text about himself (Matt 21:42), so Peter is consciously repeating the Lord's self-interpretation. The stone that was rejected is now "the head of the corner" (eis kephalēn gōnias) — placed at the most critical structural point. Peter's use before the Sanhedrin is deliberately confrontational.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
4:5-6

Keener provides extensive historical background on the Annas dynasty. Annas served as high priest AD 6-15 and retained enormous influence even after Roman deposition, placing five sons and one son-in-law in the office. His presence at the examination of Peter and John, though not currently serving, reflects his actual power. Josephus corroborates the family's wealth and influence (Antiquities 20.9.1).

4:13

Keener notes that agrammatoi ("unschooled") does not mean illiterate — it means without formal rabbinic education (scribal training, Torah certification). The Sanhedrin's amazement is not at eloquence per se but at theological authority exercised without proper credentials. They recognized what they had no category for: Spirit-given authority.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:8-12

MacArthur traces the bold progression: filled with the Spirit → addressed the rulers → cited Scripture → declared salvation's exclusivity. The Spirit's filling does not produce vague spiritual feelings but specific, articulate, scripturally-grounded proclamation. The exclusivity claim of v12 is the most controversial verse in Acts for modern pluralistic readers; MacArthur defends it as the necessary implication of monotheism and incarnation.

4:12

"There is no other name under heaven given to mankind by which we must be saved" — MacArthur notes the word "must" (dei — necessity): salvation's exclusivity is not an arbitrary narrowness but a logical necessity given the nature of sin and the nature of the atonement. Only one person has borne the penalty; only one person's name can apply the pardon.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
4:8

Calvin notes that the Spirit's repeated fillings in Acts are not contradictions of the Pentecost outpouring but demonstrations of the Spirit's inexhaustible supply. The same Spirit who came at Pentecost is given freshly for each new test. Calvin sees this as encouragement: the same Spirit who equipped apostles before councils equips believers today in analogous situations.

4:12

Calvin's commentary on this verse is one of the most important in his Acts exposition: salvation through Christ alone is not triumphalism but the logic of grace. If salvation required any human contribution, there would be many ways to achieve it. Because it is entirely the work of Christ, there is necessarily one way: through him.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
4:4

The NET Bible notes "number of men" (arithmos tōn andrōn) — Luke specifies men (andres), suggesting the 5,000 counts males only, meaning the total believing community may have been considerably larger. Luke's precision here may reflect Roman census-style counting by adult males.

4:11

The NET provides extensive textual notes on "cornerstone" (kephalē gōnias). The phrase can mean either the cornerstone at the base (setting the angle of the building) or the capstone at the apex (the final crowning stone). The imagery works theologically either way: Jesus is the foundational reference point or the culminating glory of the building.

The Believers' Prayer and the Community of Goods
23On their release, Peter and John went back to their own people and reported all that the chief priests and the elders had said to them.24When they heard this, they raised their voices together in prayer to God. "Sovereign Lord," they said, "you made the heavens and the earth and the sea, and everything in them.25You spoke by the Holy Spirit through the mouth of your servant, our father David: ''Why do the nations rage and the peoples plot in vain?26The kings of the earth rise up and the rulers band together against the Lord and against his anointed one.'27Indeed Herod and Pontius Pilate met together with the Gentiles and the people of Israel in this city to conspire against your holy servant Jesus, whom you anointed.28They did what your power and will had decided beforehand should happen.29Now, Lord, consider their threats and enable your servants to speak your word with great boldness.30Stretch out your hand to heal and perform signs and wonders through the name of your holy servant Jesus."31After they prayed, the place where they were meeting was shaken. And they were all filled with the Holy Spirit and spoke the word of God boldly.32All the believers were one in heart and mind. No one claimed that any of their possessions was their own, but they shared everything they had.33With great power the apostles continued to testify to the resurrection of the Lord Jesus. And God's grace was so powerfully at work in them all34that there were no needy persons among them. For from time to time those who owned land or houses sold them, brought the money from the sales35and put it at the apostles' feet, and it was distributed to anyone who had need.36Joseph, a Levite from Cyprus, whom the apostles called Barnabas (which means "son of encouragement"),37sold a field he owned and brought the money and put it at the apostles' feet.

Greek Word Study

παρρησία (parrēsia)boldness/confidence — literally "all-speech," meaning freedom to say everything without restraint or fear. This word recurs throughout Acts as the characteristic quality of apostolic proclamation (4:29, 31; 9:27-28; 13:46; 14:3; 18:26; 19:8; 26:26; 28:31). It is the Spirit's signature mark on the church's witness.
ἐπλήσθη (eplēsthē)was filled — aorist passive of plimplémi. The passive voice with the Spirit as implied agent recurs at every key proclamation moment in Acts: 2:4; 4:8; 4:31; 9:17; 13:9; 13:52. Being filled is always something done to the believer, not achieved by them.

Places

The Gathering Place
After release, Peter and John return to "their own people" (v23) — the broader community of 5,000. The specific venue is not named; it may be the upper room (1:13) or a larger house. The community is large enough to require significant indoor space.
Cyprus
Barnabas (v36) is identified as a Levite from Cyprus — one of the Diaspora islands with a substantial Jewish community. His Cypriot origin will matter later: when the church is scattered by persecution, it is unnamed men from Cyprus who first preach to Gentiles in Antioch (Acts 11:20).

Context

The Community's Prayer

The believers' prayer in vv.24-30 is one of the most carefully structured prayers in the NT. It moves from creation (Sovereign Lord, you made...) to Scripture (Ps 2 applied to Herod and Pilate) to petition (now, Lord, enable your servants) to the missionary goal (that signs and wonders may be done). The prayer grounds boldness in the sovereignty of the God who controls all history.

Sharing as Witness

The economic sharing described in vv.32-35 is connected directly to the apostolic testimony (v33: "with great power the apostles continued to testify"). The sharing is not separate from witness but part of it: the community's internal life is itself evidence of the resurrection's power to transform human selfishness.

Cross-References

Psalm 2:1-2

Cited in vv.25-26 — "Why do the nations rage and the peoples plot in vain? The kings of the earth rise up and the rulers band together against the Lord and against his anointed one." Applied to Herod, Pilate, Gentiles, and Israel conspiring against Jesus. The early church read Psalm 2 as the foundational charter of their situation: opposition to Christ is the rage of the nations; God's response is enthroning his King.

Isaiah 37:16

The address "Sovereign Lord, you made the heavens and the earth and the sea" (v24) echoes Hezekiah's prayer before Sennacherib's threat — another moment when God's people faced overwhelming human opposition and appealed to the Creator's sovereignty.

Timeline

c. AD 30
The Believers' Prayer Meeting
Released from Sanhedrin custody, Peter and John report to the community. The church's response is immediate corporate prayer — not strategy or protest. The place shakes; the Spirit fills; they speak boldly (vv.23-31).
c. AD 30-35
Barnabas and Economic Sharing
Joseph Barnabas's sale of his field and donation to the apostles (v36-37) models the generous community described in vv.32-35. This positive example is immediately followed by Ananias and Sapphira's negative counter-example (Acts 5:1-11).
c. AD 32-44
The Jerusalem Community
The Jerusalem church, shaped by shared goods and apostolic teaching, serves as the mother community from which Antioch and the Gentile mission will grow. Its eventual poverty (Rom 15:26) reflects both famine and the economic cost of early sharing.
c. 2000 BCc. AD 100

Literary Highlights

Prayer Structure — Psalm 2 ApplicationThe community's prayer in vv.24-30 is a masterclass in applied Scripture. They do not pray for the opposition to be removed but for themselves to be emboldened. The use of Psalm 2 reframes their situation: they are not the victims of historical accident but participants in the cosmic drama the psalm describes. This reframing from victim to participant is itself an act of theological courage.
Barnabas — Named for a ReasonLuke introduces Barnabas by name (v36-37) immediately after the description of communal sharing — not randomly but as a preview. Barnabas will become Paul's mentor and mission partner (Acts 9:27; 11:25-26; 13:1-3). His first appearance as a generous encourager characterizes everything he will do.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
4:24

Robertson notes the unusual address "Sovereign Lord" (Despota — master of slaves) used only rarely in Jewish prayer but appearing here and in Luke 2:29 and Revelation 6:10. It emphasizes absolute divine authority — the contrast with the human "lords" (rulers, kings) of Psalm 2 is implicit and pointed.

4:31

"The place where they were meeting was shaken" — Robertson notes this is a second Pentecost-like experience: wind at Pentecost (2:2), shaking here. The physical sign accompanies a fresh Spirit-filling and emboldened witness. Robertson cautions against treating these as normative experiences; they are sovereign acts of divine confirmation at critical moments.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
4:27-28

Keener notes the theological precision of vv.27-28: the conspiracy of Herod, Pilate, Gentiles, and Israel is described as doing "what your power and will had decided beforehand should happen." This is the highest statement of divine sovereignty over the crucifixion in the NT outside John 19:11. The enemies' free actions were simultaneously God's predetermined plan.

4:32-35

Keener situates the community of goods in the broader context of Hellenistic philosophical ideals about friendship (koinōnia of property was an ideal in Platonic and Pythagorean communities) while noting the distinctively Jewish covenantal motivation. The church's sharing is not Greek utopianism but the enacted covenant community described in Deuteronomy 15:4: "There need be no poor people among you."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:29-30

MacArthur notes the prayer's remarkable priorities: they ask not for safety, not for the opposition to be punished, but for boldness and for signs to accompany proclamation. This is the apostolic church's missionary prayer — everything is oriented toward witness. MacArthur uses this as a model: church prayer that is primarily about the church's comfort rather than the gospel's advance misses the apostolic pattern.

4:33

"God's grace was so powerfully at work in them all" — MacArthur notes that Luke frames the economic sharing in terms of grace (charis): it is not human generosity that enables the community but divine grace working through human generosity. The sharing is the overflow of the resurrection's reality, not a social program.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
4:23-24

Calvin on the community's prayer response to the Sanhedrin threat: the church's first resort is prayer, not counter-organization or public protest. Calvin uses this as a permanent model: when the church faces opposition, the proper first response is to bring the situation before God, not to immediately strategize human solutions.

4:32

"One in heart and mind" — Calvin connects this to Jesus's prayer in John 17:21 for unity. The unity of the early church is not uniformity but the shared orientation of all hearts toward Christ. Calvin argues that this unity is created by the Word and Spirit, not by institutional structures — which means it can be recreated wherever the Word and Spirit work.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
4:25

The NET Bible notes a textual difficulty in v25: the Greek phrase connecting "through the Holy Spirit" to David is awkwardly worded ("who through our father David your servant the mouth of the Holy Spirit spoke"). The NET footnote provides several manuscript variants. The point is clear regardless: the Psalm is attributed to the Spirit speaking through David.

4:34-35

The practice of selling land and laying the proceeds at the apostles' feet (vv.34-35) creates the apostolic community's redistributive system. The NET notes that "would sell" (imperfect — epipraskon) is iterative: this was a repeated, ongoing practice, not a single collective act. The aorist "put it at the apostles' feet" describes each completed transaction.
