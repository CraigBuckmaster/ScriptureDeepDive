Acts 8

The Church Scattered; Philip's Mission

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and close companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author. His medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart, though debated), his literary polish, and his Hellenistic historiographical style are consistent with the educated Gentile physician of Colossians 4.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul under house arrest awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the trial's outcome was known. The silence about the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) and about Paul's death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date. Liberal scholarship often dates Acts c. AD 80–90, largely on the assumption that Luke used Mark; the early date remains defensible and is preferred by a significant minority of critical scholars.

What prompted it

Luke states his purpose in the prologue: to provide an “orderly account” (Luke 1:3) of the origins and expansion of the Christian movement. Acts traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome — geographically fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Theologically, Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel's advance is providentially directed despite (and through) opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Saul Persecutes; Philip Preaches in Samaria
1And Saul approved of their killing him. On that day a great persecution broke out against the church in Jerusalem, and all except the apostles were scattered throughout Judea and Samaria.2Godly men buried Stephen and mourned deeply for him.3But Saul began to destroy the church. Going from house to house, he dragged off both men and women and put them in prison.4Those who had been scattered preached the word wherever they went.5Philip went down to a city in Samaria and proclaimed the Messiah there.6When the crowds heard Philip and saw the signs he performed, they all paid close attention to what he said.7For with shrieks, impure spirits came out of many, and many who were paralyzed or lame were healed.8So there was great joy in that city.9Now for some time a man named Simon had practiced sorcery in the city and amazed all the people of Samaria. He boasted that he was someone great,10and all the people, both high and low, gave him their attention and exclaimed, "This man is rightly called the Great Power of God."11They followed him because he had amazed them for a long time with his sorcery.12But when they believed Philip as he proclaimed the good news of the kingdom of God and the name of Jesus Christ, they were baptized, both men and women.13Simon himself believed and was baptized. And he followed Philip everywhere, astonished by the great signs and miracles he saw.14When the apostles in Jerusalem heard that Samaria had accepted the word of God, they sent Peter and John to Samaria.15When they arrived, they prayed for the new believers there that they might receive the Holy Spirit,16because the Holy Spirit had not yet come on any of them; they had simply been baptized in the name of the Lord Jesus.17Then Peter and John placed their hands on them, and they received the Holy Spirit.18When Simon saw that the Spirit was given at the laying on of the apostles' hands, he offered them money19and said, "Give me also this ability so that everyone on whom I lay my hands may receive the Holy Spirit."20Peter answered: "May your money perish with you, because you thought you could buy the gift of God with money!21You have no part or share in this ministry, because your heart is not right before God.22Repent of this wickedness and pray to the Lord in the hope that he may forgive you for having such a thought in your heart.23For I see that you are full of bitterness and captive to sin."24Then Simon answered, "Pray to the Lord for me so that nothing you have said may happen to me."25After they had further proclaimed the word of the Lord and testified about Jesus, Peter and John returned to Jerusalem, preaching the gospel in many Samaritan villages.

Greek Word Study

διασπείρω (diaspeirō)scattered — from the same root as Diaspora. Used three times in 8:1-4 (vv.1, 4 verbal; implied v1). The irony is precise: the very word for Jewish dispersion becomes the mechanism for gospel expansion. Persecution produces diaspora; diaspora produces mission. The enemies of the Gospel become its unwitting agents.
εὐαγγελίζομαι (euangelizomai)preached the word / proclaimed the good news — used in v4 and v12. The scattered believers are not passive refugees but active evangelists. Luke's verb choice is deliberate: the scattering produces not silence but speech.

Places

Samaria (Region)
The central region of Palestine between Judea (south) and Galilee (north). Its capital city Samaria/Sebaste was rebuilt by Herod the Great as a Greco-Roman city. Philip's mission to "a city in Samaria" (v5) likely refers to the Samaritan capital or the city of Shechem (near Mt Gerizim, the Samaritan holy site).
The Desert Road to Gaza
The angelic instruction to Philip (v26) directs him to "the road — the desert road — that goes down from Jerusalem to Gaza." Gaza was the last significant settlement before the Sinai desert. The road would be travelled by trade and diplomatic caravans connecting Egypt and Ethiopia to Jerusalem.

Context

Samaria — The Fractured Frontier

Samaria was the ancient Northern Kingdom's territory, populated after 722 BC with a mixed population of Israelites and imported peoples (2 Kings 17). The resulting Samaritanism — an Israelite religion with a different temple site (Mt Gerizim) and a different canon (only the Pentateuch) — was despised by mainstream Judaism. Philip's mission there is the direct fulfilment of 1:8's "Samaria" stage.

Simon Magus

Simon the sorcerer is one of Acts's most complex figures. He believes and is baptized (v13) but is later shown to be in "the gall of bitterness and captive to sin" (v23). His name gives us the word "simony" — the buying and selling of church offices. His case raises the question of whether formal conversion can coexist with an unregenerate heart.

Cross-References

Acts 1:8

"You will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria" — the Samaritan mission of Acts 8 is the explicit fulfilment of the second stage of the commission.

John 4:4-42

Jesus's mission in Samaria — the theological preparation for Philip's harvest. Jesus planted; Philip and Peter/John watered; the Spirit gave growth.

2 Kings 17:24-41

The origin of the Samaritans — the mixed population resettled in Israel after the Assyrian conquest. Stephen's background contextualizes why Samaritan evangelism was theologically significant.

Literary Highlights

Persecution as Mission EngineThe structural irony of Acts 8:1-4 is one of Luke's most important narrative moves: the persecution intended to silence the Gospel scatters its bearers, who then preach wherever they land. Luke makes this explicit in v4: "those who had been scattered preached the word wherever they went." Opposition becomes the mechanism of expansion. The principle recurs throughout Acts.
Simon Magus as FoilSimon's desire to purchase the Spirit's power (vv.18-19) is the precise inversion of the Gospel's economics: the Spirit is given (v20: "the gift of God"), not purchased. Simon has misunderstood the nature of the new order. His case is Luke's warning against importing commercial logic into spiritual reality — a warning that proved prophetic given the later history of "simony" in the church.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
8:4

"Those who had been scattered preached the word wherever they went" — Robertson notes the imperfect tense (euangelizomenoi — were continually proclaiming). The scattering was not a one-time event followed by silence; it produced a sustained, ongoing witness. Every displaced believer became a mobile evangelist. Robertson calls this "the most effective missionary advance in the early church — carried out by laypeople, not apostles."

8:17

"Then Peter and John placed their hands on them, and they received the Holy Spirit" — Robertson notes this is the one case in Acts where the Spirit is given through apostolic hands rather than directly at baptism. The delay (they had been baptized but not received the Spirit, v16) and the apostolic mediation preserved the unity of the Samaritan church with the Jerusalem church at a critical moment.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
8:9-11

Keener provides extensive background on Simon's claim to be "the Great Power of God" (v10). In Gnostic traditions, a figure named Simon Magus became the founder of Gnosticism. Whether this is the same Simon is debated; what is certain is that claims to divine power through magical arts were common in the first-century Mediterranean, and that Philip's Gospel outperformed them decisively.

8:26-28

Keener notes the significance of the Ethiopian eunuch. Ethiopia (ancient Kush) was the southernmost known civilization — the "ends of the earth" in ancient geography. The eunuch's return home with the Gospel fulfils the 1:8 commission in principle, even before Paul's Gentile mission. Keener also notes that eunuchs were excluded from the assembly of Israel (Deut 23:1) — but Isaiah 56:3-5 promised them a place in the new covenant community.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:1

MacArthur notes the deliberate exception: "all except the apostles were scattered." The apostles remain in Jerusalem, providing a stable centre while the diaspora mission begins. This is not cowardice but calling — the apostles' commission was specifically to be witnesses (1:8), and their witness must be anchored in the city where the resurrection occurred.

8:20-23

MacArthur's treatment of Simon Magus is frank: "I see that you are full of bitterness and captive to sin" (v23) suggests that Simon's baptism was not accompanied by genuine regeneration. MacArthur uses this as a cautionary lesson: formal profession and baptism do not guarantee salvation. The test is the heart's orientation — and Simon's heart was oriented toward power and prestige, not God.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
8:4

Calvin on the scattered believers preaching: this demonstrates that the proclamation of the Gospel is not confined to ordained ministers. Every believer scattered by persecution became a witness — not by usurping the ordained ministry but by fulfilling the basic calling of every Christian to confess Christ. Calvin distinguishes between public office (reserved for called and ordained ministers) and personal confession (the duty of every believer).

8:20-21

Calvin on "You have no part or share in this ministry" — Calvin notes the severity: Peter does not say Simon's part is small or deficient but that it is non-existent. The absolute language points to a heart entirely outside the covenant reality, despite external membership. This is Calvin's proof text for the distinction between the visible church (which includes the mixed congregation) and the invisible church (true believers only).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
8:16

"The Holy Spirit had not yet come on any of them" — the NET notes the theological difficulty. Why were the Samaritans baptized (presumably genuinely believing, v12) but did not receive the Spirit until apostolic hands were laid on them? The NET suggests the delay served to prevent the Samaritan church from developing as an independent movement — the apostolic confirmation in vv.14-17 united the Samaritan community to the Jerusalem church structurally before the two could diverge.

8:37

Verse 37 ("I believe that Jesus Christ is the Son of God") is absent from the earliest manuscripts and is widely considered a later addition. The NET includes it with a textual note. Its content — a baptismal confession — reflects early baptismal practice and was likely added by scribes who felt the baptism needed an explicit confession in the text.

Philip and the Ethiopian Eunuch
26Now an angel of the Lord said to Philip, "Go south to the road — the desert road — that goes down from Jerusalem to Gaza."27So he started out, and on his way he met an Ethiopian eunuch, an important official in charge of all the treasury of the Kandake (which means "queen of Ethiopia"). This man had gone to Jerusalem to worship,28and on his way home was sitting in his chariot reading the Book of Isaiah the prophet.29The Spirit told Philip, "Go to that chariot and stay near it."30Then Philip ran up to the chariot and heard the man reading Isaiah the prophet. "Do you understand what you are reading?" Philip asked.31"How can I," he said, "unless someone guides me?" So he invited Philip to come up and sit with him.32This is the passage of Scripture the eunuch was reading: "He was led like a sheep to the slaughter, and as a lamb before its shearer is silent, so he did not open his mouth.33In his humiliation he was deprived of justice. Who can speak of his descendants? For his life was taken from the earth."34The eunuch asked Philip, "Tell me, please, who is the prophet talking about, himself or someone else?"35Then Philip began with that very passage of Scripture and told him the good news about Jesus.36As they traveled along the road, they came to some water and the eunuch said, "Look, here is water. What can stand in the way of my being baptized?"37[The most reliable early manuscripts do not include this verse.]38And he gave orders to stop the chariot. Then both Philip and the eunuch went down into the water and Philip baptized him.39When they came up out of the water, the Spirit of the Lord suddenly took Philip away, and the eunuch did not see him again, but went on his way rejoicing.40Philip, however, appeared at Azotus and traveled about, preaching the gospel in all the towns until he reached Caesarea.

Greek Word Study

σπλάγχνοις (splagchnois)inner being/heart — the Ethiopian eunuch's reading is described without this word, but Philip's question "Do you understand?" uses a word (ginōskeis) implying personal, relational comprehension, not merely intellectual. The encounter is pastoral, not academic.
εὐνοῦχος (eunouch)eunuch — a castrated male official, typically employed in royal courts. The social and religious significance is considerable: eunuchs were excluded from the assembly of Israel (Deut 23:1) but Isaiah 56:3-5 promised them a specific place in the new covenant community ("a memorial and a name better than sons and daughters"). Philip's baptism of this man is the enacted fulfilment of Isaiah's promise.

Places

Gaza (Desert Road)
The ancient Philistine city at the southern edge of Palestine, gateway to Egypt. The "desert road" (erēmos) contrasts with the more travelled northern route — this was a sparse, less-populated road, which makes the encounter all the more providential. The road was part of the ancient international trade route connecting the Mediterranean world with sub-Saharan Africa.
Azotus (Ashdod)
Philip's arrival point after the Spirit transported him from the baptism scene (v40). Azotus is the ancient Ashdod on the Philistine coastal plain. Philip preaches through the coastal towns northward to Caesarea — a route along which he will settle and raise a family of prophet-daughters (Acts 21:8-9).

Context

Isaiah 53 — The Servant Passage

The passage the Ethiopian was reading (vv.32-33 = Isa 53:7-8) is from the Fourth Servant Song — the most explicit OT prediction of the Messiah's vicarious suffering. "He was led like a sheep to the slaughter... In his humiliation he was deprived of justice... his life was taken from the earth." Philip's ability to begin with this passage and proclaim "the good news about Jesus" (v35) models the apostolic hermeneutic: the OT's suffering servant is Jesus.

Ethiopian Christianity

The Ethiopian tradition holds that the eunuch's return home founded the Aksumite church — one of the oldest in the world. Ethiopia adopted Christianity as the state religion in the 4th century (under King Ezana, c. AD 330), and the Ethiopian Orthodox Church traces its origin to this encounter. The Acts 8 episode may be the historical seed of African Christianity.

Cross-References

Isaiah 53:7-8

The direct quotation (vv.32-33) — the passage that opens Philip's Christological exposition. The Servant Song describes a suffering figure whose death is vicarious and whose humiliation leads to vindication.

Isaiah 56:3-5

The promise to eunuchs of a place in God's house and an everlasting name — directly applicable to the Ethiopian eunuch's baptism. Philip enacted this promise without citing it.

Acts 1:8

"To the ends of the earth" — the Ethiopian represents the geographic fulfilment of the commission's final horizon. Ethiopia was the edge of the known world.

Literary Highlights

The Question as Evangelistic ToolPhilip's approach to the eunuch's chariot is the model of Spirit-directed evangelism: he runs, he listens, he asks "Do you understand?" — meeting the man at the point of his own inquiry. The Ethiopian's honest "How can I, unless someone guides me?" is the open door. Luke presents the encounter as a template: the Spirit prepares the seeker; the evangelist meets the need.
Isaiah 53 as Gospel KeyThe fact that the eunuch was reading Isaiah 53 when Philip arrived is Luke's theological statement about the relationship between the OT and the Gospel: the Scripture was already asking the question the Gospel answers. Philip does not impose the Gospel onto an unrelated text — he starts from where the man already is and shows how Isaiah 53 points to Jesus. This is the apostolic hermeneutic in miniature.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
8:30

"Do you understand what you are reading?" — Robertson notes the wordplay in the Greek: ginōskeis ha anaginōskeis — "Do you know what you are reading back?" The verb anadginōskō (read) literally means to "know again" — recognition of already-known letters. Philip asks if his recognizing the letters is producing genuine understanding. The playful question opens the conversation.

8:35

"Philip began with that very passage of Scripture and told him the good news about Jesus" — Robertson notes that the imperfect ēuangelisato indicates Philip took time and developed his argument fully. This was not a three-sentence gospel presentation but an extended explanation starting from Isaiah 53 and moving to the full gospel narrative.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
8:27

The "Kandake" (v27) is not a personal name but a title — the Meroitic word for queen mother of the Kushite royal court. Keener provides evidence from classical sources (Strabo, Pliny) confirming that the Kandake ruled during periods when the reigning king was considered too sacred for direct governance. The eunuch's role as treasurer to the Kandake places him at the highest level of royal administration.

8:38-39

The Spirit's transportation of Philip (v39: the Spirit "suddenly took Philip away") parallels Elijah's translation by the Spirit in 1 Kings 18:12 and 2 Kings 2:16. Keener notes that Luke presents this as a factual account of miraculous transportation, consistent with his historiographical method throughout Acts of recording extraordinary events with the same straightforward style as ordinary ones.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:32-35

MacArthur uses this passage to establish the principle that all Scripture ultimately points to Christ. Philip starts with Isaiah 53 and "tells him the good news about Jesus" — not a separate message superimposed on the OT, but the OT's own meaning unpacked. MacArthur argues that any Bible study that does not ultimately arrive at Christ has not yet arrived at the text's full meaning.

8:36-38

MacArthur notes the order: hearing the gospel (v35) → believing → requesting baptism (v36) → baptism (v38). The sequence is faith followed by baptism — not baptism producing faith. The eunuch's request "What can stand in the way of my being baptized?" implies that his faith is already present; baptism is the public seal of what God has already done.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
8:30-31

Calvin on "How can I, unless someone guides me?" — this is, for Calvin, the argument for preaching and teachers in the church. Private reading of Scripture is valuable; but the Holy Spirit works ordinarily through the ministry of explanation and application. The eunuch had the text; he lacked the interpreter. God provides Philip. Calvin uses this to defend the necessity of the preaching office.

8:38-39

Calvin on the baptism and the Spirit's transportation: the eunuch "went on his way rejoicing" — a detail Calvin treasures. The joy that follows genuine encounter with the Gospel through Word and sacrament is the Spirit's work. Calvin notes the contrast with Simon Magus's unfounded confidence: the eunuch has the joy that comes from a right relationship with God; Simon has only the appearance.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
8:32-33

The NET notes that vv.32-33 quote Isaiah 53:7-8 from the LXX, which differs from the Hebrew in several details. "In his humiliation he was deprived of justice" (LXX) versus "by oppression and judgment he was taken away" (MT). Luke uses the LXX consistently throughout Acts; the theological point — the Servant's unjust suffering — is the same in both traditions.

8:39

"The Spirit of the Lord suddenly took Philip away" — the NET notes the verb harpazō (snatched away, used in 2 Cor 12:2, 4 for Paul's heavenly vision and in 1 Thess 4:17 for the rapture). The same word used for eschatological gathering is here used for a single providential transportation. Philip's sudden disappearance leaves the eunuch without him — but not without Christ.
