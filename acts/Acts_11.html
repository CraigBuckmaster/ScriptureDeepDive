Acts 11

Peter Defends the Gentile Mission; Antioch Founded

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Verses 1–18 — Peter's Report to the Jerusalem Church
1The apostles and the believers throughout Judea heard that the Gentiles also had received the word of God.2So when Peter went up to Jerusalem, the circumcised believers criticized him3and said, "You went into the house of uncircumcised men and ate with them."4Starting from the beginning, Peter told them the whole story:5"I was in the city of Joppa praying, and in a trance I saw a vision. I saw something like a large sheet being let down from heaven by its four corners, and it came down to where I was.6I looked into it and saw four-footed animals of the earth, wild animals, reptiles and birds.7Then I heard a voice telling me, 'Get up, Peter. Kill and eat.'8"I replied, 'Surely not, Lord! Nothing impure or unclean has ever entered my mouth.'9"The voice spoke from heaven a second time: 'Do not call anything impure that God has made clean.'10This happened three times, and then it was all pulled up to heaven again.11"Right then three men who had been sent to me from Caesarea stopped at the house where I was staying.12The Spirit told me to have no hesitation about going with them. These six brothers also went with me, and we entered the man's house.13He told us how he had seen an angel appear in his house and say, 'Send to Joppa for Simon who is called Peter.14He will bring you a message through which you and all your household will be saved.'15"As I began to speak, the Holy Spirit came on them as he had come on us at the beginning.16Then I remembered what the Lord had said: 'John baptized with water, but you will be baptized with the Holy Spirit.'17So if God gave them the same gift he gave us who believed in the Lord Jesus Christ, who was I to think that I could stand in God's way?"18When they heard this, they had no further objections and praised God, saying, "So then, even to Gentiles God has granted repentance that leads to life."

Greek Word Study

ἐκρίνατο ekrinato — stood in God's way: literally 'able to hinder God' (v.17: tou kwlysai ton theon). Peter's argument is unanswerable: if God himself gave the Spirit, what human authority can legitimately refuse what God has already granted? The logic moves from divine act to human obligation.

μετάνοιαν εἰς ζωήν metanoian eis zōēn — repentance that leads to life: the Jerusalem church's summary of what God has done: repentance is not merely moral reform but the gateway to life itself — the eschatological life of the new age. That God grants this to Gentiles is the chapter's theological climax.

Context

Peter's defence is a masterclass in apostolic argument: he retells the entire Cornelius narrative from his own perspective, ending with the unanswerable question of v.17: 'If God gave them the same gift he gave us... who was I to think that I could stand in God's way?' The circumcision party's objection was not about whether Cornelius was saved but about table fellowship — 'you went into the house of uncircumcised men and ate with them' (v.3). The social boundary was the real issue. Peter's response dissolves it entirely: God himself crossed the boundary first.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 10:44–48The Cornelius episode — Peter's retelling makes the sequence clear: Spirit first, then baptism, then table fellowship. The order is God's, not the church's.
  • Acts 1:5"John baptized with water, but you will be baptized with the Holy Spirit" — Peter quotes Jesus's own promise (v.16) as the interpretive key to what happened at Cornelius's house.
  • Acts 15:7–11Peter's Jerusalem Council speech will repeat this same argument, citing the Cornelius episode as the decisive evidence for the Gentile mission without circumcision.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:17

MacArthur on "who was I to think that I could stand in God's way?" — this is Peter's most important theological statement in Acts. The question is rhetorical but devastating: no human tradition, however ancient, can nullify a direct divine act. MacArthur uses this to argue that authentic tradition must yield to clear divine revelation.

11:18

MacArthur notes the significance of the Jerusalem church's response: not grudging tolerance but praise to God. "So then, even to Gentiles God has granted repentance that leads to life" — they accept not merely the fact but the theology. This is the informal apostolic imprimatur for the Gentile mission before the Jerusalem Council formalises it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:17

Calvin on Peter's defence: he does not appeal to his own authority as an apostle but to God's sovereign action. This is Calvin's principle throughout — human authority is always derivative and must yield to direct divine action. Peter could have asserted apostolic privilege; instead he argues from divine precedent.

11:18

Calvin on "even to Gentiles God has granted repentance" — the word "even" (kai) marks the surprise. The Jerusalem community had not expected this; the Gospel's reach has exceeded their theological categories. Calvin uses this as a warning against limiting the Gospel to what we expect God to do.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:12

The NET notes that "these six brothers" (v.12) are named as accompanying witnesses. Luke's consistent attention to witness numbers (two or more witnesses) reflects the Jewish and Roman legal standards for credible testimony. Peter is not making a solo claim.

11:18

The NET notes the passive construction "God has granted repentance" — the same divine-passive used throughout Acts for salvation (2:47; 13:48). Luke consistently presents salvation as God's initiative and gift, not merely human response.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
11:12

"These six brothers also went with me" — Robertson notes that Peter brings six witnesses to corroborate his account. This is the legal two-or-three witnesses requirement (Deut 19:15) more than satisfied: six independent witnesses to the same events. Peter is presenting legally sufficient testimony.

11:17

"Who was I to think that I could stand in God's way?" — Robertson notes the aorist infinitive kwlysai (to hinder) with the genitive absolute: "God having given them the equal gift." The argument is from accomplished divine action to obligatory human acceptance.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:3

Keener notes that the circumcision party's objection was about table fellowship, not salvation per se. In Jewish practice, eating with Gentiles risked ritual impurity. The social dimension of Peter's crossing was as significant as the theological one — he had eaten in a Gentile home, making himself ritually impure by rabbinic standards.

11:18

"God has granted repentance that leads to life" — Keener notes the theological precision: repentance is described as something God grants (edōken), not merely something humans decide. Even the Gentiles' turning to God is presented as divine gift rather than human achievement.

Verses 19–30 — The Church at Antioch — First Called Christians
19Now those who had been scattered by the persecution that broke out when Stephen was killed traveled as far as Phoenicia, Cyprus and Antioch, spreading the word only among Jews.20Some of them, however, men from Cyprus and Cyrene, went to Antioch and began to speak to Greeks also, telling them the good news about the Lord Jesus.21The Lord's hand was with them, and a great number of people believed and turned to the Lord.22News of this reached the church in Jerusalem, and they sent Barnabas to Antioch.23When he arrived and saw what the grace of God had done, he was glad and encouraged them all to remain true to the Lord with all their hearts.24He was a good man, full of the Holy Spirit and faith, and a great number of people were brought to the Lord.25Then Barnabas went to Tarsus to look for Saul,26and when he found him, he brought him to Antioch. So for a whole year Barnabas and Saul met with the church and taught great numbers of people. The disciples were called Christians first at Antioch.27During this time some prophets came down from Jerusalem to Antioch.28One of them, named Agabus, stood up and through the Spirit predicted that a severe famine would spread over the entire Roman world. (This happened during the reign of Claudius.)29The disciples, as each one was able, decided to provide help for the brothers and sisters living in Judea.30This they did, sending their gift to the elders through Barnabas and Saul.

Greek Word Study

Χριστιανούς Christianous — Christians: the first use of the name 'Christian' in Scripture (v.26). Formed from Greek Christianos + Latin -ianus suffix (as in 'Herodians,' 'Caesarians') — meaning 'belonging to or partisans of Christ.' The name was likely coined by outsiders as a nickname, not by believers themselves. It stuck permanently.

χεὶρ κυρίου cheir kyriou — the Lord's hand: a recurring OT phrase for divine power in action (Exod 9:3; Isa 59:1; Ezek 1:3). Luke uses it to attribute the Antioch conversions directly to God: unnamed, unofficial men from Cyprus and Cyrene achieved something remarkable not by their credentials but by the Lord's active presence.

Places

Antioch on the Orontes
36.2021° N, 36.1603° E
The third largest city of the Roman Empire after Rome and Alexandria — population c.500,000. Located in Syria, it became the headquarters for the Gentile mission and the base from which all three of Paul's missionary journeys were launched. The church 'first called Christians' here will become the dominant expression of the faith outside Judea.
Tarsus
36.9145° N, 34.8936° E
Paul's home city in Cilicia (southeastern Turkey) — a major Hellenistic city, seat of philosophy and rhetoric, and a Roman citizen city. Barnabas retrieves Paul from here (v.25) — possibly 8–10 years after his Damascus conversion. The years in Tarsus were not wasted: they were Paul's hidden preparation for global mission.

Context

The Antioch church is one of the most important turning points in Acts: men from Cyprus and Cyrene — unnamed, unofficial, not from Jerusalem — spontaneously begin preaching to Greeks (v.20). This is the first deliberate Gentile mission not initiated by apostolic directive. 'The Lord's hand was with them' (v.21) is Luke's theological endorsement: God preceded the official church structure. Barnabas's retrieval of Saul from Tarsus (vv.25–26) reunites the two men who will carry the Gentile mission to the ends of the earth. The name 'Christians' first appears here — in the most cosmopolitan city of the Empire, not in Jerusalem.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 8:1–4The scattering after Stephen's death — the event that sends these unnamed missionaries as far as Antioch. What the persecutors intended as silence becomes the seed of the world mission.
  • Acts 13:1–3The Antioch church launches the first missionary journey — Paul and Barnabas commissioned by the Spirit through the gathered community. The church "first called Christians" becomes the church that sends the Gospel to the ends of the earth.
  • Galatians 2:11–14Paul's later confrontation with Peter at Antioch over table fellowship shows the church's ongoing struggle with the Jewish-Gentile boundary — the issue Acts 10–11 addresses is never completely resolved at the community level.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:23–24

MacArthur on Barnabas's reaction: "he was glad and encouraged them all to remain true to the Lord with all their hearts." The mark of a good pastor is that he sees grace at work — not suspicion but gladness — and his first impulse is encouragement rather than correction. Barnabas's character is consistently one of seeing the best and calling it forth.

11:26

MacArthur notes that the name "Christian" was likely a term of mockery in its origin — "Christ-people" or "Christ-followers" used by Antiochene pagans who saw a distinctive community. The fact that it stuck and became a badge of honour is itself a testimony to the community's visible distinctiveness.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:20–21

Calvin on the unnamed missionaries: God does not wait for official structures before acting. These men had no commission from Jerusalem, no apostolic authorization — only the Spirit's prompting and the Gospel's power. Calvin uses this to argue that the church's mission cannot be limited to ordained ministers: the Spirit works through every believer who speaks the word.

11:29–30

Calvin on the famine relief: this is the practical expression of the covenant community across geographic and ethnic boundaries. The Antioch church — predominantly Gentile — sends relief to the Jerusalem church — predominantly Jewish. The relief offering is the economic embodiment of the theological claim that in Christ there is neither Jew nor Greek.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:20

The NET Bible prefers Hellēnas (Greeks/Gentiles) over Hellēnistas (Hellenistic Jews) based on manuscript evidence and contextual logic: if they were preaching to Hellenistic Jews (as in v.19), no contrast would exist. The contrast with v.19 ("only among Jews") requires Hellēnas — full Gentiles.

11:26

The NET notes that chrēmatisai (to be called) in classical Greek was used for official designations — names given by public authorities or communities. The "first at Antioch" suggests this was a new coinage, not the continuation of an existing label. The cosmopolitan population of Antioch had sufficient cultural awareness of the movement to coin a distinctive name for it.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
11:20

"Began to speak to Greeks also" — Robertson notes the textual variant: some manuscripts read Hellēnistas (Hellenistic Jews) rather than Hellēnas (Greeks/Gentiles). The NET and most modern translations prefer Hellēnas, which makes this the first deliberate preaching to full Gentiles rather than Greek-speaking Jews. The distinction matters enormously for the history of the mission.

11:26

"The disciples were called Christians first at Antioch" — Robertson notes the passive chrēmatisai (were called) may indicate the name was given by outsiders (the Antiochene public) rather than adopted by believers themselves. Its subsequent adoption shows how quickly an external label became a mark of identity.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:20

Keener notes the significance of the unnamed missionaries from Cyprus and Cyrene. They are not apostles, not commissioned by Jerusalem — they are ordinary disciples who spontaneously extended the mission beyond the expected boundaries. The Gentile mission's origin is grass-roots, not hierarchical, confirming that the Spirit works through the whole body.

11:28

Keener provides historical context for Agabus's famine prediction. Several famines occurred during Claudius's reign (AD 41–54), including a severe one in Judea around AD 45–48 documented by Josephus (Antiquities 20.2.5; 20.5.2). Luke's parenthetical confirmation ("This happened during the reign of Claudius") is one of Acts' verifiable historical anchors.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

Literary Structure

Barnabas — The Bridge Builder — vv.22–26Barnabas appears at every critical junction in the early church's expansion: he vouched for Paul at Jerusalem (9:27), he was sent to Antioch to assess the Gentile mission (11:22), and he retrieved Paul from Tarsus (11:25). His consistent role is to bridge what others have separated — communities, people, movements. Luke's repeated focus on Barnabas is a literary portrait of the Spirit's instrument for unity.
Unnamed Missionaries — The Grass-Roots Mission — vv.19–21The men from Cyprus and Cyrene who spontaneously preached to Greeks in Antioch (v.20) are never named in Acts. Yet their anonymous initiative launched what became the headquarters of the world mission. Luke's omission of their names is itself a theological statement: the mission belongs to the Lord whose hand was with them, not to any individual's reputation.
Acts 11 presents two complementary pictures of the mission's advance: the Jerusalem church's formal acceptance of the Cornelius precedent (vv.1–18) and the Antioch church's spontaneous grass-roots Gentile mission (vv.19–30) — official approval and organic initiative working together under the same Spirit.
