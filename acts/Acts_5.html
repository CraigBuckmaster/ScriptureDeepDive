Acts 5

Ananias and Sapphira; Apostles Imprisoned Again

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and close companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author. His medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart, though debated), his literary polish, and his Hellenistic historiographical style are consistent with the educated Gentile physician of Colossians 4.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul under house arrest awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the trial's outcome was known. The silence about the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) and about Paul's death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date. Liberal scholarship often dates Acts c. AD 80–90, largely on the assumption that Luke used Mark; the early date remains defensible and is preferred by a significant minority of critical scholars.

What prompted it

Luke states his purpose in the prologue: to provide an “orderly account” (Luke 1:3) of the origins and expansion of the Christian movement. Acts traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome — geographically fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Theologically, Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel's advance is providentially directed despite (and through) opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Ananias and Sapphira — The Lie Against the Spirit
1Now a man named Ananias, together with his wife Sapphira, also sold a piece of property.2With his wife's full knowledge he kept back part of the money for himself, but brought the rest and put it at the apostles' feet.3Then Peter said, "Ananias, how is it that Satan has so filled your heart that you have lied to the Holy Spirit and have kept for yourself some of the money you received for the land?4Didn't it belong to you before it was sold? And after it was sold, wasn't the money at your disposal? What made you think of doing such a thing? You have not lied just to human beings but to God."5When Ananias heard this, he fell down and died. And great fear seized all who heard what had happened.6Then some young men came forward, wrapped up his body, and carried him out and buried him.7About three hours later his wife came in, not knowing what had happened.8Peter asked her, "Tell me, is this the price you and Ananias got for the land?" "Yes," she said, "that is the price."9Peter said to her, "How could you conspire to test the Spirit of the Lord? Listen! The feet of the men who buried your husband are at the door, and they will carry you out also."10At that moment she fell down at his feet and died. Then the young men came in and, finding her dead, carried her out and buried her beside her husband.11Great fear seized the whole church and all who heard about these events.12The apostles performed many signs and wonders among the people. And all the believers used to meet together in Solomon's Colonnade.13No one else dared join them, even though they were highly regarded by the people.14Nevertheless, more and more men and women believed in the Lord and were added to their number.15As a result, people brought the sick into the streets and laid them on beds and mats so that at least Peter's shadow might fall on some of them as he passed by.16Crowds gathered also from the towns around Jerusalem, bringing their sick and those tormented by impure spirits, and all of them were healed.

Greek Word Study

ἐνοσφίσατο (enosphisato)kept back — the same rare verb used in the LXX for Achan's sin at Jericho (Josh 7:1 — nosphi, to set apart for oneself what belongs to God). Luke's word choice connects Ananias to Israel's most notorious act of covenant theft. The verb implies deliberate concealment of what was owed.
ψεύσασθαί σε τὸ πνεῦμα (pseusasthai se to pneuma)you have lied to the Holy Spirit — the accusation identifies the Holy Spirit as the direct object of the lie, establishing the Spirit's full personhood. One cannot lie to an impersonal force. Peter's statement is implicitly Trinitarian: lying to the Spirit is lying to God (v4).

Places

Solomon's Colonnade
The Eastern Portico of the Temple Mount, the church's regular public gathering place (v12). "No one else dared join them" (v13) likely refers to uncommitted observers who respected the community's distinctiveness — unlike the 5,000 who had genuinely believed.
Jerusalem Streets
The remarkable detail of v15 — Peter's shadow healing the sick — places the apostolic ministry in the public streets of Jerusalem. The city's streets become mission territory; the church is not yet confined to private spaces.

Context

Covenant Boundary

The Ananias and Sapphira episode establishes the boundaries of the new covenant community. The sharing of the Jerusalem church was voluntary (4:34-37), but once a commitment was made and publicly staged, breaking it was a direct challenge to God's presence in the community. The severity of the judgment reflects the gravity of the founding moment — this is the church's equivalent of Nadab and Abihu (Lev 10:1-2).

Fear as Church-Former

The "great fear" (v11) that follows is explicitly applied to "the whole church and all who heard." Luke presents this fear as formative — it defines the community's awareness of God's holiness. The pattern will be repeated: when God acts in judgment, fear produces reverence, and reverence produces authentic worship.

Cross-References

Joshua 7:1

Achan's keeping back devoted things (using the same LXX verb as v2) brought defeat on Israel. The covenant community's integrity depends on full surrender — partial obedience is covenant violation.

Leviticus 10:1-2

Nadab and Abihu's unauthorized fire brought immediate death — the precedent for divine judgment protecting the holiness of a newly inaugurated covenant community.

Acts 5:11

"Great fear seized the whole church" — the community's awe-response mirrors the response to Sinai theophany, signaling the gravity of the new covenant's inauguration.

Literary Highlights

Ananias/Sapphira as Anti-BarnabasActs 4:36-37 introduced Barnabas as the exemplary giver; Acts 5:1-11 immediately introduces Ananias and Sapphira as his dark mirror. The contrast is Luke's literary device for presenting the community's standards: generosity is praised; hypocrisy is exposed. The pairing is deliberate and instructive.
Three-Fold Intensification — vv.12-16The summary of apostolic ministry in vv.12-16 escalates in three beats: signs and wonders in Solomon's Colonnade → sick laid in streets for Peter's shadow → sick brought from surrounding towns. Each beat expands the geographic and physical scope of the healing ministry, showing the Spirit's advance despite (and through) judgment.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
5:3

Robertson notes that "filled your heart" (eplērōsen tēn kardian sou) is the same construction as "filled with the Holy Spirit" — but here Satan is the filler. The contrast is Luke's implicit teaching on spiritual lordship: every heart is filled by one master or another. There is no neutral ground.

5:5

The immediate death upon Peter's pronouncement is not presented by Luke as Peter's act but as God's judgment. Robertson notes Luke does not say Peter killed Ananias; he reports the sequence without causal attribution to Peter. The judgment is sovereign and direct.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1-4

Keener notes that the sin of Ananias is not in keeping part of the proceeds (v4 explicitly states the money was his to keep) but in the deliberate staging of the gift as total — the lie to appear more generous than they were. In the community context of 4:32-35, where sharing was the community norm, to pretend full surrender while keeping back part was a direct claim on apostolic affirmation they had not earned.

5:12-16

Keener provides background on the idea of a healer's shadow carrying healing power — found in Hellenistic miracle traditions and Jewish accounts of holy men. Luke presents Peter's shadow (v15) without theological endorsement or condemnation; the people's faith, however misunderstood, is met with healing. This is consistent with Luke's pattern: God works graciously even through imperfect understanding.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:3-4

MacArthur draws out the implicit Trinitarian argument: v3 says they lied to the Holy Spirit; v4 says they lied to God. The equation is direct — the Holy Spirit is God. MacArthur uses this to ground the full deity of the Spirit from narrative evidence rather than doctrinal formulation.

5:11

MacArthur notes that this is the first use of the word "church" (ekklēsia) in Acts — a telling placement. The church is defined at the moment of its first internal discipline. The community of grace is simultaneously the community of holiness; God's love and God's holiness are not in tension here but are expressed together.

5:15-16

MacArthur treats the shadow healings (v15) as evidence that God can work through any means he chooses without establishing those means as normative. The shadow was not a technique to be repeated but a sovereign expression of apostolic authority at a specific founding moment. He cautions against using this verse as warrant for relic theology.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
5:3-4

Calvin connects the Holy Spirit's identification with God to his broader pneumatology: the Spirit is not a lesser divine energy but fully God, fully personal, and therefore capable of being sinned against in a way that constitutes direct offense against the Trinity. The passage is, for Calvin, one of the clearest NT proofs of the Spirit's divine personhood.

5:5

Calvin on the severity of the judgment: God chose this founding moment to establish the principle that his church is not a human institution but a covenant community under his immediate governance. Just as the first sin in the new creation (Eden) brought death, the first hypocrisy in the new covenant community brings death — to establish the seriousness of what has begun.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
5:2

The NET Bible notes that "kept back" (enosphisato) is the same verb used in the LXX of Josh 7:1 for Achan's sin. The verbal echo is almost certainly intentional — Luke signals that the Ananias episode is the Acts equivalent of the Achan story: a covenant violation at the founding moment of God's people's new beginning.

5:9

"Test the Spirit of the Lord" (peirasai to pneuma Kyriou) echoes Israel's testing of God at Massah (Exod 17:7; Ps 95:8-9). The community's hypocritical pretense is framed as a repetition of Israel's wilderness sin — putting God to the test to see how much deception he will tolerate.

The Apostles Arrested, Freed, and Flogged
17Then the high priest and all his associates, who were members of the party of the Sadducees, were filled with jealousy.18They arrested the apostles and put them in the public jail.19But during the night an angel of the Lord opened the doors of the jail and brought them out.20"Go, stand in the temple courts," he said, "and tell the people all about this new life."21At daybreak they entered the temple courts, as they had been told, and began to teach. When the high priest and his associates arrived, they called together the Sanhedrin — the full assembly of the elders of Israel — and sent to the jail for the apostles.22But on arriving at the jail, the officers did not find them there. So they went back and reported,23"We found the jail securely locked, with the guards standing at the doors; but when we opened them, we found no one inside."24On hearing this report, the captain of the temple guard and the chief priests were at a loss, wondering what this might lead to.25Then someone came and said, "Look! The men you put in jail are standing in the temple courts teaching the people."26At that, the captain went with his officers and brought the apostles. They did not use force, because they feared that the people would stone them.27The apostles were brought in and made to appear before the Sanhedrin to be questioned by the high priest.28"We gave you strict orders not to teach in this name!" he said. "Yet you have filled Jerusalem with your teaching and are determined to make us guilty of this man's blood."29Peter and the other apostles replied: "We must obey God rather than human beings!30The God of our ancestors raised Jesus from the dead — whom you killed by hanging him on a cross.31God exalted him to his own right hand as Prince and Savior that he might bring Israel to repentance and forgive their sins.32We are witnesses of these things, and so is the Holy Spirit, whom God has given to those who obey him."33When they heard this, they were furious and wanted to put them to death.34But a Pharisee named Gamaliel, a teacher of the law, who was honored by all the people, stood up in the Sanhedrin and ordered that the men be put outside for a little while.35Then he addressed the Sanhedrin: "Men of Israel, consider carefully what you intend to do to these men.36Some time ago Theudas appeared, claiming to be somebody, and about four hundred men rallied to him. He was killed, all his followers were dispersed, and it all came to nothing.37After him, Judas the Galilean appeared in the days of the census and led a band of people in revolt. He too was killed, and all his followers were scattered.38Therefore, in the present case I advise you: Leave these men alone! Let them go! For if their purpose or activity is of human origin, it will fail.39But if it is from God, you will not be able to stop these men; you will only find yourselves fighting against God."40His speech persuaded them. They called the apostles in and had them flogged. Then they ordered them not to speak in the name of Jesus, and let them go.41The apostles left the Sanhedrin, rejoicing because they had been counted worthy of suffering disgrace for the Name.42Day after day, in the temple courts and from house to house, they never stopped teaching and proclaiming the good news that Jesus is the Messiah.

Greek Word Study

πειθαρχεῖν δεῖ θεῷ (peitharchein dei theō)We must obey God — the most memorable line of Acts 5. The verb peitharcheō means "to be subject to authority" — it is obedience not just as action but as recognized lordship. The "must" (dei) is the same word for theological necessity used throughout Luke-Acts. Obedience to God is not optional but ontologically required by who God is.
ἀρχηγόν (archēgon)Prince/Pioneer — also used in Acts 3:15 ("Author of life") and Hebrews 2:10; 12:2 ("Pioneer of salvation"). The word combines the ideas of originator, leader, and champion. Jesus is the one who blazed the trail of resurrection and now leads his people through it.

Places

The Public Jail — Jerusalem
The public jail (tērēsis dēmosia, v18) was distinct from private detention. Its public, official nature means the miraculous escape (v19-20) was verifiable and undeniable — the guards were present, the doors were locked, the apostles were gone.
House to House
The closing summary (v42) reiterates the pattern of Acts 2:46: temple courts (public proclamation) and house to house (community formation). The Jerusalem church's dual-venue model combines public boldness with intimate discipleship.

Context

Gamaliel's Pragmatism

Gamaliel's speech (vv.34-39) is one of the most debated passages in Acts. His argument — if it's human, it will fail; if it's divine, you cannot stop it — sounds like open-minded wisdom but is actually pragmatic rather than principled. He does not commit to the truth of the resurrection claim; he simply counsels caution. Luke presents it as providentially useful without endorsing Gamaliel's theological epistemology.

Rejoicing in Suffering

The apostles leave the Sanhedrin "rejoicing because they had been counted worthy of suffering disgrace for the Name" (v41). This is the first record of Christians rejoicing in persecution — a theme that will recur throughout Acts and the Epistles (Rom 5:3; James 1:2; 1 Pet 4:13). The logic is the theology of the cross: suffering for Christ is participation in his pattern, not a curse.

Cross-References

Acts 4:19-20

Peter's "We must obey God rather than human beings" is the second iteration of the same principle — first stated before release (4:19-20), now repeated after flogging (5:29). The repetition reinforces the apostolic commitment: no threat will silence the witness.

Daniel 3:16-18

Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego's "we will not serve your gods" before Nebuchadnezzar is the OT parallel — faithful witness before earthly authority that claims total obedience.

1 Peter 4:13

"Rejoice inasmuch as you participate in the sufferings of Christ" — the theology Peter will write later is the theology he lives in Acts 5:41.

Theudas and Judas
Gamaliel's examples of failed messiahs (vv.36-37) reflect the volatile period of Roman-Jewish tension — multiple messianic claimants arose and were crushed. The political context explains the Sanhedrin's anxiety about the apostles' movement.
c. 2000 BCc. AD 100

Literary Highlights

The Angel's Command — v20"Tell the people all about this new life" (ta rhēmata tēs zōēs tautēs — the words of this life). The angelic commission is Luke's definition of apostolic preaching: not primarily theological argument but the proclamation of a new order of existence — life as defined by resurrection. The phrase is the theological thesis of Acts.
Gamaliel's Argument StructureGamaliel's two precedents (Theudas; Judas the Galilean) form a rhetorical argument from analogy: all human movements self-destruct; if this one is human, it will too. The implied conclusion is already undermined for the reader who knows the outcome: the Christian movement did not self-destruct. Luke's placement of the speech is ironic — the best argument against persecuting the apostles comes from the establishment's own most respected voice.

Robertson's Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Faithful Paraphrase
5:29

Robertson notes the absolute priority expressed in "We must obey God rather than human beings" — the comparative construction (mallon ē) is not a grudging concession but a categorical statement. Robertson observes that this principle does not mean Christians never obey human government; it means when genuine conflict exists between divine and human command, there is no question which takes precedence.

5:41

"Counted worthy of suffering disgrace" (katēxiōthēsan atimasthēnai) — Robertson savours the paradox: "they were honored to be dishonored." The aorist passive "were counted worthy" implies divine action — God counted them worthy, not they themselves. The joy is therefore not stoicism but theological gratitude.

Keener Commentary

Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary — Faithful Paraphrase
5:34-39

Keener provides historical context for Gamaliel's examples. Josephus mentions a Theudas who led a revolt around AD 44-46 (Antiquities 20.5.1) — later than the implied date of this speech. Some scholars see a historical problem; Keener suggests either Luke's Theudas is a different, earlier figure, or Josephus's dating is approximate. The textual point is that failed messianic movements were a known phenomenon Gamaliel could invoke.

5:41

Keener notes the cultural significance of public flogging (the standard thirty-nine lashes — 2 Cor 11:24). In the honor-shame culture of the first-century Mediterranean world, flogging was designed to humiliate and silence by destroying public reputation. The apostles' joy inverts this completely — they treat the honor-stripping as honor-conferring. This inversion of shame culture is one of early Christianity's most distinctive social features.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:29

MacArthur on "We must obey God rather than human beings" — this is the foundational principle of what is sometimes called civil disobedience in Christian ethics. MacArthur applies it carefully: it applies only when human authority commands what God forbids, or forbids what God commands. It is not a general license to disobey inconvenient authorities.

5:38-39

MacArthur notes that Gamaliel's argument, while providentially useful, is theologically insufficient. Many false religions have not self-destructed. The test of endurance cannot distinguish true from false movements — only the content of the claim (resurrection of the Messiah, attested by witnesses) can do that. Gamaliel buys time but does not get to truth.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentary on Acts — Faithful Paraphrase
5:29

Calvin uses this verse as a cornerstone of his doctrine of resistance to tyranny. When magistrates command what God forbids, the Christian's duty is clear. However, Calvin is careful to note that resistance takes the form of non-compliance and prayer — not revolution or personal vengeance. The apostles did not attack the Sanhedrin; they kept preaching.

5:41

Calvin on rejoicing in suffering: the apostles' joy is not natural courage or philosophical equanimity but the fruit of the Holy Spirit working in them. They are not indifferent to pain but transformed in their response to it — which is Calvin's argument that Christian endurance is not Stoic resignation but Spirit-given gladness in participation with Christ.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes — Faithful Paraphrase
5:30

"Hanging him on a tree" (kremasantes epi xylou) — the NET notes this is a reference to Deut 21:22-23, where hanging on a tree (in this case crucifixion) marks someone as cursed by God. Peter is directly confronting the theological objection to Jesus as Messiah: yes, he died accursed — but that is how he bore the curse for others (Gal 3:13).

5:42

The verse summarizes the apostles' activity with two infinitives: "teaching" (didaskein) and "proclaiming the good news" (euangelizesthai). Both the systematic instruction of believers and the evangelistic proclamation to outsiders are part of the apostolic mission. The church's witness is not either/or but both.
