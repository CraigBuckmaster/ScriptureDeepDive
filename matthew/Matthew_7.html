Matthew 7

Judge Not; Ask, Seek, Knock; The Two Ways; The Two Builders

Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, a tax collector called by Jesus at his customs post in Capernaum (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles and an eyewitness to the ministry of Jesus. He would have been literate and numerate by profession — uniquely equipped to compose a carefully structured written account. Unanimous early tradition attributes the Gospel to him: Papias (c.AD 125) records that "Matthew compiled the oracles in the Hebrew language, and everyone interpreted them as best he could." The Gospel as we have it is in polished literary Greek, suggesting either a Greek composition drawing on Aramaic sources, or a Greek original.

When written

c.AD 80–90, traditionally associated with Antioch of Syria, the first great Gentile church and a centre of Jewish-Christian interaction. The Gospel presupposes the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) in its apocalyptic passages (Matt 22:7; 24:15-22), suggesting composition after that date. It draws on Mark's Gospel (c.AD 65-70) and on a sayings source shared with Luke (conventionally called Q). Matthew shapes his sources with a more formal, structured literary hand than any of the other evangelists.

What prompted it

To present Jesus of Nazareth as the fulfilment of Israel's entire scriptural heritage — the promised Messiah, the new Moses, the true Israel, and the Son of God. Matthew writes primarily for a Jewish-Christian community navigating its relationship to the synagogue after AD 70. His ten fulfilment-formula citations ("all this took place to fulfil what was spoken through the prophet") are the theological spine of the work: Israel's Scripture is not superseded but completed in Jesus. The Great Commission (28:18-20) shows the universal horizon of a Gospel that begins with Abraham and ends with all nations.

Verses 1–12 — Judging, Asking, and the Golden Rule
1"Do not judge, or you too will be judged.2For in the same way you judge others, you will be judged, and with the measure you use, it will be measured to you.3"Why do you look at the speck of sawdust in your brother's eye and pay no attention to the plank in your own eye?4How can you say to your brother, 'Let me take the speck out of your eye,' when all the time there is a plank in your own eye?5You hypocrite, first take the plank out of your own eye, and then you will see clearly to remove the speck from your brother's eye.6"Do not give dogs what is sacred; do not throw your pearls to pigs. If you do, they may trample them under their feet, and turn and tear you to pieces.7"Ask and it will be given to you; seek and you will find; knock and the door will be opened to you.8For everyone who asks receives; the one who seeks finds; and to the one who knocks, the door will be opened.9"Which of you, if your son asks for bread, will give him a stone?10Or if he asks for a fish, will give him a snake?11If you, then, though you are evil, know how to give good gifts to your children, how much more will your Father in heaven give good gifts to those who ask him!12So in everything, do to others what you would have them do to you, for this sums up the Law and the Prophets."

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The opening of ch.7 transitions from the Sermon's teaching on piety (ch.6) to its teaching on community relationships and the conditions of genuine discipleship. The four units (judging, pearls/pigs, ask/seek/knock, golden rule) are united by the theme of discernment: knowing when to correct and when to hold back, knowing how to pray, knowing how to treat others as you would be treated.

Cross-Reference

  • Lev 19:18Love your neighbour as yourself — the golden rule (v.12) is its positive expression. "Do to others what you would have them do to you" activates love as the governing principle of all Torah.
  • Matt 22:40All the Law and the Prophets hang on the double love command — the same claim made of the golden rule in 7:12. The Sermon and the controversy section both point to the same summary.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1-5

Calvin: The prohibition targets the censorious spirit that appoints itself judge over all others while exempting itself. It does not forbid church discipline or discernment of false teaching. The test: is the judgment accompanied by the same standard applied to oneself?

7:13-14

Calvin on the two ways: The narrow gate is the gospel's entrance -- it requires the renunciation of all human righteousness and total dependence on Christ. The wide gate admits all -- self-righteous religion, moral achievement, human philosophy -- everything except the humble acknowledgment of need.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:1-5

The NET note on 'do not judge' discusses the judicial background: krino was used for formal legal judgment. The prohibition is against setting oneself up as final arbiter of another's standing before God. Practical discernment (v.6) and church discipline (18:15-20) are not prohibited.

7:7-11

The NET note on 'how much more' (poso mallon) identifies the classical a fortiori argument: from the lesser (human fathers) to the greater (the heavenly Father). The argument establishes the disposition of the giver: the Father is more willing to give good gifts than any human father.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
7:1-5

Robertson notes that 'judge not' does not prohibit all discernment -- v.6 immediately requires the judgment of who the dogs and pigs are. The prohibition is against hypocritical judgment: judging others by a standard you exempt yourself from. The log-and-speck image is comic in its disproportion.

7:7-11

Robertson traces ask (aiteo), seek (zeteo), knock (krouo) as progressively intensive. All three are present imperatives -- keep asking, keep seeking, keep knocking. The father-son argument establishes the a fortiori: if evil human fathers give good gifts, how much more the heavenly Father.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
7:1-5

Chrysostom: This is not the prohibition of discernment but the condemnation of self-exempting severity. The person who judges others harshly for sins they commit themselves is the target. Deal with your own sin first, and only then will you see clearly to help your brother.

7:13-14

Augustine: Enter through the narrow gate -- the road to life is characterised by difficulty and few travellers. This is not a statement about the absolute number of the saved but about the character of the way: narrow because it requires the death of self-will and submission to God's governance.

Verses 13–29 — Two Ways, Two Trees, Two Builders
13"Enter through the narrow gate. For wide is the gate and broad is the road that leads to destruction, and many enter through it.14But small is the gate and narrow the road that leads to life, and only a few find it.15"Watch out for false prophets. They come to you in sheep's clothing, but inwardly they are ferocious wolves.16By their fruit you will recognize them. Do people pick grapes from thornbushes, or figs from thistles?17Likewise, every good tree bears good fruit, but a bad tree bears bad fruit.18A good tree cannot bear bad fruit, and a bad tree cannot bear good fruit.19Every tree that does not bear good fruit is cut down and thrown into the fire.20Thus, by their fruit you will recognize them.21"Not everyone who says to me, 'Lord, Lord,' will enter the kingdom of heaven, but only the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven.22Many will say to me on that day, 'Lord, Lord, did we not prophesy in your name and in your name drive out demons and in your name perform many miracles?'23Then I will tell them plainly, 'I never knew you. Away from me, you evildoers!'24"Therefore everyone who hears these words of mine and puts them into practice is like a wise man who built his house on the rock.25The rain came down, the streams rose, and the winds blew and beat against that house; yet it did not fall, because it had its foundation on the rock.26But everyone who hears these words of mine and does not put them into practice is like a foolish man who built his house on sand.27The rain came down, the streams rose, and the winds blew and beat against that house, and it fell with a great crash."28When Jesus had finished saying these things, the crowds were amazed at his teaching,29because he taught as one who had authority, and not as their teachers of the law.

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The Sermon's close is structured around three binary choices: two gates/roads (vv.13–14), two trees (vv.15–20), two foundations (vv.24–27). The middle unit (vv.21–23) is the hinge: the crisis is not choosing the wrong road but performing the right words without the underlying relationship. "Lord, Lord" with miraculous deeds but without knowing Jesus produces the most devastating verdict in the Sermon. The parable of the two builders then places the entire Sermon before the hearer as a decision: hear and do, or hear and not do. There is no third option.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezek 13:10–16False prophets whitewash the wall — it falls. The two-builders image echoes Ezekiel's judgment on those who build on false foundations.
  • Matt 25:11–12Lord, Lord, open the door — the five foolish virgins echo the "Lord, Lord" of 7:22. Both use identical language for those excluded at judgment.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:21-23

Calvin on 'Lord, Lord': Those who say Lord, Lord but do not do the Father's will are rejected because they never knew the Lord -- the relationship of trust and obedience was absent. The miraculous works were real but not evidence of genuine covenant knowledge of Christ.

7:24-27

Calvin on the two builders: The hearing of Christ's words is common to both -- many hear who are not saved. The distinguishing mark is the doing. The person who hears and does not do has not truly heard in any saving sense.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:15-20

The NET note on 'wolves in sheep's clothing' discusses its OT background (Ezek 22:27) and application: false prophets do not announce themselves as false. They wear the prophetic garments. The fruit test is the only reliable evaluation because character cannot be concealed over time.

7:24-27

The NET note on the two builders discusses the contrast between petra (bedrock) and ammos (sand). The houses are identical in every visible respect -- only the foundation differs. The storm's violence tests what is invisible. The note identifies the eschatological dimension: the storm is the final judgment.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
7:15-20

Robertson notes false prophets in sheep's clothing identifies their deceptive method: they wear the wool of genuine prophets while being wolves. The fruit test is the reliable evaluation method -- not claims or charisma but the character their ministry produces over time.

7:24-27

Robertson identifies the two builders as identical in one respect: both hear the words. The difference is entirely in application. The house on rock hears and does; the house on sand hears and does not do. The identical storm tests both -- only the foundation's quality determines the outcome.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
7:15-20

Chrysostom: You will know them by their fruits -- not by their words, reputation, miracles, or emotional appeal. The fruit that identifies the false prophet is the character their teaching produces: pride or humility, self-sufficiency or dependence on God, moral licentiousness or covenant faithfulness?

7:24-27

Augustine on the two builders: The sand is all human wisdom, achievement, and self-sufficiency. The rock is Christ himself -- obedience to him, trust in him, the life built on the foundation of his word. The storm is the final judgment that every life will face.

Ancient Sources

The scribes' teaching method
Scribes taught by citing the tradition: "Rabbi Hillel says... Rabbi Shammai says." Jesus cites no authority except himself — "But I say to you." The contrast (v.29) is not between authoritative and unauthoritative teaching but between derived and intrinsic authority.
The contrast with scribal method is the Sermon's structural conclusion.
Palestinian house-building
In Judean hill country, flash floods (wadis) could sweep through dry riverbeds after winter rains. Building on the rock outcrop above the wadi vs. on the sandy riverbed was a life-or-death decision every builder knew.
The parable is drawn from observable Palestinian building practice.

Reception History

"I never knew you"
The most-discussed verse in the Sermon for questions of assurance and judgment. Reformation theologians (Calvin, Luther) used it to distinguish between formal religion and genuine faith. The test is not extraordinary deeds but the will of the Father being done.
The authority of Jesus
7:28–29 has been the foundational text for Christological claims about Jesus's unique authority. His teaching in the first person ("But I say to you") is without parallel in Jewish tradition.

Textual Notes

7:6Do not give dogs what is sacred; do not throw your pearls to pigs
The instruction requires discernment about who is capable of receiving sacred things.
The verse prevents the 'judge not' command from being read as requiring indiscriminate sharing of sacred things. Discernment does not contradict non-judgment. Both are required.

Reformed/evangelical majority
The rejection 'I never knew you' (ouk egnon -- never at any point) indicates no genuine relationship ever existed.

Some Arminian readings
The 'Lord, Lord' and works performed suggest genuine confession and service. The rejection is due to persistent unfaithfulness.
The Reformed reading is most consistent with 'I never knew you' -- never is emphatic. The works were genuine but not evidence of genuine saving faith.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 7 closes the Sermon on the Mount with the decisive application: the test of genuine discipleship is not hearing but doing. The two-way structures all point to the same conclusion -- the crisis of decision the entire Sermon has been building toward.
