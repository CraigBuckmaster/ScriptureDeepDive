Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector (telōnēs) at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised by his community. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles. The tradition identifying the apostle Matthew as the gospel's author is consistent from the 2nd century (Papias, Irenaeus, Origen) and has no early counter-tradition. Matthew's tax-collector background explains the gospel's distinctive precision with numbers, lists, and systematically organized material.

When written

Most scholars date Matthew between AD 50–90, with significant debate. An early date (before AD 70) is supported by the gospel's apparent familiarity with pre-destruction Jerusalem. Matthew drew on Mark (the earliest gospel), a collection of Jesus' sayings known as Q, and material unique to Matthew (M source). The gospel was likely written in Syrian Antioch or Judea for a primarily Jewish-Christian community.

What prompted it

Matthew writes for Jewish believers — people who knew their Torah, their prophets, and their Psalms — and demonstrates systematically that Jesus is the long-awaited Messiah of Israel. The governing question is: how does Jesus fulfil what Moses, David, and the prophets promised? The answer unfolds through five great discourse blocks (chs.5–7, 10, 13, 18, 23–25), mirroring the five books of Moses, and through Matthew's characteristic formula: "this took place to fulfil what was spoken by the prophet." Jesus is presented as the new Moses, the Son of David, and the Son of God — the convergence of every strand of Israel's hope.

Greek Word Study

ἀπόστολος apostolos — apostle/sent one: V.2: from apostellō (to send). The twelve are now named apostoloi — sent ones. The same word used in the LXX for official messengers. Their authority is derived: Jesus gives them exousia (v.1); they act as his representatives.

ψυχή psychē — soul/life: V.28, 39: psychē is one of the NT's most important words — it can mean "soul" (the immaterial part of the person) or "life" (the whole person). Both senses are active in vv.28–39: the one who kills the body cannot kill the psychē; whoever loses their psychē for Jesus will find it. The word encompasses both the eternal and the temporal dimensions of personhood.

Context

Chapter 10 is Matthew's second great discourse — the Missionary Discourse. The harvest prayer of 9:38 is immediately answered: Jesus calls the twelve and sends them. The commission has two phases: the immediate mission to Israel (vv.5–15) and the longer-range mission that will face persecution (vv.16–42). The instructions extend beyond the immediate mission into the church's entire missionary future — the discourse blends commissioning with prophecy.

Cross-Reference

  • Mic 7:6V.35–36 quotes Micah 7:6 — the prophet's description of family breakdown in a faithless generation. Jesus applies it to the divisions his mission will cause. The sword is not Jesus's goal but the consequence of his unavoidable claims.
  • Isa 50:6–7The Suffering Servant's willingness to be flogged and not turn away (vv.17–18) anticipates Jesus's own path.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1

Jesus called his twelve disciples to him and gave them authority to drive out impure spirits and to heal every disease and sickness. The harvest prayer (9:38) answered in the next verse. The authority is delegated: they do not generate it themselves. They act as extensions of Jesus's own authority — representatives whose word carries the weight of the one who sent them. The number twelve signals the reconstitution of Israel around Jesus.

10:16

I am sending you out like sheep among wolves. Therefore be as shrewd as snakes and as innocent as doves. The combination is unique in ancient literature. Shrewd (phronimoi — wise, prudent) like serpents acknowledges the hostile environment: naive optimism will be exploited. Innocent (akeraioi — unmixed, uncontaminated) like doves insists on moral integrity: pragmatic realism must not produce moral compromise. The kingdom requires both simultaneously, not alternately.

10:22

You will be hated by everyone because of me, but the one who stands firm to the end will be saved. The disciples are not promised exemption from persecution but endurance through it. "Because of me" — the opposition is not personal but Christological. Standing firm (hypomenō — to remain under, to endure) is the posture of the disciple who has received the hostility and not abandoned the mission. The promise of salvation (sōthēsetai) frames the endurance as ultimately secure.

10:28

Do not be afraid of those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul. Rather, be afraid of the one who can destroy both soul and body in hell. The hierarchy of fear is liberating: if the worst a human can do is kill the body, and the body is not the most important thing about you, then human threat is robbed of its ultimate power. The one who grasps this is functionally free — what humans threaten cannot reach the thing that matters most. This became the foundation of courage for Christian martyrs across two millennia.

10:39

Whoever finds their life will lose it, and whoever loses their life for my sake will find it. The most repeated saying of Jesus in the gospels (five occurrences across four gospels). The psychē paradox: the person who makes self-preservation the governing priority will lose the very thing they are trying to preserve. The person who surrenders the management of their life to Jesus discovers a quality of existence that cannot be taken away. The paradox is not obscure mysticism — it is the description of what happens when a person reorients around something larger than themselves.

Historical Sources

The Twelve
The number twelve is not incidental — it mirrors the twelve tribes of Israel. Jesus is reconstituting Israel around himself. The twelve are the founding members of the new covenant community.
The twelve represent the reconstitution of Israel around Jesus.
Sheep Among Wolves
The combination of shrewdness (serpent) and innocence (dove) as a missionary posture is unique in ancient literature. The combination acknowledges that the mission operates in a hostile environment while requiring moral integrity.
The serpent/dove combination is one of Jesus's most distinctive ethical images.

Reception History

Missionary tradition
Chapter 10 became the founding text of Christian missionary theology — the basis for itinerant mission, voluntary poverty, and the expectation of persecution. Francis of Assisi's Rule drew directly on vv.9–10.
10:28 in persecution theology
Used by martyrs across two millennia — from Polycarp to Bonhoeffer — as the foundation for courage in the face of death. If they cannot kill the soul, they cannot ultimately harm you.
