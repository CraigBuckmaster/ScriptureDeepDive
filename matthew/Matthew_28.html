Matthew 28

The Resurrection; The Great Commission; Immanuel to the End

Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector (telōnēs) at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised by his community. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9). One of the twelve apostles. Early church tradition unanimously attributes this gospel to Matthew. His gospel is the most Jewish of the four — saturated with OT fulfilment quotations, structured around five great discourses echoing the five books of Moses.

When written

c.AD 55–80. The internal evidence (no mention of the temple's destruction as a past event) suggests composition before AD 70, though many scholars date it after. Written for a Jewish-Christian community, probably in Antioch of Syria.

What prompted it

Matthew's community needed a structured account of Jesus's life and teaching that demonstrated his identity as the promised Messiah and presented his teaching as the fulfilment — not abolition — of the Torah. The gospel's five discourses, its ten fulfilment quotations, and its genealogy opening all serve this single argument: Jesus is the Christ, the Son of the living God, in whom all of Scripture finds its telos.

Verses 1–20 — The Resurrection; The Great Commission; Immanuel to the End
1After the Sabbath, at dawn on the first day of the week, Mary Magdalene and the other Mary went to look at the tomb.2There was a violent earthquake, for an angel of the Lord came down from heaven and, going to the tomb, rolled back the stone and sat on it.3His appearance was like lightning, and his clothes were white as snow.4The guards were so afraid of him that they shook and became like dead men.5The angel said to the women, "Do not be afraid, for I know that you are looking for Jesus, who was crucified.6He is not here; he has risen, just as he said. Come and see the place where he lay.7Then go quickly and tell his disciples: 'He has risen from the dead and is going ahead of you into Galilee. There you will see him.' Now I have told you."8So the women hurried away from the tomb, afraid yet filled with joy, and ran to tell his disciples.9Suddenly Jesus met them. "Greetings," he said. They came to him, clasped his feet and worshiped him.10Then Jesus said to them, "Do not be afraid. Go and tell my brothers to go to Galilee; there they will see me."11While the women were on their way, some of the guards went into the city and reported to the chief priests everything that had happened.12When the chief priests had met with the elders and devised a plan, they gave the soldiers a large sum of money,13telling them, "You are to say, 'His disciples came during the night and stole him away while we were asleep.'14If this report gets to the governor, we will satisfy him and keep you out of trouble."15So the soldiers took the money and did as they were instructed. And this story has been widely circulated among the Jewish people to this day.16Then the eleven disciples went to Galilee, to the mountain Jesus had told them about.17When they saw him, they worshiped him; but some doubted.18Then Jesus came to them and said, "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me.19Therefore go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit,20and teaching them to obey everything I have commanded you. And surely I am with you always, to the very end of the age."

Greek Word Study

πᾶσα ἐξουσία pasa exousia — all authority: V.18: the resurrection vindication expressed in legal-regal terms. The Son of Man has received the universal dominion of Dan 7:14 — "all authority in heaven and on earth." This is the basis (therefore, v.19) of the mission: the commission is grounded in the resurrection authority. It is not a human enterprise but an extension of the risen Lord's reign.

ἐγὼ μεθ᾽ ὑμῶν εἰμι πάσας τὰς ἡμέρας egō meth' hymōn eimi pasas tas hēmeras — I am with you always: V.20: the closing words of the gospel — and they are the Immanuel promise of 1:23 fulfilled. The gospel opened with "they will call him Immanuel — God with us." It ends with "I am with you always." The bracket is complete: the one who was called God-with-us promises to be God-with-us for ever. The presence promised in the name is now personally and permanently extended to all disciples.

Context

Matthew 28 is the resurrection chapter — the shortest and most theologically dense chapter in the gospel. Three scenes: the empty tomb and angel (vv.1–8), the appearance to the women (vv.9–10), the guards' bribe (vv.11–15), and the Great Commission (vv.16–20). The Commission (vv.18–20) is the resolution of every tension in the gospel: the authority question of 21:23 is answered (all authority), the Gentile inclusion of 8:11 is commanded (all nations), the Immanuel promise of 1:23 is fulfilled (I am with you always).

Cross-Reference

  • Dan 7:13–14All authority in heaven and on earth — the Son of Man receiving universal dominion. The Great Commission is the missional enactment of Daniel's vision.
  • Matt 1:23Immanuel — God with us. The opening promise fulfilled in the closing verse. The whole gospel is bracketed by divine presence.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
28:6

He is not here; he has risen, just as he said. The most important sentence in Matthew — and in human history. "Just as he said" — three times Jesus predicted his resurrection (16:21; 17:23; 20:19). The resurrection is not a surprise reversal but the fulfilment of a promised pattern. The empty tomb is the vindication of everything Jesus claimed.

28:17

When they saw him, they worshiped him; but some doubted. The most honest verse in the resurrection account. Even in the presence of the risen Jesus, some doubted. Matthew does not edit this out. Doubt in the presence of evidence is a different thing from doubt in the absence of it. The Great Commission is given to a group that includes doubters — which means it is not suspended until everyone is certain.

28:18

All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. The resurrection is the divine validation of every claim Jesus made. The authority questioned in 21:23 ("by what authority are you doing these things?") is now answered by the resurrection itself. The one who was crucified as a criminal is announced as the Lord of all creation.

28:19–20

Go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and teaching them to obey everything I have commanded you. The Great Commission has three elements: go (send the community into the world), make disciples (the goal — not decisions but formed followers), baptize and teach (the means — sacramental incorporation and instruction in obedience). The commission is Trinitarian in its formula and universal in its scope.

28:20

And surely I am with you always, to the very end of the age. The gospel's final word is presence, not commandment. The commission has been given; the basis (authority) has been stated; now the sustaining reality is named. "I am with you" — Immanuel, God with us, from the first verse to the last. The whole gospel is a commentary on this promise.

Ancient Sources

The guard's story
The chief priests' explanation (vv.12–15) — that the disciples stole the body while the guards slept — was still circulating in Matthew's day (v.15). The fact that this was the best available counter-explanation actually supports the resurrection: no one denied the tomb was empty.
The empty tomb was not disputed — only explained differently.
The mountain in Galilee
V.16: "the mountain Jesus had told them about" — not identified, but mountains in Matthew are consistently sites of revelation and authority (5:1; 17:1; 4:8). The final commission comes from an unnamed mountain, placing it in the same category as Sinai and the Sermon.
Mountains in Matthew are always sites of divine disclosure.

Reception History

The Great Commission and world mission
28:19–20 is the founding text of the modern missionary movement. William Carey's 1792 sermon on "the obligation of Christians to use means for the conversion of the heathens" opened with this text. Every subsequent missionary society, from Carey's onwards, has grounded its mandate here.
The Trinitarian formula
The baptismal formula "in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit" (v.19) is the most explicit Trinitarian formula in the Gospels. It was used in the Didache (c.AD 100) as the standard baptismal formula. The three persons are coordinate — one name, three persons.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 28 completes the gospel's great argument. The Messiah announced in chapter 1 as Immanuel — God with us — rises from the dead, claims all authority, commissions his disciples to all nations, and promises to be with them always. The gospel ends exactly where it began: God with us.
