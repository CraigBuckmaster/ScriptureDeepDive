Matthew 13

The Sower; Weeds and Growth; Treasure and Pearl; Nazareth

Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline
Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, tax collector and apostle. Eyewitness to the events recorded. Unanimous early tradition attributes this gospel to him.

When written

c.AD 80–90. Written for a Jewish-Christian community, probably in Antioch of Syria, with a five-discourse structure echoing the Pentateuch.

What prompted it

To present Jesus as the fulfilment of Israel's Scripture — simultaneously Davidic Messiah, new Moses, Son of God, and Servant of Isaiah — and to commission his community for worldwide mission.

Verses 1–23 — The Sower: The Parable and Its Explanation
1That same day Jesus went out of the house and sat by the lake.2Such large crowds gathered around him that he got into a boat and sat in it, while all the people stood on the shore.3Then he told them many things in parables, saying: "A farmer went out to sow his seed.4As he was scattering the seed, some fell along the path, and the birds came and ate it up.5Some fell on rocky places, where it did not have much soil. It sprang up quickly, because the soil was shallow.6But when the sun came up, the plants were scorched, and they withered because they had no root.7Other seed fell among thorns, which grew up and choked the plants.8Still other seed fell on good soil, where it produced a crop—a hundred, sixty or thirty times what was sown.9Whoever has ears, let them hear."10The disciples came to him and asked, "Why do you speak to the people in parables?"11He replied, "Because the knowledge of the secrets of the kingdom of heaven has been given to you, but not to them.12Whoever has will be given more, and they will have an abundance. Whoever does not have, even what they have will be taken from them.13This is why I speak to them in parables: "Though seeing, they do not see; though hearing, they do not hear or understand.14In them is fulfilled the prophecy of Isaiah: "'You will be ever hearing but never understanding; you will be ever seeing but never perceiving.15For this people's heart has become calloused; they hardly hear with their ears, and they have closed their eyes. Otherwise they might see with their eyes, hear with their ears, understand with their hearts and turn, and I would heal them.'"16"But blessed are your eyes because they see, and your ears because they hear.17For truly I tell you, many prophets and righteous people longed to see what you see but did not see it, and to hear what you hear but did not hear it."18"Listen then to what the parable of the sower means:19When anyone hears the message about the kingdom and does not understand it, the evil one comes and snatches away what was sown in their heart. This is the seed sown along the path.20The seed falling on rocky ground refers to someone who hears the word and at once receives it with joy.21But since they have no root, they last only a short time. When trouble or persecution comes because of the word, they quickly fall away.22The seed falling among the thorns refers to the one who hears the word, but the worries of this life and the deceitfulness of wealth choke the word, making it unfruitful.23But the seed falling on good soil refers to someone who hears the word and understands it. This is the one who produces a crop, yielding a hundred, sixty or thirty times what was sown."

Greek Word Study

παραβολή parabolē — parable / comparison: V.3: from para (alongside) + ballō (throw). A parable throws something familiar alongside something unfamiliar to illuminate the unknown through the known. The parables of ch.13 are not simple illustrations — they are subversive narratives that simultaneously reveal the kingdom to those with ears and conceal it from those without. The same story produces different responses depending on the orientation of the hearer.

τὰ μυστήρια τῆς βασιλείας ta mystēria tēs basileias — the secrets / mysteries of the kingdom: V.11: mustērion is a term from Jewish apocalyptic — the hidden purposes of God now being disclosed. The knowledge of these secrets has been given to the disciples (passive — given, not achieved). The parables function as the mechanism of this disclosure: for those who receive, they open up; for those who resist, they close down. Revelation and concealment are two sides of the same act.

Context

Matthew 13 is the third of the five great discourses — the parable discourse. It is given from a boat, to crowds on the shore, in the middle of ch.12's escalating opposition. The parable of the sower explains the mixed response Jesus has received throughout chs.11–12: the same word, sown in the same field, produces radically different results depending on the soil. The disciples' question (v.10) triggers the most important explanation of the parable method in the NT: Jesus speaks in parables because of the hardness of the crowd, and teaches their meaning to the disciples in private.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 6:9–10Hear but not understand; see but not perceive — the hardening oracle from Isaiah's call. Jesus applies it to the crowd's response to the parables. The parables are both judgment (hardening the resistant) and mercy (revealing to the receptive).
  • Ps 78:2I will open my mouth in parables — cited in v.35 as the introduction to the second parable series. The Psalter anticipated the parabolic method of the Messiah.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:11

The knowledge of the secrets of the kingdom has been given to you. The passive voice — given, not achieved. The disciples' understanding is itself a gift of grace, not a product of their intelligence. This is the theological basis for the distinction between disciples and crowd: the same parables, different soil, different divine illumination.

13:19–23

The explanation of the sower is a spiritual diagnostic — four soils representing four responses to the word. Path (no understanding → snatched away), rocky (joy without root → persecution withers it), thorns (word choked by wealth and worry), good soil (understanding → fruit). The diagnostic is not judgment but invitation: what kind of soil am I?

13:16–17

Blessed are your eyes because they see. The beatitude addressed specifically to the disciples — they are living in what the prophets longed to see. The privilege of the disciples is not their personal virtue but their historical position: they are present at the fulfilment of everything Israel waited for. Privilege and responsibility are inseparable.

Verses 24–43 — Weeds; Mustard Seed; Yeast: Kingdom Growth and Patience
24Jesus told them another parable: "The kingdom of heaven is like a man who sowed good seed in his field.25But while everyone was sleeping, his enemy came and sowed weeds among the wheat, and went away.26When the wheat sprouted and formed heads, then the weeds also appeared.27The owner's servants came to him and said, 'Sir, didn't you sow good seed in your field? Where then did the weeds come from?'28"'An enemy did this,' he replied. "The servants asked him, 'Do you want us to go and pull them up?'29"'No,' he answered, 'because while you are pulling the weeds, you may uproot the wheat with them.30Let both grow together until the harvest. At that time I will tell the harvesters: First collect the weeds and tie them in bundles to be burned; then gather the wheat and bring it into my barn.'"31He told them another parable: "The kingdom of heaven is like a mustard seed, which a man took and planted in his field.32Though it is the smallest of all seeds, yet when it grows, it is the largest of garden plants and becomes a tree, so that the birds come and perch in its branches."33He told them still another parable: "The kingdom of heaven is like yeast that a woman took and mixed into about sixty pounds of flour until it worked all through the dough."34Jesus spoke all these things to the crowd in parables; he did not say anything to them without using a parable.35So was fulfilled what was spoken through the prophet: "I will open my mouth in parables, I will utter things hidden since the creation of the world."36Then he left the crowd and went into the house. His disciples came to him and said, "Explain to us the parable of the weeds in the field."37He answered, "The one who sowed the good seed is the Son of Man.38The field is the world, and the good seed stands for the people of the kingdom. The weeds are the people of the evil one,39and the enemy who sows them is the devil. The harvest is the end of the age, and the harvesters are angels.40"As the weeds are pulled up and burned in the fire, so it will be at the end of the age.41The Son of Man will send out his angels, and they will weed out of his kingdom everything that causes sin and all who do evil.42They will throw them into the blazing furnace, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.43Then the righteous will shine like the sun in the kingdom of their Father. Whoever has ears, let them hear."

Greek Word Study

ζιζάνια zizania — weeds / darnel: V.25: darnel (Lolium temulentum) — a weed that mimics wheat in its early stages and cannot be reliably distinguished until harvest. The parable's agricultural realism is precise: premature weeding would uproot wheat with the weeds. The eschatological patience the parable teaches is grounded in a real agricultural problem — premature judgment destroys the good along with the bad.

σίναπι sinapi — mustard seed: V.31: the mustard seed was proverbially the smallest seed (m.Niddah 5:2). The parable's point is not that the kingdom will grow large (though it will) but the disproportion between the beginning and the end — from invisible to unmistakable. The kingdom's present hiddenness is not its permanent condition.

Context

The second section of the parable discourse addresses the kingdom's mixed composition (weeds and wheat), its unexpected growth pattern (mustard seed, yeast), and the eschatological patience required by both. The weeds parable is the most explicitly eschatological in ch.13 — the Son of Man's angels will separate at the harvest. The mustard seed and yeast parables counter the discouragement that arises from the kingdom's small beginnings: the disproportion between seed and tree, pinch and loaf, is the characteristic of God's work in history.

Cross-Reference

  • Dan 4:12; Ezek 17:23A great tree where birds nest — the OT image for a kingdom that shelters all nations. The mustard seed's tree echoes these texts: the kingdom that begins small will become the shelter of the nations.
  • Dan 12:3The righteous will shine like the stars — v.43 ("the righteous will shine like the sun") echoes Daniel's resurrection promise. The harvest-judgment produces this eschatological glory.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:28–30

Let both grow together until the harvest. The owner's restraint is eschatological wisdom: premature judgment (rooting out all apparent weeds now) would destroy wheat alongside them. The parable teaches patience — not indifference to evil but the recognition that final separation belongs to God at the end of the age, not to human agents now.

13:32

The smallest of seeds... becomes the largest of garden plants. The parable of the mustard seed addresses the discouragement of small beginnings. The kingdom's invisibility at the start is not evidence of its failure but of its nature: it grows from within, organically, until it is unmistakable. The same is true of every Christian community that begins small.

13:43

The righteous will shine like the sun. Daniel 12:3's resurrection promise applied to the harvest-judgment's outcome. The parable ends not with the burning of the weeds but with the shining of the righteous — the positive outcome is the final word. Judgment is real, but glory is the destination.

Verses 44–58 — Treasure, Pearl, Net; New and Old; Rejected at Nazareth
44"The kingdom of heaven is like treasure hidden in a field. When a man found it, he hid it again, and then in his joy went and sold all he had and bought that field.45"Again, the kingdom of heaven is like a merchant looking for fine pearls.46When he found one of great value, he went away and sold everything he had and bought it.47"Again, the kingdom of heaven is like a net that was let down into the lake and caught all kinds of fish.48When it was full, the fishermen pulled it up on the shore. Then they sat down and collected the good fish in baskets, but threw the bad away.49This is how it will be at the end of the age. The angels will come and separate the wicked from the righteous50and throw them into the blazing furnace, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.51"Have you understood all these things?" Jesus asked. "Yes," they replied.52He said to them, "Therefore every teacher of the law who has become a disciple in the kingdom of heaven is like the owner of a house who brings out of his storeroom new treasures as well as old."53When Jesus had finished these parables, he moved on from there.54Coming to his hometown, he began teaching the people in their synagogue, and they were amazed. "Where did this man get this wisdom and these miraculous powers?" they asked.55"Isn't this the carpenter's son? Isn't his mother's name Mary, and aren't his brothers James, Joseph, Simon and Judas?56Aren't all his sisters with him? Where then did this man get all these things?"57And they took offense at him. But Jesus said to them, "A prophet is not accepted in his hometown."58He did not do many miracles there because of their lack of faith.

Greek Word Study

θησαυρός thēsauros — treasure: V.44,52: the same word used for both the hidden field-treasure and the householder's storeroom. The kingdom as treasure worth everything you have (vv.44–46), and the scribe-become-disciple who brings out new and old treasure (v.52). The parable of the householder is Matthew's self-description: a Jewish teacher who has found the kingdom in Christ and now brings out both OT wisdom (old) and Jesus's teaching (new) from the same storeroom.

σκανδαλίζω skandalizō — take offence / stumble: V.57: they took offence at him (eskandalizonto). The cognate of the beatitude in 11:6 — "blessed is anyone who does not stumble on account of me." Nazareth fails the test. Familiarity becomes the stumbling block: they know his family, they know his occupation, and therefore they cannot receive him. The offence is not intellectual but relational — they think they know who he is.

Context

The final parables (treasure, pearl, net) bring the kingdom discourse to its personal application: what would you sell everything for? The treasure and pearl parables are the most personally challenging in ch.13 — they ask not about the kingdom's growth or composition but about the hearer's response to it. The net parable returns to eschatological separation (echoing the weeds). The householder saying (v.52) is Matthew's own portrait of the scribe-become-disciple. The Nazareth rejection then provides the lived illustration: the crowd's familiarity prevents them from receiving the treasure they have.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 6:21Where your treasure is, there your heart will be also. The hidden-treasure parable enacts this: the man's joy-driven sale of everything is the natural consequence of having found where his treasure is.
  • Matt 11:6Blessed is anyone who does not stumble on account of me. Nazareth fails this test — skandalizō at the familiar Jesus. The beatitude that addressed John's doubt is now denied by Jesus's hometown.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:44–46

Treasure and pearl — two parables, one point: the kingdom is worth everything. The difference between them is the finding: the first man finds the treasure unexpectedly (while working a field); the merchant finds the pearl after a deliberate search. The kingdom comes to both the seeker and the unseeking — but the response is the same: sell everything. Joy drives the sale — not reluctant sacrifice but the natural action of someone who has found what is worth more than everything else combined.

13:52

New treasures as well as old. The householder-scribe parable is widely read as Matthew's self-description. He was a Jewish scribe trained in Torah; he became a disciple of Jesus. From his storeroom he draws both: the OT (old) and Jesus's teaching (new). This is the hermeneutic of the whole gospel: the new does not replace the old but fulfils it, and both are treasures.

13:58

He did not do many miracles there because of their lack of faith. Nazareth's unbelief is not the cause of Jesus's inability but the condition in which his miracles are not done — not because he cannot but because the response of faith is the soil in which healing takes root. The parallel with the sower (v.23 — good soil produces fruit) is deliberate: Nazareth's soil is hard.

Ancient Sources

Palestinian agriculture
Fields were commonly used to hide valuables in times of invasion. A landless labourer ploughing a rented field who found buried treasure had a legal claim if he first purchased the field.
The parable's legal realism would be immediately recognised.
Darnel
Lolium temulentum — a weed that closely mimics wheat until the grain develops. Eating darnel causes intoxication (the name may derive from French "ivre" — drunk). The agricultural precision of the weeds parable is exact.
The realism confirms that Jesus or Matthew had agricultural knowledge.

Reception History

The parable chapter and allegory
Matthew 13 generated enormous allegorical interpretation from Origen onward. The weeds parable's explanation (vv.37–43) encouraged readers to find allegorical correspondences in every detail of every parable — an approach later scholarship has questioned.
The householder saying (v.52)
Widely read as Matthew's self-portrait — the Jewish scribe who has become a disciple. The saying has been used to argue for Matthew's authorship and his scribal background.

Literary Structure

Sower and explanation — vv.1–23Four soils; parable rationale; Isaiah 6; disciples' privilege
Weeds, mustard, yeast — vv.24–43Mixed kingdom; patient growth; Son of Man's harvest
Treasure, pearl, net; Nazareth — vv.44–58Worth everything; new and old; rejection at home
Chapter 13 is the kingdom chapter. Seven parables address the kingdom's reception (sower), composition (weeds), growth pattern (mustard, yeast), value (treasure, pearl), and final judgment (net). The chapter ends where it began — with the mixed response: disciples understand, Nazareth stumbles.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 13 is the most comprehensive portrait of the kingdom in the gospel. Seven parables, seven angles: reception (sower), mixed composition (weeds), hidden growth (mustard, yeast), supreme value (treasure, pearl), final separation (net). The chapter teaches not a programme but a disposition: ears to hear, eyes to see, and the willingness to sell everything for what you have found.
