Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector (telōnēs) at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised by his community. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles. The tradition identifying the apostle Matthew as the gospel's author is consistent from the 2nd century (Papias, Irenaeus, Origen) and has no early counter-tradition. Matthew's tax-collector background explains the gospel's distinctive precision with numbers, lists, and systematically organized material.

When written

Most scholars date Matthew between AD 50–90, with significant debate. An early date (before AD 70) is supported by the gospel's apparent familiarity with pre-destruction Jerusalem. Matthew drew on Mark (the earliest gospel), a collection of Jesus' sayings known as Q, and material unique to Matthew (M source). The gospel was likely written in Syrian Antioch or Judea for a primarily Jewish-Christian community.

What prompted it

Matthew writes for Jewish believers — people who knew their Torah, their prophets, and their Psalms — and demonstrates systematically that Jesus is the long-awaited Messiah of Israel. The governing question is: how does Jesus fulfil what Moses, David, and the prophets promised? The answer unfolds through five great discourse blocks (chs.5–7, 10, 13, 18, 23–25), mirroring the five books of Moses, and through Matthew's characteristic formula: "this took place to fulfil what was spoken by the prophet." Jesus is presented as the new Moses, the Son of David, and the Son of God — the convergence of every strand of Israel's hope.

Greek Word Study

μυστήρια τῆς βασιλείας mystēria tēs basileias — secrets/mysteries of the kingdom: V.11: mystērion (from which "mystery") — not a puzzle but a secret formerly hidden and now disclosed. The kingdom's secrets are not intellectual puzzles but salvific realities that require disclosure from God to be understood. The parables reveal these realities to those with ears to hear while concealing them from hardened hearts.

ὅμοια ἐστὶν ἡ βασιλεία τῶν οὐρανῶν homoia estin hē basileia tōn ouranōn — the kingdom of heaven is like: The parable formula — used seven times in this chapter. Each parable does not define the kingdom but illuminates a dimension of it through comparison. The kingdom is too large for one definition; it requires multiple angles of approach.

Context

Chapter 13 is Matthew's third great discourse — the Parable Discourse. Seven kingdom parables, the explanation of why Jesus speaks in parables (vv.10–17), and the rejection at Nazareth. The parables reveal the kingdom's structure: mixed reception (sower), mixed community (wheat/weeds), small beginnings and great growth (mustard, yeast), and the total worth of the kingdom (treasure, pearl). The rejection at Nazareth (vv.54–58) closes the chapter with the final form of the growing opposition.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 6:9–10Vv.14–15 quote Isaiah's commissioning — the prophet told to preach to a people who will not hear. Jesus applies the same pattern to his own ministry: the parables both reveal to the receptive and harden the resistant.
  • Ps 78:2V.35: "I will open my mouth in parables" — quoted from Psalm 78:2. Matthew presents Jesus as fulfilling even the psalmist's didactic method.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:11–12

The knowledge of the secrets of the kingdom of heaven has been given to you, but not to them. Whoever has will be given more, and they will have an abundance. The parables serve a dual function: they reveal to the receptive and harden the resistant. This is not arbitrary — it is the logic of light: the same sun that illuminates the seeing eye blinds the damaged one. The disciples' receptivity is itself a gift (it has been "given to you"), not a natural achievement.

13:23

But the seed falling on good soil refers to someone who hears the word and understands it. This is the one who produces a crop, yielding a hundred, sixty or thirty times what was sown. Understanding (syniēmi — to bring together, to comprehend) is the key differentiator in the sower parable. The path, rocky ground, and thorns all fail at the level of comprehension and sustained attention. The good soil hears and understands — and the yield is extraordinary. Understanding precedes application; application produces fruit.

13:33

The kingdom of heaven is like yeast that a woman took and mixed into about sixty pounds of flour until it worked all through the dough. The yeast parable was counter-intuitive to Jewish hearers for whom leaven was routinely a symbol of sin and corruption (cf. Matt 16:6). Jesus uses it positively: the kingdom permeates and transforms from within. The whole loaf is changed by the yeast hidden in it. This is the organic, invisible, unstoppable nature of the kingdom's expansion.

13:44–46

The treasure and the pearl. The two shortest parables make one point from two angles: the kingdom is worth everything. In both cases the man sells all — not reluctantly but in joy (v.44). The sacrifice is not the point; it is the overflow of having found something of surpassing value. The kingdom does not demand sacrifice; it produces the natural response of those who have discovered what everything else was pointing toward.

13:52

Every teacher of the law who has become a disciple in the kingdom of heaven is like the owner of a house who brings out of his storeroom new treasures as well as old. Matthew's self-portrait. The Jewish scribe trained in the Law who has become a kingdom disciple now draws from both the OT ("old") and Jesus's teaching ("new"). Every chapter of this gospel enacts this: OT fulfilment read through kingdom lens. The book is itself the demonstration of this verse.

Historical Sources

Parables in Jewish Tradition
Rabbinic parables (meshalim) were a common teaching form in first-century Judaism, used to explain Torah. Jesus's parables share the form but are distinctive in content: they are about himself and the kingdom he is inaugurating, not about correct legal interpretation.
Jesus uses a familiar form to deliver a radically new content.
Mustard Seed
The mustard plant could grow to 2–3 metres. Jesus's choice of the "smallest seed" (hyperbole — mustard is not literally the smallest but was proverbially used as an example of smallness) was immediately recognisable.
The mustard was a common Jewish illustration of smallness.

Reception History

Augustine on the treasure parable
Interpreted the treasure as wisdom/Christ hidden in Scripture — the OT is the field; the gospel is the treasure. His reading shaped medieval allegorical exegesis.
13:33 — Yeast
The yeast parable was counter-intuitive to Jewish hearers for whom leaven was routinely a symbol of sin and corruption (cf. Matt 16:6). Jesus uses it as a positive image — the kingdom permeates and transforms from within.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 13 is Matthew's most sustained exploration of the kingdom's character. Seven parables from seven angles: how it is received, how it grows, how it mixes, what it is worth, how it ends. The treasure and pearl parables are the theological centre: the kingdom's worth makes total sacrifice feel like total gain.
