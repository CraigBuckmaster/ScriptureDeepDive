Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9). One of the twelve apostles. The early church unanimously attributed this gospel to Matthew. His Jewish background gives the gospel its distinctive OT fulfilment texture; his tax-collector precision gives it its careful structure.

When written

Most scholars date Matthew c.AD 80–90, though some argue for a pre-70 date before the destruction of the Temple. The gospel shows awareness of the Jewish-Christian tension following AD 70, but its theology is rooted in the earliest Palestinian tradition.

What prompted it

Matthew writes for a Jewish-Christian community wrestling with its relationship to Judaism after the death and resurrection of Jesus. He presents Jesus as the fulfilment of the entire OT — the new Moses, the son of David, Immanuel. The gospel is structured around five great discourses (echoing the five books of Moses) and is the most cited gospel in the early church.

Verses 1–46 — Wedding Banquet; Caesar; Resurrection; Great Commandment; David's Lord
1Jesus spoke to them again in parables, saying:2"The kingdom of heaven is like a king who prepared a wedding banquet for his son.3He sent his servants to those who had been invited to the banquet to tell them to come, but they refused to come.4"Then he sent some more servants and said, 'Tell those who have been invited that I have prepared my dinner: My oxen and fattened cattle have been butchered, and everything is ready. Come to the wedding banquet.'5"But they paid no attention and went off—one to his field, another to his business.6The rest seized his servants, mistreated them and killed them.7The king was enraged. He sent his army and destroyed those murderers and burned their city.8"Then he said to his servants, 'The wedding banquet is ready, but those I invited did not deserve to come.9So go to the street corners and invite to the banquet anyone you find.'10"So the servants went out into the streets and gathered all the people they could find, the bad as well as the good, and the wedding hall was filled with guests.11"But when the king came in to see the guests, he noticed a man there who was not wearing wedding clothes.12He asked, 'How did you get in here without wedding clothes, friend?' The man was speechless.13"Then the king told the attendants, 'Tie him hand and foot, and throw him outside, into the darkness, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.'14"For many are invited, but few are chosen."15Then the Pharisees went out and laid plans to trap him in his words.16They sent their disciples to him along with the Herodians. "Teacher," they said, "we know you are a man of integrity and that you teach the way of God in accordance with the truth. You aren't swayed by others, because you pay no attention to who they are.17Tell us then, what is your opinion? Is it right to pay the imperial tax to Caesar or not?"18But Jesus, knowing their evil intent, said, "You hypocrites, why are you trying to trap me?19Show me the coin used for paying the tax." They brought him a denarius,20and he asked them, "Whose image is this? And whose inscription?"21"Caesar's," they replied. Then he said to them, "So give back to Caesar what is Caesar's, and to God what is God's."22When they heard this, they were amazed. So they left him and went away.23That same day the Sadducees, who say there is no resurrection, came to him with a question.24"Teacher," they said, "Moses told us that if a man dies without having children, his brother must marry the widow and raise up offspring for him.25Now there were seven brothers among us. The first one married and died, and since he had no children, he left his wife to his brother.26The same thing happened to the second and third brother, right on down to the seventh.27Finally, the woman died.28Now then, at the resurrection, whose wife will she be of the seven, since all of them were married to her?"29Jesus replied, "You are in error because you do not know the Scriptures or the power of God.30At the resurrection people will neither marry nor be given in marriage; they will be like the angels in heaven.31But about the resurrection of the dead—have you not read what God said to you,32"'I am the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob'? He is not the God of the dead but of the living."33When the crowds heard this, they were astonished at his teaching.34Hearing that Jesus had silenced the Sadducees, the Pharisees got together.35One of them, an expert in the law, tested him with this question:36"Teacher, which is the greatest commandment in the Law?"37Jesus replied: "'Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind.'38This is the first and greatest commandment.39And the second is like it: 'Love your neighbor as yourself.'40All the Law and the Prophets hang on these two commandments."41While the Pharisees were gathered together, Jesus asked them,42"What do you think about the Messiah? Whose son is he?" "The son of David," they replied.43"How is it then that David, speaking by the Spirit, calls him 'Lord'? For he says,44"'The Lord said to my Lord: Sit at my right hand until I put your enemies under your feet.'"45If then David calls him 'Lord,' how can he be his son?"46No one could say a word in reply, and from that day on no one dared to ask him any more questions.

Greek Word Study

εἰκών eikōn — image/likeness: V.20: "Whose image is this?" The denarius bore the image (eikōn) of Caesar. The word is used in Genesis 1:26–27 for the image of God in which humans are made. Jesus' response implies: give Caesar what bears his image (coins); give God what bears his image (yourself — your whole person). The answer is more radical than it appears.

ἀγαπήσεις agapēseis — you shall love: V.37–39: the Great Commandment uses agapaō — not the love of feeling but the love of committed action and will. Jesus quotes Deuteronomy 6:5 (heart, soul, mind) and Leviticus 19:18 (neighbour as yourself). The combination is Jesus' own synthesis: both texts were known but their linkage as a dyad summary of the whole Law is his distinctive contribution.

Context

Chapter 22 is the debate chapter — five consecutive confrontations in which every faction of the Jewish leadership attempts to trap Jesus and fails. The wedding banquet parable (vv.1–14) is the theological frame: the invited guests refuse; the streets are filled; but the requirement of the wedding garment remains. Then: Caesar question (Pharisees + Herodians); resurrection question (Sadducees); greatest commandment (expert in law); and Jesus' own question about David's Lord. By v.46 no one dares ask another question. Jesus has silenced every faction.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 6:5; Lev 19:18The Great Commandment — Jesus combines Deuteronomy's Shema love command with Leviticus' neighbour love. His synthesis of the two as the hinge of the entire Law is his most important legal ruling.
  • Ps 110:1The Lord said to my Lord. Jesus quotes the most important messianic psalm: David calls his descendant "Lord." The implication — the Messiah is more than David's son — silences the Pharisees completely.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:14

Many are invited but few are chosen. The parable' climactic statement. The wedding banquet invitation is universal — street corners, good and bad alike (v.10). But the wedding garment represents the righteousness required to remain: not entry by works but the transformation that genuine acceptance produces. The man without the garment is speechless — he has no defence.

22:21

Give back to Caesar what is Caesar's, and to God what is God's. The most brilliant answer in the gospels. Jesus neither endorses the tax (which would alienate the crowd) nor refuses it (which would give grounds for sedition charges). He accepts the premise — Caesar's image on the coin means it belongs to Caesar — and then transforms the question: what bears God's image? Everything. The human person is God's coin.

22:29

You are in error because you do not know the Scriptures or the power of God. The twofold diagnosis of the Sadducees' error. They have a deficient Scripture (they accepted only the Torah, not the prophets, limiting their understanding of resurrection) and a deficient theology of divine power (they could not imagine existence beyond the categories of present embodied life).

22:37–40

Love God with all your heart and all your soul and all your mind... love your neighbour as yourself. Jesus' synthesis is the greatest legal ruling in the gospel. "All the Law and the Prophets hang on these two commandments" — not that other commandments are abolished but that these two are the load-bearing wall from which everything else is suspended.

22:45–46

If David calls him Lord, how can he be his son? Jesus' counter-question exposes the inadequacy of the title "son of David" alone. The Messiah is David's descendant and David's Lord simultaneously — a paradox that the Pharisees cannot resolve within their categories and that the gospel resolves through the resurrection: Jesus is the Son of God who is also the Son of David.

Ancient Sources

The imperial tax
The denarius bore the image of Tiberius Caesar with the inscription "son of the divine Augustus." Jewish zealots refused to pay it as idolatrous; tax collectors like Matthew collected it. The question was genuinely divisive.
The tax question was a genuine political trap with no safe answer.
Sadducees and resurrection
The Sadducees accepted only the Pentateuch and denied resurrection, angels, and spirits (Acts 23:8). Jesus defeats them on their own ground: Exodus 3:6 (part of the Torah) implies that Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob are currently alive to God.
Jesus defeats the Sadducees using their own accepted scripture.

Reception History

The Great Commandment in Christian ethics
Matthew 22:37–40 became the foundation of Christian ethical reasoning from Augustine onward. "Love and do what you will" (Augustine) distils the principle. Every subsequent Christian social ethics engages with this text.
Psalm 110 in early Christianity
Quoted more frequently in the NT than any other OT text. It was the primary scriptural basis for the early church's confession of Jesus as Lord seated at the Father's right hand.
