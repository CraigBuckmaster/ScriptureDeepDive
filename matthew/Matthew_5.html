Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector (telōnēs) at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised by his community. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles. The tradition identifying the apostle Matthew as the gospel's author is consistent from the 2nd century (Papias, Irenaeus, Origen) and has no early counter-tradition. Matthew's tax-collector background explains the gospel's distinctive precision with numbers, lists, and systematically organized material.

When written

Most scholars date Matthew between AD 50–90, with significant debate. An early date (before AD 70) is supported by the gospel's apparent familiarity with pre-destruction Jerusalem. Matthew drew on Mark (the earliest gospel), a collection of Jesus' sayings known as Q, and material unique to Matthew (M source). The gospel was likely written in Syrian Antioch or Judea for a primarily Jewish-Christian community.

What prompted it

Matthew writes for Jewish believers — people who knew their Torah, their prophets, and their Psalms — and demonstrates systematically that Jesus is the long-awaited Messiah of Israel. The governing question is: how does Jesus fulfil what Moses, David, and the prophets promised? The answer unfolds through five great discourse blocks (chs.5–7, 10, 13, 18, 23–25), mirroring the five books of Moses, and through Matthew's characteristic formula: "this took place to fulfil what was spoken by the prophet." Jesus is presented as the new Moses, the Son of David, and the Son of God — the convergence of every strand of Israel's hope.

Greek Word Study

μακάριος makarios — blessed/deeply flourishing: Not the religious word for "blessed" (eulogētos) but for deep-seated well-being — the state of genuine flourishing in God's eyes. The Beatitudes are declarations of surprising kingdom reality: the person who looks least blessed by the world's standards is most genuinely flourishing under God's reign.

πτωχοὶ τῷ πνεύματι ptōchoi tō pneumati — poor in spirit: Ptōchos = the absolutely destitute, begging-poor. "In spirit" — not material poverty but spiritual bankruptcy: the recognition that before God you have nothing to bring. The kingdom belongs to those who know they are bankrupt and therefore completely dependent on God.

ἐγὼ δὲ λέγω ὑμῖν egō de legō hymin — but I say to you: The formula appearing six times in vv.21–48. Moses said "thus says the LORD"; Jesus says "but I say to you." Jesus does not appeal to a higher authority — he is the authority. Either divine or arrogant. Matthew intends the former.

τέλειος teleios — perfect/complete/whole: V.48: complete in love, having reached its telos (purpose). Not sinless perfection but the full expression of God-like impartial love (v.45). The goal is imitatio Dei — love as God loves.

Context

The Sermon on the Mount (chs.5–7) is Matthew's first of five great discourses — a deliberate echo of the five books of Moses. Jesus is the new Moses, giving new Torah on a mountain. But Moses received and mediated law; Jesus speaks with his own authority ("but I say to you"). The Beatitudes function as the preamble — describing not what kingdom citizens must do but what they are. The six antitheses (vv.21–48) show what "fulfilling the law" means: not external compliance but complete internalisation.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 25:21–22Love your enemies (v.44) → Proverbs: feed your enemy; heap burning coals. Jesus radicalises the OT principle into a positive command grounded in God's character.
  • Lev 19:2Background to v.48: "be holy as I the LORD your God am holy." Jesus replaces "holy" with "perfect" (teleios) — the same divine imitatio logic: imitate God's complete love.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:3

Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. The Beatitudes open with the most counter-cultural declaration possible. The poor in spirit (ptōchoi tō pneumati) are the spiritually bankrupt — those who know they have nothing to bring to God. The kingdom belongs to them, not to those who have accumulated spiritual credit. This is not a reward for a virtue but a description of the condition that receives the kingdom's gift.

5:9

Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will be called children of God. Peacemakers (eirēnopoioi) are not those who avoid conflict but those who actively create peace — who step into conflict and work to resolve it. The designation "children of God" is given not to those who experience peace but to those who make it. The peacemaker images the God who reconciles; they are therefore recognised as bearing the family likeness.

5:17

I have not come to abolish the Law or the Prophets but to fulfil them. The hinge of the Sermon. Jesus is not setting aside the OT — he is bringing it to its deepest intended expression. The antitheses that follow ("you have heard... but I say") show what fulfilment looks like: not abolition of the command but its intensification and internalisation. The Pharisees' righteousness was external compliance; Jesus requires transformed character.

5:39–41

Do not resist an evil person. If anyone slaps you on the right cheek, turn to them the other. If anyone wants to sue you and take your shirt, hand over your coat as well. If anyone forces you to go one mile, go with them two miles. Three specific applications of non-resistance — each subverting the logic of retaliation. The turned cheek refuses the power dynamic of violence; the extra garment exposes the injustice of legal extortion; the second mile transforms compelled servitude into voluntary service. Each act reclaims agency from the situation.

5:44

Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you. The ethical climax of the antitheses — and possibly the most demanding command in human history. The reason given is theological: God sends sun and rain on evil and good alike. The motive for enemy love is not strategy or sentiment but imitatio Dei — become like your Father, who loves impartially. The command is grounded not in the enemy's merit but in the character of God.

5:48

Be perfect, therefore, as your heavenly Father is perfect. The Sermon's summary command. Teleios — complete, whole, having reached its telos. This is the goal of the antitheses: not more rules but the character of God imaged in his people. The standard is deliberately impossible for human achievement — which is exactly the point. Only those who know they cannot achieve this by effort alone will throw themselves on the grace of the one who can transform them.

Historical Sources

Five Discourses
Matthew's five discourse structure (chs.5–7, 10, 13, 18, 23–25) parallels the five books of Moses. The Sermon is the first, on a mountain, with Jesus as the new Mosaic lawgiver.
Matthew's most sustained literary architecture.
Beatitudes and OT Background
Beatitudes echo Ps 37 (meek inherit the earth, v.5 = Ps 37:11), Isa 61:1–3 (good news to poor, comfort to mourners), Ps 24:3–4 (pure in heart see God). The Sermon is saturated with OT allusion.
The Beatitudes are OT theology radicalised around Jesus.

Reception History

Augustine, De Sermone Domini
The first extended Sermon commentary. Augustine structured his political theology around v.9 (peacemakers = children of God).
Tolstoy on 5:39
Vv.39–44 as the foundation for Christian non-resistance. Influenced Gandhi, Martin Luther King, and the entire non-violent resistance tradition.
