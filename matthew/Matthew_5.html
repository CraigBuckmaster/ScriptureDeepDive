Matthew 5

The Beatitudes; Salt and Light; The Law Fulfilled; The Antitheses

Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, a tax collector called by Jesus at his customs post in Capernaum (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles and an eyewitness to the ministry of Jesus. He would have been literate and numerate by profession — uniquely equipped to compose a carefully structured written account. Unanimous early tradition attributes the Gospel to him: Papias (c.AD 125) records that "Matthew compiled the oracles in the Hebrew language, and everyone interpreted them as best he could." The Gospel as we have it is in polished literary Greek, suggesting either a Greek composition drawing on Aramaic sources, or a Greek original.

When written

c.AD 80–90, traditionally associated with Antioch of Syria, the first great Gentile church and a centre of Jewish-Christian interaction. The Gospel presupposes the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) in its apocalyptic passages (Matt 22:7; 24:15-22), suggesting composition after that date. It draws on Mark's Gospel (c.AD 65-70) and on a sayings source shared with Luke (conventionally called Q). Matthew shapes his sources with a more formal, structured literary hand than any of the other evangelists.

What prompted it

To present Jesus of Nazareth as the fulfilment of Israel's entire scriptural heritage — the promised Messiah, the new Moses, the true Israel, and the Son of God. Matthew writes primarily for a Jewish-Christian community navigating its relationship to the synagogue after AD 70. His ten fulfilment-formula citations ("all this took place to fulfil what was spoken through the prophet") are the theological spine of the work: Israel's Scripture is not superseded but completed in Jesus. The Great Commission (28:18-20) shows the universal horizon of a Gospel that begins with Abraham and ends with all nations.

Verses 1–12 — The Beatitudes: Kingdom Character
1Now when Jesus saw the crowds, he went up on a mountainside and sat down. His disciples came to him,2and he began to teach them.3"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.4Blessed are those who mourn, for they will be comforted.5Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth.6Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they will be filled.7Blessed are the merciful, for they will be shown mercy.8Blessed are the pure in heart, for they will see God.9Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will be called children of God.10Blessed are those who are persecuted because of righteousness, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.11"Blessed are you when people insult you, persecute you and falsely say all kinds of evil against you because of me.12Rejoice and be glad, because great is your reward in heaven, for in the same way they persecuted the prophets who were before you."

Context

The Sermon on the Mount (chs.5–7) is the first of Matthew's five discourses — the new Torah delivered by the new Moses from a mountain (echoing Sinai). The Beatitudes function as the preamble to the sermon: they describe the character of the community Jesus is forming, not a programme for earning kingdom status. The eight Beatitudes are arranged chiastically, with the first and eighth sharing the same promise (kingdom of heaven), and the fourth (hunger and thirst for righteousness) at the centre.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 61:1–3The Spirit of the Lord is on me... to proclaim good news to the poor, to bind up the broken-hearted. The Beatitudes echo Isaiah's Servant mission — the poor, the mourning, the meek are the recipients of God's kingdom.
  • Ps 37:11The meek will inherit the earth — the exact language of Beatitude 3. Jesus is citing the Psalter as the portrait of kingdom character.
  • Matt 23:13–29The Seven Woes are the exact inversion of the Eight Beatitudes — blessed/woe, kingdom/condemnation.

5:1-12

MacArthur: The Beatitudes describe kingdom citizens as they are, not as they are commanded to be. Blessed are the poor in spirit does not command poverty of spirit but declares that those who are poor in spirit belong to the kingdom. The Beatitudes are descriptions before they are aspirations.

MacArthur: The first four Beatitudes — poor in spirit, mourning, meek, hungering for righteousness — all describe a posture of need. The kingdom belongs to those who know they need it. Spiritual poverty, grief over sin, meekness before God, and desperate hunger for righteousness are the conditions of kingdom receptivity.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:3-12

Calvin: The Beatitudes describe those who are broken before God and in the world. The world counts the poor in spirit unfortunate; Christ calls them blessed. These Beatitudes reverse every worldly valuation of blessedness — they describe the character that is formed by the kingdom's arrival, not the effort that produces admission to it.

5:21-22

Calvin on the first Antithesis: Christ goes beyond the letter to the spirit. The law says do not murder; Christ says do not hate. He does not contradict the law but extends it to its full demand — what the law forbade externally, Christ forbids internally. He is not adding to the law; he is uncovering what it always meant.

Robertson notes that makarios (blessed) in classical Greek described the happy state of the gods — untouched by mortal cares. The Beatitudes apply this word to the poor in spirit, the mourners, the meek — the opposite of the world's candidates for divine happiness. 5:17-20

Robertson observes that 'fulfil' (plērōsai) means to bring to complete expression — Jesus does not cancel the law but brings out its full meaning and demand. The Pharisees' righteousness is inadequate not because it is too rigorous but because it is not rigorous enough at the level of the heart.

5:3-6

Augustine on the Beatitudes: The poor in spirit are those not puffed up with vain pride. The mourners grieve for sins — their own or others. Verses 13–20 — Salt, Light, and the Fulfilment of the Law
13"You are the salt of the earth. But if the salt loses its saltiness, how can it be made salty again? It is no longer good for anything, except to be thrown out and trampled underfoot.14"You are the light of the world. A town built on a hill cannot be hidden.15Neither do people light a lamp and put it under a bowl. Instead they put it on its stand, and it gives light to everyone in the house.16In the same way, let your light shine before others, that they may see your good deeds and glorify your Father in heaven.17"Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them.18For truly I tell you, until heaven and earth disappear, not the smallest letter, not the least stroke of a pen, will by any means disappear from the Law until everything is accomplished.19Therefore anyone who sets aside one of the least of these commands and teaches others accordingly will be called least in the kingdom of heaven, but whoever practices and teaches these commands will be called great in the kingdom of heaven.20For I tell you that unless your righteousness surpasses that of the Pharisees and teachers of the law, you will certainly not enter the kingdom of heaven."

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

Vv.13–16 apply the Beatitudes to the community's social function: salt preserving, light illuminating. Both metaphors describe the kingdom community as functioning for the sake of the world, not merely for itself. Vv.17–20 establish the relationship between Jesus and Torah: fulfilment, not abolition. The startling claim of v.20 — surpassing Pharisaic righteousness — sets up the six antitheses that follow by asking: what does a deeper righteousness look like in practice?

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 42:6; 49:6A light for the Gentiles — the Servant's calling applied to Jesus's community. The "light of the world" metaphor connects the disciples' calling to the Servant's mission.
  • Deut 4:2Do not add to or subtract from what I command you. Jesus's statement about the Law's permanence echoes Deuteronomy's same insistence on Torah's integrity.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:38-42

Calvin on the non-resistance passages: Christ is not establishing civil law or governing society. He is governing the disposition of the Christian's heart. The magistrate must use the sword; the soldier must fight. But in personal relationships the Christian's spirit is to absorb injury rather than retaliate. Institutional and personal are distinct spheres.

5:43-48

Calvin on 'be perfect as your heavenly Father is perfect': This perfection is not sinless perfection — none achieve it in this life. It is the perfection of the will's direction: the heart wholly oriented toward God and neighbour rather than self. The standard governs the present as the goal the sanctified life moves toward.

Chrysostom on turning the other cheek: This is not cowardice — it is the strength of the person who does not need to defend their honour because their honour rests with God, not human opinion. The non-retaliating disciple is free from the tyranny of requiring vindication from other people.

Verses 21–48 — The Six Antitheses: You Have Heard... But I Say
21"You have heard that it was said to the people long ago, 'Do not murder, and anyone who murders will be subject to judgment.'22But I tell you that anyone who is angry with a brother or sister will be subject to judgment. Again, anyone who says to a brother or sister, 'Raca,' is answerable to the court. And anyone who says, 'You fool!' will be in danger of the fire of hell.23"Therefore, if you are offering your gift at the altar and there remember that your brother or sister has something against you,24leave your gift there in front of the altar. First go and be reconciled to that person; then come and offer your gift.25"Settle matters quickly with your adversary who is taking you to court. Do it while you are still together on the way, because your adversary may hand you over to the judge, and the judge may hand you over to the officer, and you may be thrown into prison.26Truly I tell you, you will not get out until you have paid the last penny.27"You have heard that it was said, 'Do not commit adultery.'28But I tell you that anyone who looks at a woman lustfully has already committed adultery with her in his heart.29If your right eye causes you to stumble, gouge it out and throw it away. It is better for you to lose one part of your body than for your whole body to be thrown into hell.30And if your right hand causes you to stumble, cut it off and throw it away. It is better for you to lose one part of your body than for your whole body to go into hell.31"It has been said, 'Anyone who divorces his wife must give her a certificate of divorce.'32But I tell you that anyone who divorces his wife, except for sexual immorality, makes her commit adultery, and anyone who marries the divorced woman commits adultery.33"Again, you have heard that it was said to the people long ago, 'Do not break your oath, but fulfill to the Lord the vows you have made.'34But I tell you, do not swear an oath at all: either by heaven, for it is God's throne;35or by the earth, for it is his footstool; or by Jerusalem, for it is the city of the Great King.36And do not swear by your head, for you cannot make even one hair white or black.37All you need to say is simply 'Yes' or 'No'; anything beyond this comes from the evil one.38"You have heard that it was said, 'An eye for an eye and a tooth for a tooth.'39But I tell you, do not resist an evil person. If anyone slaps you on the right cheek, turn to them the other cheek also.40And if anyone wants to sue you and take your shirt, hand over your coat as well.41If anyone forces you to go one mile, go with them two miles.42Give to the one who asks you, and do not turn away from the one who wants to borrow from you.43"You have heard that it was said, 'Love your neighbor and hate your enemy.'44But I tell you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you,45that you may be children of your Father in heaven. He causes his sun to rise on the evil and the good, and sends rain on the righteous and the unrighteous.46If you love those who love you, what reward will you get? MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
5:21-48

MacArthur: The six antitheses (you have heard... but I say) do not contradict the OT but penetrate beneath its surface to the heart-level demand. Murder begins with anger; adultery begins with lust; oath-breaking begins with the habit of deception. Jesus's ethic is not stricter legislation but the exposure of the interior reality legislation could only address externally.

5:48

MacArthur: Be perfect, therefore, as your heavenly Father is perfect. The command is not a counsel of perfectionism but the declaration of the standard that defines the direction. The heavenly Father is the measure; the disciple moves toward that measure. Perfect (teleios) means complete, mature, whole — not flawless performance.

Robertson notes that Jesus does not quote Moses and then agree with him; he quotes the tradition's reading of Moses and then deepens or corrects it. Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The six antitheses (vv.21–48) are the demonstration of what "surpassing righteousness" (v.20) and "fulfilment" (v.17) look like in practice. Each follows the pattern: "You have heard... but I say." The pairs are: murder/anger, adultery/lust, divorce, oaths, retaliation/non-resistance, neighbour love/enemy love. The progression moves from the visible act to the invisible intention, and from the management of harm to the active creation of good.

Cross-Reference

  • Lev 19:17–18Do not hate your brother in your heart... love your neighbour. Jesus's antithesis on anger extends Leviticus's own inward dimension of the command.
  • Lev 19:2Be holy because I, the LORD your God, am holy. The command to be perfect as God is perfect (v.48) echoes the Holiness Code's foundational imperative.

Ancient Sources

The Sermon on the Mount
Chs.5–7 are Matthew's first great discourse — the new Torah given on a mountain by the new Moses. The five discourses of Matthew (chs.5–7, 10, 13, 18, 23–25) mirror the five books of Moses.
The structural parallel to the Pentateuch is deliberate.
Antithesis structure
You have heard... but I say. This formula was used by rabbis to contrast opinions. Jesus uses his own authority ("I say") rather than citing tradition ("Rabbi X says"), which explains the crowds' astonishment in 7:29.
The authority claim is the most radical feature of the antitheses.

Reception History

The Sermon in church history
The most commented-upon passage in the NT. Augustine's "Our Lord's Sermon on the Mount" (c.AD 395) established the pattern of reading it as a moral charter. Luther read it as a mirror to show sin; Bonhoeffer (Cost of Discipleship) read it as a literal call.
The Beatitudes and the Psalms
The Beatitudes are saturated with Psalm language — Ps 37 (meek inherit the earth), Ps 24:3–6 (pure in heart see God), Ps 51 (mourning). The Sermon is a new Psalter for the new covenant community.

Scholarly Debates

The Antitheses: Contradiction or Deepening of Torah?
Deepening and completing — not contradicting
Most evangelical commentators
Jesus brings out the fullest meaning of the OT. The six antitheses uncover internal demands that external commands always implied.
Some antitheses represent genuine modification
Some NT scholars
The divorce and oath antitheses represent genuine modifications of Mosaic permission (Deut 24:1-4; Num 30) rather than mere deepening.
The majority reading is most consistent with 5:17-19. Where Jesus appears to modify Mosaic law (divorce, oaths), he is restoring the creation intention that the Mosaic accommodation temporarily relaxed — going behind Moses to the Creator's original design.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 5 opens the Sermon on the Mount with the Beatitudes' comprehensive character description and the Antitheses' comprehensive demand. The governing principle: fulfilment means the law's full internal demand, not its external minimum. The standard is the character of God himself.
