Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector (telōnēs) at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised by his community. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles. The tradition identifying the apostle Matthew as the gospel's author is consistent from the 2nd century (Papias, Irenaeus, Origen) and has no early counter-tradition. Matthew's tax-collector background explains the gospel's distinctive precision with numbers, lists, and systematically organized material.

When written

Most scholars date Matthew between AD 50–90, with significant debate. An early date (before AD 70) is supported by the gospel's apparent familiarity with pre-destruction Jerusalem. Matthew drew on Mark (the earliest gospel), a collection of Jesus' sayings known as Q, and material unique to Matthew (M source). The gospel was likely written in Syrian Antioch or Judea for a primarily Jewish-Christian community.

What prompted it

Matthew writes for Jewish believers — people who knew their Torah, their prophets, and their Psalms — and demonstrates systematically that Jesus is the long-awaited Messiah of Israel. The governing question is: how does Jesus fulfil what Moses, David, and the prophets promised? The answer unfolds through five great discourse blocks (chs.5–7, 10, 13, 18, 23–25), mirroring the five books of Moses, and through Matthew's characteristic formula: "this took place to fulfil what was spoken by the prophet." Jesus is presented as the new Moses, the Son of David, and the Son of God — the convergence of every strand of Israel's hope.

Greek Word Study

ὑποκριτής hypokritēs — hypocrite: A Greek theatrical term for an actor — one who plays a role for an audience. Jesus applies it to religious performers who perform piety for human approval. The hypocrite's fundamental problem is a misidentified audience: they perform for people, not God.

Πάτερ ἡμῶν Pater hēmōn — Our Father: The Lord's Prayer's address. Calling God "Father" was intimate and unusual in first-century Judaism. "Our" (plural) establishes that the prayer is inherently communal.

ἐπιούσιον epiousios — daily bread: One of the rarest words in the Greek NT — found essentially only here and Luke 11:3. From epi + ousia (for being/sustenance) or epienai (the coming day). Agur's prayer (Prov 30:8) asked for "daily bread" — the same posture of trust in sufficient provision.

Context

Chapter 6 continues the Sermon with three parallel instructions on religious practice (giving, prayer, fasting), each with the same structure: do it for God, not for human approval. The Lord's Prayer (vv.9–13) is the centrepiece — a model moving from worship to alignment to petition. The chapter then addresses anxiety (vv.19–34), culminating in the Sermon's governing summary: "seek first his kingdom and his righteousness" (v.33).

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 30:8Agur's prayer for "daily bread" — "give me neither poverty nor riches, but give me only my daily bread." Jesus teaches precisely this posture.
  • Prov 23:4–5Riches sprout wings and fly away — the same elusiveness that makes seeking first the kingdom the only stable foundation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1–4

Be careful not to practise your righteousness in front of others to be seen by them. The governing principle for all three religious practices: the audience determines the reward. The hypocrite performs for people and receives human recognition — that is the complete reward, nothing more. The disciple performs for God in secret and receives divine reward. The same act produces entirely different outcomes depending entirely on who it is directed toward.

6:9–10

Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name, your kingdom come, your will be done on earth as it is in heaven. The Lord's Prayer's first half is entirely theocentric: God's name, God's kingdom, God's will. Only in v.11 does the first human need appear. The order is the Sermon's governing priority (6:33) condensed into prayer form: God's agenda first, then human petition. "Your will be done on earth as it is in heaven" — the prayer asks for the eschatological state to be actuated in present reality.

6:12

Forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors. The one petition that carries a condition. The connection is not mechanical (forgive others and God will forgive you) but relational (the person who has genuinely received divine forgiveness will be characterised by the practice of forgiving others). Vv.14–15 reinforce the point: the refusal to forgive is evidence that one has not truly received forgiveness.

6:24

No one can serve two masters... You cannot serve both God and money. The statement is ontological: not "you should not" but "you cannot." Two masters with competing claims cannot be served simultaneously — at the point of conflict, one will be chosen and the other rejected. The only question is which one. The person who has not consciously chosen God as master has in effect chosen money.

6:33

But seek first his kingdom and his righteousness, and all these things will be given to you as well. The Sermon's governing summary. Not "seek only" but "seek first" — establishing the correct hierarchy that then governs everything else. The promise addresses the anxiety argument: the one who makes God's kingdom and righteousness the primary pursuit will find that material provision is added. Not as wealth but as adequacy — "all these things" refers to food and clothing, not prosperity.

Historical Sources

Lord's Prayer and Jewish Prayer
Strong connections to the Kaddish ("hallowed be your great name... may your kingdom be established") and the Amidah. Jesus draws on familiar prayer forms and reshapes them around his Father-language and kingdom focus.
The Lord's Prayer fulfils rather than breaks Jewish prayer tradition.
Economic Context
First-century Galilean agriculture was precarious — subsistence farming, high Roman taxation, frequent crop failures. The anxiety instructions addressed people with genuine material vulnerability.
Jesus's anti-anxiety teaching had immediate economic bite.

Reception History

The Lord's Prayer in worship
Quoted in the Didache (c.AD 100) as a three-times-daily prayer. Present in all liturgical traditions. The most prayed prayer in human history.
6:24 and the Reformation
Luther and Calvin preached extensively on "you cannot serve God and mammon" as the theological foundation for critiquing financial corruption in the church.
