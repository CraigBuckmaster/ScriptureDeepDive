Matthew 1

The Genealogy and Birth of Jesus

Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, a tax collector called by Jesus at his customs post in Capernaum (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles and an eyewitness to the ministry of Jesus. He would have been literate and numerate by profession — uniquely equipped to compose a carefully structured written account. Unanimous early tradition attributes the Gospel to him: Papias (c.AD 125) records that "Matthew compiled the oracles in the Hebrew language, and everyone interpreted them as best he could." The Gospel as we have it is in polished literary Greek, suggesting either a Greek composition drawing on Aramaic sources, or a Greek original.

When written

c.AD 80–90, traditionally associated with Antioch of Syria, the first great Gentile church and a centre of Jewish-Christian interaction. The Gospel presupposes the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) in its apocalyptic passages (Matt 22:7; 24:15-22), suggesting composition after that date. It draws on Mark's Gospel (c.AD 65-70) and on a sayings source shared with Luke (conventionally called Q). Matthew shapes his sources with a more formal, structured literary hand than any of the other evangelists.

What prompted it

To present Jesus of Nazareth as the fulfilment of Israel's entire scriptural heritage — the promised Messiah, the new Moses, the true Israel, and the Son of God. Matthew writes primarily for a Jewish-Christian community navigating its relationship to the synagogue after AD 70. His ten fulfilment-formula citations ("all this took place to fulfil what was spoken through the prophet") are the theological spine of the work: Israel's Scripture is not superseded but completed in Jesus. The Great Commission (28:18-20) shows the universal horizon of a Gospel that begins with Abraham and ends with all nations.

Verses 1–17 — The Genealogy: A New Genesis
1This is the genealogy of Jesus the Messiah the son of David, the son of Abraham:2Abraham was the father of Isaac, Isaac the father of Jacob, Jacob the father of Judah and his brothers,3Judah the father of Perez and Zerah, whose mother was Tamar, Perez the father of Hezron, Hezron the father of Ram,4Ram the father of Amminadab, Amminadab the father of Nahshon, Nahshon the father of Salmon,5Salmon the father of Boaz, whose mother was Rahab, Boaz the father of Obed, whose mother was Ruth, Obed the father of Jesse,6and Jesse the father of King David. David was the father of Solomon, whose mother had been Uriah's wife,7Solomon the father of Rehoboam, Rehoboam the father of Abijah, Abijah the father of Asa,8Asa the father of Jehoshaphat, Jehoshaphat the father of Jehoram, Jehoram the father of Uzziah,9Uzziah the father of Jotham, Jotham the father of Ahaz, Ahaz the father of Hezekiah,10Hezekiah the father of Manasseh, Manasseh the father of Amon, Amon the father of Josiah,11and Josiah the father of Jeconiah and his brothers at the time of the exile to Babylon.12After the exile to Babylon: Jeconiah was the father of Shealtiel, Shealtiel the father of Zerubbabel,13Zerubbabel the father of Abihud, Abihud the father of Eliakim, Eliakim the father of Azor,14Azor the father of Zadok, Zadok the father of Akim, Akim the father of Elihud,15Elihud the father of Eleazar, Eleazar the father of Matthan, Matthan the father of Jacob,16and Jacob the father of Joseph, the husband of Mary, and Mary was the mother of Jesus who is called the Messiah.17Thus there were fourteen generations in all from Abraham to David, fourteen from David to the exile to Babylon, and fourteen from the exile to the Messiah.

Greek Word Study

Βίβλος γενέσεως Biblos geneseōs — Book of origins: Matthew opens with the exact phrase used in the LXX of Gen 2:4 and Gen 5:1 — "book of origins." This is a deliberate literary signal: what follows is a new Genesis, a new act of creation. Matthew is writing a book that consciously begins where the Hebrew Bible began.

Χριστός Christos — Anointed One / Messiah: The Greek translation of the Hebrew mashiach (anointed). In Jewish usage, the Messiah was the coming Davidic king who would restore Israel's kingdom. Matthew's opening verse asserts that Jesus of Nazareth is this figure — a claim that would have been electrifying and controversial to a Jewish audience.

υἱοῦ Δαυίδ huiou Dauid — Son of David: The title "son of David" carries the weight of 2 Samuel 7:12-16, Nathan's oracle: "I will raise up your offspring after you... I will establish the throne of his kingdom forever." Matthew establishes this claim before any narrative begins. It is the theological key that unlocks everything that follows.

Historical Context

The genealogy uses a three-part structure: Abraham to David (the promise), David to the Exile (the failure), the Exile to the Messiah (the restoration). Matthew compresses each period into exactly fourteen generations — almost certainly the gematria of David's name in Hebrew (D-W-D = 4+6+4 = 14). The deliberate symmetry signals that history is not random but moves by divine design toward its goal. Five women are named in the genealogy — Tamar, Rahab, Ruth, Bathsheba (called "Uriah's wife"), and Mary — an extraordinary departure from convention. Each appears in circumstances that were irregular or scandalous. Most scholars read this as Matthew's preparation for the greatest irregularity of all: a virgin birth. God has consistently worked through unconventional means.

Context

Matthew opens his Gospel not with a story but with a pedigree — and not just any pedigree. In Jewish culture, lineage was identity, and Davidic lineage was the specific qualification for messianic claims. By beginning with this genealogy, Matthew is not padding his introduction; he is making the most important legal argument of his book: Jesus is the legitimate heir to the throne of David and the inheritor of the promises to Abraham. Everything that follows rests on this foundation. The structure — three groups of fourteen — is not historical precision but theological architecture. Matthew is showing that history has a shape, that it moves through recognisable phases (promise, failure, restoration), and that the Messiah appears exactly when he should.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

Matthew begins as Moses began. The phrase "biblos geneseos" is the exact LXX wording of Genesis 2:4 and 5:1 — "the book of the generations." Matthew is announcing that his Gospel is a new Genesis, a new act of divine creation. This is not accidental: Matthew's Gospel is the most Jewish of the four, deliberately structured to present Jesus as the fulfilment of Israel's entire history and Scripture.

1:1

"Son of David, son of Abraham" — the order is significant. For Matthew's Jewish audience, David and Abraham were the two poles of covenant history. The Davidic covenant promised an eternal kingdom; the Abrahamic covenant promised blessing to all nations. Jesus is named as the fulfilment of both simultaneously. This is the Gospel in two phrases.

1:3–6

The four women named before Mary — Tamar (Gen 38), Rahab (Josh 2), Ruth (Ruth 1), and Bathsheba ("Uriah's wife") — are not incidental. Tamar and Rahab were Canaanites; Ruth was a Moabite; Bathsheba's presence is marked by her first husband's name, not her own. Matthew is preparing the reader for a God who works through the unexpected, the irregular, and the scandalous. None of these women's stories ends where convention would predict.

1:11–12

The Exile is the theological crisis of the genealogy. The Davidic line appeared to end here — Jeconiah sat on the throne and was carried to Babylon, and the kingdom never recovered. What follows in vv.12–16 are names almost entirely unknown — the line persisting in obscurity through the post-exilic period. Matthew is showing that God preserved the Davidic line even through the darkest centuries of Israel's history.

1:16–17

Matthew breaks the "X was the father of Y" formula at the critical moment: "Joseph the husband of Mary, and Mary was the mother of Jesus." The passive construction — "Jesus who is called the Messiah" — and the shift from paternal to maternal naming is Matthew's grammatical preparation for v.20's revelation. Joseph is the legal father who gives Jesus Davidic status; Mary is the biological mother. The genealogy is not contradicted by the virgin birth — it is designed to accommodate it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

This verse is the title and summary of the whole Gospel. Matthew does not begin with the pre-existent Son as John does, nor with the activity of the Spirit as Luke does, but with the human lineage — because his purpose is to show that the Christ who came in the flesh is the very one promised to David and Abraham. The order (David before Abraham) places the Davidic covenant foremost, since it is the more specific promise concerning the Messiah's nature and office.

1:3–5

That women are mentioned in a genealogy is unusual; that these particular women are mentioned is remarkable. Calvin: "God frequently makes use of extraordinary means to effect his purposes, so that we may learn not to confine him to what seems regular and customary to us." The irregularities in the Messiah's ancestry — Tamar's deception, Rahab's profession, Ruth's foreignness, Bathsheba's circumstances — all serve to prepare us for the supreme irregularity: God himself born of a virgin.

1:17

The threefold fourteen is a mnemonic device — "that the Jews might more easily retain it in their memory." But it also shows divine providence governing the course of history. Calvin against those who see history as random: the very structure of the genealogy teaches that God is directing all things toward a predetermined goal. The Messiah does not arrive accidentally at the end of history — he arrives exactly when the structure of redemptive history requires him.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:1

The NET renders βίβλος γενέσεως as "record of the genealogy" but notes the phrase is identical to LXX Gen 2:4 and 5:1, where it introduces a new section of cosmic or human origins. The note flags that Matthew may intend his Gospel to be read as a "new Genesis" — the story of a new creation beginning with Jesus. The translation choice "record" is more specific than the Greek warrants; "book of origins" would preserve the allusion to Genesis more clearly.

1:16

The NET note on ἐγεννήθη (v.16) carefully distinguishes the passive here from the active egennēsen used throughout the genealogy. The note observes that the switch to the passive and the use of the feminine relative pronoun ἐξ ἧς (from whom, feminine) unambiguously indicates that only Mary is the biological parent. The grammar excludes Joseph from biological fatherhood before the narrative of the virgin birth is given — a deliberate textual anticipation.

1:17

The NET note on the three groups of fourteen acknowledges that Matthew's actual count produces only thirteen generations in the third group by most reckonings. Several explanations are noted: Jeconiah may be counted twice (vv.11,12), or the count may follow ancient conventions where the last-named person in one group is the first of the next. The note concludes that Matthew's purpose is schematic rather than strictly historical — the theological pattern matters more than arithmetic precision.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
1:1

Biblos geneseōs — "book of origin" or "book of genealogy." The genitive geneseōs here is descriptive — the kind of book, not the content of it. Robertson notes this is the exact phrase used in the LXX of Gen 2:4 (the heavens and the earth) and Gen 5:1 (the generations of Adam). Matthew's opening words place his work consciously in the tradition of the Hebrew creation and genealogical accounts.

1:16

The passive ἐγεννήθη (was born) here is grammatically significant. Throughout vv.2–15 the active form egennēsen ("fathered") is used exclusively — "X fathered Y." At v.16 Matthew breaks the pattern: "Jacob fathered Joseph the husband of Mary, from whom was born (passive) Jesus." The shift to the passive genitive ἐξ ἧς — "from whom" (feminine singular) — makes clear that Joseph is not the biological father. The grammar points to the virgin birth before the narrative states it.

1:17

Πᾶσαι οὖν — "so then all." The inferential conjunction oun draws the conclusion from the pattern just laid out. The three groups of fourteen are presented as a deliberate arrangement, not simply a count. Dekatessares — fourteen — is used as a technical number, almost certainly the gematria of David's name in Hebrew (dalet=4, waw=6, dalet=4). Robertson: "The scheme is clearly artificial but the Jewish readers would appreciate the significance."

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
1:1

Chrysostom (Homily 1 on Matthew): "Why does Matthew trace the genealogy through Joseph rather than through Mary? Because it was not the custom to trace through women, and it was not necessary to mention the maternal descent, since the whole matter of the dispensation was in the legal ancestry through Joseph. By showing that Joseph is of the line of David, he shows that Mary also is, since they would not have contracted a marriage between one of David's line and one who was not."

1:3–5

Jerome: "Among the ancestors of our Lord and Saviour there are some who are not altogether praiseworthy — Judah and Tamar, David and Bathsheba. Our Lord and Saviour was not ashamed to be born of sinners and to take their sin upon himself so that he might do away with their sins." Augustine: "The women in the genealogy show that no one need despair of obtaining God's mercy, since he chose to become incarnate from those whose memories are stained."

1:17

Hilary of Poitiers: "The three groupings of fourteen are not chance but deliberate instruction. From Abraham to David is the period of promise; from David to the exile is the period of the kingdom and its failure; from the exile to Christ is the period of hope and waiting. The number fourteen — the double of seven — carries the significance of a complete and perfect fulfilment. The Messiah comes at the fullness of time, not before it."

Verses 18–25 — The Birth: Immanuel, God with Us
18This is how the birth of Jesus the Messiah came about: His mother Mary was pledged to be married to Joseph, but before they came together, she was found to be pregnant through the Holy Spirit.19Because Joseph her husband was faithful to the law, and yet did not want to expose her to public disgrace, he had in mind to divorce her quietly.20But after he had considered this, an angel of the Lord appeared to him in a dream and said, "Joseph son of David, do not be afraid to take Mary home as your wife, because what is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit.21She will give birth to a son, and you are to give him the name Jesus, because he will save his people from their sins."22All this took place to fulfil what the Lord had said through the prophet:23"The virgin will conceive and give birth to a son, and they will call him Immanuel" (which means "God with us").24When Joseph woke up, he did what the angel of the Lord had commanded him and took Mary home as his wife.25But he did not consummate their marriage until she gave birth to a son. And he gave him the name Jesus.

Greek Word Study

Ἰησοῦς Iēsous — Jesus / Yeshua (He saves): The Greek form of the Hebrew Yeshua (Joshua) — "YHWH saves." The name is not chosen for sound or family tradition but for meaning: "he will save his people from their sins." Matthew immediately reinterprets what "saves" means — not from political enemies, as the populist messianic expectation held, but from sin itself.

Ἐμμανουήλ Emmanouel — God with us: Matthew quotes Isaiah 7:14 (LXX) but does not say "Jesus was called Immanuel" — no one in the Gospels calls Jesus by this name. Immanuel is a theological title, not a personal name. Matthew is claiming that in Jesus, the entire weight of what "God with us" means finds its fulfilment. The presence of God among his people — the great hope of the prophets — has arrived in human flesh.

δίκαιος dikaios — righteous / just: Applied to Joseph in v.19: he was "righteous" (NIV: "faithful to the law"). The word carries the full weight of OT covenant faithfulness. Joseph faces an impossible situation — the law required action, his love required mercy. Matthew presents his response (quiet divorce rather than public accusation) as an expression of righteousness, not a compromise of it. The angel's intervention then calls him to a third way: neither punishment nor quiet retreat, but faithful obedience.

Historical Context

Betrothal in first-century Judaism was legally binding — far more so than modern engagement. A betrothed woman who was found pregnant was legally an adulteress; the penalty under Jewish law (Deut 22:23-24) was death by stoning, though by the Roman period this was rarely enforced. Divorce was the normal legal remedy. Joseph's plan to "divorce her quietly" meant to provide the required bill of divorcement without specifying the grounds publicly — an act of genuine kindness given the legal situation. The angel's address to Joseph as "son of David" (v.20) is the only time in Matthew this title is applied to anyone other than Jesus. It is both a legal identification (confirming Joseph's role in the Davidic lineage) and a call to action — the son of David is being commissioned to give the Messiah his name and thereby incorporate him into the Davidic line.

Context

This is one of the most compressed and theologically dense passages in the New Testament. In eight verses Matthew accomplishes several things simultaneously: he explains the virgin birth, he establishes Joseph's legal role in giving Jesus Davidic status, he names Jesus with a name that defines his mission, and he introduces the "fulfilment formula" that will appear throughout his Gospel — "to fulfil what the Lord had said through the prophet." The fulfilment formula (v.22) is Matthew's signature. He uses it at least ten times, always following the same pattern: event → "all this took place to fulfil" → citation. This is not proof-texting; Matthew is claiming that the events of Jesus' life are not incidental to Old Testament prophecy but are its intended destination.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 7:14"The virgin will conceive and give birth to a son, and will call him Immanuel." Matthew cites the LXX parthenos (virgin) rather than the Hebrew almah (young woman / maiden). The LXX translation had already drawn the text toward a supernatural reading; Matthew claims it finds its fulfilment here.
  • Luke 1:26–38Luke's parallel account of the Annunciation — to Mary rather than Joseph. Together they establish the virgin birth from two independent witnesses.
  • Isa 9:6–7"For to us a child is born... and he will be called Wonderful Counsellor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace." The Immanuel theme of Isaiah finds its fullest expression here — a child who bears divine titles.
  • Num 6:22–27The Aaronic blessing — "the LORD make his face shine on you." The name Immanuel ("God with us") is the answer to this blessing's implicit prayer: God's presence with his people.

Places

Bethlehem
Judean hill country; birthplace of David and Jesus
Joseph is of the house and lineage of David — which means Bethlehem. Though not named in ch.1, Bethlehem is the necessary geographical implication of the genealogy (2:1 will name it). The birth in Bethlehem fulfils Micah 5:2.
Nazareth
Lower Galilee; Joseph and Mary's hometown
Matthew's ch.1 ends with the angel's message to Joseph in Nazareth — the family's home. After the birth in Bethlehem and flight to Egypt (ch.2), they will settle back in Nazareth (2:23). The Galilean origin of the Messiah fulfils Isaiah 9:1-2.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:18

The phrase "before they came together" is Matthew's delicate but unambiguous statement that the marriage was not yet consummated. The pregnancy cannot be attributed to Joseph. Matthew establishes this not to argue apologetically but to set up the angelic explanation that follows. The reader is placed in exactly Joseph's position: confronted with an inexplicable fact that demands an explanation.

1:19

"Faithful to the law, and yet did not want to expose her to public disgrace" — Matthew presents these as twin motivations in tension. The law required action; love required restraint. The word dikaios (righteous) here does not mean merely law-abiding but characterises Joseph's whole orientation toward God and others. His chosen solution — quiet divorce — attempts to honour both obligations. It is a morally admirable response to an impossible situation.

1:20

The angel's address — "Joseph son of David" — is the legal key. By identifying Joseph as the son of David and then commanding him to take Mary as his wife and name the child, the angel is commissioning Joseph to perform the legal act that will incorporate Jesus into the Davidic line. Joseph's act of naming (v.25) is the act of legal adoption in Jewish custom. The virgin birth and the Davidic lineage are not in tension — Matthew has constructed his narrative to show how both are simultaneously true.

1:21

"He will save his people from their sins" — not from Roman oppression, not from political subjugation, but from sins. Matthew corrects the dominant messianic expectation at the very moment he introduces the Messiah's name. The salvation Jesus brings is not the kind Israel was expecting. This is perhaps the most important single sentence in the entire birth narrative.

1:22–23

The fulfilment formula makes its first of ten appearances. Matthew does not say Isaiah "predicted" the virgin birth — he says this "took place to fulfil" what was spoken. The Greek plēroō (fulfil) in Matthew carries a richer meaning than mere prediction-and-completion: it means the full meaning of a text has now arrived. Isaiah 7:14 was not exhausted by its original eighth-century BC context; it was pregnant with a meaning that only the Messiah's birth could fully release.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:18–19

Joseph's righteousness, Calvin observes, is not cold legal compliance but a form of mercy. He could have demanded the full penalty; he chose the lightest available option. This is not weakness but wisdom — he was not yet certain what had happened and chose to err on the side of compassion. Calvin: "While Joseph is deliberating what to do, God anticipates him by an angel. Thus it often happens that God does not immediately explain all things to his servants, but allows them to remain for a time in doubt, that he may afterwards shed greater light."

1:20

The dream is God's condescension to Joseph's uncertainty. Calvin notes that God frequently used dreams in the OT (cf. Jacob, Joseph, Daniel) as a means of revelation adapted to human capacity. The angel's words — "do not be afraid" — address not terror but moral hesitation. Joseph is not frightened; he is uncertain what righteousness requires. The angel resolves his doubt by revealing the divine origin of the pregnancy.

1:22–23

On the Isaiah 7:14 quotation: Calvin argues that the prophecy had a double fulfilment — a near fulfilment in Hezekiah's time and a complete fulfilment in Christ. The "virgin" language points forward to a greater sign than the near context required. This is Calvin's general approach to prophetic fulfilment: prophets spoke to their immediate circumstances but their words carried a surplus of meaning that only Christ could exhaust. The "God with us" title reaches its true depth in the Incarnation alone.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:18

The NET note on πνεύματος ἁγίου (Holy Spirit) here observes that the Greek lacks the definite article — "holy spirit" rather than "the Holy Spirit." This is not unusual in Greek; the phrase with and without the article refers to the same divine person throughout the NT. The note also addresses "pledged to be married" as representing the formal betrothal state, which was legally distinct from cohabitation but as binding as marriage under Jewish law.

1:21

The NET note on Ἰησοῦς (Jesus) explains the Hebrew-Greek etymology: Hebrew Yeshua → Aramaic Yeshua → Greek Iēsous. The name means "YHWH saves" or "YHWH is salvation." The note observes that the angel's explanation ("because he will save his people from their sins") is a direct etymological gloss — the name is interpreted in terms of mission. The NET translates "save" (σώσει) as a genuine future, indicating a mission not yet accomplished at this point in the narrative.

1:23

The NET note on Isaiah 7:14 gives careful attention to the Hebrew almah (young woman of marriageable age, often presumed to be a virgin) vs. the LXX parthenos (virgin, unambiguous). Matthew cites the LXX. The note acknowledges the scholarly debate about the original Isaiah context — Ahaz's sign was near-term and historical — while affirming Matthew's typological-fulfilment reading: a pattern established in Isaiah 7 reaches its ultimate expression in Jesus' birth. The NET does not resolve the debate but documents both positions.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
1:18

Μνηστευθείσης (betrothed, passive aorist participle) — the legal state of formal betrothal, which in Jewish law was as binding as marriage and required a formal divorce to dissolve. The participle is genitive absolute, establishing the legal background. Robertson notes that the aorist aspect indicates the betrothal was completed prior to the pregnancy being discovered — the temporal sequence matters for the legal argument.

1:19

Δειγματίσαι (to expose publicly, to make a public example) — a strong word used in LXX Num 25:4 of public punishment. Joseph chose μὴ θέλων (not willing, present participle of the will) — an ongoing disposition of the heart, not a momentary decision. Robertson: "The present participle θέλων pictures Joseph's settled attitude of mind — he simply was not willing to shame her, whatever the law permitted."

1:23

Καλέσουσιν (they will call him) — third person plural subject is impersonal: "he will be called." The LXX uses καλέσεις (you will call, second person singular), but Matthew shifts to the plural, which may be his way of applying the prophecy to the entire community's recognition of Jesus rather than to a single individual's naming. The name Ἐμμανουήλ is transliterated from Hebrew and then translated — Matthew assumes readers may not know Hebrew.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
1:18

Chrysostom (Homily 4): "Consider how great the virtue of Joseph — that when he suspected something strange, he was not harsh or cruel, but most gentle. He did not even intend to punish her by the law, but sought to dismiss her quietly. His very suspicion was full of gentleness, for he did not believe the adultery had been from lust but from some divine mystery beyond his understanding." This interpretation — Joseph suspecting something miraculous rather than something criminal — is the dominant patristic reading.

1:23

Augustine: "The prophet says 'a virgin shall conceive and bear' — not 'shall conceive as a virgin and bear as a non-virgin' — so that the sign would be in the very birth, not only in the conception. Both are signs: that she conceived without a man, and that she gave birth while remaining a virgin. Matthew cites the prophecy in full to show that both are fulfilled." The perpetual virginity argument appears here in embryonic form in Augustine's reading of Matthew's fulfilment formula.

1:25

Jerome: "Some of our people, relying on this passage, think Mary had other children by Joseph after the birth of the Lord. But we hold perpetual virginity of both. The phrase 'until she gave birth' does not imply intercourse after the birth, any more than 'I will be with you until the end of the age' implies Christ will abandon us after the age ends. 'Until' in Scripture often simply marks a boundary event without implying a change of condition after it."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Joseph ben Jacob
Davidic heir; legal father of Jesus
The most legally important figure in the genealogy and birth narrative. His Davidic descent provides Jesus with legal right to the Davidic throne; his act of naming Jesus (v.25) is the formal adoption by which that right is conferred. Matthew presents Joseph as a model of righteous response to divine intervention: he receives a dream, he obeys immediately, he does not attempt to explain or negotiate. He is a new Joseph (cf. Genesis 37-50) — righteous, dreaming, providentially placed.
Mary the Theotokos
Virgin mother; bearer of the Messiah
Matthew's account focuses on Joseph's perspective, but Mary is the theological centre. The passive construction "was found to be pregnant through the Holy Spirit" and the genealogy's "from whom was born Jesus" (ἐξ ἧς) both center the biological reality in her. Matthew mentions none of Mary's inner experience — that is Luke's account — but the four named women in the genealogy (Tamar, Rahab, Ruth, Bathsheba) who precede her all share the quality of being instruments of divine purpose in unexpected ways.
Jesus the Messiah
Son of David, Son of Abraham; Saviour from sin
Matthew introduces Jesus simultaneously as the legal heir to the Davidic throne and the one whose very name defines his mission: Yeshua — YHWH saves. The Immanuel title (v.23) adds a second dimension: he is not only the Davidic king but the presence of God himself dwelling with his people. Matthew's opening chapter contains the entire christological programme of his Gospel in miniature.

Ancient Sources

Davidic Gematria — The Three Fourteens
The Hebrew name David (דוד, D-W-D) has a numerical value of 4+6+4 = 14 in the system of gematria (assigning numerical values to Hebrew letters). Matthew's three groups of fourteen generations are widely understood as a coded reference to David's name. This was a recognised device in Jewish interpretation: structuring a narrative around a significant name's number to highlight its theological importance.
The device would have been immediately recognisable to Matthew's Jewish readers. Matthew is not counting carelessly — he is making a point: this entire history is the history of David's promise, and it climaxes in the one whose number is triple-David.
The Betrothal Custom in Second Temple Judaism
Betrothal (erusin) in first-century Palestine was a formal legal contract, usually arranged between families, that created the legal status of husband and wife without cohabitation. A betrothed couple required a formal get (bill of divorce) to dissolve the union. Sexual infidelity during betrothal was treated as adultery under Torah law (Deut 22:23-24). The period between betrothal and marriage (nisuin) typically lasted about a year.
This social background explains Joseph's dilemma precisely. He was legally Mary's husband, she was legally his wife, and the law addressed infidelity during this period as a capital matter — though actual executions for adultery were extremely rare in the Roman period. His impulse to "divorce her quietly" was a maximum act of kindness within the legal constraints.
Isaiah 7:14 in Context and Reception
Isaiah 7:14 was spoken to King Ahaz of Judah (c.735 BC) during the Syro-Ephraimite crisis. The near-term sign was probably the birth of Hezekiah or another royal child in Ahaz's own time. The LXX translation of almah as parthenos (virgin) shifted the text's potential toward a supernatural reading. By the NT period, Isaiah 7:14 was already part of the messianic cluster of texts read in Jewish and early Christian interpretation.
Matthew's citation follows the LXX and makes the supernatural fulfilment claim explicit. The Qumran literature shows that first-century Jewish readers were already reading the broader Isaiah 7-12 section as messianic. Matthew is not importing a foreign reading — he is claiming that the messianic reading his contemporaries applied to the text finds its true referent in Jesus.

Reception History

Ignatius of Antioch, Letter to the Ephesians (c.AD 110)
"The virginity of Mary and her giving birth were hidden from the ruler of this age, as was also the death of the Lord — three mysteries of renown, which were wrought in silence by God." Ignatius is the earliest post-apostolic writer to reflect on the theological significance of the virgin birth as a mystery hidden from demonic powers — a theme that will recur in patristic theology.
Justin Martyr, Dialogue with Trypho (c.AD 160)
Justin explicitly defends Matthew's citation of Isaiah 7:14, arguing at length against the Jewish objection that almah does not mean "virgin." He is the first writer to preserve the Jewish counter-reading and the Christian response — a debate that continues into modern scholarship. Justin's use of the LXX parthenos as the definitive text shows how quickly the Greek translation became the standard for NT apologetics.
Jerome, Commentary on Matthew (c.AD 398)
Jerome's Latin translation (the Vulgate) renders the Isaiah quotation with virgo (virgin), following the LXX against the Hebrew. His commentary on Matthew 1 addresses the genealogical questions — why four women are named, why Jeconiah appears in both the eleventh and twelfth positions, how the three-fourteen structure works. Jerome establishes the interpretive tradition that dominated Western Christianity for over a millennium.
Raymond Brown, The Birth of the Messiah (1977)
Brown's magisterial study remains the definitive modern critical treatment. He argues that Matthew and Luke's infancy narratives are independent, each drawing on different traditions, and that the genealogies are theological constructs not intended as historical records in the modern sense. Brown affirms the virgin birth as a theological claim while treating the genealogical details as midrashic elaboration. His work shaped all subsequent NT scholarship on the infancy narratives.

Literary Structure

Structure — vv.1–17; vv.18–25Two distinct units: a genealogy (a formal literary genre) followed by a narrative episode. The genealogy is the thesis; the birth narrative is the first proof.
The Formula — vv.2–16Repeated "X ἐγέννησεν Y" (X fathered Y) — 38 times. The formula is deliberately broken at v.16, signalling the exceptional nature of Jesus' birth.
Three epochs of exactly fourteen generations: Abraham→David (promise), David→Exile (failure), Exile→Messiah (restoration). The structure is theological, not strictly historical.
Five Women — vv.3,5,6,16Tamar, Rahab, Ruth, Bathsheba ("wife of Uriah"), Mary. Each transgresses conventional expectations; each is an instrument of divine purpose. Prepares for the virgin birth.
Fulfilment Formula — vv.22–23"All this took place to fulfil what the Lord had said through the prophet." First of ten such formulae in Matthew — his signature theological claim.
Matthew 1 is structured as a legal brief: the genealogy establishes Jesus' credentials, the birth narrative introduces the theological paradox (virgin birth + Davidic adoption), and the first fulfilment formula establishes the interpretive framework for the entire Gospel. Matthew begins where Genesis begins — with a "book of origins" — and where Israel's hope ends — with the Messiah of David and Abraham.

Greek-Rooted Reading

1:1 Βίβλος γενέσεως biblos geneseōs — book of origin/genealogy (cf. LXX Gen 2:4; 5:1)  |  Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ Iēsou Christou — Jesus the Messiah (gen. of apposition)  |  υἱοῦ Δαυὶδ huiou Dauid — son of David (Davidic covenant, 2 Sam 7)  |  υἱοῦ Ἀβραάμ huiou Abraam — son of Abraham (Abrahamic covenant, Gen 12).

1:21 Ἰησοῦν Iēsoun — Jesus/Yeshua (יֵשׁוּעַ, "YHWH saves")  |  σώσει sōsei — he will save (future active, σῴζω)  |  ἁμαρτιῶν hamartiōn — from their sins (gen. of separation — saved from). The name etymologically defines the mission.

Intertextual Threading

Matt 1:1←Gen 1:1; 5:1Echo
Biblos geneseōs — "book of origins." Matthew opens with the exact LXX phrase that begins Genesis 2:4 and 5:1. The Gospel is a new Genesis: a new act of divine creation, a new human family beginning with a new Adam.
Matt 1:1←2 Sam 7:12–16Fulfilment
Nathan's oracle to David: "I will raise up your offspring after you... I will establish the throne of his kingdom forever." Matthew's first verse asserts that Jesus is this promised son. The rest of the chapter explains how.
Matt 1:23←Isa 7:14Fulfilment
"The virgin will conceive and give birth to a son." Matthew's first of ten fulfilment-formula citations. Isaiah's sign for Ahaz reaches its full meaning in the virginal conception of Jesus.
Matt 1:21←Ps 130:8Echo
"He himself will redeem Israel from all their sins." The messianic expectation in Psalms is that the coming king will deal with sin. Matthew's angel gives the same mission to Jesus — and names it as the reason for his name.
Matt 1:1→Matt 28:19–20Bracket
"Make disciples of all nations... I am with you always." Matthew ends where he begins: "son of Abraham" (blessing to the nations, Gen 12) + "God with us" (Immanuel). The Gospel's last words fulfil its first verse.
Matt 1:5←Ruth 1:16–17Echo
Ruth the Moabite in the Davidic genealogy. Her loyalty — "your people will be my people, your God my God" — prefigures the Gentile inclusion that Matthew's Gospel will culminate in the Great Commission.

Textual Notes

1:7–8Asaph vs Asa; Amos vs Amaziah
NA28/UBS5 Ἀσάφ (Asaph) in v.7 and Ἀμώς (Amos) in v.8. Later MSS Ἀσά (Asa) and Ἀμασίας (Amaziah), correcting to match 1 Kings/Chronicles.
Most modern critical editions retain Asaph and Amos as the original text. Matthew (or his source) appears to have used a Greek OT that spelled these names differently from the MT-based reading familiar from the historical books. The "errors" may be Matthew's own — they are not correctable by manuscript evidence and are likely original. This textual issue does not affect the theological argument.
1:16Joseph's relationship to Jesus — variant readings
Most MSS "Jacob fathered Joseph the husband of Mary, from whom was born Jesus." Sinaitic Syriac "Joseph, to whom the virgin Mary was betrothed, fathered Jesus who is called the Christ." The Sinaitic Syriac appears to contradict the virgin birth as Matthew narrates it.
The Sinaitic Syriac reading is isolated and almost certainly secondary — it contradicts both Matthew's own v.20 and the wider NT testimony. The standard Greek text is supported by Codex Vaticanus, Codex Sinaiticus, and the overwhelming majority of manuscripts. The Syriac variant may reflect a different theological tradition or a scribal harmonisation attempt. It is not considered original by any modern critical text.

Scholarly Debates

The Historical Value of Matthew's Genealogy
Theological construct, not historical record
Raymond Brown, John Meier, most historical-critical scholars
Matthew's genealogy contains arithmetical irregularities (the third group has only thirteen names by most counts), includes women not found in the OT genealogies, and is irreconcilable with Luke's genealogy at the post-Davidic level. The three-fourteen structure is clearly artificial. Matthew is constructing a theological argument, not transcribing a registry.
Historical foundation with theological shaping
Richard Bauckham, Craig Keener, N.T. Wright
Genealogical records were kept and consulted in Second Temple Judaism; Josephus mentions his own. Matthew may have had access to Joseph's family records and arranged them theologically. The discrepancy with Luke reflects two different lines of descent (one through Solomon, one through Nathan) and/or two different purposes (legal vs biological). The artificiality of the structure does not require historical invention.
Midrashic composition
Some Jewish-Christian scholars
Matthew is composing in the manner of Jewish midrash — using biblical materials and genealogical conventions to make theological points. Historical accuracy in the modern sense is not the genre's concern. What matters is that the theological claims (Davidic descent, Abrahamic fulfilment) are established, not that every name can be verified in an archive.
The debate ultimately concerns genre: what kind of document is Matthew 1:1-17? Modern historical scholarship tends toward the theological-construct position; evangelical scholarship defends a historical foundation. The two positions are not entirely incompatible: Matthew could have used real genealogical material and shaped it theologically — exactly what ancient historians routinely did.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 1 is the theological overture of the entire Gospel. Every major theme Matthew will develop — Jesus as fulfilment of Scripture, the Davidic Messiah, the new Genesis, the inclusion of Gentiles, the presence of God with his people — is sounded here before the narrative properly begins. The genealogy is not preliminary throat-clearing; it is the concentrated statement of Matthew's thesis: Jesus is the goal of Israel's history and the answer to the world's need.
