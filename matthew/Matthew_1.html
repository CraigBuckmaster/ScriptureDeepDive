The Genealogy of Jesus; The Birth of Immanuel

Themes

Author: Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector (telōnēs) at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised by his community. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles. The tradition identifying the apostle Matthew as the gospel's author is consistent from the 2nd century (Papias, Irenaeus, Origen) and has no early counter-tradition. Matthew's tax-collector background explains the gospel's distinctive precision with numbers, lists, and systematically organized material. When written: Most scholars date Matthew between AD 50–90, with significant debate. An early date (before AD 70) is supported by the gospel's apparent familiarity with pre-destruction Jerusalem. Matthew drew on Mark (the earliest gospel), a collection of Jesus' sayings known as Q, and material unique to Matthew (M source). The gospel was likely written in Syrian Antioch or Judea for a primarily Jewish-Christian community. What prompted it: Matthew writes for Jewish believers — people who knew their Torah, their prophets, and their Psalms — and demonstrates systematically that Jesus is the long-awaited Messiah of Israel. The governing question is: how does Jesus fulfil what Moses, David, and the prophets promised? The answer unfolds through five great discourse blocks (chs.5–7, 10, 13, 18, 23–25), mirroring the five books of Moses, and through Matthew's characteristic formula: "this took place to fulfil what was spoken by the prophet." Jesus is presented as the new Moses, the Son of David, and the Son of God — the convergence of every strand of Israel's hope.

Verses 1–17 — The Genealogy: A New Genesis
1This is the genealogy of Jesus the Messiah the son of David, the son of Abraham:2Abraham was the father of Isaac, Isaac the father of Jacob, Jacob the father of Judah and his brothers,3Judah the father of Perez and Zerah, whose mother was Tamar, Perez the father of Hezron, Hezron the father of Ram,4Ram the father of Amminadab, Amminadab the father of Nahshon, Nahshon the father of Salmon,5Salmon the father of Boaz, whose mother was Rahab, Boaz the father of Obed, whose mother was Ruth, Obed the father of Jesse,6and Jesse the father of King David. David was the father of Solomon, whose mother had been Uriah's wife,7Solomon the father of Rehoboam, Rehoboam the father of Abijah, Abijah the father of Asa,8Asa the father of Jehoshaphat, Jehoshaphat the father of Jehoram, Jehoram the father of Uzziah,9Uzziah the father of Jotham, Jotham the father of Ahaz, Ahaz the father of Hezekiah,10Hezekiah the father of Manasseh, Manasseh the father of Amon, Amon the father of Josiah,11and Josiah the father of Jeconiah and his brothers at the time of the exile to Babylon.12After the exile to Babylon: Jeconiah was the father of Shealtiel, Shealtiel the father of Zerubbabel,13Zerubbabel the father of Abihud, Abihud the father of Eliakim, Eliakim the father of Azor,14Azor the father of Zadok, Zadok the father of Akim, Akim the father of Elihud,15Elihud the father of Eleazar, Eleazar the father of Matthan, Matthan the father of Jacob,16and Jacob the father of Joseph, the husband of Mary, and Mary was the mother of Jesus, who is called the Messiah.17Thus there were fourteen generations in all from Abraham to David, fourteen from David to the exile to Babylon, and fourteen from the exile to the Messiah.

Greek Word Study

Βίβλος γενέσεως Biblos geneseōs — book of the genealogy/genesis: The opening words deliberately echo Genesis (LXX: Biblos geneseōs). Matthew signals that this is a new Genesis — the story of a new creation inaugurated by the Messiah. The genealogy is not administrative record-keeping; it is a theological argument that all of God's history with Israel reaches its culmination in Jesus.

Χριστός Christos — Messiah/Christ: The Greek translation of the Hebrew Māšîaḥ (anointed one). The title carries the weight of all prophetic expectation — the anointed king who would restore Israel and bring God's kingdom.

υἱοῦ Δαυίδ huiou Dauid — son of David: Jesus's Davidic credentials — the royal lineage making him the legitimate heir to Israel's throne. "Son of David" is the most frequent messianic title in Matthew (1:1; 9:27; 12:23; 15:22; 20:30–31; 21:9, 15).

Context

The genealogy is Matthew's thesis statement. The three-times-fourteen structure (v.17) is deliberate — fourteen is the numerical value of David's name in Hebrew (D+W+D = 4+6+4). The four women included — Tamar, Rahab, Ruth, Uriah's wife (Bathsheba) — are all associated with irregular or scandalous stories; all are connected to Gentiles. Matthew announces from the first paragraph that the Messiah's family tree is scandalously inclusive.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

Biblos geneseōs echoes Genesis 2:4 and 5:1 in the LXX. Matthew signals a new Genesis. The three titles — Jesus (the name), Christ (the office), Son of David (the royal line), Son of Abraham (the covenant line) — are a compressed theology of the Messiah's identity.

1:5–6

The four women. Tamar: conceived by her father-in-law through deception. Rahab: a Canaanite prostitute. Ruth: a Moabite. Uriah's wife: victim of David's adultery and murder. Grace operates through broken vessels — a preview of the entire gospel.

Verses 18–25 — The Birth: Immanuel, God with Us
18This is how the birth of Jesus the Messiah came about: His mother Mary was pledged to be married to Joseph, but before they came together, she was found to be pregnant through the Holy Spirit.19Because Joseph her husband was faithful to the law, and yet did not want to expose her to public disgrace, he had in mind to divorce her quietly.20But after he had considered this, an angel of the Lord appeared to him in a dream and said, "Joseph son of David, do not be afraid to take Mary home as your wife, because what is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit.21She will give birth to a son, and you are to give him the name Jesus, because he will save his people from their sins."22All this took place to fulfil what the Lord had said through the prophet:23"The virgin will conceive and give birth to a son, and they will call him Immanuel" (which means "God with us").24When Joseph woke up, he did what the angel of the Lord had commanded him and took Mary home as his wife.25But he did not consummate their marriage until she gave birth to a son. And he gave him the name Jesus.

Greek Word Study

Ἰησοῦς Iēsous — Jesus: The Greek form of Hebrew Yēšûaʿ (Joshua), meaning "the LORD saves." The angel's explanation in v.21 is the etymology: "he will save his people from their sins." The name is his mission statement.

Ἐμμανουήλ Emmanouel — Immanuel — God with us: Matthew's first fulfilment quotation. The theological claim is the most radical possible: in this child, God himself is present. The gospel ends with the same promise: "I am with you always" (28:20). Immanuel brackets the entire gospel.

δίκαιος dikaios — righteous/just: V.19: Joseph is described as dikaios. His righteousness is expressed not in harsh judgment but in mercy (quiet divorce). Matthew's first description of human righteousness — a man who chooses mercy within legal faithfulness. A preview of the Sermon on the Mount.

Context

The birth narrative focuses entirely on Joseph — unlike Luke's account which centres on Mary. Joseph is the model of righteous obedience: he receives the angelic message, trusts the divine explanation, and acts in complete compliance. He names the child (v.25), the legal act that incorporates Jesus into the Davidic line. The fulfilment formula ("all this took place to fulfil") is Matthew's distinctive marker — it appears ten times in the gospel.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 7:14The Immanuel prophecy quoted in v.23. In its original context a sign to King Ahaz; Matthew reads it as pointing to the ultimate fulfilment: God's presence not in a sign but in a person.
  • Luke 1:26–38Luke's parallel account centred on Mary. The two accounts are complementary — each author focuses on the parent through whose perspective they tell the story.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:18

The virgin birth is the foundational miracle of the gospel. If Jesus is not uniquely conceived by the Holy Spirit, his claim to be "God with us" has no basis. The virgin birth is the mechanism by which the eternal Son entered human nature without the Adamic sin nature.

1:23

Immanuel — God with us. Matthew places this first because it is the theological heart of his entire gospel. Every healing, teaching, and exorcism is an expression of God's personal presence. The gospel closes: "I am with you always, to the very end of the age." Matthew brackets his entire account with the Immanuel promise.

Historical Sources

Davidic Messianism
The three-times-fourteen structure reflects the gematria of David's name. Matthew presents Jesus' birth as the culmination of the Davidic covenant expectation rooted in 2 Sam 7:12–16.
The genealogy is structured as a theological argument.
Jewish Betrothal
Jewish betrothal (erusin) was legally binding — it required a formal bill of divorce to dissolve. Joseph's dilemma makes sense only in this legal context: Mary is legally his wife, but the marriage is not yet consummated.
The betrothal context is essential for understanding Joseph's legal options.

Reception History

Ignatius of Antioch (c.AD 110)
The virgin birth as the foundational mystery that "defeated the prince of this world." From the earliest period the Immanuel claim was the gospel's most scandalous assertion.
Calvin on Joseph
Joseph as the model of "obedient faith under incomprehension" — acting on God's word without being able to rationally verify it.
