Matthew 4

The Temptation of Jesus; The Call of the First Disciples; Ministry in Galilee

Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, a tax collector called by Jesus at his customs post in Capernaum (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles and an eyewitness to the ministry of Jesus. He would have been literate and numerate by profession — uniquely equipped to compose a carefully structured written account. Unanimous early tradition attributes the Gospel to him: Papias (c.AD 125) records that "Matthew compiled the oracles in the Hebrew language, and everyone interpreted them as best he could." The Gospel as we have it is in polished literary Greek, suggesting either a Greek composition drawing on Aramaic sources, or a Greek original.

When written

c.AD 80–90, traditionally associated with Antioch of Syria, the first great Gentile church and a centre of Jewish-Christian interaction. The Gospel presupposes the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) in its apocalyptic passages (Matt 22:7; 24:15-22), suggesting composition after that date. It draws on Mark's Gospel (c.AD 65-70) and on a sayings source shared with Luke (conventionally called Q). Matthew shapes his sources with a more formal, structured literary hand than any of the other evangelists.

What prompted it

To present Jesus of Nazareth as the fulfilment of Israel's entire scriptural heritage — the promised Messiah, the new Moses, the true Israel, and the Son of God. Matthew writes primarily for a Jewish-Christian community navigating its relationship to the synagogue after AD 70. His ten fulfilment-formula citations ("all this took place to fulfil what was spoken through the prophet") are the theological spine of the work: Israel's Scripture is not superseded but completed in Jesus. The Great Commission (28:18-20) shows the universal horizon of a Gospel that begins with Abraham and ends with all nations.

Verses 1–11 — The Temptation: Three Tests in the Wilderness
1Then Jesus was led by the Spirit into the wilderness to be tempted by the devil.2After fasting forty days and forty nights, he was hungry.3The tempter came to him and said, "If you are the Son of God, tell these stones to become bread."4Jesus answered, "It is written: 'Man shall not live on bread alone, but on every word that comes from the mouth of God.'"5Then the devil took him to the holy city and had him stand on the highest point of the temple.6"If you are the Son of God," he said, "throw yourself down. For it is written: 'He will command his angels concerning you, and they will lift you up in their hands, so that you will not strike your foot against a stone.'"7Jesus answered him, "It is also written: 'Do not put the Lord your God to the test.'"8Again, the devil took him to a very high mountain and showed him all the kingdoms of the world and their splendor.9"All this I will give you," he said, "if you will bow down and worship me."10Jesus said to him, "Away from me, Satan! For it is written: 'Worship the Lord your God, and serve him only.'"11Then the devil left him, and angels came and attended him.

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The temptation narrative is structured as a Deuteronomy exam. Jesus has just been declared God's Son at the baptism; Satan now attacks that identity three times with "if you are the Son of God." Each test targets a different dimension of messianic identity: provision (bread/Messiah as miracle-worker), protection (temple/Messiah as wonderworker), power (kingdoms/Messiah as military conqueror). Jesus answers each with Deuteronomy, recapitulating Israel's wilderness story as the obedient Son where Israel was disobedient.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 8:3; 6:16; 6:13The three texts Jesus quotes — all from the section of Deuteronomy addressing Israel's wilderness failures. Jesus is passing the test Israel failed, from the same textbook.
  • Ps 91:11–12Satan quotes Scripture (correctly) to support the second temptation. The misuse of Scripture in service of testing God is the diagnostic of the second temptation.
  • Num 14:33–34; Exod 16–17Israel's 40 years of wilderness wandering — bread complaints, water testing, idolatry. Jesus's 40 days recapitulates all three failures and passes them all.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
4:1-11

MacArthur: The three temptations each target a dimension of Jesus's messianic identity: provision (bread), protection (the pinnacle), and power (the kingdoms). Satan offers shortcuts around the cross — each temptation would give Jesus what belongs to him without the suffering required to obtain it rightly. Jesus refuses each with Scripture.

4:4

MacArthur: Man shall not live on bread alone but on every word that comes from the mouth of God (Deut 8:3). Jesus quotes from Moses's wilderness address — Israel failed in the wilderness where Jesus succeeds. The same passage context invoked for bread also invokes the manna narrative. Jesus is the faithful Israel who passes the test his nation failed.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1-11

Calvin: These three temptations comprehend all satanic assaults. The first is against trust in God's provision; the second against trust in God's protection; the third against worship of God alone. Every temptation resolves into one of these three. Christ conquers each by Scripture — teaching us that God's word is our only sufficient weapon.

4:4

Calvin on 'man shall not live on bread alone': Christ quotes Deut 8:3, where Moses reminds Israel that God fed them with manna to teach that life depends on the divine word, not on material provision alone. He will not use miraculous power to provide for himself outside the Father's appointed means.

Robertson — Word Pictures

4:1-4

Robertson notes that 'led up by the Spirit' (anēchthē hypo tou pneumatos) is a divine passive — the Spirit actively directs Jesus into the wilderness for the purpose of being tempted. The forty days echo Moses (Exod 34:28) and Elijah (1 Kgs 19:8), placing Jesus in the covenant mediator tradition.

4:8-10

Robertson observes that Jesus does not deny the devil's authority over the kingdoms — the temptation is real. The offer is the immediate crown without the cross. 4:1-3

Chrysostom: The Spirit led Jesus into the wilderness to give opportunity for the devil to attempt all his arts. God permitted it so that the one who would arm us against the devil should first overcome him in solitude and then go forth to fight him openly in the world.

4:8-10

Hilary of Poitiers: The devil offers the kingdoms of the world to him who is Lord of all creation. The audacity of the temptation reveals the tempter's blindness — he does not know whom he is tempting. Christ refuses not from weakness but from the refusal to take by any means other than the cross what is rightly his.

Verses 12–17 — Ministry Begins in Galilee: Light in Darkness
12When Jesus heard that John had been put in prison, he withdrew to Galilee.13Leaving Nazareth, he went and lived in Capernaum, which was by the lake in the area of Zebulun and Naphtali—14to fulfill what was said through the prophet Isaiah:15"Land of Zebulun and land of Naphtali, the Way of the Sea, beyond the Jordan, Galilee of the Gentiles—16the people living in darkness have seen a great light; on those living in the land of the shadow of death a light has dawned."17From that time on Jesus began to preach, "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven has come near."

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The move to Galilee (vv.12–17) fulfils Isaiah 9:1–2 and inaugurates the public ministry. Galilee of the Gentiles — the northernmost, most cosmopolitan region — is the location Matthew chooses for the first proclamation. The kingdom begins at the margins, not the centre. Jesus's first words (v.17) are identical to John's first words (3:2): the continuity is deliberate. The forerunner has been arrested; the one he prepared the way for now steps forward.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 9:1–2Land of Zebulun and Naphtali; people in darkness see a great light — the OT context is the Assyrian devastation of the northern tribes. Matthew reads it as fulfilled by Jesus's Galilean ministry: light dawns on the most oppressed region first.
  • Matt 3:2Repent, for the kingdom of heaven has come near — Jesus's first words are verbatim John's. The continuity of the message is as important as the discontinuity of the messenger.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
4:12-17

MacArthur: Jesus withdraws to Galilee after John's arrest — the same withdrawal (anachōreō) word used in ch.2. The ministry base in Galilee of the Gentiles fulfills Isaiah 9:1-2. Matthew is placing Jesus's ministry in the prophetically anticipated geography: the light that dawns in darkness dawns precisely where Isaiah said it would.

4:17

MacArthur: From that time on Jesus began to preach. The same words mark the second major turning point in Matthew (16:21: From that time on Jesus began to explain to his disciples that he must suffer). The two from-that-time-on markers divide Matthew into three major movements: preaching the kingdom, predicting the cross, accomplishing it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:17

Calvin: Christ begins with the same proclamation as John — repentance is the gateway to the kingdom. This is not a legal demand but a gracious invitation: the one who announces the kingdom is the one who provides what the kingdom requires. He commands repentance and enables it by his own presence.

4:18-22

Calvin on the disciples' immediate response: The promptness of their obedience was not natural but supernatural — Christ drew them by a hidden, powerful call working in their hearts simultaneously with the outward word. We should not imagine every disciple must literally abandon occupation and family; but this total availability is the disposition genuine discipleship requires.

4:17

The NET note on Jesus beginning with John's words (3:2) identifies deliberate parallel: Matthew signals continuity between the forerunner and the one foretold. The message is the same because the reality is the same — the kingdom has arrived in John's announcement and now in the King himself.

4:23-25

The NET note on 'teaching, proclaiming, healing' traces this triad through Matthew (4:23; 9:35; 11:1) as the structural description of the Son of Man's mission. Jerome: He who is himself the light of the world chose to begin in the region Isaiah called the land of darkness. The fulfilment of Isaiah 9 shows that Gentile inclusion was not an afterthought — it was planned from the beginning. Galilee of the Gentiles receives the light before Jerusalem.

Chrysostom on the call of the disciples: They left their nets, their ship, and their father without delay. They did not say let us settle our affairs first. They forsook everything and followed — the model for every call to discipleship. The kingdom admits no delay, no negotiation.

Verses 18–25 — The First Disciples; Teaching, Proclaiming, Healing
18As Jesus was walking beside the Sea of Galilee, he saw two brothers, Simon called Peter and his brother Andrew. They were casting a net into the lake, for they were fishermen.19"Come, follow me," Jesus said, "and I will send you out to fish for people."20At once they left their nets and followed him.21Going on from there, he saw two other brothers, James son of Zebedee and his brother John. They were in a boat with their father Zebedee, preparing their nets. Jesus called them,22and immediately they left the boat and their father and followed him.23Jesus went throughout Galilee, teaching in their synagogues, proclaiming the good news of the kingdom and healing every disease and sickness among the people.24News about him spread all over Syria, and people brought to him all who were ill with various diseases, those suffering severe pain, the demon-possessed, those having seizures, and the paralyzed; and he healed them all.25Large crowds from Galilee, the Decapolis, Jerusalem, Judea and the region across the Jordan followed him.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
4:18-22

MacArthur: Follow me, and I will send you out to fish for people. The call to the fishermen transforms their vocation into a metaphor for their mission. Jesus does not replace their skills but redirects them. The immediacy of the response — they left their nets at once — is Matthew's standard discipleship pattern: the call and the response in the same verse.

4:23-25

MacArthur: Teaching, proclaiming, healing — Matthew's summary formula appears three times (4:23, 9:35, 11:1). The triad is deliberate: Jesus's ministry is word and deed together, never one without the other. The healings authenticate the proclamation; the proclamation interprets the healings. Neither alone is the kingdom.

Historical Context

None

Context

The calling of the first disciples (vv.18–22) and the summary of Jesus's early ministry (vv.23–25) bookend each other. The disciples are called with a fishing metaphor that will govern their identity throughout the gospel; the summary describes a ministry of teaching, proclaiming, and healing — the three activities that characterise everything in chs.5–9. The geographic sweep of v.25 (Galilee, Decapolis, Jerusalem, Judea, Transjordan) anticipates the commission to "all nations" of 28:19.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 16:16I will send for many fishermen — God's gathering of Israel. Jesus's "fish for people" call re-uses the prophetic metaphor.
  • Matt 28:19The geographic spread of v.25 anticipates the Great Commission: the movement from Galilee to all nations begins here.

Ancient Sources

The structure of the temptations
Three tests, three Deuteronomy answers. The pattern is deliberate: the Torah is the sufficient defence against the tempter. Jesus wields Scripture as the instrument of victory — not miraculous power.
The Deuteronomy-exam structure is the key to understanding the temptation narrative.
Capernaum
A fishing village on the northwest shore of the Sea of Galilee, c.3km from the Jordan inlet. Jesus's base of operations throughout the Galilean ministry.
Archaeologically well-attested; excavations have uncovered a synagogue and a house identified with Peter.

Reception History

The temptation in Christian practice
The three temptations have been analysed as the pattern of all human temptation since Origen — physical appetite, presumption, and the lust for power. Augustine, Luther, and subsequent theologians have used Matthew 4 as the anatomy of sin.
The call narrative
The four disciples' immediate response has been the model of radical discipleship from the first century monastic movement through Francis of Assisi to contemporary mission.

Textual Notes

The devil's hermeneutic is correct quotation with wrong application — taking a promise of providential protection and turning it into a test of divine reliability. Scripture's promises govern the life of trust, not the manufacture of signs on demand.

Scholarly Debates

The Temptation: Real or Staged?
Genuinely real — Jesus could have yielded
Orthodox Christology
If not genuinely possible to accept, it was not a real temptation. The fully human Jesus was genuinely tempted; the fully divine Son was incapable of sinning. The tension is the Christological mystery (Heb 4:15).
Representative trial — Jesus as Israel's covenant head
Some biblical theologians
Jesus as the true Israel faces and defeats the tests Israel failed in the wilderness. The typological significance gives the event its redemptive-historical meaning.
Both readings are complementary. The temptation was genuinely directed at Jesus as a human being; its typological significance (true Israel succeeding where historical Israel failed) gives it redemptive-historical meaning.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 4 establishes Jesus' authority over the devil, his identification with the covenant tradition, and his mission's surprising geography. The governing insight: Jesus conquers the devil not by divine power displays but by covenant faithfulness to the word — the same weapon available to every disciple.
