Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector (telōnēs) at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised by his community. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles. The tradition identifying the apostle Matthew as the gospel's author is consistent from the 2nd century (Papias, Irenaeus, Origen) and has no early counter-tradition. Matthew's tax-collector background explains the gospel's distinctive precision with numbers, lists, and systematically organized material.

When written

Most scholars date Matthew between AD 50–90, with significant debate. An early date (before AD 70) is supported by the gospel's apparent familiarity with pre-destruction Jerusalem. Matthew drew on Mark (the earliest gospel), a collection of Jesus' sayings known as Q, and material unique to Matthew (M source). The gospel was likely written in Syrian Antioch or Judea for a primarily Jewish-Christian community.

What prompted it

Matthew writes for Jewish believers — people who knew their Torah, their prophets, and their Psalms — and demonstrates systematically that Jesus is the long-awaited Messiah of Israel. The governing question is: how does Jesus fulfil what Moses, David, and the prophets promised? The answer unfolds through five great discourse blocks (chs.5–7, 10, 13, 18, 23–25), mirroring the five books of Moses, and through Matthew's characteristic formula: "this took place to fulfil what was spoken by the prophet." Jesus is presented as the new Moses, the Son of David, and the Son of God — the convergence of every strand of Israel's hope.

Greek Word Study

μετεμορφώθη metemorphōthē — he was transfigured/transformed: From metamorphoō — to change form, to transform. The same root as metamorphosis. Jesus's inner glory becomes visible: face like the sun, clothes like light. This is not Jesus becoming something he was not; it is the hidden reality briefly disclosed. The disciples see Jesus as he will be in the resurrection — and as he eternally is.

ἀκούετε αὐτοῦ akouete autou — listen to him: V.5: the voice from the cloud at the Transfiguration adds one word to the baptismal formula (Matt 3:17): "Listen to him!" The instruction points directly forward to the passion prediction the disciples have just received (16:21) and refused to accept. The Father commands obedience to the Son's teaching about the cross.

Context

The Transfiguration (vv.1–8) is the visual confirmation of Peter's confession. Six days after "you are the Son of the living God," the disciples see the glory that belongs to that sonship. Moses (Law) and Elijah (Prophets) appear — the entire OT finds its fulfilment in Jesus. The voice from the cloud adds the crucial word: "Listen to him" — specifically, to what he has just said about the cross. The chapter then descends to failed discipleship (the disciples who could not heal), a second passion prediction (vv.22–23), and the temple tax episode.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 34:29–35Moses's face shone after meeting God on Sinai. Jesus's face shines with its own light — not reflected glory but intrinsic glory.
  • Mal 4:5–6The expected return of Elijah (v.11) — fulfilled in John the Baptist. The Transfiguration confirms that both Law and Prophets find their fulfilment in Jesus.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
17:2

There he was transfigured before them. His face shone like the sun, and his clothes became as white as the light. The transfiguration is not Jesus becoming something he was not; it is the hidden reality briefly made visible. His face shone with its own light — not reflected glory as Moses's did (Exod 34:29–35) but intrinsic glory. The disciples see the eternal reality of the Son of God momentarily unveiled. This is what he always is; the incarnation normally conceals it.

17:3

Just then there appeared before them Moses and Elijah, talking with Jesus. Moses represents the Law; Elijah represents the Prophets. The entire OT — both its foundational legislation and its prophetic development — is present with Jesus. The Transfiguration is a convergence point: all that Scripture pointed toward is now present in the one they are speaking with. When the voice speaks and they look up, "they saw no one except Jesus" (v.8) — he alone remains.

17:5

While he was still speaking, a bright cloud covered them, and a voice from the cloud said, "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased. Listen to him!" The Transfiguration voice repeats the baptismal formula (3:17) with one addition: "Listen to him!" (akouete autou). This addition is the chapter's most important instruction. The disciples have just received the passion prediction and watched Peter rebuked. The Father now commands direct obedience to the Son's teaching — including the teaching about the cross.

17:20

Because you have so little faith. Truly I tell you, if you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mountain, "Move from here to there," and it will move. The disciples failed because of oligopistia — not absence of faith but diminished faith. Jesus does not say they needed more faith in terms of quantity but faith of a different quality: small, genuine, directed. The mustard seed is not praised for its size but for its nature — it is genuinely a seed that will become a tree. Real faith, however small, is effective.

17:22–23

The Son of Man is going to be delivered into the hands of men. They will kill him, and on the third day he will be raised to life. The second passion prediction — briefer but more explicit than the first (16:21). The disciples are filled with grief, which means they heard the killing but could not process the raising. This is the pattern of the disciples in Matthew: they receive the information without being able to integrate it. Only after the resurrection does the entire prediction become comprehensible.

Historical Sources

Tabor or Hermon?
Traditional location of the Transfiguration is Mount Tabor; more recently scholars have suggested Mount Hermon (near Caesarea Philippi where ch.16 ends). Neither can be identified with certainty.
The mountain's exact location is historically uncertain.
Temple Tax
The two-drachma tax (v.24) was the annual half-shekel temple contribution required of all Jewish adult males (Exod 30:11–16). Jesus's coin-in-fish solution is playful and mysterious — paying the tax he theologically need not pay, to avoid causing offence.
The temple tax was a universal Jewish male obligation.

Reception History

2 Pet 1:16–18
Peter explicitly references the Transfiguration: "we were eyewitnesses of his majesty... we heard this voice that came from heaven when we were with him on the sacred mountain." The event was foundational to Peter's own theological confidence.
17:20 in mission history
The mustard-seed faith saying became a key text for missionary ventures that attempted the apparently impossible. William Carey's "Expect great things from God; attempt great things for God" is a direct application.
