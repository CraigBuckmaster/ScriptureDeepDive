Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9). One of the twelve apostles. The early church unanimously attributed this gospel to Matthew. His Jewish background gives the gospel its distinctive OT fulfilment texture; his tax-collector precision gives it its careful structure.

When written

Most scholars date Matthew c.AD 80–90, though some argue for a pre-70 date before the destruction of the Temple. The gospel shows awareness of the Jewish-Christian tension following AD 70, but its theology is rooted in the earliest Palestinian tradition.

What prompted it

Matthew writes for a Jewish-Christian community wrestling with its relationship to Judaism after the death and resurrection of Jesus. He presents Jesus as the fulfilment of the entire OT — the new Moses, the son of David, Immanuel. The gospel is structured around five great discourses (echoing the five books of Moses) and is the most cited gospel in the early church.

Greek Word Study

Ἠλί Ἠλί λεμὰ σαβαχθάνι Ēli Ēli lema sabachthani — My God, my God, why have you forsaken me: V.46: the only Aramaic sentence spoken by Jesus in Matthew, quoting the opening of Psalm 22. Matthew preserves the original language — the cry is untranslatable into something less visceral. Psalm 22 moves from forsakenness to vindication; Jesus' cry from the cross is both the depth of dereliction and the beginning of the Psalm that ends in triumph. The cross is the nadir of divine-human fellowship — and simultaneously the moment of atonement.

υἱὸς θεοῦ huios theou — Son of God: V.54: the centurion's confession — "Surely he was the Son of God!" The title that the high priest used as a blasphemy charge (26:63–65) is now confessed by a Roman soldier at the cross. Matthew's irony is complete: Israel's leadership condemns Jesus as a false Son of God; a Gentile executioner recognises him as the true one. The confession at the cross is the gospel's climactic Christological declaration.

Context

Matthew 27 is the crucifixion chapter. Every element is narrated with theological precision: Judas' remorse and the potter's field (fulfilling Zechariah/Jeremiah), Pilate's three-fold declaration of Jesus' innocence, the Barabbas exchange, the mockery, the darkness, the cry of dereliction, the tearing of the temple curtain, the resurrection of the holy people. The chapter's climax is the centurion's confession — the right answer to the question Matthew has been building toward since 1:1. The chapter ends with the sealed tomb: every precaution taken to prevent what will happen anyway.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:11

Are you the king of the Jews? Jesus answers "You have said so" — the same formula as 26:25 and 26:64. It is an affirmation that transfers responsibility to the questioner. Pilate knows the answer; the sign above the cross will confirm it (v.37). The irony is total: Jesus is crucified as the King of the Jews under a sign that is simultaneously a charge and the truth.

27:46

Eli, Eli, lema sabachthani. The cry of dereliction is the most theologically profound moment in the crucifixion. Jesus quotes Psalm 22:1 — the cry of the righteous sufferer who feels abandoned by God but continues to trust. The cry is real: the atonement requires genuine separation, the bearing of the full weight of human sin. But it is also the opening of a Psalm that ends in vindication: "for he has not despised or scorned the suffering of the afflicted one... but has listened to his cry for help."

27:51

At that moment the curtain of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom. The veil separated the Holy of Holies — the dwelling place of God — from the rest of the temple. Only the high priest entered once a year on Yom Kippur. Its tearing signals: the barrier between God and humanity has been removed. Access to God is now open to all — not just through the high priest annually, but permanently, through the one who has entered the Holy of Holies "once for all" (Heb 9:12).

27:54

Surely he was the Son of God. A Roman centurion, a Gentile soldier in the army of the occupying power, speaks the gospel's climactic confession. Matthew has been working toward this moment since the Magi's worship in chapter 2 and the disciples' confession in 16:16. The answer comes not from Israel's leadership but from a Gentile executioner. This is the fulfilment of 28:19 in advance: the nations are already beginning to recognise the Son.

27:66

They made the tomb as secure as they knew how. The religious authorities' precautions are Matthew's dark irony. They understand the resurrection prediction (v.63) better than the disciples do, and they act on it — by trying to prevent it. Their seal and guard will become the most attested resurrection evidence: the tomb is empty despite maximum security.

Ancient Sources

Roman crucifixion
Crucifixion was a Roman execution method reserved for non-citizens, slaves, and rebels. The victim was typically flogged first, then forced to carry the crossbar to the execution site. Death could take hours or days; suffocation was the primary cause as the diaphragm fatigued.
The physical details of crucifixion are historically documented.
The temple veil
The veil was said to be four inches thick and sixty feet high. The Mishnah describes it as requiring three hundred priests to manipulate. Its tearing "from top to bottom" emphasises divine action — the tear begins at the top, beyond human reach.
The direction of the tear is theologically significant.

Reception History

The crucifixion in art and literature
Matthew 27 is the most depicted chapter in the history of Western art. From Byzantine mosaics to Rembrandt, from Bach's St Matthew Passion to Mel Gibson's film — the crucifixion has generated more artistic response than any other event in human history.
27:25 and anti-Semitism
The cry "his blood be on us and on our children" has been misused for centuries to justify anti-Jewish persecution. The verse records a historical crowd's declaration; it does not establish collective Jewish guilt for all time. The Church's violent misuse of this verse represents one of the darkest chapters in Christian history.

The chapter is structured as seven movements from condemnation to sealed tomb. Each movement fulfils a scripture or sets up a fulfilment. The darkness, the torn veil, and the centurion's confession are the three theological climaxes.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 27 is the passion's fulfilment. Every element is Scripture fulfilled: Psalm 22 enacted, Isaiah 53 embodied, Zechariah's thirty pieces paid. The climax is the centurion's confession — the gospel's answer to every question Matthew has raised. The sealed tomb is the dark irony: maximum human precaution in the face of divine necessity.
