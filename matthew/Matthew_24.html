The Olivet Discourse: Signs; Abomination; Watchfulness

Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector at the customs post in Capernaum — called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9). One of the twelve apostles. The gospel bears his name by unanimous early tradition (Papias, c.AD 125). As a tax collector he was trained in record-keeping; as an apostle he was an eyewitness.

When written

Most scholars date Matthew c.AD 80–90, though some place it before AD 70. Written for a Jewish-Christian audience, probably in Antioch of Syria, with extensive use of Mark and a shared sayings source (Q). The five-discourse structure deliberately echoes the five books of Moses.

What prompted it

A Jewish-Christian community needed a comprehensive account of Jesus as the fulfilment of Israel's Scripture — simultaneously the Davidic Messiah, the new Moses, the Son of God, and the Servant of Isaiah. Matthew's gospel is the most Jewish of the four, saturated with OT quotation, and closes with the Great Commission sending the message to all nations.

Verses 1–51 — The Olivet Discourse: Signs; Abomination; Watchfulness
1Jesus left the temple and was walking away when his disciples came up to him to call his attention to its buildings.2"Do you see all these things?" he asked. or, 'Here he is, in the inner rooms,' do not believe it.27For as lightning that comes from the east is visible even in the west, so will be the coming of the Son of Man.28Wherever there is a carcass, there the vultures will gather.29"Immediately after the distress of those days the sun will be darkened, and the moon will not give its light; the stars will fall from the sky, and the heavenly bodies will be shaken."30"Then will appear the sign of the Son of Man in heaven. And then all the peoples of the earth will mourn when they see the Son of Man coming on the clouds of heaven, with power and great glory.31And he will send his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of the heavens to the other.32"Now learn this lesson from the fig tree: As soon as its twigs get tender and its leaves come out, you know that summer is near.33Even so, when you see all these things, you know that it is near, right at the door.34Truly I tell you, this generation will certainly not pass away until all these things have happened.35Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will never pass away.36"But about that day or hour no one knows, not even the angels in heaven, nor the Son, but only the Father.37As it was in the days of Noah, so it will be at the coming of the Son of Man.38For in the days before the flood, people were eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, up to the day Noah entered the ark;39and they knew nothing about what would happen until the flood came and took them all away. That is how it will be at the coming of the Son of Man.40Two men will be in the field; one will be taken and the other left.41Two women will be grinding with a hand mill; one will be taken and the other left.42"Therefore keep watch, because you do not know on what day your Lord will come.43But understand this: If the owner of the house had known at what time of night the thief was coming, he would have kept watch and would not have let his house be broken into.44So you also must be ready, because the Son of Man will come at an hour when you do not expect him.45"Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom the master has put in charge of the servants in his household to give them their food at the time?46It will be good for that servant whose master finds him doing so when he returns.47Truly I tell you, he will put him in charge of all his possessions.48But suppose that servant is wicked and says to himself, 'My master is staying away a long time,'49and he then begins to beat his fellow servants and to eat and drink with drunkards.50The master of that servant will come on a day when he does not expect him and at an hour he is not aware of.51He will cut him to pieces and assign him a place with the hypocrites, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.

Greek Word Study

παρουσία parousia — coming / presence / arrival: V.3: the disciples ask about the parousia — a technical term for the official arrival of a king or dignitary, whose coming transformed the situation of those waiting. The word appears four times in Matthew 24. The disciples conflate two questions: the fall of Jerusalem and the end of the age. Jesus addresses both, creating the interpretive challenge of the discourse.

γρηγορεῖτε grēgoreite — keep watch / stay awake: V.42: the command that closes the Olivet Discourse and opens ch.25. Grēgorein — to be awake, vigilant, alert. Jesus does not say "work harder" or "calculate the date" but "be awake." The posture required for the unknown time of the Son of Man's coming is sustained, faithful presence.

Context

The Olivet Discourse (chs.24–25) is Matthew's fifth and final great discourse — given on the Mount of Olives, hence the name. The disciples ask two questions (v.3): when will the temple fall, and what is the sign of the coming and end of the age? Matthew 24 addresses both, interweaving the near (Jerusalem's fall, AD 70) and the far (the Son of Man's return). The interpretive challenge is distinguishing the two horizons. The practical conclusion is the same regardless of interpretation: watchful faithfulness, not speculative calculation.

Cross-Reference

  • Dan 9:27; 11:31; 12:11The abomination that causes desolation (v.15) — Daniel's phrase, originally referring to Antiochus IV's desecration of the temple (167 BC), now applied to the coming Roman desecration (AD 70) and/or a final eschatological event.
  • Dan 7:13–14The Son of Man coming on the clouds (v.30) — the Danielic vision. Matthew has been citing this throughout; here it describes the final parousia.
  • Gen 7; NoahAs in the days of Noah (vv.37–39) — ordinary life continued until sudden catastrophe. The warning is not about moral decline but about surprise.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
24:2

Not one stone will be left on another. This was fulfilled precisely in AD 70 when Titus destroyed Jerusalem. Roman soldiers, seeking the gold that had melted into the stones during the temple fire, pried apart every stone. Josephus describes the destruction in harrowing detail. Jesus's prediction was specific and verifiable.

24:14

This gospel of the kingdom will be preached in the whole world, and then the end will come. The Great Commission (28:18–20) and this verse are connected: the worldwide proclamation is both the church's calling and the precondition for the end. The church lives between the resurrection and the parousia in the time of worldwide mission.

24:36

No one knows about that day or hour — not even the angels, nor the Son, but only the Father. The most important single verse for eschatological humility. Every date-setter has ignored this verse. The Son of Man's return is characterized by unknowable timing by divine design — so that every generation lives in alertness, not calculation.

24:42–44

Keep watch... be ready. The practical application of all the eschatological teaching. Jesus does not call his disciples to speculate about signs but to maintain faithful stewardship. The thief illustration makes the point: you cannot protect your house by knowing when the thief comes; you protect it by always being ready. The same logic applies to the Son of Man's return.

24:13

The one who stands firm to the end will be saved. Endurance (hypomenō — to remain under pressure) is the quality the discourse commends. Not heroic action, not prophetic accuracy, but sustained faithfulness through persecution, false teaching, and the cooling of love around them. The passage is the pastoral heart of the eschatological section.

Ancient Sources

The Fall of Jerusalem, AD 70
Josephus's Jewish War provides a detailed account of the Roman siege and destruction. The abomination, the flight, the unequalled distress — all have specific historical referents in the events of AD 66–70.
Josephus is the primary historical source for the fulfilment of vv.15–21.
The Olivet Discourse in Scholarship
A major interpretive divide: preterist (entirely fulfilled in AD 70), futurist (entirely future), and two-horizons (the discourse addresses both). Most contemporary scholars favour the two-horizons approach.
The interpretation affects eschatology but not the practical conclusion (watchfulness).

Reception History

Eschatological speculation history
Every generation since the first century has produced date-setters who ignored 24:36. Augustine, Luther, and Spurgeon all warned against date-setting; all three pointed to 24:36 as the definitive answer.
24:14 and the missionary movement
William Carey, Hudson Taylor, and the modern missionary movement cited 24:14 as the theological basis for global mission: the church's task is worldwide proclamation, and that task has eschatological significance.

Matthew 24 is the eschatological centrepiece of the gospel. Its practical conclusion is unwavering: keep watch, stand firm, do the master's work. The unknown hour is a design feature — it keeps every generation in the posture of alert faithfulness.
