Ten Virgins; Talents; Sheep and Goats

Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector (telōnēs) at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised by his community. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9). One of the twelve apostles. Early church tradition unanimously attributes this gospel to Matthew. His gospel is the most Jewish of the four — saturated with OT fulfilment quotations, structured around five great discourses echoing the five books of Moses.

When written

c.AD 55–80. The internal evidence (no mention of the temple's destruction as a past event) suggests composition before AD 70, though many scholars date it after. Written for a Jewish-Christian community, probably in Antioch of Syria.

What prompted it

Matthew's community needed a structured account of Jesus's life and teaching that demonstrated his identity as the promised Messiah and presented his teaching as the fulfilment — not abolition — of the Torah. The gospel's five discourses, its ten fulfilment quotations, and its genealogy opening all serve this single argument: Jesus is the Christ, the Son of the living God, in whom all of Scripture finds its telos.

Greek Word Study

εὖ, δοῦλε ἀγαθὲ καὶ πιστέ eu, doule agathe kai piste — well done, good and faithful servant: V.21, 23: the most longed-for words in the gospel. Doule (servant/slave) — the one who has been entrusted with another's goods and manages them faithfully. Pistos (faithful) — the same word used throughout the NT for believers. The commendation is not for spectacular achievement but for faithful stewardship of what was given.

ἐλάχιστος elachistos — least / smallest: V.40, 45: the least of these my brothers and sisters. The superlative of mikros — the absolutely smallest. Jesus identifies himself with the most socially invisible and economically worthless people. The criterion of judgment in the sheep and goats is not confession but invisible acts of mercy toward the invisible.

Context

Matthew 25 completes the Olivet Discourse with three parables about the interval between Jesus's departure and his return. All three address the danger of waiting badly: the ten virgins (readiness vs unreadiness), the talents (faithful vs fearful stewardship), the sheep and goats (visible vs invisible service). Together they answer the question of how to live while waiting for the unknown day. The sheep and goats parable is the climax — not of the Olivet Discourse only but of Matthew's entire ethical teaching.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 7:21–23Not everyone who says "Lord, Lord" — the ten virgins who say "Lord, Lord, open the door" (v.11) echo the warning of 7:23: performance without relationship. The door is shut by the same response: "I don't know you."
  • Prov 19:17Whoever is kind to the poor lends to the LORD. The sheep and goats parable is the NT fulfilment of Proverbs' most radical claim about giving to the poor.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
25:12

I don't know you. The echo of 7:23 is unmistakable. The foolish virgins are not condemned for lack of oil alone but for the condition oil represents: genuine relationship with the bridegroom. You cannot borrow someone else's readiness. The "you" is singular and personal.

25:21

Well done, good and faithful servant. The most important words in the parable of the talents. The criterion is faithfulness, not profitability. The servant with two bags who doubles his two (v.22) receives exactly the same commendation as the servant with five who doubles his five. The scale of the gift is irrelevant; the faithfulness is everything.

25:25

I was afraid and went out and hid your gold. The one-talent servant's problem is not laziness alone but a distorted theology: he sees the master as a hard man harvesting where he did not sow. His fear produces paralysis. A wrong understanding of God — severe, demanding, withholding — produces the very failure it fears.

25:40

Whatever you did for one of the least of these brothers and sisters of mine, you did for me. The most radical statement of divine identification in the NT. Jesus is not merely sympathetic to the hungry, the thirsty, the stranger, the naked, the sick, the imprisoned — he is present in them. Every act of mercy toward the invisible is an act toward the King himself, whether the actor knew it or not.

25:46

Then they will go away to eternal punishment, but the righteous to eternal life. The word for "eternal" (aiōnios) applies equally to punishment and life — the symmetry is deliberate. Matthew ends the Olivet Discourse with the starkest possible eschatological statement: the judgment is real, the distinction is real, the two destinations are real.

Ancient Sources

The talents' value
A talent was approximately 20 years' wages for a labourer. The five-talent servant was entrusted with roughly a century's wages. The scale of the gift underlines the gravity of burying it.
The parable describes enormous trust placed in servants.
Sheep and goats in ancient Palestine
Sheep and goats were mixed in the same flock during the day and separated at night — goats needing shelter, sheep tolerating cold. The image was instantly recognisable to a pastoral audience.
The agricultural detail gives the parable its immediacy.

Reception History

25:40 and Christian social ethics
Matthew 25:40 is the single most quoted Gospel verse in Christian social ethics from the patristic period onwards. John Chrysostom, Francis of Assisi, Dorothy Day, Mother Teresa — each grounded their work in the identification of Christ with the poor.
The talents and stewardship theology
The parable generated the English word "talent" for natural ability — originally a monetary unit. The theological point (faithful use of what God entrusts) became the foundation of Christian vocation and stewardship theology.

Literary Structure

Ten virgins — vv.1–13Wise/foolish; delay; midnight cry; shut door
Talents — vv.14–30Five/two/one; faithful doubling; fearful burying; well done
Sheep and goats — vv.31–46All nations; right and left; least of these; eternal life/punishment
Three parables on waiting faithfully. The movement is from readiness (virgins) to activity (talents) to compassion (sheep/goats). Together they define the faithful life during the eschatological interval.
