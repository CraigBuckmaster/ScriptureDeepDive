Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector (telōnēs) at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised by his community. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles. The tradition identifying the apostle Matthew as the gospel's author is consistent from the 2nd century (Papias, Irenaeus, Origen) and has no early counter-tradition. Matthew's tax-collector background explains the gospel's distinctive precision with numbers, lists, and systematically organized material.

When written

Most scholars date Matthew between AD 50–90, with significant debate. An early date (before AD 70) is supported by the gospel's apparent familiarity with pre-destruction Jerusalem. Matthew drew on Mark (the earliest gospel), a collection of Jesus' sayings known as Q, and material unique to Matthew (M source). The gospel was likely written in Syrian Antioch or Judea for a primarily Jewish-Christian community.

What prompted it

Matthew writes for Jewish believers — people who knew their Torah, their prophets, and their Psalms — and demonstrates systematically that Jesus is the long-awaited Messiah of Israel. The governing question is: how does Jesus fulfil what Moses, David, and the prophets promised? The answer unfolds through five great discourse blocks (chs.5–7, 10, 13, 18, 23–25), mirroring the five books of Moses, and through Matthew's characteristic formula: "this took place to fulfil what was spoken by the prophet." Jesus is presented as the new Moses, the Son of David, and the Son of God — the convergence of every strand of Israel's hope.

Greek Word Study

λύτρον ἀντὶ πολλῶν lytron anti pollōn — a ransom for many: V.28: lytron — the price paid to free a slave or prisoner. Anti — in the place of, instead of. The ransom is substitutionary: Jesus gives his life as the price that sets many free. This is the most explicit atonement statement in Matthew — the theological purpose of the passion that the preceding chapters have been anticipating.

διάκονος / δοῦλος diakonos / doulos — servant / slave: Vv.26–27: two escalating words — diakonos (servant, one who waits on others) and doulos (slave, one who has no rights). Greatness in the kingdom is measured by descent — moving toward zero social claims. The model is Jesus himself (v.28): the Son of Man who could command service gives service instead.

Context

Chapter 20 brings three contrasting episodes around a single theme: the economy of the kingdom is grace, not merit, and greatness is service, not status. The workers-in-the-vineyard parable (vv.1–16) illustrates divine generosity that transcends strict merit. The third passion prediction (vv.17–19) is the most detailed yet — now including crucifixion explicitly. James and John's request for status (vv.20–28) occasions the most important leadership statement in Matthew: the Son of Man came to serve and give his life as a ransom. The chapter ends with the healing of two blind men near Jericho — they cry "Son of David," follow Jesus, and the city of palms is behind them. Jerusalem is ahead.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 53:10–12The ransom saying (v.28) is saturated with the Suffering Servant language: "the LORD makes his life an offering for sin... he will bear their iniquities... he bore the sin of many." Matthew has been quoting Isaiah throughout; here the connection is thematic rather than verbal.
  • Mark 10:45The parallel verse — "the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many." Matthew and Mark share this as the theological heart of Jesus's self-understanding of his mission.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
20:1–7

The kingdom of heaven is like a landowner who went out early in the morning to hire workers... He went out again about nine in the morning... about noon and about three in the afternoon... about five in the afternoon. The five hiring times establish the parable's central reality: the landowner keeps going out, keeps hiring, right up to the last hour. This is not efficient workforce management — it is a picture of persistent, inclusive divine invitation. No one is excluded because they were not hired early enough.

20:13–15

I am not being unfair to you, friend. Didn't you agree to work for a denarius?... Don't I have the right to do what I want with my own money? Or are you envious because I am generous? The landowner's response has three movements: he kept his agreement (justice); he exercised his freedom to be generous (grace); the all-day workers' complaint is envy, not injustice. The parable demolishes merit-based theology: the kingdom operates by grace, not wages. Those who have served longest are not disadvantaged — they receive exactly what was agreed. Their complaint is envy of grace.

20:22

Can you drink the cup I am going to drink? The cup is the OT metaphor for God's wrath and suffering (Ps 75:8; Isa 51:17; Jer 25:15). Jesus knows what his cup contains. James and John say "we can" — and they will be right, eventually: both are persecuted; James is the first apostolic martyr (Acts 12:2). But their current request reveals that they want the thrones without the cup. Jesus says the two are inseparable.

20:25–28

You know that the rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them... Not so with you. Instead, whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be your slave — just as the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many. The most important leadership passage in Matthew. The Gentile model of leadership is power exercised downward; the kingdom model is power expressed in service. The model is Jesus himself: the ransom saying is the explanation of everything he has taught about servant leadership.

20:34

Jesus had compassion on them and touched their eyes. Immediately they received their sight and followed him. The final miracle before the triumphal entry. Splagchnizomai — the same gut-level compassion as 9:36. Jesus stops on his way to Jerusalem — on his way to the cross — and touches two blind men beside the road. Their healing is the last act of the journey narrative: they received sight and followed him. Seeing clearly and following Jesus are presented as identical acts.

Historical Sources

Denarius as a Day's Wage
A denarius was approximately one day's wage for a labourer in first-century Palestine — a subsistence wage, not generous. The point is not the amount but the equality: everyone receives the same regardless of how long they worked.
The denarius establishes the economy-of-grace logic.
Ransom in Hellenistic World
Lytron was used in the Hellenistic world for the price paid to free slaves or prisoners of war. It was a commercial term repurposed for a theological reality: the liberation of people held in bondage to sin and death.
The commercial term is repurposed for an unprecedented theological claim.

Reception History

Servant Leadership
Matthew 20:25–28 became the founding text for the entire Christian tradition of servant leadership. Robert Greenleaf's influential Servant Leadership (1977) grounds his theory in exactly these verses, though he did not acknowledge the source.
20:28 and Atonement theology
The ransom saying is one of the three foundational NT atonement texts (with Isa 53 and Heb 9:15). It generated the "ransom theory" of atonement (Origen, Gregory of Nyssa) — later critiqued but never replaced as the most literal reading of Jesus's own self-description.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 20 gives the theological answer to every question the gospel has raised about the cross: the Son of Man came to give his life as a ransom for many. This is why he has been moving toward Jerusalem since ch.16. The workers-in-the-vineyard parable and the servant-of-all teaching frame the same truth from two directions: the kingdom operates by grace and service, not merit and status.
