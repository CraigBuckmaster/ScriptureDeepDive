Matthew 2

The Magi; Flight to Egypt; Return to Nazareth

Historical

Timeline
Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, a tax collector called by Jesus at his customs post in Capernaum (Matt 9:9). One of the twelve apostles; eyewitness. Unanimous early tradition (Papias, c.AD 125) attributes this gospel to him.

When written

c.AD 80–90, though some date it before AD 70. Written for a Jewish-Christian audience, probably in Antioch of Syria. The five-discourse structure deliberately echoes the five books of Moses.

What prompted it

A Jewish-Christian community needed a comprehensive account of Jesus as fulfilment of Israel's Scripture — simultaneously the Davidic Messiah, the new Moses, the Son of God, and the Servant of Isaiah. Matthew is the most Jewish of the four gospels, saturated with OT quotation, and closes with the Great Commission sending the message to all nations.

Verses 1–12 — The Magi: Star, Worship, and Herod's Plot
1After Jesus was born in Bethlehem in Judea, during the time of King Herod, Magi from the east came to Jerusalem2and asked, "Where is the one who has been born king of the Jews? We saw his star when it rose and have come to worship him."3When King Herod heard this he was disturbed, and all Jerusalem with him.4When he had called together all the people's chief priests and teachers of the law, he asked them where the Messiah was to be born.5"In Bethlehem in Judea," they replied, "for this is what the prophet has written:6"'But you, Bethlehem, in the land of Judah, are by no means least among the rulers of Judah; for out of you will come a ruler who will shepherd my people Israel.'"7Then Herod called the Magi secretly and found out from them the exact time the star had appeared.8He sent them to Bethlehem and said, "Go and search carefully for the child. As soon as you find him, report to me, so that I too may go and worship him."9After they had heard the king, they went on their way, and the star they had seen when it rose went ahead of them until it stopped over the place where the child was.10When they saw the star, they were overjoyed.11On coming to the house, they saw the child with his mother Mary, and they bowed down and worshiped him. Then they opened their treasures and presented him with gifts of gold, frankincense and myrrh.12And having been warned in a dream not to go back to Herod, they returned to their country by another route.

Greek Word Study

μάγοι magoi — Magi / wise men: Not kings — that tradition comes from later Christian interpretation of Ps 72:10–11. The magoi were likely Zoroastrian astrologers or court scholars from Babylon or Persia, trained in reading celestial signs. Their Gentile identity is theologically significant: the nations are drawn to Israel's Messiah from the very beginning. Matthew's gospel ends with "make disciples of all nations" — and begins with Gentiles worshipping.

προσκυνέω proskyneō — worship / bow down: V.11: the Magi "bowed down and worshipped." This is the first act of proskyneō toward Jesus in Matthew — performed by Gentiles. The word will appear again at the temptation (Satan demands it, 4:9), at the disciples' response to Jesus walking on water (14:33), and at the resurrection (28:9,17). The arc of worship in Matthew begins here.

Context

Matthew 2 is built around four OT fulfilment quotations — more than any other chapter. The Magi scene (vv.1–12) establishes the central irony of the chapter: Gentile outsiders from the east recognise and worship the King of the Jews, while the official Jewish establishment in Jerusalem is "disturbed" (v.3). Herod knows where the Messiah is to be born (the scholars can tell him precisely) but uses that knowledge to plot murder rather than worship.

Cross-Reference

  • Mic 5:2Bethlehem as the birthplace of the ruler who will shepherd Israel — quoted v.6. Matthew adds a phrase from 2 Sam 5:2 ("who will shepherd my people Israel"), combining both texts.
  • Num 24:17A star shall come out of Jacob — Balaam's oracle. The Magi's star may be the fulfilment of this prophecy, connecting Israel's Gentile opponents in Numbers to the Gentile worshippers in Matthew.
  • Isa 60:6Gold and frankincense from the nations — Isaiah's vision of Gentiles bringing tribute to Zion. Matthew's Magi enact this vision.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:2

King of the Jews. The title the Magi use for Jesus appears again in 27:37 — the charge nailed above his cross. Matthew brackets the birth narrative and the passion with the same title. The Magi who come to worship the newborn King anticipate the centurion who confesses the crucified King.

2:4–6

Herod calls the scholars; they answer accurately from Micah 5:2. The scholars know where the Messiah is to be born — but they don't go. Knowledge without response is the chapter's diagnosis of the Jerusalem establishment. The Magi had far less information and traveled hundreds of miles; the scholars had the text and didn't cross the street.

2:11

Gold, frankincense, and myrrh. Patristic interpretation: gold for a king, frankincense for a priest (offered to deity), myrrh for burial (anticipating death). Whether or not Matthew intended all three meanings, the gifts signal that this child is simultaneously royal, divine, and destined to die.

Verses 13–18 — Flight to Egypt; The Massacre of the Innocents
13When they had gone, an angel of the Lord appeared to Joseph in a dream. "Get up," he said, "take the child and his mother and escape to Egypt. Stay there until I tell you, for Herod is going to search for the child to kill him."14So he got up, took the child and his mother during the night and left for Egypt,15where he stayed until the death of Herod. And so was fulfilled what the Lord had said through the prophet: "Out of Egypt I called my son."16When Herod realized that he had been outwitted by the Magi, he was furious, and he gave orders to kill all the boys in Bethlehem and its vicinity who were two years old and under, in accordance with the time he had learned from the Magi.17Then what was said through the prophet Jeremiah was fulfilled:18"A voice is heard in Ramah, weeping and great mourning, Rachel weeping for her children and refusing to be comforted, because they are no more."

Greek Word Study

ἐξ Αἰγύπτου ἐκάλεσα τὸν υἱόν μου ex Aigyptou ekalesa ton huion mou — Out of Egypt I called my son: V.15: Hos 11:1 — originally referring to Israel's exodus. Matthew applies it to Jesus's return from Egypt, reading the OT typologically: Jesus recapitulates Israel's history as the true Israel. He goes down to Egypt as an infant; he comes up out of Egypt; he passes through water (baptism); he is tested 40 days in the wilderness. Where Israel failed, Jesus succeeds.

Context

The flight to Egypt (vv.13–15) and the massacre of the innocents (vv.16–18) are Matthew's two darkest scenes — and both serve typological purposes. The flight recapitulates the Exodus (Jesus = Israel / Moses). The massacre echoes Pharaoh's killing of Hebrew infants (Exod 1:16) and the Exile's grief (Jer 31:15). Matthew is reading the birth of Jesus as the convergence point of Israel's entire history.

Cross-Reference

  • Hos 11:1Out of Egypt I called my son — originally Israel at the Exodus. Jesus recapitulates Israel's story as the true, faithful Israel.
  • Jer 31:15Rachel weeping for her children — Jeremiah's lament over the Exile. Matthew applies it to the Bethlehem massacre, reading the massacre as a new Exile-level catastrophe.
  • Exod 1:15–22Pharaoh's massacre of Hebrew infant boys. Herod mirrors Pharaoh; Jesus mirrors Moses — the one who escapes to bring liberation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:13

An angel of the Lord appeared to Joseph in a dream. The fourth dream in Matthew 1–2 (1:20; 2:12,13,19). Dreams are the mode of divine communication throughout the infancy narrative — connecting to the OT patriarchs (Gen 37–41) and to the Joseph who first went down to Egypt. Matthew's Joseph is a second Joseph.

2:16

Herod ordered the massacre of all boys two years old and under. The exact age range tells us the star appeared about two years before the massacre — the Magi arrived not at the manger but at a house (v.11), some time after the birth. The "holy family" had settled in Bethlehem before the flight.

2:18

Rachel weeping. The mourning of Bethlehem's mothers is not explained away by Matthew's typology — it is given full weight in Jeremiah's language of utter inconsolable grief. The Messiah's birth causes real suffering for real families. Matthew does not resolve this tension.

Verses 19–23 — Return: Out of Egypt; Settled in Nazareth
19After Herod died, an angel of the Lord appeared in a dream to Joseph in Egypt20and said, "Get up, take the child and his mother and go to the land of Israel, for those who were trying to take the child's life are dead."21So he got up, took the child and his mother and went to the land of Israel.22But when he heard that Archelaus was reigning in Judea in place of his father Herod, he was afraid to go there. Having been warned in a dream, he withdrew to the district of Galilee,23and he went and lived in a town called Nazareth. So was fulfilled what was said through the prophets, that he would be called a Nazarene.

Context

The return from Egypt (vv.19–23) completes the Exodus typology: as Israel came up out of Egypt into the promised land, so Jesus comes up from Egypt into Israel. But the destination is Galilee, not Judea — a detail Matthew uses to fulfil "he will be called a Nazarene." The move to Galilee also sets up 4:12–17, where Jesus's Galilean ministry fulfils Isaiah's "Galilee of the Gentiles."

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 4:19Go back to Egypt, for all those who were trying to take your life are dead — God's words to Moses almost verbatim. The angel's words to Joseph in v.20 echo this precisely, underlining the Moses typology.
  • Isa 11:1A shoot from the stump of Jesse / a Branch (nēṣer) from his roots. The wordplay on Nazareth/Nazarene may connect to this — the "Branch" prophecy pointing to the Davidic king from Jesse's line.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:23

He would be called a Nazarene. Matthew says "through the prophets" (plural) — this is not a direct quote of any single OT text. It may be a composite reference to the Branch (nēṣer) prophecies (Isa 11:1; Jer 23:5) combined with the Nazirite theme (Judg 13:5, 7 — "set apart for God from birth"). Or it may reflect an oral tradition about Nazareth that did not survive in written form. Matthew is citing a pattern, not a single proof-text.

Ancient Sources

Herod the Great
Roman client-king of Judea, known for grandiose building projects and violent paranoia. He executed several of his own sons. The massacre of Bethlehem's infant boys fits his documented character exactly.
Josephus confirms Herod's murderous paranoia though doesn't record this specific event.
The Magi's star
Various proposals: conjunction of Jupiter and Saturn (7 BC), a nova, Halley's Comet (12 BC), or a supernatural phenomenon. Matthew does not identify it astronomically — its function in the narrative is to lead and to stop.
The star's movement (going ahead of them, stopping) is not astronomical behaviour.

Reception History

Epiphany
The Magi's visit has been celebrated as Epiphany (6 January) since at least the 4th century — the manifestation of Christ to the Gentiles.
The Innocents
The massacre of the Holy Innocents (28 December in the Western calendar) is among the oldest commemorations in the Christian calendar.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 2 is the most typologically dense chapter in the gospel. Every scene recapitulates OT patterns: new Israel (Hos 11:1), new Moses (Exod 1–2), Rachel's grief (Jer 31:15), Gentile worshippers (Isa 60:6). The chapter's central irony: Gentiles worship while Jerusalem is disturbed.
