Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector (telōnēs) at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised by his community. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles. The tradition identifying the apostle Matthew as the gospel's author is consistent from the 2nd century (Papias, Irenaeus, Origen) and has no early counter-tradition. Matthew's tax-collector background explains the gospel's distinctive precision with numbers, lists, and systematically organized material.

When written

Most scholars date Matthew between AD 50–90, with significant debate. An early date (before AD 70) is supported by the gospel's apparent familiarity with pre-destruction Jerusalem. Matthew drew on Mark (the earliest gospel), a collection of Jesus' sayings known as Q, and material unique to Matthew (M source). The gospel was likely written in Syrian Antioch or Judea for a primarily Jewish-Christian community.

What prompted it

Matthew writes for Jewish believers — people who knew their Torah, their prophets, and their Psalms — and demonstrates systematically that Jesus is the long-awaited Messiah of Israel. The governing question is: how does Jesus fulfil what Moses, David, and the prophets promised? The answer unfolds through five great discourse blocks (chs.5–7, 10, 13, 18, 23–25), mirroring the five books of Moses, and through Matthew's characteristic formula: "this took place to fulfil what was spoken by the prophet." Jesus is presented as the new Moses, the Son of David, and the Son of God — the convergence of every strand of Israel's hope.

Greek Word Study

στραφῆτε καὶ γένησθε ὡς τὰ παιδία straphēte kai genēsthe hōs ta paidia — change and become like little children: V.3: straphēte (to turn/be converted) — the same root as metanoia. Kingdom entry requires a turning that re-orients the entire axis of the personality: from greatness-seeking to child-likeness. The child in the ancient world had no status, no rights, no social leverage — total dependence. That is the model.

ἑβδομηκοντάκις ἑπτά hebdomēkontakis hepta — seventy-seven / seventy times seven: V.22: Peter offers seven times as generous forgiveness; Jesus says seventy-seven (or seventy times seven — the Greek is ambiguous). Both readings signify the same thing: unlimited. The number evokes Gen 4:24 where Lamech boasts of seventy-sevenfold vengeance. Jesus inverts Lamech's unlimited vengeance into unlimited forgiveness.

Context

Chapter 18 is Matthew's fourth great discourse — the Community Discourse. It addresses life in the kingdom community: greatness (vv.1–5), care for the vulnerable (vv.6–14), conflict resolution (vv.15–20), and forgiveness (vv.21–35). The centrepiece is the lost sheep parable (vv.12–14) and the unmerciful servant parable (vv.23–35). The discourse is the most ecclesial in Matthew — it contains the only two uses of the word "church" (ekklēsia) in the gospels.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 4:24Lamech's boast of seventy-sevenfold vengeance → Jesus's seventy-sevenfold forgiveness. The exact inversion: where Genesis's culture of violence escalates, the kingdom culture de-escalates.
  • Prov 28:13Whoever conceals their sins does not prosper; whoever confesses finds mercy — the OT basis for the conflict resolution process of vv.15–17.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
18:3

Unless you change and become like little children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven. The disciples asked "who is greatest?" — a question about the kingdom's social hierarchy. Jesus answers with a child — a social zero in the ancient world. The instruction is to straphēte — turn, be converted. This is the same root as metanoia. Kingdom entry requires a turning that completely reorients the axis of the personality from greatness-seeking to childlike dependence.

18:6

If anyone causes one of these little ones to stumble, it would be better for them to have a large millstone hung around their neck and to be drowned in the depths of the sea. The protection of vulnerable believers is stated in the strongest possible terms. The "little ones" (mikroi) are not primarily children but the vulnerable in faith — those who could be caused to stumble (skandalizo — to be led into sin or abandonment of faith). The millstone judgment exceeds any merely human punishment: it is worse than drowning.

18:12–14

What do you think? If a man owns a hundred sheep, and one of them wanders away... your Father in heaven is not willing that any of these little ones should perish. The lost sheep parable in Matthew is applied differently than in Luke (15:4–7 where it illustrates God seeking the lost sinner). Here it grounds the command to care for the wandering believer: the Father's will is that none of the little ones should perish. The community's obligation to the straying member is rooted in the Father's own disposition toward them.

18:20

For where two or three gather in my name, there am I with them. The Immanuel promise (1:23) applied to the community at prayer. The presence of Christ is not confined to spectacular events or individual mystical experience — it is promised specifically to the gathered community. Two or three in Jesus's name is the minimum definition of the church: not a building, not a programme, not a size, but his presence among his people.

18:35

This is how my heavenly Father will treat each of you unless you forgive your brother or sister from your heart. The parable's application is sobering. The refusal to forgive is not merely a failure of kindness — it exposes a fundamental failure to comprehend what one has been forgiven. The person who understands that God has cancelled an infinite debt will find the cancellation of finite debts not merely possible but natural. The retention of unforgiveness is evidence that the forgiveness received has not been truly grasped.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The little child
Status-zero model
Not a moral exemplar (innocence) but a social exemplar (no claims, no leverage). Greatness = willingness to be small.
The unmerciful servant
The negative model
Received infinite forgiveness; refused finite forgiveness. The disproportion exposes the moral incoherence.

Historical Sources

Ten Thousand Talents
A talent was approximately 20 years' wages for a labourer. Ten thousand talents = 200,000 years' wages — a deliberate absurdity to make the point about the incomprehensible scale of divine forgiveness.
The parable's debt is designed to be beyond all reckoning.
Ekklēsia in Matthew
The word "church" (ekklēsia) appears only twice in the gospels, both in Matthew (16:18; 18:17). In the LXX it translates qāhāl — the assembly of God's people. Matthew is already thinking ecclesially — the community Jesus is forming is the renewed Israel.
Two occurrences of "church" in the gospels, both in Matthew.

Reception History

18:20 and Christian gathering
Where two or three are gathered in my name — became the foundational text for understanding Christian corporate worship as the place of Christ's presence. Augustine, Luther, and Calvin all used it as the definition of the church.
18:15–17 and church discipline
The three-stage process became the standard for Protestant church discipline. Calvin's Geneva and the Anabaptist communities structured their discipline around exactly this text.
