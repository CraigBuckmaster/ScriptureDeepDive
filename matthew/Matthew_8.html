Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, c.AD 1–70. A tax collector (telōnēs) at the customs post in Capernaum — a Jew working for the Roman fiscal system, socially despised by his community. Called by Jesus while at work (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles. The tradition identifying the apostle Matthew as the gospel's author is consistent from the 2nd century (Papias, Irenaeus, Origen) and has no early counter-tradition. Matthew's tax-collector background explains the gospel's distinctive precision with numbers, lists, and systematically organized material.

When written

Most scholars date Matthew between AD 50–90, with significant debate. An early date (before AD 70) is supported by the gospel's apparent familiarity with pre-destruction Jerusalem. Matthew drew on Mark (the earliest gospel), a collection of Jesus' sayings known as Q, and material unique to Matthew (M source). The gospel was likely written in Syrian Antioch or Judea for a primarily Jewish-Christian community.

What prompted it

Matthew writes for Jewish believers — people who knew their Torah, their prophets, and their Psalms — and demonstrates systematically that Jesus is the long-awaited Messiah of Israel. The governing question is: how does Jesus fulfil what Moses, David, and the prophets promised? The answer unfolds through five great discourse blocks (chs.5–7, 10, 13, 18, 23–25), mirroring the five books of Moses, and through Matthew's characteristic formula: "this took place to fulfil what was spoken by the prophet." Jesus is presented as the new Moses, the Son of David, and the Son of God — the convergence of every strand of Israel's hope.

Greek Word Study

ἅψεσθαι haptesthai — to touch: Jesus touches the leper (v.3). Touching a person with skin disease made the toucher ritually unclean (Lev 13–14). Jesus reverses the flow: instead of becoming unclean by contact, his holiness cleanses. The touch is deliberate — the man could have been healed with a word (as the centurion's servant was). Jesus chose to touch the untouchable.

υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου huios tou anthrōpou — Son of Man: V.20: Jesus's favourite self-designation in Matthew, appearing 30 times. From Daniel 7:13–14 — the one who comes on the clouds to receive dominion over all nations. But here (v.20) the Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head — the heavenly figure is also a homeless wanderer. The paradox of the Incarnation compressed into one phrase.

Context

Chapters 8–9 form a unified miracle section — ten miracles in two chapters, demonstrating in deed what the Sermon proclaimed in word. Chapter 8 contains three healing miracles (leper, centurion's servant, Peter's mother-in-law), a summary, two discipleship sayings, the stilling of the storm, and the Gadarene demoniac. Each miracle enacts the kingdom's arrival: disease, death, nature, and demonic power all yield to Jesus's authority.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 53:4V.17: Matthew quotes the Suffering Servant song — Jesus's healings are the embodiment of Isaiah's vision of the Servant who bears Israel's infirmities. The miracles are not separate from the cross but anticipate it.
  • Dan 7:13–14The Son of Man (v.20) — the Danielic figure who receives eternal dominion. Matthew deploys this title at the moment of Jesus's homelessness to show the paradox: the cosmic sovereign is without a home.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:3

Jesus reached out his hand and touched the man. Touching a leper made the toucher ritually unclean (Lev 13–14). Jesus reverses the flow: instead of becoming unclean through contact, his holiness cleanses. He could have healed with a word alone (as he does for the centurion's servant). He chose to touch the untouchable — a deliberate act of inclusion that anticipates the entire ministry to the marginalised.

8:8–10

I do not deserve to have you come under my roof. But just say the word... When Jesus heard this, he was amazed. The centurion understands Jesus's authority by analogy from military command: authority operates by word alone, across distance. His understanding exceeds anything Jesus has encountered in Israel. This is the only time in Matthew that Jesus is described as amazed at a human being — and it is a Gentile. The first great faith in the gospel belongs to a Roman soldier.

8:17

He took up our infirmities and bore our diseases. Matthew quotes Isa 53:4 to interpret Jesus's healing ministry. The healings are not separate from the cross — they are the Suffering Servant's mission in action. Jesus does not merely cure symptoms; he takes the sickness upon himself. Every healing is a preview of the cross: the same substitutionary bearing of human affliction.

8:26

You of little faith, why are you so afraid? Oligopistoi — little-faithed ones. The disciples are not faithless: they called out to Jesus, which is faith. But they were afraid, which is little faith. The "little" refers to the inadequacy of their faith relative to the situation and relative to what they know about Jesus. The rebuke is not "you have no faith" but "your faith has not yet grown to match what you have seen." This is Matthew's characteristic diagnostic of the disciples.

8:29

What do you want with us, Son of God? Have you come here to torture us before the appointed time? The demoniacs recognise Jesus immediately as Son of God — the title Peter will confess at Caesarea Philippi (16:16). The demons know the eschatological timetable: there is an appointed time for their final judgment. Jesus' authority over them is not in question; they only negotiate the timing of their departure. The spiritual world recognises what Israel's leaders miss.

Historical Sources

Roman Centurion
A centurion commanded approximately 80 soldiers. Romans serving in Judea would have been deeply conscious of Jewish sensitivities around Gentile entry into Jewish homes. The centurion's "I do not deserve to have you come under my roof" (v.8) reflects this cultural awareness.
The centurion's cultural sensitivity adds depth to his faith.
Gadarenes / Gerasenes
The region of the Gadarenes (v.28) corresponds to the eastern shore of the Sea of Galilee — Gentile territory. Pigs (v.30) confirm this: no Jewish community would maintain a herd of pigs.
Jesus deliberately crosses into Gentile territory in ch.8.

Reception History

8:8 in the Mass
The centurion's words — "Lord, I am not worthy to have you come under my roof, but only say the word and my servant will be healed" — are prayed by Roman Catholics before communion in every Mass, adapted verbatim from v.8.
The stilling of the storm
The disciples' question "What kind of man is this?" (v.27) became one of the central Christological questions of the early church, explored in every major council.
