Mark 15

The Crucifixion: Trial, Cross, Death, Burial

Author

John Mark, son of Mary of Jerusalem (Acts 12:12), cousin of Barnabas (Col 4:10), and close associate of both Paul and Peter. Early and unanimous tradition identifies him as “the interpreter of Peter”: Papias of Hierapolis (c. AD 125, preserved in Eusebius, HE 3.39.15) records that Mark “wrote down accurately, though not in order, as much as he remembered of the things said or done by the Lord” — drawing entirely on Peter’s teaching. Irenaeus (Against Heresies 3.1.1), Clement of Alexandria (Hypotyposeis, in Eusebius HE 6.14.6), and the Anti-Marcionite Prologue all confirm the Petrine connection. Mark himself is mentioned in Acts 12:12, 25; 13:13; 15:37–39; Col 4:10; Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11; and 1 Pet 5:13 (“my son Mark”). He was present at some events he records (the young man fleeing naked, 14:51–52, is widely identified as a Markan self-cameo), and his vividness throughout reflects the eyewitness testimony he received directly from Peter in Rome.

When written

c. AD 55–65, almost certainly in Rome, most likely before the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 — which Mark 13 predicts but nowhere records as fulfilled. Clement of Alexandria states the Gospel was written “while Peter was still preaching publicly in Rome.” If so, the most probable window is the late 50s to mid-60s, during Paul’s Roman imprisonment (Acts 28) and Peter’s residence in the capital. Mark is the earliest of the four Gospels (the Markan Priority hypothesis, held by the majority of NT scholars), and both Matthew and Luke appear to have used it as a primary source. The Gospel’s Latin loanwords (legiōn, denarius, praetorium, centurio), its explanation of Jewish customs for non-Jewish readers (7:3–4), and its Latinisms of idiom all point to a Roman Gentile audience.

What prompted it

To preserve Peter’s eyewitness testimony in permanent written form for a church facing persecution under Nero (AD 64), and to answer the most urgent question that question raised: who is Jesus, and why did he die? Mark’s opening declaration — “The beginning of the good news about Jesus the Messiah, the Son of God” (1:1) — is simultaneously title, thesis, and answer. The Gospel is structured around the progressive revelation of that identity: demons recognise Jesus before disciples do (1:24); Peter confesses “You are the Messiah” at the midpoint (8:29); a Roman centurion declares “Surely this man was the Son of God” at the cross (15:39). The relentless pace — euthys (“immediately”) appears 41 times — reflects both Peter’s preaching style and the urgency of a community for whom the Passion narrative was not ancient history but recent, costly, and still unfolding.

Verses 1–20 — Before Pilate; Barabbas; The Mocking

What crime has he committed?” asked Pilate. But they shouted all the louder, “Crucify him!”15Wanting to satisfy the crowd, Pilate released Barabbas to them. He had Jesus flogged, and handed him over to be crucified.16The soldiers led Jesus away into the palace (that is, the Praetorium) and called together the whole company of soldiers.17They put a purple robe on him, then twisted together a crown of thorns and set it on him.18And they began to call out to him, “Hail, king of the Jews!”19Again and again they struck him on the head with a staff and spit on him. Falling on their knees, they paid homage to him.20And when they had mocked him, they took off the purple robe and put his own clothes on him. Then they led him out to crucify him.

Greek Word Study

phragelloō fra-GEL-oh-oh — to flog/scourge: He had Jesus flogged — ephragelōsen. Roman flogging (verberatio) used a flagellum — a whip with leather thongs embedded with bone or metal fragments. It was used as pre-crucifixion torture to weaken the condemned. Isaiah 53:5 (“by his wounds we are healed”) refers to exactly this punishment.

Context

Pilate’s trial of Jesus is a study in political cowardice that mirrors Herod’s in chapter 6: both men know the prisoner is innocent, both protect him briefly, both capitulate to external pressure. Barabbas is the paradigm of substitutionary atonement: the guilty party goes free while the innocent one is condemned. The soldiers’ mock coronation (purple robe, crown of thorns, “Hail, king!”) is an unwitting enactment of the truth.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 53:5The punishment that brought us peace was on him; by his wounds we are healed — the flogging of v.15 fulfils this text.
  • Ps 22:7All who see me mock me; they hurl insults, shaking their heads — the mockery of the Passion psalm begins here.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:2

Marcus: "Are you the king of the Jews?" — the charge is political: claiming royal status is sedition against Caesar. Marcus notes the bitter irony: Jesus will be executed under a charge that is simultaneously false (he never led an armed revolt) and true (he is the Davidic king). The INRI placard makes the irony permanent and public. Marcus argues that Pilate's triple affirmation of Jesus' innocence (15:10, 14, "Why? What crime has he committed?") functions as a Roman legal testimony to the injustice of the execution.

15:15

Marcus: The Barabbas exchange is the Passion narrative's clearest enacted substitution: the guilty goes free; the innocent is condemned. Marcus notes that the name Barabbas (bar Abba — son of the father) may carry deliberate irony: the "son of the father" who is released is replaced by the Son of the Father who is executed. Whether or not the irony was intended in the historical event, Mark's narrative deploys it — the name is given without explanation, as if its resonance would be immediately apparent.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:1-20

Rhoads: Pilate is characterized through a sequence of questions and reactions rather than through direct evaluation. He asks the right questions ("What crime has he committed?"); he perceives the real motive ("he knew it was out of self-interest"); he wants to release Jesus (15:9-11); and he capitulates to the crowd. Rhoads argues that Pilate is Mark's most fully developed portrait of moral failure: not ignorance, not malice, but the preference for social safety over just action. He is the narrative's mirror of Herod Antipas (6:26).

15:16-20

Rhoads: The soldiers' mock coronation is the Gospel's most sustained sustained enactment of irony. Purple robe, crown of thorns, homage ("Hail, King of the Jews!"), striking, spitting, kneeling — each gesture of mockery performs the truth it intends to deny. Rhoads argues that Mark's narrator presents this scene without evaluative comment, trusting the reader to supply the irony. The soldiers unknowingly crown, robes, and enthrone the actual king. The mockery is the coronation.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
15:1-20

The Roman trial before Pilate is, for Chrysostom, a study in the cowardice of power. Pilate knows Jesus is innocent (v14: "What crime has he committed?") and fears the crowd. The choice of Barabbas over Jesus is, for Origen, a figure charged with substitutionary theology: the genuinely guilty man is released; the genuinely innocent man is condemned. Bede notes the irony of the soldiers' mock homage — the purple robe, the crown of thorns, "Hail, King of the Jews" — as the truth spoken in derision: he is the king, the crown is his, the homage is due. The mockery is the inadvertent liturgy of those who do not know what they are doing.

15:21-47

The crucifixion narrative in Mark is, for Chrysostom, the most compressed of the four Gospels — there is no word from the cross except the cry of dereliction. Origen devotes extensive reflection to "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Psalm 22:1): the question is not theological despair but the opening of a psalm that ends in vindication (Psalm 22:24-31). The tearing of the Temple curtain (v38) is, for Ambrose, the simultaneous end of the old covenant economy and the opening of access to the holy of holies for all people. The centurion's confession "Surely this man was the Son of God!" is, for Theophylact, the Gentile world's first act of faith — the cross itself is the first proclamation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:6-14

Calvin: The Barabbas exchange is the clearest image in the Gospels of substitutionary release. Barabbas’s freedom is the direct result of Jesus’s condemnation. Calvin uses this to explain justification: the guilty go free because the innocent bears the punishment.

15:5

Jesus made no reply, and Pilate was amazed — Calvin: the silence fulfils Isa 53:7. Jesus’s silence is not passivity but the active submission of the Servant who has chosen to bear what is being laid on him.

15:2

Greek Word Study

Eloi Eloi lema sabachthani e-LO-i e-LO-i le-MA sa-BAKH-ta-ni — My God my God why have you forsaken me (Aramaic): The last Aramaic words preserved in Mark — the cry of Ps 22:1, spoken by the dying Jesus. This is not despair but the prayer of the righteous sufferer: Psalm 22 opens with abandonment and ends with vindication (Ps 22:24-31). Jesus prays the whole psalm from its opening cry.

Places

Golgotha
Just outside Jerusalem’s first-century walls; traditional site: Church of the Holy Sepulchre
Golgotha (“place of the skull”) was outside the city walls — execution was performed outside the city (Lev 24:14; Heb 13:12). The Church of the Holy Sepulchre stands on the site identified since the fourth century as both Golgotha and the tomb. First-century archaeology confirms the site was outside the walls of Jesus’s time.
Joseph’s tomb
Near Golgotha; a newly cut rock tomb
The tomb is near the execution site — the Preparation Day urgency required burial before Sabbath (sundown). Joseph’s previously unused tomb is both a mark of honour (Isa 53:9: “with the rich in his death”) and a guarantee of the empty tomb’s significance: no confusion with previous burials.

Context

The crucifixion account is structured around three sets of three: three inscriptions (Title, King of the Jews, Messiah); three groups of mockers (passers-by, chief priests, the crucified); and the three hours of darkness (noon to three). The moment of death is framed by two tearing events: the darkness and the torn veil. The centurion’s confession (“Son of God”) is the Gospel’s climax — the first human being to say what the opening verse (1:1) declared.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 22:1-31My God, my God, why have you forsaken me — the Passion psalm that structures the crucifixion narrative: lots for clothing (Ps 22:18), the taunts (Ps 22:7-8), the cry (Ps 22:1), the vindication (Ps 22:24-31).
  • Isa 53:12He was numbered with the transgressors — fulfilled in v.27: crucified between two rebels.
  • Exod 26:31-33The Temple veil separating the Holy of Holies — torn from top to bottom at the moment of death.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:34

Marcus: "Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani" — the Aramaic cry preserved verbatim is the most emotionally raw moment in the Gospel. Marcus argues it is not despair but prayer: Psalm 22 opens with abandonment and ends with vindication and worldwide praise (Ps 22:22-31). To pray the opening of Ps 22 is to invoke the whole psalm, including its ending. The dereliction is real — not theatrical — but it is the dereliction of the Righteous Sufferer who trusts in divine rescue even from the depths of God-forsakenness.

15:38-39

Marcus: The simultaneous tearing of the Temple veil (katapetasma) and the centurion's confession is Mark's most compressed theological statement. Marcus argues the two events are causally related in Mark's narrative logic: the veil that separated the Holy of Holies (God's dwelling) from the outer courts is torn, and immediately a Gentile — standing on the threshold of Gentile access to God — confesses the truth. The tearing opens the access; the confession is the first act of the newly opened communion.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:21-41

Rhoads: The crucifixion narrative is the most scripturally dense passage in Mark. Rhoads identifies seven allusions to Psalm 22 in seven verses (15:24-36): dividing garments (Ps 22:18), mocking (Ps 22:7-8), the cry (Ps 22:1), the vinegar (Ps 22:15), the dogs surrounding (Ps 22:16). The narrator does not cite Psalm 22 directly — the reader must recognise the allusions. This positions the ideal reader as one steeped in the psalms: the crucifixion's meaning is revealed only to those who can hear the scriptural resonance.

15:39

Rhoads: The centurion's confession closes the bracket opened in 1:1. The Gospel begins with the narrator's declaration ("the Son of God"); it ends with a human character — a Gentile, a soldier, an agent of the execution — making the same declaration. Rhoads argues this is the narrative's final irony: the people who should have recognized Jesus (the religious establishment, the disciples) did not; the pagan executioner who sees him die does. The title "Son of God" frames the entire Gospel; the last human voice to say it is the least expected.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
15:21-32

Simon of Cyrene is, for Origen, the first person explicitly named in Mark as carrying the cross — Chrysostom notes that his sons Alexander and Rufus are named as known to Mark's community, suggesting they became believers. The offer of wine mixed with myrrh (v23) which Jesus refuses is, for Bede, a deliberate acceptance of the full experience of death without analgesic — he does not diminish the suffering. The mocking at the cross ("Come down from the cross and save yourself!", v30) is, for Ambrose, the supreme temptation in the passion narrative: save yourself, or save others. The cross is the answer.

15:33-47

The three hours of darkness (v33) are, for Chrysostom, the creation's mourning for its Creator — an echo of the Egyptian plague of darkness and a sign that cosmic order is disrupted when the light of the world is extinguished. The cry of dereliction in Aramaic (v34) is preserved by Mark in the original language, which Origen says is because the sound carries the full weight of the moment. Joseph of Arimathea is, for Bede, the representative of the hidden believers — members of the establishment who had not been able to declare themselves, now emboldened by the cross itself.

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:34

My God, my God, why have you forsaken me — Calvin: the dereliction is genuine and necessary. The Son experiences the God-forsakenness that sin deserves, so that sinners do not have to. The cry is not the absence of faith but its most extreme expression: prayer in the darkness without the assurance of answer.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:34

Why have you forsaken me (egkatelipes) — the LXX of Ps 22:1. The perfect tense in the Greek: “you have forsaken me.” The experience of abandonment is being spoken from within it. Psalm 22 proceeds from this opening to a final affirmation of divine rescue (Ps 22:24-31); Jesus prays the whole psalm from its beginning.

15:44

Pilate was surprised that he was already dead — victims of crucifixion typically survived 1-3 days. Jesus died after approximately 6 hours — likely due to the severity of the flogging. Pilate’s verification through the centurion is an early historical attestation against later claims of a swoon.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Pontius Pilate
Roman prefect of Judea; AD 26-36
Pilate’s portrait in Mark follows the Herod pattern: knowledge of innocence, temporary protection, capitulation to pressure. His three-part question-sequence (v.2, 4, 9, 12) and final capitulation structure the trial as a moral failure rather than a legal one.
The centurion
Unnamed Roman officer; first human confessor of the Son of God
His confession in 15:39 closes the bracket opened in 1:1. Mark’s Gospel begins with the narrator identifying Jesus as Son of God; it ends with a pagan Roman soldier identifying him the same way, at the cross.

Ancient Sources

Confirms the physical reality of the veil and helps the reader understand the dramatic impact of its tearing.

One of the strongest non-Christian sources confirming the historical fact of Jesus’s execution under Pilate.

Reception History

Anselm, Cur Deus Homo (1098)
Anselm’s satisfaction theory of atonement is built on the logic of the cross: the infinite offense of sin against God required an infinite satisfaction, possible only from one who was both human (to represent humanity) and divine (to offer infinite value).
Jürgen Moltmann, The Crucified God (1972)
Moltmann argued that the cry of dereliction (15:34) reveals that God is not immune to suffering. The cross is the event in which God takes the God-forsakenness of human experience into the divine life itself. The Trinity is implicated in the crucifixion.

Textual Notes

15:25"The third hour" vs "the sixth hour" — conflict with John
Mark 15:25 says Jesus was crucified at "the third hour" (9am); John 19:14 says it was "about the sixth hour" (noon) when Pilate handed him over. No MS tradition resolves the discrepancy.
The most common explanations: (1) different hour-counting systems; (2) John's theological symbolism (sixth hour = when the Passover lambs begin to be slaughtered); (3) scribal adjustment in one Gospel to match the other. The NA28 accepts both readings as original to their respective Gospels.
15:34"Eloi, Eloi" or "Eli, Eli" — Aramaic vs Hebrew form
Mark reads Elōi, Elōi (Aramaic: my God, my God); Matthew 27:46 reads Ēli, Ēli (Hebrew). Some MSS of Mark harmonise toward Matthew's Hebrew form.
Mark's Aramaic form (Eloi) is original to Mark and explains why bystanders thought he was calling Elijah — the name sounds more like Elijah in the Aramaic. Matthew's Hebrew form makes the misunderstanding less explicable. No copyist problem; both forms preserve authentic tradition.

Scholarly Debates

What did the torn veil signify?
Access to God
The veil that separated the Holy of Holies from the Holy Place — the barrier to the divine presence — was torn, signifying that through Christ’s death direct access to God is now available (Heb 9:8-12).
Access to the Most Holy Place
Temple judgment
The tearing is a sign of God’s judgment on the Temple system, anticipating its physical destruction in AD 70.
Judgment on the Temple
"The curtain of the Temple was torn in two from top to bottom" — the divine direction of the tear and the Hebrews 9-10 interpretation both support the access reading. The two interpretations (access + judgment) are not mutually exclusive: the cross both opens access and renders the Temple system obsolete.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 15 is the atonement chapter: the ransom for many (10:45) is paid. The crucifixion is structured by Psalm 22, the torn veil opens access to God, and the centurion’s confession names what has been accomplished.
