Mark 10

On the Road to Jerusalem: Divorce, Children, Wealth, Service, Sight

Author

John Mark, son of Mary of Jerusalem (Acts 12:12), cousin of Barnabas (Col 4:10), and close associate of both Paul and Peter. Early and unanimous tradition identifies him as “the interpreter of Peter”: Papias of Hierapolis (c. AD 125, preserved in Eusebius, HE 3.39.15) records that Mark “wrote down accurately, though not in order, as much as he remembered of the things said or done by the Lord” — drawing entirely on Peter’s teaching. Irenaeus (Against Heresies 3.1.1), Clement of Alexandria (Hypotyposeis, in Eusebius HE 6.14.6), and the Anti-Marcionite Prologue all confirm the Petrine connection. Mark himself is mentioned in Acts 12:12, 25; 13:13; 15:37–39; Col 4:10; Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11; and 1 Pet 5:13 (“my son Mark”). He was present at some events he records (the young man fleeing naked, 14:51–52, is widely identified as a Markan self-cameo), and his vividness throughout reflects the eyewitness testimony he received directly from Peter in Rome.

When written

c. AD 55–65, almost certainly in Rome, most likely before the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 — which Mark 13 predicts but nowhere records as fulfilled. Clement of Alexandria states the Gospel was written “while Peter was still preaching publicly in Rome.” If so, the most probable window is the late 50s to mid-60s, during Paul’s Roman imprisonment (Acts 28) and Peter’s residence in the capital. Mark is the earliest of the four Gospels (the Markan Priority hypothesis, held by the majority of NT scholars), and both Matthew and Luke appear to have used it as a primary source. The Gospel’s Latin loanwords (legiōn, denarius, praetorium, centurio), its explanation of Jewish customs for non-Jewish readers (7:3–4), and its Latinisms of idiom all point to a Roman Gentile audience.

What prompted it

To preserve Peter’s eyewitness testimony in permanent written form for a church facing persecution under Nero (AD 64), and to answer the most urgent question that question raised: who is Jesus, and why did he die? Mark’s opening declaration — “The beginning of the good news about Jesus the Messiah, the Son of God” (1:1) — is simultaneously title, thesis, and answer. The Gospel is structured around the progressive revelation of that identity: demons recognise Jesus before disciples do (1:24); Peter confesses “You are the Messiah” at the midpoint (8:29); a Roman centurion declares “Surely this man was the Son of God” at the cross (15:39). The relentless pace — euthys (“immediately”) appears 41 times — reflects both Peter’s preaching style and the urgency of a community for whom the Passion narrative was not ancient history but recent, costly, and still unfolding.

Verses 1–16 — Marriage and Divorce; Children Welcomed
1Jesus then left that place and went into the region of Judea and across the Jordan. Again crowds of people came to him, and as was his custom, he taught them.2Some Pharisees came and tested him by asking, “Is it lawful for a man to divorce his wife?”3What did Moses command you?” he replied.4They said, “Moses permitted a man to write a certificate of divorce and send her away.”5“It was because your hearts were hard that Moses wrote you this law,” Jesus replied.6But at the beginning of creation God “made them male and female.”7“For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife,8and the two will become one flesh.” So they are no longer two, but one flesh.9Therefore what God has joined together, let no one separate.10When they were in the house again, the disciples asked Jesus about this.11He answered, “Anyone who divorces his wife and marries another woman commits adultery against her.12And if she divorces her husband and marries another man, she commits adultery.”13People were bringing little children to Jesus for him to place his hands on them, but the disciples rebuked those who brought them.14When Jesus saw this, he was indignant. He said to them, “Let the little children come to me, and do not hinder them, for the kingdom of God belongs to such as these.15Truly I tell you, anyone who will not receive the kingdom of God like a little child will never enter it.”16And he took the children in his arms, placed his hands on them and blessed them.

Greek Word Study

arkhē ktiseōs ar-KAY ktee-SE-os — beginning of creation: At the beginning of creation — Jesus grounds the marriage ethic not in Mosaic law but in creation order. The appeal to Genesis 1 goes behind the Mosaic concession to the original divine design. The creation ethic is prior to and normative over all subsequent law.

Places

Region of Judea and across the Jordan (Perea)
East of the Jordan, common route from Galilee to Jerusalem
The journey through Perea marks the deliberate turn toward the Passion. Jesus’s movement into Judean territory begins the geographic arc that will end at Golgotha.

Context

The movement from Galilee into Judea (10:1) is the geographic turning point: the journey to Jerusalem has begun. The Pharisees’ divorce test receives a creation-order answer that goes behind Moses. The children episode immediately follows — kingdom entry requires becoming like a child: the opposite of the disciples’ gatekeeping.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 1:27God made them male and female — Jesus grounds the marriage ethic in creation order, not Mosaic concession.
  • Gen 2:24A man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife — the one-flesh permanence Jesus quotes.
  • Deut 24:1-4The Mosaic divorce certificate — Jesus reinterprets it as a concession to hardness of heart, not divine ideal.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:5

Marcus: "Because of your hardness of heart Moses wrote you this law" — Jesus appeals to the creation order as theologically prior to Mosaic concession. Marcus notes this argument-form (from creation prior to Torah) has parallels in the Qumran Damascus Document (CD 4:19-5:6), which similarly appeals to creation-order marriage norms against what it regards as corruptions in contemporary practice. The technique — going behind a problematic law to its pre-law foundation — was a live exegetical strategy.

10:14

Marcus: "He was indignant" (ēganaktēsen) — the same word appears in 10:41 when the ten are indignant at James and John. The disciples gatekeep children from Jesus; the disciples gatekeep positions of glory from each other. Both forms of gatekeeping are rebuked. Marcus argues that the child-blessing episode is not merely about affection for children but is a statement about kingdom-entry conditions: the child, having no claims, no status, and no leverage, exemplifies the posture of one who receives the kingdom as a gift.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:1-12

Rhoads: The Judean setting (10:1) marks the final geographic transition: Jesus is now in the territory that will lead to Jerusalem. The Pharisees' divorce question is presented as a "test" (peirazō) — the same word used for the wilderness temptation (1:13 is implied) and the demand for a sign (8:11). The narrator consistently uses this word to signal that questioners are not seeking truth but trying to entrap. Jesus's answer through the creation-order argument places him above the test — he refuses the premise.

10:13-16

Rhoads: The children episode is placed between two texts about the kingdom's demands (marriage, wealth). Its function in the sequence is to demonstrate the kingdom's entry condition by contrast: wealth and social standing are obstacles (10:17-22); child-like reception is the model. Rhoads argues the sequence is not accidental — the juxtaposition of the impossible camel (10:25) with the blessed child (10:15) encodes the Gospel's core paradox: the way in is for those who have nothing to offer.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:5

Moses permitted divorce because of your hardness of heart — the concession principle. Mosaic law accommodates human sin without endorsing it. The appeal to creation exposes the difference between divine design and pastoral concession.

10:14

He was indignant — the same righteous anger (ēganaktēsen) as 10:41. The disciples’ gatekeeping of children provokes it. The kingdom’s entry condition is child-like reception, not adult qualification.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:9

Let no one separate — the prohibition is addressed to human will, not to God’s providence. Calvin acknowledges the church must have processes for genuine dissolution while maintaining the creational ideal.

10:13-16

The children episode rebukes those who think the kingdom is for the capable and qualified. The entry requirement is precisely the absence of qualification — willingness to receive without earning.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press

Tested him (peirazō) — the same word as the wilderness temptation. Any answer will alienate either the conservative school of Shammai or the liberal school of Hillel. Jesus refuses the premise and appeals to creation.

Verses 17–45 — The Rich Young Man; Third Passion Prediction; The Ransom
17As Jesus started on his way, a man ran up to him and fell on his knees before him. “Good teacher,” he asked, “what must I do to inherit eternal life?”18“Why do you call me good?” Jesus answered. “No one is good—except God alone.19You know the commandments: “You shall not murder, you shall not commit adultery, you shall not steal, you shall not give false testimony, you shall not defraud, honor your father and mother.”’20“Teacher,” he declared, “all these I have kept since I was a boy.”21Jesus looked at him and loved him. “One thing you lack,” he said. “Go, sell everything you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven. Then come, follow me.”22At this the man’s face fell. He went away sad, because he had great wealth.23Jesus looked around and said to his disciples, “How hard it is for the rich to enter the kingdom of God!”24The disciples were amazed at his words. But Jesus said again, “Children, how hard it is to enter the kingdom of God!25It is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for someone who is rich to enter the kingdom of God.”26The disciples were even more amazed, and said to each other, “Who then can be saved?”27Jesus looked at them and said, “With man this is impossible, but not with God; all things are possible with God.”28Then Peter spoke up, “We have left everything to follow you!”29“Truly I tell you,” Jesus replied, “no one who has left home or brothers or sisters or mother or father or children or fields for me and the gospel30will fail to receive a hundred times as much in this present age: homes, brothers, sisters, mothers, children and fields—along with persecutions—and in the age to come eternal life.31But many who are first will be last, and the last first.”32They were on their way up to Jerusalem, with Jesus leading the way, and the disciples were astonished and those who followed were afraid. Again he took the Twelve aside and told them what was going to happen to him.33“We are going up to Jerusalem,” he said, “and the Son of Man will be delivered to the chief priests and the teachers of the law. They will condemn him to death and will hand him over to the Gentiles,34who will mock him and spit on him, flog him and kill him. Three days later he will rise.”35Then James and John, the sons of Zebedee, came to him. “Teacher,” they said, “we want you to do for us whatever we ask.”36What do you want me to do for you?” he asked.37They replied, “Let one of us sit at your right and the other at your left in your glory.”38You don’t know what you are asking,” Jesus said. “Can you drink the cup I drink or be baptized with the baptism I am baptized with?”39We can,” they answered. Jesus said to them, “You will drink the cup I drink and be baptized with the baptism I am baptized with,40but to sit at my right or left is not for me to grant. These places belong to those for whom they have been prepared.”41When the ten heard about this, they became indignant with James and John.42Jesus called them together and said, “You know that those who are regarded as rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their high officials exercise authority over them.43Not so with you. Instead, whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant,44and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all.45For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many.”

Greek Word Study

lytron LY-tron — ransom/redemption price: The Son of Man came to give his life as a ransom for many (v.45) — lytron anti pollōn. Lytron is the price paid to free a slave or captive. The anti indicates substitution: his life in the place of many. This is the clearest substitutionary atonement statement in Mark.

Context

The third and most detailed passion prediction (10:33-34) is bracketed by two failure stories: the rich young man who cannot surrender wealth (10:17-22) and James and John who request the best seats (10:35-40). Mark 10:45 is the theological climax of the entire pre-Passion narrative: the ransom saying defines why Jesus is going to Jerusalem.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 53:10-12He will see the result of his suffering… he bore the sin of many — the servant song that 10:45 directly echoes.
  • Dan 7:13-14The Son of Man receiving authority — here re-described in servant terms: the Son of Man comes not to be served but to give his life.
  • Exod 21:30A ransom for his life — the legal background for lytron: the payment that redeems a life otherwise forfeit.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:45

Marcus: "A ransom for many" (lytron anti pollōn) — Marcus's most sustained exegetical argument is that this phrase is the culmination of Mark's use of Isa 53 throughout the Gospel. The "many" (pollōn) echoes Isa 53:11-12 ("he will justify many"; "he bore the sin of many"). The lytron (ransom) draws on Exod 21:30 and the broader Levitical system of substitutionary payment. Marcus argues that the ransom saying is not a Pauline interpolation but the theological fulcrum of Mark's own narrative: it defines why the Son of Man goes to Jerusalem.

10:38

Marcus: "The cup I drink" and "the baptism I am baptized with" — both metaphors draw on OT imagery. The cup of divine wrath (Isa 51:17-22; Jer 25:15-17; Ps 75:8) and the flood/baptism of overwhelming disaster (Ps 42:7; 69:1-2; Isa 43:2) are two ways of describing the coming passion. Marcus argues that the double metaphor prepares for the Gethsemane prayer (14:36: "take this cup from me") and the cry of dereliction (15:34) — both moments when the passion-language becomes literal.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:17-22

Rhoads: The rich young man is the only person in Mark who approaches Jesus correctly (running, kneeling), asks the right question (eternal life), receives the right answer, and refuses it. His characterization is the most complex of any minor figure: virtuous, sincere, and ultimately deficient in the one thing that counts. Rhoads argues that the scene functions as a narrative mirror for the reader: the right approach + the right question + the right answer still leaves the person unchanged if the one demand is refused. Hearing is not enough.

10:32

Rhoads: "Jesus was leading the way, and the disciples were astonished, and those who followed were afraid." The visual portrait — Jesus striding ahead, disciples astonished, followers afraid — is one of the most evocative in the Gospel. The narrator gives us a spatial image: Jesus ahead, the gap between him and the disciples visible. Rhoads reads the spatial description as characterization: Jesus's deliberateness and the disciples' inability to keep up is the relationship in miniature. His pace is the pace of the narrative's inevitability.

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:21

Jesus looked at him and loved him — the only explicit statement in Mark that Jesus loved an individual who is not a disciple. The love and the demand are inseparable.

10:45

The ransom for many — the theological climax of Mark’s journey section. Every teaching on greatness and self-denial has been building to this: Jesus himself is the paradigm. The cross is not an accident; it is the ransom.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:18

Why do you call me good? No one is good except God alone — the question is not a denial of divinity but an invitation: if I am good, consider what that implies about who I am.

10:27

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:33-34

The most detailed passion prediction: seven elements — delivered to chief priests and scribes, condemned, handed to Gentiles, mocked, spit upon, flogged, killed, raised. Each is fulfilled in chs.14-16.

10:45

Anti pollōn — the preposition anti consistently means substitution in NT Greek. The death is a specific exchange: his life in the place of many, echoing Isa 53:11-12.

Verses 46–52 — Blind Bartimaeus at Jericho
46Then they came to Jericho. As Jesus and his disciples, together with a large crowd, were leaving the city, a blind man, Bartimaeus (which means “son of Timaeus”), was sitting by the roadside begging.47When he heard that it was Jesus of Nazareth, he began to shout, “Jesus, Son of David, have mercy on me!”48Many rebuked him and told him to be quiet, but he shouted all the more, “Son of David, have mercy on me!”49Jesus stopped and said, “Call him.” So they called to the blind man, “Cheer up! On your feet! He’s calling you.”50Throwing his cloak aside, he jumped to his feet and came to Jesus.51What do you want me to do for you?” Jesus asked him. The blind man said, “Rabbi, I want to see.”52Go,” said Jesus, “your faith has healed you.” Immediately he received his sight and followed Jesus along the road.

Greek Word Study

huios Dauid hwi-OS da-WID — Son of David: Son of David, have mercy on me — the first use of this messianic title by a human being in Mark. The blind beggar uses it twice, insistently. Where the religious authorities demanded silence and the disciples argued about greatness, a blind beggar sees clearly who Jesus is.

Places

Jericho
Jordan Valley; 250m below sea level; last city before Jerusalem
Jericho is the gateway to Jerusalem on the eastern approach. After Jericho the road climbs steeply through the Judean wilderness to Jerusalem (from 250m below sea level to 750m above). Bartimaeus is healed at the foot of the final ascent.

Context

Bartimaeus is Mark’s most individualized healing recipient — the only one given a name and its meaning. His persistence against the crowd’s silencing, his immediate response to the call (throwing off his cloak — his begging equipment), and his following Jesus on the road make him the model disciple of the entire journey section. His healing is the last act before the Triumphal Entry.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 35:5Then will the eyes of the blind be opened — the eschatological healing Bartimaeus receives.
  • Ps 146:8The LORD gives sight to the blind — what YHWH does, Jesus does.
  • 2 Sam 7:12-14The eternal Davidic throne — the “Son of David” title rooted in the Davidic covenant.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:47

Marcus: "Son of David, have mercy on me" — this is the first human use of the Son of David title in Mark (the scribes discuss it in 12:35-37, but no one addresses Jesus by it until Bartimaeus). Marcus argues that the Davidic messianology implicit in the title is being deliberately invoked: Bartimaeus approaches Jesus as the heir of David's royal-healing tradition. The blindness recalls 2 Sam 5:8 and the legend of David's Jerusalem conquest, where the blind and lame were associated with the city's pre-Davidic state.

10:52

Marcus: "He followed Jesus along the road" (en tē hodō) — Marcus notes that hodos (road, way) is a structural keyword in Mark. The disciples were called on the road (1:16-20); the parables were explained on the road (8:27); the passion predictions were given on the road (8:27; 9:33-34; 10:32). In the Acts of the Apostles, "the Way" (hē hodos) becomes the earliest name for the Christian movement. Bartimaeus's following Jesus on the road at the end of ch.10 is, narratively, his entry into the story's ongoing movement toward Jerusalem and the cross.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:46-52

Rhoads: Bartimaeus closes a structural bracket opened by the blind man at Bethsaida (8:22-26). Both healings are done with unusual physical detail; both frame the central section of the Gospel (8:22-10:52) which contains all three passion predictions and all three failed-disciple responses. The bracket is interpretive: the Gospel's central section is flanked by two blind men receiving sight. The disciples who receive the light of three explicit passion predictions remain effectively blind. Bartimaeus sees; they do not.

10:50

Rhoads: "Throwing his cloak aside" — the single action is one of the most economical characterizations in the Gospel. The cloak is the begging equipment, the night covering, the one possession. To throw it aside before receiving the healing is an act of absolute trust: he has abandoned the old life before the new one has been granted. Rhoads argues that this detail does more characterization work than paragraphs of interior monologue would: we see who Bartimaeus is entirely through what he does.

Ancient Sources

Confirms the Isaiah 53 background for the ransom saying and Jesus’s self-understanding as the Servant of YHWH.

Reception History

Anselm, Cur Deus Homo (1098)
Anselm’s satisfaction theory of atonement is built substantially on Mark 10:45. The ransom for many is the satisfaction of divine justice that no human being could offer — requiring one both human and divine.
N.T. Wright, The Day the Revolution Began (2016)
Wright argued the ransom saying should be understood in Exodus-liberation terms: Jesus ransoms the people from captivity to sin as YHWH ransomed Israel from Egypt. The two readings are complementary.

Textual Notes

10:2"The Pharisees came and tested him" — with or without "came to him"
NA28 reads Pharisaioi proseltho̱ntes (coming to him, the Pharisees); some MSS omit proseltho̱ntes as redundant.
Minor variation with no impact on meaning. The fuller reading is better attested.
10:24"How hard it is to enter the kingdom of God" — with or without wealth clause
NA28/UBS5 reads the shorter text; later MSS add "for those who trust in riches" (tois pepoitho̱tas epi chrēmasin), making the warning specifically about wealth rather than universally about kingdom entry.
The shorter reading is original and theologically harder: it makes all kingdom entry difficult, not merely entry for the wealthy. The addition softens the statement by limiting it to those with attachment to riches.

Scholarly Debates

Is Mark 10:45 substitutionary atonement?
Substitutionary view
Anti pollōn (for many) uses the preposition of substitution. The lytron pays what the many owe. Jesus takes the place of those who are ransomed.
Substitutionary
Liberation/Exodus view
The ransom language draws on the Exodus pattern: YHWH ransomed Israel from Egypt without paying a price to Pharaoh. The liberation is real without requiring a payment to a captor.
Liberation
"Ransom for many" echoes Isa 53:10-12 and the Exodus pattern simultaneously. The two models are complementary: substitutionary satisfaction and liberation from bondage are two angles on the same saving act.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 10 is the discipleship chapter: the cost of following Jesus (wealth, ambition, status) is defined against the ransom saying (10:45) as Jesus deliberately walks toward Jerusalem.
