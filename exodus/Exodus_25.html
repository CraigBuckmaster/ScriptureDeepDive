Exodus 25

Tabernacle Offerings; Ark, Table, and Lampstand

Hebrew

Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. Moses is not only the principal character of Exodus — he is its author, and the events recorded are largely his own experience. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC. The events of Exodus span approximately 80 years (Moses's birth to the Tabernacle's completion), but the book itself was written after the fact — most likely during the years at Sinai and the wilderness wandering that followed. The Tabernacle instructions and legal material (chs.19–40) may have been committed to writing earliest, as they were immediately actionable. What prompted it: Israel needed a founding narrative — a record of how God delivered them from 430 years of slavery, made them his covenant people at Sinai, and established the Tabernacle as his dwelling among them. Exodus is the story of liberation, but more than that, it is the story of covenant formation and divine habitation. It answers: How did God redeem us? What does he require of us? And how can a holy God dwell with a sinful people?

Verses 1–9 — The Offering; the Command to Build
1The Lord said to Moses,2"Tell the Israelites to bring me an offering. You are to receive the offering for me from everyone whose heart prompts them to give.3These are the offerings you are to receive from them: gold, silver and bronze;4blue, purple and scarlet yarn and fine linen; goat hair;5ram skins dyed red and another type of durable leather; acacia wood;6olive oil for the light; spices for the anointing oil and for the fragrant incense;7and onyx stones and other gems to be mounted on the ephod and breastpiece.8"Then have them make a sanctuary for me, and I will dwell among them.9Make this tabernacle and all its furnishings exactly like the pattern I will show you."

Hebrew Word Study

מִשְׁכָּן miškan — tabernacle / dwelling: From šākan (to dwell, to settle). This is the central word of the entire Tabernacle section — God's dwelling among his people. The Incarnation echoes this: John 1:14 "the Word became flesh and dwelt (eskēnōsen — tabernacled) among us."

תְּרוּמָה tĕrûmāh — offering / contribution: A voluntary gift, lifted up (rûm) to God. The Tabernacle is built from the willing overflow of grateful hearts, not from taxation. The model of covenant generosity: "from everyone whose heart prompts them."

Historical Context

Ancient Near Eastern temples were understood as dwelling places for deities — the god's house on earth, where heaven and earth met. Egyptian, Mesopotamian, and Ugaritic temples all reflect this cosmic geography. Israel's Tabernacle is both continuous with and radically different from this tradition: the God of Israel genuinely dwells among his people, but he cannot be confined, imaged, or manipulated.

Context

The sequence of Exodus is theologically precise: redemption (chs.1–15) → covenant offer (chs.19–24) → Tabernacle instructions (chs.25–31). God rescues Israel, ratifies the covenant, then immediately specifies how he will live with them. The Tabernacle is the answer to the Sinai holiness problem: how can the Holy God dwell with sinful people? Through a precisely ordered system of approach, sacrifice, and priestly mediation.

Cross-Reference

  • John 1:14"The Word became flesh and dwelt (tabernacled) among us." The Incarnation is the fulfilment of the Tabernacle's promise.
  • Rev 21:3"God's dwelling place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them." The eschatological Tabernacle fulfils Exod 25:8.
  • Heb 8:5The earthly Tabernacle is "a copy and shadow of what is in heaven" — the heavenly pattern of v.9.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
25:8

"I will dwell among them." This is the central promise of the entire Tabernacle section — and the central promise of the whole Bible. From Eden to the New Jerusalem, God's purpose is to dwell with his people. The Tabernacle is the first institutional answer to how that is possible after the Fall: through the blood of sacrifice, through priestly mediation, through the precise ordering of approach to the Holy.

25:9

"Exactly like the pattern I will show you." The pattern (tabnît) comes from heaven; Moses only executes it. This is not human religious creativity; it is divine architectural instruction. The reason precision matters: the Tabernacle is a model of the heavenly reality (Heb 8:5). Any deviation from the pattern is a deviation from the heavenly truth it represents.

Verses 10–22 — The Ark and the Mercy Seat
10"Have them make an ark of acacia wood—two and a half cubits long, a cubit and a half wide, and a cubit and a half high.11Overlay it with pure gold, both inside and out, and make a gold molding around it.12Cast four gold rings for it and fasten them to its four feet, with two rings on one side and two rings on the other.13Then make poles of acacia wood and overlay them with gold.14Insert the poles into the rings on the sides of the ark to carry it.15The poles are to remain in the rings of this ark; they are not to be removed.16Then put in the ark the tablets of the covenant law, which I will give you.17"Make an atonement cover of pure gold—two and a half cubits long and a cubit and a half wide.18And make two cherubim out of hammered gold at the ends of the cover.19Make one cherub on one end and the second cherub on the other; make the cherubim of one piece with the cover, at the two ends.20The cherubim are to have their wings spread upward, overshadowing the cover with them. The cherubim are to face each other, looking toward the cover.21Place the cover on top of the ark and put in the ark the tablets of the covenant law that I will give you.22"There, above the cover between the two cherubim that are over the ark of the covenant law, I will meet with you and give you all my commands for the Israelites."

Hebrew Word Study

כַּפֹּרֶת kappōret — atonement cover / mercy seat: From kāpar (to cover, atone). The lid of the ark is the place of atonement — where blood is sprinkled on the Day of Atonement (Lev 16). Paul uses the same concept in Rom 3:25 (hilastērion) for Christ as the propitiation — the new mercy seat.

אָרוֹן ʾārôn — ark / chest: A wooden chest overlaid with gold. Its contents (the tablets of the law) define what it is: the repository of the covenant obligations. God's presence above the mercy seat and the law beneath create the theological architecture of atonement: the law accuses; the blood covers.

Context

The ark is the most sacred object in Israel's worship — it is the footstool of God's invisible throne (1 Chr 28:2). The mercy seat (kappōret) is the specific site of atonement on Yom Kippur: the high priest sprinkles blood on it to atone for the nation's sins. God's meeting-place with Moses (v.22: "I will meet with you") is not the burning bush or Sinai but here — above the blood-covered law. This is the logic of the entire sacrificial system in one piece of furniture.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 3:25Christ presented as a "sacrifice of atonement" (hilastērion — the same word as LXX kappōret). Jesus is the true mercy seat.
  • Heb 9:5The cherubim of glory overshadowing the mercy seat — the author of Hebrews explicitly references the Tabernacle furniture.
  • 1 Sam 4:4The ark called "the ark of the covenant of the Lord Almighty, who is enthroned between the cherubim."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
25:17,22

The mercy seat is both the place of atonement (blood sprinkled on it, Lev 16) and the place of meeting ("I will meet with you there"). These two functions are inseparable: access to God requires atonement. You cannot have communion with the Holy without the covering of blood. The entire architecture of the Tabernacle, from the outermost gate to the innermost ark, is a graduated approach toward the Holy — and blood makes every stage possible.

25:16

The tablets of the covenant law inside the ark, the mercy seat above them. Sin (the broken law) beneath; atonement (the sprinkled blood) above. This spatial arrangement is the gospel in furniture form.

Verses 23–40 — The Table and the Lampstand
23"Make a table of acacia wood—two cubits long, a cubit wide and a cubit and a half high.24Overlay it with pure gold and make a gold molding around it.25Also make around it a rim a handbreadth wide and put a gold molding on the rim.26Make four gold rings for the table and fasten them to the four corners, where the four legs are.27The rings are to be close to the rim to hold the poles used in carrying the table.28Make the poles of acacia wood, overlay them with gold and carry the table with them.29"And make its plates and dishes of pure gold, as well as its pitchers and bowls for the pouring out of offerings.30Put the bread of the Presence on this table to be before me at all times.31"Make a lampstand of pure gold. Hammer out its base and shaft, and make its flowerlike cups, buds and blossoms of one piece with it.32Six branches are to extend from the sides of the lampstand—three on one side and three on the other.33Three cups shaped like almond flowers with buds and blossoms are to be on one branch, three on the next branch, and the same for all six branches extending from the lampstand.34And on the lampstand there are to be four cups shaped like almond flowers with buds and blossoms.35One bud shall be under the first pair of branches extending from the lampstand, a second bud under the second pair, and a third bud under the third pair—six branches in all.36The buds and branches shall all be of one piece with the lampstand, hammered out of pure gold.37"Then make its seven lamps and set them up on it so that they light the space in front of it.38Its wick trimmers and trays are to be of pure gold.39A talent of pure gold is to be used for the lampstand and all these accessories.40See that you make them according to the pattern shown you on the mountain."

Hebrew Word Study

לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים leḥem happānîm — bread of the Presence: Literally "bread of the face" — bread placed before God's face. Twelve loaves, refreshed weekly (Lev 24:5–9), representing Israel's twelve tribes in continuous communion at the table of God. Covenant meals create and sustain relationship; this is Israel's perpetual table fellowship with God.

מְנוֹרָה mĕnôrāh — lampstand: The seven-branched lampstand — a tree of light, almond-shaped, hammered from one piece of gold. Its sevenfold light fills the Holy Place. Zechariah's vision (Zech 4) and Revelation's seven lampstands (Rev 1:12) draw directly on this image.

Context

The three furnishings of the Holy Place — ark (atonement), table (bread/communion), and lampstand (light) — form a complete theology: atonement enables communion; communion is sustained by light/revelation. The lampstand lit the table; the table held the bread; the bread signified Israel's covenant relationship with the God enthroned above the ark. Each piece interprets the others.

Cross-Reference

  • John 6:35"I am the bread of life" — Jesus as the true bread of the Presence.
  • John 8:12"I am the light of the world" — Jesus as the true lampstand.
  • Rev 1:12–13The seven golden lampstands are the seven churches; Christ walks among them — the true light of the Presence.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
25:30

"Put the bread of the Presence on this table to be before me at all times." The bread never left the table. There was never a moment when the covenant communion symbol was absent from God's house. This is the permanence of covenant: Israel was always at the table. Jesus at the Last Supper takes the Passover bread and says "this is my body" — he is not departing from this symbolism but fulfilling it. He is the bread that is always before God.

25:40

"See that you make them according to the pattern shown you on the mountain." The repetition of the pattern command (also v.9) frames the entire chapter. Everything about the Tabernacle is derivative of the heavenly reality. The earthly copy points to the heavenly original. Hebrews 8:5 makes this explicit. The Tabernacle does not generate meaning; it reflects it.

Ancient Sources

ANE Temple Parallels
Mesopotamian and Egyptian temples were conceived as divine dwelling places where the god's statue resided. Building instructions from the gods are attested in texts like the Gudea Cylinders (c.2100 BC), where the king receives temple plans in a dream.
The Tabernacle fits within the ANE temple-building tradition while radically transforming it: God cannot be imaged; his presence is real but not localised in a statue.
Ark Parallels
Egyptian sacred chests (carrying shrines for statues) were overlaid with gold and carried on poles. The ark uses the same form but contains the law, not an image — the covenant document, not a representation of God.
Form is borrowed; theology is transformed.

Reception History

Hebrews 8–10
The sustained NT typological reading of the Tabernacle: every element points to Christ. The mercy seat is his atonement; the bread of Presence is his body; the lampstand is his light. The earthly copy gives way to the heavenly reality.
John's Gospel
The Prologue's "tabernacled among us" (1:14) sets the entire Gospel as the new Tabernacle narrative — Jesus is where God dwells, where the glory is seen, where the bread and light are found.

The chapter moves from the concept (I will dwell among you, vv.1–9) to the innermost furnishings (ark, table, lampstand). The order is inside-out: God begins with the most holy and works outward. This is the order of theology: atonement first, then communion, then light.
