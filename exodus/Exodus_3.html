Exodus 3

The Burning Bush; The Divine Name

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref

Timeline

Exodus 3 contains the burning bush theophany — the most theologically dense single chapter in the Pentateuch. God reveals his personal name YHWH (the I AM, vv.14–15), commissions Moses as deliverer, and frames the entire Exodus as covenant-keeping. The chapter answers the question raised by ch.2's four verbs: when God hears, remembers, sees, and knows — this is what happens next.

Verses 1–9 — The Burning Bush; God Reveals Himself
1Now Moses was tending the flock of Jethro his father-in-law, the priest of Midian, and he led the flock to the far side of the wilderness and came to Horeb, the mountain of God.2There the angel of the Lord appeared to him in flames of fire from within a bush. Moses saw that though the bush was on fire it did not burn up.3So Moses thought, "I will go over and see this strange sight—why the bush does not burn up."4When the Lord saw that he had gone over to look, God called to him from within the bush, "Moses! Moses!" And Moses said, "Here I am."5"Do not come any closer," God said. "Take off your sandals, for the place where you are standing is holy ground."6Then he said, "I am the God of your father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac and the God of Jacob." At this, Moses hid his face, because he was afraid to look at God.7The Lord said, "I have indeed seen the misery of my people in Egypt. I have heard them crying out because of their slave drivers, and I am concerned about their suffering.8So I have come down to rescue them from the hand of the Egyptians and to bring them up out of that land into a good and spacious land, a land flowing with milk and honey—the home of the Canaanites, Hittites, Amorites, Perizzites, Hivites and Jebusites.9And now the cry of the Israelites has reached me, and I have seen the way the Egyptians are oppressing them.

Hebrew Word Study

מַלְאַךְ יְהוָה malʾak YHWH — the angel of the LORD: The Angel of the LORD in the OT is consistently a theophanic figure — not a created angel but a manifestation of YHWH himself (v.4: "the LORD saw... God called"). In this verse the Angel and YHWH are equated. Early Christian interpretation identifies this figure with the pre-incarnate Son.

אַדְמַת קֹדֶשׁ ʾadmat qōdeš — holy ground: Ground is made holy by divine presence, not inherent quality. The holiness travels with YHWH. This principle will organise the entire Tabernacle system: the degree of holiness corresponds to proximity to the divine presence.

Historical Context

Horeb (= Sinai) is the theological mountain of the entire Pentateuch — the site of the law-giving (Exod 19), Elijah's flight (1 Kgs 19), and now Moses's commissioning. Its location is debated (traditional identification: Jebel Musa in the Sinai Peninsula; alternative: Jebel al-Lawz in Arabia). The forty years in Midian would place Moses in the region of the Hejaz/Sinai peninsula — consistent with either location.

Context

Moses at the bush is at the lowest point of his trajectory: eighty years old, tending sheep in the wilderness for forty years, having failed in his first attempt at deliverance. The bush burns and is not consumed — the image of Israel under oppression: afflicted but not destroyed. God's appearance here reverses every human expectation: the deliverer is not found in a palace but in a desert; not in his strength but in his weakness; not when he seeks God but when he notices something unusual in his ordinary work.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 7:30–34Stephen: "the angel appeared to him in the wilderness of Mount Sinai, in a flame of fire in a bush." The speech draws the direct parallel: Moses rejected, then sent — as Christ was rejected, then glorified.
  • Matt 22:32Jesus uses v.6 to prove the resurrection: "I am the God of Abraham..." — God is God of the living, not the dead.
  • 1 Kgs 19:8Elijah flees to Horeb — the same mountain. The pattern: prophet in despair, theophany, recommissioning.

Places & Geography

Horeb / Sinai
Sinai Peninsula or northwestern Arabia
The mountain of God — site of Moses's commissioning, the Law-giving (Exod 19–24), and Elijah's renewal (1 Kgs 19). The holiest geographic point in the Pentateuch.
Midian
Northwest Arabia / Sinai region
Where Moses spent 40 years as a shepherd. The Midianites were descendants of Abraham and Keturah (Gen 25:2). Jethro's household preserved some knowledge of YHWH.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:2

The burning bush that is not consumed is one of Scripture's great symbols. It is a picture of Israel: in the fire of affliction, not consumed. It is a picture of the divine presence: holy, unapproachable, yet drawing near. Augustine sees it as a type of the Incarnation: the divine fire dwelling in human nature without consuming it. The bush is also a picture of Moses himself at this moment: his past is ash, his future is fire, and something is happening that defies natural explanation.

3:6

God introduces himself not by his power but by his relationships: "the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob." He is not first "the Creator" or "the Almighty" but the covenant-keeper — defined by his faithfulness to specific people across generations. Jesus will later use this exact verse to prove the resurrection (Matt 22:32): if God is their God, they must be alive, for God is not the God of the dead. The burning bush carries the resurrection inside it.

Hebrew Word Study

אֶהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶהְיֶה ʾehyeh ʾăšer ʾehyeh — I AM WHO I AM: The name is a first-person imperfect of the verb hāyāh (to be/become). Possible translations: "I AM WHAT I AM," "I WILL BE WHAT I WILL BE," "I CAUSE TO BE WHAT I CAUSE TO BE." The LXX renders it "I am the one who is" (Ego eimi ho ōn) — emphasising ontological being. The name refuses to be contained in a category while asserting absolute, self-existing presence.

יְהוָה YHWH — The LORD / Yahweh: The Tetragrammaton — derived from the same hāyāh root. It is the personal covenant name of Israel's God, used 6,828 times in the OT. Ancient Jews ceased to pronounce it and substituted ʾĂdōnāy (Lord), which is why most English translations render it LORD (small caps).

Context

Moses's question "what is his name?" is not ignorance of a name but a request for the character, authority, and reliability behind the name. In the ancient world, knowing a god's name was knowing their nature and the basis of relationship with them. YHWH's answer — I AM WHO I AM — refuses domestication while asserting radical, self-grounding presence. The name is a promise: I will be with you (v.12: "I will be with you," ʾehyeh ʿimmak — the same ʾehyeh root) as I have always been.

Cross-Reference

  • John 8:58"Before Abraham was, I am" — Jesus uses the present-tense "I am" (ego eimi) in a context where the past tense is expected, claiming the divine name of Exod 3:14 for himself.
  • Rev 1:8"I am the Alpha and the Omega... who is and who was and who is to come" — the Revelation expands I AM into a temporal formula, unpacking the name's eternal self-existence.
  • Exod 6:3God tells Moses: "I appeared to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob as God Almighty, but by my name YHWH I did not make myself known to them" — the name is the Exodus revelation, not the patriarchal one.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:14

"I AM WHO I AM." This is the most important self-disclosure in all of Scripture before the Incarnation. The name YHWH is not a classification but a category-breaking assertion: I am self-existent, self-defining, self-grounding. I am not subject to Israel's categories or Pharaoh's categories or Moses's categories. I simply am — and I am present with you. Every subsequent act of God in Exodus is an unpacking of this name: what does it mean that I AM is with Israel? It means Egypt's gods cannot stop him, Pharaoh's army cannot catch him, the desert cannot kill his people. I AM covers everything.

3:15

"This is my name forever, and thus I am to be remembered throughout all generations." The name YHWH is not a secret or a temporary self-disclosure — it is the permanent identity by which God will be known. The Westminster Catechism asks: "What is God?" The burning bush has already answered: I AM WHO I AM. The question behind every human heart — is there a God? — is answered not with a philosophical argument but with a burning bush, a covenant promise, and a name that means: I am here, I have always been here, and I always will be.

People of the Chapter

Moses
80-year-old shepherd; commissioned at Horeb
His five objections (3:11,13; 4:1,10,13) show a man broken by past failure, not false modesty. God's answers are not arguments but promises.

The burning bush theophany
The Angel who speaks is YHWH himself. The identification in vv.2–4 (Angel → LORD → God) is the pattern throughout the OT theophanies.

Ancient Sources

Ugaritic El texts
Canaanite texts use similar "I am" formulas for El (the high god) in covenant contexts — showing the formula is recognisable in the ancient Semitic world while being filled with unique content by Israel's God.
The divine-name formula has ANE context while being theologically unprecedented.
LXX Exod 3:14
The LXX rendering (ego eimi ho ōn, "I am the one who is") shaped Greek philosophical theology of divine being — Philo of Alexandria built his entire theology of God on this rendering.
The name YHWH became the axis of both Jewish and Christian philosophical theology.

Reception History

John 8:58
Jesus's "before Abraham was, I am" is the most explicit NT claim to the divine name of Exod 3:14 — and the moment the Jewish leaders take up stones.
Aquinas, Summa Theologica I.13
Thomas Aquinas argues that "He Who Is" (Qui Est) is the most proper name of God — building directly on the LXX rendering of Exod 3:14. The burning bush became the foundation of medieval philosophical theology.

The bush — vv.2–5Fire; not consumed; holy ground — approach and removal of sandals
Identification — v.6I am the God of your fathers — covenant continuity
Commission (A) — vv.7–10I have seen, I have heard, I know, I have come down, I will send you
Objection 1 & Answer — vv.11–12"Who am I?" — "I will be with you" (ʾehyeh)
Objection 2 & Answer — vv.13–22"What is your name?" — I AM WHO I AM; YHWH; covenant name forever
The commission structure (vv.7–10) uses five "I" statements of God: I have seen, I have heard, I know, I have come down, I will send — five divine commitments that answer Israel's cry from ch.2. Moses's two objections in ch.3 are answered by divine presence (v.12) and divine identity (vv.14–15).

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 3 is the theological centre of the Pentateuch. The I AM revelation is not just the beginning of the Exodus — it is the answer to every question the Bible raises about who God is. Everything that follows in both Testaments is an unpacking of the burning bush.
