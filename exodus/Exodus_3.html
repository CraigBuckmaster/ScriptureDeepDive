Exodus 3

The Burning Bush; "I AM WHO I AM"

Hebrew
Historical

Timeline

Exodus is traditionally attributed to Moses, with near-universal ancient attestation in both Jewish and Christian sources. The book covers the period from Israel's oppression in Egypt through the construction of the Tabernacle. Internal evidence (first-person passages, detailed legal material, wilderness topography) and early dating arguments place composition in the 15th–13th century BC. The narrative directly continues Genesis 46–50.

Verses 1–9 — The Burning Bush; Holy Ground; God Sees Israel's Suffering
1Now Moses was keeping the flock of his father-in-law, Jethro, the priest of Midian, and he led his flock to the west side of the wilderness and came to Horeb, the mountain of God.2And the angel of the LORD appeared to him in a flame of fire out of the midst of a bush. He looked, and behold, the bush was burning, yet it was not consumed.4When the LORD saw that he turned aside to see, God called to him out of the bush, "Moses, Moses!" And he said, "Here I am."5Then he said, "Do not come near; take your sandals off your feet, for the place on which you are standing is holy ground."

Hebrew Word Study

מַלְאַךְ יְהוָה malʾak YHWH — Angel of the LORD: The Angel of the LORD is a theophanic figure throughout the OT — simultaneously distinct from God and identified with him. In v.2 the Angel appears; in v.4 "God called." Many theologians identify the Angel of the LORD as a pre-incarnate appearance of the Son.

אֲדַמַת קֹדֶשׁ ʾădat qōdeš — holy ground: The ground is holy because God is present, not the reverse. Holiness is not an inherent property of Sinai/Horeb — it is a relational property derived from divine presence. The shoe-removal is the physical acknowledgment of that asymmetry.

הַסְּנֶה hasseneh — the bush: The burning bush (seneh) may contain a wordplay on Sinai — the same consonants. The location where God first appears becomes the location where the Law will be given.

Historical Context

Horeb and Sinai are used interchangeably for the same mountain (Horeb in Deuteronomy, Sinai in Exodus/Leviticus). The traditional site is Jebel Musa in the southern Sinai Peninsula, though some scholars propose alternative locations in northwestern Arabia (Midian). The "west side of the wilderness" from Midian would place Moses in the Sinai region — consistent with traditional identification.

Context

The burning bush is the hinge of the entire book. Moses has spent 40 years being stripped of his Egyptian identity and palace status — a shepherd, a nobody, tending someone else's flocks. God meets him at the exact moment he has nothing left to rely on but God. The commission that follows is not given to the palace Moses but to the wilderness Moses. Formation precedes commission.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kings 19:8Elijah flees to Horeb — the same mountain — to meet God after his own crisis. The mountain is the place of divine encounter for both great prophets.
  • Acts 7:30–34Stephen: Moses sees the burning bush at Horeb "in the wilderness of Mount Sinai." The Angel of the Lord appears.
  • Heb 12:29"Our God is a consuming fire" — the burning-but-not-consumed bush is the visual statement of God's nature: holiness that does not destroy what it indwells.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:2

The bush burns but is not consumed. This is the defining image of God's presence: holy fire that does not destroy. Israel will be indwelt by God's presence — the pillar of fire, the Shekinah in the Tabernacle — without being annihilated. The same pattern appears at Pentecost: tongues of fire rest on the disciples without consuming them. God's presence is fire that refines rather than destroys those he claims.

3:4–5

God calls Moses by name — twice ("Moses, Moses!") — before issuing any command. The double-name call signals urgency and intimacy (cf. "Abraham, Abraham!" in Gen 22:11; "Samuel, Samuel!" in 1 Sam 3:10). Then, before a word of commission: "Take off your sandals." Holiness is the first category. Moses must understand who he is encountering before he can receive what he is being sent to do.

Verses 10–22 — The Commission; "I AM WHO I AM"; Moses' First Objection
11"But Moses said to God, "Who am I that I should go to Pharaoh and bring the children of Israel out of Egypt?"12He said, "But I will be with you, and this shall be the sign for you, that I have sent you: when you have brought the people out of Egypt, you shall serve God on this mountain."13Then Moses said to God, "If I come to the people of Israel and say to them, 'The God of your fathers has sent me to you,' and they ask me, 'What is his name?' what shall I say to them?"14God said to Moses, "I AM WHO I AM." And he said, "Say this to the people of Israel: 'I AM has sent me to you.'"

Hebrew Word Study

אֶהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶהְיֶה ʾehyeh ʾăšer ʾehyeh — I AM WHO I AM: The most profound divine self-disclosure in the OT. From the root hāyāh (to be). Grammatically: first-person imperfect — "I will be what I will be" or "I am that I am." The name asserts self-existence, self-determination, and covenant presence. This is the name Jesus appropriates in John's seven "I AM" statements.

יְהוָה YHWH — the LORD: The Tetragrammaton — the personal covenant name of God. Related to ʾehyeh: "He who is/will be." Used throughout Exodus as the name by which God acts in redemption. Never to be pronounced carelessly — the name carries the weight of every covenant promise.

Context

The divine name revelation is the theological summit of chapter 3. Moses asks for a name because names in the ancient world carried authority and identity — to know God's name is to be able to invoke him, to understand his character. The answer "I AM WHO I AM" is simultaneously a revelation and a refusal: God cannot be fully defined or controlled by a name. He is the self-existent one who defines himself by his own freedom and faithfulness.

Cross-Reference

  • John 8:58"Before Abraham was, I AM." Jesus uses the Exodus divine name in the present tense for himself — the most explicit divine claim in John's Gospel.
  • John 6:35; 8:12; 10:9; 11:25; 14:6; 15:1; 15:5The seven "I AM" statements in John — each appropriates the divine name of Exodus 3:14.
  • Rev 1:8"I am the Alpha and the Omega, who is and who was and who is to come" — the Revelation's exposition of the Exodus name.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:11

Moses' first objection — "Who am I?" — is the right question asked in the wrong direction. God's answer is not "you are capable" or "you are qualified." It is: "I will be with you." The commission is not based on Moses' adequacy but on divine presence. This reframes every subsequent objection: the question is never Moses' ability but God's presence and promise.

3:14

The divine name "I AM WHO I AM" is the most important theological statement in Exodus. God defines himself by his own self-existence — he is not contingent on anything outside himself. He is not "the God of the mountain" or "the God of storms" (like Canaanite deities tied to forces). He simply IS. And because he IS, his promises are as certain as his existence. Every "I will" in the covenant is grounded in the "I AM" of his nature.

Ancient Sources

Philo, Life of Moses
Philo's philosophical reading of "I AM WHO I AM" — God as pure Being (to on) — became the bridge between Hebrew theology and Greek philosophy, influencing all subsequent Jewish and Christian discussion of divine nature.
The name of Exod 3:14 generated the entire tradition of divine ontology in both traditions.
Dead Sea Scrolls, 4QExod
Fragments of Exodus from Qumran confirm the antiquity of the text and show minor variants — the name revelation is consistent across all manuscript traditions.
Textual stability of the theophany narrative across 2,000+ years of transmission.

Reception History

Aquinas, Summa Theologica I.13
Aquinas uses Exod 3:14 as the foundation for the doctrine of divine simplicity: God's essence is existence itself — esse ipsum subsistens. "I AM" is the most proper name of God.
Calvin, Institutes 1.1
The burning bush and the divine name are for Calvin the paradigm of the knowledge of God: we cannot know God in himself, only as he reveals himself to us. The name is revelation, not definition.

The chapter's structure moves from theophany (vv.1–5) through commission (vv.6–10) to objection and response (vv.11–12) to the name revelation (vv.13–14). The name is the answer to every objection: the one who IS guarantees what he promises.

Intertextual Threading

Gen 28:15→Exod 3:12Echo
"I will be with you" — God's promise to Jacob at Bethel is the exact promise given to Moses at Horeb. The covenant presence-formula is consistent across centuries.
Exod 3:14→John 8:58Fulfillment
"I AM WHO I AM" → "Before Abraham was, I AM." Jesus appropriates the divine name of Exodus 3 as his own identity claim.
Exod 3:5→Josh 5:15Echo
Joshua meets the commander of the LORD's army: "Take off your sandal from your foot, for the place where you are standing is holy." The identical command at the threshold of the conquest.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 3 is the theological summit of the book's opening. The burning bush, the divine name, and the commission together establish the foundation for everything that follows: God is the self-existent covenant-keeper who acts in history, and his presence is the only qualification the deliverer needs.
