Exodus 27

The Bronze Altar; the Courtyard; the Perpetual Lamp

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. Moses is not only the principal character of Exodus — he is its author, and the events recorded are largely his own experience.

When written

Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC. The events of Exodus span approximately 80 years (Moses's birth to the Tabernacle's completion), but the book itself was written after the fact — most likely during the years at Sinai and the wilderness wandering that followed. The Tabernacle instructions and legal material (chs.19–40) may have been committed to writing earliest, as they were immediately actionable.

What prompted it

Israel needed a founding narrative — a record of how God delivered them from 430 years of slavery, made them his covenant people at Sinai, and established the Tabernacle as his dwelling among them. Exodus is the story of liberation, but more than that, it is the story of covenant formation and divine habitation. It answers: How did God redeem us? What does he require of us? And how can a holy God dwell with a sinful people?

Verses 1–8 — The Bronze Altar
1"Build an altar of acacia wood, three cubits high; it is to be square, five cubits long and five cubits wide.2Make a horn at each of the four corners, so that the horns and the altar are of one piece, and overlay the altar with bronze.3Make all its utensils of bronze—its pots to remove the ashes, and its shovels, sprinkling bowls, meat forks and firepans.4Make a grating for it, a bronze network, and make a bronze ring at each of the four corners of the network.5Put it under the ledge of the altar so that it is halfway up the altar.6Make poles of acacia wood for the altar and overlay them with bronze.7The poles are to be inserted into the rings so they will be on two sides of the altar when it is carried.8Make the altar hollow, out of boards. It is to be made just as you were shown on the mountain."

Hebrew Word Study

מִזְבֵּחַ mizbēaḥ — altar: From zābaḥ (to slaughter, sacrifice). The altar is the place of slaughter — where life is given so that life may be sustained. The bronze altar is the gateway to the Tabernacle: you cannot enter God's presence without first passing through the place of sacrifice.

קַרְנֹתָיו qarnōtāyw — its horns: The four horns of the altar are corners of power and sanctuary — a person could cling to them for refuge (1 Kgs 1:50). Blood was applied to the horns of the altar in the sin offering (Lev 4:7). They represent the altar's authority to atone.

Context

The bronze altar stood in the outer courtyard — the first furnishing encountered by anyone entering the Tabernacle complex. Its placement is theological: there is no access to God except through the altar. The journey from the gate to the Most Holy Place required passing the altar first. The NT equivalent: "No one comes to the Father except through me" (John 14:6) — the cross is the altar you must pass.

Cross-Reference

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:1–2

The bronze altar is the first object inside the Tabernacle gate. You cannot bypass it to reach the sanctuary. The theology of approach is built into the architecture: atonement first, then access. This is why the cross is not incidental to Christianity — it is the entry point. There is no path to the Father that does not go through the place of sacrifice.

27:2

The horns of the altar — one at each corner, of one piece with the altar. Blood was applied to these horns (Lev 4:7,18,25,34) in the sin offering. They were the points where atonement was most concentrated. To flee to the altar and grasp its horns (1 Kgs 1:50) was to claim the altar's atoning power as sanctuary. There is safety at the place of sacrifice.

Verses 9–21 — The Courtyard; the Perpetual Lamp
9"Make a courtyard for the tabernacle. The south side shall be a hundred cubits long and is to have curtains of finely twisted linen,10with twenty posts and twenty bronze bases and with silver hooks and bands on the posts.11The north side shall also be a hundred cubits long and is to have curtains, with twenty posts and twenty bronze bases and with silver hooks and bands on the posts.12"The west end of the courtyard shall be fifty cubits wide and have curtains, with ten posts and ten bases.13"The east end of the courtyard shall also be fifty cubits wide.14Curtains fifteen cubits long are to be on one side of the entrance, with three posts and three bases,15and curtains fifteen cubits long are to be on the other side, with three posts and three bases.16"For the entrance to the courtyard, provide a curtain twenty cubits long, of blue, purple and scarlet yarn and finely twisted linen—the work of an embroiderer—with four posts and four bases.17All the posts around the courtyard are to have silver bands and hooks, and bronze bases.18The courtyard shall be a hundred cubits long and fifty cubits wide, with curtains of finely twisted linen five cubits high, and with bronze bases.19All the other articles used in the service of the tabernacle, whatever their function, including all the tent pegs for it and those for the courtyard, are to be of bronze.20"Command the Israelites to bring you clear oil of pressed olives for the light so that the lamps may be kept burning.21In the tent of meeting, outside the curtain that shields the ark of the covenant law, Aaron and his sons are to keep the lamps burning before the Lord from evening till morning. This is to be a lasting ordinance among the Israelites for the generations to come."

Context

The courtyard (100 x 50 cubits) enclosed the entire Tabernacle complex and provided a defined sacred space distinct from the common camp. Its linen curtains were five cubits high — tall enough that no one could peer over into the sacred space but not so tall as to be overwhelming. The entrance on the east side (always the sunrise side in the ancient world) was the only way in. The perpetual lamp (vv.20–21) is the lampstand's continuous function: God's house is never dark.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 27:4"To gaze on the beauty of the Lord and to seek him in his temple." The courtyard was the space where Israelites encountered the Tabernacle — the outer precinct of divine dwelling.
  • John 10:9"I am the gate; whoever enters through me will be saved." The single gate of the courtyard → Christ as the single way of access.

Places & Geography

The Courtyard
Outer enclosure of the Tabernacle complex
100 x 50 cubits; linen curtains; one entrance facing east. The only defined sacred space accessible to all Israelites — the outer court was as far as most could come.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:16

The one gate of the courtyard — embroidered, beautiful, prominent. There was only one way in. The theology is unmistakeable: there is one access to God's presence, one gate, one entrance. Jesus says "I am the gate; whoever enters through me will be saved." The Tabernacle's single gate is one of the OT's clearest types of Christ as the exclusive way of access.

27:20–21

The perpetual lamp — never extinguished, tended evening to morning by the priests. The lampstand in God's house was always burning. Darkness never fell in the Tabernacle. The light of God's presence is permanent, continuous, unceasing. The priests' job was to keep it burning. In Christ, who is the true light of the world, the lamp burns eternally without human tending.

Ancient Sources

ANE Courtyard Temples
Ancient Near Eastern temples regularly featured an outer courtyard separating the sacred interior from the profane world. Egyptian temples like Karnak and Luxor have successive courtyards of increasing holiness leading to the innermost sanctuary.
The Tabernacle courtyard fits within the known ANE temple typology.
Perpetual Fire
Sacred fires that were never extinguished are attested in ancient religions from Rome (the Vestal flame) to Persia. Israel's perpetual lamp is distinct: it is not a magical fire but a priestly ordinance serving the practical function of illuminating God's house while signifying his continuous presence.
The perpetual light is functionally intelligible within its cultural context while carrying unique covenant significance.

Reception History

John 10:9
The single gate of the courtyard as the type of Christ as the one gate: "I am the gate; whoever enters through me will be saved." Early church commentators universally made this connection.
Ps 27:4
Dwelling in the house of the Lord — the courtyard is as far as the people could come. Yet the Psalmist longs to dwell there. The outer court of the earthly tabernacle was the fullest access available to most of Israel.
