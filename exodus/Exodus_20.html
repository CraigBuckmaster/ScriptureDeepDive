Exodus 20

The Ten Commandments; the People's Fear; Laws on Altars

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–11 — The Ten Commandments: The First Table
1And God spoke all these words:2"I am the Lord your God, who brought you out of Egypt, out of the land of slavery.3"You shall have no other gods before me.4"You shall not make for yourself an image in the form of anything in heaven above or on the earth beneath or in the waters below.5You shall not bow down to them or worship them; for I, the Lord your God, am a jealous God, punishing the children for the sin of the parents to the third and fourth generation of those who hate me,6but showing love to a thousand generations of those who love me and keep my commandments.7"You shall not misuse the name of the Lord your God, for the Lord will not hold anyone guiltless who misuses his name.8"Remember the Sabbath day by keeping it holy.9Six days you shall labor and do all your work,10but the seventh day is a sabbath to the Lord your God. On it you shall not do any work, neither you, nor your son or daughter, nor your male or female servant, nor your animals, nor any foreigner residing in your towns.11For in six days the Lord made the heavens and the earth, the sea, and all that is in them, but he rested on the seventh day. Therefore the Lord blessed the Sabbath day and made it holy.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
20:2

MacArthur: I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of Egypt -- the commandments begin with grace, not law. The redemption precedes the obligation. Indicative (I have redeemed you) before imperative (therefore obey).

20:5-6

MacArthur: Punishing the children to the third and fourth generation... showing love to a thousand generations. The contrast emphasises the overwhelming asymmetry of divine mercy over divine judgment.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The Decalogue's preamble ("I am the LORD your God who brought you out of Egypt") is the key to understanding the entire law. The commandments are not a mechanism for earning relationship with God — they are the description of life for those already in relationship. Every commandment assumes the Exodus has already happened. You are free; here is how free people live before God and each other.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 5:6–21The Decalogue repeated in Moses' farewell sermon — with slight variations, particularly the Sabbath rationale (Exodus: creation; Deuteronomy: redemption from Egypt).
  • Matt 22:37–40Jesus' summary: love God with all your heart, soul, mind (commandments 1–4) and love your neighbour as yourself (5–10).
  • Rom 3:20The law reveals sin: "through the law comes knowledge of sin." The Decalogue does not save; it diagnoses.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:2

The preamble 'I am the LORD your God who brought you out of Egypt' is the covenantal self-identification that grounds all the commandments. Sarna notes its structure matches ancient suzerainty treaty preambles — the great king identifies himself, recites his historical relationship with the vassal, then specifies treaty terms.

20:8-11

The Sabbath command's grounding in creation (v.11) distinguishes Exodus from Deuteronomy's grounding in the Exodus (Deut 5:15). Sarna notes both grounds are valid and complementary: the Sabbath is rooted in both creation order and covenant redemption. The two versions together establish the Sabbath as simultaneously cosmic and historical.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:2–17

The Decalogue's structure moves from vertical (God-Israel, commandments 1–4) to horizontal (Israel-neighbour, commandments 5–10). Alter notes this is not a random list but a structured theological architecture: right relationship with God is the foundation; right relationship with neighbour is the expression.

20:8-11

The fourth commandment is the longest of the ten — the single commandment requiring an extended theological explanation (the creation week foundation). Alter notes the length signals its complexity: unlike the prohibitions, the Sabbath command requires positive description of what constitutes observance and why.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:2

Calvin: 'Before he lays down the rule of holy and righteous living, God begins with gratuitous adoption... God does not demand of us what is due him until he has first bound us to himself by his blessing.' The law follows grace; obligation follows gift.

20:8-11

Calvin on the fourth commandment: the Sabbath has a threefold purpose — meditation on creation, rest for servants, and sacred assembly for worship. The first purpose (creation meditation) grounds the day's significance; the second (servants' rest) grounds its social ethics; the third (assembly) grounds its covenant function.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:3

The NET note on 'before me' (al panai, literally 'before my face') discusses whether this means 'in addition to me' or 'in my presence.' The note favours 'in my presence' — the commandment is relational, not ontological. It does not assert other gods do not exist; it forbids their worship by those in covenant with YHWH.

20:8-11

The NET note on 'remember the Sabbath day to keep it holy' discusses the verbal nuance: 'remember' (zakhor) implies ongoing cognitive attention, not merely initial compliance. The note traces the Sabbath commandment through its Deuteronomic parallel ('observe,' shamar) and discusses the complementary emphases.

Verses 12–21 — The Second Table; the People's Fear
12"Honor your father and your mother, so that you may live long in the land the Lord your God is giving you.13"You shall not murder.14"You shall not commit adultery.15"You shall not steal.16"You shall not give false testimony against your neighbor.17"You shall not covet your neighbor's house. You shall not covet your neighbor's wife, or his male or female servant, his ox or donkey, or anything that belongs to your neighbor."18When the people saw the thunder and lightning and heard the trumpet and saw the mountain in smoke, they trembled with fear. They stayed at a distance19and said to Moses, "Speak to us yourself and we will listen. But do not have God speak to us or we will die."20Moses said to the people, "Do not be afraid. God has come to test you, so that the fear of God will be with you to keep you from sinning."21The people remained at a distance, while Moses approached the thick darkness where God was.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
20:12

MacArthur: Honor your father and mother. The fifth commandment is the first with a promise (Eph 6:2). The honour of parents is the foundation of social stability; the society that does not honour parents will not survive across generations.

20:17

MacArthur: You shall not covet -- the last commandment addresses the heart rather than the action. Paul (Rom 7:7-8) identifies this commandment as the one that revealed his sin to him. Coveting is the internal root of every external violation.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The fifth commandment ("honour your father and your mother") is the bridge between the two tables — it is the first social commandment, concerning the most fundamental social institution (the family). Paul identifies it as "the first commandment with a promise" (Eph 6:2). The commandments 6–10 describe the minimum conditions for community life: life protected, marriage protected, property protected, reputation protected, and the heart guarded against desire that destroys.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 5:21–48The Sermon on the Mount extends the Decalogue to the heart — anger is murder; lust is adultery; covetousness is idolatry.
  • Rom 13:8–10"The commandments... are summed up in this word: You shall love your neighbour as yourself." Paul shows the second table as the shape of neighbourly love.
  • James 2:10–11Violating one commandment makes one guilty of all — the unity of the Decalogue as a single covenant obligation.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:18–21

Sarna notes the people's request for Moses as mediator is the constitutive moment of Israelite prophecy. The people cannot sustain direct divine encounter; they need a representative who goes between. Moses accepts: he 'approached the thick darkness where God was.' The mediator enters what the people cannot enter.

20:19-21

The people's request for mediated revelation constitutes the legal basis of the prophetic office. Sarna notes the theological significance: from this point forward, the divine word comes to Israel through human mediators. The directness of Sinai is immediately qualified by the people's own request — the covenant was too overwhelming for unmediated reception.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:18–21

The shift from direct divine speech (Decalogue) to mediated speech (Moses) is the chapter's structural pivot. Alter notes the people's request — 'speak to us yourself' — is candid human limitation. Moses' role as mediator is from this point the structure of Israelite covenant life.

20:19-21

The people's fear and distance from Sinai is the narrative's explanation for why they need a mediator. Alter notes the irony: the God who came down to speak directly with his people (v.20: 'God has come to test you') now speaks through Moses because the people cannot bear direct speech. The Incarnation reverses this: God becomes approachable.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:19

Calvin: 'God was pleased to accommodate himself to their infirmity, and to supply them with a teacher of their own kind.' The incarnation of Christ is the ultimate fulfilment of this accommodation — God provides a mediator who is fully human, approachable without the terror of direct divine encounter.

20:18-21

Calvin: The people's request for Moses as mediator was granted because of human weakness, not as a rebuke. God graciously accommodated their inability to sustain direct encounter. The accommodation prefigures the Incarnation: God provides a mediator of human form precisely because humans cannot sustain the direct divine encounter.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:21

The NET note on 'thick darkness' (araphel) explains it is a more intense darkness than the earlier 'thick cloud' (anan). The note cites Ps 18:11 ('he made darkness his hiding place') — the divine presence is simultaneously light (pillar of fire) and darkness (araphel), transcending both categories.

20:18-21

The NET note on Moses approaching 'the thick darkness where God was' discusses the paradox: God is light (Ps 27:1) yet Moses enters darkness to meet him. The note traces the tradition of divine hiddenness-in-light through 1 Kgs 8:12 (Solomon: 'the LORD has said he would dwell in thick darkness') and Ps 18:11.

Verses 22–26 — Laws About Altars
22Then the Lord said to Moses, "Tell the Israelites this: 'You have seen for yourselves that I have spoken to you from heaven:23Do not make any gods to be alongside me; do not make for yourselves gods of silver or gods of gold.24"'Make an altar of earth for me and sacrifice on it your burnt offerings and fellowship offerings, your sheep and goats and your cattle. Wherever I cause my name to be honored, I will come to you and bless you.25If you make an altar of stones for me, do not build it with dressed stones, for you will defile it if you use a tool on it.26And do not go up to my altar on steps, or your private parts may be exposed.'"

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
20:24-25

MacArthur: The altar law establishes that the fundamental covenant approach to God requires sacrifice and must avoid human elaboration. Natural uncut stones prevent the altar from becoming a work of human artisanship -- worship must not be a display of human creativity.

20:22-26

MacArthur: The altar instructions immediately following the Ten Commandments are not supplementary but interpretive. The prohibition of worked stone and steps grounds the worship of YHWH in simplicity and accessibility. The altar must be approachable by all without requiring elaborate craftsmanship — the covenant God meets people where they are.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:25

The prohibition of cut stone for altars protests against elaboration of religious infrastructure. Sarna notes dressed stone was used in Canaanite high places — the prohibition distinguishes Israelite worship from pagan religious architecture. God does not need elaborate stone monuments; simple altars suffice.

20:24-26

The altar law immediately following the Decalogue grounds covenant worship in simplicity and locality. Sarna notes the deliberate contrast with Canaanite cultic elaboration: YHWH requires only earth or unhewn stone, not the dressed stone and elaborate architecture of pagan high places. Simplicity in worship honours the worshipped.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:24

The promise 'wherever I cause my name to be remembered' establishes the divine name as the principle of sacred presence — God present wherever his name is legitimately invoked, not confined to a particular geography. This will eventually be centralised (Deut 12) and then universalised (Matt 18:20).

20:24-26

The altar instructions close the Decalogue section with immediate practical application. Alter notes the movement from the most abstract (the commandments) to the most concrete (how to build an altar). The covenant is not only heard but enacted; worship is not only commanded but given a physical form, however simple.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:24

Calvin: 'God promises his blessing wherever men legitimately call upon him.' The promise is conditional on legitimate worship but geographically open. Calvin uses this against restricting valid worship to ecclesiastically defined spaces.

20:24-26

Calvin: The altar law teaches that God is not worshipped by human inventions but by his own prescriptions. The prohibition of dressed stone and steps is not arbitrary: elaboration in worship tends toward showmanship and self-display. God prefers the simple worship that depends entirely on his presence, not on the worshipper's craft.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:25

The NET note explains iron tools were associated with warfare. The prohibition may reflect an ancient taboo against bringing implements of violence into sacred space — consistent with 1 Kgs 6:7 where the temple was built without iron tools heard on site.

20:24-26

The NET note on 'at every place where I cause my name to be honoured' discusses whether this anticipates the Deuteronomy 12 centralisation or permits pre-centralisation multiple worship sites. The note surveys the scholarly debate and concludes the verse establishes a principle (legitimate altar = where God designates) that Deut 12 will later specify.

Ancient Sources

Hittite Suzerainty Treaty Form
The Decalogue precisely follows the Hittite treaty structure: (1) preamble identifying the sovereign; (2) historical prologue of the king's benefits; (3) obligations. The form was known throughout the ANE and gave the Decalogue immediate intelligibility as a covenant document.
The Decalogue is not just moral philosophy; it is a covenant treaty between the divine King and his redeemed people.
The Numbering Question
Jewish, Protestant, and Catholic/Lutheran traditions number the commandments differently. The most common Protestant numbering (used above) takes vv.3 and 4–6 as two separate commandments. The Catholic/Lutheran tradition combines them into one and splits the coveting (v.17) into two.
The content is identical across traditions; only the numbering differs.

Reception History

Augustine, On the Spirit and the Letter
The Decalogue written on stone vs. the new covenant law written on the heart (Jer 31:33; 2 Cor 3:3) — the stone tablets point toward their own transcendence.
Luther, Large Catechism
The Decalogue as the foundation of Christian ethics — the law that kills (shows sin) in order that grace may give life. The commandments are not abolished but fulfilled in Christ and written on the heart by the Spirit.

Literary Structure

The Preamble: Covenant Identity Before Command20:2I am the LORD your God who brought you out of Egypt. The command begins with identity, not obligation. The covenant relationship (I am... your God) precedes and grounds every command that follows. Obedience flows from identity.
The People Request Mediation: The Prophetic Office Founded20:18-20The people draw back and ask Moses to be their mediator. The terrifying directness of the Sinai theophany is immediately qualified by the people's own request -- the prophetic office originates at the people's recognition of their own inability to sustain direct divine encounter.

Scholarly Debates

How Many Commandments and How Are They Numbered?
Protestant/Reformed numbering
Calvin, Reformed tradition
Commandment 1: no other gods. Commandment 2: no images. Commandments 3–10 follow sequentially.
Catholic/Lutheran numbering
Augustine, Luther
Commandments 1 and 2 combined; commandment 10 split into two (coveting wife; coveting property).
Jewish traditional numbering
Philo, Maimonides
The preamble 'I am the LORD your God' is Commandment 1. 'No other gods/no images' is Commandment 2.
The different numbering traditions reflect genuine exegetical choices. None represents a text-critical variant. The theological content is identical across all three systems.
