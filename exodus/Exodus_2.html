Exodus 2

The Birth of Moses; Flight to Midian; God Remembers

Hebrew

Timeline

Exodus is traditionally attributed to Moses, with near-universal ancient attestation in both Jewish and Christian sources. The book covers the period from Israel's oppression in Egypt through the construction of the Tabernacle. Internal evidence (first-person passages, detailed legal material, wilderness topography) and early dating arguments place composition in the 15th–13th century BC. The narrative directly continues Genesis 46–50.

Verses 1–10 — The Birth of Moses; The Basket; Pharaoh's Daughter
1Now a man from the house of Levi went and took as his wife a Levite woman.2The woman conceived and bore a son, and when she saw that he was a fine child, she hid him three months.3When she could hide him no longer, she took for him a basket made of bulrushes and daubed it with bitumen and pitch. She put the child in it and placed it among the reeds by the river bank.5Now the daughter of Pharaoh came down to bathe at the river, while her attendants walked beside the river. She saw the basket among the reeds and sent her servant woman, and she took it.10When the child grew older, she brought him to Pharaoh's daughter, and he became her son. She named him Moses, "Because," she said, "I drew him out of the water."

Hebrew Word Study

תֵּבָה tēbāh — basket/ark: The same Hebrew word used for Noah's ark (Gen 6:14). This is not coincidence — Moses' basket is a second ark, carrying the covenant's deliverer through the waters of judgment just as Noah's ark carried humanity's remnant. Only two uses of tēbāh in the entire OT.

מֹשֶׁה mōšeh — Moses: The name given by Pharaoh's daughter, explained as "I drew him out of the water" (māšāh = to draw out). Irony: the daughter of the man who ordered Hebrew babies drowned names the deliverer with a water-rescue etymology.

כֹּמֶר וָזָפֶת kōmer wāzāpet — bitumen and pitch: The same waterproofing materials used across Mesopotamia for river vessels. The detail is authentic to the Nile Delta environment — pitch-sealed reed baskets are archaeologically attested.

Historical Context

Egyptian princess bathing at the Nile is consistent with royal practice — the Nile was sacred to Egyptians, and bathing in it was both practical and ritual. Egyptian court records document the adoption of foreign children into royal households. The motif of a hero preserved in a waterproofed basket appears in the Sargon of Akkad legend (c.2300 BC), though Moses' account is distinct in its theological framing.

Context

The birth narrative of Moses is structured to show divine providence working through human actions without explicit divine intervention — God is not named in 2:1–10, yet every detail works toward the preservation of the one he will commission. The mother hides him; the sister watches; Pharaoh's daughter has compassion; the sister brokers the nursing arrangement. Moses ends up raised in the palace on his own mother's milk, at Egypt's expense.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 6:14Tēbāh — Noah's ark and Moses' basket are the only two uses. Both carry covenant survivors through water.
  • Heb 11:23Moses' parents "hid him for three months... not afraid of the king's edict." Faith as the motive for the basket.
  • Acts 7:21–22Stephen: Moses was educated in all the wisdom of the Egyptians — palace upbringing prepares the deliverer.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:2–3

Moses' mother's action is described in Heb 11 as faith. She sees her son is "fine" (tôb — the same word used of God's creation in Gen 1) and acts. The basket in the Nile is not desperation; it is faith in the God who can preserve a child in a pitch-sealed reed box in the river where babies are supposed to drown.

2:10

Moses is raised in Pharaoh's court — educated, positioned, prepared. The very institution that ordered his death pays for his upbringing. This is the pattern: God uses the enemy's resources to train his deliverer. Moses will spend 40 years in the palace, 40 in the wilderness, and 40 leading Israel. The palace years give him access and preparation he will need.

Verses 11–25 — Moses Kills the Egyptian; Flees to Midian; God Remembers
11One day, when Moses had grown up, he went out to his people and looked on their burdens, and he saw an Egyptian beating a Hebrew, one of his people.12He looked this way and that, and seeing no one, he struck down the Egyptian and hid him in the sand.15When Pharaoh heard of it, he sought to kill Moses. But Moses fled from Pharaoh and stayed in the land of Midian. And he sat down by a well.23During those many days the king of Egypt died, and the people of Israel groaned because of their slavery and cried out for help. Their cry for rescue from slavery came up to God.24And God heard their groaning, and God remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac, and with Jacob.

Hebrew Word Study

וַיִּזְכֹּר אֱלֹהִים wayyizkōr ʾĕlōhîm — God remembered: Remembered (zākar) is covenant language — not "became aware of again" but "acted on what he had committed to." God's memory is always covenantal action. The same verb is used when God remembered Noah (Gen 8:1) and when he will remember his covenant at the end of Leviticus.

בְּרִית bĕrît — covenant: The covenant remembered is tri-partite: Abraham, Isaac, Jacob. The Exodus is not a new beginning — it is the fulfilment of promises made centuries earlier. Every plague, every miracle, every step of the Exodus is covenant-keeping.

Context

Moses' murder of the Egyptian and flight to Midian is a compressed identity crisis: he is neither fully Egyptian (he identifies with his suffering people) nor accepted by his Hebrew brothers ("who made you a ruler and judge over us?"). In Midian he is a nobody — a shepherd, a stranger, sitting by a well. The 40 years in Midian strip away the palace, the status, the Egyptian identity. God meets him at the lowest point.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 8:1God remembered Noah — same verb, same covenant structure. Divine memory = covenant action.
  • Acts 7:25Stephen: Moses "supposed that his brothers would understand that God was giving them salvation by his hand, but they did not understand." The first rejection foreshadows Israel's rejection of Christ.
  • Heb 11:24–26Moses chose to identify with the suffering people rather than enjoy Pharaoh's court — "considering the reproach of Christ greater wealth than the treasures of Egypt."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:23–24

The turn of the chapter is the most important theological pivot in Exodus. After Moses's flight, after Pharaoh's death, after years of silence — "their cry came up to God. And God heard... and God remembered his covenant." The covenant is the explanation for everything that follows. The plagues, the Exodus, the wilderness — all of it is covenant-keeping. God is not responding to Israel's cry as though it surprised him; he is activating a promise made to Abraham 400 years earlier.

2:12

Moses' impulsive killing of the Egyptian is the act of a man who has the right impulse (justice for his people) but the wrong method and the wrong timing. He is not yet ready. 40 years of wilderness formation — tending sheep, learning patience, becoming a nobody — are required before God can use him. The act is neither approved nor condemned by the narrator; it is simply the beginning of Moses' education.

Ancient Sources

Sargon Birth Legend
Akkadian text (c.2300 BC) describes Sargon of Akkad's birth: placed in a basket of rushes sealed with pitch, set on the river, rescued by a water-drawer.
The Moses birth story shares surface features with the Sargon legend but inverts its theology — Moses is placed by a mother of faith, not abandoned; rescued by royalty who adopts him into the very power he will confront.
Egyptian Adoption Records
Middle Kingdom papyri document foreign children being adopted into Egyptian households, including royal ones.
The adoption of Moses by Pharaoh's daughter is consistent with known Egyptian practice.

Reception History

Philo, Life of Moses
Philo's biography of Moses emphasises the providential nature of every detail — the basket, the sister, the princess. He reads the whole sequence as divine choreography with human actors.
Hebrews 11:23–26
The NT treats both Moses' parents' faith (hiding him) and Moses' own choice (refusing palace identity) as paradigm acts of faith under pressure.

Literary Structure

Verses 23–25 contain four divine verbs: heard, remembered, saw, knew. Each is a covenant verb — God activating his commitment to Abraham. The chapter's structure moves from human crisis to divine response.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 2 is the chapter of hidden preparation. God prepares his deliverer through palace education, identity crisis, and wilderness formation — without appearing or speaking until chapter 3. The final verses (23–25) are the theological key: God's covenant memory is the engine of the Exodus.
