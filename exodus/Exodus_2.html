Exodus 2

The Birth of Moses; His Flight to Midian

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–10 — The Birth and Rescue of Moses
1Now a man of the tribe of Levi married a Levite woman,2and she became pregnant and gave birth to a son. When she saw that he was a fine child, she hid him for three months.3But when she could hide him no longer, she got a papyrus basket for him and coated it with tar and pitch. Then she placed the child in it and put it among the reeds along the bank of the Nile.4His sister stood at a distance to see what would happen to him.5Then Pharaoh's daughter went down to the Nile to bathe, and her attendants were walking along the riverbank. She saw the basket among the reeds and sent her female slave to get it.6She opened it and saw the baby. He was crying, and she felt sorry for him. "This is one of the Hebrew babies," she said.7Then his sister asked Pharaoh's daughter, "Shall I go and get one of the Hebrew women to nurse the baby for you?"8"Yes, go," she answered. So the girl went and got the baby's mother.9Pharaoh's daughter said to her, "Take this baby and nurse him for me, and I will pay you." So the woman took the baby and nursed him.10When the child grew older, she took him to Pharaoh's daughter and he became her son. She named him Moses, saying, "I drew him out of the water."

Hebrew Word Study

תֵּבָה tevah — basket / ark: V.3: The same Hebrew word used for Noah's ark (Gen 6:14). The connection is exact and deliberate: both are vessels of preservation during divine judgment on the world, both are sealed with pitch, both carry the one through whom the next phase of God's saving plan will unfold. Moses in his basket is Noah in his ark — the rescue of one person against a background of destruction.

בַּת פַּרְעֹה bat paroh — daughter of Pharaoh: V.5: Pharaoh's own daughter reverses his decree. The man who commanded the Nile to swallow Hebrew boys has his daughter fish one out. The irony is total: the deliverer of Israel is rescued by the household of the oppressor, raised on Pharaoh's own resources, educated in his own court. God's plan routes straight through the enemy's house.

מֹשֶׁה mosheh — Moses: V.10: The name is explained by a Hebrew wordplay on mashâ ("to draw out"). The daughter of Pharaoh names him: "I drew him out of the water." But the name also anticipates his role: Moses will "draw out" Israel from Egypt. The one drawn from the water will draw the nation through the sea. The name is given by a pagan woman who unknowingly prophecies the deliverer's calling.

Historical Context

The Sargon birth legend (Akkadian, c.2300 BC) describes a baby placed in a basket of rushes, waterproofed with bitumen, set adrift on a river, and rescued by a royal figure — a striking parallel to Moses's birth narrative. This "exposed infant" motif was used across the ancient Near East to mark destined leaders. The parallels show Moses's story using conventional heroic-birth literary forms, while its theological distinctives (YHWH's purpose, the midwives, the mother's faith) remain unique.

Context

Pharaoh's decree was "throw every son into the Nile" (1:22). Jochebed uses the Nile to save her son — the instrument of Pharaoh's genocide becomes the vehicle of Israel's deliverer. Moses's very survival is an ironic reversal of Pharaoh's plan. His adoption into Pharaoh's household gives him the education and access that will later enable him to confront Pharaoh directly.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 6:14The basket (tevah) is the same word as Noah's ark. Both are instruments of preservation through water — both preserve the bearer of God's next covenant purpose.
  • Heb 11:23"By faith Moses' parents hid him for three months after he was born, because they saw he was no ordinary child, and they were not afraid of the king's edict."
  • Acts 7:20–21Stephen: "At this time Moses was born, and he was beautiful before God. And he was brought up for three months in his father's house... and Pharaoh's daughter adopted him."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:3

Jochebed's basket is waterproofed with bitumen and pitch — the same materials used on Noah's ark. The linguistic connection is intentional: the narrator wants us to see that the same God who saved creation through an ark is now saving his covenant people through a tiny basket. The faith that puts a baby in a floating basket on a death-river is the same faith that builds an ark at God's command when there is no rain.

2:10

Pharaoh's daughter names Moses and he becomes her son. The man who will destroy Pharaoh is raised in Pharaoh's house, educated at Pharaoh's expense, and given Pharaoh's name — the irony is architectural. God uses the oppressor's household to prepare the deliverer. Every resource Moses will need to confront Egypt has been provided by Egypt itself.

2:1–2

Sarna notes the extraordinary narrative restraint of Moses' birth account. His parents are unnamed (we learn them only from Exod 6:20); the child is simply "a son." This anonymity is striking given the magnitude of what follows. The phrase "she saw that he was good" (kî ṭôb hûʾ) echoes God's evaluation of creation in Genesis 1 — this child, too, is seen as good. Sarna reads the basket as a deliberate echo of Noah's ark (same word: tēbāh), positioning Moses as a second saved-from-water figure.

2:10

The name Moshe (Moses) is given an Egyptian-context etymology: "I drew him out of the water." Sarna notes the name is actually Egyptian in form (mose/mesu = "child/son" — found in names like Thutmose, Ahmose), but the narrative provides a Hebrew folk etymology from māšāh ("to draw out"). This is common biblical naming practice: the given name and the narrative etymology illuminate the person's role. Moses is the one drawn out — and the one who will draw others out.

2:3–4

Alter marks the careful parallel structure: the mother places the child in the ark; the sister stands watch. Two women act; neither is named yet. The basket itself is described with architectural detail — papyrus, bitumen, pitch — that Alter calls "the narrator's way of insisting on the mother's ingenuity and care." She builds a tiny ark with the same seriousness Noah built the great one. A mother's love expressed through practical skill is the first instrument of Israel's salvation.

2:5–6

Pharaoh's daughter seeing the child crying and "feeling compassion for him" is, Alter notes, a remarkable narrative turn. The instrument of salvation is the enemy's own daughter. Her compassion overrides the royal decree in one sentence — she "saw the child, and behold, the infant was crying." That single visual-auditory detail (the crying baby) undoes Pharaoh's genocide. The narrative suggests that creation's instinct toward life — even in an Egyptian princess — is stronger than imperial death-policy.

2:2

Calvin on "she saw that he was good": "There was something unusual in the infant's appearance which gave token of something great." Calvin allows a providential beauty or distinction visible to the mother — not mere maternal bias but a genuine sign. He cites Heb 11:23 ("by faith Moses, when he was born, was hidden... because they saw that the child was beautiful") as confirming that faith and perception worked together in the parents. Faith sees what grace has prepared.

2:10

Calvin on the name: "For though Moses was by birth a Hebrew, yet in having been brought up in the royal palace, and educated in all the learning of the Egyptians (Acts 7:22), he was so transformed as to appear Egyptian." The name given by Pharaoh's daughter is a kind of irony: the one drawn from the water will draw his people from Egypt. Calvin sees providence threading through every detail — the very name given by the enemy becomes prophetic of the deliverer's work.

2:3

The NET note on תֵּבַת גֹּמֶא (têbat gōmeʾ) — "papyrus basket" — observes this is the only other use of tēbāh (ark/box) in the OT besides Genesis 6–9. The connection to Noah's ark is deliberate: both are structures designed to preserve life through water. The basket is waterproofed with חֵמָר (ḥēmār, bitumen) and זָפֶת (zāpet, pitch), the same waterproofing described for Noah's ark in Gen 6:14. The linguistic and structural parallel positions Moses as a new Noah figure.

2:10

The NET note addresses the name Moshe (מֹשֶׁה): the Hebrew etymology "I drew him out" (מָשִׁיתִהוּ, māšîtihû) is technically a passive participle meaning "drawn out" rather than an active form meaning "one who draws out." Ironically, the passive ("drawn out of water") is transformed in the narrative into the active: Moses becomes the one who draws Israel out of Egypt. The Egyptian background of the name (mose = son/child) is likely the historical origin; the Hebrew etymology is the theological reinterpretation.

2:1–2

The birth of Moses is preceded by the narrator's description of both parents as Levites — the priestly tribe. Moses' lineage is significant: he comes from the tribe that will carry the ark, minister at the tabernacle, and stand closest to the divine presence. The detail that his mother saw he was a "fine child" (tov) — the same word used of God's creation in Genesis 1 — is not merely aesthetic. She sees something of divine quality in this child.

2:3

The "ark of bulrushes" is waterproofed with bitumen and pitch — the same materials used to seal Noah's ark. The parallel is unmistakable. Jochebed builds a tiny ark for a tiny Moses, just as Noah built a great ark. Both preserve the one through whom God's plan will continue. The Nile that Pharaoh commanded to be an instrument of death becomes the river that preserves Israel's deliverer. The weapon is turned.

2:4

Miriam standing at a distance to see what would happen to her brother is one of the Bible's quiet acts of courage. She is not mentioned by name here — just "his sister" — but her watchfulness will lead to the reconnection of Moses with his birth mother. Her role is small in this chapter; it will grow. The child Moses will grow into the leader who leads a nation; the watching girl will grow into the prophetess who leads the women in song (Exod 15:20-21).

2:5–6

Pharaoh's daughter seeing the crying baby is a pivotal scene. She knew immediately it was a Hebrew child (v.6) — yet she chose to rescue him. She had every reason to comply with her father's decree; she chose otherwise. Her compassion (vattechemol, "she had compassion") overrode ethnic hostility and political loyalty. She is the first person in Exodus whose character is described positively — and she is an Egyptian princess, the daughter of the oppressor.

2:9–10

Jochebed is paid wages to nurse her own son — and paid by Pharaoh's daughter from Pharaoh's household. The irony accumulates: the oppressor funds the preservation and education of Israel's deliverer. Moses is raised in the finest court in the ancient world — educated in all the wisdom of Egypt (Acts 7:22) — while knowing (presumably from his mother's nursing) who he is and where he comes from. God prepares his servant comprehensively, using the enemy's resources to do it.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:1–2

Sarna notes the birth narrative uses the minimum of names and details — the parents are not named here, the mother is identified only as a "daughter of Levi." The sparseness is not carelessness; it is narrative technique. The focus is entirely on the child. The extraordinary elements — the fine baby, the three months of concealment, the ark — are foregrounded. The parents' names (Amram and Jochebed) appear only in 6:20, a retrospective insertion. The birth narrative keeps Moses' story unencumbered by genealogy.

2:3

The tevah (basket/ark) is a hapax construction in this context — the term appears elsewhere only for Noah's vessel. Sarna argues the echo is intentional: both Noah and Moses are preserved through waters that bring death to others; both emerge as agents of a new beginning for God's people. The parallel is literary and theological, not incidental. Moses' survival is framed as cosmically significant as the survival of Noah.

2:10

The name "Moses" (Mosheh) is given by Pharaoh's daughter with the explanation "I drew him out of the water." Sarna notes that in Hebrew the name most naturally means "the one who draws out" (active), not "the one drawn out" (passive, which would be moshui). The princess names him with what he will do, not what was done to him. This is either the narrator's subtle wordplay on the name the princess gave, or a divine irony built into the naming — the name she chooses for the rescued child is the name of the rescuer he will become.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:1–2

The birth narrative is stripped of almost all individuating detail — no names for the parents, no specification of the house, no description of the concealment. Alter notes the technique creates a kind of fairy-tale or type-scene quality: a beautiful child is born, hidden, endangered, discovered. The universality of the type-scene (the birth of the hero) means Moses' story plugs into a deep narrative archetype. But the specific details — the Levitical heritage, the Nile decree, the Egyptian princess — make it distinctively this story.

2:3–4

The scene at the river is choreographed with precision. The mother places the basket, the sister watches from a distance. Two women, two stations, two perspectives on the same child. Alter notes the narrative withholds any internal perspective on the mother's feelings — we are shown only the action. The restraint makes the scene more affecting, not less. The reader fills in what the text does not say.

2:5–9

The dialogue in vv.7-9 is the scene's narrative climax, told in rapid exchanges. Miriam's offer — "shall I go and call a Hebrew woman to nurse him?" — is delivered directly to the princess with confident brevity. The princess's response — "yes, go" — is equally terse. Jochebed is then summoned and the princess instructs her to nurse her own child. The irony of the scene is achieved without any editorial comment: the narrator presents it flatly and lets the reader see what the characters apparently do not.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:2

Calvin on Jochebed seeing Moses was a "fine child": "The singular beauty of the child was a kind of presage of his future excellence." He reads the mother's perception as prophetic intuition — she perceives something extraordinary in this child before anyone else does. But he also emphasises the faith aspect: "She was not led away by blind maternal love, but by a divine instinct." Hebrews 11:23 will confirm this reading — it was faith, not sentiment, that drove her decision.

2:5–6

Calvin on Pharaoh's daughter: "God had destined her to perform a great office — to nourish his prophet and the future redeemer of his people, and thus to frustrate the counsel of the tyrant." Her compassion is providentially arranged: she happens to bathe at that place, at that time, and her compassion overrides her political loyalty. For Calvin, this is not chance but governance: "Nothing is more effectual for softening our hearts than the sight of distress."

2:9

The divine irony of Jochebed being paid to nurse her own son is not lost on Calvin: "The providence of God, in a wonderful manner, provided for the safety of the infant... God had even arranged that the boy should be nursed by his own mother, and maintained at the king's expense." The oppressor funds the education of his own downfall. Calvin sees this as characteristic of divine providence — working through the instruments of opposition to achieve its own ends.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:3

The NET note on tevah (basket/ark) discusses the Egyptian loan-word dbt (a box or chest) as the possible etymology, though the Hebrew connection to Noah's ark is unmistakable. The note also explains "bulrushes" (gome) as papyrus reeds, widely used for basket-making in ancient Egypt. The bitumen and pitch (hemar and zefet) waterproofing is paralleled in Egyptian texts on papyrus boat construction. The basket was a real, practical object — waterproofed, buoyant, large enough for an infant.

2:10

The NET note on the name Moses discusses the Egyptian etymology (ms/mose, "son of" or "is born," as in Thut-mose, Ra-messes). The Hebrew popular etymology ("drawn out") is a wordplay on the Egyptian name that also encapsulates Moses' role. Ancient names frequently carried both an official etymology and a popular folk etymology that expressed the name's deeper significance. Both etymologies — Egyptian birth-name and Hebrew theological wordplay — are operating simultaneously in the text.

Verses 11–22 — Moses Kills an Egyptian; Flees to Midian
11One day, after Moses had grown up, he went out to where his own people were and watched them at their hard labor. He saw an Egyptian beating a Hebrew, one of his own people.12Looking this way and that and seeing no one, he killed the Egyptian and hid him in the sand.13The next day he went out and saw two Hebrews fighting. He asked the one in the wrong, "Why are you hitting your fellow Hebrew?"14The man said, "Who made you ruler and judge over us? Are you thinking of killing me as you killed the Egyptian?" Then Moses was afraid and thought, "What I did must have become known."15When Pharaoh heard of this, he tried to kill Moses, but Moses fled from Pharaoh and went to live in Midian, where he sat down by a well.16Now a priest of Midian had seven daughters, and they came to draw water and fill the troughs to water their father's flock.17Some shepherds came along and drove them away, but Moses got up and came to their rescue and watered their flock.18When the girls returned to Reuel their father, he asked them, "Why have you returned so early today?"19They answered, "An Egyptian rescued us from some shepherds. He even drew water for us and watered the flock."20"And where is he?" Reuel asked his daughters. "Why did you leave him? Invite him to have something to eat."21Moses agreed to stay with the man, who gave his daughter Zipporah to Moses in marriage.22Zipporah gave birth to a son, and Moses named him Gershom, saying, "I have become a foreigner in a foreign land."

Hebrew Word Study

וַיִּפֶן כֹּה וָכֹה wayyifen koh vakoh — he looked this way and that: V.12: The gesture reveals Moses' awareness of risk — he is acting outside any legal authority. The looking around is both practical caution and narrative foreshadowing: Moses' act of vigilante justice will not go unnoticed. The buried Egyptian (he hid him in the sand) is a detail of guilty concealment that will return to haunt him.

מִי שָׂמְךָ mi samkha — who made you: V.14: The Hebrew man's challenge — "who made you a ruler and judge over us?" — is the central question of Moses' calling. He has the impulse to deliver but not yet the authority. The challenge is the same one he will face from Israel in the wilderness (Num 16:13). His authority must come from God, not from self-appointment. He is not yet ready; forty more years in Midian will complete his preparation.

גֵּר הָיִיתִי ger hayiti — I have been a sojourner: V.22: Moses names his son Gershom from ger (stranger/sojourner). He is a foreigner in Midian, just as Israel is a foreigner in Egypt. But the word ger also anticipates a theological category: Israel in Egypt is a ger (sojourner); God's law will repeatedly command Israel to treat the ger well because they were strangers in Egypt (Exod 22:21; 23:9). Moses lives what Israel lives.

Historical Context

Midian was a tribal region in the northwestern Arabian Peninsula, east of the Gulf of Aqaba. The Midianites were descendants of Abraham through Keturah (Gen 25:2) — distant relatives of Israel. The identification of Jethro/Reuel as a "priest of Midian" has puzzled scholars: was he a pagan priest or a worshipper of YHWH? His later reception of Moses' God-story with worship (18:10-12) suggests pre-existing monotheistic inclinations. Moses' forty years in Midian were years of formation — shepherd, husband, father — preparing him for the wilderness years ahead.

Context

Moses's killing of the Egyptian (v.12) and his subsequent flight are not a mere biographical episode — they reveal the tension in his identity (Egyptian by upbringing, Hebrew by birth) and the fact that his first attempt at deliverance is done in his own strength and fails. The bush will come later, when God equips him. The rejection by his own people (v.14: "who made you a prince?") anticipates the pattern of prophetic rejection throughout Israel's history.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 7:23–29Stephen quotes this episode directly: "When he was forty years old, it came into his heart to visit his brothers... he supposed that his brothers would understand that God was giving them salvation by his hand."
  • Heb 11:24–25"By faith Moses... refused to be called the son of Pharaoh's daughter, choosing rather to be mistreated with the people of God than to enjoy the fleeting pleasures of sin."
  • Exod 18:3–4Moses' two sons' names — Gershom ("sojourner there") and Eliezer ("my God is help") — together summarise Moses' Midian experience: alien and rescued.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:24–25

Four verbs in two verses: God heard, God remembered, God saw, God knew. The fourfold engagement is a reversal of Pharaoh's fourfold escalation (labour, beatings, midwife decree, Nile decree). Pharaoh acts to destroy; God acts to deliver. And the trigger is the cry — "their cry for rescue from slavery came up to God." God's covenant engagement is activated by prayer, by the honest cry of the helpless. The most theological chapter transition in Exodus is these two silent verses: God heard. God remembered. God knew. Chapter 3 follows.

2:11–12

Moses "went out to his people" — Sarna reads this as the decisive moment of identity-claiming. Despite his Egyptian upbringing, Moses identifies with the Hebrew slaves. The killing of the Egyptian is not presented as morally simple: Sarna notes the narrative neither condemns nor commends it explicitly. The act reveals Moses' passion for justice (he will later defend Jethro's daughters too, v.17) but also his inability, at this stage, to lead — he acts alone, secretly, and fails. The act is the seed of the calling; the wilderness is the school.

2:15–22

Sarna identifies the Midianite episode as a "type scene" — a conventional biblical narrative pattern (also in Gen 24, Gen 29) in which the future patriarch meets his wife at a well. The well is a liminal space: a meeting point between populations, a place of water (life) in the wilderness. Moses' action in driving away the hostile shepherds and watering the flocks mirrors his later role: he will again lead people through water and defend them from hostile forces.

2:11

Alter on "he saw their burdens": the verb rāʾāh (to see) is key throughout this chapter. Moses sees the suffering (v.11), sees the Egyptian beating a Hebrew (v.11), looks this way and that (v.12). Alter notes that Moses is characterised as a man who cannot avert his eyes from injustice — he must look, must respond. This capacity for moral attention — to see what others ignore — is the fundamental qualification for the role he is being called to. God will later say to Moses: "I have surely seen" (3:7), using the same verb.

2:16–17

The well scene is framed as a rescue, not a romance — at least initially. Alter: "Moses does what comes naturally to a man of action and justice: he rises and saves them." The daughters report to their father: "An Egyptian man delivered us." They call him Egyptian — which he is, by appearance and upbringing. The irony is Alter's main point: the man who looks Egyptian is a Hebrew; the man who acts as a deliverer has not yet been called as one. Identity and vocation are still coming into alignment.

2:11–12

Calvin is careful on the killing of the Egyptian: "Although Moses was not yet furnished with public authority, he was not altogether without excuse, in that he interposed on behalf of his oppressed brother. But the mode of proceeding was wrong." He distinguishes the impulse (just: defending the oppressed) from the method (wrong: private violence without authority). This anticipates Calvin's political theology: righteous impulses require legitimate channels. Moses needed to be sent; he jumped ahead.

2:14

The Hebrew's challenge — "Who made you a ruler and judge over us?" — Calvin notes is the permanent taunt against divine calling. Every prophet, every reformer, every servant of God faces this question. Calvin: "This reproach was just enough to break the spirit of one conscious of his fault in having slain the Egyptian; and we see that it did break it." The failure humbles Moses; the wilderness will form him. God often calls through the wound of perceived failure.

2:12

The NET note on "he looked this way and that" observes the Hebrew וַיִּפֶן כֹּה וָכֹה (wayyipen kōh wāḵōh) signals situational awareness and the calculation of consequences. Moses is not acting in rage but with deliberation. The killing is then followed by concealment — he "hid him in the sand." The NET translators note this is the beginning of Moses' education in the gap between raw zeal and effective leadership: zeal without wisdom accomplishes nothing durable.

2:16

Jethro is called "the priest of Midian" (כֹּהֵן מִדְיָן, kōhēn midyān). The NET note discusses the identification of Jethro across different passages where he is called Reuel (here, Exod 2:18), Jethro (Exod 3:1), and Hobab (Num 10:29; Judg 4:11). The most common scholarly resolution: Reuel is the clan/family name; Jethro is his personal name; Hobab may be a son. The priest of Midian likely served the high god El, whose worship overlapped with YHWH-worship in the patriarchal tradition.

2:11

"Moses went out to his people and looked on their burdens." This is the turning point. Moses knows who he is — Hebrews 11:24-25 tells us he chose his identity by faith. He goes out not as an Egyptian prince curious about his workforce but as a man who recognises his people. The phrase "his people" (ammo) is deliberate: the text marks his self-identification before his action. He goes out already having chosen.

2:12

Moses' killing of the Egyptian taskmaster is an act of passion, not command. He has the desire for justice but not the mandate. Looking both ways, killing, hiding the body — these are the actions of a man acting in the flesh, outside the call of God. He is forty years old (Acts 7:23) and will spend another forty in Midian being humbled and prepared. God does not use the impulsive act; he uses the prepared man. The killing is the thing Moses had to be trained out of, not built on.

2:15

Pharaoh's response — seeking to kill Moses — is exactly the same instinct that drove him to kill Hebrew boys: eliminate the threat. Moses, who escaped the Nile as an infant, now flees to Midian as a fugitive. Both flights preserve him for the moment when God is ready to send him back. The pattern of preservation through flight will recur throughout his story.

2:17–21

Moses at the well of Midian is a type-scene parallel to Isaac's servant (Gen 24) and Jacob (Gen 29). The man arrives at a well, meets women drawing water, acts on their behalf, is invited to the family home, and begins a new life. Moses defends Jethro's daughters from the shepherds who drove them away — the same protective impulse he exercised in Egypt, now channelled without violence. He has begun to learn the shape of true leadership: service without compulsion.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:11

Sarna notes Moses' emergence from Pharaoh's palace to "his brethren" is the narrative hinge between his Egyptian formation and his Israelite identity. The word "brethren" (echav) appears three times in vv.11-13, each time marking a kinship claim that overrides his adopted status. Sarna compares this to the moment when Joseph identified himself to his brothers — the hidden identity disclosed. Moses makes his identification active, going out rather than waiting to be found.

2:15b–17

The well-scene in Midian is the third type-scene in Genesis-Exodus involving a man meeting his future wife at a well (Gen 24; 29; Exod 2). Sarna identifies the standard elements: journey to foreign land, meeting at well, act of assistance, invitation to family home, eventual betrothal. Each instance of the type-scene carries variations; here Moses acts with force (driving away the shepherds) rather than the tender gestures of Jacob. His character is already the character of a leader who acts decisively on behalf of the vulnerable.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:11–12

The triple use of "his brothers" / "his people" in vv.11-13 marks Moses' decisive identification. Alter notes the contrast: in Egypt he was the son of Pharaoh's daughter; here he identifies himself repeatedly as belonging to the Hebrews. The identification costs him everything — he will lose his palace, his status, his safety. The narrative does not pause to note this cost; it simply shows the act. The reader supplies the enormity of the choice.

2:21

"Moses was content to dwell with the man" — a remarkable statement after forty years in the Egyptian court. Contentment with simplicity, with the life of a shepherd, with a foreign wife and a name that means "foreigner": Moses' Midian years are years of deliberate reduction. Alter reads the contentment as preparation: the man who will lead a people through the wilderness must first live the wilderness, must first know what it is to be a ger, an outsider, dependent on strangers' hospitality.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:11–12

Calvin on Moses' killing of the Egyptian: he does not excuse it but contextualises it — Moses acted "with more of human zeal than of the spirit of God." The impulse was right; the method was premature. Moses had not yet received the call, the authority, or the preparation. His act prefigures his role but does not constitute it. "God wishes his servants to attempt nothing except at his call and by his command." The forty years in Midian are the schooling that this precipitate act made necessary.

2:15

Moses' flight to Midian is providentially arranged. Calvin: "God, by his wonderful providence, so guided the course of Moses that he might be fitted by a long exercise of patience for the discharge of his office." The wilderness of Midian, the simplicity of shepherding, the foreignness and obscurity — all of these are divine pedagogy. Moses will lead Israel through wilderness; he must first be shaped by wilderness himself.

Verses 23–25 — Israel's Groaning; God Remembers
23During that long period, the king of Egypt died. The Israelites groaned in their slavery and cried out, and their cry for help because of their slavery went up to God.24God heard their groaning and he remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac and with Jacob.25So God looked on the Israelites and was concerned about them.

Hebrew Word Study

וַיִּזְעָקוּ wayyizaqu — they cried out: V.23: The cry (ze'aqah) is the cry of the afflicted — the same word used for the cry of Sodom (Gen 18:20) and the cry of the oppressed throughout the Psalms. It is not a prayer directed to God specifically; it is the raw voice of suffering. God hears it anyway. The theology is striking: God responds to the inarticulate cry of the suffering, not only to the articulate petition of the faithful.

וַיִּזְכֹּר wayyizkor — and he remembered: V.24: God remembered his covenant with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. Not that he had forgotten — "remembered" in Hebrew means to act on what one knows, to bring the relationship to bear on the present moment. The covenant made in Genesis is the ground of the rescue about to begin. God's action in Exodus is not a new initiative; it is the keeping of an ancient promise.

Context

Exodus 2:23-25 is a theological hinge paragraph — the transition from the human story to the divine response. Four verbs describe God's engagement: he heard their groaning, he remembered his covenant, he saw the people of Israel, he knew (v.25). The final verb — "God knew" — echoes the Pharaoh who "did not know" (1:8). The contrast is total: the one who chose not to know is answered by the One who fully knows. The stage is set for the burning bush.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 15:13–14God told Abraham: "your offspring will be sojourners in a land that is not theirs... I will bring judgment on the nation that they serve." Exodus 2:24 is the moment God begins to act on this ancient promise.
  • Ps 22:24"He has not hidden his face from him, but has heard, when he cried to him." The pattern of God hearing the cry of the afflicted is the foundation of the lament Psalms.
  • Luke 1:72Zechariah's song: "to show the mercy promised to our fathers and to remember his holy covenant." The same category — God remembering covenant — applies to the Incarnation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:23

The death of Pharaoh and the groaning of Israel mark the passage of time and the accumulation of suffering. Nothing in the text has changed politically; the oppression continues. But something has changed cosmically: the time has arrived. God's timing is not responsive to human timetables; it is according to his own covenant commitments. The cry of Israel rises at the moment when God's purposes are ready to move. The suffering did not accelerate the schedule; it was part of the schedule.

2:24–25

Four divine verbs compress the entirety of God's covenant commitment: heard, remembered, saw, knew. The fourfold repetition answers the fourfold description of Israel's oppression in 1:13-14. For every dimension of the suffering, there is a dimension of divine response. "God knew" — the final verb — is the most intimate: the Hebrew yada carries the weight of personal, relational knowing. God does not merely observe Israel's suffering; he knows it from the inside, with the full weight of covenant relationship.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:23–25

Sarna identifies this paragraph as a structural transition unit — it closes the introductory section (chs.1-2) and opens the main narrative (ch.3 onward). The four verbs (heard, remembered, saw, knew) have been called a "divine soliloquy" — they record God's internal response before any external action. Sarna notes that "knew" (vayyeda) in v.25 is used absolutely, without an object in the Hebrew — "God knew" — an expression of complete divine awareness that does not require specification. He knows everything, including what is not said.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:23–25

The shift from human action to divine perception is marked by the absence of dialogue. For the first time, we are inside God's perspective. Alter notes the four verbs are arranged in ascending intimacy: hearing (acoustic) → remembering (cognitive) → seeing (visual) → knowing (relational). The progression moves from external to internal, from response to involvement. "God knew" is the most compressed and the most profound — and by leaving it without an object, the narrator makes it the most open: God knew everything that needed to be known.

Literary Structure

The Birth of the Hero — 2:1–10Moses' birth narrative (vv.1-10) participates in the ancient "birth of the hero" pattern: the hero is born under threat, exposed or hidden, rescued by an unexpected agent, raised in obscurity before emerging to fulfil his destiny. Parallels include Sargon of Akkad. But Moses' story subverts the pattern: he is rescued not by divine intervention alone but by the courage of women — his mother, his sister, and his enemy's daughter.
The Well Type-Scene — 2:15–21Moses' meeting with Zipporah at the Midian well (vv.15-21) is the third instance of the biblical well type-scene after Gen 24 and Gen 29. The standard elements appear: journey, well meeting, act of assistance, invitation home, betrothal. The variations reveal character: Moses acts with force (driving away the shepherds) rather than tenderness. His leadership instinct is already evident — he will protect the vulnerable without being asked.

Textual Notes

2:10Etymology of Moses: Hebrew or Egyptian?
The text gives a Hebrew folk etymology (mashâ, "to draw out"). Modern scholars note that Moses (Mosheh) is likely Egyptian: mose/mss meaning "son of" or "is born" (as in Ramesses, Thutmose). The LXX preserves the Hebrew explanation. No textual variant challenges the MT reading; the debate is etymological, not manuscript-based.
Both etymologies can be simultaneously true: an Egyptian name (mose) was given a Hebrew folk etymology that captured the child's destiny. This kind of bilingual wordplay is common in biblical naming narratives. The narrative does not require us to choose — the Egyptian birth in Pharaoh's household and the Hebrew theological destiny are both encoded in the name.
2:18Jethro's name: Reuel or Jethro?
In 2:18 Moses' father-in-law is called Reuel; in 3:1 and throughout chs.18 he is called Jethro. In Num 10:29 he is Reuel son of Hobab, or Hobab son of Reuel. The variations have generated extensive source-critical discussion.
The most common explanation: Reuel is the clan/family name; Jethro is his personal name or title. "Hobab" may be another name or a son/relative. Ancient Near Eastern naming conventions allowed multiple names for one individual depending on context (personal, honorific, clan-title). The variants are more likely evidence of a complex tradition history than of error or multiple sources.

Scholarly Debates

Was Moses' Killing of the Egyptian Morally Justified?
Justified: Righteous Resistance
Philo of Alexandria, Stephen (Acts 7:24)
"He avenged him that was oppressed" — Stephen treats Moses' act as righteous deliverance. Philo calls it execution of a just sentence on an unjust aggressor. Moses acted on behalf of a weaker party against an oppressor. The killing was not murder but justice.
Not Justified: Premature Self-Appointment
Calvin, MacArthur
Moses had not received the divine commission. He acted "with more human zeal than divine authority." The subsequent flight and forty years of preparation show that God did not endorse the method. The impulse was right; the timing and authority were absent. Moses needed to be trained into proper leadership, not rewarded for vigilante action.
Complex: Morally Ambiguous Act of a Developing Leader
Brueggemann, Fretheim
The text neither commends nor condemns the act explicitly. Moses' moral development is the subject — he is a man of passion and justice who must learn patience and divine dependence. The ambiguity is the point: leaders are not born fully formed. Moses' story is the story of preparation, and this act is part of what needed to be trained.
The narrative's silence is significant: no divine comment on the killing is given. The literary function of the act is to show Moses' passionate identification with his people and his unreadiness for leadership. The text trusts the reader to hold both the rightness of the impulse and the wrongness of the method.
