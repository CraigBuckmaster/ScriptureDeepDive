Exodus 29

The Ordination Ceremony; the Daily Offering; I Will Dwell Among You

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–14 — The Ordination Ceremony; the Sin Offering
1"This is what you are to do to consecrate them, so they may serve me as priests: Take a young bull and two rams without defect.2And from the finest wheat flour make round loaves without yeast, thick loaves without yeast and with olive oil mixed in, and thin loaves without yeast and brushed with olive oil.3Put them in a basket and present them in it—along with the bull and the two rams.4Then bring Aaron and his sons to the entrance to the tent of meeting and wash them with water.5Take the garments and dress Aaron with the tunic, the robe of the ephod, the ephod itself and the breastpiece. Fasten the ephod on him by its skillfully woven waistband.6Put the turban on his head and attach the sacred emblem to the turban.7Take the anointing oil and anoint him by pouring it on his head.8Bring his sons and dress them in tunics9and fasten caps on them. Then tie sashes on Aaron and his sons. The priesthood is theirs by a lasting ordinance. In this way you shall ordain Aaron and his sons.10"Bring the bull to the front of the tent of meeting, and Aaron and his sons shall lay their hands on its head.11Slaughter it in the Lord's presence at the entrance to the tent of meeting.12Take some of the bull's blood and put it on the horns of the altar with your finger, and pour out the rest of it at the base of the altar.13Then take all the fat on the internal organs, the long lobe of the liver, and both kidneys with the fat on them, and burn them on the altar.14But burn the bull's flesh and its hide and its intestines outside the camp. It is a sin offering.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
29:1

MacArthur: The ordination procedure takes seven days -- a complete cycle, a new creation. Washing (cleansing), clothing (covering), anointing (divine enabling), sacrifice (atonement maintained).

29:7

MacArthur: Take the anointing oil and anoint him by pouring it on his head. The anointing of the priest is the origin of the title Messiah. The high priest anointed for his mediating role prefigures the ultimate High Priest anointed with the Spirit without measure (John 3:34).

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The seven-day ordination ceremony mirrors creation's seven days — the priesthood is a new creation, a new beginning of ordered relationship with God. The three sacrifices (sin offering/bull, burnt offering/ram, ordination offering/ram) move from atonement to dedication to celebration: the priests are first cleansed of guilt, then wholly given to God, then established in the fellowship of the sacred meal.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 133:2The anointing oil running down Aaron's beard and onto his collar — the Psalm uses this as the image of brotherly unity.
  • Heb 7:27Christ's once-for-all sacrifice contrasted with the priests' daily/annual sacrifices — the ordination of Exod 29 points to the final high priest.
  • Lev 8The execution of the ordination ceremony as commanded here — Exod 29 gives the instructions; Lev 8 records the performance.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:4

The washing (rachatz) that begins the ordination is a purification ritual that removes ritual contamination. Sarna notes that priestly purification rites are attested throughout the ancient Near East — the unique feature of Israel's version is its connection to covenant appointment rather than merely cultic cleansing. Aaron and his sons are washed into a new status, not merely cleaned from an old one.

29:7

The anointing oil (shemen hamishchah) poured over Aaron's head is the act that makes him 'the anointed priest' (hakohen hamashiach, Lev 4:3). Sarna notes that anointing was used in the ancient Near East for the consecration of kings, priests, and sacred objects. The oil poured on Aaron's head makes him a mashiach (anointed one) — the same word applied to kings and prophets, and ultimately to the Messiah.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:4–7

The ordination sequence is a physical transformation narrative: the ordinary man is washed, clothed in sacred garments, and anointed with oil. Alter notes the sequence moves from the body (washing, dressing) to the person's status (anointing). The ceremony is not merely preparation for service; it is a constitutive act that creates the priest. Without the ceremony, there is no priest; after it, there is.

29:12–14

The sin offering's disposal — blood on the horns, fat on the altar, flesh outside the camp — is the most complex of the ordination sacrifices. Alter notes the spatial movement: the most sacred elements (blood, fat) go to the altar; the remainder goes outside the camp. The spatial contrast between inside (holy) and outside (expelled) will develop throughout Leviticus. Christ's death 'outside the gate' (Heb 13:12) reverses the spatial logic: the holy One goes outside to bear the expelled.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
29:4–9

Calvin on the ordination ceremony: 'These were not empty ceremonies without any spiritual meaning; for God had one end in view — to direct his people to Christ.' The three acts — washing, clothing, anointing — are for Calvin the OT form of the Christian's reception of the Holy Spirit: 'The priests were washed that they might represent that purity and holiness of life which is required; clothed, that they might sustain the honour of the office; anointed, that they might be endued with spiritual gifts for its discharge.'

29:12

Calvin on blood at the altar horns: 'As the blood was the symbol of expiation, this was a confirmation that the altar was purified by blood; and that God was only to be approached through an expiatory sacrifice.' The ordination ceremony is itself a sacrificial act — the priests are consecrated through the same blood-altar structure that governs all subsequent priestly service. The system's beginning is the system's pattern.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
29:7

The NET note on anointing the priest discusses the anointing oil formula (30:22–25): myrrh, cinnamon, cane, and cassia in olive oil. The note explains that the anointing of the high priest is described as oil 'poured' on the head (Ps 133:2), while the anointing of ordinary priests may have been a sprinkling on the garments (Lev 8:30). The high priest's anointing is the most abundant and the most visible — the oil flowing over the garments as the ceremony proceeds.

29:10-14

The NET note on the sin offering bull discusses the laying of hands on the bull's head before slaughter — the identification gesture. The note traces the theological mechanics: the priest identifies himself with the sacrifice; the sacrifice bears what the priest brings to it. This is the foundational gesture of substitutionary atonement.

Verses 15–37 — Burnt Offering and Ordination Ram; Seven-Day Consecration
15"Take one of the rams, and Aaron and his sons shall lay their hands on its head.16Slaughter it and take the blood and splash it against the sides of the altar.17Cut the ram into pieces and wash the internal organs and the legs, putting them with the head and the other pieces.18Then burn the entire ram on the altar. It is a burnt offering to the Lord, a pleasing aroma, a food offering presented to the Lord.19"Take the other ram, and Aaron and his sons shall lay their hands on its head.20Slaughter it, take some of its blood and put it on the lobes of the right ears of Aaron and his sons, on the thumbs of their right hands, and on the big toes of their right feet. Then splash blood against the sides of the altar.21And take some blood from the altar and some of the anointing oil and sprinkle it on Aaron and his garments and on his sons and their garments. Then he and his sons and their garments will be consecrated.22"Take from this ram the fat, the fat tail, the fat on the internal organs, the long lobe of the liver, both kidneys with the fat on them, and the right thigh. (This is the ram for the ordination.)23From the basket of bread made without yeast, which is before the Lord, take one round loaf, one thick loaf with olive oil mixed in, and one thin loaf.24Put all these in the hands of Aaron and his sons and have them wave them before the Lord as a wave offering.25Then take them from their hands and burn them on the altar along with the burnt offering for a pleasing aroma to the Lord, a food offering presented to the Lord.26After you take the breast of the ram for Aaron's ordination, wave it before the Lord as a wave offering, and it will be your share.27"Consecrate those parts of the ordination ram that belong to Aaron and his sons: the breast that was waved and the thigh that was presented.28This is always to be the perpetual share from the Israelites for Aaron and his sons. It is the contribution the Israelites are to make to the Lord from their fellowship offerings.29"Aaron's sacred garments will belong to his descendants so they may be anointed and ordained in them.30The son who succeeds him as priest and comes to the tent of meeting to minister in the Holy Place is to wear them seven days.31"Take the ram for the ordination and cook the meat in a sacred place.32At the entrance to the tent of meeting, Aaron and his sons are to eat the meat of the ram and the bread that is in the basket.33They are to eat these offerings by which atonement was made for their ordination and consecration. But no one else may eat them, because they are sacred.34And if any of the meat of the ordination ram or any bread is left over till morning, burn it up. It must not be eaten, because it is sacred.35"Do everything I have commanded you to do to Aaron and his sons. Take seven days to ordain them.36Sacrifice a bull each day as a sin offering to make atonement. Purify the altar by making atonement for it, and anoint it to consecrate it.37For seven days make atonement for the altar and consecrate it. Then the altar will be most holy, and whatever touches it will be holy."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
29:20-21

MacArthur: The blood applied to Aaron's right earlobe, right thumb, and right big toe consecrates the priest's hearing, doing, and going. The complete body consecration signifies total dedication to covenant service.

29:33

MacArthur: The atonement offerings of the ordination are for Aaron and his sons -- the priests must be personally atoned for before they can atone for others. No priest serves from personal holiness sufficient for the task.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The blood on right ear, thumb, and big toe (v.20) is the ordination's most distinctive element. It does not appear in any other sacrificial context — it is unique to priestly consecration. The same rite is used in the cleansing of lepers (Lev 14:14), signifying complete restoration. The priest is entirely consecrated: everything he hears, everything he does, everywhere he goes is claimed by God.

Cross-Reference

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    29:38–42

    The daily (tamid) offering — two lambs, morning and evening — is the covenant's foundational sacrifice. Sarna notes the tamid ('perpetual/regular') offering structures Israelite time: morning and evening sacrifices bracket the day. The God of Israel is approached twice daily, every day, through the same prescribed sacrifice. The regularity is the theological statement: covenant worship is not occasional but perpetual, not emergency-driven but calendar-structured.

    29:45–46

    The concluding statement of the ordination instructions — 'I will dwell among the Israelites' — is the teleological statement of the entire tabernacle project. Sarna identifies this as the climax of the Exodus narrative: the purpose of liberation was not freedom per se but divine-human co-habitation. The tabernacle section that began with 'let them make me a sanctuary so that I may dwell among them' (25:8) finds its resolution here: the dwelling is coming; it will be accomplished.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    29:45–46

    The chapter's final verses are its theological summit. Alter notes that the long, technical chapters of tabernacle design and priestly ordination are framed by the simplest possible divine commitment: 'I will dwell among the Israelites and be their God.' All the complexity — the cubits, the materials, the priestly garments, the ordination sacrifices — exists to enable the most intimate possible relationship: God with his people, in their midst, as their God.

    29:42-43

    The 'meeting with the Israelites' promised at the tent's entrance — 'there I will meet with the Israelites, and the tabernacle will be sanctified by my glory' — is the architectural climax. Alter notes the tabernacle's purpose is not the building itself but the meetings it enables. God is building a place to meet with his people, not a monument to himself.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    29:45–46

    Calvin on divine dwelling: 'This is the chief happiness of men — that God should be their God, and that they should be his people.' The divine-human cohabitation is not a theological abstraction but the substance of covenant happiness. Calvin applies this to the Christian's experience of the Spirit's indwelling: what the tabernacle enacted locally and temporally, the Spirit enacts universally and permanently in the new covenant community.

    29:42-46

    Calvin: God dwells among his people not because he needs a house but because we need to know where to find him. The tabernacle is for Israel's benefit, not God's. It gives the covenant people a specific, accessible, reliable place to encounter the God who is everywhere — condescension to human limitation.

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    29:46

    The NET note on 'so that I might dwell among them' (leshokni betokham) connects to the tabernacle's name (mishkan, dwelling place). The note identifies this as the programmatic statement of the tabernacle section (25:8) repeated as its conclusion. The entire architectural and liturgical system described in chs.25–31 exists to achieve this single goal: divine indwelling among the human community. The NT's application (John 1:14: 'tabernacled among us'; Rev 21:3: 'God's dwelling is with humanity') extends this goal to its ultimate eschatological fulfilment.

    29:43-46

    The NET note on 'I will dwell among the Israelites and be their God' discusses this as the covenant formula in its most concentrated form. The note traces the formula through Lev 26:12, Ezek 37:27, and Rev 21:3 — from tabernacle to temple to eschatological fulfilment. The entire canon is the progressive realisation of this single promise.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    29:43-45

    MacArthur: I will dwell among the Israelites and be their God. The stated goal of the entire tabernacle project: divine dwelling in the midst of the covenant community. This is the covenant's ultimate promise, fulfilled progressively through tabernacle, temple, incarnation, Pentecost, and the new Jerusalem (Rev 21:3).

    29:38-46

    MacArthur: The daily burnt offerings — morning and evening — establish the rhythm that all subsequent covenant worship will follow.     Hebrew Word Study

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    The daily burnt offering (vv.38–42) frames the Tabernacle's ongoing life: a lamb at dawn, a lamb at dusk. Every day began and ended with sacrifice. The people woke to the smoke of a burnt offering and went to sleep to the same. Their daily rhythm was punctuated by the reminder that approach to God requires the giving of life. Hebrews argues that Christ's single sacrifice replaced this perpetual offering — once for all instead of once every morning.

    Cross-Reference

    • Heb 10:11–14Daily priests stand and offer repeatedly; Christ sat down after one offering. The daily sacrifice of Exod 29 is fulfilled and superseded.
    • Rev 21:3"God's dwelling place is now among the people" — the final fulfilment of v.45.
    • John 1:14"The Word became flesh and dwelt among us" — the first fulfilment of v.45.

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    29:43

    The sanctuary is 'sanctified by my glory' (veniqdash bikhvodi). Sarna notes the divine passive: the tabernacle's holiness is not inherent in its materials or construction but derived from the divine presence that inhabits it. The same principle appears in 40:34–35: the glory cloud fills the completed tabernacle, making it holy. Holiness always flows from the divine presence to the structure, never the reverse.

    29:44-46

    The triple consecration — of the tent, the altar, Aaron, and his sons — by divine glory rather than human action establishes that the tabernacle's sanctity is God's gift, not a human achievement. Sarna notes the covenant logic: God is the one who makes holy; the human beings are those who receive the holiness extended to them.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    29:42–46

    The chapter's conclusion zooms out from the specific (ordination sacrifices) to the general (the tabernacle as meeting place) to the cosmic (God dwelling with Israel as the Exodus's purpose). Alter notes the rhetorical movement enacts the theological argument: the specific practices exist to achieve the general purpose; the general purpose is the expression of the ultimate cosmic commitment.

    29:44-46

    The chapter's climax — 'I will be their God' — is the covenant's most concentrated self-statement. Alter notes the movement from the specific (ordination sacrifices) to the universal (covenant relationship) encodes the tabernacle's theology: every specific ritual act is grounded in and points toward the fundamental covenant reality.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    29:44

    Calvin on God consecrating the tent: 'God ratifies and gives effect to all the outward forms of religion, which he has appointed for the guidance of his people, by the efficacy of his Spirit.' The ceremony is necessary; the Spirit's work is what makes it effective. Human ordination (the outward form) and divine consecration (the Spirit's work) are not alternatives but cooperating causes. The visible and the invisible, the external and the internal, work together in the covenant's worship system.

    29:44-46

    Calvin: This is the most comforting promise in the whole ordinance — God himself promises to be present. All the material provisions (the tent, the furnishings, the sacrifices) are means to this end: the personal, covenantal presence of God with his people.

Later texts develop the tamid's theological significance: Dan 8:11–14 uses its cessation as the ultimate sign of covenant desecration; Jesus' crucifixion is theologically related to the tamid in Hebrews' argument for a better sacrifice.

    Ancient Sources

    ANE Ordination Rites
    Mesopotamian priestly initiation rites involved ritual washing, investiture, and animal sacrifice. The Aaronic ordination uses similar forms but unique content: the blood on ear, thumb, and toe is without parallel in known ANE sources.
    The uniqueness underlines its distinctive covenant theology.
    Daily Sacrifice
    The tāmîd was the backbone of Temple worship. Its cessation in 70 AD — when the Romans destroyed the Temple — was the defining catastrophe of ancient Judaism.
    The tāmîd's importance across Israel's history reflects the theological centrality of Exod 29:38–42.

    Scholarly Debates

    Ordination: Does the Ceremony Make the Priest or Confirm Divine Appointment?
    The ceremony constitutes the priest
    Catholic, Orthodox, high-church tradition
    The ordination ceremony is a sacramental act that effects what it signifies: the person is not a priest before the ceremony and is a priest after it. The washing, clothing, and anointing are not merely symbols of an appointment already made; they are the means by which the appointment is executed. Holy orders is a sacrament that confers priestly character.
    The ceremony confirms and publicly marks a prior divine appointment
    Protestant/Reformed tradition
    God's appointment precedes the ceremony; the ceremony publicly marks and confirms what God has already determined. Calvin reads the priestly garments and ordination as divine accommodation to human weakness — Israel needed visible, enacted signs of what God was doing spiritually. The ceremony is meaningful and necessary but not independently efficacious.
    The text supports the view that the ceremony is constitutive: Aaron was not the high priest before it (he was Moses' spokesman) and is after it (he performs priestly functions). Whether the ceremony is the means of the appointment or the public enactment of it, the ceremony is required and transformative. The Reformed/Catholic distinction matters for NT ecclesiology; for the Exodus context, both readings affirm the ceremony's necessity.
