Exodus 22

Property Laws; Social and Religious Laws; Care for the Vulnerable

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–15 — Property Laws; Restitution
1"Whoever steals an ox or a sheep and slaughters it or sells it must pay back five head of cattle for the ox and four sheep for the sheep.2"If a thief is caught breaking in at night and is struck a fatal blow, the defender is not guilty of bloodshed;3but if it happens after sunrise, the defender is guilty of bloodshed. "Anyone who steals must certainly make restitution, but if they have nothing, they must be sold to pay for their theft.4If the stolen animal is found alive in their possession—whether ox or donkey or sheep—they must pay back double.5"If anyone grazes their livestock in a field or vineyard and lets them stray and they graze in another person's field, the offender must make restitution from the best of their own field or vineyard.6"If a fire breaks out and spreads into thornbushes so that it burns shocks of grain or standing grain or the whole field, the one who started the fire must make restitution.7"If anyone gives a neighbor silver or goods for safekeeping and they are stolen from the neighbor's house, the thief, if caught, must pay back double.8But if the thief is not found, the owner of the house must appear before the judges, and they must determine whether the owner of the house has taken the neighbor's property.9In all cases of illegal possession of an ox, a donkey, a sheep, a garment, or any other lost property about which somebody says, 'This is mine,' both parties are to bring their cases before the judges. The one whom the judges declare guilty must pay back double to the other.10"If anyone gives a donkey, an ox, a sheep or any other animal to their neighbor for safekeeping and it dies or is injured or is taken away while no one is looking,11the issue between them will be settled by the taking of an oath before the Lord that the neighbor did not lay hands on the other person's property. The owner is to accept this, and no restitution is required.12But if the animal was stolen from the neighbor, restitution must be made to the owner.13If it was torn to pieces by a wild animal, the neighbor shall bring in the remains as evidence and shall not be required to pay for the torn animal.14"If anyone borrows an animal from their neighbor and it is injured or dies while the owner is not present, they must make restitution.15But if the owner is with the animal, the borrower will not have to pay. If the animal was hired, the money paid for the hire covers the loss."

Historical Context

None

Context

The care-for-the-vulnerable laws (vv.21–27) are grounded in three foundations: (1) historical memory — "you were strangers"; (2) theological accountability — "if they cry to me, I will hear"; (3) divine character — "I am compassionate." The laws are not humanitarian sentimentalism; they are the overflow of the covenant's core values: the God who heard Israel's cry in Egypt hears the cry of the widow and orphan in Israel. Ignoring their cry is to act against the character of God himself.

Cross-Reference

  • Lev 19:33–34"You shall treat the stranger who sojourns with you as the native among you, and you shall love him as yourself, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt."
  • Jas 1:27"Religion that is pure and undefiled before God the Father is this: to visit orphans and widows in their affliction." James applies Exod 22:22–23.
  • Matt 25:34–40The judgment of the nations on how they treated "the least of these" — the widow/orphan/stranger criteria of Exod 22 become the eschatological criterion.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:1

The restitution values (five for an ox, four for a sheep) reflect their relative economic importance in the agrarian economy. Sarna notes that the ox was the primary farm tool — losing an ox was losing productive capacity for the entire agricultural season. The fourfold/fivefold restitution (rather than simple return) serves as deterrence proportional to the economic damage of the crime.

22:7–9

The cases involving disputed property left in a neighbour's keeping (vv.7–9) establish the legal framework of bailment — the deposit of property with another for safekeeping. Sarna notes these laws are remarkably sophisticated: they distinguish between simple theft by the bailee, claimed theft by third parties (requiring oath), and disputed ownership (requiring judicial determination). The laws anticipate modern contract and tort law by three millennia.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:2–3

The law allowing lethal force against a burglar at night but not by day is one of the Book of the Covenant's most legally precise distinctions. Alter notes the reasoning: at night, a householder cannot know the burglar's intent or see the danger clearly — lethal defence is understandable. By day, the situation is visible and less threatening — the householder is 'guilty of bloodshed' if he kills a daytime intruder. Intent and context matter in the covenant law.

22:4-8

The progressive liability structure — thief caught with animal pays double; animal found alive pays double; theft not recovered by thief must be proven by oath — establishes a graduated system in which the penalty fits the specific form of the wrong. Alter notes the detail reflects the practical legal problems of a livestock-based economy.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:1

Calvin on restitution law: 'The thief does not merely restore what he has taken, but makes fourfold or fivefold satisfaction... for the law will not allow him to profit by his injustice.' The principle is not merely restoration but consequence — the thief is made worse off by the theft than if he had not stolen. Calvin reads this as proportional deterrence: the penalty must outweigh the gain of the crime.

22:10–11

On the oath before the LORD: 'God is brought in as the judge between them, before whom the conscience of the innocent man will stand firm, while the guilty one will be terror-stricken.' The divine oath serves both as evidence (invoking the divine witness) and as moral pressure (the guilty person cannot comfortably swear falsely before God). The covenant community's justice system works through conscience as well as procedure.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:9

The NET note on 'before God' (el ha'Elohim) in legal disputes discusses whether this means before a judge (Elohim used for judges, cf. 21:6), before a divine oracle (Urim and Thummim), or before God directly in an oath. The note favours the judge-as-divine-representative reading — the human judge functions as God's representative in the legal process. Either way, the legal dispute is elevated to a theological act.

22:10-12

The NET note on the sacred oath before God discusses how 'el haElohim' (before God) functioned in Israelite legal procedure: the parties appeared at the sanctuary where the oath was sworn before the divine judge. The note discusses whether this refers to an actual sanctuary visit or a metaphorical divine witness invocation.

Historical Context

None

Context

The theft laws (vv.1–15) establish restitution as the primary mechanism of justice — not imprisonment but restoration. The restitution multiples (five oxen for an ox, four sheep for a sheep) both compensate the victim and serve as deterrent. The seduction law (vv.16–17) protects the woman's future by requiring the man to pay the bride price whether or not she chooses to marry him. These laws reveal a concern for the victim's restoration, not merely the offender's punishment.

  • Acts 23:5Paul quotes Exod 22:28 (do not curse a ruler) when confronted with the high priest, showing its continued applicability in the NT period.
  • "Rom 12:19Do not avenge yourselves — the restitution principle of Exod 22 transformed into NT ethics.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:21

The foreigner-protection command is unique in the ancient world — no other ancient law code specifically protects resident aliens. Sarna notes the 36 separate OT commands to protect the ger (resident alien) make this the most legislated ethical requirement in the Torah. The theological ground is always the same: 'for you were strangers in Egypt' (22:21; 23:9; Lev 19:34; Deut 10:19). Memory of oppression creates ethical obligation.

22:25

The prohibition of interest on loans to the poor (lo titen lo neshek) is radical in the ancient economy, where interest-bearing loans were standard. Sarna notes that in the ancient Near East, interest rates of 20–33% were common. Israel's law prohibits charging interest to fellow Israelites in need (distinguished from commercial loans to foreigners in Deut 23:20). The poor person's loan is a covenant obligation of generosity, not a commercial transaction.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:22–27

The section on widow, orphan, foreigner, and poor moves from civil law to prophetic ethics. Alter notes the shift in tone: earlier laws use the third-person case form ('if a man...'); these laws use second-person address ('you shall not...') with direct divine threat ('my wrath will burn'). The most vulnerable members of society receive the most direct divine protection. The law's emotional register rises when the law addresses those with no legal advocate.

22:25-27

The prohibition on charging interest to the poor and on keeping the poor person's garment overnight are not merely humanitarian recommendations but covenant obligations. Alter notes the theological grounding in v.27: 'When he cries out to me, I will hear, for I am compassionate.' Divine responsiveness to the poor's cry is both warning and promise.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:21

Calvin on the foreigner law: 'By the example of their own condition, they were reminded to exercise kindness toward strangers... for it is a feeling natural to all, to dislike strangers; and hence it is that almost everywhere they are treated unkindly.' Calvin takes the law's psychological realism seriously: the natural tendency to exclude foreigners must be actively resisted by the discipline of memory. The covenant law works against natural self-preference.

22:27

On the cloak-pledge: 'It is as if God had said that he was the protector of the poor, and that their complaints would not be without effect.' The theological claim is striking: God is the legal advocate of the poor man who has no court to appeal to. Calvin uses this to establish the principle that divine justice compensates for human legal failure — when courts do not protect the vulnerable, God does.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:26

The NET note on 'that is his only covering, it is his cloak for his body' explains the economic reality: in the ancient world, a poor person's outer garment (simla) served as both daytime clothing and nighttime blanket. Taking it permanently as a loan pledge was equivalent to removing the person's bed. The law's specificity reflects knowledge of actual poverty — the legislator knows what it is to be cold without a blanket.

22:27-31

The NET note on the cluster of laws closing the chapter — not cursing God or the ruler, offering firstfruits, giving firstborn sons, avoiding carrion — discusses how these varied commands are held together by the holiness principle: the covenant community is 'holy people' (v.31) whose everyday practices express that status.

The gēr laws have no precise equivalent in any other ANE legal tradition.

Scholarly Debates

The Book of the Covenant and Ancient Near Eastern Law Codes
Dependent on Hammurabi's Code
Some critical scholars (Westbrook, Paul)
Several laws in the Book of the Covenant have close parallels in Hammurabi's Code (c.1754 BC): goring ox (Laws 250–252), deposit of property (Laws 120–126), seduction of an unbetrothed woman (Law 130). The parallels suggest literary dependence or a common ancient Near Eastern legal tradition that Israel inherited.
Independent composition reflecting common legal culture
Sarna, Kitchen, most evangelical scholars
The parallels reflect the common legal problems of agricultural societies (goring animals, property disputes, personal injury) that every ancient law code must address. The differences are as significant as the similarities: Israel's law is notably more egalitarian (same standard for all social classes) and more theologically grounded (law flows from covenant) than Hammurabi. Parallel structures do not require literary dependence.
Both positions acknowledge the parallels. The key question is whether the parallels indicate borrowing or reflect the common legal heritage of the ancient Near East. The Book of the Covenant's theological distinctives — the covenant preamble (20:2), the protection of the vulnerable, the divine oath, the egalitarian standard — are genuinely different from Hammurabi and resist reduction to literary dependence.
