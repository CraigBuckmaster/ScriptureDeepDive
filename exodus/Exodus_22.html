Exodus 22

Property, Compassion, and Covenant Ethics

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline
Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. Moses is not only the principal character of Exodus — he is its author, and the events recorded are largely his own experience.

When written

Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC. The events of Exodus span approximately 80 years (Moses's birth to the Tabernacle's completion), but the book itself was written after the fact — most likely during the years at Sinai and the wilderness wandering that followed. The Tabernacle instructions and legal material (chs.19–40) may have been committed to writing earliest, as they were immediately actionable.

What prompted it

Israel needed a founding narrative — a record of how God delivered them from 430 years of slavery, made them his covenant people at Sinai, and established the Tabernacle as his dwelling among them. Exodus is the story of liberation, but more than that, it is the story of covenant formation and divine habitation. It answers: How did God redeem us? What does he require of us? And how can a holy God dwell with a sinful people?

Hebrew Word Study

גֵּר gēr — sojourner / stranger / alien: The gēr is the resident alien — not a full citizen but not fully foreign. The Torah's most frequently commanded category of legal protection. "You shall not oppress the gēr, for you were gēr in Egypt" (21 times in the Torah). Israel's experience of vulnerability is the ground of their obligation to protect the vulnerable.

חַנּוּן אָנִי ḥannûn ʾānî — I am compassionate: The divine self-description in the context of the widow and orphan's cry. The same root (ḥānan) appears in the covenant formula of Exod 34:6: "The LORD, the LORD, a God merciful and gracious." Compassion is not a secondary attribute of God; it is a defining one.

Context

The care-for-the-vulnerable laws (vv.21–27) are grounded in three foundations: (1) historical memory — "you were strangers"; (2) theological accountability — "if they cry to me, I will hear"; (3) divine character — "I am compassionate." The laws are not humanitarian sentimentalism; they are the overflow of the covenant's core values: the God who heard Israel's cry in Egypt hears the cry of the widow and orphan in Israel. Ignoring their cry is to act against the character of God himself.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:21

You were strangers in Egypt. Israel's entire ethical responsibility toward the vulnerable is rooted in their experience of vulnerability. They were the oppressed; they were the aliens; they cried out; God heard. Now they are not to become the oppressor. The Exodus is not just a one-time event; it is a permanent moral formation. "You were there" is the most powerful basis for compassion.

22:22–23

"If they cry out to me, I will surely hear their cry." The widow and the orphan have no human advocate — no husband, no father to represent them in court. So God takes on their case personally. The most powerful being in the universe makes himself the lawyer for the powerless. This is not peripheral to his character; it is definitional. Psalm 68:5: "Father of the fatherless and protector of widows is God in his holy habitation."

Hebrew Word Study

קֹדֶשׁ qōdeš — holy / consecrated: v.31: "You shall be consecrated (qĕdōšîm) to me." The holiness laws at the end of the chapter ground all the preceding ethics: Israel is set apart (qādaš) for God, which means their community standards must reflect his character.

אֱלֹהִים לֹא תְקַלֵּל ʾĕlōhîm lōʾ tĕqallēl — you shall not revile God: The command against blasphemy (v.28) is included amid social laws — the connection between proper worship and proper community life is organic. Reviling God and cursing rulers are listed together: both subvert the order that makes community possible.

Context

The theft laws (vv.1–15) establish restitution as the primary mechanism of justice — not imprisonment but restoration. The restitution multiples (five oxen for an ox, four sheep for a sheep) both compensate the victim and serve as deterrent. The seduction law (vv.16–17) protects the woman's future by requiring the man to pay the bride price whether or not she chooses to marry him. These laws reveal a concern for the victim's restoration, not merely the offender's punishment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:1

The restitution multiples (five oxen for an ox) are not arbitrary — they account for economic disruption, loss of production, and deterrence. The thief restores more than he took because theft is not just a personal transgression; it disrupts the community's economic fabric. Justice restores, not just punishes.

22:31

"You shall be consecrated to me." This verse closes the section of civil and social laws. The frame is holiness: Israel's ethical obligations are not secular social contracts but expressions of their covenant identity as God's set-apart people. The food law at the end (torn flesh) is a reminder that every area of life — including what you eat — is shaped by the reality that you belong to the Holy One.

Ancient Sources

ANE Widow and Orphan Laws
Both Hammurabi's code and Ugaritic texts include provisions for widows and orphans, showing the universal recognition of their vulnerability. Israel's laws are distinctive in grounding the obligation in divine character rather than royal benevolence.
The protection of widows and orphans is a universal ANE concern; Exodus grounds it in the character of God himself.
Alien Protection
Egyptian texts document the complex status of foreign residents. Israel's legislation is unusual in the ancient world for the extent of protection extended to aliens — grounded in the covenantal memory of Egypt.
The gēr laws have no precise equivalent in any other ANE legal tradition.

Reception History

Amos 2:6–7
The prophets use Exod 22's language to indict Israel for selling the poor and oppressing the stranger — Exodus 22 becomes the prophetic standard for social justice.
James 1:27
The NT's application of the widow/orphan care principle — "pure religion" defined by Exod 22:22.

Personal ethics — vv.16–20Seduction; sorcery; bestiality
Vulnerable persons — vv.21–27Strangers, widows, orphans, the poor — grounded in Exodus memory
Religious obligations — vv.28–31Do not revile God; firstfruits; consecration
The chapter moves from property to persons to the poor to the holy. The underlying principle is consistent: the Exodus creates obligation — to the vulnerable, to God, to the community.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 22's most powerful section is not about property but about people. The stranger, the widow, the orphan, the poor — their cry reaches God directly. Israel's ethics of compassion are not sentiment but theology: the God who heard us cry in Egypt hears them cry in Israel.
