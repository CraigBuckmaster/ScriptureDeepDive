Exodus 12

Exodus 12

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–20 — The Passover Instructions
1The Lord said to Moses and Aaron in Egypt,2"This month is to be for you the first month, the first month of your year.3Tell the whole community of Israel that on the tenth day of this month each man is to take a lamb for his family, one for each household.4If any household is too small for a whole lamb, they must share one with their nearest neighbor, having taken into account the number of people there are. You are to determine the amount of lamb needed in accordance with what each person will eat.5The animals you choose must be year-old males without defect, and you may take them from the sheep or the goats.6Take care of them until the fourteenth day of the month, when all the members of the community of Israel must slaughter them at twilight.7Then they are to take some of the blood and put it on the sides and tops of the doorframes of the houses where they eat the lambs.8That same night they are to eat the meat roasted over fire, along with bitter herbs, and bread made without yeast.9Do not eat the meat raw or boiled in water, but roast it over fire—with the head, legs and internal organs.10Do not leave any of it till morning; if some is left till morning, you must burn it.11This is how you are to eat it: with your cloak tucked into your belt, your sandals on your feet and your staff in your hand. Eat it in haste; it is the Lord's Passover.12"On that same night I will pass through Egypt and strike down every firstborn of both people and animals, and I will bring judgment on all the gods of Egypt. I am the Lord.13The blood will be a sign for you on the houses where you are, and when I see the blood, I will pass over you. No destructive plague will touch you when I strike Egypt.14"This is a day you are to commemorate; for the generations to come you shall celebrate it as a festival to the Lord—a lasting ordinance.15For seven days you are to eat bread made without yeast. On the first day remove the yeast from your houses, for whoever eats anything with yeast in it from the first day through the seventh must be cut off from Israel.16On the first day hold a sacred assembly, and another one on the seventh day. Do no work at all on these days, except to prepare food for everyone to eat; that is all you may do.17"Celebrate the Festival of Unleavened Bread, because it was on this very day that I brought your divisions out of Egypt. Celebrate this day as a lasting ordinance for the generations to come.18In the first month you are to eat bread made without yeast, from the evening of the fourteenth day until the evening of the twenty-first day.19For seven days no yeast is to be found in your houses. And anyone, whether foreigner or native-born, who eats anything with yeast in it must be cut off from the community of Israel.20Eat nothing made with yeast. Wherever you live, you must eat unleavened bread."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
12:3-5

MacArthur: The Passover lamb specifications -- male, one year old, without defect -- foreshadow the ultimate Passover Lamb. John the Baptist's identification of Jesus as the Lamb of God (John 1:29) and Paul's statement Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed (1 Cor 5:7) make the typology explicit.

12:13

MacArthur: When I see the blood, I will pass over you. The blood is covenant identification: the household under the blood is covenantally protected. This is the structure of atonement -- covenantal application, not universal automatic protection.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

The Passover was Israel's foundational calendrical institution — everything in the Jewish liturgical year orbits this night. The lamb selection on Nisan 10, slaughter on Nisan 14, and the night of Nisan 15 are the precise dates the NT gospels use for Jesus' final week. The blood-on-doorposts ritual has no Egyptian parallel — it is wholly distinctive, a new sign-act for a new people being constituted as a nation.

Context

The Passover institution precedes the plague's execution — Israel is not rescued after the fact but prepared before it. The blood must be applied before midnight; faith acts before seeing. The lamb is killed at twilight (v.6: "between the two evenings"), the blood applied, the household inside, the meal eaten in haste with sandals on (v.11) — everything about the ritual encodes urgency and readiness for departure. This is not a comfortable memorial; it is a mobilisation.

Cross-Reference

  • John 1:29"Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!" — John the Baptist identifies Jesus as the Passover lamb.
  • 1 Cor 5:7"Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed" — Paul's definitive identification.
  • 1 Pet 1:18–19Redeemed "with the precious blood of Christ, like a lamb without blemish or spot" — the tāmîm requirement fulfilled.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:2

Sarna notes that the reorganisation of the calendar around the Exodus makes it the generative event of Israelite time. In the ancient Near East, calendars were typically structured around agricultural cycles or royal accessions. Israel's calendar is structured around divine redemption. Every month begins from the month of liberation. The theological implication is that all of history is read through the lens of what God did at the Exodus.

12:6

The 'whole assembly of the congregation' (kol qahal adat Yisrael) slaughtering the lamb together is significant. Sarna notes this is the first time the entire Israelite community acts as a unit in a liturgical act. The Passover creates the community — the shared ritual constitutes them as a worshipping assembly before they are a politically free nation. Community precedes territory in Israel's formation.

12:13

The blood as a sign (ot) on the houses is for God to see, not for humans. Sarna notes that neighbouring houses know which are Israelite by community — the blood is not a public marker. Its function is covenantal: God recognises the sign of the substitutionary lamb and 'passes over' (pasach) the household. The theology of substitution is physically enacted: the lamb dies; the firstborn lives.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:3–11

The Passover instructions are notable for their elaboration within a narrative that has otherwise moved rapidly. Alter notes the shift from narrative to legislative mode — the passage reads as a ritual code embedded in a historical account. This blending of narrative and law is characteristic of the Pentateuch and has provoked source-critical discussion; Alter reads it as a deliberate interpretive strategy: the ritual encodes the historical meaning so that future practice recalls the event.

12:11

'You shall eat it in haste; it is the LORD's Passover.' The haste (chipazon) is built into the ritual. The meal must be eaten as if departure is imminent — because it is. Every element of the first Passover meal (sandals on, staff in hand, loins girded) is a physical posture of readiness. The eating is not rest but preparation. The ritual posture encodes the historical urgency.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:3–7

Calvin on the Passover lamb: 'In the lamb was prefigured the Lamb of God, who was to be sacrificed once for the redemption of the world.' The typological reading is immediate for Calvin — the Passover lamb is a type of Christ from its institution. The unblemished lamb (v.5), the blood on the doorposts, the deliverance from the destroying angel — all find their antitype in the sacrifice of Christ and the covering of his blood.

12:13

On 'when I see the blood': 'Although God needs no token to distinguish between his friends and enemies, yet he graciously condescends to accommodate himself to our weakness.' The blood is not information for God — he knows his people. It is a covenant sign that structures Israel's faith: the act of applying the blood is an act of trust that God will honour the sign he himself established. Salvation works through appointed means.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:2

The NET note on the calendar reform explains that the Israelite calendar had two systems: a religious calendar beginning in Nisan (spring, the month of the Exodus) and an agricultural/civil calendar beginning in Tishri (autumn, the month of Rosh Hashanah). The religious calendar established here structures Passover, Pentecost, and Tabernacles as spring-autumn sequence. The civil calendar coexisted with it throughout Israel's history.

12:7

The NET note on the doorposts (mezuzot) and lintel (mashqof) explains the structural components: the two vertical door-frames and the horizontal beam above the door. The blood applied to all three sides creates a frame around the door — the entry point of the household. The note discusses the theological significance of the threshold: the bloodied entry marks the household as under divine protection.

Verses 21–36 — Moses Gives the Instructions; The Plague Falls
21Then Moses summoned all the elders of Israel and said to them, "Go at once and select the animals for your families and slaughter the Passover lamb.22Take a bunch of hyssop, dip it into the blood in the basin and put some of the blood on the top and on both sides of the doorframe. None of you shall go out of the door of your house until morning.23When the Lord goes through the land to strike down the Egyptians, he will see the blood on the top and sides of the doorframe and will pass over that doorway, and he will not permit the destroyer to enter your houses and strike you down.24"Obey these instructions as a lasting ordinance for you and your descendants.25When you enter the land that the Lord will give you as he promised, observe this ceremony.26And when your children ask you, 'What does this ceremony mean to you?'27then tell them, 'It is the Passover sacrifice to the Lord, who passed over the houses of the Israelites in Egypt and spared our homes when he struck down the Egyptians.'" Then the people bowed down and worshiped.28The Israelites did just what the Lord commanded Moses and Aaron.29At midnight the Lord struck down all the firstborn in Egypt, from the firstborn of Pharaoh, who sat on the throne, to the firstborn of the prisoner, who was in the dungeon, and the firstborn of all the livestock as well.30Pharaoh and all his officials and all the Egyptians got up during the night, and there was loud wailing in Egypt, for there was not a house without someone dead.31During the night Pharaoh summoned Moses and Aaron and said, "Up! Leave my people, you and the Israelites! Go, worship the Lord as you have requested.32Take your flocks and herds, as you have said, and go. And also bless me."33The Egyptians urged the people to hurry and leave the country. "For otherwise," they said, "we will all die!"34So the people took their dough before the yeast was added, and carried it on their shoulders in kneading troughs wrapped in clothing.35The Israelites did as Moses instructed and asked the Egyptians for articles of silver and gold and for clothing.36The Lord had made the Egyptians favorably disposed toward the people, and they gave them what they asked for; so they plundered the Egyptians.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
12:26-27

MacArthur: When your children ask you, What does this ceremony mean to you? -- the institutionalised question-and-answer of the Seder is commanded before the event even occurs. The event is designed from its inception to generate intergenerational testimony.

12:35-36

MacArthur: The Israelites plundered Egypt -- this fulfils God's promise to Abraham (Gen 15:14: they will come out with great possessions). Paul (1 Cor 10:1-11) uses the Exodus events as types for the Christian life.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

Pharaoh's command — "Up, go out from among my people" — is the complete reversal of his earlier defiance. Every "I will not let Israel go" is cancelled in a single midnight sentence. He does not just permit; he drives them out (v.33: "the Egyptians were urgent with the people to send them out of the land in haste"). The departure that Pharaoh refused for ten plagues he now begs for. The Exodus is not Israel escaping; it is Israel being expelled — pushed out by the very power that held them.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 15:13–14"Your offspring will be sojourners in a land that is not theirs... they shall come out with great possessions." Fulfilled precisely.
  • Acts 7:36Stephen: "This man led them out, performing wonders and signs in Egypt and at the Red Sea."
  • Rom 9:17"For this purpose I have raised you up, that I might show my power in you." Pharaoh as the instrument of God's self-revelation.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:29

The death of the firstborn falls across the entire social spectrum: Pharaoh's son (the heir of divine kingship), the captive's son (the lowest member of Egyptian society), and the livestock's firstborn (even the animal kingdom). Sarna notes the universality is theological — there is no Egyptian household exempt, no social standing that purchases immunity. The judgment is comprehensive and impartial.

12:35–36

The 'plundering of Egypt' (vv.35–36) is the fulfilment of Gen 15:14 ('they shall come out with great possessions'). Sarna notes that the Egyptians' willingness to give — 'the LORD had given the people favor in the sight of the Egyptians' — means this is not theft but gift. The transfer is both divinely engineered and humanly willing. Egypt's wealth funds Israel's wilderness tabernacle.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:29–36

The midnight execution and the Exodus departure are narrated with unusual pace — Moses' announcement slows the narrative; the events themselves happen in rapid sequence. Alter notes the acceleration: plague strikes, Pharaoh summons, surrender is given, Israelites ask for silver and gold, Egyptians give, Israelites depart. The story that has been building for twelve chapters resolves in a few verses. The compression after the buildup is the narrative's expression of decisiveness.

12:31–32

Pharaoh's midnight summons and surrender is delivered in direct speech — every term of Moses' original demand is granted verbatim. Alter notes the structural irony: Pharaoh uses the exact language Moses had used in the negotiations. The man who defined himself by not knowing YHWH now surrenders using the vocabulary of YHWH's servant. The language of the oppressor becomes the language of liberation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:29

Calvin on the death of the firstborn: 'This last plague, the heaviest of all, alone subdued the obstinacy of Pharaoh.' The graduated nature of the plagues was divinely purposeful — the light before the heavy, the warning before the fatal blow. But the fatal blow is what was always necessary: no lesser demonstration could have broken the will of one so hardened. The tenth plague is the measure of how deeply Pharaoh had entrenched himself.

12:36

On the Egyptians giving silver and gold: 'Whatever the Egyptians transferred to the Israelites was restored to its right owners, since the Israelites had long been defrauded of their just wages.' The spoiling of Egypt is Calvin's restitution theology — the wealth that was extracted from Israel through forced labour is returned through the willing gifts of Egypt. Divine justice operates through human generosity.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:29

The NET note on 'the LORD struck all the firstborn' discusses whether the agent is YHWH directly or the 'destroyer' (mashchit, v.23). The note explains that v.23 implies a destroying angel as the immediate agent while YHWH is the ultimate agent — a common biblical pattern where divine action is attributed both to God and to his angelic instrument (cf. 2 Sam 24:16). The distinction does not affect the theological point: the judgment is YHWH's.

12:40

The NET note on '430 years' discusses the variant in LXX and Samaritan Pentateuch: 'The time that the Israelites lived in Egypt and Canaan was 430 years.' The MT reads just 'in Egypt.' The longer LXX/SP reading includes the patriarchal sojourning in Canaan, which would date the 430 years from Abraham's entrance into Canaan (c.2091 BC) rather than from Jacob's descent (c.1876 BC). Paul's reference in Gal 3:17 supports the longer reckoning.

Verses 37–51 — The Exodus Begins; Passover Regulations
37The Israelites journeyed from Rameses to Sukkoth. There were about six hundred thousand men on foot, besides women and children.38Many other people went up with them, and also large droves of livestock, both flocks and herds.39With the dough the Israelites had brought from Egypt, they baked loaves of unleavened bread. The dough was without yeast because they had been driven out of Egypt and did not have time to prepare food for themselves.40Now the length of time the Israelite people lived in Egypt was 430 years.41At the end of the 430 years, to the very day, all the Lord's divisions left Egypt.42Because the Lord kept vigil that night to bring them out of Egypt, on this night all the Israelites are to keep vigil to honor the Lord for the generations to come.43The Lord said to Moses and Aaron, "These are the regulations for the Passover meal: No foreigner may eat it.44Any slave you have bought may eat it after you have circumcised him,45but a temporary resident or a hired worker may not eat it.46"It must be eaten inside the house; take none of the meat outside the house. Do not break any of the bones.47The whole community of Israel must celebrate it.48"A foreigner residing among you who wants to celebrate the Lord's Passover must have all the males in his household circumcised; then he may take part like one born in the land. No uncircumcised male may eat it.49The same law applies both to the native-born and to the foreigner residing among you."50All the Israelites did just what the Lord had commanded Moses and Aaron.51And on that very day the Lord brought the Israelites out of Egypt by their divisions.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:37

Sarna discusses the 600,000 men figure (shesh me'ot elef) at length. The word elef can mean 'thousand' (standard reading) or 'clan unit/military contingent' (alternative reading). If elef means military unit, the number could be much smaller — perhaps 5,000–6,000 fighting men, implying a total population of 20,000–40,000. This reading better fits the archaeological evidence for the period. The interpretive choice significantly affects calculations of the Exodus's historical scale.

12:43–49

The Passover regulations regarding foreigners (vv.43–49) establish that Passover membership is not ethnic but covenantal: circumcised resident aliens may participate; uncircumcised foreigners may not. Sarna notes this establishes the principle that covenant incorporation (circumcision) is the boundary of the covenant community, not ethnic descent. The door is open to converts who undergo the covenant sign.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:37–39

The departure scene is narrated with surprising domesticity: the dough that hadn't risen, the kneading bowls wrapped in cloaks on their shoulders. After twelve chapters of divine drama, the Exodus begins with people carrying unleavened dough because they left in haste. Alter notes the contrast is deliberate — the most theologically momentous departure in history is encoded in the mundane details of a hasty middle-of-the-night evacuation.

12:40-42

The 430 years in Egypt and the night of vigil are narrated with unusual solemnity. Alter notes the double characterisation — 'a night of vigil unto the LORD' / 'a night of vigil for all Israelites throughout their generations' — makes the Passover night simultaneously historical event and perpetual liturgical reality. The original night is relived in every subsequent Passover.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:38

On the mixed multitude: 'Various persons of the Gentile nations joined themselves to the people, attracted by a report of their deliverance.' The mixed multitude represents the first fruits of Gentile incorporation into the covenant community. Calvin uses it as a type: just as Gentiles joined Israel at the Exodus, so Gentiles are incorporated into the new Israel through faith in Christ. The Exodus is not a narrowing of God's saving reach but a model of its expansion.

12:38-39

Calvin on the mixed multitude: 'The Gentiles who joined Israel in the Exodus received the same divine deliverance as the covenant people. This teaches the principle of covenant inclusion by faith — the mixed multitude became part of Israel not by ancestry but by participation in the covenant event and the covenant meal.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:46

The NET note on 'you shall not break any of its bones' (etsem lo tishberu vo) notes that John 19:36 cites this as fulfilled in Christ's crucifixion. The note discusses the typological relationship: the Passover lamb's unbroken bones are a detail of the ritual that anticipates the death of the one John identifies as 'the Lamb of God' (John 1:29). The specificity of the physical detail (unbroken bones) in both narratives establishes the typological connection as historically concrete, not merely abstract.

12:43-49

The NET note on the Passover regulations for foreigners discusses the inclusivity principle: the alien who wishes to celebrate Passover must be circumcised, and then 'he shall be as a native of the land.' The ordinance is ethnically open and covenantally bounded — anyone who bears the covenant sign may participate.

Ancient Sources

Mishnah Pesachim
The full rabbinic elaboration of Passover practice, tracing details back to the Exodus institution. The Seder order developed over centuries but the core elements — lamb, unleavened bread, bitter herbs, recitation — trace directly to Exod 12.
Jewish Passover practice has preserved the Exodus text's ritual requirements for over three millennia.
Temple Passover Records
Josephus reports that at one Passover in the first century, 256,500 lambs were slaughtered in the Temple. The scale confirms that Passover remained the most observed festival in Judaism.
The Passover's durability across history is itself a testimony to Exod 12.

Reception History

Justin Martyr, Dialogue with Trypho
Extensive typological reading of the Passover lamb as Christ: the lamb's bones not broken (v.46) fulfilled in John 19:36; the blood on doorposts as the sign of the cross; the haste of the meal as the urgency of salvation.
1 Corinthians 5:7
Paul's "Christ our Passover has been sacrificed" is the definitive NT typological statement — Exodus 12 is not merely historical background but the interpretive key to the atonement.

Literary Structure

The Passover Lamb: Blood on the Doorposts12:7-13The blood is the sign -- when I see the blood, I will pass over you. The lamb's blood on the doorposts is the household's only distinction from Egypt. The Passover is the covenant rescue marked by blood.
The Rush of the Exodus: Dough Without Leaven12:33-39The Egyptians urged them to hurry and leave. There was no time to let the dough rise. The urgency of the departure is baked into the Passover's unleavened bread -- the bread of haste that becomes the annual memorial of God's haste to rescue.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

Passover and Atonement: Substitution or Apotropaic Sign?
Substitutionary atonement
Calvin, MacArthur, evangelical tradition
The lamb dies in place of the firstborn. The blood covers the household; the destroying angel 'passes over' those protected by the blood. The structure is substitutionary: an innocent life is taken so that another life is spared. This is the same structure as the sin offering (Lev 4) and finds its fulfilment in Christ's sacrifice (1 Cor 5:7: 'Christ our Passover has been sacrificed').
Apotropaic ritual: warding off divine/demonic harm
Some critical scholars (de Vaux, Noth)
The blood-on-doorposts ritual has parallels in ancient Near Eastern apotropaic practices — blood or red ochre applied to doorways to ward off harmful spirits. The Passover may have begun as a pre-Israelite shepherding festival (marking the transition from winter to spring pastures) that was historicised into the Exodus narrative. The 'destroyer' (mashchit, v.23) is not YHWH himself but a sub-divine agent.
Divine recognition sign within covenant
Sarna, Childs
The blood is a covenant sign — a visible marker that identifies the household as belonging to YHWH and therefore exempt from judgment. The mechanism is not strictly substitutionary (the lamb doesn't die for the firstborn's specific sin) nor merely apotropaic (warding off evil) but covenantal: the sign identifies covenant membership, and the covenant member is protected.
The New Testament's typological reading (1 Cor 5:7; John 1:29; 1 Pet 1:19) clearly presents Christ as the Passover lamb in a substitutionary framework. The Exodus text itself emphasises divine recognition of the blood sign more than the mechanism of substitution. The covenantal and substitutionary readings are complementary rather than competing.
