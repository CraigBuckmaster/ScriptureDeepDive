Exodus 8

Plagues 2–4: Frogs, Gnats, Flies

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline

Exodus 8 records Plagues 2–4: frogs (vv.1–15), gnats/lice (vv.16–19), and flies (vv.20–32). The chapter contains two theological turning points: the magicians' first defeat ("This is the finger of God," v.19) and the first distinction between Israel and Egypt ("I will set apart the land of Goshen," v.22). Pharaoh's negotiating pattern begins here: partial concession under pressure, full retraction when pressure lifts.

Verses 1–19 — Plagues 2–3: Frogs; Gnats
6So Aaron stretched out his hand over the waters of Egypt, and the frogs came up and covered the land of Egypt.8Then Pharaoh called Moses and Aaron and said, "Plead with the LORD to take away the frogs from me and from my people, and I will let the people go to sacrifice to the LORD."15But when Pharaoh saw that there was a respite, he hardened his heart and would not listen to them, as the LORD had said.19Then the magicians said to Pharaoh, "This is the finger of God." But Pharaoh's heart was hardened, and he would not listen to them, as the LORD had said.

Hebrew Word Study

אֶצְבַּע אֱלֹהִים ʾeṣbaʿ ʾĕlōhîm — finger of God: The magicians' admission — the same phrase Jesus uses in Luke 11:20 ("If it is by the finger of God that I cast out demons...") and which appears in the Decalogue inscription (Exod 31:18). Divine agency that is visible even to those who oppose it.

וַיַּכְבֵּד wayyakbēd — he hardened / made heavy: Here Pharaoh's own act — kābēd (heavy/burdensome). The same root described the heavy labour of the slaves (5:9). Pharaoh's heart is as heavy as the burdens he placed on Israel. The vocabulary is ironic.

Context

Plague 2 (frogs) targets Heket, the frog-headed Egyptian goddess of fertility and childbirth — ironic given Pharaoh's programme of killing Hebrew children. The frogs' stench in death (v.14) mirrors the stench of the dead fish in plague 1. Each plague compounds the previous in sensory misery. The magicians' failure at plague 3 (gnats) is the first crack in Egyptian counter-power. Their "finger of God" declaration is the testimony of former opponents — the most credible possible witness.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 11:20Jesus casts out demons "by the finger of God" — the same phrase the magicians used about plague 3. Jesus is performing a greater Exodus.
  • Exod 31:18The tablets of the law are "written with the finger of God" — the same creative/judicial power.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:8–15

Pharaoh bargains — "take away the frogs and I will let the people go." Moses prays; the frogs die. The pressure lifts; Pharaoh hardens. This is the anatomy of unrepentant religion: prayer when in distress, retreat when relief comes. Pharaoh's pattern here recurs in every subsequent plague. Relief is always enough to harden him further. The pattern is a spiritual anatomy of wilful self-deception.

8:19

"This is the finger of God" — the magicians admit what Pharaoh will not. Even Egypt's own spiritual practitioners acknowledge the superior power. Pharaoh hardens in the face of his own experts' testimony. The hardening is not intellectual — it is volitional. He chooses not to see what even his own court sees clearly.

Verses 20–32 — Plague 4: Flies; Goshen Set Apart; Pharaoh Negotiates
22"But on that day I will set apart the land of Goshen, where my people dwell, so that no swarms of flies shall be there, that you may know that I am the LORD in the midst of the earth."23"Thus I will put a division between my people and your people. Tomorrow this sign shall happen."28So Pharaoh said, "I will let you go to sacrifice to the LORD your God in the wilderness; only you must not go very far away. Plead for me."32But Pharaoh hardened his heart this time also, and did not let the people go.

Hebrew Word Study

פְדוּת pĕdût — distinction / redemption: The word for the distinction God places between Goshen and Egypt (v.23) is related to pādāh — to ransom/redeem. The distinction is itself a redemptive act. God's people are set apart; the separation is the beginning of liberation-in-principle before liberation-in-fact.

כֹּבֵד עָרֹב kōbēd ʿārōb — heavy/dense swarms: ʿārōb is often translated "flies" but may include various biting insects — the root means "mixture." A dense, mixed swarm of biting insects. The weight (kōbēd) of the swarm echoes Pharaoh's heavy heart — the plague mirrors the person.

Context

The distinction placed on Goshen beginning with plague 4 is theologically significant: the later plagues do not touch Israel at all. This signals a transition in the plague narrative from universal judgment to discriminating judgment — God's people are set apart visibly. The pattern anticipates the Passover, where the blood on the doorposts is the visible mark of distinction. Pharaoh's compromise offer ("not very far away," v.28) is his first negotiating move — a half-concession that misses the point entirely.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 9:4; 11:7The distinction continues through plagues 5–10 — Israel unaffected by livestock death, boils, hail, darkness, or the final plague (with the Passover). The separation is absolute.
  • Rev 9:4In Revelation's trumpet judgments, the locusts are commanded "not to harm... those who have the seal of God on their foreheads" — the Goshen-distinction pattern in eschatological form.

Places & Geography

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:22–23

The Goshen distinction is not merely practical — it is revelatory. "That you may know that I am the LORD in the midst of the earth." The plagues are not random disasters; they are calibrated demonstrations. The fact that Israel is unaffected while Egypt is devastated makes the theological point unmistakeable: this is not a natural disaster. This is the LORD acting in discriminating, covenantal judgment.

8:28–32

Pharaoh's "not very far away" is the quintessential religious compromise: he will concede the form of the request while gutting its substance. God said "let my people go" — three days' journey into the wilderness, full and permanent freedom. Pharaoh offers "go, but stay within reach." Every subsequent negotiation follows this pattern: the concession is always a restriction of the full demand. The plagues continue because the full demand is the only acceptable answer.

Ancient Sources

Egyptian Frog Goddess Heket
Heket was the Egyptian goddess of fertility depicted with a frog's head. She was associated with childbirth and the protection of newborns. Plague 2 targeting frogs is a direct assault on this deity — and ironic given Pharaoh's programme of killing Hebrew children.
Each plague targets an Egyptian deity; plague 2 directly counters the goddess of the very children Pharaoh tried to kill.
Plague 3 — Gnats/Lice
The Hebrew kinim (gnats or lice) was possibly the first plague that could not be replicated by the magicians. Egyptian priests required ritual purity for temple service; an infestation of biting insects would defile the priesthood and disrupt temple worship throughout Egypt.
Plague 3 targets Egyptian temple worship and priestly purity.

Reception History

Augustine, City of God
The frog plague targets Egyptian religious pride — the very symbol of fertility is turned to stench and death. What a culture worships becomes the instrument of its judgment.
Calvin, Commentary
Pharaoh's repeated pattern (concede/retract) is for Calvin the paradigm of impenitence: relief removes the memory of the lesson. True repentance is changed behaviour; Pharaoh's changes last only as long as the pressure does.

Literary Structure

Plague 2 — Frogs — vv.1–15Heket targeted; Pharaoh bargains; relief; hardening
Plague 3 — Gnats — vv.16–19No warning; magicians fail; "finger of God"
Plague 4 — Flies — vv.20–32Goshen distinction begins; Pharaoh's first compromise offer
The plague triads are structured: Plague 1 warned at Nile; Plague 2 warned at palace; Plague 3 no warning. Plagues 4–6 follow the same pattern. Each triad escalates in severity. The no-warning plagues fall without any further opportunity for Pharaoh to relent.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 8 establishes two major themes for the plague narrative: the Goshen distinction (God's people set apart) and Pharaoh's negotiating pattern (concede/retract). Both will intensify through chapter 12. The magicians' "finger of God" is the most important admission in the plague sequence — even Egypt's own experts acknowledge the superior power.
