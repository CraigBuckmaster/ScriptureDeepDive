Exodus 10

Plagues 8–9: Locusts and Darkness

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref

Timeline

Exodus 10 records Plagues 8–9: locusts (vv.1–20) and darkness (vv.21–29). The chapter opens with God's explicit statement of his purpose in the hardening: "that I may show these signs of mine among them, and that you may tell in the coming generations" (vv.1–2). The chapter contains Pharaoh's most advanced negotiating concessions and Moses' final exit from Pharaoh's presence before the tenth plague.

Verses 1–20 — Plague 8: Locusts; "Egypt Is Ruined"
1Then the LORD said to Moses, "Go in to Pharaoh, for I have hardened his heart and the heart of his servants, that I may show these signs of mine among them,"2"and that you may tell in the hearing of your son and of your grandson how I have dealt harshly with the Egyptians and what signs I have done among them, that you may know that I am the LORD."7Then Pharaoh's servants said to him, "How long shall this man be a snare to us? Let the men go, that they may serve the LORD their God. Do you not yet understand that Egypt is ruined?"

Hebrew Word Study

לְמַעַן תְּסַפֵּר lĕmaʿan tĕsappēr — that you may tell: The purpose of the signs extends beyond the immediate generation — they are for the telling, for children and grandchildren, for all subsequent generations of Israel's liturgy and memory. The plagues are not just redemptive acts; they are catechetical events, designed to be retold in every Passover celebration forever.

אַרְבֶּה ʾarbeh — locusts: From rābāh (to multiply) — the plague of multiplication that devours the multiplication. The locusts' darkness (v.15: "the land was darkened") anticipates plague 9. Their completeness (v.14: "never before such locusts") mirrors the completeness language throughout the plague narrative.

Context

The purpose statement of vv.1–2 is the most complete explanation of the hardening in the plague narrative: the signs are for the children to tell, for Israel's generational memory. Pharaoh's servants' intervention (v.7: "Egypt is ruined") shows the internal pressure on Pharaoh — even his own court is breaking. He is now isolated in his hardening. His final negotiating offer (v.11: only the men may go) is a new restriction — a deliberately partial concession that Moses cannot accept.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 78:1–8The Psalmist's command to tell the children the acts of God — fulfilling the purpose of 10:2: "that you may tell in the hearing of your son and grandson."
  • Joel 2:25"I will restore to you the years that the swarming locust has eaten" — the locust as judgment and restoration, both under divine control.
  • Rev 9:3Eschatological locusts from the abyss — the Exodus pattern scaled to cosmic judgment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1–2

The hardening is stated here with its purpose most clearly: "that I may show these signs... and that you may tell." The plagues are catechetical. They are designed to be remembered, rehearsed, retold at Passover tables across every generation. The child who asks "why do we do this?" (12:26; 13:14) is to hear about the locusts, the darkness, the blood on the door. The Exodus is not just a historical event; it is the founding narrative of Israel's identity and the paradigm of all future redemption.

10:7

Pharaoh's servants break first — "Egypt is ruined." The man who could not be moved by plagues, who withstood the verification of the Goshen distinction, who confessed and retracted three times, is now the last man in his own court who still refuses. His isolation is the final stage of the hardening: everyone around him can see clearly; he alone cannot. Or will not.

Verses 21–29 — Plague 9: Darkness; Final Break with Pharaoh
21Then the LORD said to Moses, "Stretch out your hand toward heaven, that there may be darkness over the land of Egypt, a darkness to be felt."22So Moses stretched out his hand toward heaven, and there was pitch darkness in all the land of Egypt three days.23They did not see one another. Everyone stayed in place for three days. All the people of Israel had light where they lived.28Then Pharaoh said to him, "Get away from me; take care never to see my face again, for on the day you see my face you shall die."29Moses said, "As you say! I will not see your face again."

Hebrew Word Study

חֹשֶׁךְ אֲפֵלָה ḥōšek ʾăpēlāh — darkness to be felt / thick darkness: ʾăpēlāh is not ordinary night but supernatural, tangible darkness — the same darkness as Exod 14:20 and Deut 28:29. The phrase "darkness to be felt" may anticipate the plague's effect on human movement and consciousness. This is the reversal of creation light.

לְכֻלָּם אוֹר lĕkullām ʾôr — all of them had light: The Israelites had light in Goshen during three days of total Egyptian darkness. The light/dark distinction is the most theologically charged of all the Goshen separations — recalling the creation of light (Gen 1:3) and anticipating Christ as "the light of the world" (John 8:12).

Context

Plague 9 (darkness) targets Ra and Atum, the sun gods — the most important deities in the Egyptian pantheon. Pharaoh was the son of Ra; his divine legitimacy rested on the sun's power. Three days of utter darkness is a direct attack on the foundation of Egyptian royal theology. The darkness also recalls the pre-creation chaos of Gen 1:2 — it is an un-creation event. Pharaoh's explosion (v.28) is the reaction of a man who has nothing left to offer except threats. Moses' calm response ("as you say") signals that this is the last confrontation — the final plague will arrive without warning.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 1:3"Let there be light" — plague 9 reverses the first creative act. Darkness returns to Egypt; light remains in Goshen.
  • John 8:12"I am the light of the world" — Jesus as the fulfilment of the Goshen-light. Those in him have light; those outside have darkness.
  • Matt 27:45Three hours of darkness at the crucifixion — the final plague and Calvary share the darkness motif.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:21–23

Three days of palpable darkness — and Goshen has light. This is the creation narrative reversed: God separated light from darkness (Gen 1:3–4); now he removes light from Egypt and keeps it in Goshen. The theological statement is total: YHWH, not Ra, controls the light. The Egyptian king who was the son of Ra sits in three days of darkness while Hebrew slaves in Goshen go about their days in light. The reversal of power is complete before the final plague falls.

10:28–29

"You shall never see my face again" (Pharaoh); "As you say — I will not see your face again" (Moses). The final confrontation. Moses does not threaten, bargain, or argue. He simply agrees. He knows what is coming (11:1 follows immediately). The calm of Moses' departure is the calm of a man who has finished arguing with someone who has made his choice irrevocably. The final plague needs no further negotiation.

Ancient Sources

Egyptian Sun Religion
Ra (Re) was the supreme deity of the Egyptian state pantheon; Pharaoh was his son and earthly representative. The darkness plague directly attacked the foundation of Egyptian royal theology and national identity. Three days — the complete unit of time — of utter darkness was a direct challenge to the sun god's existence.
Plague 9 is the most theologically targeted of all the plagues: it attacks the central pillar of Egyptian religion.
Locust Ecology
A severe locust swarm of the density described in v.14–15 ("the land was darkened") would be consistent with the largest swarms recorded in modern East Africa. The "darkness" of the locust cloud anticipates the literal darkness of plague 9.
The two consecutive plagues (8–9) share the darkness motif — literary and ecological coherence.

Reception History

Origin, Homilies on Exodus
The three days of darkness are read typologically as the three days of Christ's death — the darkness of sin that precedes the light of resurrection. Goshen's light = the church's hope during spiritual darkness.
Calvin, Commentary
Moses' calm departure ("as you say") is for Calvin the model of prophetic composure — the person who knows God's word will accomplish what it was sent to do does not need to escalate or argue. Silence and departure are sometimes the most powerful response.

The third plague triad (7–9) ends with the final break. Plague 10 will follow without warning, without negotiation, without further hardening — the series is complete. The darkness of plague 9 and the Goshen light create the theological final image before the death of the firstborn.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 10 brings the plague sequence to its theological penultimate. The darkness plague defeats the sun god, reverses creation light, and sets Pharaoh's isolation in stark relief — surrounded by advisors urging capitulation, sitting in three days of darkness while Goshen has light. Moses' calm departure ("as you say") is the composure of a man who knows the final word belongs to God.
