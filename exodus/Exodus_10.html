Plagues 8–9: Locusts and Darkness

Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. Moses is not only the principal character of Exodus — he is its author, and the events recorded are largely his own experience. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC. The events of Exodus span approximately 80 years (Moses's birth to the Tabernacle's completion), but the book itself was written after the fact — most likely during the years at Sinai and the wilderness wandering that followed. The Tabernacle instructions and legal material (chs.19–40) may have been committed to writing earliest, as they were immediately actionable. What prompted it: Israel needed a founding narrative — a record of how God delivered them from 430 years of slavery, made them his covenant people at Sinai, and established the Tabernacle as his dwelling among them. Exodus is the story of liberation, but more than that, it is the story of covenant formation and divine habitation. It answers: How did God redeem us? What does he require of us? And how can a holy God dwell with a sinful people?

Hebrew Word Study

אַרְבֶּה ʾarbeh — locusts: From rābāh (to multiply/be numerous) — locust swarms are millions of individuals consuming everything in their path. Joel 1–2 uses the locust plague as the paradigm for divine judgment. Egyptian texts (Anastasi Papyrus) describe locust swarms destroying crops.

לְמַעַן תְּסַפֵּר lĕmaʿan tĕsappēr — that you may tell: The memorial purpose: the Exodus events are designed not just for this generation but to be told to sons and grandsons. The Passover Haggadah is the fulfilment of this command — every Seder is a retelling of the plagues.

Historical Context

Locust swarms (Schistocerca gregaria) are documented in Egyptian administrative texts — one papyrus describes crops consumed, starvation following. The eastern wind (v.13: "the LORD brought an east wind upon the land") is the sirocco (khamsin) — a hot wind from the Arabian desert that periodically devastates Egyptian agriculture. The western wind that removed the locusts (v.19) is equally natural — and equally under YHWH's command.

Context

Two new elements appear in plague eight: (1) the explicit memorial purpose — "that you may tell your son and grandson" (v.2) — establishes the Exodus as a story to be retold in every generation; (2) Pharaoh's officials break ranks and urge capitulation (v.7: "Egypt is ruined"). Pharaoh's court has reached the same conclusion as the magicians in plague three — but Pharaoh still hardens his heart. He is now isolated even from his own advisors.

  • Rev 9:3–11The fifth trumpet releases locusts as eschatological judgment — the Exodus plague as end-time template.
  • Exod 12:26–27The "tell your children" command of 10:2 is fulfilled in the Passover ritual: "What does this ceremony mean to you?" — the institutionalised retelling of the Exodus.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:2

The memorial command — "that you may tell in the hearing of your son and of your grandson how I have dealt with the Egyptians" — is one of the most important verses in Exodus for understanding the purpose of the plagues. They are not just historical events; they are theological curriculum. Every generation of Israel is to know them, retell them, and draw from them the same conclusion: "that you may know that I am the LORD." The Passover Haggadah, the Psalms (78, 105, 106, 136), Stephen's sermon in Acts 7, Hebrews 11 — all are fulfilments of this command.

10:7

"Egypt is ruined." Pharaoh's own officials say what has been obvious for eight plagues. They are begging him to release Israel. He negotiates — "who exactly is going?" — and then hardens his heart when the answer is "everyone." This is the anatomy of his bondage: he can see the destruction clearly, receive counsel clearly, almost reach the right decision — and then pull back. Each time. The hardening at this point is not imposed from outside; it is the momentum of every previous choice. He cannot let go because every "no" has made the next "yes" harder.

Verses 21–29 — Plague Nine: Darkness
21Then the Lord said to Moses, "Stretch out your hand toward the sky so that darkness spreads over Egypt—darkness that can be felt."22So Moses stretched out his hand toward the sky, and total darkness covered all Egypt for three days.23No one could see anyone else or move about for three days. Yet all the Israelites had light in the places where they lived.24Then Pharaoh summoned Moses and said, "Go, worship the Lord. Even your women and children may go with you; only leave your flocks and herds behind."25But Moses said, "You must allow us to have sacrifices and burnt offerings to present to the Lord our God.26Our livestock too must go with us; not a hoof is to be left behind. We have to use some of them in worshiping the Lord our God, and until we get there we will not know what we are to use to worship the Lord."27But the Lord hardened Pharaoh's heart, and he was not willing to let them go.28Pharaoh said to Moses, "Get out of my sight! Make sure you do not appear before me again! The day you see my face you will die."29"Just as you say," Moses replied. "I will never appear before you again."

Hebrew Word Study

חֹשֶׁךְ אֲפֵלָה ḥōšek ʾăpēlāh — thick/palpable darkness: A darkness to be "felt" — the intensified formulation. The Egyptian sun-god Ra was the supreme deity. Three days of absolute darkness is the most direct theological assault in the plague sequence: YHWH extinguishes Ra. The ninth plague is a declaration of divine supremacy over Egypt's highest god.

וּלְכָל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל הָיָה אוֹר ûlĕkol bĕnê yiśrāʾēl hāyāh ʾôr — but all the people of Israel had light: The contrast is absolute: total darkness for Egypt; light for Israel. This is the Goshen distinction at its most graphic — the same divine presence that is darkness to Egypt is light to Israel. The same principle returns at the Red Sea (14:20).

Context

The darkness plague is no-warning (like the boils) and reaches the deepest theological point of the plague sequence: Ra, the sun-god, the supreme deity of Egypt, whose image Pharaoh bears as divine son — YHWH extinguishes him for three days. The darkness is "to be felt" — not merely visual absence but a presence of darkness. Three days echoes creation (light on day one) and anticipates the three days of Jonah and the three days of Christ in the tomb. The final verse — Pharaoh's threat and Moses's acceptance of it — sets up the last and greatest plague.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 1:3"Let there be light" — YHWH is the creator of light. The darkness plague reverses creation in Egypt while preserving it in Goshen. YHWH does with Egypt's sun what he did with the world's light: he controls it absolutely.
  • Exod 14:20The pillar of cloud: light to Israel, darkness to Egypt. The same polarity at the Red Sea.
  • Matt 27:45Three hours of darkness at the crucifixion — the ninth plague's three days of darkness as typological background.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:21–23

A darkness to be felt. Three days of absolute darkness in the land of Egypt — while Israel has light in Goshen. This is YHWH's answer to Ra, the sun-god. The supreme deity of Egypt's pantheon is simply turned off. For three days. Pharaoh, who claimed to be the divine son of Ra, sits in darkness he cannot dispel. YHWH does not argue with Ra; he simply extinguishes him. The theological simplicity is overwhelming: there is no contest. There is YHWH, and there is darkness where other gods claim to rule.

10:28–29

"Never see my face again, or you will die." Moses answers: "As you say. I will not see your face again." The relationship between Moses and Pharaoh has reached its end. The next time Moses appears before Pharaoh will be midnight of the tenth plague (11:4–8) — and after that, never again. Pharaoh's threat is his last act of power. Moses's acceptance is not submission but prophetic knowledge: the next encounter will be on YHWH's terms, not Pharaoh's. The breaking of the relationship prepares for the breaking of Pharaoh's resistance.

Ancient Sources

Ra and Pharaonic Theology
Pharaoh was considered the divine son of Ra (the sun-god), the embodiment of his rule on earth. Ra's daily journey across the sky was the theological cornerstone of Egyptian religion.
The darkness plague targets Egyptian theology at its pinnacle — the supreme deity's primary domain.
Khamsin / Sirocco
The hot desert wind (khamsin) can bring sandstorms that blot out the sun for days — known as "the darkness that can be felt." Whether the plague was a hypernatural khamsin or something entirely different, the description matches an Egyptian meteorological phenomenon taken to an extreme.
The "felt darkness" description is culturally grounded.

Reception History

Matt 27:45
Three hours of darkness at the crucifixion during Jesus's death — early Christian interpreters read this as the darkness plague's typological fulfilment: as YHWH darkened Egypt's sun-god in judgment, so the sun is darkened at the death of the Son of God.
Rev 16:10
The fifth bowl judgment darkens the throne of the beast — the darkness plague as eschatological template, targeting the center of anti-God power.

The plague sequence is approaching its climax. Nine plagues; nine hardening events. Pharaoh is now isolated from his court, stripped of all his gods' domains, sitting in darkness. One plague remains — and it will be unlike all the others: no extended hardening-sequence, no recovery, no negotiation. The tenth plague will be final.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 10 brings the plague sequence to the edge of its climax. Ra is extinguished; Pharaoh's court has given up; the memorial command has been given. Egypt is ruined. One plague remains — and the tenth will not be a demonstration of power. It will be the death of the firstborn, the mirror of Pharaoh's own decree, the price that finally breaks the hardened heart. Chapter 11 will announce it; chapter 12 will execute it.
