Exodus 7

Aaron's Serpent; First Plague — Nile to Blood

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline
Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. Moses is not only the principal character of Exodus — he is its author, and the events recorded are largely his own experience.

When written

Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC. The events of Exodus span approximately 80 years (Moses's birth to the Tabernacle's completion), but the book itself was written after the fact — most likely during the years at Sinai and the wilderness wandering that followed. The Tabernacle instructions and legal material (chs.19–40) may have been committed to writing earliest, as they were immediately actionable.

What prompted it

Israel needed a founding narrative — a record of how God delivered them from 430 years of slavery, made them his covenant people at Sinai, and established the Tabernacle as his dwelling among them. Exodus is the story of liberation, but more than that, it is the story of covenant formation and divine habitation. It answers: How did God redeem us? What does he require of us? And how can a holy God dwell with a sinful people?

Verses 1–13 — Aaron's Staff; the First Plague Announced
1Then the Lord said to Moses, "See, I have made you like God to Pharaoh, and your brother Aaron will be your prophet.2You are to say everything I command you, and your brother Aaron is to tell Pharaoh to let the Israelites go out of his country.3But I will harden Pharaoh's heart, and though I multiply my signs and wonders in Egypt,4he will not listen to you. Then I will lay my hand on Egypt and with mighty acts of judgment I will bring out my divisions, my people the Israelites.5And the Egyptians will know that I am the Lord when I stretch out my hand against Egypt and bring the Israelites out of it."6Moses and Aaron did just as the Lord commanded them.7Moses was eighty years old and Aaron eighty-three when they spoke to Pharaoh.8The Lord said to Moses and Aaron,9"When Pharaoh says to you, 'Prove yourselves by performing a miracle,' then say to Aaron, 'Take your staff and throw it down before Pharaoh,' and it will become a snake."10So Moses and Aaron went to Pharaoh and did just as the Lord commanded. Aaron threw his staff down in front of Pharaoh and his officials, and it became a snake.11Pharaoh then summoned wise men and sorcerers, and the Egyptian magicians also did the same things by their secret arts:12Each one threw down his staff and it became a snake. But Aaron's staff swallowed up their staffs.13Yet Pharaoh's heart became hard and he would not listen to them, just as the Lord had said.

Hebrew Word Study

אֲחַזֵּק ʾăḥazzēq — I will harden: The hardening of Pharaoh's heart is the most theologically discussed element of the plague narrative. The OT uses three verbs: ḥāzaq (strengthen/harden, God's action), kābēd (make heavy, God's action), and qāšāh (be stubborn, Pharaoh's action). In the early plagues Pharaoh hardens his own heart; later God confirms the hardening. Both are true simultaneously.

תַּנִּין tannîn — serpent / sea-monster: Tannîn is a large reptile — sometimes the great sea-dragon (Isa 27:1; Job 41), here a large snake. The Egyptians held serpents as sacred (the uraeus cobra on Pharaoh's crown symbolised divine protection). Aaron's tannîn swallowing the Egyptian serpents is a direct challenge to Egyptian serpent-theology.

Historical Context

Egyptian magicians' ability to manipulate serpents is documented — a text from the First Intermediate Period describes a magician handling serpents. The uraeus cobra was the symbol of pharaonic power and divine protection. The staff-to-serpent sign directly confronts Egypt's most potent religious symbol: YHWH's serpent swallows Pharaoh's serpent.

Context

The framework speech (vv.1–7) is crucial for reading all ten plagues. Three elements: (1) purpose — "the Egyptians shall know that I am the LORD"; (2) mechanism — the hardening; (3) scope — "great acts of judgment." The plagues are not primarily punishments but revelations. Each plague targets a domain Egypt claims is controlled by one of its gods — YHWH systematically dismantles the Egyptian theological worldview domain by domain.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 9:17–18Paul uses the hardening of Pharaoh's heart to discuss divine sovereignty and human responsibility — both are real; neither cancels the other.
  • Rev 16The seven bowl judgments echo the ten plagues — blood (bowl 2), darkness (bowl 5), etc. Exodus's plague sequence is the typological template for eschatological judgment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:3–5

The hardening is stated before the plagues begin — this is God's declared purpose, not a post-hoc explanation. The ten plagues will accomplish two things simultaneously: liberate Israel and reveal YHWH to Egypt. Both require Pharaoh's resistance. If Pharaoh had released Israel after the first plague, Egypt would not have known YHWH. The hardening is not injustice — it is the mechanism of maximum revelation. God uses Pharaoh's natural stubbornness and confirms it, that his own glory might be fully displayed.

7:12

Aaron's serpent swallowing the Egyptian serpents is the first plague's sign in miniature: YHWH's power over Egypt's power. The magicians can replicate the form (serpent-making) but cannot replicate the substance (swallowing). This pattern repeats: the magicians match the first two plagues, partially match the third, fail from the fourth onward. The point is not that Egyptian magic is real — it is that YHWH's power is categorically greater and finally incomparable.

Verses 14–25 — The First Plague: Water Turned to Blood
14Then the Lord said to Moses, "Pharaoh's heart is unyielding; he refuses to let the people go.15Go to Pharaoh in the morning as he goes out to the river. Confront him on the bank of the Nile, and take in your hand the staff that was changed into a snake.16Then say to him, 'The Lord, the God of the Hebrews, has sent me to say to you: Let my people go, so that they may worship me in the wilderness. But until now you have not listened.17This is what the Lord says: By this you will know that I am the Lord: With the staff that is in my hand I will strike the water of the Nile, and it will be changed into blood.18The fish in the Nile will die, and the river will stink; the Egyptians will not be able to drink its water.'"19The Lord said to Moses, "Tell Aaron, 'Take your staff and stretch out your hand over the waters of Egypt—over the streams and canals, over the ponds and all the reservoirs—and they will turn to blood.' Blood will be everywhere in Egypt, even in vessels of wood and stone."20Moses and Aaron did just as the Lord had commanded. He raised his staff in the presence of Pharaoh and his officials and struck the water of the Nile, and all the water was changed into blood.21The fish in the Nile died, and the river smelled so bad that the Egyptians could not drink its water. Blood was everywhere in Egypt.22But the Egyptian magicians did the same things by their secret arts, and Pharaoh's heart became hard; he would not listen to Moses and Aaron, just as the Lord had said.23Instead, he turned and went into his palace, and did not take even this to heart.24And all the Egyptians dug along the Nile to get drinking water, because they could not drink the water of the river.25Seven days passed after the Lord struck the Nile.

Hebrew Word Study

יְאֹר yĕʾōr — the Nile: The Egyptian Nile (from Egyptian itr.w, "river"). It was the source of Egypt's entire existence — fertility, food, commerce. Its annual flooding was sacred. The Nile was identified with Osiris (and Hapi, god of the flood). Turning it to blood is a direct assault on Egypt's most fundamental deity and life-source.

דָּם dām — blood: Blood signals death throughout the OT. The life is in the blood (Lev 17:11). The Nile becoming blood means Egypt's life-source is dying. The irony: Egypt drowned Israelite babies in the Nile (1:22); the Nile is now blood. The instrument of Israel's killing is turned against Egypt.

Historical Context

The Ipuwer Papyrus (Egyptian, c.1850 or 1650 BC, date disputed) describes a catastrophe in Egypt: "The river is blood... plague is throughout the land." While some scholars see this as evidence for the plagues, most date it earlier. Regardless, it shows the Egyptians had a literary category for such national catastrophe. The Nile's red discolouration from algae blooms (red tide) is a known natural phenomenon — but the text emphasises the completeness and immediacy of the event as miraculous.

Context

The first plague targets the Nile — Egypt's most sacred resource and the source of its agricultural power. The Egyptian gods of the Nile (Hapi, Osiris) cannot protect their domain. Fish die; water is undrinkable for seven days (v.25). The magicians' ability to replicate the plague (v.22) does not diminish YHWH — they cannot reverse it, only add to the problem. Pharaoh's response (v.23: "he did not take even this to heart") sets the pattern for the entire plague sequence: each judgment is met with hardened refusal.

Places & Geography

The Nile
Egypt's lifeline
The longest river in the world (6,650 km). Its annual flooding deposited the fertile silt that made Egyptian civilization possible. Its source, timing, and volume were mysteries to the Egyptians — attributed to the gods.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:19–21

The blood plague is total: rivers, canals, ponds, pools, wooden vessels, stone vessels — every water source in Egypt. This is not a localised event. The comprehensiveness is the point: YHWH does not share the Nile with Hapi; he overrules it entirely. Fish die; the land stinks; the water is undrinkable. For seven days (v.25) Egypt is without its primary resource. And Pharaoh hardens his heart.

7:23

The most chilling verse in the plague sequence so far: "Pharaoh turned and went into his house, and he did not take even this to heart." He is unmoved. The hardening has reached the point where catastrophe does not register as meaningful. This is the anatomy of hardened unbelief: not intellectual inability but moral refusal. The evidence is visible; the heart is closed. This pattern will intensify through nine more plagues before the tenth breaks it.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Pharaoh
The hardened king
His dismissal of each sign — including Aaron's serpent swallowing the Egyptian serpents — shows the pattern: the evidence is real, the heart is closed.
Aaron
The sign-performer
Primarily the instrument in the early plagues — he stretches the staff. Moses speaks; Aaron acts. Their complementarity is the concession of 4:14 made functional.
The Egyptian Magicians
Jannes and Jambres (2 Tim 3:8)
Named in tradition but not in text. They can replicate but not reverse. Their failure escalates through the plague sequence.

Ancient Sources

Ipuwer Papyrus
Egyptian lamentation text describing national catastrophe including a river of blood, widespread death, and social collapse. Disputed dating (c.1850 or 1650 BC).
Whether or not directly related to the Exodus plagues, it demonstrates Egypt's literary capacity to describe such catastrophe.
Egyptian Serpent Cult
The uraeus cobra adorned Pharaoh's crown as the divine protector. Snake-handling by Egyptian priests is documented in magical papyri.
Aaron's serpent swallowing Pharaoh's is a deliberate theological challenge to Egyptian royal theology.

Reception History

Revelation 16:3–4
The second and third bowl judgments turn sea and rivers to blood — the first plague pattern applied to eschatological judgment.
Origen, Homilies
The serpent that swallows the Egyptian serpents typifies Christ's cross swallowing the power of death — "death is swallowed up in victory" (1 Cor 15:54).

Literary Structure

Framework — vv.1–7Commission renewed; hardening explained; purpose stated: "Egyptians shall know"
Sign — vv.8–13Staff/serpent — Aaron's swallows Egyptian serpents; Pharaoh hardened
Plague 1 — warning — vv.14–18Stand by the Nile; YHWH's announcement
Plague 1 — execution — vv.19–21Water to blood — rivers, canals, vessels
Response — vv.22–25Magicians replicate; Pharaoh unmoved; seven days
The plague pattern established here recurs: warning → execution → Pharaoh hardens → interval. Some plagues have no warning (surprise); some have a warning but no hardening phrase. The variations are intentional, building intensity toward the tenth.

Theological Themes Tracker
