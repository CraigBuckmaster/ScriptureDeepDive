Exodus 4

The Signs; The Bridegroom of Blood

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline

Exodus 4 completes the commission sequence (chs.3–4) with Moses' remaining objections and the three confirming signs (staff/serpent, leprous hand, water-to-blood). The chapter also contains the mysterious "bridegroom of blood" episode (vv.24–26), the reunion with Aaron, and Moses' arrival in Egypt. The five objections of chs.3–4 form one of the most sustained divine-human dialogues in the OT.

Verses 1–17 — Three Signs; Five Objections Completed
1Then Moses answered, "But behold, they will not believe me or listen to my voice, for they will say, 'The LORD did not appear to you.'"2The LORD said to him, "What is that in your hand?" He said, "A staff."3And he said, "Throw it on the ground." So he threw it on the ground, and it became a serpent, and Moses ran from it.10But Moses said to the LORD, "Oh, my Lord, I am not eloquent, either in the past or since you have spoken to your servant, but I am slow of speech and of tongue."11Then the LORD said to him, "Who has made man's mouth? Who makes him mute, or deaf, or seeing, or blind? Is it not I, the LORD?"

Hebrew Word Study

מַטֶּה maṭṭeh — staff: The staff becomes the serpent (naḥāš) and back again. The same staff will part the Red Sea (14:16), strike the rock (17:6), and be held up in battle (17:11). It is Moses' most ordinary possession — and God's sign that ordinary things in surrendered hands become instruments of extraordinary purpose.

כָּבַד פֶּה kābad peh — heavy of mouth / slow of speech: Moses' fifth objection: I cannot speak. The same root (kbd) describes Pharaoh's hardened (heavy) heart throughout chs.7–14. The irony is structural: the man with the heavy mouth will face the man with the heavy heart.

Context

The three signs (staff/serpent, leprous hand, water-to-blood) are not magic tricks — they are credentials. Each sign involves reversal: life becomes death-symbol (serpent) then life again; clean becomes unclean then clean; water becomes blood. They preview the plague sequence and demonstrate that YHWH controls the very elements Pharaoh's magicians serve. The speech objection (v.10) is Moses' most personal — and receives the most pointed answer: "Who made your mouth?"

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 7:22Stephen: Moses was "mighty in words and deeds" — either the speech impediment was healed, or Luke reads his Egyptian eloquence as the dominant fact.
  • 2 Cor 12:9"My power is made perfect in weakness" — the pattern of the burning bush commission. God regularly chooses those who cannot so that the power is clearly his.
  • 1 Cor 1:27God chose what is foolish, what is weak — the Mosaic pattern becomes the Pauline principle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:2

"What is that in your hand?" God does not ask Moses to acquire something new; he asks about what Moses already possesses. The shepherd's staff — the emblem of forty years of wilderness obscurity — is the instrument God will use to reshape history. God's pattern: ordinary surrendered to him becomes extraordinary.

4:11

"Who has made man's mouth?" The answer to Moses' self-pity is a question about God's sovereignty over creation. The Creator of the mouth is not limited by the limitations of any mouth. Moses' weakness does not diminish God's power; it displays it more clearly. Every one of Moses' objections has been answered with a promise of divine sufficiency. The fifth is answered with a question that ends the argument.

Verses 18–31 — The Bridegroom of Blood; Reunion with Aaron; Israel Believes
24At a lodging place on the way the LORD met him and sought to put him to death.25Then Zipporah took a flint and cut off her son's foreskin and touched Moses' feet with it and said, "Surely you are a bridegroom of blood to me."27The LORD said to Aaron, "Go into the wilderness to meet Moses." So he went and met him at the mountain of God and kissed him.31And the people believed; and when they heard that the LORD had visited the people of Israel and that he had seen their affliction, they bowed their heads and worshiped.

Hebrew Word Study

חֲתַן דָּמִים ḥătan dāmîm — bridegroom of blood: One of the most enigmatic phrases in the OT. ḥātan can mean "bridegroom" or "father-in-law" (related to circumcision covenant). The blood of circumcision averts death — the covenant rite becomes the shield. The episode foreshadows the Passover: blood that averts the destroyer.

צֹר ṣōr — flint: Flint is the ancient material for circumcision (cf. Josh 5:2–3). The detail is archaic and authentic — it preserves very early practice before metal knives replaced flint.

Context

The "bridegroom of blood" episode (vv.24–26) is the most obscure passage in Exodus. The most natural reading: Moses had neglected to circumcise his son, violating the covenant sign of Gen 17. God, who has just commissioned Moses to lead the covenant people into their covenant obligations, cannot use a messenger who himself is out of covenant. Zipporah's quick action — circumcising the son and touching Moses with the blood — averts divine judgment. The covenant sign must be in the body of the deliverer's household before he can demand it of Pharaoh.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 17:14The covenant of circumcision: "any uncircumcised male... shall be cut off from his people; he has broken my covenant."
  • Josh 5:2–9Israel re-circumcised at Gilgal after the wilderness — the covenant sign re-established before Canaan. Both passages show that covenant entry requires the covenant sign.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:24–26

God "sought to put him to death" — the same God who just commissioned Moses, who will protect Israel from the destroyer at Passover, now threatens Moses himself. The reason: circumcision neglected. The covenant has requirements even for those God commissions. The messenger cannot stand outside the covenant he is called to proclaim. Zipporah's decisive act saves Moses' life. The blood of covenant circumcision averts death — a pattern that will recur at every Passover door.

4:31

The people believed and worshipped. The signs worked — the first audience, the elders of Israel, received the message. Before confronting Pharaoh, Moses has confirmation that Israel will follow. But the same people who worship here will murmur and rebel throughout the wilderness. The belief of v.31 is real but fragile.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Moses
The reluctant prophet
Five objections overcome by five divine responses. He returns to Egypt as a commissioned, equipped, and accompanied deliverer.
Aaron
The spokesman
Given to Moses as his "mouth" (v.16). His eloquence complements Moses' hesitancy — but Aaron's weakness for the crowd will emerge in ch.32.
Zipporah
The decisive wife
Her quick action in the "bridegroom of blood" episode saves Moses' life. Unsung and crucial.

Ancient Sources

Circumcision with Flint
The use of flint (ṣōr) for circumcision is attested in Josh 5:2–3 and is consistent with ancient practice predating widespread metal tool use. The detail in v.25 is an authentic archaic marker.
The flint detail preserves very early tradition.
Egyptian Court Protocol
Egyptian texts describe the staff as a symbol of administrative authority. Moses' staff-sign is also a challenge to Egyptian symbolic power — the snake (uraeus) was the emblem of pharaonic authority.
The serpent-staff sign directly challenges Pharaoh's own royal symbol.

Reception History

Augustine, Questions on Exodus
The bridegroom of blood episode is read typologically: the blood of circumcision is the type of Christ's blood, which averts divine judgment. Zipporah prefigures the church applying the blood of Christ.
Luther, Lectures on Exodus
Moses' five objections are for Luther examples of "the theology of the cross" — God chooses the weak, the reluctant, the stammering, to shame the confident and eloquent.

Literary Structure

Bridegroom of blood — vv.24–26Crisis and aversion; covenant sign required
Reunion & reception — vv.27–31Aaron meets Moses; elders believe and worship
The five-objection sequence (3:11, 3:13, 4:1, 4:10, 4:13) is the most sustained negotiation between God and a human commissioner in Scripture. Each objection receives a patient, specific answer. The fifth (v.13: "please send someone else") finally provokes divine anger — but even then, God provides Aaron rather than withdrawing the call.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 4 completes the commissioning sequence. Every objection has been answered; every credential provided. Moses leaves for Egypt equipped, accompanied, and under covenant obligation. The "bridegroom of blood" episode shows that covenant requirements apply to the deliverer himself before he can demand them of others.
