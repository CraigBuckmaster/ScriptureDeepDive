Exodus 4

Moses' Objections; Signs; Aaron; Return to Egypt

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–17 — Moses' Objections; Signs and Aaron
1Moses answered, "What if they do not believe me or listen to me and say, 'The Lord did not appear to you'?"2Then the Lord said to him, "What is that in your hand?" "A staff," he replied.3The Lord said, "Throw it on the ground." Moses threw it on the ground and it became a snake, and he ran from it.4Then the Lord said to him, "Reach out your hand and take it by the tail." So Moses reached out and took hold of the snake and it turned back into a staff in his hand.5"This," said the Lord, "is so that they may believe that the Lord, the God of their fathers—the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac and the God of Jacob—has appeared to you."6Then the Lord said, "Put your hand inside your cloak." So Moses put his hand into his cloak, and when he took it out, the skin was leprous—it had become as white as snow.7"Now put it back into your cloak," he said. So Moses put his hand back into his cloak, and when he took it out, it was restored, like the rest of his flesh.8Then the Lord said, "If they do not believe you or pay attention to the first sign, they may believe the second.9But if they do not believe these two signs or listen to you, take some water from the Nile and pour it on the dry ground. The water you take from the river will become blood on the ground."10Moses said to the Lord, "Pardon your servant, Lord. I have never been eloquent, neither in the past nor since you have spoken to your servant. I am slow of speech and tongue."11The Lord said to him, "Who gave human beings their mouths? Who makes them deaf or mute? Who gives them sight or makes them blind? Is it not I, the Lord?12Now go; I will help you speak and will teach you what to say."13But Moses said, "Pardon your servant, Lord. Please send someone else."14Then the Lord's anger burned against Moses and he said, "What about your brother, Aaron the Levite? I know he can speak well. He is already on his way to meet you, and he will be glad to see you.15You shall speak to him and put words in his mouth; I will help both of you speak and will teach you what to do.16He will speak to the people for you, and it will be as if he were your mouth and as if you were God to him.17But take this staff in your hand so you can perform the signs with it."

Hebrew Word Study

כְּבַד פֶּה kĕbad peh — heavy of mouth / slow of speech: Literally "heavy of mouth and heavy of tongue." The same adjective (kābēd, heavy) is used for Pharaoh's hardened heart throughout the plague narratives. Moses sees his speech as a burden; God will use Pharaoh's hardened heart — and Moses's heavy mouth — both for his glory.

מַטֶּה maṭṭeh — staff: The ordinary shepherd's staff becomes the staff of God (v.20: "the staff of God"). The pattern of God using the ordinary — a bush, a basket, a staff — to accomplish the extraordinary is Exodus's recurring motif.

Historical Context

None

Context

Moses's five objections (3:11; 3:13; 4:1; 4:10; 4:13) are a literary structure — each answered by a divine word or sign. The fifth is simply: "please send someone else," which provokes the first recorded divine anger in Exodus. The five objections are not false modesty but genuine inadequacy — the broken man of ch.2 who failed at his own initiative. God's response is not to find a more qualified person but to work through weakness, equipping as he goes.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Cor 1:27God chose the weak things of the world to shame the strong — Moses's "heavy mouth" is the paradigm case.
  • 2 Cor 12:9"My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness" — Paul reads his own thorn in the light of Moses's objections.
  • Jer 1:6Jeremiah: "I do not know how to speak, for I am only a youth." The pattern of prophetic commissioning includes genuine protest of inadequacy.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:11

"Who has made man's mouth? Who makes him mute, or deaf, or seeing, or blind? Is it not I, the LORD?" This is one of the most arresting divine responses in Scripture. Moses says: I cannot speak well. God says: I made mouths. The one who designed human speech is asking Moses to speak. If God made the instrument, he can use the instrument — including broken ones. The answer to every "I can't" is "I AM."

4:13–14

"Please send someone else." This is the only objection that makes God angry. The first four were honest inadequacy; the fifth is refusal. God's anger does not remove Moses from the task — it grants the concession of Aaron while keeping Moses as the primary agent. The lesson: God accommodates human weakness but does not accept abdication of the calling he has given.

4:1–2

Moses' second and third objections (4:1: "they will not believe me"; 4:10: "I am not eloquent") receive signs as answers. Sarna notes the three signs — staff to serpent, leprous hand, water to blood — are arranged in escalating power: the first is reversible at will (4:4), the second requires divine action (4:7), the third will actually occur in Egypt (4:9). They function as credentials for Moses' audience but also as a preview of the plagues. The serpent (nachash) foreshadows the defeat of Egypt's serpent power.

4:10

"I am slow of speech and slow of tongue" — Sarna identifies two possible meanings: a literal speech impediment (possibly a stammer, as Josephus suggests, Ant. 2.271), or the conventional self-deprecation of the prophet who feels unqualified. Either reading is consistent with Moses' general posture of inadequacy throughout the call narrative. God's answer — "Who gave man his mouth?" (4:11) — is the definitive counter: the God who created speech can use any vessel.

4:1–3

The transformation of Moses' staff into a serpent (nāḥāš) and back is, Alter notes, both a credential and a foreshadowing. The staff that will part the sea, strike water from rock, and hold up Israel's hands in battle begins as a simple shepherd's rod — then becomes a snake, then a rod again. Alter: "The ordinary tool of Moses' vocation is invested with the power of the extraordinary." The shepherd's crook becomes the instrument of national history. The transformation is not random — it is a declaration that ordinary means will carry divine power.

4:13–14

"Please send someone else" — Moses' final objection is the most naked. He has run out of specific reasons and simply does not want to go. Alter notes this is the only point in the call narrative where God's anger is kindled against Moses (4:14). Even the anger is gracious — God provides Aaron rather than withdrawing the commission. The patience of the divine call outlasts the stubbornness of the human recipient. Moses' reluctance, paradoxically, makes his eventual obedience more credible.

4:11

"Who gave man his mouth?" — Calvin: "God here claims as his own the faculty of speech, to teach us that all the gifts of nature flow from himself alone." This becomes a cornerstone of Calvin's doctrine of common grace: every human capacity — speech, reason, skill — is a divine gift, not a human achievement. The argument also functions pastorally: if God made the mouth, God can use any mouth. No one is disqualified by natural inadequacy from being used by divine grace.

4:14

Aaron as a provision of grace: Calvin notes that Aaron's assignment is not God's Plan B but a gift to Moses' weakness. "God, in his goodness, bears with Moses's feeble faith, and grants him a companion to encourage him." Calvin sees in this the pattern of the church: no one is called alone; God always provides community, fellowship, and support for his servants. The solitary prophet is not God's normal pattern. Moses needs Aaron; all of us need others.

4:11

The NET note on "who makes someone mute or deaf" notes this verse is one of the OT's strongest assertions of God's sovereignty over disability. The fourfold question — who makes mute, deaf, seeing, or blind? — asserts divine causation over the full range of human sensory capacity. The NET translators note this is not a theodicy for suffering but a ground for confidence in God's ability to use Moses regardless of his limitations. The creator of speech is not limited by its imperfections in a given vessel.

4:16

"He will be a mouth for you, and you will be as God to him" — the NET note observes this is a remarkable statement of delegated prophetic authority. Moses will be to Aaron as God (Elohim) — not claiming divine status but functioning in the role of the one who gives the word and authorises the messenger. The pattern is: God → Moses → Aaron → people. This chain of prophetic mediation becomes the structural model for the whole Mosaic tradition: the prophet speaks what God gives; the priest/leader communicates it to the people.

4:1

Moses' second objection — "they will not believe me" — is answered with three signs: a staff turned to snake, a leprous hand restored, water turned to blood. These are not random tricks; each sign attacks a dimension of Egyptian power. The Nile-to-blood sign previews the first plague. The signs prove that the God who sends Moses is the God who controls the natural order.

4:10–12

Moses' fourth objection — "I am not eloquent" — receives the most direct divine rebuke: "Who made your mouth?" God does not promise Moses eloquence; he promises his presence. The question shifts the entire frame: inadequacy is irrelevant when the Creator of speech is the one speaking through you.

4:14

God's anger at Moses (v.14) is notable — the only place in the call narrative where divine displeasure surfaces. Moses' persistent objections are wearing on divine patience. The concession of Aaron is not ideal; it is accommodation to unbelief. The Aaron-as-spokesman arrangement will later cause significant trouble (ch.32).

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:2–4

The staff-to-serpent sign is layered with Egyptian resonance. The serpent (nachash) was a powerful symbol in Egyptian religion — the uraeus cobra on Pharaoh's crown represented royal power and divine protection. Moses' staff becoming a serpent, then being mastered by Moses' hand, enacts the superiority of Israel's God over the symbols of Egyptian sovereignty before a single word is spoken to Pharaoh.

4:10

Moses' claim to be 'not a man of words' (lo ish devarim) is striking given the literary sophistication of the speeches attributed to him throughout the Pentateuch. Sarna notes this is not necessarily biographical embarrassment but a theological point: Moses' eloquence, when it comes, will be evidence of divine empowerment, not natural ability. The objection sets up the fulfilment.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:1–9

The three signs are presented in descending order of drama: staff-to-snake (transformation of nature), leprous hand (transformation of the body), water-to-blood (preview of plague). Alter notes the escalating rhetoric — each sign is introduced with 'if they will not believe,' building a conditional chain. The third sign is never actually performed for Israel; it is held in reserve for Pharaoh and Egypt.

4:13–17

Moses' final objection — 'send someone else' — drops the pretence of specific inadequacies and reveals the real issue: he simply does not want to go. The divine response is anger (v.14), then accommodation. The Aaron concession is narratively significant: it introduces the character who will be both Moses' voice and his greatest liability. The narrative does not comment on the irony; the reader assembles it from the later chapters.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:3–4

Calvin on the snake-staff sign: 'God condescended to accommodate himself to the weakness of Moses, and therefore chose to confirm his faith by signs.' The signs are not for Pharaoh's benefit first but for Moses'. God knows his servant needs proof before he can give proof to others. The pedagogy moves from Moses' faith to Israel's faith to Pharaoh's confrontation — in that order.

4:11

'Who made man's mouth?' — Calvin: 'By this question God reproves Moses for his excessive distrust, since he should have immediately reflected that he who created the faculty of speech could easily give fluency to his servant.' The argument is from creation to providence: the Creator of the organ does not lack the ability to operate the organ. Moses' objection is a failure of theological logic, not merely of courage.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:6–7

The NET note on the leprous hand sign explains that the Hebrew tsara'at (often translated 'leprosy') covers a range of skin conditions, not necessarily modern Hansen's disease. The sign's significance is that both inflicting and healing the condition demonstrate divine sovereignty over the body — creation and restoration equally in God's hand.

4:24–26

The cryptic 'bridegroom of blood' episode is one of Exodus's most puzzling passages. The NET note surveys the interpretive options: (1) God attacks Moses for failing to circumcise his son; (2) the son is the object of the attack; (3) Zipporah's words avert divine wrath through a symbolic circumcision. The note favours (1) — Moses, about to call Israel to covenant faithfulness, had neglected the covenant sign in his own household.

Verses 18–31 — Return to Egypt; the Circumcision; Israel Believes
18Then Moses went back to Jethro his father-in-law and said to him, "Let me return to my own people in Egypt to see if any of them are still alive." Jethro said, "Go, and I wish you well."19Now the Lord had said to Moses in Midian, "Go back to Egypt, for all those who wanted to kill you are dead."20So Moses took his wife and sons, put them on a donkey and started back to Egypt. And he took the staff of God in his hand.21The Lord said to Moses, "When you return to Egypt, see that you perform before Pharaoh all the wonders I have given you the power to do. But I will harden his heart so that he will not let the people go.22Then say to Pharaoh, 'This is what the Lord says: Israel is my firstborn son,23and I told you, "Let my son go, so he may worship me." But you refused to let him go; so I will kill your firstborn son.'"24At a lodging place on the way, the Lord met Moses and was about to kill him.25But Zipporah took a flint knife, cut off her son's foreskin and touched Moses' feet with it. "Surely you are a bridegroom of blood to me," she said.26So the Lord let him alone. (At that time she said "bridegroom of blood," referring to circumcision.)27The Lord said to Aaron, "Go into the wilderness to meet Moses." So he met Moses at the mountain of God and kissed him.28Then Moses told Aaron everything the Lord had sent him to say, and also about all the signs he had commanded him to perform.29Moses and Aaron brought together all the elders of the Israelites,30and Aaron told them everything the Lord had said to Moses. He also performed the signs before the people,31and they believed. And when they heard that the Lord was concerned about them and had seen their misery, they bowed down and worshiped.

Hebrew Word Study

בְּכֹרִי bĕkōrî — my firstborn: Israel as God's firstborn son — the most intimate covenant term. The firstborn receives the special status, inheritance, and responsibility. Israel's identity is not slaves but sons; not subjects but heirs. This designation will be answered by the tenth plague: "let my son go... or I will kill your firstborn."

חֲתַן דָּמִים ḥătan dāmîm — bridegroom of blood: One of the most obscure phrases in the OT. Zipporah's act circumcises the son Moses had neglected to circumcise, averting the divine threat. The blood of circumcision — the covenant sign — protects Moses. The phrase anticipates the blood of the Passover lamb.

Context

The Zipporah episode (vv.24–26) is famously difficult. The most natural reading: Moses had not circumcised his son (neglecting the Abrahamic covenant sign, Gen 17:14), and God's attack is the consequence. Zipporah acts to avert it, performing the circumcision herself. The episode establishes a crucial principle before the Exodus begins: the deliverer cannot be uncircumcised — uncircumcision would place him under the same covenant curse he is being sent to witness executed on Egypt.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 17:14Uncircumcision = being cut off from the people. Moses as an uncircumcised father would be under covenant judgment — God's attack is the enforcement of his own covenant law.
  • Exod 12:48Circumcision is required to participate in the Passover — the principle established here in Moses's own household.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:22–23

"Israel is my firstborn son." This is the interpretive key to the entire plague sequence and especially the tenth plague. The confrontation is not primarily political (Israel vs. Egypt) but filial: God's firstborn son vs. Pharaoh's firstborn son. Every plague is a contest between what Egypt worships and who Israel's God is. The tenth plague is not cruelty — it is the precise, symmetrical answer to Pharaoh's own decree (Exod 1:22: throw every Hebrew son into the Nile). The one who killed Israel's sons will lose his own.

4:24–26

The Zipporah episode is placed here — between commission and Egypt — for a reason. God cannot send an uncircumcised man to lead the circumcised people out. The covenant sign matters. Zipporah's act is uncomfortable but decisive: she performs what Moses has neglected, and the blood of circumcision — covenant blood — protects her husband. The blood theme that will climax in the Passover begins here, in a lodging place on the road to Egypt.

4:21

"I will harden Pharaoh's heart" — the hardening of Pharaoh's heart is first announced here, before the plagues begin, and Sarna devotes careful attention to its significance. The Hebrew uses three different roots: ḥāzaq (to make strong/firm), kābēd (to make heavy), and qāšāh (to harden). The theological and ethical problem — is Pharaoh responsible if God hardens his heart? — Sarna addresses by noting the pattern: in the first five plagues Pharaoh hardens his own heart; only after the sixth does God harden it. God's hardening ratifies and confirms a direction Pharaoh has already chosen.

4:24–26

The circumcision episode — the most enigmatic passage in Exodus — is treated carefully by Sarna. Moses or his son is attacked by the LORD; Zipporah circumcises the son and touches the foreskin to "his feet" (euphemism for genitals, as in Ruth 3:7–8), declaring "you are a bridegroom of blood to me." The passage is syntactically and semantically difficult; the referents are unclear. Sarna suggests Moses had neglected to circumcise his son during the wilderness years, and the divine attack is the immediate consequence. The covenantal mark is not optional for the one commissioned to lead the covenant people.

4:22–23

"Israel is my firstborn son. Let my son go" — Alter notes this is the first use of "son" language for Israel in the Exodus narrative, and it transforms the entire plague sequence into a contest between two firstborns. Pharaoh wants to kill Israel's sons (Exod 1:16, 1:22); God will kill Egypt's firstborn (Exod 12:29). The language of divine paternity ("my son") sets up the final plague as an act of proportionate judgment: you killed my son; I will take your son. The logic is brutal but coherent within the narrative's own moral framework.

4:24–26

Alter on the circumcision episode: "This is one of the most cryptic passages in all of the Bible." He notes the pronoun ambiguity — "him" could be Moses, Moses' son, or even the LORD — and the obscurity of "bridegroom of blood." Alter reads it as a story that "has lost much of its original context in transmission," possibly a very ancient tale about the apotropaic function of circumcision (warding off divine attack), here secondarily attached to Moses. The narrative's sheer difficulty argues for its antiquity.

4:21

Calvin's treatment of Pharaoh's hardening is the longest sustained discussion of divine sovereignty and human responsibility in his Exodus commentary. He is unambiguous: "Both things are true — that Pharaoh hardens his own heart, and that God hardens it." Calvin's resolution: God is the ultimate cause of all things, including Pharaoh's hardening, but Pharaoh's own will is the proximate cause. God does not compel Pharaoh against his nature — he withholds the grace that would soften him, and Pharaoh's own corruption does the rest. This is Calvin's doctrine of reprobation applied to a specific historical case.

4:31

"They believed... they bowed their heads and worshipped" — Calvin notes this as the ideal response to the divine word: first belief (intellectual assent and trust), then worship (the affective and practical response). The sequence is not arbitrary: worship without belief is empty form; belief without worship is incomplete. The elders of Israel model what Moses himself must learn — that the right response to the promise of God is prostrate trust. Calvin sees this as a touchstone for corporate worship: it begins in the word, ends in adoration.

4:24–26

The NET note on "the LORD met him and sought to put him to death" identifies the "him" as Moses (not the son), though this requires Moses to be spared by the son's circumcision. Alternatively, if "him" is the son, Zipporah circumcises the son to heal the attack on the son. The NET translators acknowledge the ambiguity is "irresolvable from the text alone" and represents "one of the most difficult passages in the OT." The phrase "bridegroom of blood" (חֲתַן דָּמִים, ḥătan dāmîm) appears to be a formulaic expression, possibly from a rite in which circumcision blood established covenant kinship — making Moses "kin" to Zipporah's family through blood.

4:22

The NET note on "Israel is my firstborn son" observes this is covenantal adoption language. The title "firstborn" (בְּכֹרִי, bĕkōrî) confers honour, priority, and inheritance rights — not merely birth order. As God's firstborn, Israel has the legal status of the principal heir. Pharaoh's refusal to release God's heir is therefore an act of kidnapping/enslaving the divine family's primary inheritor, which sets up the final plague as the proportionate response within ancient Near Eastern legal categories of family honour and protection.

4:21

God's statement — 'I will harden Pharaoh's heart' — appears here for the first time before Moses returns to Egypt. The hardening is part of the plan, not a reaction to Pharaoh's resistance. It will ensure the full display of divine power through the plague sequence. Pharaoh's hardness serves the revelation of God's glory to Egypt and to Israel.

4:22

'Israel is my firstborn son' — the covenant language of sonship applied to the nation. This is the theological ground of Moses' demand: you shall let my son go. The contrast with Pharaoh's firstborn (11:5) is already being set up. Pharaoh holds Israel enslaved; God will take Pharaoh's son in return. The logic of equivalence will govern the final plague.

4:27–31

The reunion of Moses and Aaron at the mountain of God, the assembly of the elders, the signs performed, and the people's worship — all of this happens before a single encounter with Pharaoh. Israel believes and worships before the liberation begins. Their faith is the precondition of the journey, not the reward at the end.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:22–23

The language of divine sonship — 'Israel is my firstborn son' — is covenantal and royal language. In the ancient Near East, the king was called the 'son of god.' Sarna notes that here God applies this language to the entire nation, not to a monarch. Israel is corporate royalty, the collective bearer of the divine sonship that pagan kings claimed individually. The demand to release God's son challenges the claim that Pharaoh's son (the heir of divine kingship) takes precedence.

4:31

The people's response — they believed, bowed, and worshipped — is the narrative's first positive note after extended tension. Sarna notes the sequence: hearing (the words), seeing (the signs), believing, then worshipping. The ordered response is the pattern the plagues will eventually produce in Pharaoh — but he will never complete the sequence. Israel responds immediately; Pharaoh will resist through ten plagues.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:18–20

Moses' departure from Midian is narrated with minimal ceremony. He asks Jethro's leave (receiving a gracious 'go in peace'), takes wife and sons, returns. The donkey carrying Moses, wife, sons, and the staff of God in his hand is a quietly powerful image. The staff that will divide the sea is here carried on a family journey. The ordinary and the extraordinary share the same road.

4:24–26

The 'bridegroom of blood' episode is one of the Bible's most deliberately compressed narratives. Alter notes the extreme economy: no explanation, no names (Zipporah acts; 'his son' is unnamed), no resolution statement. The reader must infer everything. The compression signals that the narrator expects the reader to supply the covenant context. Zipporah acts with complete competence, apparently understanding what Moses or the narrator does not explain. She is the real agent of rescue here.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:21

Calvin on the hardening: 'It is asked how God can consistently harden the heart of man, and then punish the hardness which he himself has caused? I reply, that God hardens by deserting and withdrawing his grace.' For Calvin, hardening is not direct divine causation of evil but the withdrawal of restraining grace from a heart already inclined toward sin. Pharaoh's hardness is Pharaoh's own; God's role is to remove the restraint that would have limited its expression.

4:30–31

The elders' belief is Calvin's occasion to note the priority of the word over signs: 'Signs by themselves profit nothing unless the word be added.' The people believe because they hear that God has visited them and seen their affliction — the verbal promise precedes and grounds the visual confirmation. Signs confirm faith that has already been created by the word; they do not create faith independently.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:21

The NET note on 'harden' (chazaq, 'strengthen/make firm') distinguishes it from the other hardening verb (kabed, 'make heavy/dull') used later. Chazaq in the Hiphil means to make resolute, firm, determined. The note discusses the theological problem at length: the hardening passages in chs.4–14 alternate between God hardening Pharaoh's heart (chs.4–7) and Pharaoh hardening his own heart (chs.8–9), suggesting a complex interplay between divine sovereignty and human agency rather than simple divine determinism.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Commissioning Narratives
The five-objection structure in Moses's commissioning is paralleled in Mesopotamian and Egyptian royal-commission narratives — protest and reassurance are standard elements.
The literary form of divine commissioning is recognisable across the ANE while being filled with unique theological content.
Circumcision in Egypt
Egyptian males were commonly circumcised (depicted in tomb reliefs at Saqqara, c.2300 BC). Moses, raised in the palace, may have been circumcised himself — but had not circumcised his son, born in Midian (where circumcision was not practised).
The Zipporah episode reflects the cultural reality of Midianite non-circumcision.

Reception History

Luther, Lectures on Exodus
Moses's objections are for Luther the model of false humility — the refusal to trust God's promise of sufficiency. God's anger at the fifth objection (v.14) is a warning against making weakness an excuse for disobedience.
Origen, Homilies on Exodus
The staff of Moses that becomes a serpent and is then taken back is a type of the cross: what appears as curse (serpent/death) is transformed by God's power into salvation.

Literary Structure

The Call Narrative Conclusion — 4:1–17Exodus 4 completes the five-objection call narrative. Each objection receives a direct divine answer, building a cumulative case for Moses' qualification — not his own but God's provision of it. The form is repeated in Jer 1 and Ezek 1–3, where the prophet's inadequacy becomes the canvas for God's sufficiency.
The most enigmatic episode in the call sequence. Zipporah's act of circumcision and her declaration 'you are a bridegroom of blood to me' has generated centuries of commentary. Its placement — between commission and commencement — signals that covenant fidelity in the household must precede covenant mediation to the nation.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 4 resolves Moses' objections not by making him adequate but by making God's presence the only adequacy required. The hardening announcement introduces the paradox that will run through the plague narrative: divine sovereignty and human agency operating simultaneously, producing the full revelation of God's power.
