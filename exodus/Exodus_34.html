Exodus 34

The New Tablets; God's Name Proclaimed; Moses' Radiant Face

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline
Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. Moses is not only the principal character of Exodus — he is its author, and the events recorded are largely his own experience.

When written

Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC. The events of Exodus span approximately 80 years (Moses's birth to the Tabernacle's completion), but the book itself was written after the fact — most likely during the years at Sinai and the wilderness wandering that followed. The Tabernacle instructions and legal material (chs.19–40) may have been committed to writing earliest, as they were immediately actionable.

What prompted it

Israel needed a founding narrative — a record of how God delivered them from 430 years of slavery, made them his covenant people at Sinai, and established the Tabernacle as his dwelling among them. Exodus is the story of liberation, but more than that, it is the story of covenant formation and divine habitation. It answers: How did God redeem us? What does he require of us? And how can a holy God dwell with a sinful people?

Verses 1–9 — The New Tablets; God Proclaims His Name
1The Lord said to Moses, "Chisel out two stone tablets like the first ones, and I will write on them the words that were on the first tablets, which you broke.2Be ready in the morning, and then come up on Mount Sinai. Present yourself to me there on top of the mountain.3No one is to come with you or be seen anywhere on the mountain; not even the flocks and herds may graze in front of the mountain."4So Moses chiseled out two stone tablets like the first ones and went up Mount Sinai early in the morning, as the Lord had commanded him; and he carried the two stone tablets in his hands.5Then the Lord came down in the cloud and stood there with him and proclaimed his name, the Lord.6And he passed in front of Moses, proclaiming, "The Lord, the Lord, the compassionate and gracious God, slow to anger, abounding in love and faithfulness,7maintaining love to thousands, and forgiving wickedness, rebellion and sin. Yet he does not leave the guilty unpunished; he punishes the children and their children for the sin of the parents to the third and fourth generation."8Moses bowed to the ground at once and worshiped.9"Lord, if I have found favor in your eyes," he said, "then let the Lord go with us. Although this is a stiff-necked people, forgive our wickedness and our sin, and take us as your inheritance."

Hebrew Word Study

יְהוָה יְהוָה אֵל רַחוּם וְחַנּוּן yhwh yhwh ʾēl raḥûm wĕḥannûn — The Lord, the Lord, the compassionate and gracious God: The double divine name is emphatic — the very being of God proclaimed. The thirteen attributes that follow are the most complete divine self-disclosure in the OT. Every major NT statement about God's character is grounded here.

אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם ʾerek ʾappayim — slow to anger: Literally "long of nose/nostrils" — the Hebrew idiom for patience, derived from the image of nostrils flaring in anger. God's anger is not quick; it takes a long time to be kindled. This is not weakness but the self-control of infinite power channelled through infinite patience.

Context

The thirteen attributes of vv.6–7 are the answer to Moses' request in 33:18 ("show me your glory"). God's glory is not a visual spectacle but a character disclosure. The attributes are cited repeatedly throughout the OT: Num 14:18, Neh 9:17, Ps 86:15, 103:8, 145:8, Joel 2:13, Jon 4:2, Nah 1:3. They became the foundation of Israel's theology and liturgy — the character of God that makes covenant possible despite human faithlessness.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 103:8"The Lord is compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, abounding in love" — a direct quotation of Exod 34:6.
  • John 1:14"Full of grace and truth" — the NT echo of Exod 34:6's "abounding in love (ḥesed) and faithfulness (ʾemet)."
  • Jon 4:2Jonah's complaint about God: "I knew that you are a gracious and compassionate God, slow to anger and abounding in love." He cites Exod 34:6 as the problem — God is too merciful.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
34:6–7

This is the most important speech in the Pentateuch. When God proclaims his own name — his own character — he chooses these thirteen attributes. Not omnipotence. Not omniscience. Not sovereignty. Compassion, grace, patience, love, faithfulness, forgiveness. And then justice: "he does not leave the guilty unpunished." Both poles of the divine character in one proclamation. This is the tension the cross resolves: how can God be both the God who "does not leave the guilty unpunished" and the God who forgives "wickedness, rebellion and sin"? Only through the substitution of the innocent for the guilty.

34:8–9

Moses' response to the divine character proclamation is immediate worship — then immediate intercession. He does not rest in the comfort of God's goodness; he applies it: "forgive our wickedness... take us as your inheritance." Knowledge of God's character is not an end in itself; it is the ground for prayer. The thirteen attributes become the basis for Moses' plea.

Verses 10–28 — The Covenant Renewed; the Forty Days
10Then the Lord said: "I am making a covenant with you. Before all your people I will do wonders never before done in any nation in all the world. The people you live among will see how awesome is the work that I, the Lord, will do for you.11Obey what I command you today. I will drive out before you the Amorites, Canaanites, Hittites, Perizzites, Hivites and Jebusites.12Be careful not to make a treaty with those who live in the land where you are going, or they will be a snare among you.13Break down their altars, smash their sacred stones and cut down their Asherah poles.14Do not worship any other god, for the Lord, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God.15"Be careful not to make a treaty with those who live in the land; for when they prostitute themselves to their gods and sacrifice to them, they will invite you and you will eat their sacrifices.16And when you choose some of their daughters as wives for your sons and those daughters prostitute themselves to their gods, they will lead your sons to do the same.17"Do not make any idols.18"Celebrate the Festival of Unleavened Bread. For seven days eat bread made without yeast, as I commanded you. Do this at the appointed time in the month of Aviv, for in that month you came out of Egypt.19"The first offspring of every womb belongs to me, including all the firstborn males of your livestock, whether from herd or flock.20Redeem the firstborn donkey with a lamb, but if you do not redeem it, break its neck. Redeem all your firstborn sons. No one is to appear before me empty-handed.21"Six days you shall labor, but on the seventh day you shall rest; even during the plowing season and harvest you must rest.22"Celebrate the Festival of Weeks with the firstfruits of the wheat harvest, and the Festival of Ingathering at the turn of the year.23Three times a year all your men are to appear before the Sovereign Lord, the God of Israel.24I will drive out nations before you and enlarge your territory, and no one will covet your land when you go up three times each year to appear before the Lord your God.25"Do not offer the blood of a sacrifice to me along with anything containing yeast, and do not let any of the sacrifice from the Passover Festival remain until morning.26"Bring the best of the firstfruits of your soil to the house of the Lord your God. Do not cook a young goat in its mother's milk."27Then the Lord said to Moses, "Write down these words, for in accordance with these words I have made a covenant with you and with Israel."28Moses was there with the Lord forty days and forty nights without eating bread or drinking water. And he wrote on the tablets the words of the covenant—the Ten Commandments.

Context

The renewed covenant (vv.10–28) focuses primarily on the dangers of Canaanite religious influence — the very threat that produced the golden calf. The lessons of ch.32 are immediately applied: no treaties, no intermarriage, no Asherah poles, no other gods. God's jealousy (v.14: "the Lord, whose name is Jealous") is the appropriate response to covenant love that cannot be shared. The three annual festivals (Unleavened Bread, Weeks, Ingathering) frame Israel's calendar with covenant remembrance.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 4:2Jesus fasts forty days and forty nights — echoing Moses' forty days in Exod 34:28.
  • Deut 4:23–24"The Lord your God is a consuming fire, a jealous God." Moses later cites this chapter as the ground for the warning against Canaanite religion.
  • 2 Cor 6:14"Do not be unequally yoked with unbelievers" — the NT application of the treaty prohibition of Exod 34:12.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
34:14

"The Lord, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God." Jealousy in God is not a vice; it is the necessary correlate of covenant love. God is jealous because he has given himself to Israel in covenant, and covenant love by nature cannot be shared with rivals. The parent who is jealous for their child's exclusive loyalty; the spouse who is jealous of infidelity — these are appropriate jealousies. God's jealousy is the jealousy of the one who has loved completely and will not accept rivals for that love.

34:28

Moses was with God forty days and forty nights without eating or drinking. The physical sustenance was replaced by the divine presence. This is not mystical poetry — it is the narrative testimony that proximity to the living God satisfies in ways that bread cannot. Jesus in the wilderness (Matt 4:4): "Man does not live on bread alone, but on every word that comes from the mouth of God." Moses lived the prototype.

Verses 29–35 — Moses' Radiant Face; the Veil
29When Moses came down from Mount Sinai with the two tablets of the covenant law in his hands, he was not aware that his face was radiant because he had spoken with the Lord.30When Aaron and all the Israelites saw Moses, his face was radiant, and they were afraid to come near him.31But Moses called to them; so Aaron and all the leaders of the community came back to him, and he spoke to them.32Afterward all the Israelites came near him, and he gave them all the commands the Lord had given him on Mount Sinai.33When Moses finished speaking to them, he put a veil over his face.34But whenever he entered the Lord's presence to speak with him, he removed the veil until he came out. And when he came out and told the Israelites what he had been commanded,35they saw that his face was radiant. Then Moses would put the veil back over his face until he went in to speak with the Lord.

Hebrew Word Study

קָרַן עוֹר פָּנָיו qāran ʿôr pānāyw — the skin of his face shone / was radiant: The verb qāran (to emit rays) is related to the noun qeren (horn) — hence the Vulgate's mistranslation that gave Michelangelo's horned Moses. The correct meaning is "to radiate rays of light" — the skin of Moses' face literally emitted light after speaking with God.

מַסְוֶה masweh — veil: The veil Moses placed over his face between encounters with God. Paul's interpretation in 2 Cor 3:13–16 is that Moses veiled his face to prevent Israel seeing the glory fade — a type of the veil over Israel's hearts removed only in Christ.

Context

The shining face is the physical consequence of sustained encounter with God's glory. Moses did not know his face was radiant (v.29) — the transformation was not self-generated but received. This is the pattern of genuine holiness: it is not performed but reflected. The people who encounter the glory of God are changed by it without knowing it. 2 Cor 3:18 promises all believers this transformation: "we all, who with unveiled faces contemplate the Lord's glory, are being transformed into his image with ever-increasing glory."

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Cor 3:7–18Paul's extended meditation: the fading glory of Moses' face → the unfading glory of the new covenant → "we all... are being transformed into his image with ever-increasing glory."
  • Matt 17:2The Transfiguration: "His face shone like the sun." Jesus on the mountain of transfiguration fulfils what Moses on Sinai foreshadowed.
  • Rev 1:16The glorified Christ: "His face was like the sun shining in all its brilliance." The ultimate radiance beyond Moses' reflected glory.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
34:29

Moses did not know his face was radiant. This is the mark of genuine encounter with God: you don't know the transformation has happened. The person who says "I am very spiritual and close to God" may be performing religiosity. The person who has actually been with God is often unaware of the change. The disciples said of Peter and John: "they had been with Jesus" (Acts 4:13) — they saw the transformation that Peter and John didn't announce. Holiness shows; it doesn't advertise.

34:33–35

The veil goes on when Moses speaks to the people; it comes off when he speaks with God. In God's presence: unveiled. In public: veiled. Paul reverses this pattern for the new covenant believer: with unveiled face, always contemplating the Lord's glory (2 Cor 3:18). The veil has been removed in Christ. We no longer alternate between veiled and unveiled — in Christ we are always unveiled before God.

Ancient Sources

Thirteen Attributes in Judaism
The thirteen divine attributes (raḥûm, ḥannûn, etc.) of Exod 34:6–7 became the most quoted passage in Jewish liturgy. They are recited on Yom Kippur and on every fast day. Every petition for forgiveness in Jewish prayer returns to this passage as its foundation.
The theological centrality of these attributes in Judaism demonstrates their formative power in OT theology.
Paul's Midrash on the Veil
2 Corinthians 3:7–18 is Paul's sustained midrashic interpretation of Exod 34:29–35. His reading is not arbitrary; it follows rabbinic interpretive patterns while applying them to Christ.
2 Corinthians 3 is the most sustained NT engagement with any single Exodus passage.

Reception History

2 Cor 3:7–18
Paul's most extended engagement with a single Exodus passage — the veil of Moses as the type of the veil over Israel's hearts, removed in Christ; the transformation of believers with unveiled faces.
Transfiguration (Matt 17, Mk 9, Lk 9)
Moses appears at the Transfiguration alongside Elijah — the two great Sinai figures. Jesus' face shines like the sun, surpassing the reflected glory of Moses' face. The Transfiguration is the Sinai theophany fulfilled and exceeded.

The chapter moves from the broken tablets (32:19) to new tablets (34:1,28) — the covenant broken and restored. The thirteen attributes are the theological ground of the restoration: God can renew the covenant because his character is compassion, grace, patience, love, faithfulness, and forgiveness.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 34 is the Pentateuch's theological summit. When God is asked to show his glory, he responds with character: compassion, grace, patience, love, faithfulness, forgiveness. The glory of God is his goodness. The shining face of Moses is the physical afterglow of the encounter with that goodness — a foretaste of the transformation promised to all who behold the glory of Christ with unveiled faces.
