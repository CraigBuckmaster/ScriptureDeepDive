Exodus 40

The Tabernacle Erected; the Glory of the Lord Fills It

Hebrew

Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. Moses is not only the principal character of Exodus — he is its author, and the events recorded are largely his own experience. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC. The events of Exodus span approximately 80 years (Moses's birth to the Tabernacle's completion), but the book itself was written after the fact — most likely during the years at Sinai and the wilderness wandering that followed. The Tabernacle instructions and legal material (chs.19–40) may have been committed to writing earliest, as they were immediately actionable. What prompted it: Israel needed a founding narrative — a record of how God delivered them from 430 years of slavery, made them his covenant people at Sinai, and established the Tabernacle as his dwelling among them. Exodus is the story of liberation, but more than that, it is the story of covenant formation and divine habitation. It answers: How did God redeem us? What does he require of us? And how can a holy God dwell with a sinful people?

Verses 1–16 — God Commands the Erection; Moses Obeys
1Then the Lord said to Moses:2"Set up the tabernacle, the tent of meeting, on the first day of the first month.3Place the ark of the covenant law in it and shield the ark with the curtain.4Bring in the table and set out what belongs on it. Then bring in the lampstand and set up its lamps.5Place the gold altar of incense in front of the ark of the covenant law and put the curtain at the entrance to the tabernacle.6"Place the altar of burnt offering in front of the entrance to the tabernacle, the tent of meeting;7place the basin between the tent of meeting and the altar and put water in it.8Set up the courtyard around it and put the curtain at the entrance to the courtyard.9"Take the anointing oil and anoint the tabernacle and everything in it; consecrate it and all its furnishings, and it will be holy.10Then anoint the altar of burnt offering and all its utensils; consecrate the altar, and it will be most holy.11Anoint the basin and its stand and consecrate them.12"Bring Aaron and his sons to the entrance to the tent of meeting and wash them with water.13Then dress Aaron in the sacred garments, anoint him and consecrate him so he may serve me as priest.14Bring his sons and dress them in tunics.15Anoint them just as you anointed their father, so they may serve me as priests. Their anointing will be to a priesthood that will continue throughout their generations."16Moses did everything just as the Lord commanded him.

Hebrew Word Study

בְּאֶחָד לַחֹדֶשׁ הָרִאשׁוֹן bĕʾeḥād laḥōdeš hārîʾšôn — on the first day of the first month: The Tabernacle is erected on the first of Nisan — the month of the Passover, the month of the Exodus. Exactly one year after the departure from Egypt, God's dwelling stands in Israel's midst. The covenant fulfilled in twelve months.

מָשַׁח māšaḥ — anoint: The Tabernacle and all its furnishings are anointed with the sacred oil — consecrated by anointing, set apart from common use. The same verb that consecrates the high priest now consecrates the building and everything in it. Everything in the Tabernacle is "Messiah" — anointed.

Context

The command to erect the Tabernacle on the first of the first month creates a precise one-year symmetry: Israel left Egypt on the fifteenth of Nisan; the Tabernacle stands on the first of Nisan, twelve months later. The year of wilderness formation — plagues, Passover, Red Sea, Sinai, the calf, the covenant renewal — culminates in God moving in. The entire year's journey was preparation for this moment.

Cross-Reference

  • John 1:14"The Word became flesh and dwelt (tabernacled) among us, and we have seen his glory." The Tabernacle's completion is the OT foreshadowing of the Incarnation.
  • Rev 21:3"God's dwelling place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them." The ultimate fulfilment of Exod 40.
  • Heb 9:11Christ entered the greater and more perfect tabernacle — the earthly is surpassed by the heavenly.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
40:2

The first day of the first month. One year after leaving Egypt, God's dwelling stands in Israel's midst. The Exodus was not complete until God moved in. Liberation was the beginning; habitation was the goal. This is the shape of salvation: God frees his people not so they can be free but so they can be with him. Freedom in the wilderness without God's presence would have been another form of slavery. Freedom with God's presence is the Exodus's purpose.

40:16

Moses did everything just as the Lord commanded him. The last occurrence of the book's governing phrase — and the most sweeping: not just the Tabernacle construction but the entire erection and consecration. Moses has been the faithful steward throughout. His final act is complete obedience.

Verses 17–33 — The Tabernacle Erected; Moses Finishes the Work
17So the tabernacle was set up on the first day of the first month in the second year.18When Moses set up the tabernacle, he put the bases in place, erected the frames, inserted the crossbars and set up the posts.19Then he spread the tent over the tabernacle and put the covering over the tent, as the Lord commanded him.20He took the tablets of the covenant law and placed them in the ark, attached the poles to the ark and put the atonement cover over it.21Then he brought the ark into the tabernacle and hung the shielding curtain and shielded the ark of the covenant law, as the Lord commanded him.22Moses placed the table in the tent of meeting on the north side of the tabernacle outside the curtain23and set out the bread on it before the Lord, as the Lord commanded him.24He placed the lampstand in the tent of meeting opposite the table on the south side of the tabernacle25and set up the lamps before the Lord, as the Lord commanded him.26Moses placed the gold altar in the tent of meeting in front of the curtain27and burned fragrant incense on it, as the Lord commanded him.28Then he put up the curtain at the entrance to the tabernacle.29He set the altar of burnt offering near the entrance to the tabernacle, the tent of meeting, and offered on it burnt offerings and grain offerings, as the Lord commanded him.30He placed the basin between the tent of meeting and the altar and put water in it for washing,31and Moses and Aaron and his sons used it to wash their hands and feet.32They washed whenever they entered the tent of meeting or approached the altar, as the Lord commanded Moses.33Then Moses set up the courtyard around the tabernacle and altar and put up the curtain at the entrance to the courtyard. And so Moses finished the work.

Context

The erection narrative (vv.17–33) repeats "as the Lord commanded him" with each element — six more times in addition to v.16. The total across Exod 39–40 reaches the theological number. Moses is meticulous: every furnishing placed, every lamp lit, every piece of bread set out, every offering made. The book's final verse of human action is "so Moses finished the work" — the same phrase used of God at creation (Gen 2:2).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
40:33

Moses finished the work. Genesis 2:2: "By the seventh day God had finished the work he had been doing." The deliberate echo is the text's theological signature: the Tabernacle's completion is a new creation. God finished the heavens and earth; Moses finished the Tabernacle. The new creation is a sanctuary — a place where God and humanity share the same space. This is the goal of all creation: God with his people.

Verses 34–38 — The Glory of the Lord Fills the Tabernacle
34Then the cloud covered the tent of meeting, and the glory of the Lord filled the tabernacle.35Moses could not enter the tent of meeting because the cloud had settled on it, and the glory of the Lord filled the tabernacle.36In all the travels of the Israelites, whenever the cloud lifted from above the tabernacle, they would set out;37but if the cloud did not lift, they did not set out—until the day it lifted.38So the cloud of the Lord was over the tabernacle by day, and fire was in the cloud by night, in the sight of all the Israelites during all their travels.

Hebrew Word Study

וַיְכַס הֶעָנָן waykas heʿānān — the cloud covered: The cloud (ʿānān) that led Israel from Egypt (13:21) now covers the completed Tabernacle. The same presence that guided the Exodus now inhabits the dwelling place. The journey and the destination share the same cloud.

כְּבוֹד יְהוָה מָלֵא kĕbôd yhwh māleʾ — the glory of the Lord filled: Māleʾ — filled, full, to the brim. The same word as the "filled" craftsmen (31:3: filled with the Spirit). The Tabernacle is filled with divine glory as the craftsmen were filled with divine skill. Both fillings are given; neither is manufactured.

Context

The glory filling the Tabernacle (vv.34–35) is the climax of the entire book of Exodus — indeed the climax of the entire Pentateuch's narrative of covenant relationship. God descends into the dwelling his people built for him. The cloud that led them out of Egypt now leads from within. The fire that burned at Sinai now burns at the centre of the camp. The covenant promise — "I will dwell among them" (29:45) — is fulfilled.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 8:10–11Solomon's Temple: when it is completed, the same cloud fills the temple and the priests cannot stand to minister. The same glory that filled the Tabernacle fills the Temple.
  • John 1:14"The Word became flesh and tabernacled among us, and we have seen his glory." The Incarnation is the personal fulfilment of Exod 40:34.
  • Rev 21:3,22The New Jerusalem has no temple "because the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are its temple." The Tabernacle's glory is finally universalised — God's presence fills everything, not just one tent.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
40:34–35

The cloud covered the tent and the glory filled the Tabernacle — and Moses could not enter. Even the greatest human being in the OT, the man who spoke with God face to face, could not enter the space where God's fullness dwelt. The glory was too much. This is not failure; it is revelation: the Holy God's presence is overwhelming even to the most intimate human. The problem will not be resolved until "a new and living way" is opened through Christ's flesh (Heb 10:20).

40:38

The cloud by day and fire by night — in the sight of all the Israelites, during all their travels. The book ends where it began: a people on a journey, under God's care, led by his presence. Exodus begins with a people enslaved under Pharaoh's cloud of oppression; it ends with a people free under God's pillar of cloud and fire. The whole book is the story of whose cloud you travel under.

Ancient Sources

Temple Dedication Parallels
Ancient Near Eastern temple dedication texts describe the deity's arrival into the completed temple — the god "entering" the new house. The Tabernacle's glory-filling fits this pattern but with a radical difference: the God of Israel arrives uninvited, spontaneously, in overwhelming glory that even Moses cannot withstand.
God's arrival in the Tabernacle is sovereign and unmanageable — not the controlled "entrance" of a deity into a managed ritual.
Cloud and Fire
The pillar of cloud and fire appeared at the Exodus (13:21) and now closes the book at the Tabernacle (40:38). The literary inclusio frames the entire Exodus narrative: cloud and fire from departure to dwelling.
The cloud and fire form a literary bracket around the entire book.

Reception History

1 Kgs 8:10–11
Solomon's Temple dedication: the same cloud fills the temple; the priests cannot minister. The Tabernacle's glory-filling is the template for all subsequent divine indwelling in the OT.
John 1:14
The supreme NT interpretation: "The Word became flesh and tabernacled among us, and we have seen his glory." Jesus is the ultimate Tabernacle — God dwelling with humanity in a way that exceeds even Moses's face-to-face relationship. In Christ, the glory can be seen without consuming.

The book's ending is not a command or a law but a presence: the glory of the Lord filling the tent. Exodus began with a people enslaved; it ends with God dwelling among them. The liberation was always for this — not freedom from but freedom with. The cloud covers; the glory fills; the journey continues under God's presence.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 40 is the Pentateuch's first climax: the glory of the Lord fills the Tabernacle. The book that began with a people crying out under Pharaoh's oppression ends with God dwelling in their midst, his glory so present that even Moses cannot enter. Liberation was not the goal — habitation was. "I will dwell among them" is now "the glory of the Lord filled the Tabernacle." The promise is kept.
