Exodus 16

Exodus 16

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–21 — Manna and Quail; the Daily Provision
1The whole Israelite community set out from Elim and came to the Desert of Sin, which is between Elim and Sinai, on the fifteenth day of the second month after they had come out of Egypt.2In the desert the whole community grumbled against Moses and Aaron.3The Israelites said to them, "If only we had died by the Lord's hand in Egypt! There we sat around pots of meat and ate all the food we wanted, but you have brought us out into this desert to starve this entire assembly to death."4Then the Lord said to Moses, "I will rain down bread from heaven for you. The people are to go out each day and gather enough for that day. In this way I will test them and see whether they will follow my instructions.5On the sixth day they are to prepare what they bring in, and that is to be twice as much as they gather on the other days."6So Moses and Aaron said to all the Israelites, "In the evening you will know that it was the Lord who brought you out of Egypt,7and in the morning you will see the glory of the Lord, because he has heard your grumbling against him. Who are we, that you should grumble against us?"8Moses also said, "You will know that it was the Lord when he gives you meat to eat in the evening and all the bread you want in the morning, because he has heard your grumbling against him. Who are we? You are not grumbling against us, but against the Lord."9Then Moses told Aaron, "Say to the entire Israelite community, 'Come before the Lord, for he has heard your grumbling.'"10While Aaron was speaking to the whole Israelite community, they looked toward the desert, and there was the glory of the Lord appearing in the cloud.11The Lord said to Moses,12"I have heard the grumbling of the Israelites. Tell them, 'At twilight you will eat meat, and in the morning you will be filled with bread. Then you will know that I am the Lord your God.'"13That evening quail came and covered the camp, and in the morning there was a layer of dew around the camp.14When the dew was gone, thin flakes like frost on the ground appeared on the desert floor.15When the Israelites saw it, they said to each other, "What is it?" For they did not know what it was. Moses said to them, "It is the bread the Lord has given you to eat.16This is what the Lord has commanded: 'Everyone is to gather as much as they need. Take an omer for each person you have in your tent.'"17The Israelites did as they were told; some gathered much, some little.18And when they measured it by the omer, the one who gathered much did not have too much, and the one who gathered little did not have too little. Everyone had gathered just as much as they needed.19Then Moses said to them, "No one is to keep any of it until morning."20However, some of them paid no attention to Moses; they kept part of it until morning, but it was full of maggots and began to smell. So Moses was angry with them.21Each morning everyone gathered as much as they needed, and when the sun grew hot, it melted away.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The test structure (v.4: "that I may test them, whether they will walk in my law") makes the manna not just a provision but a discipline. Gathering exactly enough for each day, not storing overnight, observing double on the sixth and resting on the seventh — each rule tests whether Israel trusts God for tomorrow. The failure to trust (some leaving manna overnight, some going out on the seventh day) is the first record of sabbath violation and the proto-pattern of every covenant failure.

Cross-Reference

  • John 6:31–35The crowd quotes "He gave them bread from heaven to eat" (citing Ps 78:24/Exod 16). Jesus responds: "Moses did not give you the bread from heaven, but my Father gives you the true bread from heaven... I am the bread of life."
  • Deut 8:3"He humbled you and let you hunger and fed you with manna... that he might make you know that man does not live by bread alone, but by every word that comes from the mouth of God." Jesus quotes this against Satan (Matt 4:4).
  • Rev 2:17The hidden manna promised to the overcomers — the eschatological fulfilment of the manna provision.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:4

The daily omer (the measure of manna) is precisely calibrated to daily need — not more, not less. Sarna notes the Deuteronomic reflection on this: 'He humbled you and let you hunger and fed you with manna... that he might make you know that man does not live by bread alone' (Deut 8:3). The design of the provision — daily, just enough — is a theological curriculum in human dependence on divine sustenance rather than stored resources.

16:22–26

The double portion on the sixth day and the Sabbath rest is the first presentation of the Sabbath as a structured weekly practice in the Exodus narrative. Sarna notes the Sabbath was not invented at Sinai (it appears in creation, Gen 2:2–3); it is here applied to the wilderness provision cycle. The manna that does not appear on the seventh day makes the Sabbath tangible — the day off is written into the food supply itself.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:3

The idealisation of Egypt is the wilderness narrative's most consistent irony. Alter notes that Israel's memory of Egypt is systematically distorted in moments of hardship: 'where we sat by the meat pots and ate bread to the full.' The slavery is erased; only the food remains. This is the psychology of hardship — it distorts memory to make the known misery look better than the unknown freedom. The wilderness narrative treats this pattern with realism rather than condemnation.

16:14–15

The manna's arrival is narrated with deliberate mystery: 'fine, flake-like thing, fine as frost.' When Israel sees it they ask 'What is it?' (man hu?) — from which the word manna is derived. Alter notes the naming preserves the mystery: Israel eats what they do not understand, provided by the One they are learning to trust. The unknown substance from heaven is the perfect symbol of faith that receives without comprehending.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:4

Calvin on the daily provision: 'God, in his great liberality, wished to exercise his people in the practice of frugality, and in contentment with a little.' The measure is sufficient; excess is withheld. Calvin reads the manna as divine pedagogy in the theological virtue of contentment — learning to receive daily what God provides without hoarding what he has not given. The NT application is the Lord's Prayer: 'give us this day our daily bread.'

16:28

On the Sabbath-breakers: 'God justly reproves those who by their disobedience show that they despise his commands.' The Sabbath test is not arbitrary; it reveals whether Israel trusts the God who promises provision on day seven without requiring their labour. Those who go out on the seventh day looking for manna demonstrate that their trust extends only to what their own effort can secure. The test measures the depth of trust, not merely the keeping of a rule.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:31

The NET note on manna's description ('like white coriander seed, tasting like wafers made with honey') discusses the proposed natural identifications. Several scholars identify it with the secretion of the tamarisk scale insect (Trabutina mannipara), which produces a white, sweet substance in the Sinai Peninsula. The note observes that natural identification does not exclude the miraculous: the scale, timing, quantity, and Sabbath-specific absence all require divine direction.

16:36

The NET note on the omer (1/10 of an ephah) discusses ancient measurements: an omer was approximately 2 litres or 2 quarts. The daily provision per person was thus roughly what a modern person would consume in a day of simple carbohydrates. The precision of the measurement — and the miraculous calibration to each person's exact need regardless of how much or little they collected (vv.17–18) — is one of the chapter's strongest supernatural markers.

Verses 22–36 — The Sabbath; Double Portion; Forty Years of Manna
22On the sixth day, they gathered twice as much—two omers for each person—and the leaders of the community came and reported this to Moses.23He said to them, "This is what the Lord commanded: 'Tomorrow is to be a day of sabbath rest, a holy sabbath to the Lord. So bake what you want to bake and boil what you want to boil. Save whatever is left and keep it until morning.'"24So they saved it until morning, as Moses commanded, and it did not stink or get maggots in it.25"Eat it today," Moses said, "because today is a sabbath to the Lord. You will not find any of it on the ground today.26Six days you are to gather it, but on the seventh day, the Sabbath, there will not be any."27Nevertheless, some of the people went out on the seventh day to gather it, but they found none.28Then the Lord said to Moses, "How long will you refuse to keep my commands and my instructions?29Bear in mind that the Lord has given you the Sabbath; that is why on the sixth day he gives you bread for two days. Everyone is to stay where they are on the seventh day; no one is to go out."30So the people rested on the seventh day.31The people of Israel called the bread manna. It was white like coriander seed and tasted like wafers made with honey.32Moses said, "This is what the Lord has commanded: 'Take an omer of manna and keep it for the generations to come, so they can see the bread I gave you to eat in the wilderness when I brought you out of Egypt.'"33So Moses said to Aaron, "Take a jar and put an omer of manna in it. Then place it before the Lord to be kept for the generations to come."34As the Lord commanded Moses, Aaron put the manna with the tablets of the covenant law, so that it could be kept.35The Israelites ate manna forty years, until they came to a land that was settled; they ate manna until they reached the border of Canaan.36(An omer is one-tenth of an ephah.)

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The forty-year manna provision (v.35) is the longest sustained divine provision narrative in Scripture. Every morning for forty years, Israel woke to bread on the ground. Every Sabbath, they rested because the bread had been pre-provided. The provision ends the moment they enter Canaan (Josh 5:12) — when they eat the fruit of the land, the manna ceases. God provides exactly what is needed for exactly as long as it is needed.

Cross-Reference

  • Josh 5:12The manna ceases the day after Israel eats the produce of Canaan — provision calibrated precisely to the period of need.
  • Heb 9:4The golden jar of manna in the ark of the covenant — a permanent memorial of the wilderness provision, kept "before the LORD."
  • Matt 4:4Jesus quotes Deut 8:3 (reflecting on the manna) against Satan: "Man does not live by bread alone."

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:32–33

The command to preserve an omer of manna 'before the LORD' connects the wilderness provision to the sanctuary. Sarna notes that the jar of manna eventually placed in the Ark of the Covenant (cf. Heb 9:4) makes the tabernacle a repository of covenant memory — the golden jar alongside the tablets and Aaron's rod. The three Ark items summarise three aspects of wilderness faithfulness: the word given, the priesthood established, and the bread provided.

16:35-36

The manna ceased on the day the Israelites entered Canaan (Josh 5:12). Sarna notes the forty-year provision is precisely calibrated: manna was given for exactly the duration of the wilderness period and ceased the moment it was no longer needed. The provision's exact termination is itself a covenant sign — God's care is perfectly fitted to the need.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:22–26

The Sabbath emerges from within the manna provision rather than being legislated from above. Alter notes this narrative strategy — the Sabbath is discovered rather than commanded — makes it feel like a natural rhythmic necessity built into the creation order. Before Sinai codifies it, the wilderness enacts it. The double portion on day six is creation's pattern reasserting itself in the desert.

16:27-30

The Sabbath violation of some Israelites — going out to gather manna on the seventh day and finding none — is the first instance of Sabbath testing in the wilderness. Alter notes the two-fold design: God tests the people to see whether they will follow his instruction, and the empty field on the seventh day confirms the instruction.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:33

Calvin on the jar of manna: 'God desired to have a memorial of this miracle perpetually preserved, that in future ages the recollection of it might be handed down to posterity.' The material object supplements the verbal tradition. Future generations who have never tasted manna can see what God provided. The physical memorial serves the same function as the commemorative stones in Joshua and the annual festivals — embodied memory of divine faithfulness.

16:34-36

Calvin on the preserved manna: the jar of manna placed before the Testimony was both historical reminder and ongoing sign. It declared to every subsequent generation that God had miraculously sustained their ancestors in the wilderness. The preservation of the sign is the preservation of the faith it grounds.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:34

The NET note on 'before the Testimony' (lipnei ha'edut) notes a chronological problem: the Ark is not built until ch.37 (after the Sinai theophany). The note explains this as a proleptic editorial note — the command was given prospectively, and the jar was placed before the Testimony when the Ark was eventually constructed. Alternatively, some understand 'before the Testimony' to refer to the sacred tent of meeting before the Ark was built.

16:35-36

The NET note on the omer measurement discusses its relationship to the ephah (one omer = one-tenth ephah). The note also traces the narrative function of the measurement notice: it grounds the supernatural provision in a specific, verifiable quantity. The manna was real food in real amounts, not a vague spiritual nourishment.

Ancient Sources

Natural Manna
Several natural substances have been proposed as the manna's basis: the secretion of scale insects on tamarisk trees (Sinai), or the resin of certain desert plants. These provide a possible natural substrate; the miracle is in the timing, the scale, the Sabbath doubling, and the cessation at Canaan.
The theological text does not exclude natural mechanisms; it insists on divine agency and purpose.
Quail Migration
Quail (Coturnix coturnix) migrate across the Sinai Peninsula in spring and autumn, sometimes arriving exhausted and easily caught in large numbers. The quail provision is consistent with known ornithological patterns.
The quail provision uses a natural phenomenon at the precisely needed moment — characteristic of Exodus providence.

Reception History

John 6:31–59
The Bread of Life discourse is the most extended NT engagement with the manna. Jesus identifies himself as "the true bread from heaven" — not bread that perishes but bread that gives eternal life. The Eucharist is the manna's fulfilment.
Augustine
The manna as the type of the Eucharist — both are bread from heaven, both are given daily, both are meant to be received in daily dependence and trust, not hoarded.

Scholarly Debates

What Was Manna? Natural Substance or Pure Miracle?
Pure miracle
Calvin, MacArthur, traditional
The manna was a supernatural creation — daily bread from heaven with properties no natural substance possesses: double portion on day six, none on day seven, rapid decay (except on day six). Ps 78:24–25 calls it 'grain of heaven' and 'food of angels.' John 6:31–35 uses it as the type of Christ as the bread of life. The miraculous character is essential to its theological meaning.
Naturalistically identified substance divinely directed
Many mainstream scholars
The tamarisk honeydew secretion (or similar substance) is identified as the natural base. God works through a real natural phenomenon, directing its scale, timing, and Sabbath absence. This approach respects both the text's natural description and the archaeological reality of the Sinai environment.
The Sabbath-specific absence of manna (no natural substance stops appearing on day seven and resumes on day one) is the strongest argument for a miraculous element that transcends any natural identification. The two positions are not mutually exclusive if the natural substance is what God chose to use while directing its miraculous properties. Jesus' identification as the true manna (John 6) treats the OT manna as genuinely miraculous and typologically significant.
