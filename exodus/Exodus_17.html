Water from the Rock; Battle with Amalek

Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. Moses is not only the principal character of Exodus — he is its author, and the events recorded are largely his own experience. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC. The events of Exodus span approximately 80 years (Moses's birth to the Tabernacle's completion), but the book itself was written after the fact — most likely during the years at Sinai and the wilderness wandering that followed. The Tabernacle instructions and legal material (chs.19–40) may have been committed to writing earliest, as they were immediately actionable. What prompted it: Israel needed a founding narrative — a record of how God delivered them from 430 years of slavery, made them his covenant people at Sinai, and established the Tabernacle as his dwelling among them. Exodus is the story of liberation, but more than that, it is the story of covenant formation and divine habitation. It answers: How did God redeem us? What does he require of us? And how can a holy God dwell with a sinful people?

Verses 1–7 — Water from the Rock at Massah and Meribah
1The whole Israelite community set out from the Desert of Sin, traveling from place to place as the Lord commanded. They camped at Rephidim, but there was no water for the people to drink.2So they quarreled with Moses and said, "Give us water to drink." Moses replied, "Why do you quarrel with me? Why do you put the Lord to the test?"3But the people were thirsty for water there, and they grumbled against Moses. They said, "Why did you bring us up out of Egypt to make us and our children and livestock die of thirst?"4Then Moses cried out to the Lord, "What am I to do with these people? They are almost ready to stone me."5The Lord answered Moses, "Go out in front of the people. Take with you some of the elders of Israel and take in your hand the staff with which you struck the Nile, and go.6I will stand there before you by the rock at Horeb. Strike the rock, and water will come out of it for the people to drink." So Moses did this in the sight of the elders of Israel.7And he called the place Massah and Meribah because the Israelites quarreled and because they tested the Lord saying, "Is the Lord among us or not?"

Hebrew Word Study

מַסָּה וּמְרִיבָה massāh ûmĕrîbāh — Massah and Meribah / Testing and Quarrelling: The two place names become the permanent shorthand for Israel's faithless testing of God. Ps 95:8 ("do not harden your hearts, as at Meribah") and Heb 3:8 cite these names as the warning against unbelief. The rock's water is grace; the question "Is the LORD among us?" is the failure.

הַצּוּר haṣṣûr — the rock: Paul identifies the rock as Christ (1 Cor 10:4: "the Rock was Christ"). The water from the rock is a type — the spiritual sustenance in the wilderness flows from Christ, the true Rock.

Context

The question "Is the LORD among us or not?" (v.7) is the sharpest statement of wilderness doubt in Exodus. After ten plagues, the Red Sea, the Song of the Sea, Marah, and manna — Israel still asks whether God is present. This is not one complaint among many; it is the root complaint: Is God actually here? Moses' anger (v.4: "they are almost ready to stone me") shows the crisis has reached its peak. God's answer is to stand on the rock himself — his presence demonstrated in the water that flows from it.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 95:7–8The Psalm uses Meribah as the warning: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts, as at Meribah." Heb 3–4 quotes this extensively.
  • 1 Cor 10:4Paul: "they drank from the spiritual Rock that followed them, and the Rock was Christ" — the typological identification.
  • Num 20:8–12Moses strikes the rock at Meribah a second time (disobedience) instead of speaking to it — the failure that costs him entrance to Canaan.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
17:7

"Is the LORD among us or not?" is the question that underlies every wilderness complaint. The meat, the water, the manna — these are surface needs. The root question is always presence: is God actually with us? God answers not with rebuke but with water. The rock struck by Moses' staff becomes the sign of presence the people doubted. He was there all along. The water proves it.

17:5–6

God says "I will stand before you there on the rock." The LORD places himself at the site of the complaint, before the rod that strikes. The water that comes out flows from the place where God stands. 1 Corinthians 10:4 sees this as the pre-incarnate Christ providing sustenance — the spiritual rock that followed Israel through the wilderness is Christ himself.

Verses 8–16 — The Battle with Amalek; Moses' Raised Arms
8The Amalekites came and attacked the Israelites at Rephidim.9Moses said to Joshua, "Choose some of our men and go out to fight the Amalekites. Tomorrow I will stand on top of the hill with the staff of God in my hands."10So Joshua fought the Amalekites as Moses had ordered, and Moses, Aaron and Hur went to the top of the hill.11As long as Moses held up his hands, the Israelites were winning, but whenever he lowered his hands, the Amalekites were winning.12When Moses' hands grew tired, they took a stone and put it under him and he sat on it. Aaron and Hur held his hands up—one on one side, one on the other—so that his hands remained steady till sunset.13So Joshua overcame the Amalekite army with the sword.14Then the Lord said to Moses, "Write this on a scroll as something to be remembered and make sure that Joshua hears it, because I will completely blot out the memory of Amalek from under heaven."15Moses built an altar and called it The Lord is my Banner.16He said, "Because hands were lifted up against the throne of the Lord, the Lord will be at war against the Amalekites from generation to generation."

Hebrew Word Study

יְהוֹשֻׁעַ yĕhôšûaʿ — Joshua: First appearance of Joshua — "the LORD saves." His name anticipates his role: he will be the one who leads Israel into Canaan. His debut is military, fitting the book that bears his name.

יָד עַל כֵּס יָהּ yād ʿal kēs yāh — a hand upon the throne of Yah: The most enigmatic verse in the chapter. The "throne" of God is invoked as the ground of permanent war against Amalek. God's throne is the seat of final authority; Amalek's attack on Israel is an attack on the people of the throne — and will be answered from the throne "from generation to generation."

Context

The Amalek battle is the first military engagement of Israel as a nation. Joshua commands in the valley; Moses intercedes on the hill with the staff of God. The correlation between Moses' raised arms and Israel's prevailing is the chapter's theological lesson: the battle is won by intercession, not by military force alone. Aaron and Hur holding up Moses' arms is the first image of corporate intercession — the community sustaining the intercessor so the prayer can continue.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 25:17–18Amalek attacked the stragglers — the weakest — and "did not fear God." This characterisation grounds the permanent enmity.
  • 1 Sam 15Saul's failure to destroy Amalek as commanded — the consequence of the Rephidim decree echoes centuries later.
  • Esth 3:1Haman the Agagite — descendant of Amalek's king Agag — the Amalek conflict persisting to the Persian period.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
17:11–12

Moses' arms grow weary. The intercessor is human; the intercession is not automatic; it costs physical effort and requires support. Aaron and Hur hold up his arms until sunset. This is the community of prayer — the body of God's people sustaining the one who prays so the prayer does not fail. Every church that supports its pastors, every community that holds up the arms of its intercessors, is doing what Aaron and Hur did at Rephidim.

17:16

The decree — "The LORD will have war with Amalek from generation to generation" — is not ethnic hatred; it is the judgment of God on a people who attacked the weak without fear of God. The throne of God is invoked: every assault on God's vulnerable people is an assault on his throne. He will answer, generation by generation.

Ancient Sources

Amalekite Identity
The Amalekites are identified in Gen 36:12 as descendants of Esau — Edomites who became nomadic raiders in the Negev and Sinai. Their attack on Israel at Rephidim establishes the long antagonism documented through Judges, Samuel, and Esther.
The Amalek conflict has deep genealogical and geographic roots in the patriarchal narrative.
Intercession and Battle
Ancient Near Eastern texts frequently describe royal or priestly intercession as determining military outcomes. Moses' raised staff is the Israelite equivalent — divine power mediated through the covenant representative.
The battle-by-intercession pattern is culturally intelligible in its ANE context while being theologically distinctive.

Reception History

Origen, On Prayer
Moses' raised arms as the pre-eminent OT type of prayer — specifically of Christ's arms extended on the cross. "The cross is the eternal intercession."
Chrysostom
Aaron and Hur holding Moses' arms as the model of corporate prayer — the church holds up its intercessors; no one bears the weight of intercession alone.

Literary Structure

Complaint — vv.1–3No water; quarrel with Moses; test of God
Provision — vv.4–7God commands the rock-striking; water flows; Massah and Meribah named
Battle — vv.8–10Amalek attacks; Joshua commands; Moses to the hill
Intercession — vv.11–13Arms raised = victory; arms fallen = defeat; Aaron and Hur hold up Moses
Decree — vv.14–16Record the war; build the altar; war with Amalek forever
The chapter's two halves mirror each other: God provides water from rock (divine provision despite human failure) and wins battle through intercession (divine victory through human prayer). Both establish the principle: the battle belongs to God, mediated through the covenant representative.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 17 establishes two permanent principles: Israel's life depends on the Rock (Christ), and Israel's battles are won by intercession. At Massah-Meribah they asked "Is the LORD among us?" — the water answered yes. At Rephidim they raised arms — and God answered with victory.
