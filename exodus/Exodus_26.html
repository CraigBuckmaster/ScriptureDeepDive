Exodus 26

The Four Coverings; the Frames; the Inner Veil and Outer Curtain

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–14 — The Four Coverings of the Tabernacle
1"Make the tabernacle with ten curtains of finely twisted linen and blue, purple and scarlet yarn, with cherubim woven into them by a skilled worker.2All the curtains are to be the same size—twenty-eight cubits long and four cubits wide.3Join five of the curtains together, and do the same with the other five.4Make loops of blue material along the edge of the end curtain in one set, and do the same with the end curtain in the other set.5Make fifty loops on one curtain and fifty loops on the end curtain of the other set, with the loops opposite each other.6Then make fifty gold clasps and use them to fasten the curtains together so that the tabernacle is a unit.7"Make curtains of goat hair for the tent over the tabernacle—eleven altogether.8All eleven curtains are to be the same size—thirty cubits long and four cubits wide.9Join five of the curtains together into one set and the other six into another set. Fold the sixth curtain double at the front of the tent.10Make fifty loops along the edge of the end curtain in one set and also along the edge of the end curtain in the other set.11Then make fifty bronze clasps and put them in the loops to fasten the tent together as a unit.12As for the additional length of the tent curtains, the half curtain that is left over is to hang down at the rear of the tabernacle.13The tent curtains will be a cubit longer on both sides; what is left will hang over the sides of the tabernacle so as to cover it.14Make for the tent a covering of ram skins dyed red, and over that a covering of the other durable leather.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

Portable tent-shrines are well attested in the ancient Near East. An Egyptian portable canopy shrine from Tutankhamun's tomb (c.1325 BC) features similar construction: wooden frames, fabric coverings, and carrying poles. The Tabernacle's portability was essential for the wilderness period — a divine dwelling that could travel with the people.

Context

The four layers of covering (fine linen with cherubim, goat hair, ram skins dyed red, durable leather) move from most glorious inside to most durable/protective outside. The invisible glory within; the weather-resistant hide without. The architecture images the theology of the hidden God: his glory is real but veiled, accessible only through the ordained approach.

Cross-Reference

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    26:1

    The inner curtain of linen with cherubim is the decorative masterpiece of the tabernacle. Sarna notes that cherubim woven into fabric is attested in both Egyptian and Mesopotamian sacred textiles from the Bronze Age — the tabernacle's artistic vocabulary is drawn from the wider ancient Near Eastern sacred-art tradition while being theologically reoriented toward YHWH. The craftsmen work within a known aesthetic tradition; the theological interpretation is distinctively Israelite.

    26:7

    The goat-hair curtains (eleven, slightly larger than the ten linen curtains) form the tent proper over the linen sanctuary. Sarna notes the practical dimensions: the goat-hair covering was the standard nomadic tent material in the ancient Near East — it provides superior waterproofing and durability for desert conditions. The tabernacle's design uses the most practical available materials at each layer while reserving the finest materials for the most sacred interior.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    26:1–14

    The layered covering descriptions — from the exquisite inner linen to the protective outer hides — produce a literary effect of progressive revelation in reverse: the reader (like the worshipper) begins with the outside and works inward toward the increasingly beautiful interior. Alter notes this inside-out description of sacred space is a literary enactment of the theology: holiness increases as one approaches the divine centre.

    26:7-13

    The second covering — goat hair curtains — is slightly larger than the inner linen covering, deliberately overlapping to protect the inner curtains from weather. Alter notes the layered structure of coverings from the most precious inward to the most practical outward mirrors the tabernacle's theology: holiness intensifies toward the centre.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    26:1

    Calvin on the tabernacle's four coverings: 'The diversity of coverings was not without its use; for, under the veil of these coverings, God set before his people a figure of the inward and spiritual worship which he requires.' The coverings are pedagogical: the worshipper sees only the outer, ordinary material; the priest sees the inner beauty. This graduated access enacts the principle that deeper knowledge of God comes with deeper covenant access.

    26:7

    On the goat-hair covering: 'As the tabernacle outwardly was very mean, so God would have his people to learn to seek him under a humble and contemptible appearance.' The external modesty of the tabernacle is Calvin's anti-grandeur theological statement: God does not require opulence on the outside because his glory is internal and spiritual. The humble exterior teaches worshippers not to confuse material splendour with divine presence.

The note observes that whatever the animal, the outer covering's function was to provide waterproofing for the structure. The outermost layer was functional rather than ornamental.

    Hebrew Word Study

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    The silver bases are one of the Tabernacle's deepest theological details: they were cast from the silver collected in the census ransom (Exod 30:11–16, 38:25–27). Every Israelite's ransom payment literally became part of the Tabernacle's foundation. God's dwelling rested on the redemption of his people. The connection between atonement and the dwelling is structural, not merely symbolic.

    Cross-Reference

      Sarna — JPS Commentary

      Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
      26:19

      Each frame has two silver sockets (adanim). Sarna notes that the 100 silver talents used for the bases (38:27) came from the census tax — one bekah (half-shekel) per person from 603,550 registered men. Every Israelite contributed equally to the silver foundation of God's sanctuary. The equality of the contribution (same amount regardless of wealth) is itself a theological statement: all stand equally before the covenant God.

      26:23-29

      The frame construction uses connecting crossbars (beriyachim) that run the full length of the structure through rings — a structural system that distributes tension across the entire frame rather than concentrating it at individual joints. Sarna notes the engineering is sound: the tabernacle was designed to be repeatedly assembled, transported, and reassembled.

      Alter — Literary Reading

      Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
      26:15–30

      The frame construction is the tabernacle's structural engineering, described with the same precision as the ornamental furnishings. Alter notes that the repetition of dimensions and construction details throughout the tabernacle chapters serves a mnemonic function — these are practical instructions that a craftsman must be able to follow without visual aids. The elaborate verbal description substitutes for architectural drawings that do not exist.

      26:23-29

      The corner frames and crossbars create the tabernacle's structural stability. Alter notes the attention to structural engineering in the midst of theological instruction: the dwelling of the holy God must be built to last and to travel. Covenant durability requires practical construction quality.

Every dimension, every material, every construction detail is divinely specified.       26:33

      MacArthur: The curtain will separate the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place. The curtain identified in Hebrews (10:20) as Christ's body -- torn to open the way into God's presence -- is this very curtain, torn from top to bottom at the crucifixion.

      Context

      The inner veil (v.31–33) and the outer curtain (v.36–37) create two zones of the Tabernacle: the Holy Place (table, lampstand — accessed by priests daily) and the Most Holy Place (ark, mercy seat — accessed by the high priest alone, once a year). The veil is the theological centre of the structure: it marks the point beyond which ordinary human beings cannot go. The torn veil of Matt 27:51 is the Tabernacle's most dramatic moment.

      Sarna — JPS Commentary

      Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
      26:31–33

      The parochet (inner veil) is the most theologically significant architectural feature of the tabernacle. Sarna notes it creates the Holy of Holies as the most restricted sacred space in Israelite religion — accessible only to the high priest, only on Yom Kippur, only with blood. The veil is the boundary of the unapproachable divine presence. Its tearing (Matt 27:51) signals that Christ's sacrifice has permanently removed the barrier — the once-annual access is now perpetual through the great high priest.

      26:33-37

      The veil's placement between the holy place and the most holy creates the graduated-access system that structures all subsequent Israelite worship. Sarna notes the two curtains — inner veil and outer screen — correspond to the two degrees of separation: the priests could enter the Holy Place daily, but only the high priest entered behind the veil once annually.

      Alter — Literary Reading

      Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
      26:31–37

      The chapter closes with the two curtains — the inner veil (parochet) and the outer entrance curtain (masach). Alter notes the chapter's movement from outside (coverings) to structure (frames) to interior divisions (veil, curtain) has produced a complete cross-section of the sanctuary's architecture. The reader now knows what it looks like from every angle — inside and outside, horizontal and vertical.

      26:33-37

      The two curtains — the veil separating the Holy of Holies from the Holy Place, and the screen at the entrance — create the tabernacle's architectural theology of approach. Alter notes the approach is graduated: from outside in, from common to holy, each step requiring greater priestly qualification.

      Ancient Sources

      Egyptian Portable Shrines
      The shrine from Tutankhamun's tomb (c.1325 BC) has striking structural parallels: gilded wooden framework, fabric coverings, carrying poles. The Tabernacle uses the same portable-shrine technology but fills it with radically different theology.
      The Tabernacle is culturally intelligible within its Egyptian context while being theologically unprecedented.
      Temple Veil in Antiquity
      Josephus (Jewish War 5.5.4) describes the Temple veil as embroidered with a panorama of heaven — consistent with the cherubim-woven veil of Exod 26:31. The veil's size in Herod's Temple: 60 x 30 feet.
      The tearing of this massive veil at the crucifixion was a publicly visible, catastrophic event.

      Reception History

      Hebrews 9–10
      The sustained NT typological reading: the veil as Christ's body, torn to open access; the two compartments as the two covenants; the annual high priest as the type of Christ's once-for-all entry.
      Origin of "Holy of Holies"
      The English phrase "Holy of Holies" comes directly from the Hebrew superlative qōdeš haqqŏdāšîm (v.33) — the most common description of the innermost sanctuary in all subsequent Jewish and Christian theology.

      Scholarly Debates

      The Tabernacle and the Jerusalem Temple: Continuity or Displacement?
      Continuity: the temple fulfils the tabernacle
      Traditional, evangelical
      Solomon's temple is a permanent, elaborated version of the portable tabernacle. The same sacred spaces (Holy Place, Holy of Holies), the same furnishings (Ark, lampstand, incense altar, table), and the same glory-cloud (1 Kgs 8:10–11) connect the two structures. The tabernacle is not replaced but fulfilled: the mobile presence becomes the settled presence.
      Displacement: the temple represents a theological compromise
      Some scholars, particularly in the Deuteronomic tradition
      The tabernacle was the legitimate worship space; the temple represents the accommodation of Israelite religion to Canaanite and ancient Near Eastern royal temple traditions. Nathan's oracle (2 Sam 7:5–7) has God questioning whether he ever asked for a house of cedar. The tabernacle's mobility was theologically appropriate to the covenant God who accompanies his people; the temple's permanence introduced a dangerous theology of guaranteed presence.
      The temple's legitimacy is affirmed by the glory-cloud's arrival (1 Kgs 8:10–11) and God's acceptance of Solomon's prayer (1 Kgs 9:3). Nathan's oracle questions the initiative (David's desire, not God's request) rather than the legitimacy. The continuity position is better supported by the text. Jeremiah's temple sermon (Jer 7) and Ezekiel's departure-of-glory vision (Ezek 10) correct the presumption of guaranteed presence — the theology that the temple sometimes generated — without questioning the temple's legitimacy per se.
