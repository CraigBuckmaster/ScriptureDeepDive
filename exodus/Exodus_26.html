Exodus 26

The Tabernacle: Curtains, Frames, and the Veil

Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. Moses is not only the principal character of Exodus — he is its author, and the events recorded are largely his own experience. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC. The events of Exodus span approximately 80 years (Moses's birth to the Tabernacle's completion), but the book itself was written after the fact — most likely during the years at Sinai and the wilderness wandering that followed. The Tabernacle instructions and legal material (chs.19–40) may have been committed to writing earliest, as they were immediately actionable. What prompted it: Israel needed a founding narrative — a record of how God delivered them from 430 years of slavery, made them his covenant people at Sinai, and established the Tabernacle as his dwelling among them. Exodus is the story of liberation, but more than that, it is the story of covenant formation and divine habitation. It answers: How did God redeem us? What does he require of us? And how can a holy God dwell with a sinful people?

Verses 1–14 — The Four Coverings of the Tabernacle
1"Make the tabernacle with ten curtains of finely twisted linen and blue, purple and scarlet yarn, with cherubim woven into them by a skilled worker.2All the curtains are to be the same size—twenty-eight cubits long and four cubits wide.3Join five of the curtains together, and do the same with the other five.4Make loops of blue material along the edge of the end curtain in one set, and do the same with the end curtain in the other set.5Make fifty loops on one curtain and fifty loops on the end curtain of the other set, with the loops opposite each other.6Then make fifty gold clasps and use them to fasten the curtains together so that the tabernacle is a unit.7"Make curtains of goat hair for the tent over the tabernacle—eleven altogether.8All eleven curtains are to be the same size—thirty cubits long and four cubits wide.9Join five of the curtains together into one set and the other six into another set. Fold the sixth curtain double at the front of the tent.10Make fifty loops along the edge of the end curtain in one set and also along the edge of the end curtain in the other set.11Then make fifty bronze clasps and put them in the loops to fasten the tent together as a unit.12As for the additional length of the tent curtains, the half curtain that is left over is to hang down at the rear of the tabernacle.13The tent curtains will be a cubit longer on both sides; what is left will hang over the sides of the tabernacle so as to cover it.14Make for the tent a covering of ram skins dyed red, and over that a covering of the other durable leather.

Hebrew Word Study

כְּרֻבִים kĕrubîm — cherubim: Woven into the innermost curtain — the ceiling of the Most Holy Place is cherubim. The worshipper inside looked up into the faces of the guardian creatures of the divine throne. The Tabernacle interior was heaven made visible.

חֹשֶׁן ḥōšen — (the tent as) breastpiece/enclosure: The Tabernacle's layered coverings create a progressive approach to holiness — each layer more costly than the outer, each closer to the presence. The structure embodies the theological truth that approach to God requires preparation and covering.

Historical Context

Portable tent-shrines are well attested in the ancient Near East. An Egyptian portable canopy shrine from Tutankhamun's tomb (c.1325 BC) features similar construction: wooden frames, fabric coverings, and carrying poles. The Tabernacle's portability was essential for the wilderness period — a divine dwelling that could travel with the people.

Context

The four layers of covering (fine linen with cherubim, goat hair, ram skins dyed red, durable leather) move from most glorious inside to most durable/protective outside. The invisible glory within; the weather-resistant hide without. The architecture images the theology of the hidden God: his glory is real but veiled, accessible only through the ordained approach.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
26:1

The innermost curtain — the ceiling of the Most Holy Place — is woven with cherubim. The worshipper who entered (the high priest on Yom Kippur) was surrounded by heaven: cherubim above, the ark below. He stood in the throne room of God. The Tabernacle was not a building where people came to feel religious; it was the intersection of heaven and earth, where God actually dwelt and actual atonement was made.

26:14

The outermost covering — durable leather — was the most weathered and plain. A desert traveller walking past the Tabernacle camp would have seen nothing glorious: a plain leather tent. All the glory was within. The hidden God, whose glory is veiled from the outside world, revealed only to those who approach through the ordained way. This is the consistent pattern of divine revelation.

Verses 15–30 — The Frames and Silver Bases
15"Make upright frames of acacia wood for the tabernacle.16Each frame is to be ten cubits long and a cubit and a half wide,17with two projections set parallel to each other. Make all the frames of the tabernacle in this way.18Make twenty frames for the south side of the tabernacle19and make forty silver bases to go under them—two bases for each frame, one under each projection.20For the other side, the north side of the tabernacle, make twenty frames21and forty silver bases—two under each frame.22Make six frames for the far end, that is, the west end of the tabernacle,23and make two frames for the corners at the far end.24At these two corners they must be double from the bottom all the way to the top and fitted into a single ring; both shall be like that.25So there will be eight frames and sixteen silver bases—two under each frame.26"Also make crossbars of acacia wood: five for the frames on one side of the tabernacle,27five for those on the other side, and five for the frames on the west, at the far end of the tabernacle.28The center crossbar is to extend from end to end at the middle of the frames.29Overlay the frames with gold and make gold rings to hold the crossbars. Also overlay the crossbars with gold.30"Set up the tabernacle according to the plan shown you on the mountain."

Hebrew Word Study

אַדְנֵי כֶסֶף ʾadnê kesep — silver bases / sockets: Each upright frame rests in two silver sockets. Silver in the Tabernacle was raised by the half-shekel census tax — one socket per person counted. The Tabernacle literally rested on the ransom paid for Israel's life (Exod 30:11–16). The foundation of God's dwelling was the redemption price of his people.

בְּרִיחִים bĕrîḥîm — crossbars / bolts: The horizontal crossbars that locked the vertical frames into a rigid structure. The center crossbar ran the full length of each wall. The Tabernacle's structural integrity came from within: hidden bars holding everything together.

Context

The silver bases are one of the Tabernacle's deepest theological details: they were cast from the silver collected in the census ransom (Exod 30:11–16, 38:25–27). Every Israelite's ransom payment literally became part of the Tabernacle's foundation. God's dwelling rested on the redemption of his people. The connection between atonement and the dwelling is structural, not merely symbolic.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
26:19

The silver bases are not decorative — they are the foundation. And they were cast from the ransom silver. Every time a priest stepped into the Tabernacle, he was standing on the price paid for Israel. The physical structure proclaimed the theological truth: God's presence among his people is possible because their debt has been paid. Nothing has changed in the new covenant, except that the price was not silver but blood.

26:30

"Set up the tabernacle according to the plan shown you on the mountain." The plan command appears three times in the Tabernacle section (25:9, 25:40, 26:30). Repetition is emphasis. The Tabernacle's authority comes entirely from its correspondence to the heavenly reality. Deviation from the pattern is departure from the truth.

Verses 31–37 — The Inner Veil and the Outer Curtain
31"Make a curtain of blue, purple and scarlet yarn and finely twisted linen, with cherubim woven into it by a skilled worker.32Hang it with gold hooks on four posts of acacia wood overlaid with gold and standing on four silver bases.33Hang the curtain from the clasps and place the ark of the covenant law behind the curtain. The curtain will separate the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place.34Put the atonement cover on the ark of the covenant law in the Most Holy Place.35Place the table outside the curtain on the north side of the tabernacle and put the lampstand opposite it on the south side.36"For the entrance to the tent make a curtain of blue, purple and scarlet yarn and finely twisted linen—the work of an embroiderer.37Make gold hooks for this curtain and five posts of acacia wood overlaid with gold. And cast five bronze bases for them."

Hebrew Word Study

פָּרֹכֶת pārōket — the veil / curtain: The inner veil separating the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place. This is the veil torn in two at the crucifixion (Matt 27:51). Its tearing was not an accident but the definitive statement: the barrier between God and humanity has been removed by Christ's death.

קֹדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים qōdeš haqqŏdāšîm — Most Holy Place / Holy of Holies: The superlative of holiness — the holiest of holy places. Only the high priest enters, once a year, with blood. The restricted access defines the problem: sin separates humanity from God's presence. Christ's death resolves it permanently.

Context

The inner veil (v.31–33) and the outer curtain (v.36–37) create two zones of the Tabernacle: the Holy Place (table, lampstand — accessed by priests daily) and the Most Holy Place (ark, mercy seat — accessed by the high priest alone, once a year). The veil is the theological centre of the structure: it marks the point beyond which ordinary human beings cannot go. The torn veil of Matt 27:51 is the Tabernacle's most dramatic moment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
26:33

The veil separates the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place. Its tearing at the crucifixion (Matt 27:51) was not dramatic decoration — it was the definitive end of the old order of restricted access. The veil said: you may not enter. Christ's death said: you may. Not because God has become less holy, but because atonement has been made once for all. The blood of the true high priest has been presented in the true Most Holy Place, and the way is permanently open.

26:31

The veil was woven with cherubim — the guardian figures who stand at the boundary of the divine (Gen 3:24, Ezek 1). To pass through the veil was to pass through the cherubim's barrier. The high priest passed through it with blood. Christ passed through it with his own blood, and in doing so opened the cherubim's barrier permanently.

Ancient Sources

Egyptian Portable Shrines
The shrine from Tutankhamun's tomb (c.1325 BC) has striking structural parallels: gilded wooden framework, fabric coverings, carrying poles. The Tabernacle uses the same portable-shrine technology but fills it with radically different theology.
The Tabernacle is culturally intelligible within its Egyptian context while being theologically unprecedented.
Temple Veil in Antiquity
Josephus (Jewish War 5.5.4) describes the Temple veil as embroidered with a panorama of heaven — consistent with the cherubim-woven veil of Exod 26:31. The veil's size in Herod's Temple: 60 x 30 feet.
The tearing of this massive veil at the crucifixion was a publicly visible, catastrophic event.

Reception History

Hebrews 9–10
The sustained NT typological reading: the veil as Christ's body, torn to open access; the two compartments as the two covenants; the annual high priest as the type of Christ's once-for-all entry.
Origin of "Holy of Holies"
The English phrase "Holy of Holies" comes directly from the Hebrew superlative qōdeš haqqŏdāšîm (v.33) — the most common description of the innermost sanctuary in all subsequent Jewish and Christian theology.

The chapter is inside-out theology in architecture: the most glorious materials nearest to God, the most protective nearest to the world. The veil at the centre is the theological hinge — the boundary between holy and most holy, removed by Christ's death.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 26 builds the architecture of the holy. Every layer, every base, every curtain encodes the same message: God is holy, access requires atonement, and the way through the veil is blood. The veil torn at the crucifixion is the most dramatic event in Tabernacle theology.
