Exodus 5

First Confrontation with Pharaoh; Increased Oppression

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. Moses is not only the principal character of Exodus — he is its author, and the events recorded are largely his own experience. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC. The events of Exodus span approximately 80 years (Moses's birth to the Tabernacle's completion), but the book itself was written after the fact — most likely during the years at Sinai and the wilderness wandering that followed. The Tabernacle instructions and legal material (chs.19–40) may have been committed to writing earliest, as they were immediately actionable. What prompted it: Israel needed a founding narrative — a record of how God delivered them from 430 years of slavery, made them his covenant people at Sinai, and established the Tabernacle as his dwelling among them. Exodus is the story of liberation, but more than that, it is the story of covenant formation and divine habitation. It answers: How did God redeem us? What does he require of us? And how can a holy God dwell with a sinful people?

Verses 1–9 — Moses and Aaron Before Pharaoh; Bricks Without Straw
1Afterward Moses and Aaron went to Pharaoh and said, "This is what the Lord, the God of Israel, says: 'Let my people go, so that they may hold a festival to me in the wilderness.'"2Pharaoh said, "Who is the Lord, that I should obey him and let Israel go? I do not know the Lord and I will not let Israel go."3Then they said, "The God of the Hebrews has met with us. Now let us take a three-day journey into the wilderness to offer sacrifices to the Lord our God, or he may strike us with plagues or with the sword."4But the king of Egypt said, "Moses and Aaron, why are you taking the people away from their labor? Get back to your work!"5Then Pharaoh said, "Look, the people of the land are now numerous, and you are stopping them from working."6That same day Pharaoh gave this order to the slave drivers and overseers in charge of the people:7"You are no longer to supply the people with straw for making bricks; let them go and gather their own straw.8But require them to make the same number of bricks as before; don't reduce the quota. They are lazy; that is why they are crying out, 'Let us go and sacrifice to our God.'9Make the work harder for the people so that they keep working and pay no attention to lies."

Hebrew Word Study

מִי יְהוָה mî YHWH — who is the LORD: Pharaoh's question is the central question of Exodus — and the answer is the entire book. "Who is YHWH?" will be answered ten times in the plagues: YHWH is the one who controls the Nile, the frogs, the darkness, the death of the firstborn. The plagues are not primarily about liberation; they are about revelation. "That you may know that I am the LORD" (7:17; 8:10,22; 9:14,29; 10:2; 14:4,18).

לֹא יָדַעְתִּי lōʾ yādaʿtî — I do not know: The same yādaʿ (know) used of the Pharaoh who "did not know Joseph" (1:8). The Exodus is a story about knowing YHWH. Pharaoh's "I do not know" will become involuntary knowledge through ten judgments.

Historical Context

Egyptian administrative papyri document the brick-quota system in detail — the Anastasi Papyrus records daily brick quotas for workers. Straw was necessary to bind the clay; withholding straw while maintaining quotas is a documented form of administrative punishment in Egyptian labour management. The specific detail of Pithom and Rameses (1:11) and the brick-making process are archaeologically grounded.

Context

Pharaoh's response — "Who is the LORD?" — is not ignorance but contempt. He knows there is a god called YHWH; he is dismissing his authority. His counter-move is brilliant: increase the work, create resentment between Israelites and their own foremen, make Moses's message look like the cause of their suffering. The strategy almost works — the foremen blame Moses (v.21), and Moses blames God (v.22). The chapter is a study in how God's purposes can appear to make things worse before they get better.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 14:4"I will harden Pharaoh's heart... and I will get glory over Pharaoh... and the Egyptians shall know that I am the LORD." The purpose of Pharaoh's resistance is YHWH's self-revelation.
  • Rom 9:17Paul quotes Exod 9:16 — "for this very purpose I have raised you up, that I might show my power in you." Pharaoh's hardness serves God's revelatory purpose.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:2

"Who is the LORD, that I should obey his voice?" This question will be answered by ten plagues. But note: Pharaoh does not ask "does the LORD exist?" He asks "who is the LORD?" — what authority does he have over me? This is the question of sovereignty, not existence. Pharaoh considers himself sovereign over Egypt and everything in it, including the Hebrews' labour. The plagues are YHWH's systematic answer to "who are you?" — demonstrating domain by domain that everything Pharaoh claims to control belongs to Israel's God.

5:22–23

"O Lord, why have you done evil to this people? Why did you ever send me? For since I came to Pharaoh to speak in your name, he has done evil to this people, and you have not delivered your people at all." This is Moses's lament — raw, honest, angry. And God does not rebuke him for it. The very next verse (6:1) is "Now you shall see what I will do to Pharaoh." God's response to honest complaint is not silence or rebuke but the unfolding of his plan. Moses's prayer sounds like failure; it is the prelude to the plagues.

Verses 10–23 — The Increased Burden; the People Blame Moses
10Then the slave drivers and the overseers went out and said to the people, "This is what Pharaoh says: 'I will not give you any more straw.11Go and get your own straw wherever you can find it, but your work will not be reduced at all.'"12So the people scattered all over Egypt to gather stubble to use for straw.13The slave drivers kept pressing them, saying, "Complete the work required of you for each day, just as when you had straw."14And Pharaoh's slave drivers beat the Israelite overseers they had appointed, demanding, "Why haven't you met your quota of bricks yesterday or today, as before?"15Then the Israelite overseers went and appealed to Pharaoh: "Why have you treated your servants this way?16Your servants are given no straw, yet we are told, 'Make bricks!' Your servants are being beaten, but the fault is with your own people."17Pharaoh said, "Lazy, that's what you are—lazy! That is why you keep saying, 'Let us go and sacrifice to the Lord.'18Now get to work. You will not be given any straw, yet you must produce your full quota of bricks."19The Israelite overseers realized they were in trouble when they were told, "You are not to reduce the number of bricks required of you for each day."20When they left Pharaoh, they found Moses and Aaron waiting to meet them,21and they said, "May the Lord look on you and judge you! You have made us obnoxious to Pharaoh and his officials and have put a sword in their hand to kill us."22Moses returned to the Lord and said, "Why, Lord, why have you brought trouble on this people? Is this why you sent me?23Ever since I went to Pharaoh to speak in your name, he has brought trouble on this people, and you have not rescued your people at all."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Pharaoh
The defiant king
"Who is the LORD?" — his contemptuous question will be answered by ten plagues. His hardness is both his own choice and God's instrument for revelation.
Moses
The doubting deliverer
His lament in vv.22–23 is the first of several — a pattern of honest protest that God consistently receives and answers.
The Israelite Foremen
Caught between
Beaten by the Egyptians (v.14), blamed by their own people (v.21), blaming Moses — the suffering of the middle is Exodus's most overlooked tragedy.

Ancient Sources

Anastasi Papyrus (c.1210 BC)
Documents daily brick-making quotas for Egyptian workers — provides the administrative background for the quota system in Exod 5. The papyrus records foremen reporting on worker output.
The brick-quota episode is consistent with documented Egyptian labour administration.
Harris Papyrus
Records Ramesses III's construction projects using conscripted labour — confirming the large-scale use of forced labour for state building projects in the New Kingdom period.
The Exodus labour context is well-grounded in Egyptian administrative practice.

Reception History

Calvin, Commentary on Exodus
The increased oppression after Moses's first confrontation is for Calvin the normal pattern of God's redemptive action: things often appear to get worse before the deliverance. Faith is tested precisely at the point where the promise seems most contradicted by circumstances.
Origen, Homilies
Moses's complaint to God (vv.22–23) is read by Origen as the soul's honest prayer in spiritual darkness — God welcomes the honest complaint far more than performance of contentment.

Literary Structure

Demand — vv.1–5"Let my people go" — Pharaoh's dismissal: "Who is YHWH?"
Counter-move — vv.6–9Pharaoh increases brick quota — no straw, same numbers
Breakdown — vv.10–19Foremen fail; beaten by Egyptian taskmasters
Accusation — vv.20–21Foremen blame Moses and Aaron
Lament — vv.22–23Moses complains to God: "Why have you done evil to this people?"
The chapter has no resolution — it ends in complaint. This is deliberate. The narrative refuses to skip the darkness. God's answer comes in 6:1, but not before the full weight of the setback is registered. The Exodus story includes the experience of unanswered prayer, increased suffering, and honest protest.

Intertextual Threading

Exod 5:2→Exod 14:18Answered
Pharaoh: "Who is YHWH?" → "the Egyptians shall know that I am the LORD" (14:18). The question of ch.5 is answered definitively at the Red Sea.
Exod 3:18–19→Exod 5:1–2Predicted
God warned Moses: "the king of Egypt will not let you go unless compelled by a mighty hand." Exod 5 is the beginning of that compulsion.
Exod 5:22–23→Exod 6:1Answer
Moses's lament ends ch.5; God's "Now you shall see" opens ch.6. The complaint and the response are a single movement.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 5 is the chapter of the dark before the dawn. The commission was given; the confrontation happened; things got worse. This is the Exodus pattern — and the pattern of most redemptive history. God's response to Moses's complaint will be the six-fold covenant renewal speech of chapter 6.
