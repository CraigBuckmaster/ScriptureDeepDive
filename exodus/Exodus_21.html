Exodus 21

The Book of the Covenant: Civil and Criminal Law

Hebrew

Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. Moses is not only the principal character of Exodus — he is its author, and the events recorded are largely his own experience. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC. The events of Exodus span approximately 80 years (Moses's birth to the Tabernacle's completion), but the book itself was written after the fact — most likely during the years at Sinai and the wilderness wandering that followed. The Tabernacle instructions and legal material (chs.19–40) may have been committed to writing earliest, as they were immediately actionable. What prompted it: Israel needed a founding narrative — a record of how God delivered them from 430 years of slavery, made them his covenant people at Sinai, and established the Tabernacle as his dwelling among them. Exodus is the story of liberation, but more than that, it is the story of covenant formation and divine habitation. It answers: How did God redeem us? What does he require of us? And how can a holy God dwell with a sinful people?

Verses 1–11 — Hebrew Servants
1"These are the laws you are to set before them:2"If you buy a Hebrew servant, he is to serve you for six years. But in the seventh year, he shall go free, without paying anything.3If he comes alone, he is to go free alone; but if he has a wife when he comes, she is to go with him.4If his master gives him a wife and she bears him sons or daughters, the woman and her children shall belong to her master, and only the man shall go free.5"But if the servant declares, 'I love my master and my wife and children and do not want to go free,'6then his master must take him before the judges. He shall take him to the door or the doorpost and pierce his ear with an awl. Then he will be his servant for life.7"If a man sells his daughter as a servant, she is not to go free as male servants do.8If she does not please the master who has selected her for himself, he must let her be redeemed. He has no right to sell her to foreigners, because he has broken faith with her.9If he selects her for his son, he must grant her the rights of a daughter.10If he marries another woman, he must not deprive the first one of her food, clothing and marital rights.11If he does not provide her with these three things, she is to go free, without any payment of money.

Hebrew Word Study

עַיִן תַּחַת עַיִן ʿayin taḥat ʿayin — eye for an eye: The lex talionis — not a mandate for personal revenge but a principle of proportional justice for courts. It limits punishment to equivalence, replacing the disproportionate vengeance cycles common in ANE cultures. Jesus addresses its misuse (Matt 5:38–39) but not its original purpose: proportional legal justice.

מִשְׁפָּטִים mišpāṭîm — judgements / ordinances: The casuistic case laws (if-then format) that follow the Decalogue. They are the Decalogue's principles applied to concrete situations: how does "you shall not murder" apply when someone is struck accidentally? The mišpāṭîm are the covenant's practical wisdom.

Historical Context

The Book of the Covenant contains the closest parallels in the Pentateuch to ancient Near Eastern law codes: Code of Hammurabi (c.1750 BC), Laws of Eshnunna, and Hittite Laws. The parallels demonstrate shared legal culture while the differences are theologically significant — Israelite law uniquely protects slaves' rights (vv.2–11), limits punishments more strictly, and grounds all law in the character of the covenant God.

Context

Hebrew slavery in these laws is not chattel slavery — it is debt servitude with a six-year maximum and a jubilee mechanism. The slave who chooses to remain (vv.5–6) does so out of love, not compulsion. The ear-boring ceremony expresses voluntary permanent commitment. The laws for female slaves (vv.7–11) provide protections far exceeding ANE norms — they cannot be sold to foreigners and must be freed if their rights are violated.

Cross-Reference

  • Lev 25:39–55The jubilee expansion of the slavery laws — freedom every fifty years, grounded in the Exodus: "they are my servants whom I brought out of Egypt."
  • Matt 5:38–39Jesus on the lex talionis: "You have heard... an eye for an eye... but I say to you, do not resist the one who is evil." He addresses its misapplication in personal vengeance.
  • Ps 40:6–8The ear-boring imagery: "You have dug ears for me" — the Psalmist uses the voluntary-slave metaphor for joyful covenant obedience. "I delight to do your will, O my God."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
21:5–6

The voluntary slave who loves his master and chooses to remain is one of the most striking images in the law. He could go free — the six years are completed, the debt is paid. He chooses to stay. His ear is bored at the doorpost — the same doorposts that bore the Passover blood. The imagery is not accidental: the one who was freed and chooses to return is the slave of love, not compulsion. Paul calls himself this (Rom 1:1: "Paul, a servant/slave of Christ Jesus").

21:24

Eye for eye is the most misunderstood law in the Pentateuch. It is not a mandate for personal retaliation — in its original legal context, it is a limitation on disproportionate punishment by courts. Before lex talionis, the powerful took sevenfold vengeance (Gen 4:24). The law says: the punishment may not exceed the injury. This is civilisation, not cruelty.

Verses 12–27 — Personal Injuries
12"Anyone who strikes a person with a fatal blow is to be put to death.13However, if it is not done intentionally, but God lets it happen, they are to flee to a place I will designate.14But if anyone schemes and kills someone deliberately, that person is to be taken from my altar and put to death.15"Anyone who attacks their father or mother is to be put to death.16"Anyone who kidnaps someone is to be put to death, whether the victim has been sold or is still in the kidnapper's possession.17"Anyone who curses their father or mother is to be put to death.18"If people quarrel and one person hits another with a stone or with their fist and the victim does not die but is confined to bed,19the one who struck the blow will not be held liable if the other can get up and walk around outside with a staff; however, the guilty party must pay the injured person for any loss of time and see that the victim is completely healed.20"Anyone who beats their male or female slave with a rod must be punished if the slave dies as a direct result,21but they are not to be punished if the slave recovers after a day or two, since the slave is their property.22"If people are fighting and hit a pregnant woman and she gives birth prematurely but there is no serious injury, the offender must be fined whatever the woman's husband demands and the court allows.23But if there is serious injury, you are to take life for life,24eye for eye, tooth for tooth, hand for hand, foot for foot,25burn for burn, wound for wound, bruise for bruise.26"An owner who hits a male or female slave in the eye and destroys it must let the slave go free to compensate for the eye.27And an owner who knocks out the tooth of a male or female slave must let the slave go free to compensate for the tooth.

Verses 28–36 — Laws About Oxen; Property and Negligence
28"If a bull gores a man or woman to death, the bull is to be stoned to death, and its meat must not be eaten. But the owner of the bull will not be held responsible.29If, however, the bull has had the habit of goring and the owner has been warned but has not kept it penned up and it kills a man or woman, the bull is to be stoned and its owner also is to be put to death.30However, if payment is demanded, the owner may redeem his life by the payment of whatever is demanded.31This law also applies if the bull gores a son or daughter.32If the bull gores a male or female slave, the owner must pay thirty shekels of silver to the master of the slave, and the bull is to be stoned to death.33"If anyone uncovers a pit or digs one and fails to cover it and an ox or a donkey falls into it,34the one who opened the pit must pay the owner for the loss and take the dead animal in exchange.35"If anyone's bull injures someone else's bull and it dies, the two parties are to sell the live bull and divide both the money and the dead animal equally.36However, if it was known that the bull had the habit of goring, yet the owner did not keep it penned up, the owner must pay, animal for animal, and take the dead animal in exchange."

The elohim
God or judges
"He shall bring him to God" (v.6) — in many laws, ʾĕlōhîm in this context refers to judges, not directly to God. The legal system mediates the divine claim.

Ancient Sources

Code of Hammurabi
The Babylonian law code (c.1750 BC) contains numerous parallels to Exod 21 — similar case-law format and many of the same situations (goring oxen, injury compensation, slave regulations). The key differences: Hammurabi's code is class-stratified; Exodus equalises punishment regardless of social rank.
The parallels demonstrate shared legal tradition; the differences reveal the covenant's distinctive values.
Hittite Laws
Hittite legal texts address similar cases (homicide, bodily harm, slavery) with striking structural parallels to Exod 21.
The Book of the Covenant participates in the broader ANE legal tradition while transforming it through covenant theology.

Reception History

Luther
The civil law of Israel as the expression of natural law — the lex talionis as the universal principle of proportional justice underlying all just legal systems.
John Calvin
The Book of the Covenant as applied covenant theology — the Decalogue provides the principles; the mišpāṭîm provide the cases. The law is not arbitrary but the expression of God's character in community.

Literary Structure

Slavery laws — vv.1–11Hebrew servants; the voluntary permanent slave; female slave protections
Capital offences — vv.12–17Murder; manslaughter; striking parents; kidnapping
Bodily harm — vv.18–27Proportional compensation; lex talionis; slave rights
Property offences — vv.28–36Goring ox; negligence; restitution principles
The laws move from persons to property, from intentional to unintentional, always applying the same principles: human dignity, proportional justice, protection of the vulnerable.

Intertextual Threading

Exod 20:2→21:2Foundation
The Exodus grounds the slavery law: "I brought you out of the house of slavery" → Hebrew slaves go free in the seventh year. The redeemed do not enslave permanently.
21:5–6→Ps 40:6; Phil 1:1Type
The voluntary ear-bored slave → "I delight to do your will" (Ps 40:8); Paul's "servant of Christ Jesus" — the freed person who chooses loving service.
21:24→Matt 5:38–39Extension
Lex talionis (proportional court justice) → Jesus extends to personal non-retaliation — the law's principle exceeded by kingdom grace.

Theological Themes Tracker

Exodus 21 is justice with a human face. The laws protect the slave, limit punishment, compensate the injured, and hold the negligent responsible. The God who freed Israel from Egypt writes into their law the values that made slavery a theological problem: every human bears his image.
