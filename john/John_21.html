John 21

The Risen Jesus Appears and Restores Peter

Author

John the son of Zebedee — fisherman, apostle, and “the disciple whom Jesus loved” (John 13:23; 19:26; 20:2; 21:7, 20). The fourth Gospel never names its author directly, but the evidence converges on John: (1) The “beloved disciple” is an eyewitness (19:35; 21:24) present at the Last Supper, the cross, and the empty tomb. (2) Early and unanimous tradition: Irenaeus (c. AD 180), Clement of Alexandria, Origen, and Eusebius all identify the author as John the apostle writing from Ephesus in old age. (3) The Gospel’s intimate knowledge of Jerusalem topography (Pool of Bethesda, Pool of Siloam, the Pavement/Gabbatha) is confirmed by archaeology. (4) The eyewitness claim of 19:35 and the closing verse of 21:24 point to direct authorial witness. Liberal scholarship has proposed an anonymous Johannine community or the Elder John of Ephesus; the traditional identification with the apostle remains the best-attested position.

When written

c. AD 85–95, probably from Ephesus, making it the latest of the four Gospels. The Rylands Papyrus (P52, c. AD 125) — the earliest surviving NT manuscript fragment — contains John 18:31-33, 37-38, confirming the Gospel was in circulation in Egypt by the early second century. A pre-AD 70 date has been argued from the Temple language in chs 2, 5, and 10, which reads as description of a standing building; the majority of scholars favour a post-70 date for the final composition. The three Johannine letters and Revelation share vocabulary and theological concerns with the Gospel, suggesting a common Ephesian milieu.

What prompted it

John states his purpose explicitly — a rarity in the Gospels: “These are written that you may believe that Jesus is the Messiah, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name” (20:31). The Gospel was written to produce and sustain faith in the person of Jesus as divine Son. It supplements the Synoptics rather than duplicates them: John omits the Baptism, the Transfiguration, the Olivet Discourse, and the institution of the Lord’s Supper as a formal narrative, while providing material found nowhere else — the wedding at Cana, Nicodemus, the Samaritan woman, the raising of Lazarus, the Farewell Discourse (chs 14–17), and the resurrection appearances to Mary Magdalene and Thomas. Its elevated Christology (“In the beginning was the Word”) addresses late first-century challenges to the full divinity of Christ and lays the foundation for all subsequent trinitarian theology.

It was full of large fish, 153, but even with so many the net was not torn.12Jesus said to them, "Come and have breakfast." None of the disciples dared ask him, "Who are you?" They knew it was the Lord.13Jesus came, took the bread and gave it to them, and did the same with the fish.14This was now the third time Jesus appeared to his disciples after he was raised from the dead.

Greek Word Study

anthrakia an-THRAX-ia — charcoal fire: John specifies "a fire of burning coals" (anthrakia, v.9) — the same word used for the charcoal fire in the high priest's courtyard where Peter warmed himself as he denied Jesus (18:18). The verbal echo is deliberate: the restoration of Peter (vv.15-19) takes place beside the same kind of fire as the scene of his failure. The charcoal fire is the site of both the denial and the restoration, connecting them as a deliberate act of memory and grace.

ischysan autous helkysai is-KHY-san au-TOUS hel-KY-sai — they were not able to haul it in: The catch of 153 large fish that does not tear the net (v.11) — a net that could not hold the weight but did not break — has generated extensive allegorical interpretation: 153 = all the species of fish known to ancient natural history (Jerome's tradition), or a triangular number (1+2+3...+17=153), or the numerical value of certain Hebrew phrases. More importantly, the unbroken net is a sign of the resurrection community: the net that holds all peoples gathered by the risen Lord does not break.

Places

Sea of Galilee / Sea of Tiberias
32.8208° N, 35.5847° E
The Gospel's final appearance takes place on the same lake where Jesus walked on water (6:19), where Peter and Andrew were called (Matt 4:18-19), and where the disciples spent so much of their time during the Galilean ministry. The return to Galilee after the resurrection is the return to the beginning — the mission is recommissioned from where it started. The north shore of the lake, where Capernaum, Bethsaida, and the traditional site of Peter's restoration (Church of the Primacy of Peter at Tabgha) are located, is the setting for this final chapter.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
21:3

"I'm going out to fish" — Peter's return to fishing after the resurrection has been read as despondency (he's given up on the mission), as practical provision (they needed food and income), or simply as not knowing what to do in the interval between resurrection and Pentecost. John does not indicate despondency; the disciples simply go fishing — and the risen Jesus meets them exactly where they are, in a boat, doing what they know.

21:6-7

The beloved disciple recognises Jesus first; Peter acts first. The same pattern as 20:4-6 (the beloved disciple reaches the tomb first; Peter enters first). The disciple who loves most sees most clearly; the man of action is moved by what the loving disciple sees. Together they embody the full response to the risen Lord: perception and passionate action.

21:9

"A fire of burning coals there with fish on it, and some bread" — the risen Jesus has been cooking breakfast for his disciples. This is one of the most domestic scenes in the resurrection narratives: the Lord of creation, risen from the dead, making breakfast on a charcoal fire by the lake. The intimacy of the provision — not a vision, not a theological discourse, but breakfast made with care for specific people he knows are cold and hungry from fishing all night — is the resurrection's most tender detail.

21:11-12

"153 large fish, but the net was not torn... Come and have breakfast" — the impossible abundance (153 large fish in one haul) followed by the simplest possible invitation (breakfast). The miracle and the meal belong together: the missionary abundance and the pastoral intimacy. Jesus provides overwhelmingly and then sits down to share the meal with his disciples.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:6

Calvin uses the net-on-the-right-side command to illustrate the dependence of all fruitful ministry on Christ's direction. The disciples had fished all night without result; a single word from Jesus produces an overwhelming catch. The principle: human effort in ministry — however skilled and diligent — is without fruit until directed by Christ. The productive cast is made on his word, not on the fishermen's experience.

21:11

Calvin resists allegorical readings of 153 but draws the pastoral conclusion from the unbroken net: the church gathered by the gospel does not lose its members through the pressure of numbers. The net that holds 153 large fish without tearing is a sign of the mission's success under Christ's protection — the elect are gathered and none lost (17:12).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:11

153 — the number has generated more commentary than almost any other detail in John. The three main interpretive approaches: (1) The number of known fish species (Jerome, following ancient natural history lists) symbolising the universal mission; (2) The triangular number 17×18/2=153 (Augustine), representing the perfection of law and grace; (3) The actual number of fish caught, recorded precisely because John was present. The last is the simplest and most plausible. The symbolic readings may be secondary meanings John intended to evoke.

21:3

"That night they caught nothing" — the night/nothing contrast with the morning/abundance sets up the appearance: it is only when the risen Jesus speaks that the empty effort becomes fruitful. The pattern mirrors the wedding at Cana ("they have no wine" → abundance), the feeding of five thousand ("only five loaves" → twelve baskets), and the foot-washing ("you do not understand now" → understanding after resurrection).

Greek Word Study

agapas me / philō se a-GA-pas me / phi-LO se — Do you love me? / I love you: The threefold question and answer (vv.15-17) uses two different Greek verbs: Jesus asks agapas (do you love with self-giving love?) twice; Peter answers philō (I love you with brotherly affection). Jesus asks a third time using philō — meeting Peter at his own level. Interpretations differ: (1) John uses the two words interchangeably throughout his Gospel (a common John stylistic pattern); (2) the shift is deliberate — Jesus finally accepting Peter's more modest word rather than demanding the higher. Either way, three affirmations address three denials.

boske / poimaine BOS-ke / poi-MAI-ne — feed (lambs) / tend, shepherd (sheep): The three commission formulas vary: "Feed my lambs" (boske arnia, v.15), "Take care of my sheep" (poimaine probata, v.16), "Feed my sheep" (boske probata, v.17). The variation in both verb and noun is deliberate: boske (feed, provide food) and poimaine (shepherd, lead, care for) together encompass the full pastoral office. Both lambs (young, vulnerable) and sheep (mature) are included. The triple commission mirrors and reverses the triple denial.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
21:15-17

"Simon, son of John, do you love me?" — Jesus uses the pre-call name (Simon) rather than the post-call name (Peter) in all three questions. He is addressing the Peter who denied him, not the Peter who confessed him. The pastoral genius: he does not pretend the failure did not happen; he addresses it at its root (love) and at its foundation (identity). After three affirmations, he will restore the name and the calling: "follow me" (v.19).

21:15

"Do you love me more than these?" — the comparison may be with the other disciples ("more than these disciples love me?") or with the fishing gear and boats ("more than these things?"). If the former, it reminds Peter of his boast ("even if all fall away, I will not," Matt 26:33). If the latter, it asks whether Peter loves Jesus more than the career he has just returned to. Either way, the question goes to the root: is Jesus the supreme love of Peter's life?

21:18-19

"When you are old you will stretch out your hands, and someone else will dress you and lead you where you do not want to go" — the prediction of martyrdom is also a prediction of the manner: "stretch out your hands" is the posture of crucifixion. Then: "Follow me!" The call with which Peter's discipleship began (1:43) is renewed at the end — this time with the knowledge of where following leads. Peter will follow Jesus to his own cross.

21:22

"If I want him to remain alive until I come, what is that to you? You must follow me" — the repeated command strips away comparison. Peter's question about the beloved disciple is not wrong but irrelevant: each disciple has their own path of following. The command is personal and non-comparative: "you must follow me" — not the beloved disciple, not anyone else. The community of the Resurrection is not a community of identical stories but of a single command received and obeyed differently by each.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:15-17

Calvin insists that the love Jesus asks for is the foundation of all genuine ministry. The question "do you love me?" before "feed my sheep" establishes the order: the pastor who feeds the flock out of duty or ambition or self-interest is not fulfilling Jesus's commission; only the one who feeds out of love for Jesus is. Ministry that is not an expression of love for Christ is not Christian ministry.

21:22

Calvin uses "what is that to you?" to warn against the sin of spiritual comparison — measuring one's own calling against another's. Each believer has their own path of following. The beloved disciple's path (apparently natural death and long life) is not Peter's path (martyrdom). Neither is superior; both are "follow me." The comparison that diminishes one calling in favour of another is a failure to hear the personal "you must follow me."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:15-17

The agapaō/phileō question — whether John uses them interchangeably or distinguishably. Most modern scholars (Carson, Ridderbos, Keener) argue John uses them as synonyms throughout his Gospel (cf. 3:35 and 5:20 use both for the Father's love of the Son). The shift in v.17 to Jesus using phileō may be intentional (meeting Peter at his level) or stylistic variation. The debate has been ongoing for centuries; neither side can be certain.

21:24

"This is the disciple who testifies to these things and who wrote them down. We know that his testimony is true" — the final editorial note identifying the beloved disciple as the author. The "we know" suggests the final chapter (or the book) was edited or attested by a community that knew the author — perhaps the Johannine community at Ephesus. This is consistent with the hypothesis that John the apostle wrote the Gospel and a disciple or community of disciples provided the final edition.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Peter
Restored and Recommissioned
His trajectory in John: fisherman called (1:42) → confessor ("We believe and know," 6:69) → denier (18:15-27) → restored and commissioned ("Feed my sheep," 21:15-17) → martyr predicted ("stretch out your hands," 21:18). Three denials met by three affirmations; a charcoal fire of failure met by a charcoal fire of restoration. The man who said "even if I have to die with you, I will never disown you" is the man whose death Jesus predicts — and who follows him to it.
The Beloved Disciple
The Author, Attested by the Community
His final appearance (vv.20-24) reveals his identity as the Gospel's author — "the disciple who testifies to these things and who wrote them down." He follows Jesus (v.20) even as Peter follows Jesus (v.19) — the two paths of discipleship, different in form and outcome, both faithful. The Gospel ends where it began: with a disciple following Jesus, and the author bearing witness.

Reception History

"Do You Love Me?" — The Question at the Root of Ministry
John 21:15-17 is the foundational text for Christian pastoral theology. The sequence — "do you love me?" three times, then "feed my sheep" — establishes that ministry is rooted in love for Christ, not competence, ambition, or duty. Every ordination service, every pastoral charge, every commissioning of Christian workers returns to this scene. The question is not "are you qualified?" or "are you experienced?" or even "have you repented?" — it is "do you love me?" The restoration is not conditional on Peter's confessing the denials but on his affirming his love.
The Unbroken Net — 153 Fish — in Mission Theology
The 153-fish catch with the unbroken net has been a persistent image in Christian mission theology. The interpretation of 153 as all the species of fish (therefore all the nations) was standard from Jerome through the medieval period. Whether or not this was John's intention, the image has generated the claim that the church's mission is universal in scope and that the net that gathers all peoples does not break under the weight of diversity. The Church of the Primacy of Peter at Tabgha on the Sea of Galilee marks the traditional site of the breakfast and Peter's restoration.

Literary Structure

The charcoal fire at the restoration mirrors the charcoal fire at the denial — John's most deliberate narrative echo. Peter failed beside a charcoal fire; he is restored beside one. The setting is the grace: the risen Jesus chooses the location that will remind Peter of his failure and transform the memory through restoration.
The structural parallel between the three denials (18:17, 25, 27) and the three commissions (21:15-17) is John's most extended narrative inclusio within the passion and resurrection sequence. The number three is not coincidental — it is the precise number required to address each denial with an affirmation.
The Gospel's Closing Hyperbole — v.25"Even the whole world would not have room for the books that would be written" — the rhetorical flourish that closes the Gospel. John does not claim to have told everything; he claims that what he has told is sufficient for the purpose (20:31). The inexhaustible Jesus exceeds all record; the record that exists is enough.

Textual Notes

21:15"More than these" — what does "these" refer to?
"Do you love me more than these?" — the antecedent of "these" (toutōn) is disputed: (1) more than these disciples love me? (2) more than you love these things (boats, fish, fishing life)? NA28 does not resolve the ambiguity; the Greek allows both. The first reading connects to Peter's boast (Matt 26:33); the second connects to the return to fishing in v.3.
The boast-comparison reading has the stronger contextual support (Peter had specifically claimed superior commitment). Both readings are legitimate; John may have intended the ambiguity.
21:25The closing hyperbole
No textual variant. The statement is authentic to John. It is one of the few clearly hyperbolic statements in the Gospel — used to close with a gesture toward the inexhaustibility of Jesus that no record could capture.
The hyperbole is original and deliberate. John is aware of the incompleteness of any record of Jesus — the Gospel he has written is sufficient for its purpose (20:31) but not exhaustive.

Scholarly Debates

Is John 21 original to the Gospel or a later addition?
A later addition by the same author or a disciple
Many critical scholars (Bultmann, Brown)
Chapter 20:30-31 reads as a conclusion; chapter 21 is an appendix. The different location (Galilee vs Jerusalem) and the note about the beloved disciple's death rumour (v.23) suggest it was added after the author's death, possibly by the Johannine community. The style is consistent with the rest of the Gospel.
Original, integral part of the Gospel
Carson, Morris, Keener
No manuscript of John lacks ch.21; it was always part of the Gospel. Chapter 20:30-31 is not a "conclusion" but a purpose statement that can appear at the end of a major section (as Paul puts purpose statements mid-letter). Chapter 21 provides the necessary rehabilitation of Peter and the identification of the author.
Added by the same author as an epilogue
Majority scholarly position
The style is authentically Johannine; the content (Peter's restoration, author identification) is necessary for the Gospel's completeness. The author added it after the main body was complete, perhaps after the death of the beloved disciple prompted the clarification of v.23.
The "integral epilogue" position is most widely held: ch.21 is authentically Johannine (same style, same theology, all manuscripts include it) but was added as an epilogue after the main narrative concluded at 20:31. It rounds out the Gospel with the restoration of Peter and the identification of the author.

Theological Themes Tracker

John 21 is the epilogue that makes the Gospel complete. The miraculous catch recommissions the disciples for mission from the beginning-place (Galilee, by the lake). The breakfast beside the charcoal fire restores Peter from the failure-place (charcoal fire, three denials). The three questions replace the three denials not by erasing them but by overwriting them with love. And the final command — "follow me" — is the same word Jesus spoke when Peter first left his nets by this same lake. The Gospel that began with disciples coming to see where Jesus stays (1:38-39) ends with the command to follow where Jesus leads. The last word of the Gospel is "follow."
