Author

Solomon ben David, c.990–931 BC. Israel's third king, son of David and Bathsheba. Credited by Scripture with 3,000 proverbs and 1,005 songs (1 Kgs 4:32). His wisdom was divinely given — not merely accumulated experience but a gift granted at Gibeon (1 Kgs 3:5–14). He built the Temple, forged commercial alliances across the ancient world, and attracted foreign dignitaries to Jerusalem including the Queen of Sheba (1 Kgs 10). Proverbs 1–9 are the most clearly Solomonic; chs. 25–29 were copied by the men of Hezekiah (25:1); chs. 30–31 are attributed to Agur and Lemuel respectively.

When written

The core Solomonic material (chs. 1–9, 10–22:16) dates to Solomon's reign, c.970–931 BC. The final compilation and editing likely continued through the Hezekiah period (c.715–686 BC), when his scribes copied additional Solomonic proverbs (25:1). The book reached its present form sometime in the late monarchic or early post-exilic period.

What prompted it

Proverbs is a father's sustained address to a son standing on the threshold of adult life. The driving question is: How does a young person navigate the real world — its temptations, its social pressures, its competing voices — without being destroyed? The book's answer is not a list of rules but a vision of reality: the world was made with Wisdom woven into it (ch. 8), and the person who aligns their life with that grain will flourish; the person who fights it will be broken by it. "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7) is the thesis of the entire book.

Hebrew Word Study

כְּבֹד אֱלֹהִים הַסְתֵּר דָּבָר kĕbôd ʾĕlōhîm hastēr dābār — the glory of God is to conceal a matter: V.2: one of the most epistemologically rich proverbs in the collection. God's glory is expressed in concealment — the depths of reality are not exhausted by human inquiry. The king's glory is in investigation — royal wisdom is expressed in the diligent search for truth. Both are honoured: divine hiddenness and human inquiry.

גֶּחָלִים אַתָּה חֹתֶה gĕḥālîm ʾattāh ḥōteh — you will heap burning coals: V.22: the burning coals metaphor is one of the most discussed in Proverbs. The most likely ancient interpretation: generosity to an enemy produces burning shame — a hot-faced contrition — in the recipient. The LORD then rewards the giver. Paul quotes this in Rom 12:20 as the basis for non-retaliation and enemy love.

Context

Chapter 25 opens the "Hezekiah collection" (25–29) — Solomonic proverbs copied by the scribes of King Hezekiah (c.715–686 BC). The chapter is dominated by extended comparative proverbs ("like... is...") — one of the most artistically developed forms in the book. V.2's concealment/investigation parallelism is among the book's most philosophically rich. V.21–22's enemy-feeding instruction is the boldest ethical challenge in the collection — a direct anticipation of Jesus' "love your enemies."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
25:2

It is the glory of God to conceal a matter; to search out a matter is the glory of kings. Divine glory and human glory are expressed in opposite activities. God's greatness is expressed in depth and hiddenness — the depths of reality are not exhausted by human inquiry. Human greatness (specifically royal/governmental greatness) is expressed in diligent investigation. Both are honoured. This proverb legitimises both theology (probing what God has revealed) and science (investigating what God has embedded in creation).

25:11

Like apples of gold in settings of silver is a ruling rightly given. The visual image is precise: gold objects (dabar — here a word or ruling) displayed in silver settings. The right word at the right moment has this aesthetic quality — it is both intrinsically valuable (gold) and exquisitely fitted to its context (silver settings). Timing and precision in speech are not merely pragmatic but aesthetic goods.

25:15

Through patience a ruler can be persuaded, and a gentle tongue can break a bone. The power of patient, gentle speech to accomplish what force cannot. A bone (one of the hardest materials in the body) can be broken by a soft tongue — not by argument or force but by sustained, patient gentleness. This is one of Proverbs' most counter-intuitive power proverbs: the gentle is stronger than the forceful.

25:21–22

If your enemy is hungry, give him food to eat; if he is thirsty, give him water to drink. In doing this, you will heap burning coals on his head, and the LORD will reward you. The most ethically demanding instruction in Proverbs. The "burning coals" most likely refers to burning shame/conviction in the recipient — generosity to an enemy produces the hot-faced contrition of someone who has received undeserved kindness. Paul quotes this in Rom 12:20 as the foundation of Christian non-retaliation.

25:28

Like a city whose walls are broken through is a person who lacks self-control. The military metaphor is precise. City walls existed precisely to control who entered — without them, the city was open to any enemy. The person without self-control has no internal governance — every impulse, every temptation, every mood can enter and take up residence. Self-control is not mere willpower; it is the structural integrity of the person.

Ancient Sources

Hezekiah Collection
The superscription at v.1 is historically significant: it identifies real scribal activity under a named king. Hezekiah's reign (c.715–686 BC) included religious reforms (2 Kgs 18–20) and literary activity. The collection suggests a royal court that valued and preserved wisdom literature.
The Hezekiah superscription provides one of the few datable events in Proverbs' editorial history.

Reception History

Rom 12:20
Paul quotes vv.21–22 directly as the basis for "do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good." The enemy-feeding principle becomes the heart of Pauline ethics of non-retaliation.
Matt 5:44
Jesus' "love your enemies" is the fulfilment of the trajectory begun in v.21.
