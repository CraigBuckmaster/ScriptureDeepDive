Themes

Author: Solomon ben David, c.990–931 BC. Israel's third king, son of David and Bathsheba. Credited by Scripture with 3,000 proverbs and 1,005 songs (1 Kgs 4:32). His wisdom was divinely given — not merely accumulated experience but a gift granted at Gibeon (1 Kgs 3:5–14). He built the Temple, forged commercial alliances across the ancient world, and attracted foreign dignitaries to Jerusalem including the Queen of Sheba (1 Kgs 10). Proverbs 1–9 are the most clearly Solomonic; chs. 25–29 were copied by the men of Hezekiah (25:1); chs. 30–31 are attributed to Agur and Lemuel respectively. When written: The core Solomonic material (chs. 1–9, 10–22:16) dates to Solomon's reign, c.970–931 BC. The final compilation and editing likely continued through the Hezekiah period (c.715–686 BC), when his scribes copied additional Solomonic proverbs (25:1). The book reached its present form sometime in the late monarchic or early post-exilic period. What prompted it: Proverbs is a father's sustained address to a son standing on the threshold of adult life. The driving question is: How does a young person navigate the real world — its temptations, its social pressures, its competing voices — without being destroyed? The book's answer is not a list of rules but a vision of reality: the world was made with Wisdom woven into it (ch. 8), and the person who aligns their life with that grain will flourish; the person who fights it will be broken by it. "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7) is the thesis of the entire book.

Hebrew Word Study

גַּאֲוָה לִפְנֵי שֶׁבֶר gaʾăwāh lipnê šeber — pride before destruction: V.18: the most quoted single verse in Proverbs. Gaʾăwāh (pride, arrogance) + šeber (breaking, shattering). Pride is not just a moral failure; it is a structural instability. The proud person is building on a foundation that cannot hold. The sequence is inevitable: pride → destruction, haughty spirit → fall. The verse does not say pride causes punishment; it says pride is the condition from which collapse is inevitable.

לֵב אָדָם יְחַשֵּׁב דַּרְכּוֹ lēb ʾādām yĕḥaššēb darkô — the human heart plans its course: V.9: the great sovereignty proverb. Humans plan (ḥāšab — think, devise, calculate); the LORD directs (kûn — establishes, makes firm). This is not fatalism — planning is affirmed. It is the recognition that all human planning operates within a larger divine ordering. The chapter's first verse makes the same point: humans plan the words they will say; the right response comes from the LORD.

Context

Chapter 16 has been called the theological centre of the Solomonic collection. Its opening nine verses are almost entirely theocentric — every verse mentions the LORD or a divine attribute. The chapter contains the collection's two most famous proverbs: v.18 (pride before a fall) and v.9 (humans plan, LORD directs). A cluster of royal proverbs (vv.10–15) places the king as the earthly embodiment of divine justice. The chapter closes with the lot proverb (v.33): even the seemingly random is under divine direction.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:1–3

To humans belong the plans of the heart, but from the LORD comes the proper answer of the tongue. All a person's ways seem pure to them, but motives are weighed by the LORD. Commit to the LORD whatever you do, and he will establish your plans. Three consecutive theocentric proverbs open the chapter's most concentrated theological section. Human planning is real and affirmed — but it operates within divine governance. The LORD weighs motives, not merely actions. Committing plans to the LORD is not passivity but the recognition that all human projects are dependent on divine sustaining.

16:9

In their hearts humans plan their course, but the LORD determines their steps. The great sovereignty proverb. Humans plan (ḥāšab — devise, calculate); the LORD directs (kûn — establishes, makes firm). This is neither fatalism (don't plan) nor humanism (your plan is decisive) — it holds both in tension. Human agency and divine sovereignty operate simultaneously.

16:18

Pride goes before destruction, a haughty spirit before a fall. The most quoted verse in Proverbs. Pride (gaʾăwāh) is not self-esteem or healthy confidence — it is the assignment to oneself of significance, security, and sufficiency that belongs only to God. The collapse is structural, not merely punitive: the proud person is building on a foundation that cannot hold. The fall is inevitable.

16:24

Gracious words are a honeycomb, sweet to the soul and healing to the bones. The medical imagery recurs: wise speech is not merely pleasant but therapeutic. The word "healing" (marpe — from the same root as Raphael) describes a medicinal effect. Words that are gracious (ḥen — attractive, fitting) produce both psychological satisfaction (sweet to the soul) and physical restoration (healing to the bones).

16:33

The lot is cast into the lap, but its every decision is from the LORD. The final sovereignty proverb: even apparent randomness is under divine direction. This brackets the chapter with v.1 — from the most deliberate human planning to the most random outcome, all is within God's governance. The chapter that begins with human plans ends with divine determination of the random.

Translation Comparison

16:18 — Pride Before a Fall

VersionTranslation
NIV"Pride goes before destruction, a haughty spirit before a fall."
NoteThe English idiom "pride goes before a fall" is a compression of this verse. The original is more precise: pride precedes shattering; a haughty spirit precedes stumbling. Both metaphors are physical — structural collapse and tripping.

Ancient Sources

Royal Proverbs
Vv.10–15 form a cluster of royal proverbs — unusual in the collection. They present an idealized picture of kingship: the king's lips as oracle, his face as life, his throne as established by righteousness. This is prescriptive as much as descriptive: what a king should be, not necessarily what any specific king is.
The royal proverbs cluster appears to draw on court wisdom tradition.

Reception History

Luke 14:11; 18:14
Jesus' repeated "everyone who exalts himself will be humbled" is the NT form of v.18's principle, applied eschatologically.
Jas 4:13–14
James's warning against saying "today or tomorrow we will go to this city" echoes the v.9 principle: humans plan; the LORD directs.
