Proverbs 21

The LORD Weighs the King's Heart; Justice over Sacrifice; Victory Belongs to the LORD

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–15 — The LORD Weighs the King's Heart; Justice Over Sacrifice
1In the LORD's hand the king's heart is a stream of water that he channels toward all who please him.2A person may think their own ways are right, but the LORD weighs the heart.3To do what is right and just is more acceptable to the LORD than sacrifice.4Haughty eyes and a proud heart— the unplowed field of the wicked— produce sin.5The plans of the diligent lead to profit as surely as haste leads to poverty.6A fortune made by a lying tongue is a fleeting vapor and a deadly snare.7The violence of the wicked will drag them away, for they refuse to do what is right.8The way of the guilty is devious, but the conduct of the innocent is upright.9Better to live on a corner of the roof than share a house with a quarrelsome wife.10The wicked crave evil; their neighbors get no mercy from them.11When a mocker is punished, the simple gain wisdom; by paying attention to the wise they get knowledge.12The Righteous One takes note of the house of the wicked and brings the wicked to ruin.13Whoever shuts their ears to the cry of the poor will also cry out and not be answered.14A gift given in secret soothes anger, and a bribe concealed in the cloak pacifies great wrath.15When justice is done, it brings joy to the righteous but terror to evildoers.

Hebrew Word Study

חֵקֶר cheqer — weighs / plumbs: V.2: the LORD weighs (pōlēs) hearts. The same root as v.1's channels — the king's heart is in the LORD's hand like irrigation water, directed where he will. The divine assessment of motivation reaches what human judgment and self-assessment cannot.

Context

The first half of ch.21 opens with the divine weighing of the king's heart (v.1) — establishing God's sovereignty over all earthly authority. V.3 is the chapter's moral centre: doing what is right and just is more acceptable to the LORD than sacrifice. The contrast between ritual compliance and genuine justice is the Proverbs echo of the great prophets (Isa 1:11–17; Amos 5:21–24).

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 1:11–17What to me is the multitude of your sacrifices? — the prophetic parallel to v.3. Justice is what the LORD requires; ritual cannot substitute for it.
  • Prov 16:9The LORD establishes steps — the same sovereignty theme as v.1's king-as-channel.

The king's heart is a stream of water in the LORD's hand. The most powerful human figure — the king — is still a river channel in God's irrigation system. Divine sovereignty is not in competition with human authority; it encompasses and directs it.

21:2

A person may think their own ways are right, but the LORD weighs the heart. The companion to 16:2. Subjective moral certainty is not the criterion; divine assessment is. The gap between self-perceived rightness and the LORD's weighing is the space where repentance is needed.

21:3

To do what is right and just is more acceptable to the LORD than sacrifice. The prophetic principle applied in wisdom's register: ritual without ethics is not acceptable. Sacrifice cannot substitute for justice.

Verses 16–31 — Diligence; Pride Leads to Conflict; Wisdom Overcomes Strength
16Whoever strays from the path of prudence comes to rest in the company of the dead.17Whoever loves pleasure will become poor; whoever loves wine and olive oil will never be rich.18The wicked become a ransom for the righteous, and the unfaithful for the upright.19Better to live in a desert than with a quarrelsome and nagging wife.20The wise store up choice food and olive oil, but fools gulp theirs down.21Whoever pursues righteousness and love finds life, prosperity and honor.22One who is wise can go up against the city of the mighty and pull down the stronghold in which they trust.23Those who guard their mouths and their tongues keep themselves from calamity.24The proud and arrogant person— "Mocker" is their name— behaves with insolent fury.25The craving of a sluggard will be the death of him, because his hands refuse to work.26All day long he craves for more, but the righteous give without sparing.27The sacrifice of the wicked is detestable— how much more so when brought with evil intent!28A false witness will perish, but a careful listener will testify successfully.29The wicked put up a bold front, but the upright give thought to their ways.30There is no wisdom, no insight, no plan that can succeed against the LORD.31The horse is made ready for the day of battle, but victory rests with the LORD.

Hebrew Word Study

שֶׁמֶן נְאֶמָן shemen neeman — precious oil / choice ointment: V.20: treasure and oil in the dwelling of the wise — stored resources of the prudent. The fool consumes everything immediately. The wise person maintains reserves. Prudence is foresight about future need.

Context

The second half addresses diligence versus laziness (vv.25–26), pride as the source of conflict (v.24), and the chapter's climactic verse: no wisdom, insight, or plan can succeed against the LORD (v.30). The divine sovereignty that opened the chapter (v.1) closes it (vv.30–31): human preparation matters, but victory belongs to the LORD.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 33:17A horse is a vain hope for deliverance — the exact parallel to v.31. The Psalmist and the sage both locate victory with the LORD, not with military preparation.
  • Prov 16:18Pride goes before destruction — the same pride-conflict connection as v.24.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
21:24

The proud and arrogant person — "Mocker" is their name; they behave with insolent fury. Arrogance names itself through its behaviour. The mocker is not merely wrong; they are contemptuous of correction, which makes them unreachable by wisdom.

21:30

There is no wisdom, no insight, no plan that can succeed against the LORD. The chapter's final statement of divine sovereignty. Human wisdom, human intelligence, and human strategy are all real goods — but none of them can prevail if they are directed against the LORD's purpose.

21:31

The horse is made ready for the day of battle, but victory rests with the LORD. The most practical application of the sovereignty theology in ch.21. Preparation is obligatory; outcomes are not in human hands. The wise person prepares fully and trusts completely.

21:17

Alter notes these proverbs establish that poverty proceeds from character (the love of immediate pleasure) before circumstance.

21:31

Alter notes the military context makes the sovereignty principle more striking than in everyday planning. Battle is where human power is most concentrated — and even there, the outcome belongs to the LORD.

21:17

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs.
The editorial note is one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.

Reception History

Wisdom and virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics.
Practical application
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, economics, and social teaching.

Literary Structure

Prepare thoroughly; trust completely — the sovereignty principle at its most extreme application. Human preparation is legitimate and necessary; divine sovereignty determines the outcome.
Ethics over Ritual — 21:3To do right and just is more acceptable than sacrifice — the substance over the form, the character over the ceremony.

Textual Notes

MT: asot tsedaqah umishpat nivchar laYHWH mizevach. LXX: poiein dikaiosyna kai alētheian aresta theō mallon ē thysian.
Ethics over cult is a consistent OT witness. Righteousness is the substance sacrifice expresses; without the substance, the ritual is hollow.

Scholarly Debates

Divine Sovereignty and Evil Rulers
Sovereignty is comprehensive
Calvin, Reformed tradition
Pharaoh, Nebuchadnezzar, Cyrus — God directs even pagan rulers. Royal moral responsibility coexists with comprehensive divine governance.
God permits but does not directly cause evil
Arminian readings
Sovereignty operates through permission and governance of consequences rather than direct causation.
The Reformed reading is most consistent with the breadth of OT teaching on divine sovereignty over political power.

Theological Themes Tracker

Proverbs 21 applies divine sovereignty to every dimension of human endeavour. Its most theologically surprising claim: doing right and just is more acceptable to the LORD than sacrifice.
