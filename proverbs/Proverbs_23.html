Author

Solomon ben David, c.990–931 BC. Israel's third king, son of David and Bathsheba. Credited by Scripture with 3,000 proverbs and 1,005 songs (1 Kgs 4:32). His wisdom was divinely given — not merely accumulated experience but a gift granted at Gibeon (1 Kgs 3:5–14). He built the Temple, forged commercial alliances across the ancient world, and attracted foreign dignitaries to Jerusalem including the Queen of Sheba (1 Kgs 10). Proverbs 1–9 are the most clearly Solomonic; chs. 25–29 were copied by the men of Hezekiah (25:1); chs. 30–31 are attributed to Agur and Lemuel respectively.

When written

The core Solomonic material (chs. 1–9, 10–22:16) dates to Solomon's reign, c.970–931 BC. The final compilation and editing likely continued through the Hezekiah period (c.715–686 BC), when his scribes copied additional Solomonic proverbs (25:1). The book reached its present form sometime in the late monarchic or early post-exilic period.

What prompted it

Proverbs is a father's sustained address to a son standing on the threshold of adult life. The driving question is: How does a young person navigate the real world — its temptations, its social pressures, its competing voices — without being destroyed? The book's answer is not a list of rules but a vision of reality: the world was made with Wisdom woven into it (ch. 8), and the person who aligns their life with that grain will flourish; the person who fights it will be broken by it. "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7) is the thesis of the entire book.

Context

Chapter 23 contains the most vivid description of drunkenness in Scripture (vv.29–35) — a sustained dramatic monologue ending with the drunk asking for another drink even after being beaten. The chapter moves through table manners with rulers (vv.1–3), the elusiveness of wealth (vv.4–5), the stingy host (vv.6–8), discipline (vv.13–14), and a long passage on envy and the fear of the LORD (vv.17–18). The wine passage (vv.29–35) is extraordinary in its realism — it describes addiction with clinical precision before the word existed.

Cross-Reference

  • Eph 5:18Paul's "do not get drunk on wine, which leads to debauchery" is the NT application of the ch.23 wine warning.
  • 23:18The "future hope" that will not be cut off is one of wisdom's most eschatological moments — the assurance that the person who fears the LORD has a future beyond present circumstances.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
23:4–5

Do not wear yourself out to get rich; do not trust your own cleverness. Cast but a glance at riches, and they are gone, for they will surely sprout wings and fly off to the sky like an eagle. Proverbs is not against wealth but against the obsessive pursuit of it. The "cleverness" (bînāh) being warned against is the use of one's best cognitive resources in the service of accumulation. The image of winged money (riches that fly away) is one of the ancient world's most vivid pictures of wealth's elusiveness.

23:7

For he is the kind of person who is always thinking about the cost. The diagnostic of the stingy host: his words say "eat and drink" but his heart is not with you. The ancient understanding of hospitality was that it required full attention and generosity of spirit, not merely provision of food. The host who calculates throughout the meal has violated the essence of hospitality even while performing its form.

23:17–18

Do not let your heart envy sinners, but always be zealous for the fear of the LORD. There is surely a future hope for you, and your hope will not be cut off. The temptation to envy the wicked is addressed with the most eschatological assurance in the early chapters of the collection: there is a future (ʾaḥărît — end, outcome, destiny) for the person who fears the LORD, and that future is secure. The wicked's present prosperity is not the final word.

23:23

Buy the truth and do not sell it — wisdom, instruction and insight as well. Truth is a commodity — but with an asymmetric market rule: buy always; never sell. The instruction to buy (qānāh — the same verb as "get wisdom" in 4:5) treats truth as worth every resource; the prohibition against selling treats it as beyond price. What wisdom is worth everything to acquire is worth even more to keep.

23:29–35

Who has woe? Who has sorrow? Who has strife? — the anatomy of addiction. This is one of the earliest clinical descriptions of alcohol dependency in world literature. The seven diagnostic questions of v.29 precede a description of intoxication that maps to modern criteria: visual disturbances, confusion, loss of spatial orientation. The ending is the most devastating: "When will I wake up so I can find another drink?" The addicted person does not ask to stop; he asks to start again.

Ancient Sources

Portrait of Addiction
Vv.29–35 are one of the earliest clinical descriptions of alcohol dependency in world literature. The characteristics described — tolerance (needs more), withdrawal (waking and wanting more), impaired judgment, physical damage without sensation — map to modern diagnostic criteria for alcohol use disorder.
Proverbs 23 predates modern addiction medicine by 3,000 years.
