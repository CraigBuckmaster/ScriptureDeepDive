Proverbs 23

Thirty Sayings: Wealth, Parents, Wine; Wisdom's Future Hope

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–18 — Thirty Sayings: Don't Crave; Move Not the Boundary; Discipline Your Heart
1When you sit to dine with a ruler, note well what is before you,2and put a knife to your throat if you are given to gluttony.3Do not crave his delicacies, for that food is deceptive.4Do not wear yourself out to get rich; do not trust your own cleverness.5Cast but a glance at riches, and they are gone, for they will surely sprout wings and fly off to the sky like an eagle.6Do not eat the food of a begrudging host, do not crave his delicacies;7for he is the kind of person who is always thinking about the cost. "Eat and drink," he says to you, but his heart is not with you.8You will vomit up the little you have eaten and will have wasted your compliments.9Do not speak to fools, for they will scorn your prudent words.10Do not move an ancient boundary stone or encroach on the fields of the fatherless,11for their Defender is strong; he will take up their case against you.12Apply your heart to instruction and your ears to words of knowledge.13Do not withhold discipline from a child; if you punish them with the rod, they will not die.14Punish them with the rod and save them from death.15My son, if your heart is wise, then my heart will be glad indeed;16my inmost being will rejoice when your lips speak what is right.17Do not let your heart envy sinners, but always be zealous for the fear of the LORD.18There is surely a future hope for you, and your hope will not be cut off.

Hebrew Word Study

אַל תִּתְאָו al titav — do not crave / covet: V.3: do not crave the ruler's delicacies — kitannûtemāʿammîm, food of deceptions. The warning addresses the temptation to curry favour with the powerful through sharing their table. The sycophant who craves the ruler's food becomes dependent and compromised.

Context

The first half of ch.23 continues the Thirty Sayings with warnings against craving the ruler's food (v.3), exploiting the poor (v.10), and neglecting discipline (vv.13-14). The boundary-stone warning (v.10) is among the most pointed social ethics in the collection.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 19:14Do not move your neighbour's boundary stone — the legal basis for v.10's boundary-stone warning.
  • Prov 22:28Do not move an ancient boundary stone — the direct parallel, establishing the principle as a repeated warning.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
23:3

Do not crave his delicacies, for that food is deceptive. The ruler's table is a trap dressed as an invitation. The food that seems like favour is actually a mechanism of control. The wise person maintains independence from the powerful by not craving their gifts.

23:10-11

Do not move an ancient boundary stone or encroach on the fields of the fatherless, for their Defender is strong. The fatherless have no human protector — but the LORD is their gōʾēl (kinsman-redeemer, defender), and he is strong. To encroach on their land is to challenge their Divine Advocate.

23:13-14

Do not withhold discipline from a child. The rod-and-discipline passage echoes 13:24. The parent who withholds correction out of tenderness is sacrificing the child's future for present comfort.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:5

The eagle-wings image — Alter notes the personification of riches as a bird that chooses to leave captures the experience of sudden financial loss more vividly than any abstract statement about impermanence.

23:23

Buy truth, wisdom, instruction, understanding, and do not sell them — Alter notes placing wisdom in the economic register of irreplaceable assets establishes it as the currency against which all other exchanges are measured.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:4-5

Do not wear yourself out to get rich — Waltke notes the eagle-wings image for vanishing wealth is one of Proverbs' most vivid. The counsel is against the exhausting pursuit of wealth as the supreme good, not against diligence.

23:10-11

Do not move the ancient boundary stone — the fatherless have a Defender (go'el) who is strong: the LORD himself. Their boundary is protected by the most powerful possible advocate.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:4

Calvin: 'Do not wear yourself out to get rich — the exhausted pursuit of wealth is both spiritually and physically destructive. It is the idolatry of mammon.'

23:10

Calvin: 'The fatherless have no human defender; their defender is God himself. Those who encroach on the fatherless make the LORD their adversary.'

Verses 19–35 — Listen to Parents; Wisdom's Future Hope; The Warning Against Wine
19Listen, my son, and be wise, and set your heart on the right path:20Do not join those who drink too much wine or gorge themselves on meat,21for drunkards and gluttons become poor, and drowsiness clothes them in rags.22Listen to your father, who gave you life, and do not despise your mother when she is old.23Buy the truth and do not sell it— wisdom, instruction and insight as well.24The father of a righteous child has great joy; a man who fathers a wise son rejoices in him.25May your father and mother rejoice; may she who gave you birth be joyful!26My son, give me your heart and let your eyes delight in my ways,27for an adulterous woman is a deep pit, and a wayward wife is a narrow well.28Like a bandit she lies in wait and multiplies the unfaithful among men.29Who has woe? Who has sorrow? Who has strife? Who has complaints? Who has needless bruises? Who has bloodshot eyes?30Those who linger over wine, who go to sample bowls of mixed wine.31Do not gaze at wine when it is red, when it sparkles in the cup, when it goes down smoothly!32In the end it bites like a snake and poisons like a viper.33Your eyes will see strange sights, and your mind will imagine confusing things.34You will be like one sleeping on the high seas, lying on top of the rigging.35"They hit me," you will say, "but I'm not hurt! They beat me, but I don't feel it! When will I wake up so I can find another drink?"

Hebrew Word Study

תִּקְוָה tiqvah — hope / future: V.18: for surely there is a future hope for you, and your hope will not be cut off. Tiqwâ — cord, hope, expectation. Among the most encouraging verses in the Thirty Sayings: wisdom's discipline (vv.13-14) has a guaranteed future. The path of wisdom is not closed; it opens toward a secure hope.

Context

The second half addresses the parental heart's joy when children are wise (vv.19-25), the warning against the adulterous woman (vv.26-28), and the chapter's most extended passage: the warning against wine (vv.29-35). The wine passage is the most vivid description of addiction in the OT.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 31:4-7It is not for kings to drink wine — the royal wine-warning that mirrors ch.23's extended treatment.
  • Eph 5:18Do not get drunk on wine, which leads to debauchery — the NT application of the wine-warning principle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
23:18

Surely there is a future hope for you, and your hope will not be cut off. The most encouraging verse in ch.23. All the discipline, all the restraint, all the refusal of easy pleasures — it is not pointless. There is a future secured by wisdom's path.

23:29-30

Who has woe? Who has sorrow? — those who linger over wine. The interrogative opening of the most vivid addiction passage in the OT. The description of wine's seduction and consequences is psychologically precise.

23:34-35

You will be like one sleeping on the high seas, lying on top of the rigging. "They hit me but I'm not hurt!" The final portrait of the drunk is one of dangerous insensibility: unable to feel injury, eager to repeat the cycle.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:23

The buy-wisdom instruction — Alter notes the sequence (truth, wisdom, instruction, understanding) moves from the most general (truth) to the most comprehensive (understanding), covering the full range of what wisdom encompasses.

23:35

The drunkard who wakes seeking another drink — Alter notes this is one of the Bible's most precise descriptions of the addiction cycle. The extended wine poem is unique in the proverb collections for its sustained dramatic development.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:22-25

The parental-honour cluster — Waltke notes the verb qanah (buy) applied to truth, wisdom, instruction, and understanding places wisdom in the economic register: it is worth acquiring at cost. The parent who has acquired wisdom has the authority to transmit it.

23:29-35

The wine poem is Proverbs' most vivid anti-alcohol passage. The cycle: misery and redness of eyes (v.29) → appeal of wine's colour (v.31) → disorienting effects (vv.33–34) → waking and seeking another drink (v.35). Addiction precisely described.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:23

Calvin: 'Buy truth and do not sell it — once you have genuine wisdom, it must never be traded away for temporal advantage. It is the asset that all other assets depend on.'

23:35

Calvin: 'The drunkard seeking another drink upon waking — the pleasure is brief, the pain enduring, and the first desire upon recovery is to return to the source of the pain. Solomon presents this with the irony that makes it recognisable.'

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions, especially Egyptian.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs — one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.
The editorial activity shows Proverbs as a living, curated collection.

Reception History

Virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics — the practical outworking of the fear of the LORD.
Practical wisdom
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, governance, and social teaching.

Literary Structure

Wisdom, truth, instruction, understanding — acquired at cost; never traded away. Once genuine wisdom is possessed, it becomes the currency against which all other exchanges are measured.
The Addiction Cycle Described — 23:29-35The wine poem describes the full cycle from temptation to disorientation to the waking drunkard seeking another drink. One of the Bible's most precise descriptions of addiction.

Textual Notes

23:7'As a man thinks in his heart, so he is'
MT: ki kemo sha'ar benarmo ken hu. Context: the stingy man's hospitality. The general reading (inner life shapes outer reality) is often quoted outside its context.
The immediate context is the stingy host who outwardly offers food but inwardly resents every morsel. The general psychological principle is true; the specific application is a warning about false hospitality.

Scholarly Debates

Proverbs 23:13-14 and Corporal Punishment
Physical discipline endorsed within appropriate limits
MacArthur, conservative evangelicals
The rod-passages form a consistent pattern. Applied wisely and in love, corporal discipline is a legitimate tool.
The principle universal; the method contextualised
Most contemporary evangelical scholars
The principle (firm discipline is an act of love) is non-negotiable; the specific method requires contextualisation to contemporary understanding.
The 'discipline in love' principle is universal. Specific application must be evaluated in light of contemporary child development understanding and the command not to exasperate children (Eph 6:4).

Theological Themes Tracker

Proverbs 23 develops the Thirty Sayings through its most extended passages: the vanishing-wealth eagle, the false hospitality of the stingy man, the buy-wisdom instruction, and the vivid wine poem.
