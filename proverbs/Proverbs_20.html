Author

Solomon ben David, c.990–931 BC. Israel's third king, son of David and Bathsheba. Credited by Scripture with 3,000 proverbs and 1,005 songs (1 Kgs 4:32). His wisdom was divinely given — not merely accumulated experience but a gift granted at Gibeon (1 Kgs 3:5–14). He built the Temple, forged commercial alliances across the ancient world, and attracted foreign dignitaries to Jerusalem including the Queen of Sheba (1 Kgs 10). Proverbs 1–9 are the most clearly Solomonic; chs. 25–29 were copied by the men of Hezekiah (25:1); chs. 30–31 are attributed to Agur and Lemuel respectively.

When written

The core Solomonic material (chs. 1–9, 10–22:16) dates to Solomon's reign, c.970–931 BC. The final compilation and editing likely continued through the Hezekiah period (c.715–686 BC), when his scribes copied additional Solomonic proverbs (25:1). The book reached its present form sometime in the late monarchic or early post-exilic period.

What prompted it

Proverbs is a father's sustained address to a son standing on the threshold of adult life. The driving question is: How does a young person navigate the real world — its temptations, its social pressures, its competing voices — without being destroyed? The book's answer is not a list of rules but a vision of reality: the world was made with Wisdom woven into it (ch. 8), and the person who aligns their life with that grain will flourish; the person who fights it will be broken by it. "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7) is the thesis of the entire book.

Hebrew Word Study

נֵר יְהוָה נִשְׁמַת אָדָם nēr yhwh nišmat ʾādām — the spirit of a person is the lamp of the LORD: V.27: one of the most profound anthropological statements in Proverbs. Nēr (lamp) yhwh (of the LORD) — the human spirit (nišmāh — breath, the animating principle given by God in Gen 2:7) is a lamp that God himself uses to search the inmost being (ḥadrê bāṭen — chambers of the belly). God searches human interiority using the God-given spirit as his instrument.

מַיִם עֲמֻקִּים עֵצָה בְלֶב אִישׁ mayim ʿămūqqîm ʿēṣāh bĕlēb ʾîš — the purposes of a heart are deep waters: V.5: the deepest motivations of a person are like deep wells — not visible on the surface, not easily accessible. The person with insight can draw them out (dālāh — to lift water from a well). Wisdom involves the skill of understanding what actually drives people, including oneself.

Context

Chapter 20 contains several of the collection's most important observations about human nature. V.5's deep waters of the heart and v.27's lamp of the LORD both probe inner motivation — one noting the hiddenness of the heart, the other God's penetrating search of it. V.9's rhetorical question ("who can say, I have kept my heart pure?") is one of the collection's clearest acknowledgements of universal human fallibility. The chapter also touches on the theme of waiting on the LORD for vindication (v.22) rather than personal retaliation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
20:5

The purposes of a person's heart are deep waters, but one who has insight draws them out. The deepest motivations of a person are like deep wells — not visible on the surface. The person with insight (bînāh — discernment) can draw them out (dālāh — the physical act of drawing water from a well with a bucket). Wisdom includes the skill of understanding what actually drives people, including oneself. This is the OT's most explicit statement about the complexity of human motivation.

20:9

Who can say, "I have kept my heart pure; I am clean and without sin"? The rhetorical answer is: no one. This is one of Proverbs' most explicit acknowledgements of universal human fallibility. Wisdom is not achieved through moral self-sufficiency. The fear of the LORD is necessary precisely because no one is self-sufficient in righteousness. The NT's "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God" (Rom 3:23) is the doctrinal articulation of what Proverbs acknowledges rhetorically.

20:22

Do not say, "I'll pay you back for this wrong!" Wait for the LORD, and he will avenge you. One of Proverbs' most direct ethical instructions on non-retaliation. The reason is not strategic but theological: God is the righteous avenger, and taking personal vengeance usurps his role. Paul develops this in Romans 12:19 — "Do not take revenge, my dear friends, but leave room for God's wrath" — applying Proverbs' principle directly to Christian community life.

20:24

A person's steps are directed by the LORD. How then can anyone understand their own way? The companion to 16:9 and 19:21. The question is rhetorical: no one fully understands their own path, because it is ultimately directed by One who sees further than any human perspective can reach. This is not paralyzing (plan and act) but humbling (hold your plans loosely).

20:27

The human spirit is the lamp of the LORD, searching out the inmost being. One of the most profound anthropological statements in Proverbs. The God-given spirit (nišmāh — the breath of life from Gen 2:7) is the instrument God uses to search the innermost being. Conscience, in the biblical framework, is not merely a social construct — it is the divine lamp within. This is why Proverbs consistently calls for inward attention: the spirit within is the LORD's own searchlight.

Ancient Sources

Universal Fallibility
V.9's rhetorical question about moral purity ("who can say?") is part of a larger OT tradition: Ps 14:1–3 ("there is no one who does good"), Job's repeated protests of innocence tested by God, Eccl 7:20 ("there is not a righteous person on earth who does what is right and never sins"). Proverbs' wisdom is not naive moralism; it acknowledges universal fallibility.
V.9 connects Proverbs to the wider OT's realistic anthropology.
