Proverbs 31

A Mother's Instruction; The Excellent Wife: Wisdom Embodied

Author

Solomon ben David, c.990–931 BC. Israel's third king, son of David and Bathsheba. Credited by Scripture with 3,000 proverbs and 1,005 songs (1 Kgs 4:32). His wisdom was divinely given — not merely accumulated experience but a gift granted at Gibeon (1 Kgs 3:5–14). He built the Temple, forged commercial alliances across the ancient world, and attracted foreign dignitaries to Jerusalem including the Queen of Sheba (1 Kgs 10). Proverbs 1–9 are the most clearly Solomonic; chs. 25–29 were copied by the men of Hezekiah (25:1); chs. 30–31 are attributed to Agur and Lemuel respectively.

When written

The core Solomonic material (chs. 1–9, 10–22:16) dates to Solomon's reign, c.970–931 BC. The final compilation and editing likely continued through the Hezekiah period (c.715–686 BC), when his scribes copied additional Solomonic proverbs (25:1). The book reached its present form sometime in the late monarchic or early post-exilic period.

What prompted it

Proverbs is a father's sustained address to a son standing on the threshold of adult life. The driving question is: How does a young person navigate the real world — its temptations, its social pressures, its competing voices — without being destroyed? The book's answer is not a list of rules but a vision of reality: the world was made with Wisdom woven into it (ch. 8), and the person who aligns their life with that grain will flourish; the person who fights it will be broken by it. "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7) is the thesis of the entire book.

Verses 1–9 — A Mother's Instruction to a King: Speak Up
1The sayings of King Lemuel— an oracle his mother taught him:2"What, my son? What, son of my womb? What, son of my vows?3Do not spend your strength on women, your vigor on those who ruin kings.4It is not for kings, Lemuel— it is not for kings to drink wine, not for rulers to crave beer,5lest they drink and forget what has been decreed, and deprive all the oppressed of their rights.6Let beer be for those who are perishing, wine for those who are in anguish!7Let them drink and forget their poverty and remember their misery no more.8Speak up for those who cannot speak for themselves, for the rights of all who are destitute.9Speak up and judge fairly; defend the rights of the poor and needy."

Context

Proverbs 31 opens with the sayings of King Lemuel — another non-Israelite wisdom figure, like Agur. Uniquely, this instruction was taught to him by his mother. The instruction for a king is brief and pointed: avoid women who ruin kings, avoid wine that clouds royal judgment, and speak up for the poor and destitute. V.8–9 are among the most activist verses in Proverbs: "Speak up for those who cannot speak for themselves." The book that began with a father's instruction to a son ends with a mother's instruction to a king and a poem about a woman.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:8–9

Speak up for those who cannot speak for themselves, for the rights of all who are destitute. Speak up and judge fairly; defend the rights of the poor and needy. The climactic ethical obligation of the wisdom tradition. The person who has received wisdom — who understands how the world works, who has resources and access — is obligated to use that advantage on behalf of those who have none. This is advocacy, not mere charity: the active use of voice, position, and power on behalf of the voiceless.

31:10

A wife of noble character who can find? She is worth far more than rubies. The opening of the acrostic poem. The rhetorical question does not suggest impossibility but signals supreme value: this person is rare and precious. ʿĒšet ḥayil — woman of strength/excellence/ability — the same phrase as 12:4. The poem will expand "excellence" across every dimension of life: commercial, domestic, philanthropic, pedagogical.

31:20

She opens her arms to the poor and extends her hands to the needy. The generosity of the excellent wife is placed at the poem's centre. Her excellence is not merely domestic efficiency — it extends outward to the community. The Hebrew is physical and deliberate: she opens (pĕrĕsāh) and extends (šilĕḥāh) — active, purposeful, outward-reaching generosity. The excellent wife embodies the ethical obligations Proverbs has taught throughout the book.

31:26

She speaks with wisdom, and faithful instruction is on her tongue. The pedagogical dimension: the excellent wife is a teacher. Tôrat ḥesed — the law/instruction of steadfast love — is on her tongue. This is not merely wise advice but covenant instruction: the teaching that flows from committed, loyal relationship. She is the embodiment of the father's instruction to the son throughout chs.1–9, now enacted in the household by a woman.

31:30

Charm is deceptive, and beauty is fleeting; but a woman who fears the LORD is to be praised. The poem's climax and the book's final theological anchor. The two things that most typically define a woman's value in ancient culture — charm (ḥēn — grace, attractiveness) and beauty (yōpî) — are explicitly dismissed as deceptive and temporary. The lasting foundation is yirʾat yhwh — the fear of the LORD. The book that began with "the fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7) ends with "a woman who fears the LORD is to be praised." The thesis and its embodiment bracket the entire collection.

Verses 10–31 — The Excellent Wife: The Book's Final Answer
10A wife of noble character who can find? She is worth far more than rubies.11Her husband has full confidence in her and lacks nothing of value.12She brings him good, not harm, all the days of her life.13She selects wool and flax and works with eager hands.14She is like the merchant ships, bringing her food from afar.15She gets up while it is still night; she provides food for her family and portions for her female servants.16She considers a field and buys it; out of her earnings she plants a vineyard.17She sets about her work vigorously; her arms are strong for her tasks.18She sees that her trading is profitable, and her lamp does not go out at night.19In her hand she holds the distaff and grasps the spindle with her fingers.20She opens her arms to the poor and extends her hands to the needy.21When it snows, she has no fear for her household; for all of them are clothed in scarlet.22She makes coverings for her bed; she is clothed in fine linen and purple.23Her husband is respected at the city gate, where he takes his seat among the elders of the land.24She makes linen garments and sells them, and supplies the merchants with sashes.25She is clothed with strength and dignity; she can laugh at the days to come.26She speaks with wisdom, and faithful instruction is on her tongue.27She watches over the affairs of her household and does not eat the bread of idleness.28Her children arise and call her blessed; her husband also, and he praises her:29"Many women do noble things, but you surpass them all."30Charm is deceptive, and beauty is fleeting; but a woman who fears the LORD is to be praised.31Honor her for all that her hands have done, and let her works bring her praise at the city gate.

Hebrew Word Study

אֵשֶׁת חַיִל ʾēšet ḥayil — a wife of noble character/excellence: The same phrase as 12:4. Ḥayil is strength, ability, excellence — the full range of human capability deployed in covenant love. This is not a domestic ideal but a portrait of comprehensive excellence: she is a merchant, a real estate investor, a textile manufacturer, a philanthropist, a teacher, and a manager — all from a foundation of fear of the LORD (v.30).

יִרְאַת יְהוָה yirʾat yhwh — fear of the LORD: V.30: the poem's climax and the book's final theological anchor. The excellent woman's excellence is not produced by her capabilities; it is produced by her relationship with God. The fear of the LORD (1:7; 9:10) which opened the book closes it. The entire collection is bracketed by this single principle.

Context

The poem of the excellent wife (vv.10–31) is an acrostic — each verse begins with the next letter of the Hebrew alphabet, from aleph to taw. This is not merely a literary flourish; it signals completeness. The A-to-Z of human excellence is embodied in this woman. She is the answer to the question with which the book wrestled: what does wisdom look like when fully embodied in a human life? The answer is not a philosopher or a king but a woman whose fear of the LORD produces comprehensive excellence in every dimension of her life.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:10–31

The acrostic poem is the book's answer to its own question. Chs.1–9 warned against Lady Folly and invited the young man to choose Lady Wisdom. Now, in the final chapter, wisdom is embodied in a specific human person. She is not an abstraction; she is a wife, a mother, a businesswoman, a philanthropist, a teacher. Her activity spans the household (vv.11–15, 21, 27), the market (vv.13–14, 18, 24), the land (v.16), and the community (v.20). She is comprehensive.

31:30

Charm is deceptive, and beauty is fleeting. The poem culminates by explicitly rejecting the adulteress's primary appeal (ch.7: beautiful appearance, smooth words) and affirming the fear of the LORD as the only enduring foundation of excellence. The book ends where it began: at the only place where lasting wisdom can be found.

Ancient Sources

Acrostic Poem
The Hebrew acrostic (each verse beginning with successive letters of the alphabet, aleph to taw) signals completeness. Hebrew acrostics appear in Ps 119 (the longest), Lamentations 1–4, and Pss 9–10, 25, 34, 37. In each case the form communicates totality — the full range of a subject.
The acrostic form makes a claim about completeness: this is wisdom embodied A to Z.
Lady Wisdom Embodied
Scholars have noted that the excellent wife (ʾēšet ḥayil) is the human embodiment of Lady Wisdom (chs.1–9). Both are praised; both are worth more than rubies; both build their houses; both are found by those who seek them. The poem deliberately completes the Lady Wisdom motif: the personification of wisdom in chs.1–9 finds its human expression in a real woman.
The ʾēšet ḥayil and Lady Wisdom are the two poles of Proverbs' female wisdom portrait.
