Author

Solomon ben David, c.990–931 BC. Israel's third king, son of David and Bathsheba. Credited by Scripture with 3,000 proverbs and 1,005 songs (1 Kgs 4:32). His wisdom was divinely given — not merely accumulated experience but a gift granted at Gibeon (1 Kgs 3:5–14). He built the Temple, forged commercial alliances across the ancient world, and attracted foreign dignitaries to Jerusalem including the Queen of Sheba (1 Kgs 10). Proverbs 1–9 are the most clearly Solomonic; chs. 25–29 were copied by the men of Hezekiah (25:1); chs. 30–31 are attributed to Agur and Lemuel respectively.

When written

The core Solomonic material (chs. 1–9, 10–22:16) dates to Solomon's reign, c.970–931 BC. The final compilation and editing likely continued through the Hezekiah period (c.715–686 BC), when his scribes copied additional Solomonic proverbs (25:1). The book reached its present form sometime in the late monarchic or early post-exilic period.

What prompted it

Proverbs is a father's sustained address to a son standing on the threshold of adult life. The driving question is: How does a young person navigate the real world — its temptations, its social pressures, its competing voices — without being destroyed? The book's answer is not a list of rules but a vision of reality: the world was made with Wisdom woven into it (ch. 8), and the person who aligns their life with that grain will flourish; the person who fights it will be broken by it. "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7) is the thesis of the entire book.

Hebrew Word Study

בַּרְזֶל בְּבַרְזֶל יָחַד barzel bĕbarzel yāḥad — iron sharpens iron: V.17: one of the most quoted friendship proverbs in the collection. Yāḥad (together, at the same time) — iron sharpens iron together. The image is of mutual friction that produces mutual sharpening. True friendship is not smooth or effortless; it involves the friction of honest engagement that makes both parties sharper, more effective.

כַּמַּיִם פָּנִים לַפָּנִים kammayim pānîm lappānîm — as water reflects face to face: V.19: one of the most psychologically precise proverbs. Water is a mirror — it reflects exactly what is presented to it. So the heart is the true mirror of the life: what you actually are is reflected in what you actually do and say. Character is not hidden; it is expressed.

Context

Chapter 27 contains three of Proverbs' most enduring single-verse observations: v.6 (wounds from a friend; kisses from an enemy), v.17 (iron sharpens iron), and v.19 (heart reflected in life). The chapter is notably concerned with the texture of sustained relationships — friendship that involves honest rebuke (vv.5–6), the nearness of a good neighbour (v.10), mutual sharpening (v.17). The chapter closes with the agricultural wisdom of vv.23–27 — attend to what you actually have, because wealth doesn't last.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:1

Do not boast about tomorrow, for you do not know what a day may bring. The epistemological foundation of humility about the future: genuine ignorance, not false modesty. Tomorrow is not merely uncertain — it is unknowable to any human. James develops this directly: "if it is the Lord's will, we will live and do this or that" (Jas 4:13–15). Planning is legitimate; boasting about what you will do is not.

27:5–6

Better is open rebuke than hidden love. Wounds from a friend can be trusted, but an enemy multiplies kisses. Love that never speaks hard truth is not love — it is comfort masquerading as care. V.6 sharpens this: wounds from a friend are trustworthy (neʾĕmān — reliable, faithful); an enemy's kisses are multiplied (yaʿtîr — excessive, profuse). The friend who hurts you in order to help you is more valuable than the flatterer who pleases you in order to manipulate you.

27:17

As iron sharpens iron, so one person sharpens another. Proverbs' clearest statement that wisdom requires community. You cannot sharpen yourself — you need the friction of honest engagement with another person. The image is not of conflict but of purposeful friction. The person who avoids all friction, all challenge, all disagreement, remains dull. Formation happens through relationship.

27:19

As water reflects the face, so one's life reflects the heart. Water is a mirror — it reflects exactly what is presented to it. The heart is the true mirror of the life: what you actually are is expressed in what you actually do and say. Character is not hidden — it is expressed. Deception about character is possible in the short term but unsustainable over time.

27:23–24

Be sure you know the condition of your flocks, give careful attention to your herds; for riches do not endure forever. The agricultural wisdom closing the chapter. Know what you have; give it careful attention; do not trust in the permanence of accumulated wealth. The flocks and herds require daily attention — they are a living responsibility, not a fixed asset. Wisdom involves the discipline of concrete, regular attention to what you are actually responsible for.

Ancient Sources

Iron Sharpens Iron
The metallurgical image of v.17 has become one of the most widely used metaphors for mentoring, discipleship, and accountability in Christian community. Its range of application — from small groups to spiritual direction — testifies to its precision.
V.17 has become one of the most applied single proverbs in Christian formation practice.
