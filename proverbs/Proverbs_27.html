Proverbs 27

Don't Boast of Tomorrow; Faithful Wounds; Iron Sharpens Iron; Know Your Flocks

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–12 — Don't Boast of Tomorrow; Faithful Wounds; Iron Sharpens Iron
1Do not boast about tomorrow, for you do not know what a day may bring.2Let someone else praise you, and not your own mouth; an outsider, and not your own lips.3Stone is heavy and sand a burden, but a fool's provocation is heavier than both.4Anger is cruel and fury overwhelming, but who can stand before jealousy?5Better is open rebuke than hidden love.6Wounds from a friend can be trusted, but an enemy multiplies kisses.7One who is full loathes honey from the comb, but to the hungry even what is bitter tastes sweet.8Like a bird that flees its nest is anyone who flees from home.9Perfume and incense bring joy to the heart, and the pleasantness of a friend springs from their heartfelt advice.10Do not forsake your friend or a friend of your family, and do not go to your relative's house when disaster strikes you— better a neighbor nearby than a relative far away.11Be wise, my son, and bring joy to my heart; then I can answer anyone who treats me with contempt.12The prudent see danger and take refuge, but the simple keep going and pay the penalty.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half of ch.27 contains three of Proverbs' most quoted relational sayings: the faithful wounds of a friend (v.6), iron sharpening iron (v.17), and the face-reflected-in-water metaphor for heart-knowledge through relationship (v.19).

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 17:17A friend loves at all times — the faithful-wounds proverb (v.6) specifies what that love sometimes requires: the wound that serves genuine good.
  • Prov 13:20Walk with the wise and become wise — the iron-sharpens-iron principle of v.17 is the mechanism behind this companionship proverb.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
27:1-12

MacArthur: Do not boast about tomorrow, for you do not know what a day may bring. The counsel against presumptuous planning is not laziness but epistemology: the future is genuinely unknown. Wisdom plans without claiming to control outcomes, and acts without claiming to guarantee results.

27:6

MacArthur: Wounds from a friend can be trusted, but an enemy multiplies kisses. The counterintuitive social wisdom: the painful truth from a proven friend is more trustworthy than the pleasant deception from a flatterer. The wounds are trustworthy because the friend's character is trustworthy; the kisses are suspect because the enemy's character is suspect.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:1

Do not boast of tomorrow -- Alter notes this acknowledges human temporal limitation. The person who boasts of tomorrow treats the future as under their control.

27:17

Iron sharpens iron; one person sharpens another -- Proverbs' most positive statement about social wisdom formation. Sharpness comes from friction with other sharp people.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:1

Do not boast about tomorrow -- epistemological humility about the future. Prohibition against presumption -- treating the future as already secured. Jas 4:13-15 applies directly.

27:6

Wounds from a friend can be trusted; an enemy multiplies kisses — Waltke notes the neemanin (trustworthy/reliable) quality of the friend's wounds is grounded in the friendship itself. The wound is trusted not because pain is good but because the one inflicting it has demonstrated consistent care for your flourishing. An enemy's kiss costs them nothing; a friend's rebuke costs them the relationship's comfort.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
27:5

Calvin: Open rebuke is better than hidden love — the love that cannot bring itself to correct is not love but self-protection. To protect someone from uncomfortable truth in order to avoid your own discomfort is to prioritise your peace over their growth. Genuine love accepts the relational cost of honest correction.

27:17

Calvin: Iron sharpens iron — God uses the friction of genuine relationship to form the character of both parties. The metaphor is mechanical but the reality is personal: you become sharper through the resistance of engagement with another sharp person. Isolation produces dullness; genuine community produces sharpness.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
27:1

The NET note on 'do not boast about tomorrow, for you do not know what a day may bring' connects to Jas 4:13-15 directly. The note traces the epistemological humility theme as consistent with Proverbs' overall recognition of human limits: we cannot fully know what a single day will bring, let alone plan for years ahead without covenantal trust.

27:6

The NET note on neemanin (trustworthy, reliable) characterises the friend's wounds as faithful even though they hurt. The same root gives amen (reliability, faithfulness). The note also discusses ataroth (many, excessive) for the enemy's kisses — the contrast is between painful reliability and pleasant unreliability. Wisdom chooses the former.

Verses 13–27 — A Nagging Spouse; Know Your Flocks; Heart Shaped by What It Sees
13Take the garment of one who puts up security for a stranger; hold it in pledge if it is done for an outsider.14If anyone loudly blesses their neighbor early in the morning, it will be taken as a curse.15A quarrelsome wife is like the dripping of a leaky roof in a rainstorm;16restraining her is like restraining the wind or grasping oil with the hand.17As iron sharpens iron, so one person sharpens another.18The one who tends a fig tree will eat its fruit, and whoever protects their master will be honored.19As water reflects the face, so one's life reflects the heart.20Death and Destruction are never satisfied, and neither are human eyes.21The crucible for silver and the furnace for gold, but people are tested by their praise.22Though you grind a fool in a mortar, grinding them like grain with a pestle, you will not remove their folly from them.23Be sure you know the condition of your flocks, give careful attention to your herds;24for riches do not endure forever, and a crown is not secure for all generations.25When the hay is removed and new growth appears and the grass from the hills is gathered in,26the lambs will provide you with clothing, and the goats with the price of a field.27You will have plenty of goats' milk to feed your family and to nourish your female servants.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half addresses household management — the prudent wife (v.15-16), knowing your flocks and herds (vv.23-27) — and the chapter's closing observation: riches do not endure forever, but tending what you have does. The chapter is the most relationally focused in Hezekiah's collection.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 31:10-31Know the condition of your flocks and herds (v.23) — the agricultural management wisdom that connects to the 'eshet ḥayil who manages the household economy.
  • Phil 4:11I have learned, in whatever state I am, to be content — the same contentment-through-diligent-management that vv.23-27 describe.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
27:13-27

MacArthur: Iron sharpens iron, and one person sharpens another (v.17). Wisdom is formed in community, not in isolation. The friction of genuine engagement — honest pushback, real challenge — sharpens both parties. Comfort without friction produces neither sharper iron nor sharper people.

27:23-27

MacArthur: The agricultural epilogue is wisdom applied to stewardship: know the condition of your flocks, give careful attention to your herds. The responsible management of what God has entrusted is itself a form of wisdom. The practical and the theological are not separate in Proverbs — managing well is honoring God.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:19

As water reflects the face, so the heart reflects the person — the water-mirror image is one of Proverbs' most elegant metaphors. Alter notes still water provides a perfect, unsparing reflection: no flattery, no editing. So a person's life provides a perfect, unsparing reflection of the heart that produced it. Character is not hidden; it is visible in the life.

27:23-27

Know your flocks — the agricultural wisdom grounds the chapter's abstract relational observations in concrete management of tangible things. Alter notes wisdom is always practical before it is philosophical: the person who attends to the abstract while neglecting the actual has inverted wisdom's priorities.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:19

As water reflects the face, so one's life reflects the heart — Waltke notes the water-mirror image is Proverbs' most concentrated statement of the heart-life correspondence. The lev (heart) shapes the life; the life reveals the heart. No performance of character can be sustained indefinitely — eventually the heart's actual condition becomes visible in the life's pattern.

27:23-27

Know well the condition of your flocks — Waltke notes the agricultural coda grounds wisdom in the most basic economic reality: the wealth that sustains life requires attentive, ongoing stewardship. The person who knows the state of their flocks (what they actually have, what it needs) will sustain what they have; the person who does not will lose it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
27:19

Calvin: As water reflects the face, so the human heart reflects what the person truly is — character is not hidden. The life you live is the mirror of the heart you have. No pretence is ultimately sustainable: eventually the heart's orientation becomes visible in the pattern of choices, reactions, and relationships that constitute a life.

27:23

Calvin: Know the condition of your flocks — attend to the concrete responsibilities God has given. The person who attends to the abstract while neglecting the actual has inverted wisdom's priorities. Genuine responsibility begins with knowing what you actually have and what it actually needs.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
27:17

The NET note on iron sharpens iron explains the mechanical metaphor for relational formation: the friction of genuine engagement with another person sharpens both parties. The note discusses how this applies to the wisdom tradition specifically: the student who engages a challenging teacher becomes sharper through the challenge; so does the teacher through the student's questions.

27:23

The NET note on 'know the condition of your flocks' grounds wisdom in concrete stewardship of what one actually has. The note traces the stewardship theme as consistent with Proverbs' practical orientation: wisdom manages what is actually in hand rather than speculating about what might be obtained.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions, especially Egyptian.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs — one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.
The editorial activity shows Proverbs as a living, curated collection.

Reception History

Virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics — the practical outworking of the fear of the LORD.
Practical wisdom
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, governance, and social teaching.

Literary Structure

Scholarly Debates

Faithful Wounds: What Makes Rebuke Loving?
Love requires honesty even when it hurts
Waltke, most evangelical scholars
The wound from a friend is trustworthy because it serves the friend's actual flourishing rather than the comfort of both parties.
Rebuke can be weaponised; discernment required
Pastorally cautious readers
Not all rebuke is loving. The qualification is the rebuker's character and proven friendship.
The trustworthiness of the friend's wound is grounded in their proven character. The alternative to faithful rebuke is not comfortable peace but corrupting flattery.
