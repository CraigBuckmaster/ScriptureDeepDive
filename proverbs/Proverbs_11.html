Proverbs 11

Honest Scales; Pride and Humility; Community and Generosity

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–15 — Honest Scales; Pride and Humility; Community Integrity
1Dishonest scales are an abomination to the LORD, but accurate weights find favor with him.2When pride comes, then comes disgrace, but with humility comes wisdom.3The integrity of the upright guides them, but the unfaithful are destroyed by their duplicity.4Wealth is worthless in the day of wrath, but righteousness delivers from death.5The righteousness of the blameless makes their paths straight, but the wicked are brought down by their own wickedness.6The righteousness of the upright delivers them, but the unfaithful are trapped by evil desires.7Hopes placed in mortals die with them; all the promise of their power comes to nothing.8The righteous person is rescued from trouble, and it falls on the wicked instead.9With their mouths the godless destroy their neighbors, but through knowledge the righteous escape.10When the righteous prosper, the city rejoices; when the wicked perish, there are shouts of joy.11Through the blessing of the upright a city is exalted, but by the mouth of the wicked it is destroyed.12Whoever derides their neighbor has no sense, but the one who has understanding holds their tongue.13A gossip betrays a confidence, but a trustworthy person keeps a secret.14For lack of guidance a nation falls, but victory is won through many advisers.15Whoever puts up security for a stranger will surely suffer, but whoever refuses to shake hands in pledge is safe.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

Proverbs 11 opens with the marketplace (v.1) and the central contrast: upright versus wicked. The city's prosperity or destruction depends on the moral quality of its residents (vv.10-11). Wisdom is not private virtue but a public good.

Cross-Reference

  • Lev 19:35-36Do not use dishonest standards -- the legal basis for v.1.
  • Prov 16:18Pride goes before destruction -- the same pride-disgrace sequence as v.2.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
11:1-7

MacArthur: The honest scale versus the dishonest weight is the chapter's opening image for a reason: the integrity of measurement is the foundation of all social trust. A society whose scales are false is a society whose relationships are false. Covenant community requires honest representation of reality.

11:4

MacArthur: Wealth is worthless in the day of wrath, but righteousness delivers from death. The day of wrath may refer to sudden calamity or to the final judgment — the proverb works in both directions. What you have accumulated does not protect you when the ultimate reckoning comes; who you have become does.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:1–9

The opening section of ch.11 moves rapidly across economic ethics (scales, v.1), character (pride/humility, v.2), integrity in crisis (v.3–5), righteousness in death (v.4), and sexual ethics (adultery, v.6). Alter notes this variety is not random — the proverbs are illustrations of the same underlying principle: covenant faithfulness produces life; covenant violation produces destruction, regardless of the domain.

11:8

'The righteous person is rescued from trouble, and it falls on the wicked instead.' This lex talionis proverb — the trouble meant for the righteous falls on the wicked instead — is characteristic of Proverbs' worldview. Alter notes the reversal pattern (the righteous delivered; the wicked get what was aimed at the righteous) is a compressed theodicy: God's moral order inverts human injustice.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:1–6

The honest-scales opening places covenant commerce at the heart of wisdom ethics. Waltke notes that dishonest weights were a form of economic violence against the poor and a theological offence against the God who is the standard of all measurement. The 'detestable' (to'evah) and 'delight' (ratson) language frames the economic ethics within the category of divine character — not merely illegal but an abomination.

11:2

'When pride comes, then comes disgrace, but with humility comes wisdom.' This is Proverbs' most direct statement of the pride-wisdom antithesis. Waltke notes 'pride' (zadon) is the arrogant self-assertion that refuses the epistemological submission that wisdom requires (3:5–7). Pride and wisdom are structurally incompatible: the proud person cannot receive wisdom because wisdom requires the acknowledgment of one's limitation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:1

Calvin: 'God has appointed equal weights for commerce not as a mere legal requirement but because he is himself the standard of all justice. To defraud with false weights is not merely to cheat a customer but to defy the God who weighs all things in perfect balance. The merchant who falsifies his scales declares that his standard is better than God's.'

11:2

Calvin on humility: 'Wisdom is given to the humble because they are the only ones who can receive it. The proud man is already convinced he knows — so he cannot learn. The humble man acknowledges he does not know — and so can be taught. Humility is not a virtue added to wisdom; it is the posture that makes wisdom possible.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:1

The NET note on 'honest scales are the LORD's delight; dishonest scales are his abomination' (to'avat YHWH mozen mirmah veeven shelemah retsono) discusses the ancient Near Eastern background: merchants carried two sets of weights — heavier ones when buying (to pay less) and lighter ones when selling (to receive more). Lev 19:35–36 and Deut 25:13–16 also prohibit this. The 'abomination' language places economic fraud in the same moral category as idolatry.

11:5

The NET note on 'the righteousness of the blameless person keeps his way straight' discusses 'blameless' (tamim, complete/whole, the same word used of Job). The blameless person is not sinlessly perfect but whole in their orientation — fully committed to covenant faithfulness rather than divided between wisdom and folly.

Verses 16–31 — Generosity's Paradox; Beauty Without Discretion; Tree of Life
16A kindhearted woman gains honor, but ruthless men gain only wealth.17Those who are kind benefit themselves, but the cruel bring ruin on themselves.18A wicked person earns deceptive wages, but the one who sows righteousness reaps a sure reward.19Truly the righteous attain life, but whoever pursues evil finds death.20The LORD detests those whose hearts are perverse, but he delights in those whose ways are blameless.21Be sure of this: The wicked will not go unpunished, but those who are righteous will go free.22Like a gold ring in a pig's snout is a beautiful woman who shows no discretion.23The desire of the righteous ends only in good, but the hope of the wicked only in wrath.24One person gives freely, yet gains even more; another withholds unduly, but comes to poverty.25A generous person will prosper; whoever refreshes others will be refreshed.26People curse the one who hoards grain, but they pray God's blessing on the one who is willing to sell.27Whoever seeks good finds favor, but evil comes to one who searches for it.28Those who trust in their riches will fall, but the righteous will thrive like a green leaf.29Whoever brings ruin on their family will inherit only wind, and the fool will be servant to the wise.30The fruit of the righteous is a tree of life, and the one who is wise saves lives.31If the righteous receive their due on earth, how much more the ungodly and the sinner!

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half addresses the generous paradox (vv.24-26), wealth-trust (v.28), and the social fruitfulness of the righteous (vv.30-31). The gold-ring-in-a-pig's-snout image (v.22): beauty divorced from character is grotesque.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 2:9The tree of life in Eden -- Proverbs reclaims this image for righteousness.
  • 2 Cor 9:6-7The one who sows generously will reap generously -- NT application of v.24's generous paradox.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
11:16-31

MacArthur: The righteous person's fruit blesses not only themselves but the whole city (v.11). Covenant ethics are not merely personal but communal: the wise person's flourishing creates space for others to flourish. By the blessing of the upright a city is exalted.

11:24-25

MacArthur: The paradox of generosity — give more, have more; withhold, have less — is not naive optimism but covenant economics. The person who participates in the divine generosity that sustains all life receives back more than they give; the hoarder closes the circuit of provision and discovers that the circuit then closes against them.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:24–26

The generosity paradox (vv.24–26) is one of Proverbs' most counter-intuitive observations. Alter notes its structure: three proverbs in a row that all point to the same reversal — giving produces increase; hoarding produces decrease; blessing on the generous; curses on the hoarder. The cluster creates an economic principle that works against human instinct and for divine economy.

11:29–31

The closing proverbs of ch.11 return to the household (trouble in one's own house, v.29) and the community (fruit of the righteous, tree of life, v.30) before the final observation: 'if the righteous receive their due on earth, how much more the ungodly and the sinner!' Alter notes the argument a fortiori — if even the righteous experience consequence, the wicked's consequences will be greater.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:16–31

The second half of ch.11 develops the community-and-generosity theme. Waltke notes vv.24–26 form a paradox cluster: 'one person gives freely yet gains even more; another withholds unduly but comes to poverty.' This is Proverbs' most concentrated statement of generosity economics — the more you give, the more you have; the more you hoard, the less you have. It is both practical observation and theological claim about how God's moral order works.

11:30

'The fruit of the righteous is a tree of life, and the one who is wise saves lives.' The tree of life image (from Gen 2–3, explicitly) appears twice in Proverbs (11:30; 13:12; 15:4). Waltke notes Proverbs reclaims for wisdom the cosmic symbol of Eden's forbidden tree: wisdom produces what the unfaithful grasping of Genesis 3 sought to seize.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:24

Calvin on generous giving: 'This is the economy of grace — it is contrary to carnal reason, which says that the more we give the less we have. But God's ordering reverses this: he who distributes liberally is enriched; he who hoards for fear of want impoverishes himself. The difference is not in the mathematics but in whether God is involved.'

11:30

Calvin on the tree of life: 'The righteous person's fruit is not merely beneficial but life-giving in the deepest sense — it communicates the life of God. The wise man who saves souls is imitating God, who is himself the source of all life. To cultivate wisdom in others is to participate in the divine work of giving life.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:24

The NET note on the liberality paradox ('one person gives freely yet gains even more') discusses the economic background: in ancient Israel, generous grain-lending during famines created social credit that was repaid multiply in good years. The 'gain even more' is not mystical multiplication but the social-economic reality of covenant community reciprocity.

11:30

The NET note on 'tree of life' (ets chayyim) traces the image from Gen 2:9 and 3:22–24 through Prov 3:18; 11:30; 13:12; 15:4 and into Rev 2:7; 22:2, 14. The tree of life is wisdom's symbol in Proverbs (3:18: 'she is a tree of life to those who take hold of her'). The righteous person whose life produces the tree of life is enacting what the original tree of life represents: access to the eternal life of the covenant God.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

Proverbs 11 and the Prosperity Gospel: Does Generosity Guarantee Material Return?
A general principle, not a transactional guarantee
Waltke, most evangelical scholars
The liberality proverbs (11:24–26) describe God's moral order, not a transactional formula. Generally, generous community members receive more communal support; generally, hoarders lose social capital. This is observation, not promise. The prosperity gospel's transactional version ('give and God must give back materially') distorts Proverbs' observation into a manipulative formula.
A theological principle of divine economics
Some Reformed scholars
The principle is genuinely theological — God's economy works differently from human calculation. The liberality proverbs are making a claim about how God orders material reality, not merely a sociological observation. The principle is real, even if its mechanism is not always visible and its timing is not always immediate.
A wisdom idealisation that experience sometimes contradicts
Fox, Ecclesiastes tradition
Proverbs states the principle as generally true; experience sometimes contradicts it. Qohelet (Ecclesiastes) and Job both observe that the generous sometimes lose everything while the hoarders sometimes prosper. The principle needs to be held with epistemic humility.
The generalisation reading holds together the principle's reality and its limits. Proverbs makes genuine theological claims about how God's order works — and those claims are generally true. They are not transactional formulas that bind God to specific material responses on a timeline of human choosing.
