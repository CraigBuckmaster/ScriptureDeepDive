Author

Solomon ben David, c.990–931 BC. Israel's third king, son of David and Bathsheba. Credited by Scripture with 3,000 proverbs and 1,005 songs (1 Kgs 4:32). His wisdom was divinely given — not merely accumulated experience but a gift granted at Gibeon (1 Kgs 3:5–14). He built the Temple, forged commercial alliances across the ancient world, and attracted foreign dignitaries to Jerusalem including the Queen of Sheba (1 Kgs 10). Proverbs 1–9 are the most clearly Solomonic; chs. 25–29 were copied by the men of Hezekiah (25:1); chs. 30–31 are attributed to Agur and Lemuel respectively.

When written

The core Solomonic material (chs. 1–9, 10–22:16) dates to Solomon's reign, c.970–931 BC. The final compilation and editing likely continued through the Hezekiah period (c.715–686 BC), when his scribes copied additional Solomonic proverbs (25:1). The book reached its present form sometime in the late monarchic or early post-exilic period.

What prompted it

Proverbs is a father's sustained address to a son standing on the threshold of adult life. The driving question is: How does a young person navigate the real world — its temptations, its social pressures, its competing voices — without being destroyed? The book's answer is not a list of rules but a vision of reality: the world was made with Wisdom woven into it (ch. 8), and the person who aligns their life with that grain will flourish; the person who fights it will be broken by it. "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7) is the thesis of the entire book.

Hebrew Word Study

מַעֲנֶה רַּךְ maʿăneh rak — a gentle answer: V.1: maʿăneh (answer, reply) + rak (soft, tender). The gentle answer does not capitulate or become shapeless — it retains its content while surrendering aggression in its delivery. The verb "turns away" (šûb) is the standard OT word for repentance/turning. A gentle answer produces a turning-away of anger — a kind of emotional repentance in the hearer.

טֹוב מְעַט בְּיִרְאַת יְהוָה ṭôb mĕʿaṭ bĕyirʾat yhwh — better a little with the fear of the LORD: The "better than" proverbs (tôb...min) appear repeatedly in chs.15–17. They subvert the natural equation of more = better. The fear of the LORD with poverty exceeds wealth with turmoil. Love with small portions exceeds hatred with feasting. The comparison deliberately destabilises material definitions of the good life.

Context

Chapter 15 is one of the most theologically rich chapters in the collection. It opens with the famous gentle answer proverb (v.1), moves through three direct statements about the LORD's omniscience and preferences (vv.3, 8–9, 11), and introduces the "better than" form (vv.16–17). The LORD's delight in prayer over sacrifice (v.8) is particularly significant — it is one of Proverbs' clearest statements about the priority of the heart over external religious performance. The chapter closes with the thesis of the entire book restated: "wisdom's instruction is to fear the LORD, and humility comes before honour" (v.33).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
15:1

A gentle answer turns away wrath, but a harsh word stirs up anger. The gentle answer does not capitulate — it retains its content while surrendering aggression in its delivery. The Hebrew for "turns away" (šûb) is the standard word for repentance/turning: a gentle answer produces a kind of emotional repentance in the hearer. This is not conflict avoidance but conflict transformation.

15:3

The eyes of the LORD are everywhere, keeping watch on the wicked and the good. Divine omniscience is not primarily a threat but a stabilising reality. Nothing escapes God's notice — not the wickedness of the oppressor or the faithfulness of the righteous. This is the theological foundation for why justice ultimately prevails: the Judge sees everything.

15:8

The LORD detests the sacrifice of the wicked, but the prayer of the upright pleases him. Among the most radical claims in Proverbs: religious observance without righteous character is not merely insufficient — it is detestable. The elaborate sacrificial system cannot substitute for integrity. Samuel's rebuke of Saul (1 Sam 15:22: "to obey is better than sacrifice") and the prophets' oracles against empty worship (Amos 5:21–24) all develop this same principle.

15:16–17

Better a little with the fear of the LORD than great wealth with turmoil. Better a small serving of vegetables with love than a fattened calf with hatred. The "better than" proverbs subvert the natural equation of more = better. The fear of the LORD with poverty exceeds wealth with turmoil. Love with small portions exceeds hatred with feasting. These proverbs function as value inversions — teaching the reader to see past the obvious metric to what actually constitutes human flourishing.

15:33

Wisdom's instruction is to fear the LORD, and humility comes before honour. The thesis of 1:7 and 9:10 restated for the third time in the collection. The governing principle keeps reasserting itself because every other observation in Proverbs flows from it. Wisdom is not a technique for success; it is a life lived in right relationship to the God who made the world with wisdom.

Ancient Sources

Better-Than Proverbs
The "better than" (ṭôb...min) form appears throughout chs.15–17. It is a deliberate counter to popular assumptions: better little with fear of God than wealth with turmoil; better vegetables with love than feasting with hatred. These proverbs function as value inversions — teaching the student to see past the obvious metric.
The "better than" form is one of Proverbs' most rhetorically sophisticated devices.
