Author

Solomon ben David, c.990–931 BC. Israel's third king, son of David and Bathsheba. Credited by Scripture with 3,000 proverbs and 1,005 songs (1 Kgs 4:32). His wisdom was divinely given — not merely accumulated experience but a gift granted at Gibeon (1 Kgs 3:5–14). He built the Temple, forged commercial alliances across the ancient world, and attracted foreign dignitaries to Jerusalem including the Queen of Sheba (1 Kgs 10). Proverbs 1–9 are the most clearly Solomonic; chs. 25–29 were copied by the men of Hezekiah (25:1); chs. 30–31 are attributed to Agur and Lemuel respectively.

When written

The core Solomonic material (chs. 1–9, 10–22:16) dates to Solomon's reign, c.970–931 BC. The final compilation and editing likely continued through the Hezekiah period (c.715–686 BC), when his scribes copied additional Solomonic proverbs (25:1). The book reached its present form sometime in the late monarchic or early post-exilic period.

What prompted it

Proverbs is a father's sustained address to a son standing on the threshold of adult life. The driving question is: How does a young person navigate the real world — its temptations, its social pressures, its competing voices — without being destroyed? The book's answer is not a list of rules but a vision of reality: the world was made with Wisdom woven into it (ch. 8), and the person who aligns their life with that grain will flourish; the person who fights it will be broken by it. "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7) is the thesis of the entire book.

Context

Chapter 28 is one of the most politically engaged chapters in Proverbs, containing more observations about rulers and governance than any other. It also contains v.13 — one of the most important single verses in Proverbs on the spiritual life: "whoever conceals their sins does not prosper, but the one who confesses and renounces them finds mercy." This is Proverbs' clearest statement about repentance and restoration.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 John 1:9V.13's "confesses and renounces finds mercy" → "if we confess our sins, he is faithful and just and will forgive us our sins." John develops the principle into the NT doctrine of ongoing confession and forgiveness.
  • Ps 32:3–5Psalm 32 is the extended meditation on v.13: David's description of what it felt like to conceal sin (bones wasting, groaning, hand heavy on him) and then confess it (forgiven, shout of deliverance).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
28:1

The wicked flee though no one pursues, but the righteous are as bold as a lion. The psychology of guilt-driven anxiety. Guilt is its own punishment before any external consequence arrives. The wicked person's conscience pursues them — there is no rest. The righteous person is functionally free: no internal accuser means no background anxiety. Clear conscience produces courage. This is Proverbs' statement about the psychological cost of hidden sin.

28:5

Evildoers do not understand what is right, but those who seek the LORD understand it fully. Moral understanding is not morally neutral — it depends on one's fundamental orientation. Those who seek the LORD understand (bîn — discern) justice fully; evildoers do not understand it. The person who has oriented their life around God has calibrated their moral perception; the person who has not lacks the fundamental reference point.

28:13

Whoever conceals their sins does not prosper, but the one who confesses and renounces them finds mercy. The most important single verse on repentance in Proverbs. Hiding sin preserves the appearance of righteousness while the inner reality deteriorates. The two required actions are confession (yādāh — to throw, to acknowledge openly) and renunciation (ʿāzab — to leave, forsake) — acknowledgement plus abandonment. Neither alone is sufficient; both together receive mercy.

28:20

A faithful person will be richly blessed, but one eager to get rich will not go unpunished. The contrast is between faithfulness (ʾĕmûnāh — reliability, constancy) and eagerness to get rich (bāhal — to be hasty, hurried). Faithfulness means attending to what is actually your responsibility and doing it well. Eagerness to get rich is the replacement of faithful stewardship with obsessive focus on accumulation. The first is blessed; the second is punished.

28:27

Those who give to the poor will lack nothing, but those who close their eyes to them receive many curses. The final generosity proverb of the collection: no lack for the generous; many curses for the wilfully blind. The closing of the eyes is an act of will — a deliberate refusal to see. The consequences are proportionally severe: not merely missing a blessing but receiving "many curses" — the active reciprocal of the blessing refused.

Ancient Sources

Political Proverbs
Chs.28–29 have a higher concentration of political/royal observations than any other section of Proverbs. This likely reflects the Hezekiah court context — wisdom was being applied to governance. The observations include the relationship between righteous leadership and national prosperity, and the danger of tyranny.
The political concentration in chs.28–29 suggests a court wisdom context.
