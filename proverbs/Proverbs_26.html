Proverbs 26

The Fool's Anatomy: Snow in Summer; The Sluggard; Gossip's Choice Morsels

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–12 — The Fool's Anatomy: Snow in Summer; Dog Returns to Its Vomit
1Like snow in summer or rain in harvest, honor is not fitting for a fool.2Like a fluttering sparrow or a darting swallow, an undeserved curse does not come to rest.3A whip for the horse, a bridle for the donkey, and a rod for the backs of fools!4Do not answer a fool according to his folly, or you yourself will be just like him.5Answer a fool according to his folly, or he will be wise in his own eyes.6Sending a message by the hands of a fool is like cutting off one's feet or drinking poison.7Like the useless legs of one who is lame is a proverb in the mouth of a fool.8Like tying a stone in a sling is the giving of honor to a fool.9Like a thornbush in a drunkard's hand is a proverb in the mouth of a fool.10Like an archer who wounds at random is one who hires a fool or any passer-by.11As a dog returns to its vomit, so fools repeat their folly.12Do you see a person wise in their own eyes? There is more hope for a fool than for them.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half of ch.26 is Proverbs' most extended treatment of the fool — 12 verses dedicated to anatomising foolishness in its various forms. The gallery includes: snow-in-summer honour (v.1), the curse without cause (v.2), the dog-returns-to-its-vomit proverb (v.11), and the person who is wise in their own eyes (v.12).

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Pet 2:22A dog returns to its vomit — Peter cites v.11 directly for the false prophet who returns to their sin after apparent escape.
  • Prov 14:12The way that appears right but leads to death — the wise-in-own-eyes person of v.12 is the same character.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
26:1-12

MacArthur: The fool chapters (chs.26-27) concentrate the book's portrait of folly. The extended treatment of the fool (vv.1-12) builds the composite picture: the fool is unreliable (snow in summer), undeserving of honor, not to be answered and also to be answered (the consecutive contradiction of vv.4-5 teaches situational wisdom).

26:11

MacArthur: As a dog returns to its vomit, so fools repeat their folly. Peter quotes this proverb (2 Pet 2:22) for its unflinching accuracy. The image is repulsive by design: the fool's return to the behavior that has already proven destructive is not merely unwise but revolting. Repulsion is the appropriate emotional response to folly's cycle.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
26:1-12

The eleven fool-proverbs build a complete character study. Alter notes each proverb adds a different dimension to the portrait: his unsuitability for honour (vv.1-3), the paradox of engaging him (vv.4-5), the danger of trusting him with messages (vv.6-11), and his self-perception as wise (v.12). The accumulation is the point: the fool is comprehensively a fool.

26:11

As a dog returns to its vomit, so fools repeat their folly — the grotesque physiological image is deliberate. Alter notes the disgust the image produces is calibrated to the disgust the reality deserves. The fool who returns to folly after experiencing its consequences is described with an image that makes the irrationality of the return viscerally apparent.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
26:1

Like snow in summer, honour is not fitting for a fool — the seasonal incongruity makes the mismatch categorical. Waltke notes the proverb is not merely saying honour is wasted on a fool but that it is categorically wrong — as wrong as snow in summer. Honour given to a fool is a category error, not just a mistake.

26:4-5

Answer / do not answer a fool -- consecutive contradictory instructions. No universal rule; wisdom discerns which approach the specific situation requires.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
26:4

Calvin: Answer a fool / do not answer a fool — these contradictory instructions teach that wisdom is situational. No universal rule exists for engaging fools. The wise person must discern which of the two responses the specific situation requires. The very existence of the contradiction is the lesson: wisdom is judgment, not rule-following.

26:12

Calvin: More hope for a fool than for one wise in their own eyes — self-conceit is more incurable than simple foolishness. The fool can in principle be taught; the self-wise person has already decided they need no teaching. Pride that has convinced itself it is wisdom is the most effective barrier to receiving wisdom.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
26:4-5

The NET note on the consecutive contradictions explains their didactic function: both responses are genuinely correct in different circumstances. The placement of the two verses together forces the student of wisdom to recognise that wisdom is contextual judgment, not algorithmic rule-following. The contradiction is the lesson.

26:11

The NET note on the dog-returns-to-vomit image traces 2 Pet 2:22, which quotes this proverb as the pattern of false teachers who return to their former corruption after being delivered from it. The animal image makes the irrational self-destruction of persistent folly as viscerally clear as possible.

Verses 13–28 — The Sluggard; The Gossip's Choice Morsels; Concealed Hatred
13A sluggard says, "There's a lion in the road, a fierce lion roaming the streets!"14As a door turns on its hinges, so a sluggard turns on his bed.15A sluggard buries his hand in the dish; he is too lazy to bring it back to his mouth.16A sluggard is wiser in his own eyes than seven people who answer discreetly.17Like one who grabs a stray dog by the ears is someone who rushes into a quarrel not their own.18Like a maniac shooting flaming arrows of death19is one who deceives their neighbor and says, "I was only joking!"20Without wood a fire goes out; without a gossip a quarrel dies down.21As charcoal to embers and as wood to fire, so is a quarrelsome person for kindling strife.22The words of a gossip are like choice morsels; they go down to the inmost parts.23Like a coating of silver dross on earthenware are fervent lips with an evil heart.24Enemies disguise themselves with their lips, but in their hearts they harbor deceit.25Though their speech is charming, do not believe them, for seven abominations fill their hearts.26Their malice may be concealed by deception, but their wickedness will be exposed in the assembly.27Whoever digs a pit will fall into it; if someone rolls a stone, it will roll back on them.28A lying tongue hates those it hurts, and a flattering mouth works ruin.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half addresses the sluggard (vv.13-16), the dangerous meddler (v.17), the gossip (v.20-22), and the person who conceals hatred with smooth speech (vv.24-28). The chapter closes with the trap-digger's self-destruction: whoever digs a pit will fall into it (v.27).

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 16:28A perverse person stirs up conflict, and a gossip separates close friends — the same gossip diagnosis as v.20.
  • Eccl 10:8Whoever digs a pit may fall into it — the self-defeating trap of v.27 echoed in Ecclesiastes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
26:13-28

MacArthur: The sluggard says, there is a lion in the road, a fierce lion roaming the streets! (v.13). The sluggard's lion is the classic excuse dressed as danger. The same lion that has never appeared becomes the permanent rationale for permanent inaction. Laziness justifies itself through perpetually unchecked fears.

26:20

MacArthur: Without wood a fire goes out; without a gossip a quarrel dies down. The most structural community-conflict insight in Proverbs: the gossip is the quarrel's fuel supply, not merely one of its participants. Remove the fuel and the fire ends. Every sustained conflict has someone keeping it alive by talking.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
26:13-16

The four consecutive sluggard proverbs build a complete comic portrait: the absurd excuse (lion in the road), the door on its hinges (moves but goes nowhere), the hand in the dish (too lazy to bring food to the mouth), and the supreme irony — the sluggard who is wiser in his own eyes than seven wise counsellors. Alter notes the comedy escalates to its peak in v.16.

26:20

Without gossip a quarrel dies — Alter notes the structural observation about community conflict is one of Proverbs' most practically useful. Remove the gossip and the conflict resolves; keep the gossip and the conflict persists regardless of all other interventions. The gossip is identified as the fire's fuel rather than its cause.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
26:13

The sluggard says there is a lion in the road -- any reason to avoid work will do. The laziness is revealed by the excuse's function, not its plausibility.

26:20

Without wood a fire goes out; without a gossip a quarrel dies — Waltke notes the word nirgan (gossip, whisperer) describes the person whose speech choice morsels go down to the innermost being (v.22). The gossip's influence is deep and structural, not merely surface-level. The fire-without-fuel image identifies the gossip as the quarrel's fuel, not its cause — but without fuel, even a justified conflict dies.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
26:17

Calvin: Like one who grabs a stray dog by the ears is someone who rushes into a quarrel not their own — both are setting themselves up for a painful and pointless injury. The grabbed dog bites; the quarrel wounds. The wisdom of non-interference in disputes that do not belong to you is among the most neglected of Proverbs' practical instructions.

26:20

Calvin: Without wood a fire goes out — the most direct thing you can do for community peace is to stop feeding gossip. The gossip who stops talking is more effective than any mediator. The mediator tries to address the quarrel; the reformed gossip removes the fuel that sustains it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
26:13

The NET note on the lion-in-the-road excuse illustrates the structure of sloth: any reason to avoid work, however implausible, will be deployed. The point is not the specific excuse (lions are unlikely in the street) but the character that generates it: the sluggard's creativity is entirely devoted to justifying inaction.

26:20

The NET note on the structural insight about gossip as conflict's fuel traces the gossip-conflict connection through Prov 16:28 (a gossip separates close friends), 17:9 (the tongue that repeats an offense separates close friends), and here (without a gossip, conflict dies). The consistent pattern identifies the gossip as the key variable in community conflict.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions, especially Egyptian.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs — one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.
The editorial activity shows Proverbs as a living, curated collection.

Reception History

Virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics — the practical outworking of the fear of the LORD.
Practical wisdom
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, governance, and social teaching.

Scholarly Debates

The Fool: Type or Continuum?
A distinct character type: the kesil
Waltke, most commentators
The kesil has made a settled choice against wisdom's formation. The eleven portraits describe a character, not occasional failures.
A continuum from peti to kesil to letz
Some scholars
The three fool-words represent a spectrum of increasing resistance to wisdom.
The spectrum reading is most consistent with Proverbs' overall structure. The character types are points on a continuum of increasing resistance.
