Proverbs 13

Teachable Son; Speech that Preserves; Walk with the Wise

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–12 — Teachable Son; Speech that Preserves; Hope Deferred
1A wise son heeds his father's instruction, but a mocker does not respond to rebukes.2From the fruit of their lips people enjoy good things, but the unfaithful have an appetite for violence.3Those who guard their lips preserve their lives, but those who speak rashly will come to ruin.4A sluggard's appetite is never filled, but the desires of the diligent are fully satisfied.5The righteous hate what is false, but the wicked make themselves a stench and bring shame on themselves.6Righteousness guards the person of integrity, but wickedness overthrows the sinner.7One person pretends to be rich, yet has nothing; another pretends to be poor, yet has great wealth.8A person's riches may ransom their life, but the poor cannot respond to threatening rebukes.9The light of the righteous shines brightly, but the lamp of the wicked is snuffed out.10Where there is strife, there is pride, but wisdom is found in those who take advice.11Dishonest money dwindles away, but whoever gathers money little by little makes it grow.12Hope deferred makes the heart sick, but a longing fulfilled is a tree of life.

Hebrew Word Study

tocheleth tocheleth — hope / expectation: V.12: hope deferred (tocheleth memashecha) makes the heart sick. The verb means drawn out, prolonged. Prolonged disappointment produces a specific illness of the soul. A longing fulfilled is a tree of life.

menorat reshaim menorat reshaim — lamp of the wicked: V.9: the lamp of the wicked is snuffed out. The lamp is the ancient symbol of life and family continuity. A snuffed lamp means the end of the family line.

Context

The first half addresses the teachable son versus the mocker (v.1), speech that preserves or destroys (vv.2-3), diligent versus sluggard (v.4), and the light/lamp contrast (v.9). V.12 is the section's emotional peak: wisdom acknowledges the real suffering of hope deferred before affirming that fulfilment is life-giving.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 11:30Fruit of the righteous is a tree of life -- 13:12 is its second occurrence, now attached to fulfilled longing.
  • Prov 10:1A wise son makes a glad father -- the father-son relationship anchoring v.1.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:3

Those who guard their lips preserve their lives. Reckless speech is not merely impolite -- it is life-threatening. Discretion is a survival skill.

13:11

Dishonest money dwindles away. Patient, incremental accumulation of honest gain has structural durability that shortcuts lack.

13:12

Hope deferred makes the heart sick. The wisdom tradition names the real suffering of waiting before affirming that fulfilment is life-giving.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:1–4

The first four proverbs of ch.13 create a character portrait by accumulation: the wise son accepts instruction; the good man eats well from his speech; the talkative person who guards his lips saves himself; the sluggard craves and gets nothing, the diligent is fully satisfied. Alter notes the accumulation method — multiple proverbs each adding a dimension — is the primary teaching strategy of the proverb collections.

13:20

'Walk with the wise and become wise, for a companion of fools suffers harm.' This is Proverbs' most direct social-formation proverb. Alter notes its mechanism: you become like the people you spend time with. Wisdom is transferable through proximity; folly is equally transferable. The choice of companions is therefore a wisdom decision of the first order.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:1–6

The teachable-son opening connects to 12:1's teachability theme. Waltke notes ch.13 develops the social consequences of wisdom and folly: the wise person builds social capital (v.2: fruit of one's mouth), while the violent reap destruction. Wisdom is not merely an internal virtue but a social force — it shapes relationships, reputation, and community.

13:12

'Hope deferred makes the heart sick, but a longing fulfilled is a tree of life.' Waltke notes this is one of Proverbs' most psychologically precise observations — the physiology of hope and its frustration. The 'tree of life' image for fulfilled longing places genuine satisfaction in the category of cosmic blessing: to receive what you have hoped for is to taste Eden's gift.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:7

Calvin: 'Some make themselves rich while having nothing; others make themselves poor while having great wealth. This is a paradox that ordinary accounting cannot explain — it is the spiritual arithmetic of covenant faithfulness. The man who gives generously appears to have little; he actually has much. The man who grasps for more appears rich; he is actually poor.'

13:24

Calvin on 'spare the rod, spoil the child': 'The father who refuses to discipline his son out of misplaced tenderness is not showing love but hatred — he is allowing his son to pursue the path that leads to destruction. True love for a child includes the willingness to cause pain for the sake of character formation.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:12

The NET note on 'hope deferred makes the heart sick' discusses the word yachal (hope, wait expectantly). Deferred hope (mamshikhah, prolonged) produces sickness — a psychosomatic observation that is as accurate to modern experience as it was to ancient. The tree of life for fulfilled longing places emotional fulfilment within the cosmic framework of life and blessing.

13:20

The NET note on 'walk with the wise and become wise' (holekh et-chakhamim yechkam) discusses the sociological mechanism: wisdom is transferred through intentional association. The word 'walk with' (halakh et) means accompaniment — the regular, sustained companionship that shapes character over time. This is the positive version of the warning about companionship with sinners in 1:10–19.

Verses 13–25 — Fountain of Life; Walk with the Wise; Spare the Rod
13Whoever scorns instruction will pay for it, but whoever respects a command is rewarded.14The teaching of the wise is a fountain of life, turning a person from the snares of death.15Good judgment wins favor, but the way of the unfaithful leads to their destruction.16All who are prudent act with knowledge, but fools expose their folly.17A wicked messenger falls into trouble, but a trustworthy envoy brings healing.18Whoever disregards discipline comes to poverty and shame, but whoever heeds correction is honored.19A longing fulfilled is sweet to the soul, but fools detest turning from evil.20Walk with the wise and become wise, for a companion of fools suffers harm.21Trouble pursues the sinner, but the righteous are rewarded with good things.22A good person leaves an inheritance for their children's children, but a sinner's wealth is stored up for the righteous.23An unplowed field produces food for the poor, but injustice sweeps it away.24Whoever spares the rod hates their children, but the one who loves them is careful to discipline them.25The righteous eat to their hearts' content, but the stomach of the wicked goes hungry.

Hebrew Word Study

meqor chayyim meqor chayyim — fountain of life: V.14: the teaching of the wise is a spring of life that continuously turns people from death-traps (pache mavet). Wisdom's teaching is not a static deposit but a flowing source.

chosek shivto chosek shivto — sparing the rod: V.24: the rod (shevet) is the shepherd's staff -- the instrument of guidance and protection. The love that withholds correction is negligent: it hates the child by leaving them unformed.

Context

The second half contains three of Proverbs' most quoted sayings: fountain of life (v.14), walk with the wise (v.20), and spare the rod (v.24). They share a common logic: formation requires active engagement with wisdom sources.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 14:27Fear of the LORD is a fountain of life -- the teaching-of-the-wise (13:14) and the fear-of-the-LORD (14:27) are aligned sources.
  • 1 Cor 15:33Bad company corrupts good character -- NT application of v.20's companion proverb.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:20

Walk with the wise and become wise. Wisdom is transmitted person-to-person. The community of the wise is itself a formation environment.

13:22

A good person leaves an inheritance for their children's children. The multigenerational horizon of wisdom's ethics.

13:24

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:13–14

The fountain-of-life and snare-of-death imagery for the wise person's teaching appears here in summary form. Alter notes this pair (fountain of life; snare of death) distils the entire Proverbs project: wisdom leads to life; folly leads to death. The extremes — life and death, fountain and snare — are not rhetorical exaggeration but the genuine theological stakes of ordinary choices.

13:22

'A good person leaves an inheritance for their children's children, but a sinner's wealth is stored up for the righteous.' The three-generation perspective is rare in Proverbs. Alter notes the observation functions both as a general principle about wealth accumulation (the prudent build across generations) and as a theological promise (ultimately, the wicked's wealth doesn't stay with the wicked).

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:13–25

The second section develops the long-term perspectives that wisdom maintains. Waltke notes vv.13–25 are held together by the theme of future-orientation: the wise person weighs consequences (v.13–14), hopes for fulfilled longing (v.12), builds wealth gradually (v.11), and disciplines children for their future (v.24). Wisdom lives with one eye on tomorrow.

13:24

'Whoever spares the rod hates their children, but the one who loves them is careful to discipline them.' Waltke notes 'careful to discipline' (meaher musaro) literally means 'seeks their discipline' — the loving parent is proactively looking for opportunities for character-forming correction, not merely responding to crises.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:13

Calvin: 'Those who despise the divine word are destroying themselves. The word of God is not merely advice to be accepted or rejected at pleasure — it is the expression of the divine will, and to treat it with contempt is to set oneself against the order of reality. The consequences are not arbitrary punishment but the natural outcome of choosing against the grain of how things actually are.'

13:25

Calvin: 'God takes care that the belly of the righteous is filled — not always with abundance, but with what is sufficient and appropriate. This is a covenant promise: the person who walks in covenant faithfulness will not be abandoned to starvation. The wicked may have abundance, but it is temporary and unblessed; the righteous may have little, but it is genuine and satisfying.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:11

The NET note on 'wealth obtained by fraud dwindles, but whoever gathers money little by little makes it grow' discusses the Hebrew vathal (fraud, worthlessness, vanity) — the wealth gained by deceit or speculation. The note identifies this as a general economic observation: quick wealth gained dishonestly tends to disappear; patient accumulation tends to grow. The principle is both prudential and theological.

13:22

The NET note on 'a sinner's wealth is stored up for the righteous' (chote' tsafun letsadiq) discusses the eschatological resonance: the sinner accumulates wealth that ultimately transfers to the covenant community. The note cites Eccl 2:26 as a parallel (the sinner 'gathers and stores up wealth to hand it over to the one who pleases God') and Prov 28:8 as a third witness.

Literary Structure

Proverbs 13:20 is the book's clearest statement about social formation: you become like the people you spend time with. Wisdom is contagious through proximity; so is folly. The choice of companions is therefore one of wisdom's highest-stakes practical decisions — not merely about reputation but about character formation.
Fulfilled Longing as Tree of Life — 13:12The tree of life imagery for fulfilled longing (13:12) places emotional satisfaction in the category of cosmic blessing. To hope for something and receive it is not merely pleasant — it is a taste of Eden's gift, the original life-giving tree that the covenant restoration is working to restore. Proverbs' wisdom is not ascetic but eucharistic.

Textual Notes

13:24'Spare the rod, hate the child'
MT: chosekh shivto soneh beno (he who spares his rod hates his son). The verse is unambiguous in the MT — sparing the rod = hatred; disciplining = love. LXX: ho pheidōmenos tēs bakterias misei ton huion autou — identical meaning. The rod (shevet) is the same word used for the shepherd's staff and the ruler's scepter.
The verse states a principle about parental love that is counter-intuitive to modern sensibility: love is not the absence of discipline but its presence. The application of corporal punishment is culturally and contextually conditioned; the underlying principle — that genuine love for a child includes willingness to cause discomfort for character formation — is the durable core of the proverb.

Scholarly Debates

Does Proverbs 13:24 Endorse Corporal Punishment?
Yes, within appropriate limits
MacArthur, conservative evangelical scholars
The rod is a real rod, and the proverb is a real endorsement of corporal punishment as a legitimate tool of parental discipline. The multiple rod-passages in Proverbs (13:24; 22:15; 23:13–14; 29:15) form a consistent pattern that cannot be allegorised away. Applied wisely and in love, corporal discipline is the covenant parent's tool.
The rod is wisdom's authority, not necessarily a physical instrument
Some evangelical scholars
The 'rod' in Proverbs represents the general authority and firmness of discipline, not necessarily a specific physical implement. Ancient shepherds used the rod to guide and correct sheep — the image is of authoritative, engaged, corrective guidance, which may or may not involve physical punishment depending on context.
Culturally conditioned; the principle (discipline in love) is universal
Most contemporary evangelical scholars
The specific cultural practice (corporal punishment in a specific ancient Near Eastern context) is distinct from the universal principle (loving parental discipline is essential to character formation). The principle is non-negotiable; the specific application must be contextualised to contemporary understanding of child development and wellbeing.
The 'discipline in love' principle is universal and non-negotiable. The specific application of physical punishment is contextually conditioned. The most responsible pastoral reading takes the principle (loving parents discipline their children) with full seriousness while acknowledging that specific practices must be evaluated within our contemporary understanding of child psychology and the command 'do not exasperate your children' (Eph 6:4).

Theological Themes Tracker

Proverbs 13 develops the social and temporal dimensions of wisdom: who you walk with shapes who you become (v.20); how you discipline your children expresses whether you love them (v.24); how you acquire wealth determines whether you keep it (v.11). The chapter's most memorable observation is its most counter-intuitive: sparing discipline is a form of hatred, not love.
