Proverbs 29

Proverbs 29

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–14 — Stubborn Neck; Righteous Rule; Wise Servant; Anger vs Self-Control
1Whoever remains stiff-necked after many rebukes will suddenly be destroyed—without remedy.2When the righteous thrive, the people rejoice; when the wicked rule, the people groan.3A man who loves wisdom brings joy to his father, but a companion of prostitutes squanders his wealth.4By justice a king gives a country stability, but those who are greedy for bribes tear it down.5Those who flatter their neighbors are spreading nets for their feet.6Evildoers are snared by their own sin, but the righteous shout for joy and are glad.7The righteous care about justice for the poor, but the wicked have no such concern.8Mockers stir up a city, but the wise turn away anger.9If a wise person goes to court with a fool, the fool rages and scoffs, and there is no peace.10The bloodthirsty hate a person of integrity and seek to kill the upright.11Fools give full vent to their rage, but the wise bring calm in the end.12If a ruler listens to lies, all his officials become wicked.13The poor and the oppressor have this in common: The LORD gives sight to the eyes of both.14If a king judges the poor with fairness, his throne will be established forever.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half of ch.29 addresses governance and social order: stubborn resistance to correction (v.1), the rejoicing of a people under righteous rule (v.2), the king who loves justice (v.4), and the contrast between the hot-tempered fool and the self-controlled wise person (v.11).

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 32:25Moses saw that the people had broken loose — 'pāraʿ' — the same word as v.18's "cast off restraint." Without the word of God, the people break loose as Israel did at Sinai.
  • Prov 14:34Righteousness exalts a nation — the same social-righteousness principle as the rejoicing under righteous rule (v.2).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
29:1-14

MacArthur: Whoever remains stiff-necked after many rebukes will suddenly be destroyed — without remedy (v.1). The proverb describes not a single failure but a pattern of refused correction culminating in catastrophe. The stiff neck is the posture of the person who will not bend; the sudden destruction is the result of the structural rigidity that makes no adjustment possible.

29:7

MacArthur: The righteous care about justice for the poor, but the wicked have no such concern. Care for the poor is a diagnostic of righteousness, not merely an expression of it. The righteous person does not merely refrain from oppressing the poor but actively cares about the justice they receive. The wicked's defining characteristic is the absence of this care.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:1

Stiff-necked after many rebukes -- Alter notes the cumulative nature of the warning: not one rebuke refused but many. The resistance to correction hardens over time into a structural condition.

29:11

A fool gives full vent to their rage, but a wise person quietly holds it back -- Alter notes the contrast between uncontrolled emotional expression (full vent) and the wisdom of regulation. Emotional intelligence is a wisdom practice.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:1

Whoever remains stiff-necked after many rebukes will suddenly be destroyed -- Waltke notes the 'suddenly' mirrors the earlier wisdom warnings. The person who refuses cumulative correction forfeits the gradual path and faces abrupt consequence.

29:18

Where there is no vision the people perish; but blessed is the one who heeds wisdom's instruction -- Waltke notes chazon (vision/revelation) is prophetic revelation from God. Absence of prophetic revelation produces social disorder (yipara, runs wild). The verse is about authoritative divine word, not personal goal-setting.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
29:1

Calvin: Whoever is often reproved but stiffens his neck will suddenly be broken -- the increasing resistance to correction produces an increasingly severe eventual judgment.

29:18

Calvin: Where there is no vision the people perish -- this is about the absence of God's word. When prophetic revelation is absent, social order breaks down. The verse is not about personal ambition but about the communal necessity of divine revelation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
29:1

NET: the stiffened neck (qashah oref) -- the standard Hebrew idiom for stubborn resistance to correction, applied to Israel throughout the OT (Exod 32:9; Deut 9:6; Isa 48:4).

29:18

NET: chazon (prophetic vision/revelation) -- not a personal vision or goal but authoritative divine revelation. The note distinguishes this from modern self-help applications of the verse.

Verses 15–27 — Correction and Rest; Where There Is No Vision; Fear of Man Is a Snare
15A rod and a reprimand impart wisdom, but a child left undisciplined disgraces its mother.16When the wicked thrive, so does sin, but the righteous will see their downfall.17Discipline your children, and they will give you peace; they will bring you the delights you desire.18Where there is no revelation, people cast off restraint; but blessed is the one who heeds wisdom's instruction.19Servants cannot be corrected by mere words; though they understand, they will not respond.20Do you see someone who speaks in haste? There is more hope for a fool than for them.21A servant pampered from youth will turn out to be insolent.22An angry person stirs up conflict, and a hot-tempered person commits many sins.23Pride brings a person low, but the lowly in spirit gain honor.24The accomplices of thieves are their own enemies; they are put under oath and dare not testify.25Fear of man will prove to be a snare, but whoever trusts in the LORD is kept safe.26Many seek an audience with a ruler, but it is from the LORD that one gets justice.27The righteous detest the dishonest; the wicked detest the upright.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half contains the chapter's most famous verse (v.18 — where there is no vision) alongside the fear-of-man snare (v.25), and the chapter's closing observation: the LORD's judgments are compared to those of human authorities. The fear of the LORD overcomes the fear of man.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 14:26Whoever fears the LORD has a secure fortress — the fear-of-man snare (v.25) is the negative counterpart to the fear-of-the-LORD security.
  • Acts 5:29We must obey God rather than human beings — the NT application of v.25's trust-in-the-LORD over fear-of-man principle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
29:15-27

MacArthur: Where there is no revelation, people cast off restraint; but blessed is the one who heeds wisdom's instruction (v.18). The proverb is about communal necessity of authoritative divine word, not individual vision. A community without prophetic revelation — without the word of God — loses the moral framework that holds behavior within covenant bounds.

29:25

MacArthur: Fear of man will prove to be a snare, but whoever trusts in the LORD is kept safe. The fear of human opinion is explicitly named as a trap. The person governed by the fear of man can never achieve moral consistency — their behavior shifts with whoever's opinion is dominant in the room. Trust in the LORD produces the stability that the fear of man can never give.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:15

Rod and reproof give wisdom, but a child left to himself disgraces his mother — the paired instruments of discipline (rod and reproof, physical correction and verbal instruction) work together. Alter notes the child left entirely to his own inclinations does not merely fail to become wise — he becomes a source of shame to the parent who abdicated the disciplinary responsibility.

29:27

The righteous detest the dishonest; the wicked detest the upright -- Alter notes the mutual-detesting observation closes ch.29 with the sharpest statement of the two-ways antithesis. The righteous and wicked are not merely different; they are structurally repellent to each other.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:15

A rod and a reproof give wisdom, but a child left to himself disgraces his mother -- Waltke notes the parental discipline pair (rod + reproof) works through both physical correction and verbal instruction together.

29:25

Fear of man will prove to be a snare, but whoever trusts in the LORD is kept safe -- Waltke notes the man-fearing/LORD-trusting antithesis is one of Proverbs' most practically useful. The fear of human opinion is specifically named as a trap.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
29:15

Calvin: A child left to himself brings shame -- the parental abdication of discipline is not kindness but dereliction. The undisciplined child develops a character that ultimately shames the parent.

29:25

Calvin: The fear of man is a snare -- no analysis of temptation is more accurate. The fear of what others will think is one of the most powerful forces that prevents covenant faithfulness.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
29:15

The NET note on rod and reproof together discusses how the two instruments of correction function in tandem throughout the Proverbs rod-passages. The physical correction (shevet) addresses the body; the reproof (tokhachah) addresses the mind and will. Both are required because character formation is both somatic and rational — neither alone is sufficient.

29:25

NET: fear of man (cherdath adam) -- the note traces the man-fearing trap through the OT. The person whose behaviour is primarily governed by human opinion has made a fundamental epistemological error about who ultimately matters.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions, especially Egyptian.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs — one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.
The editorial activity shows Proverbs as a living, curated collection.

Reception History

Virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics — the practical outworking of the fear of the LORD.
Practical wisdom
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, governance, and social teaching.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

Proverbs 29:18 and Personal Vision: A Misapplication?
About prophetic revelation, not personal goals
Waltke, most OT scholars
Chazon is consistently used for prophetic/divine revelation throughout the OT (Isa 1:1; Ezek 12:22-24; Dan 8:1). The verse addresses the communal need for authoritative divine word, not individual goal-setting.
Can be legitimately extended to personal vision
Some application-focused preachers
While the primary meaning is prophetic revelation, the principle that purposeful direction from God prevents aimlessness can be legitimately applied to the individual level.
The primary exegetical meaning is clear: prophetic divine revelation is necessary for social order. The modern 'vision-casting' application is a legitimate extension that requires acknowledging the distance from the primary meaning.
