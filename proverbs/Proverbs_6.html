Proverbs 6

Four Warnings; Seven Abominations; The Cost of Adultery

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–15 — Four Warnings: Surety, Laziness, and the Troublemaker
1My son, if you have put up security for your neighbor, if you have shaken hands in pledge for a stranger,2you have been trapped by what you said, ensnared by the words of your mouth.3So do this, my son, to free yourself, since you have fallen into your neighbor's hands: Go—to the point of exhaustion— and give your neighbor no rest!4Allow no sleep to your eyes, no slumber to your eyelids.5Free yourself, like a gazelle from the hand of the hunter, like a bird from the snare of the fowler.6Go to the ant, you sluggard; consider its ways and be wise!7It has no commander, no overseer or ruler,8yet it stores its provisions in summer and gathers its food at harvest.9How long will you lie there, you sluggard? When will you get up from your sleep?10A little sleep, a little slumber, a little folding of the hands to rest—11and poverty will come on you like a thief and scarcity like an armed man.12A troublemaker and a villain, who goes about with a corrupt mouth,13who winks maliciously with his eye, signals with his feet and motions with his fingers,14who plots evil with deceit in his heart— he always stirs up conflict.15Therefore disaster will overtake him in an instant; he will suddenly be destroyed—without remedy.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

Chapter 6 collects four distinct warnings: financial surety (vv.1–5), laziness (vv.6–11), the troublemaker (vv.12–15), and the seven abominations (vv.16–19), before returning to the adulteress (vv.20–35). The ant passage (vv.6–11) is one of Proverbs' most famous — wisdom observed in creation, not just received in instruction. The ant has no supervisor, yet acts with perfect diligence. The sluggard is not just lazy; he is refusing to read the lessons written into creation.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 24:30–34The second ant passage — an extended narrative version of vv.9–11: the narrator walks past the sluggard's field and sees the thorns and weeds, drawing the same conclusion.
  • 2 Thess 3:10Paul's instruction: "the one who is unwilling to work shall not eat" echoes the Proverbs logic that diligence is connected to provision, and idleness to poverty.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1–5

The surety warning is the first financial instruction in Proverbs. Pledging security for a stranger's debt was one of the most common paths to financial ruin in the ancient world. The advice is not uncharitable — it is realistic about human nature and the structure of social obligation. If you have done this, the instructions are urgent: go immediately, exhaust yourself in getting free, allow no rest until the obligation is resolved.

6:6–8

Wisdom from the ant. Proverbs regularly finds wisdom in creation — the ant, the badger, the lizard (30:24–28). This is not incidental. Wisdom is woven into creation; the observant person can read it from the world around them. The ant's wisdom is specifically: it acts without external compulsion (no commander) and it acts with long-range vision (stores food for winter during summer). The sluggard has both problems: he needs external pressure to act, and he cannot think beyond the present moment.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:6–11

The ant passage is one of Proverbs' most sustained examples of observation-based wisdom. Alter notes the method: watch the ant, consider her ways, become wise. The ant has no overseer, commander, or ruler yet works diligently. The observation grounds the ethical instruction in nature rather than divine command — wisdom can be read directly from the observable world.

6:16–19

The seven-abominations list is a numerical proverb — a 'x, yes x+1' structure (six things the LORD hates, yes seven). Alter notes this is a standard ancient Near Eastern numerical device that creates suspense: you expect six, you get seven, and the seventh is the climax. The final item — 'one who stirs up conflict in a community' — is surprisingly social: the greatest abomination is not personal vice but community destruction.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:1–5

The surety warning opens with a practical legal concern: going guarantor for a neighbour's debt. Waltke notes this is the book's least 'spiritual' instruction — but it is wisdom because financial over-commitment is a form of the pride that assumes one can guarantee what is not in one's control. The practical and the theological are never separate in Proverbs.

6:16–19

The seven abominations list is one of Proverbs' most memorable passages. Waltke notes the seven (a number of completeness) are structured from body-part to action: haughty eyes → lying tongue → hands that shed innocent blood → heart that devises evil → feet that run to evil → false witness → one who stirs up discord. The progression from inner attitude (eyes, heart) to outer action (hands, feet) encodes the Proverbs moral psychology: character is the source of conduct.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:6–8

Calvin on the ant: 'The ant, a tiny creature without intelligence or authority, accomplishes more by industry than many humans accomplish with all their resources. God shows us in this small creature our own condemnation — we have a Creator, a Redeemer, a spirit, a conscience, and yet are outworked by an insect.'

6:16–17

Calvin on 'haughty eyes': 'The eyes are mentioned first because pride first betrays itself through the look. The proud man cannot conceal his contempt for others — it appears in his very gaze. This is the first of the abominations and the root of most of the others that follow.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:1

The NET note on 'if you have put up security for your neighbour' (im aravta lere'akha) explains the ancient Near Eastern surety system: a guarantor pledged their own assets against another person's debt. If the debtor defaulted, the guarantor was legally liable. The instruction is to extricate oneself from such commitments — the risk is disproportionate to the benefit.

6:16

The NET note on the 'six things the LORD hates, seven that are detestable to him' discusses the X / X+1 numerical pattern (also Prov 30:15–33; Amos 1–2; Job 5:19). The pattern suggests completeness with a climax — the seventh item is the culminating point. Here the social sin (sowing discord among brothers) is the apex of the list of individual sins.

Verses 16–35 — Seven Abominations; The Cost of Adultery
16There are six things the LORD hates, seven that are detestable to him:17haughty eyes, a lying tongue, hands that shed innocent blood,18a heart that devises wicked schemes, feet that are quick to rush into evil,19a false witness who pours out lies and a person who stirs up conflict in the community.20My son, keep your father's command and do not forsake your mother's teaching.21Bind them always on your heart; fasten them around your neck.22When you walk, they will guide you; when you sleep, they will watch over you; when you awake, they will speak to you.23For this command is a lamp, this teaching is a light, and correction and instruction are the way to life,24keeping you from your neighbor's wife, from the smooth talk of a wayward woman.25Do not lust in your heart after her beauty or let her captivate you with her eyes.26For a prostitute can be had for a loaf of bread, but another man's wife preys upon your very life.27Can a man scoop fire into his lap without his clothes being burned?28Can he walk on hot coals without his feet being scorched?29So is he who sleeps with another man's wife; no one who touches her will go unpunished.30People do not despise a thief if he steals to satisfy his hunger when he is starving.31Yet if he is caught, he must pay sevenfold, though it costs him all the wealth of his house.32But a man who commits adultery has no sense; whoever does so destroys himself.33Blows and disgrace are his lot, and his shame will never be wiped away.34For jealousy arouses a husband's fury, and he will show no mercy when he takes revenge.35He will not accept any compensation; he will refuse a bribe, however great it is.

Hebrew Word Study

שֶׁבַע תּוֹעֵבוֹת šebaʿ tôʿēbôt — seven abominations/detestable things: Tôʿēbāh (abomination, detestable thing) is one of the strongest words in the Hebrew vocabulary of moral disgust — used for idolatry, sexual perversion, and now these seven things. The list is structured as six + seven (a Hebrew numerical device for "a complete set"): it climaxes with "one who stirs up conflict" — the person whose characteristic work is destroying community.

אֵין חֵקֶר ʾên ḥēqer — no sense/lacks judgment: The adulterer "has no sense" (ḥāsar lēb, literally "lacks heart/mind"). This is Proverbs' standard diagnosis of the fool: not primarily wickedness but profound stupidity about how reality works. He thinks he is gaining something; he is destroying himself.

Context

The seven abominations list (vv.16–19) is notable for what tops it — haughty eyes — and what ends it: "a person who stirs up conflict in the community." The list traces an inward-to-outward movement: eyes (attitude), tongue (words), hands (violence), heart (planning), feet (action), false witness (speech), community conflict (social destruction). The adulteress passage (vv.20–35) that follows uses fire imagery (vv.27–28) to make the point graphically: adultery is picking up fire in your lap and expecting not to be burned.

Cross-Reference

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
    6:16–19

    The seven abominations are united by their relationship to community: they destroy the social fabric — through pride, deception, violence, scheming, impetuosity, false witness, and strife. The list moves from individual character (haughty eyes) to community destruction (stirs up conflict). God's hatred is particularly strong for things that tear apart the covenant community.

    6:32

    "A man who commits adultery has no sense." The Hebrew is stark: ḥāsar lēb — he lacks a heart, lacks mind, lacks understanding. Proverbs doesn't primarily frame adultery as a crime (though it is); it frames it as supreme stupidity. The man who does it doesn't understand what he's doing to himself. This is consistent with the book's entire approach: the fool is fundamentally blind.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    6:27–29

    The three comparisons (fire in bosom → burnt clothes; walking on coals → burnt feet; sleeping with neighbour's wife → not going unpunished) are one of Proverbs' finest sequences of analogical argument. Alter notes the first two are perfectly obvious physical causation; the third exploits the logical parallel. The obviousness of consequences 1 and 2 makes consequence 3 feel equally inevitable — which is the rhetorical point.

    6:34–35

    The husband's jealousy (qin'ah, zeal/jealousy) 'will not accept any compensation; he will refuse the bribe, however great it is.' Alter notes this is a surprising ending: throughout the chapter economic comparisons have governed the argument (surety, theft, value of wisdom). Now the one calculus that cannot be economic is the wronged husband's honour. No price can compensate it. The economic frame of the chapter breaks down at exactly the point where honour does.

    Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

    Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
    6:20–35

    The adultery section of ch.6 (vv.20–35) is the most economically argued of the book's adultery warnings. Waltke notes the comparison structure: thieves steal from hunger (v.30) and are treated with more mercy than the adulterer; but the adulterer destroys a soul (v.32), and the wronged husband's jealousy is implacable (v.34–35). The calculation is precisely inverted from the adulteress's implied offer of easy pleasure.

    6:32

    'A man who commits adultery has no sense; whoever does so destroys himself.' The Hebrew for 'has no sense' (chasar-lev) is literally 'lacks heart/mind.' Waltke notes the characterisation of the adulterer as 'heartless' is deliberate: the 4:23 command to 'guard your heart' is answered here by the portrait of the man who has, in the deepest sense, failed to do so. He is heartless — lacking the wisdom-orientation that the heart represents.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    6:32

    Calvin on 'destroys himself': 'The adulterer is not merely sinning against another but against his own soul — he is ruining the very faculty by which he could have chosen otherwise. This is the peculiar nature of sexual sin: it corrupts the judge (the heart) along with the action.' Calvin anticipates Paul's observation in 1 Cor 6:18 that sexual sin is uniquely self-destructive.

    6:34

    Calvin on the husband's jealousy: 'The wrath of the injured husband is a just and implacable anger — and this natural law of honour, which no bribe can satisfy, is itself a witness to the gravity of the sin. Even pagan law recognises what the adulterer pretends not to know: that this violation cannot be bought off.'

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    6:23

    The NET note on 'the reproofs of discipline are the way of life' (vetokhachot musar derekh chayyim) explains the word musar (discipline, instruction, correction) as the pathway — not merely a means to life but the road itself. Life is found in the disciplined process of wisdom formation, not at the end of it as a reward.

    6:32

    The NET note on chasar-lev ('lacking heart/mind') explains this is a standard Hebrew idiom for stupidity or moral obtuseness (also Prov 7:7; 9:4, 16; 10:13; 11:12; 12:11). The note traces the idiom: having a heart = wisdom orientation; lacking a heart = the absence of that orientation. The adulterer is not evil so much as fundamentally witless.

    People of the Chapter

    The ant
    Wisdom from creation
    No commander but perfectly diligent. Represents the internalized wisdom of someone who acts rightly without external compulsion.
    The sluggard
    Negative example
    Refuses to observe creation's lessons. Defined by delay and self-deception about consequences.

    Social destroyer
    The villain who uses body language to plot — winks, signals, motions. His work is conflict; his end is sudden destruction.

    Ancient Sources

    Numerical Sayings
    The "x, x+1" numerical pattern appears widely in ANE and OT wisdom literature. Amos uses it in his oracle series; Job uses it; Proverbs uses it here and in 30:15–31. The form signals a climactic list — the final item is the key one. Here the climax is "one who stirs up conflict," suggesting that social destruction is the ultimate expression of the vices listed.
    The numerical form places this list within a well-established literary tradition.
    Fire Imagery
    The fire imagery of vv.27–29 (scoop fire into lap, walk on hot coals) is one of Proverbs' most vivid rhetorical devices. It argues from obvious physical law to moral law: just as you cannot handle fire without being burned, you cannot commit adultery without being burned. The universe has a consistent structure.
    The fire argument functions as a physical analogy for moral law.

    Reception History

    Matt 5:28
    Jesus' instruction on lust: "anyone who looks at a woman lustfully has already committed adultery with her in his heart." This deepens v.25's "do not lust in your heart after her beauty" — Jesus moves the prohibition from action to intention, consistent with his development of OT ethics throughout the Sermon on the Mount.
    2 Thess 3:10
    Paul's "if a man will not work, he shall not eat" is the practical application of the ant/sluggard passage. The NT endorses Proverbs' connection of diligence to provision.

    Literary Structure

    The Ant: Wisdom from Nature — 6:6–11The ant passage is Proverbs' clearest example of its observational method: watch how the world works, extract the principle, apply it to human life. The ant has no overseer yet works; humans have a Creator, a conscience, and a community yet are often slothful. Wisdom observes the discrepancy and takes the lesser creature as teacher.
    The description of the adulterer as 'lacking heart' (chasar-lev) is Proverbs' most damning characterisation of sexual folly. It answers 4:23's apex command ('guard your heart') with its anti-type: the man who commits adultery has demonstrably failed to guard the heart. He is 'heartless' — lacking the wisdom-orientation that makes a person truly human in the Proverbs sense.

    Textual Notes

    6:22'When you walk, they will guide you'
    MT: behistallekhkha tancheh otkha (when you walk, she/they will guide you). The pronoun is ambiguous — 'she' (referring to wisdom/the father's commands) or 'they' (referring to the commandments). LXX: when you go about, it will lead you. Most English versions take 'they' = the commands of vv.20–21.
    The ambiguity between wisdom personified (she) and the commands (they) is unlikely to be accidental — in Proverbs, keeping the commands and following Lady Wisdom are the same act. The grammatical ambiguity encodes the theological identification: to follow the commandments is to follow wisdom herself.

    Scholarly Debates

    The Seven Abominations: A Moral Hierarchy or Illustrative List?
    Illustrative, not exhaustive or hierarchical
    Most commentators
    The seven items are representative examples of the character dispositions the LORD finds detestable — not an exhaustive moral catalogue or a ranked hierarchy. The numerical structure (six/seven) signals completeness by example rather than completeness by enumeration.
    The final item is the climax: communal sin is the greatest
    Waltke, some commentators
    The X/X+1 structure places the final item (stirring up discord among brothers) as the climactic abomination. The movement from individual sins (lying, murder, deceit) to the communal sin of divisiveness suggests that covenant community destruction is worse than any individual sin — a counter-intuitive priority that Proverbs articulates in several places.
    The numerical structure strongly supports reading the final item as a climax rather than an afterthought. Proverbs' consistent concern for community (the assembly, the neighbour, brothers) throughout the book makes the climactic placement of communal discord coherent with the book's broader social ethics. Individual sins are serious; sins that destroy the community are uniquely serious.

    Theological Themes Tracker

    Proverbs 6 is the book's most practically diverse chapter — surety, laziness, seven abominations, adultery — united by the underlying question: what does it look like to be truly foolish? The lazy man, the dishonest man, the community-destroyer, and the adulterer all share the 'chasar-lev' character: they lack the wisdom-orientation that the heart must be guarded for.
