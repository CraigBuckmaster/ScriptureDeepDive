Proverbs 10

The Solomonic Proverbs Begin: Righteousness and Wickedness

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–16 — The Proverb Collection Begins: Righteousness and Speech
1The proverbs of Solomon: A wise son brings joy to his father, but a foolish son brings grief to his mother.2Ill-gotten treasures have no lasting value, but righteousness delivers from death.3The LORD does not let the righteous go hungry, but he thwarts the craving of the wicked.4Lazy hands make for poverty, but diligent hands bring wealth.5He who gathers crops in summer is a prudent son, but he who sleeps during harvest is a disgraceful son.6Blessings crown the head of the righteous, but violence overwhelms the mouth of the wicked.7The name of the righteous is used in blessings, but the name of the wicked will rot.8The wise in heart accept commands, but a chattering fool comes to ruin.9Whoever walks in integrity walks securely, but whoever takes crooked paths will be found out.10Whoever winks maliciously causes grief, and a chattering fool comes to ruin.11The mouth of the righteous is a fountain of life, but the mouth of the wicked conceals violence.12Hatred stirs up conflict, but love covers over all wrongs.13Wisdom is found on the lips of the discerning, but a rod is for the back of one who has no sense.14The wise store up knowledge, but the mouth of a fool invites ruin.15The wealth of the rich is their fortified city, but poverty is the ruin of the poor.16The wages of the righteous is life, but the earnings of the wicked are sin and death.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

Chapter 10 marks the transition from extended instruction (chs.1–9) to the individual proverb collection (chs.10–22:16). The literary form changes completely: instead of sustained addresses, we now have self-contained two-line observations, almost all structured as antithetical parallelism (wise/fool, righteous/wicked). Chapter 10 is dominated by speech and character: the mouth of the righteous is a fountain of life; the wicked conceals violence. The collection's dominant concern is how wisdom and folly show up in the texture of everyday life.

Cross-Reference

  • Jas 5:20James quotes the principle behind v.12 ("love covers a multitude of sins") — Peter does the same in 1 Pet 4:8. Both NT letters preserve the Proverbs principle as settled teaching.
  • Matt 12:34–37Jesus' words about the mouth in Matthew echo the repeated emphasis of ch.10: "out of the overflow of the heart the mouth speaks... by your words you will be acquitted, and by your words you will be condemned."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1

The book's second superscription — "The proverbs of Solomon" — marks the beginning of the main collection. The first proverb returns to the father-son theme: the wise son makes his father glad; the foolish son is a sorrow to his mother. The entire preceding discourse (chs.1–9) is now condensed into a single observation: wisdom or folly will be visible in what you become, and your parents will know it.

10:12

"Hatred stirs up conflict, but love covers over all wrongs." The word "covers" (kāsāh) is significant — not ignorance or excuse but the deliberate act of covering, the way a sacrifice's blood covers sin. Love is the active force that absorbs and covers offence rather than inflaming it. James and Peter both quote this principle as definitive.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:1–32

Chapter 10 introduces the antithetical proverb form that dominates chs.10–15: two half-lines that present contrasting truths, usually with 'but' connecting them. Alter notes the antithetical proverb is formally elegant — it makes its point by juxtaposition, not explanation. The reader is trained to supply the implied principle that connects the two halves.

10:11

'The mouth of the righteous is a fountain of life, but the mouth of the wicked conceals violence.' The fountain/violence antithesis is characteristic of ch.10's speech-ethics: what comes from the mouth reveals and enacts what is in the heart. Alter notes the physicality of 'fountain of life' — it is not merely true speech but life-giving speech, water that sustains.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:1–5

Chapter 10 opens the main proverb collection with the family-centred contrast: wise son/foolish son, diligent/slack. Waltke notes the transition from the extended wisdom poems of chs.1–9 to the individual proverbs of chs.10–31 is abrupt but theologically continuous — the proverbs are applications of the framework the poems established. The father-son theme that opened ch.1 returns immediately.

10:12

'Hatred stirs up conflict, but love covers over all wrongs.' One of Proverbs' most theologically weighted individual proverbs. Waltke notes 'covers' (kasa) is not denial or concealment but the active overlooking of offences in the service of relationship. The same verb is used for the Passover blood 'covering' sin. Love that covers is not naive but redemptive.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:3

Calvin: 'God does not allow the righteous to hunger — not because the righteous always have abundance, but because whatever they have, however little, is blessed and sufficient. The contrast with the wicked is not necessarily material but teleological: the wicked's getting always ultimately fails; the righteous' having, even when modest, satisfies.'

10:12

Calvin on love covering all wrongs: 'This is the love that Paul describes in 1 Corinthians 13 — love that suffers long and is kind, that covers a multitude of sins (1 Pet 4:8 quotes this proverb). This covering is not complicity in evil but the active choice to preserve relationship rather than prosecute every offence.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:1

The NET note on 'A wise son brings joy to his father, but a foolish son grief to his mother' notes the deliberate asymmetry: the joy goes to the father, the grief to the mother. Some commentators find significance in the gender distribution; others read it as simple variation. The note suggests the antithesis is primarily about the child's wisdom affecting both parents, with the gender distinction being stylistic rather than theological.

10:11

The NET note on 'the mouth of the righteous is a fountain of life' discusses the water imagery for life-giving speech throughout Proverbs (10:11; 13:14; 14:27; 16:22). The fountain is a spring that continually produces fresh water — life-giving speech is not occasional but continuous, not a special act but a character quality that flows from a rightly formed heart.

Verses 17–32 — The Tongue; Standing Firm; Final Security
17Whoever heeds discipline shows the way to life, but whoever ignores correction leads others astray.18Whoever conceals hatred with lying lips and spreads slander is a fool.19Sin is not ended by multiplying words, but the prudent hold their tongues.20The tongue of the righteous is choice silver, but the heart of the wicked is of little value.21The lips of the righteous nourish many, but fools die for lack of sense.22The blessing of the LORD brings wealth, without painful toil for it.23A fool finds pleasure in wicked schemes, but a person of understanding delights in wisdom.24What the wicked dread will overtake them; what the righteous desire will be granted.25When the storm has swept by, the wicked are gone, but the righteous stand firm forever.26As vinegar to the teeth and smoke to the eyes, so are sluggards to those who send them.27The fear of the LORD adds length to life, but the years of the wicked are cut short.28The prospect of the righteous is joy, but the hopes of the wicked come to nothing.29The way of the LORD is a refuge for the blameless, but it is the ruin of those who do evil.30The righteous will never be uprooted, but the wicked will not remain in the land.31From the mouth of the righteous comes the fruit of wisdom, but a perverse tongue will be silenced.32The lips of the righteous know what finds favor, but the mouth of the wicked only what is perverse.

Hebrew Word Study

כֶּסֶף נִבְחָר kesep nibḥār — choice silver: The tongue of the righteous is choice silver — nibḥār, carefully selected, of the highest quality. Speech, in Proverbs, is a form of wealth. The righteous person's words have genuine value; the wicked person's heart (not just words) is worth little. The comparison of speech to precious metal is the book's consistent valuation of what we say.

עֵץ חַיִּים ʿēṣ ḥayyîm — tree of life (implied): V.21: "the lips of the righteous nourish many." The image of righteous speech as nourishment connects to the tree of life and to Wisdom's feast in ch.9. The righteous person, through their words, becomes a source of life for others.

Context

The second half of ch.10 continues the emphasis on speech (vv.18–21, 31–32) while introducing the stability contrast: the righteous stand firm forever (v.25); the wicked are swept away when the storm comes. This is not naive optimism about the short term but a claim about the ultimate structure of reality. The storm image is eschatological as well as temporal — in the final accounting, the righteous are rooted; the wicked are gone. V.27's "fear of the LORD adds length to life" returns the chapter to the book's governing principle.

Cross-Reference

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
    10:19

    "Sin is not ended by multiplying words, but the prudent hold their tongues." This is one of Proverbs' most practical insights. The one who speaks too much inevitably sins with their mouth — not because each word is carefully chosen to harm, but because volume creates opportunity for error. The wise person's self-restraint in speech is not timidity but precision.

    10:25

    "When the storm has swept by, the wicked are gone, but the righteous stand firm forever." The storm image carries eschatological weight. This is not a promise of worldly stability in every season — the righteous suffer too. It is a claim about the ultimate outcome. When everything is stripped away by the final storm, what remains? The person whose life was built on the fear of the LORD. This is the NT's "rock vs sand" parable (Matt 7:24–27) in condensed form.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    10:17–21

    The speech-ethics cluster of vv.17–21 is structurally significant. Alter notes the movement from the general (instruction accepted/rejected, v.17) to the specific speech organs (lying lips, v.18; chattering fool, v.19; silver/refined gold, vv.19–20) to the final evaluation (the tongue of the righteous feeds many; the fool dies for lack of sense, v.21). The progression moves from individual character to social impact.

    10:24–25

    The 'whirlwind' image of v.25 is one of Proverbs' most dramatic: the wicked swept away by the tempest, but the righteous standing as an eternal foundation. Alter notes the spatial contrast — swept away vs. standing on an everlasting foundation — encodes the entire Proverbs cosmology: the wicked have no root; the righteous are planted in the order of reality itself.

    Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

    Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
    10:17–32

    The second half of ch.10 intensifies the speech-ethics theme. Waltke notes vv.18–21 form a cluster on the lips and tongue that covers lying, hatred, chattering, and the valuable vs. worthless mouth. The cluster establishes speech as the primary arena in which wisdom and folly are most directly expressed: what you say tells what you are.

    10:27

    'The fear of the LORD adds length to life, but the years of the wicked are cut short.' The fear-of-the-LORD theme from the prologue appears here in the proverb collections — Waltke notes this is not coincidence but design. The prologue's thesis is woven into the collection's fabric, appearing as individual proverbs that apply the foundational principle to specific observations about life.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    10:19

    Calvin: 'In the multitude of words there wanteth not sin — this is one of the most practically important observations in Proverbs. The person who talks too much will inevitably say something harmful, false, or foolish. The discipline of speech is one of wisdom's most concrete daily practices.' James 1:19 ('let every person be quick to hear, slow to speak') is a NT application of this proverb.

    10:27

    Calvin on 'the fear of the LORD adds length to life': 'This is not a mechanical guarantee of long life for the religious — Scripture knows too many faithful martyrs for that. The principle is true generally: the life ordered by wisdom is more likely to avoid the self-destructive consequences of folly. The general principle admits specific exceptions while remaining statistically true.'

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    10:19

    The NET note on 'sin is not absent in the multitude of words' (bevrov devarim lo yechdalpe'sha) discusses the syntax: 'the multitude of words' is the subject; 'sin is not absent' is the predicate. The note cites Eccl 5:3 and Jas 3:2–8 as parallel warnings. The application is both personal (guard your speech) and social (distrust those who talk without ceasing).

    10:25

    The NET note on 'the righteous have an everlasting foundation' (vetsadiq yesod olam) discusses the foundation image. Yesod olam (eternal/everlasting foundation) occurs in rabbinic literature as a title for the extremely righteous person (tzaddik yesod olam). The cosmic resonance is intentional: the righteous person is not merely stable but grounded in the eternal order of things — in God himself.

    Ancient Sources

    Antithetical Parallelism
    The dominant form of chs.10–15 is antithetical parallelism: a positive statement followed by its negative contrast (wise/fool, righteous/wicked, diligent/lazy). This form does not merely state a difference — it creates the difference vividly in the mind of the hearer. The proverb is a rhetorical instrument that sharpens moral perception.
    Antithetical parallelism is the defining literary form of the Solomonic collection.
    Proverb Collections in ANE
    Proverb collections structured as numbered sayings appear in Sumerian (Instructions of Shuruppak), Egyptian (Amenemope, Ptahhotep), and Aramaic (Ahiqar) traditions. The Solomonic collection in chs.10–22:16 is the most extensive wisdom collection in the OT and compares in scope with the largest ANE examples.
    The Solomonic collection is one of the longest wisdom collections known from the ancient world.

    Reception History

    Jas 5:20 / 1 Pet 4:8
    Both NT letters quote the principle of v.12 ("love covers sins"). It became one of the most cited Proverbs in early Christian literature, applied to church discipline, forgiveness, and community life.
    Matt 7:24–27
    The parable of the wise and foolish builders is the NT development of v.25: "when the storm has swept by, the wicked are gone, but the righteous stand firm forever." Jesus applies the storm image to the eschatological judgment.

    Literary Structure

    The Antithetical Proverb: Form as Theology — 10:1–32The antithetical proverb form of chs.10–15 presents the world as binary: wise/foolish, righteous/wicked, speech that gives life/speech that conceals violence. The form itself is a theological claim: there are two ways, and they have different destinations. The aesthetic elegance of the antithesis is the form's contribution to wisdom pedagogy — truth through juxtaposition.
    Righteous and Wicked: The Antithetical Structure10:3The LORD does not let the godly go hungry, but he thwarts the craving of the wicked. The chapter's antithetical structure -- righteous/wicked in almost every verse -- is the covenant's description of how moral reality is ordered.
    Proverbs 10's sustained cluster on speech (the mouth as fountain of life, lying lips, chattering fool, silver tongue, life-giving words) establishes speech as the primary arena where wisdom and folly are most visibly expressed. What you say tells who you are — and what you say to others enacts what you are for them. Speech is both a character indicator and a character-forming practice.

    Textual Notes

    10:12'Love covers all wrongs'
    MT: al kol pesha'im tekhaseh ahavah (over all transgressions love covers). LXX: pantas de tous mē philoneikountas kalyptei philia (friendship covers all who are not contentious). 1 Pet 4:8 quotes a form of this proverb: 'love covers a multitude of sins.' The verb kasa (to cover) is the same root as kapporet (mercy seat) — covering in the OT is associated with atonement.
    The NT quotation in 1 Pet 4:8 establishes this proverb as a principle of the new covenant community — love that covers is the community's answer to the multiplication of offences. The atonement resonance (same root as kapporet) may be intentional: the love that covers wrongs participates in the same covering dynamic as the Day of Atonement.

    Scholarly Debates

    The Retribution Principle in Proverbs: Doctrine or Generalisation?
    A generally reliable principle, not an absolute guarantee
    Waltke, most evangelical scholars
    Proverbs' consistent pairing of righteousness-with-blessing and wickedness-with-consequence is a generally reliable observation about how the ordered world works, not an absolute theological guarantee. The book of Job exists precisely to address cases where the principle appears to fail. Proverbs itself acknowledges exceptions (e.g., the wicked who prosper temporarily).
    A theological doctrine about God's moral governance
    Some traditional interpreters
    The retribution pairings in Proverbs reflect God's active moral governance of the world — not merely statistical regularity but divine justice. Every antithetical proverb is ultimately a statement about God's character: he rewards the righteous and punishes the wicked. The exceptions are apparent, not real.
    A idealised picture that Proverbs itself qualifies
    Fox, Longman
    Proverbs describes the world as it ought to be and generally is, but with awareness that it is not always so. The book's own qualifications (ch.30's limitations of wisdom; the 'better than' proverbs that qualify simplistic prosperity theology) show the tradition's self-awareness about the principle's limits.
    The generalisation reading is most consistent with the biblical wisdom tradition as a whole (Proverbs, Job, Ecclesiastes together). Proverbs states the general principle; Job interrogates it; Ecclesiastes relativises it. The three books together produce a wisdom theology that is honest about the principle's limits without abandoning it as a real, generally reliable observation about God's moral order.

    Theological Themes Tracker

    Proverbs 10 launches the main collection with its governing antithesis — righteous vs. wicked — applied systematically to family, economics, and speech. The chapter's most concentrated theme is speech ethics: the mouth is the most direct instrument of wisdom or folly in daily communal life. The fear of the LORD that the prologue established as foundational reappears (v.27) as a practical proverb about the quality of life it produces.
