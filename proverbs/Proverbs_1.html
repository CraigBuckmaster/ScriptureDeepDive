Proverbs 1

The Prologue; Two Paths; Lady Wisdom Speaks

Themes

Author: Solomon ben David, c.990–931 BC. Israel's third king, son of David and Bathsheba. Credited by Scripture with 3,000 proverbs and 1,005 songs (1 Kgs 4:32). His wisdom was divinely given — not merely accumulated experience but a gift granted at Gibeon (1 Kgs 3:5–14). He built the Temple, forged commercial alliances across the ancient world, and attracted foreign dignitaries to Jerusalem including the Queen of Sheba (1 Kgs 10). Proverbs 1–9 are the most clearly Solomonic; chs. 25–29 were copied by the men of Hezekiah (25:1); chs. 30–31 are attributed to Agur and Lemuel respectively. When written: The core Solomonic material (chs. 1–9, 10–22:16) dates to Solomon's reign, c.970–931 BC. The final compilation and editing likely continued through the Hezekiah period (c.715–686 BC), when his scribes copied additional Solomonic proverbs (25:1). The book reached its present form sometime in the late monarchic or early post-exilic period. What prompted it: Proverbs is a father's sustained address to a son standing on the threshold of adult life. The driving question is: How does a young person navigate the real world — its temptations, its social pressures, its competing voices — without being destroyed? The book's answer is not a list of rules but a vision of reality: the world was made with Wisdom woven into it (ch. 8), and the person who aligns their life with that grain will flourish; the person who fights it will be broken by it. "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7) is the thesis of the entire book.

Verses 1–7 — The Prologue: Purpose and Thesis
1The proverbs of Solomon son of David, king of Israel:2for gaining wisdom and instruction; for understanding words of insight;3for receiving instruction in prudent behavior, doing what is right and just and fair;4for giving prudence to those who are simple, knowledge and discretion to the young—5let the wise listen and add to their learning, and let the discerning get guidance—6for understanding proverbs and parables, the sayings and riddles of the wise.7The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge, but fools despise wisdom and instruction.

Hebrew Word Study

יִרְאַת יְהוָה yirʾat yhwh — fear of the LORD: The governing phrase of the entire book. Yirʾāh is not terror but reverential awe — a recognition of who God is that reshapes all perception of reality. It is not the beginning of wisdom in the sense of a first step after which one moves on; it is the beginning in the sense of the foundational principle without which nothing else is rightly understood.

חָכְמָה ḥokmāh — wisdom: Not abstract philosophical wisdom but practical skill in living — the ability to navigate life in alignment with created order. The same word is used of Bezalel's craftsmanship (Exod 31:3). Wisdom is both intellectual and practical: it sees truly and acts accordingly.

מוּסָר mûsār — instruction/discipline: From the root yāsar (to chastise, correct, discipline). Wisdom is not merely received; it is formed through correction. The willingness to receive mûsār — to accept correction from father, teacher, and ultimately from God — is the distinguishing mark of the wise over the fool.

Context

The prologue (vv.1–7) is the hermeneutical key to the entire book. It announces who wrote it, who it is for (the simple, the young — but also the wise, v.5), and what it aims to produce: not just moral behaviour but a way of perceiving reality. The thesis statement is v.7: the fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge. Everything that follows is an elaboration of this claim.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:2–4

Six purpose statements in vv.2–4 define the book's aims: wisdom (ḥokmāh), instruction (mûsār), understanding (bînāh), prudent behaviour, righteousness, and discretion. These are not separate virtues but facets of a single integrated vision of human flourishing — the life lived in alignment with the way things actually are.

1:7

"The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge." This verse is the thesis of Proverbs, Psalms 111, and in a sense the entire wisdom tradition. It is not saying that religion is the starting point of education. It is saying that the right relationship to God — worshipful, submissive, trusting — is the epistemological foundation without which reality cannot be rightly read. The fool of Proverbs is not primarily stupid; he is someone who has attempted to navigate life without this foundation.

Verses 8–19 — The First Address: The Enticement of Violent Men
8Listen, my son, to your father's instruction and do not forsake your mother's teaching.9They are a garland to grace your head and a chain to adorn your neck.10My son, if sinful men entice you, do not give in to them.11If they say, "Come along with us; let's lie in wait for innocent blood, let's ambush some harmless soul;12let's swallow them alive, like the grave, and whole, like those who go down to the pit;13we will get all sorts of valuable things and fill our houses with plunder;14cast in your lot with us, and we will share a common purse"—15my son, do not go along with them, do not set foot on their paths;16for their feet rush into evil, they are swift to shed blood.17How useless to spread a net where every bird can see it!18These men lie in wait for their own blood; they ambush only themselves!19Such are the paths of all who go after ill-gotten gain; it takes away the life of those who get it.

Context

The first address (vv.8–19) introduces the book's characteristic form: a father speaking to his son. This is not merely a literary device. Proverbs is rooted in the family as the primary institution of wisdom transmission — both parents are named (v.8). The specific temptation addressed here is the gang of violent men who offer belonging, wealth, and excitement. The counter-argument is not moral but practical: they are setting a trap that will catch themselves (v.18). The fool's error is not just wickedness but stupidity — he cannot see what is obvious.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 1:1The first psalm parallels the first address: "Blessed is the one who does not walk in step with the wicked or stand in the way that sinners take." Both texts begin with the fork between the two ways.
  • Rom 3:15–17Paul quotes vv.15–16 in his indictment of humanity in Romans 3 — Proverbs' description of the violent becomes part of the universal diagnosis of sin.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:10

"If sinful men entice you" — the word for entice (pātāh) is the same used for seduction in Samson's story and elsewhere. The violent gang uses the language of friendship, belonging, and shared adventure. This is the anatomy of peer pressure: it appeals to the legitimate desire for community and replaces it with a counterfeit that destroys. The father's instruction is the true offer of belonging.

1:17–18

The ironic reversal: the hunters become the hunted. The men who lie in wait for others are, in reality, lying in wait for themselves. This is Proverbs' consistent logic: wickedness is not merely wrong — it is self-destructive. The universe is built so that the foolish path leads back on its walker.

Verses 20–33 — Lady Wisdom Speaks: The Open Offer and Its Rejection
20Out in the open wisdom calls aloud, she raises her voice in the public square;21on top of the wall she cries out, at the city gate she makes her speech:22"How long will you who are simple love your simple ways? How long will mockers delight in mockery and fools hate knowledge?23Repent at my rebuke! Then I will pour out my thoughts to you, I will make known to you my teachings.24But since you refuse to listen when I call and no one pays attention when I stretch out my hand,25since you disregard all my advice and do not accept my rebuke,26I in turn will laugh when disaster strikes you; I will mock when calamity overtakes you—27when calamity overtakes you like a storm, when disaster sweeps over you like a whirlwind, when distress and trouble overwhelm you.28"Then they will call to me but I will not answer; they will look for me but will not find me,29since they hated knowledge and did not choose to fear the LORD.30Since they would not accept my advice and spurned my rebuke,31they will eat the fruit of their ways and be filled with the fruit of their schemes.32For the waywardness of the simple will kill them, and the complacency of fools will destroy them;33but whoever listens to me will live in safety and be at ease, without fear of harm."

Hebrew Word Study

חָכְמוֹת ḥokmôt — Wisdom (personified): The plural form (lit. "wisdoms") used for the personified figure — a grammatical intensification suggesting the fullness or completeness of wisdom. Wisdom here is not an abstraction but a person who speaks, calls, is rejected, and promises consequences. The NT identifies this personified Wisdom with Christ (1 Cor 1:30).

שׁוּבוּ šûbû — repent / turn back: The standard OT word for repentance — a turning, a change of direction. Wisdom's offer is not passive; she calls for a concrete change of trajectory. The offer is open now; the passage warns it will close.

Context

Lady Wisdom's public speech (vv.20–33) is one of the most dramatic passages in Proverbs. She does not speak in the classroom or the temple but in the public square, at the city gate — the busiest, most exposed place in ancient city life. Her message is urgent and eventually terrible: the time for response is now; those who mock and refuse will find that calamity comes, and when it does, she will not answer. The laughter of v.26 is not cruelty but the judicial irony of consequences — the same mockers who laughed at wisdom will find calamity laughing back.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:20–21

Wisdom in the street. The gospel offer of wisdom is public, accessible, universal — not hidden in esoteric tradition or available only to the elite. She is at the city gate where everyone passes. The tragedy of the fool is not lack of access but lack of attention.

1:24–28

The terrible reversal: "Then they will call to me but I will not answer." This is not an arbitrary divine rejection but the inevitable consequence of hardened refusal. There is a point at which persistent rejection of wisdom closes the door. Proverbs is remarkably sober about this — wisdom is not infinitely patient in the face of deliberate, sustained mockery. The NT parallel is the parable of the ten virgins: "I don't know you" (Matt 25:12).

Ancient Sources

ANE Instruction Literature
Proverbs shares the "father-to-son" address form with Egyptian wisdom texts: the Instruction of Amenemope, the Instruction of Ptahhotep, and the Instruction of Any. These texts address proper conduct, speech, and relationships. Proverbs shares the form but radically recentres it on the LORD rather than on pharaoh or social advancement.
The parallels in form highlight the theological distinction in content.
Personification of Wisdom
Lady Wisdom in vv.20–33 has no precise parallel in ANE literature, though Egyptian Maʿat (order/truth) is sometimes personified. The biblical figure is more dramatically personal — she speaks, calls, is rejected, and responds with judicial consequence.
Lady Wisdom is a distinctively biblical development of the ANE wisdom tradition.

Reception History

NT Christology
Paul identifies Christ as "the wisdom of God" (1 Cor 1:30) and "in whom are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge" (Col 2:3). Lady Wisdom's cry in the streets is heard in Jesus' public teaching in the Temple courts.
Jewish tradition
The Talmud (b. Shabb. 30b) records debates about Proverbs' canonical status but ultimately affirms it. Ben Sira (Sirach 24) extensively develops the personified Wisdom motif, identifying Wisdom with Torah.
Augustine
Used Prov 1:7 as the foundation of his educational philosophy: true learning begins with the right posture before God. "Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee" (Confessions) echoes the book's thesis.

Theological Themes Tracker

Proverbs 1 opens the book with its full theological programme: the fear of the LORD is the foundation of knowledge; two paths diverge from youth; Lady Wisdom calls publicly; the fool who refuses her will be abandoned to his own consequences. Everything in chs.2–9 unfolds from this chapter.
