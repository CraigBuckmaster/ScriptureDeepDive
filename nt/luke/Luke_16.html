Luke 16

Wealth, Faithfulness, and Eternity

Verses 1‑13 — The Shrewd Manager; You Cannot Serve Both God and Money

Greek Word Study

phronimōs phro-NI-mos — shrewdly / wisely / with practical wisdom: The manager is commended not for dishonesty but for phronimōs (v.8) — practical cleverness, the capacity to assess a situation and act decisively. Jesus points to this worldly trait as something the "children of light" conspicuously lack in managing their own affairs.

mamōnas ma-MO-nas — mammon / money / wealth: The Aramaic loanword (vv.9, 11, 13) personifies wealth as a rival lord. Jesus does not say money is evil in itself but that it has the character of a master — it demands loyalty, shapes priorities, and competes with God for the allegiance of the heart.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:8

"The master commended the dishonest manager because he had acted shrewdly" — the commendation is for shrewdness, not ethics. Jesus is using a negative example positively: if a scoundrel can act with decisive creativity to secure his future, how much more should disciples act with at least equal shrewdness in securing their eternal future.

16:9

"Use worldly wealth to gain friends for yourselves" — give generously now, so that when earthly wealth ends at death, you will have laid up relational and spiritual capital in heaven. The "friends" are the poor who have been served; the "eternal dwellings" are the heavenly welcome awaiting those who used wealth as a servant rather than serving it as a master.

16:13

"You cannot serve both God and money" — this is one of Jesus's most absolute statements. Money is not merely a tool; it is a master with its own demands, its own claim on loyalty, its own logic that subtly displaces God. The Pharisees' money-love (v.14) is the practical demonstration of what serving mammon looks like in a religious person.

16:17

"Easier for heaven and earth to disappear than for the least stroke of a pen to drop out of the Law" — the new epoch of the kingdom does not abrogate Torah but fulfils it. Jesus is the Law's completion, not its cancellation. The divorce saying that follows (v.18) is an example: Jesus intensifies the Law's original intent rather than relaxing it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:9

Calvin insists that Jesus is not advising the purchase of heaven with money. The "friends" who welcome us are not God and the angels who owe us for our generosity, but the poor whose intercessions on our behalf, whose testimony to our character, rises before God. Generosity does not purchase salvation; it demonstrates the transformed heart that salvation produces.

16:16-17

The two verses in sequence (the kingdom epoch has arrived / not one stroke of the Law will fail) correct two opposite misunderstandings: (1) the kingdom has nothing to do with the Law (wrong: the Law's deepest intentions are fulfilled in the kingdom); (2) the Law continues in its letter as before (wrong: the epoch has changed, though not the moral substance of the Law).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:8

The "master" who commends the manager could be the landowner in the parable or Jesus himself ("the Lord"). If Jesus is the one commending, the commendation is even more pointed. NA28's ambiguity is probably deliberate — the narrative keeps the layers of meaning open.

16:16

"Everyone is forcing their way into it" (NIV) — biazetai is middle or passive and can mean "everyone presses urgently into it" (positive) or "everyone is violently entering it" (negative, suggesting opposition). The ambiguity has generated extensive scholarly debate; most modern translations follow the active/positive reading.

Basil of Caesarea: "It is not possible to serve two masters whose commands are in direct opposition. God commands: despise earthly things, seek eternal ones. Money commands: seek earthly things, despise eternal ones. Where money rules, the love of God finds no place."

Verses 14‑18 — The Pharisees Ridiculed; The Law and the Kingdom
1415161718

Greek Word Study

porphyran por-PHY-ran — purple: The colour of imperial and priestly garments — extravagant luxury. Only the very wealthy could afford purple dye (extracted from murex shellfish). The detail places the rich man at the apex of economic privilege.

kolpon Abraam kol-PON ab-ra-AM — Abraham's bosom / Abraham's side: The metaphor (v.22) is of reclining at a banquet in the place of honour next to the host — the position of the most honoured guest. Lazarus, who had no place at any table in his lifetime, now reclines in the most honoured place at the eternal feast.

Context

[('Hades in Jewish Thought', "First-century Jewish understanding of the afterlife was varied. The Pharisees believed in resurrection; the Sadducees did not. The parable's picture of Hades — a place of the dead with different compartments for the righteous and the wicked, separated by an impassable chasm — draws on the apocalyptic tradition visible in 1 Enoch 22. Jesus uses the categories his audience recognised without necessarily making ontological claims about the precise geography of the afterlife."), ('"Someone Rising from the Dead"', 'The parable\'s closing punch — "they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead" (v.31) — is both Jesus\'s commentary on the unresponsive heart and Luke\'s retrospective commentary on the resurrection. The Pharisees who refused to believe in Jesus were not convinced even when he rose. The parable ends as both prediction and indictment.')]

Cross-Reference

  • Amos 6:1-7"Woe to you who are complacent in Zion... you feast and lounge... but you do not grieve over the ruin of Joseph" — the OT prophetic indictment of wealthy indifference that the rich man embodies.
  • 1 Enoch 22The apocalyptic text that provides the closest parallel to the parable's geography of the afterlife: different compartments for the righteous and wicked, separated before the final judgment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:19-21

The contrast is not between a good poor man and a bad rich man but between a man who had everything and a man who had nothing, at the same gate every day. The rich man's sin is not enumerated — it is the sin of not seeing. Lazarus was at his gate; the dogs noticed him. The rich man apparently did not.

16:25

"Son, remember" — Abraham calls him "son," recognising his covenant heritage. The rich man is not excluded from the covenant family; he is excluded by his own choices. The "good things" received in lifetime were not themselves the sin — their possession without compassionate use was.

16:26

"A great chasm has been set in place" — the permanence of the division after death is the parable's most sobering element. There is no purgatorial crossing, no second chance, no late repentance. The time of decision is the present life; the separation at death is final.

16:31

"They will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead" — this is the parable's most transparent reference to the resurrection. The Pharisees who demanded signs (11:16), who had Moses and the Prophets and rejected them, would not be convinced by the resurrection either. And they were not.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:22-23

Calvin insists that the parable teaches the reality of the soul's conscious existence between death and resurrection — against the doctrine of "soul sleep." Lazarus is comforted; the rich man is in agony. Both are conscious, both remember, both can converse. The intermediate state is a real state, not a condition of unconscious waiting.

16:29-31

"They have Moses and the Prophets" — Scripture is sufficient. The demand for miraculous confirmation of what Scripture already teaches is a sign of unbelief, not of genuine inquiry. If the word of God does not persuade, no miracle will. This is Calvin's foundational epistemology: the Spirit who inspired Scripture illumines the reading of Scripture; no additional external confirmation is required.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:22

"Angels carried him to Abraham's side" — the image of angels escorting the righteous dead is attested in Jewish literature (T. Asher 6:4-6; T. Abraham 20:12). Luke's Jesus uses the image without necessarily endorsing all the apocalyptic detail that surrounds it in the Jewish sources.

16:19

The rich man is unnamed in the earliest manuscripts. The tradition that gave him the name "Dives" (Latin for "rich man") developed in the Western church from the Vulgate's description. Some MSS add "Nineve" or other names. NA28 follows the unnamed form as original.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
16:23

Hades — not Gehenna (the place of final punishment) but the intermediate state of the dead. The distinction matters: the parable describes the period between death and final judgment, not the eternal state. However, the chasm suggests that the categories are already fixed.

16:26

Chasma mega estēraktai — "a great chasm has been fixed/established" (perfect passive). The perfect tense indicates a completed, settled action. The chasm is not being set; it has been set. The arrangement is permanent.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
16:20-21

Chrysostom: "He did not pass by Lazarus; he merely did not help him. This is the sin: not noticing the one lying at the gate. The beggar lay there day after day. The rich man went out and came in past him, and Lazarus became invisible to him through familiarity. This is how the comfortable develop the skill of not seeing."

16:31

Augustine: "Even if one rose from the dead, they would not believe — and so it proved. Christ rose, and many did not believe. The resurrection does not produce faith in those who have hardened themselves against the scriptures. Faith comes by hearing the word of God."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The Rich Man
Unnamed wealthy man
Named by tradition as "Dives" (Latin: rich man). His sin is absence of compassion rather than explicit wickedness — he was at his gate every day, but Lazarus was invisible to him. In Hades he still treats Lazarus as a servant to be sent on errands.
Lazarus
The named poor man
The only named character in any of Jesus's parables — his name (Greek form of Hebrew Eleazar: "God helps") is the parable's first theological statement. The man the world overlooked was named before God.
Abraham
Patriarch and judge
Abraham's role in the afterlife reflects Jewish apocalyptic tradition. He addresses the rich man as "son" — recognising his covenant identity — while maintaining the finality of the separation.

Ancient Sources

1 Enoch 22
The apocalyptic text describes Sheol divided into chambers for different categories of the dead — a righteous chamber and a wicked chamber, with those awaiting judgment in intermediate states.
The closest Jewish parallel to the parable's geography of Hades. Jesus uses categories familiar to his audience without necessarily endorsing every detail of the apocalyptic picture.
Amos 6:1-7
"Woe to you who are complacent in Zion... you eat lambs from the flock... but you do not grieve over the ruin of Joseph."
The prophetic indictment of luxurious indifference to suffering that the rich man embodies. Jesus's parable is a narrative dramatisation of Amos's prophetic charge.

Reception History

The Parable and Economic Ethics
The story of the rich man and Lazarus has been consistently invoked in Christian social ethics. Basil's famous Homily to the Rich (4th century) argued directly from this parable that surplus wealth belongs to the poor, not to the wealthy person who possesses it. Liberation theologians in the 20th century returned to it as a central text for the church's "preferential option for the poor."
"Dives and Lazarus" in English Literature
The parable entered English literature and folk tradition as one of the most familiar biblical stories. It appears in medieval morality plays, in Langland's Piers Plowman, and in the English folk ballad "Dives and Lazarus" (also known as "Come all you worthy Christians"). The name "Dives" passed into common usage as a synonym for the callously wealthy.

Literary Structure

Reversal — vv.19-25The parable's entire structure is built on reversal: the rich man's daily luxury becomes post-mortem agony; Lazarus's daily suffering becomes post-mortem comfort. The reversal is not mechanical (poverty does not automatically produce heaven) but reflects the logic of the beatitudes (6:20-26).
Irony — v.31The final line — "they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead" — is retrospective irony. Luke's reader knows that someone did rise from the dead, and many were not convinced. The parable's ending is both prediction and post-resurrection commentary.

Greek Word Study

oikonomos oi-KO-no-mos — manager / steward: The key figure of the parable of the shrewd manager (vv.1-9). Oikonomos (from oikos = house + nemō = to manage): the one entrusted with another's resources. The parable turns on the manager's recognition that the resources he has managed are not his own — and his shrewd use of them before the moment of reckoning. Jesus commends the worldly wisdom (not the dishonesty) of acting decisively with borrowed time and borrowed resources.

mamōnas ma-MO-nas — wealth / mammon (Aramaic loanword): "You cannot serve both God and Money (mamōnas)" (v.13). The Aramaic word for wealth/property — the personification of material possessions as a rival lord. The parable section (vv.1-13) is framed by two mamōnas sayings (vv.9, 13): wealth can be used in the service of eternal purposes, or it can become the object of ultimate allegiance. The choice is binary.

abyssos A-bys-sos — great chasm / abyss: "A great chasm has been fixed" (v.26) — chasma mega. The word carries eschatological finality: the separation between the rich man and Abraham's side is permanent, irreversible, and unbridgeable. Luke 16's parable is one of the NT's clearest statements of irreversible post-mortem destinies. The chasm is not the punishment; it is the permanent consequence of choices made before death.

Textual Notes

16:9"When it is gone" vs "when you fail"
The Greek hotan eklipē can mean "when it [worldly wealth] runs out/is gone" or "when you fail/die." NA28 follows the majority reading; some MSS and commentators read it as referring to the disciple's death rather than wealth's expiry.
Both readings make theological sense. The temporal reading (when wealth runs out) fits the immediate context of the steward parable; the personal reading (when you die) fits the broader context of 16:19-31.
16:19The rich man unnamed
Earliest MSS give no name; later traditions supply "Nineue," "Fineas," or (via the Latin tradition) "Dives." NA28 follows the unnamed form.
The unnamed form is almost certainly original. The tradition of naming the rich man developed to balance the named Lazarus; the unnamed form is more pointed — he who had everything has no name in eternity.

Scholarly Debates

Is the Parable Literal Theology or Narrative Illustration?
Literal afterlife geography
MacArthur, Bock, some evangelicals
The parable describes actual conditions in the intermediate state: conscious existence, recognition, memory, the chasm, and the finality of post-death conditions. Jesus is teaching doctrine through narrative.
Narrative illustration using conventional imagery
Green, Fitzmyer, Jeremias
Jesus uses the conventional Jewish imagery of Hades (from 1 Enoch, rabbinic tradition) to illustrate his point about wealth and poverty. The point is the reversal and the sufficiency of Scripture, not a map of the afterlife.
Most scholars hold a mediating position: the parable makes genuine theological claims (the finality of death's conditions, the reality of conscious existence after death) while using conventional imagery that should not be pressed for precise geographic or ontological detail.

Theological Themes Tracker

Luke 16 develops the chapter's wealth theology in two directions. The shrewd manager parable argues for the wise use of wealth before it expires — use what you have now to lay up eternal capital. The rich man and Lazarus illustrates the consequence of failing to do so: wealth enjoyed without compassion for the visible poor produces a post-mortem reversal of devastating completeness. Both parables converge on the same point: money is a master that competes with God, and how we use it now shapes what awaits us then. The chapter's closing twist — "if they do not listen to Moses and the Prophets, they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead" — indicts the Pharisees who have Scripture but refuse its demand.
