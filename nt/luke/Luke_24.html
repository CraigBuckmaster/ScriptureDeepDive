Luke 24

The Resurrection: Empty Tomb, Emmaus, and Ascension

Author

Luke, a Gentile physician (Col 4:14) and the most accomplished literary stylist in the New Testament. His Greek is the most polished in the NT corpus — modelled on the style of educated Hellenistic historians such as Thucydides and Polybius. Early and unanimous church tradition (Irenaeus c. AD 180, Clement of Alexandria, Origen, Eusebius, Jerome) identifies him as the author of both this Gospel and the Acts of the Apostles. He was a close companion of Paul (Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11, “only Luke is with me”), and the “we” passages in Acts (16:10–17; 20:5–15; 21:1–18; 27:1–28:16) confirm direct eyewitness participation in events he narrates. Luke is the only Gentile author in the canon. He explicitly describes his method in the prologue (1:1–4): careful investigation of all available tradition, consultation of eyewitnesses and ministers of the word, and orderly composition for a patron named Theophilus — a preface that conforms to the best conventions of Hellenistic historiography.

When written

c. AD 62–70, most likely during Paul’s two-year imprisonment in Caesarea (Acts 24:27, c. AD 57–59) or shortly thereafter, with publication before Acts (which presupposes it, Acts 1:1). A pre-AD 70 date is supported by the absence of any reference to the destruction of Jerusalem as a past event; the Gospel’s detailed prediction of the siege (21:20–24) reads as prophecy, not retrospective commentary. A minority of scholars favour AD 70–85, arguing that the precision of 21:20 reflects post-event knowledge. The two-volume work (Luke–Acts) is approximately 27,000 words — the largest single contribution to the NT by word count.

What prompted it

Luke writes for Theophilus (“friend of God”), probably a Gentile patron of high social standing (“most excellent,” Acts 1:1, a term used for Roman officials), who has already received some instruction in the faith but needs a reliable, ordered account to confirm its certainty (1:4). More broadly, Luke’s two-volume work addresses the expanding Gentile mission: How does the story of a Jewish Messiah become good news for all nations? His Gospel foregrounds the inclusion of the poor, women, Samaritans, and Gentiles; his Acts shows the gospel moving from Jerusalem to Rome. Luke also addresses a pastoral crisis: the delay of the Parousia. His travel narrative (9:51–19:28) reframes discipleship as a sustained journey of formation, not merely expectation of an imminent end. The Gospel’s persistent themes — prayer, joy, the Holy Spirit, reversal of fortune, universal salvation — all serve this dual apologetic and pastoral purpose.

Greek Word Study

apokalyptō / dianoigō a-po-ka-LYP-to / di-a-NOI-go — revealed / opened: Two verbs of opening that frame the chapter: "their eyes were opened" (diēnoichthēsan, v.31) and "he opened their minds" (diēnoixen, v.45). The resurrection does not merely remove an obstacle to sight — it actively opens what was closed. The same verb is used in Acts 16:14 for Lydia's heart opened by the Lord. Understanding Scripture and recognising the risen Christ require a divine opening.

dei dei — must / it is necessary: The word of divine necessity (v.26: "Did not the Messiah have to suffer?") that runs through Luke's Gospel (4:43; 9:22; 13:33; 17:25; 22:37). The resurrection proves that the "must" was fulfilled, not defeated. What seemed like catastrophe was divine necessity; what seemed like the end was the predetermined path to glory.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
24:5

"Why do you look for the living among the dead?" — the angels' question is the chapter's hermeneutical key. The tomb is the wrong place to look for the risen Christ. The disciples' confusion throughout Luke 24 stems from looking in wrong places: in the tomb (where he is not), in the category of "failed prophet" (24:19-21), in the class of ghost or spirit (24:37). Jesus himself re-educates them through Scripture and through meals.

24:21

"We had hoped that he was the one who was going to redeem Israel" — the most poignant confession of dashed hope in the Gospels. The disciples had expected a political messiah who would liberate Israel from Rome. The crucifixion had destroyed that hope. Their reconstruction of what happened — "it is the third day since all this took place" — shows they know the resurrection tradition but cannot connect it to their experience. The hope was correct; the category of fulfilment was wrong.

24:30-31

"He took bread, gave thanks, broke it and began to give it to them. Then their eyes were opened and they recognized him, and he disappeared from their sight." The recognition comes at the moment of eucharistic action — not during the Scripture exposition, not during conversation, but in the meal. Luke connects the risen Christ to the broken bread in a way that has shaped Christian eucharistic theology ever since: the Table is where he is known.

24:32

"Were not our hearts burning within us while he talked with us on the road and opened the Scriptures to us?" — the retrospective confirmation that the Scripture exposition was itself a resurrection experience. The burning heart is the mark of divine encounter in Scripture, even when the one speaking is not yet recognised. Christian preaching participates in this same dynamic.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:25-27

Calvin emphasises that Jesus rebukes their slowness to believe "all that the prophets have spoken" — not some of it, not the easy parts, but all of it. The passion was predicted; they had refused to hear it. The resurrection now forces the full reading of all the prophets, including the parts about suffering and rejection. The Scripture that they thought contradicted the messianic expectation was actually its clearest prediction.

24:30-31

Calvin connects the breaking of bread to both the Last Supper and to the ordinary meal of hospitality, deliberately avoiding an exclusively sacramental reading. The recognition of Christ at the table is a pattern for all meals that are received in faith and gratitude — the risen Christ is present wherever bread is broken with thankfulness and where Scripture has been opened.

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
24:13

"About seven miles from Jerusalem" — literally sixty stadia (about 7 miles or 11 km). The location of Emmaus is disputed: candidates include Moza (4 miles), Nicopolis/Emmaus Nicopolis (20 miles), and Qaloniyya (4 miles). The 60-stadia measurement (present in the majority of manuscripts) fits Moza or Qaloniyya; some MSS read 160 stadia, matching Nicopolis.

24:34

"The Lord has risen and has appeared to Simon" — this appearance to Peter alone is mentioned but not narrated in Luke. Paul lists it first among the resurrection appearances (1 Cor 15:5 — "he appeared to Cephas"). Its mention in 24:34 suggests Luke's readers were familiar with the tradition even without a full account.

24:11

Lēros — "nonsense, foolishness, idle tale." A strong word implying contempt or dismissal. The medical vocabulary of Luke-Acts uses lēros for hallucination or delirious speech. The disciples' initial response to the resurrection report is dismissal, not merely scepticism.

24:34

Ōphthē Simōni — "appeared to Simon." The aorist passive of horaō — "was seen by" or "appeared to." The same passive is used throughout Paul's list in 1 Cor 15:5. The passive form suggests that the appearances are initiated by Christ, not by the disciples' seeing.

Places

Jerusalem -- Upper Room
31.7726 N, 35.2290 E
The assembled disciples in Jerusalem (24:33, 36) -- most likely the same upper room used for the Last Supper (22:12) in the upper city. Here the risen Jesus appeared suddenly among them, showed his hands and feet, ate broiled fish, opened the Scriptures, and commissioned them as witnesses to all nations beginning at Jerusalem.
Bethany / Mount of Olives -- Ascension Site
31.7764 N, 35.2590 E
Jesus led the disciples to the vicinity of Bethany (24:50) on the eastern slope of the Mount of Olives, approximately 2km from Jerusalem. Here, while blessing them with upraised hands, he was taken up into heaven. Acts 1:9-12 locates the Ascension on the Mount of Olives. The ridge from which Jesus wept over Jerusalem (19:41) and delivered the Olivet Discourse (21:37) is also the site of his departure -- and promised return.
Temple -- Conclusion
31.7781 N, 35.2354 E
The disciples returned to Jerusalem and "stayed continually at the temple, praising God" (24:52-53). The Gospel that opened in the Temple with Zechariah's vision (1:5-23) closes in the Temple with sustained praise. Between those brackets, Jesus has replaced the Temple as the locus of divine presence; the disciples now inhabit that space as the Spirit-filled community he is building.

Greek Word Study

martyres mar-TY-res — witnesses: The final commission (v.48) uses the Greek word from which "martyr" derives — originally "witness" in a legal sense. The disciples are appointed as legal witnesses to the resurrection: they have seen, touched, and eaten with the risen Jesus. Their testimony is not opinion or interpretation; it is eyewitness evidence. The Acts narrative will show what witness costs in practice.

eparchē ep-ar-CHE — promise / what was promised: The "promise" of the Father (v.49) that the disciples are to wait for is the Holy Spirit — explicitly identified in Acts 1:4-5 as the content of this promise. Luke ends his Gospel pointing toward the Spirit; Acts begins with the Spirit's arrival (Pentecost). The ascension is the pivot between the two volumes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
24:39

"It is I myself! Touch me and see; a ghost does not have flesh and bones, as you see I have." — the resurrection is physical. Not a spiritual vision, not a subjective experience, not a metaphor for the disciples' continued community or inspiration — flesh and bones, capable of being touched, visible to multiple witnesses simultaneously. Luke's anti-docetic emphasis here is consistent with 1 John 4:2 and the early church's insistence on bodily resurrection against Gnostic spiritualisation.

24:44-47

The hermeneutical revolution: "everything written about me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets and the Psalms must be fulfilled." The entire Hebrew canon is now read as a single narrative about the Messiah who suffers and rises. This is not a finding but a revelation: "then he opened their minds so they could understand the Scriptures." The capacity to read Scripture in this way is a gift of the risen Christ, not an achievement of human study.

24:47

"Repentance for the forgiveness of sins will be preached in his name to all nations, beginning at Jerusalem" — the resurrection is not the conclusion of Jesus's story but its launch-pad. The death and resurrection that the disciples had hoped would "redeem Israel" now turns out to be the means of redeeming all nations. The mission that begins in Jerusalem will reach Rome and beyond — the theme of Acts.

24:51-53

"He left them and was taken up into heaven. Then they worshiped him and returned to Jerusalem with great joy." The disciples who scattered at the arrest, who hid behind locked doors, who dismissed the women's testimony as nonsense — these same disciples now worship the risen, ascended Jesus and return to Jerusalem with great joy. The resurrection has not merely consoled them; it has transformed them.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:44-45

Calvin insists that the opening of minds (24:45) is the subjective correlate of the objective resurrection. The same event that is externally attested by the empty tomb and the appearances requires an internal illumination before it can be truly received. This illumination is the work of the Holy Spirit — the "promise of the Father" (24:49) for which the disciples are told to wait. Easter and Pentecost belong together.

24:50-53

The Ascension and the disciples' joy: Calvin notes that the disciples' joy at the departure of Jesus is paradoxical — one would expect grief at his leaving, as they grieved at the prospect of his departure in John 14. But the Ascension produces joy because it is not a removal but an enthronement. The one who was rejected and crucified is now seated at the right hand of power, from which position he rules, intercedes, and will return.

The geographical progression of Acts is already encoded in Luke 24:47. The Gospel that followed Jesus from Galilee to Jerusalem now sends the disciples from Jerusalem to the world.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The Women
First witnesses of the resurrection
Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Mary the mother of James (24:10). Luke names them after the fact — their testimony was dismissed as nonsense (24:11) and yet they were correct. The resurrection's first witnesses are women whose testimony was legally inadmissible in first-century Jewish courts.
The Two Emmaus Disciples
Models of resurrection recognition
Cleopas and an unnamed companion whose confusion, grief, and gradual recognition embody the disciples' collective journey from despair to worship. Their burning hearts and opened eyes are the chapter's emotional and theological centre.
Peter
Restored witness
He ran to the tomb first (24:12) and received the first individual resurrection appearance (24:34) — restoring him to a central role after his triple denial. The appearance to Simon is mentioned but not narrated: a gap that John 21 fills.

Reception History

The Emmaus Road in Christian Spirituality
The Emmaus narrative has been one of the most generative passages in Christian spirituality. Dorothy L. Sayers' dramatisations, Henri Nouwen's reflections, and countless sermons have used it as a model for how the risen Christ accompanies the disheartened, opens Scripture, and reveals himself in the breaking of bread. It has shaped eucharistic theology, homiletics, and the understanding of Christ's presence in both word and sacrament.
The Ascension in Christian Theology and Art
The Ascension — Luke's unique contribution to the resurrection narrative — has generated sustained theological reflection on Christ's heavenly session (Heb 1:3; 4:14-16), his intercession (Rom 8:34), and his Lordship over all creation (Eph 1:20-23). In art, the Ascension became one of the standard scenes of the Christian iconographic programme, particularly in Byzantine and medieval Western traditions. The departing Christ with raised, blessing hands has entered permanent visual vocabulary.

Literary Structure

Inclusio: Temple to Temple — chs.1-2 / 24:52-53Luke's Gospel begins in the Temple (Zechariah's vision, 1:5-23) and ends in the Temple (disciples praising God continually, 24:52-53). The Temple frames the entire narrative: the place of God's presence is the beginning and end of the story. Between these brackets, Jesus has replaced the Temple as the locus of divine presence.
Opened/Closed Eyes — vv.16, 31, 45Eyes kept from recognising (v.16) → eyes opened (v.31) → minds opened (v.45). The chapter's structure is a progressive opening: first physical recognition at the table, then full scriptural understanding. Both openings are divine acts, not human achievements.

Greek Word Study

ōphthē oph-THE — he appeared / was seen: The resurrection appearances use ōphthē — divine passive: "he was seen/appeared" — consistently. The same word used in the LXX for God appearing (theophanies): Abraham (Gen 12:7), Moses (Exod 3:2), Isaiah (Isa 6:1). Luke frames the resurrection appearances as theophanies — encounters with the living God. "He appeared to Simon" (v.34) uses ōphthē, placing the resurrection appearance in the category of divine self-disclosure.

diermēneuō di-er-me-NEU-o — interpret / explain / translate: "And beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he explained to them what was said in all the Scriptures concerning himself" (v.27). Diermēneuō — to thoroughly interpret, to translate fully. The Emmaus road is the founding hermeneutical event of Christian interpretation: the risen Jesus himself is the key to reading the entire OT. The burning hearts (v.32) are the sign that the interpretation has been received.

martyres MAR-ty-res — witnesses: "You are witnesses of these things" (v.48). Martyres — witnesses in the legal sense: those who have direct first-hand knowledge of events and are commissioned to report them. The disciples' witness is grounded in what they have seen, heard, and touched (1 John 1:1-3). Luke's Gospel ends with a commission: the witnesses are sent. Acts 1:8 will name the scope: "to the ends of the earth."

Verse 12 is almost certainly original to Luke. Its mention of Peter prepares for the appearance to Simon (24:34) and establishes Peter's continued centrality even after the denial.

Scholarly Debates

Does the Emmaus recognition at the breaking of bread presuppose a eucharistic setting?
Yes, eucharistic allusion is deliberate
Fitzmyer, Green, most Catholic and many Protestant scholars
The sequence — took bread, gave thanks, broke it, gave it (24:30) — exactly parallels the Last Supper (22:19) and the feeding of the five thousand (9:16). Luke uses this four-verb pattern consistently for the Eucharist. The recognition at the breaking of bread is a model for Eucharistic presence of the risen Christ.
Ordinary hospitality, not eucharistic
Some Protestant interpreters
The disciples are guests at Emmaus, not officiants at a sacrament. The four-verb pattern is simply how meals are described. The theological point is about hospitality as the occasion of recognition, not about a specific sacramental theology.
The eucharistic reading is strongly supported by the consistent Lukan pattern. The broader significance (Christ known in Scripture and at table) encompasses both readings — the eucharistic is the institutional form of a wider pattern.

Theological Themes Tracker

Luke 24 is the Gospel's resolution in every sense: narrative, theological, and emotional. The empty tomb (the absence that means presence), the road to Emmaus (the presence that is not yet recognised), the meal (the recognition in breaking bread), the opened minds (understanding Scripture), and the Ascension (departure that is enthronement) — each movement deepens the chapter's central claim: the one who was crucified is alive, present, and Lord. The disciples who ended chapter 23 preparing spices for a dead body end chapter 24 in the Temple with great joy, praising God. The Gospel ends where it began — in the Temple, in praise — but everything has changed.
