Luke 18

Prayer, Humility, Wealth, and Healing

Author

Luke, a Gentile physician (Col 4:14) and the most accomplished literary stylist in the New Testament. His Greek is the most polished in the NT corpus — modelled on the style of educated Hellenistic historians such as Thucydides and Polybius. Early and unanimous church tradition (Irenaeus c. AD 180, Clement of Alexandria, Origen, Eusebius, Jerome) identifies him as the author of both this Gospel and the Acts of the Apostles. He was a close companion of Paul (Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11, “only Luke is with me”), and the “we” passages in Acts (16:10–17; 20:5–15; 21:1–18; 27:1–28:16) confirm direct eyewitness participation in events he narrates. Luke is the only Gentile author in the canon. He explicitly describes his method in the prologue (1:1–4): careful investigation of all available tradition, consultation of eyewitnesses and ministers of the word, and orderly composition for a patron named Theophilus — a preface that conforms to the best conventions of Hellenistic historiography.

When written

c. AD 62–70, most likely during Paul’s two-year imprisonment in Caesarea (Acts 24:27, c. AD 57–59) or shortly thereafter, with publication before Acts (which presupposes it, Acts 1:1). A pre-AD 70 date is supported by the absence of any reference to the destruction of Jerusalem as a past event; the Gospel’s detailed prediction of the siege (21:20–24) reads as prophecy, not retrospective commentary. A minority of scholars favour AD 70–85, arguing that the precision of 21:20 reflects post-event knowledge. The two-volume work (Luke–Acts) is approximately 27,000 words — the largest single contribution to the NT by word count.

What prompted it

Luke writes for Theophilus (“friend of God”), probably a Gentile patron of high social standing (“most excellent,” Acts 1:1, a term used for Roman officials), who has already received some instruction in the faith but needs a reliable, ordered account to confirm its certainty (1:4). More broadly, Luke’s two-volume work addresses the expanding Gentile mission: How does the story of a Jewish Messiah become good news for all nations? His Gospel foregrounds the inclusion of the poor, women, Samaritans, and Gentiles; his Acts shows the gospel moving from Jerusalem to Rome. Luke also addresses a pastoral crisis: the delay of the Parousia. His travel narrative (9:51–19:28) reframes discipleship as a sustained journey of formation, not merely expectation of an imminent end. The Gospel’s persistent themes — prayer, joy, the Holy Spirit, reversal of fortune, universal salvation — all serve this dual apologetic and pastoral purpose.

Greek Word Study

ekkakein ek-KA-kein — give up / lose heart / become weary: The verb (v.1) means to grow tired, to lose the energy of persistence. The parable's primary application is stated before the story begins: pray and do not lose heart. The widow's persistence is the positive model; ekkakein is what the disciples are to refuse.

hilasthēti moi hi-LAS-thē-ti moi — have mercy on me / be propitiated toward me: The tax collector's prayer (v.13) uses a word from the Temple's sacrificial vocabulary: be propitiated, be turned toward me in mercy. He does not ask to be understood, excused, or compared favourably — he asks for mercy from a God whose holiness his life has violated.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
18:7-8

"Will not God bring about justice for his chosen ones, who cry out to him day and night? Will he keep putting them off? I tell you, he will see that they get justice, and quickly." The argument is a fortiori: the unjust judge eventually yields; God is not unjust and not reluctant. The question at the end ("when the Son of Man comes, will he find faith on the earth?") reframes the parable: the issue is not whether God will answer but whether anyone will still be praying when he does.

18:11-12

The Pharisee's prayer is not prayer at all — it is self-congratulation addressed to God. He compares himself favourably to others, lists his religious achievements, and asks for nothing. The form is prayer; the content is a résumé. He leaves exactly as he arrived — "justified in his own eyes" but not before God.

18:13

"God, have mercy on me, a sinner" — in Greek: ho hamartōlos, with the definite article: "the sinner" — as if he is the only one in the category, or at least the representative of the category. The tax collector does not say "a sinner among many" but "the sinner" — total self-assessment without mitigation.

18:22-23

"Sell everything you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven. Then come, follow me." — "When he heard this, he became very sad." The ruler's sadness is not anger or defiance but genuine grief — he wants the eternal life, he cannot pay the price. The encounter is one of the most poignant in the Gospels: a sincere man who cannot do the one necessary thing.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:9

Calvin notes that the parable's target is specifically "those who were confident of their own righteousness and looked down on everyone else" — not the openly wicked but the religiously self-satisfied. The Pharisee's prayer is a case study in the most dangerous form of irreligion: the religion that makes its practitioners worse, not better, by filling them with self-congratulation in the very act of appearing to worship God.

18:27

"What is impossible with man is possible with God" — Calvin uses this as a statement about the nature of salvation itself, not merely about exceptional conversions of the wealthy. Salvation is impossible with man in every case; it is possible with God in every case. The camel-needle image is a universal statement about human inability that the divine response reverses.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
18:12

"I fast twice a week and give a tenth of all I get" — fasting twice a week (Monday and Thursday) was a supererogatory practice of particularly devout Jews, beyond the single annual fast of Yom Kippur required by Torah. Tithing "all I get" may mean tithing even what he purchased from others in case they had not already tithed it — extreme religious conscientiousness. The Pharisee's religious practice is genuine and extraordinary, which makes his self-justification all the more tragic.

18:25

The camel-through-a-needle saying has a rabbinic parallel (b. Berachot 55b: "A man is shown in a dream only what is suggested by his own thoughts... a man is not shown a golden palm tree or an elephant going through the eye of a needle, except..."). The rabbinic use confirms that "impossibility through the eye of a needle" was a standard hyperbolic idiom in first-century Judaism.

Greek Word Study

huios Dauid HY-os Da-UID — Son of David: The messianic title (vv.38-39) is significant on the lips of a blind man. He cannot see Jesus but has heard enough to know the Davidic claim — and to press it with increasing urgency against the crowd's attempts to silence him. The blind man's insight is the chapter's final irony: those who can see do not recognise Jesus; the blind man sees him correctly.

anablepō a-na-BLE-po — receive sight / look up: The verb (v.41) means both to recover sight and to look up — it is the same word used for Jesus looking up at the tax collectors and sinners who gathered around him. The physical recovery of sight is a transparent image of the spiritual sight that the chapter's first half described: the tax collector who saw himself clearly, the children who received with clear simplicity.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
18:31-33

The third passion prediction is the most specific and the disciples understand nothing. This is not a failure of intelligence but of theological category — they could not integrate the messianic expectation with suffering and death. No one in first-century Judaism expected the Messiah to be crucified. The disciples' incomprehension is historically plausible and theologically important: the cross was genuinely unexpected.

18:38-39

"Son of David, have mercy on me!" — the blind man uses the most explicitly messianic address in the Gospel and will not be silenced. The crowd's attempts to shut him up parallel the disciples' attempts to shut out the children (18:15). In both cases, Jesus overrules the gatekeepers. Those who should be guardians of access to Jesus become obstacles to it.

18:42

"Your faith has healed you" (sesōken se) — the same word as "saved." Physical healing and spiritual salvation use the same vocabulary throughout Luke. The blind man's faith — his persistent, insistent, unreasoned trust in Jesus's capacity and willingness to heal — is the model the chapter's first half sought to describe.

18:43

"He followed Jesus, praising God" — the healing's aftermath is described in two verbs: followed and praised. These are the marks of genuine healing in Luke: not just restored function but a new direction (following) and a new orientation (praise). The crowd's response mirrors the individual's: they also praised God.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:34

Calvin notes that the disciples' incomprehension was providentially ordered — they were not ready to receive what the passion prediction announced. Full understanding came after the resurrection (24:44-45) when Jesus himself opened their minds to understand the scriptures. The hiddenness is not a failure of teaching but a preparation for a later illumination.

18:41

"What do you want me to do for you?" — Jesus asks the obvious question. Calvin sees in this question a pattern of prayer: God already knows what we need, yet he invites us to express it. The asking is not for God's information but for ours — to articulate our need is to acknowledge our dependence, which is itself the beginning of faith.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
18:35

"As Jesus approached Jericho" — Mark 10:46 says "as Jesus and his disciples were leaving Jericho." Luke says approaching. The discrepancy has generated extensive harmonisation attempts: two Jerichos (old and new), two blind men, the healing happening between them. Most likely Luke and Mark drew on slightly different traditions, or Luke's approach/depart is imprecise. No textual variant resolves the difference.

18:38

"Son of David, have mercy on me" — this is the earliest use in Luke's Gospel of "Son of David" as an address to Jesus. The title is implicitly rejected by Jesus in 20:41-44, not because it is wrong but because it is insufficient. The Messiah is more than David's son; he is David's Lord.

18:34

Synekan — imperfect active of syniēmi: they were not understanding, continuously. The three-fold description of incomprehension (did not understand / its meaning was hidden from them / they did not know what he was talking about) is unusually emphatic. Luke piles up the vocabulary of non-understanding to make the point unmistakeable.

18:39

Ekrazen — imperfect active: "he was crying out" continuously. The crowd tried to silence him; he kept on shouting. The imperfect tenses in vv.38-39 create a picture of sustained, escalating insistence — the exact quality the persistent widow parable commended.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The Persistent Widow
Model of prayer
Unnamed, voiceless except for her repeated plea. Her persistence in the face of the unjust judge's repeated refusals is the parable's virtue — not her cause (which may or may not be just) but her refusal to give up.
The Tax Collector
Model of humility
Unnamed, standing at a distance, not lifting his eyes. His four-word prayer (in Greek: hilasthēti moi tō hamartōlō) is the model petition — asking for mercy, nothing else, on the basis of need, not merit.
The Rich Ruler
The almost-disciple
A leading figure (archōn) who has kept the commandments, comes to Jesus with a genuine question, and leaves very sad. His departure is the chapter's most poignant moment — sincere, observant, unable.
The Blind Beggar
Model of persistent faith
His shouting through the crowd's opposition embodies the persistent widow's quality — he will not be silenced. His request is simple and direct; his response to healing is immediate following and praise.

Reception History

The Tax Collector's Prayer in Christian Spirituality
The tax collector's four-word prayer became the basis of the Jesus Prayer of Eastern Orthodox spirituality: "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner." The Philokalia and the anonymous Russian spiritual memoir The Way of a Pilgrim (19th century) trace the practice of repeating this prayer in rhythm with breathing — a contemplative tradition rooted in Luke 18:13.
"Camel Through the Eye of a Needle": Interpretation History
The saying's difficulty prompted creative interpretation throughout church history. Origen interpreted it allegorically; medieval commentators invented the "Needle's Gate" to soften it. Luther and Calvin insisted on its literal force, using it to argue that rich Christians can be saved only by the same miraculous divine intervention that saves all sinners — the camel-needle image is a statement about human inability universalised, not merely applied to the wealthy.

Literary Structure

Double Parable — vv.2-14Two parables of prayer back to back: the persistent widow (model of persistence) and the Pharisee/tax collector (model of posture). Both involve coming before a judge/God; both contrast two figures; both teach something essential about prayer.
Bracket — vv.15-17 / 35-43Children receive the kingdom; the blind man receives sight. Both are about reception — receptive simplicity and receptive persistence. The chapter's central section (rich ruler, camel-needle) is bracketed by two images of those who receive what the self-sufficient cannot obtain.

Greek Word Study

ekdikēson ek-di-KE-son — grant justice / vindicate / avenge: The widow's persistent plea to the unjust judge: "Grant me justice (ekdikēson) against my adversary" (v.3). The word encompasses legal vindication — not personal revenge but having one's case heard and upheld. Jesus uses the parable to argue from the lesser (an unjust judge who yields to persistence) to the greater: how much more will God vindicate his elect who cry to him day and night (v.7).

hilasthēti hi-LAS-the-ti — have mercy / be propitious / make atonement: The tax collector's prayer: "God, have mercy on me, a sinner" (v.13). Hilaskomai is not merely "be kind" but the language of the Temple's atoning sacrifice — "make atonement for me." The tax collector positions himself as one who needs the full weight of the sacrificial system applied to his case. Jesus declares him justified — the one whose prayer assumes the need for propitiation goes home righteous.

adynaton a-DY-na-ton — impossible: "What is impossible with man is possible with God" (v.27). The word adynatos (not-able) frames the rich ruler episode: the disciples' "who then can be saved?" (v.26) assumes that if the wealthy are excluded, everyone is hopeless. Jesus redirects from human impossibility to divine possibility — salvation is not a human achievement but a divine act.

Textual Notes

18:35"As Jesus approached Jericho" vs "as he was leaving"
Luke reads "approached" (engizein); Mark 10:46 reads "leaving" (ekporeuomenou). No manuscript of Luke harmonises with Mark; the difference is in the traditions, not the manuscripts.
The discrepancy is a genuine historical-critical puzzle. Most scholars either accept it as a difference in tradition or propose that Luke's "approached" refers to the approach to the district of Jericho while Mark's "leaving" refers to the city proper.
18:24"How hard it is for the rich"
Some MSS (including early ones) add "those who have riches" after "how hard it is" — a clarifying expansion. NA28 follows the shorter reading.
The shorter form is original; the expansion makes explicit what is already clear from context.

Scholarly Debates

Why does Jesus ask "Why do you call me good?" (18:19)?
Jesus denies his own goodness
Liberal interpreters
Jesus is deflecting the title "good teacher" because he does not consider himself divine. This reading uses the saying to argue against early high Christology.
Jesus is testing the ruler's theology
MacArthur, Calvin, most evangelicals
Jesus is not denying his own goodness but pressing the ruler: do you understand what you're saying? "Good" belongs to God alone — so who do you think I am? The question is an invitation to Christological recognition, not a denial of divinity.
Jesus is correcting flattery
Fitzmyer, Green
The ruler is using "Good teacher" as a social honorific. Jesus redirects: goodness is a divine attribute, not a teaching compliment. The saying is about theological seriousness, not Christology.
The majority of NT scholars favour the second or third reading. The first (denial of divinity) is inconsistent with the broader Christological claims of Luke's Gospel and is not required by the Greek text.

Theological Themes Tracker

Luke 18 is a sustained meditation on who enters the kingdom. The persistent widow enters through patient prayer; the tax collector enters through humble honesty; children enter by simple reception; the rich ruler fails to enter because of attachment; the blind beggar enters through persistent faith. The chapter's pattern is the beatitudes in narrative form: the poor in spirit, those who mourn for their condition, those who cannot secure entry on their own terms — these are the ones who receive the kingdom. The self-sufficient, the self-congratulatory, and the self-protected cannot enter.
