Luke 11

Prayer, Exorcism, and Confrontation

Verses 1‑13 — The Lord’s Prayer; The Friend at Midnight; Ask Seek Knock
12345678910111213

Greek Word Study

anaideia an-ai-DEI-a — shameless audacity: Used only here in the NT (v.8). The word commends brazen persistence before God — the cultural shame of making a nuisance of yourself is reimagined as a virtue in prayer.

daktylō theou dak-TY-lō the-OO — finger of God: An OT theophanic image (Exod 8:19; 31:18). Luke preserves this archaic phrase where Matthew writes "Spirit of God" — both assert that Jesus's exorcisms are direct divine action.

Context

[('Prayer in Jewish Practice', 'First-century Jewish prayer was formal and communal — the Amidah, fixed blessing formulae. Jesus\'s "Father" address replaces elaborate doxologies with direct filial intimacy. His instruction to pray for the Holy Spirit (11:13) reorients the tradition toward eschatological expectation.'), ("Luke's Prayer Emphasis", "Luke depicts Jesus at prayer more than any other Gospel: at baptism (3:21), before choosing the Twelve (6:12), at the Transfiguration (9:29). The Lord's Prayer here is prompted by a disciple's request — Luke frames it as teaching from relationship, not formal sermon.")]

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 6:9-13Matthew's fuller Lord's Prayer in the Sermon on the Mount, with the doxology in later manuscripts.
  • Exod 8:19"The finger of God" — Pharaoh's magicians identify the plague as direct divine action, the same image Jesus applies to his exorcisms.
  • Matt 12:22-32Parallel Beelzebul controversy with "Spirit of God" instead of Luke's "finger of God".

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:2-4

"Father" — the intimate filial address was radical in its Jewish context. "Hallowed be your name" is a prayer that God's own character be honoured in the world, beginning with the one praying.

11:9-13

"Ask, seek, knock" — all present imperatives implying continuous action. Prayer shapes the one who prays, aligning human desire with divine provision over time.

11:20

"Finger of God" applied to himself claims that divine power in history works through Jesus personally. The kingdom's arrival is present-tense — in his exorcisms it has already come.

11:24-26

Moral reformation without spiritual transformation leaves a person more vulnerable. An empty, swept house invites worse occupants — the warning is against self-improvement that omits the indwelling of the Spirit.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:5-8

The parable argues from lesser to greater. If even human annoyance yields to persistence, how much more certainly will the Father who delights to give respond to his children's prayer? The argument is explicitly a fortiori.

11:20

When Christ says the kingdom of God "has come upon you" he means not that it is approaching but that it has arrived — its presence is demonstrated in the destruction of demonic power before their eyes.

"Daily bread" — epiousios is of uncertain origin. Proposed meanings: (1) necessary for subsistence; (2) for the coming day; (3) supernatural. The traditional "daily" captures the sense without resolving the philological debate.

Verses 14–26 — Beelzebul Controversy; The Unclean Spirit’s Return

Greek Word Study

sēmeion SAY-mei-on — sign / authenticating miracle: The demand reflects the expectation that genuine prophets and messiahs would authenticate themselves through spectacular public acts. Jesus refuses this economy of proof — the only credential offered is the "sign of Jonah": his death and resurrection.

haplous / ponēros HAP-lous / po-NAY-ros — single/healthy vs evil/diseased: The haplous eye is undivided, generous; the ponēros eye is corrupt, stingy. The metaphor unites moral character with perceptual capacity — what we see clearly depends on who we are.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:29-30

The sign of Jonah in Luke emphasises Jonah's preaching mission — his whole ministry is the sign. Matthew (12:40) focuses on the three days in the fish as resurrection type. Both are valid: Luke emphasises the preached word, Matthew the vindication.

11:39-41

Pharisaic hand-washing was a tradition of the elders, not a Mosaic command. Jesus attacks not Torah but tradition that substituted external compliance for internal transformation. The remedy: generosity to the poor — inside-out renewal.

11:49-51

"God in his wisdom said" — the indictment is total: this generation bears responsibility for the entire history of prophetic rejection, from Abel (first martyr) to Zechariah (last in the canon). The scriptural record closes against them.

11:52

The Torah was given as the key to knowing God. The lawyers had turned it into a locked door. The guardians of revelation had become its gatekeepers against entry.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:33-36

The lamp passage connects perception to moral character. A healthy eye signifies a mind open to truth; an evil eye is clouded by greed or pride. The light within — conscience, reason, and the Spirit's illumination — must not become darkness through religious posturing.

11:47-51

The builders of prophets' tombs honour the dead while persecuting the living — a universal pattern. Every age venerates its previous generation's martyrs while repeating the same crime. Jesus identifies the scribes' hostility to himself as the contemporary expression of this ancient cycle.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:29

The demand for a sign reflects specific first-century Jewish expectation: messianic figures would authenticate themselves through cosmic signs (cf. Josephus, Ant. 20.167-168). Jesus refuses this framework while pointing to the greater sign of his own death and vindication.

11:51

"Between the altar and the sanctuary" — Zechariah was killed in the Temple precincts (2 Chr 24:21), at the holiest geographic point in Israel. The endpoint of a comprehensive scriptural indictment from first to last.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
11:31

Pleion Solomōnos — "something greater than Solomon" (neuter), referring to the reality rather than the person. The same construction with Jonah in v.32. Jesus forces the hearer to supply the implied subject — a deliberately indirect messianic claim.

11:42

Apodekatoute — present active indicative: you are continually tithing. The tithing of garden herbs was a rabbinic extension. Jesus does not deny its validity — "without neglecting the former" — but insists it cannot substitute for justice and the love of God.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
11:34

Augustine: "The eye of the heart is the intention. If your intention is pure and right, all your works done according to that intention will be good. But if the intention is twisted, then however good the works may seem, they are corrupt."

11:42

Cyril of Alexandria: "They tithed even mint and rue — things of the smallest value — but neglected justice and the love of God. They strained at the gnat and swallowed the camel."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Jesus
Teacher and Lord
Characteristically Lukan: Jesus at prayer, at table fellowship, in direct challenge to religious authority. The chapter moves from his prayer life to his exorcistic power to his confrontation with the establishment.
The Disciples
Learners in prayer
One unnamed disciple requests prayer instruction, prompting the Lord's Prayer — Luke's relational framing places this among the most significant teaching moments of the journey narrative.
The Pharisees
Religious establishment
The dinner host's surprise at Jesus's failure to wash triggers the woe-discourse. Characterised throughout Luke as concerned with public reputation at the expense of justice and mercy.
The Experts in the Law
Legal scholars (nomikoi)
Distinct from the Pharisees; they receive three specific accusations: burdening others with obligations, honouring dead prophets while rejecting living ones, and locking others out of knowledge.

Translation Comparison

11:3, 8, 34

VersionTranslation
epiousios (11:3)NIV/KJV/ESV/NRSV all render "daily" though the Greek is of uncertain origin — possibly "necessary for subsistence" or "for the coming day."
anaideia (11:8)NIV: "shameless audacity"; KJV: "importunity"; ESV: "impudence"; NRSV: "persistence". Debate reflects genuine uncertainty about whether the virtue is boldness or persistence.
haplous (11:34)KJV: "single"; NIV/ESV: "healthy"; NRSV: "sound". "Single" preserves the moral dimension of undivided loyalty that "healthy" obscures.

Ancient Sources

Sirach 35:17-19
"The prayer of the humble pierces the clouds; it will not rest until it reaches its goal."
Deuterocanonical background for Luke's ask/seek/knock emphasis — persistent prayer is a well-established Jewish ideal.
Mishnah Berakhot
The tractate on prayer prescribes the Amidah three times daily. Jesus's simpler prayer contrasts sharply with this formal liturgical structure.
The contrast highlights how radical the Lord's Prayer's directness and brevity were in their original context.

Reception History

Lord's Prayer in Liturgical History
The Didache (c. AD 100) prescribes Matthew's version for daily use three times a day, with the doxology, showing how quickly the prayer became fixed liturgically while retaining the relational intimacy Jesus taught.
The Woes in the Reformation
Luther cited 11:46 against the burden of medieval canon law; Calvin cited 11:52 against scholastic theology that obscured rather than opened Scripture. The woes became a standard Reformation reference point.

Literary Structure

Chiasm — vv.9-13Ask/receive → Seek/find → Knock/opened; then elaborated with father/son pairs (fish/snake, egg/scorpion). The three-part ascending structure followed by concrete illustration is a classical chiastic elaboration.
Contrast — vv.37-54Dinner invitation (hospitality) frames the woe-discourse (confrontation). Luke uses the table throughout his Gospel as the site of both grace and judgment — the same meal reveals the host's heart and occasions Jesus's most direct attack on religious hypocrisy.

Greek Word Study

proskartereite pros-kar-te-REI-te — persist / be devoted to: The disciples' request "teach us to pray" (v.1) leads to the Lord's Prayer. Proskartereō combines pros (toward) + karterōs (steadfast). Persistent, devoted prayer — not occasional petition — is the posture Jesus models throughout Luke.

hamartias ha-mar-TI-as — sins (in the Lord's Prayer): Luke's Lord's Prayer (v.4) uses hamartias (sins) where Matthew uses opheilēmata (debts). The petition flows both ways: the same verb aphiomen (we forgive) governs both the forgiveness we receive and the forgiveness we extend.

ekballō ek-BAL-lo — drive out / cast out: "If I drive out demons by the finger of God, then the kingdom of God has come upon you" (v.20). The logic is tight: the same power cannot be both divine and demonic. Ekballō — the language of conquest — signals the strong man's house is being plundered (v.22).

Textual Notes

11:2Shorter vs. longer form of the Lord's Prayer
NA28 reads the shorter form ("Father, hallowed be your name"); later MSS add "Our" and "in heaven" harmonising toward Matt 6:9. The doxology appears only in very late MSS.
Luke's shorter form is almost certainly original; additions bring it into liturgical conformity with Matthew's version.
11:11"A fish... a snake"
Some MSS add "if he asks for bread, will he give him a stone?" harmonising with Matt 7:9. NA28 follows the shorter form.
The shorter reading is original; the bread/stone clause was imported by scribes familiar with Matthew's parallel.

Scholarly Debates

The "Sign of Jonah": Preaching or Resurrection?
Luke's emphasis: the preacher
Green, Fitzmyer, Bock
Luke's version focuses on Jonah as a preacher — his whole mission to Nineveh is the sign. The Son of Man's preached word will be the sign to this generation.
Matthew's emphasis: the resurrection
Davies and Allison, Keener
Matthew (12:40) specifies the three days in the fish as a type of the resurrection. The sign is death and vindication.
Most scholars see the two versions as complementary. The original saying was likely brief and allusive; both evangelists draw out different implications.
The Lord's Prayer: One Form or Two?
Luke preserves the original
Jeremias, Fitzmyer, Luz
Luke's shorter form is likely closer to the original. Matthew expanded it for liturgical use — the additions are consistent with his other expansions of Q material.
Matthew preserves the original
Gerhardsson
Matthew's fuller form may reflect a carefully transmitted liturgical tradition from the Jerusalem community.
Most NT scholars favour Luke's shorter form as closer to the original. The doxology in Matthew is almost certainly a later liturgical addition absent from the oldest manuscripts.

Theological Themes Tracker

Luke 11 frames prayer as bold filial confidence before a generous Father. The friend-at-midnight parable commends brazen persistence; the ask-seek-knock triad underlines prayer as continuous relational practice. The lamp and eye sayings (11:33-36) connect spiritual perception to moral orientation — the Pharisees' blindness is moral, not intellectual. The six woes anatomise religious dimness: external observance without internal transformation, public honour without justice, scholarship that obscures rather than opens the word of God.
