Acts 20

Farewell to Ephesus; Miletus Speech

See on Map — Paul's 3rd Journey
Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Greek Word Study

κλάσαι ἄρτον klasai arton — to break bread: the gathering 'on the first day of the week to break bread' (v.7) is the earliest explicit reference to Sunday worship in Acts. The phrase carries eucharistic weight — the same verb (klaō) used at the Last Supper (Luke 22:19) and Emmaus (Luke 24:30, 35). Sunday has replaced Saturday as the community's primary gathering day.

νεανίας neanias — young man: Eutychus is a neanias — a young man in the prime of life (unlike the diminutive neaniskos). His death from a fall while listening to Paul is historically vivid and theologically instructive: even the most earnest teaching cannot always keep sleep at bay. Luke's honesty about the scene is characteristic.

The major port city at the mouth of the Maeander river in Asia Minor — 48 km south of Ephesus. By bypassing Ephesus and stopping at Miletus, Paul avoids being delayed by the Ephesian church while still summoning its elders for his farewell address.

Context

The delegate list of v.4 — men from Berea, Thessalonica, Derbe, Asia — represents the collection party Paul is bringing to Jerusalem. His great collection for the Jerusalem church (1 Cor 16:1–4; 2 Cor 8–9; Rom 15:25–28) was a major theological statement: Gentile churches expressing solidarity with and indebtedness to the mother church. The Sunday gathering at Troas (v.7) is the earliest explicit reference to Sunday as the Christian community's primary worship day — a practice rooted in the resurrection's day.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Corinthians 16:1–4"Now about the collection for the Lord's people... when I arrive, I will give letters of introduction to the men you approve and send them with your gift to Jerusalem." The collection party of Acts 20:4 is the human implementation of this apostolic directive.
  • Acts 16:9–10The Macedonian call at Troas — Paul's first visit. His return visit completes the European mission arc: called to go west at Troas, now returning east through Troas.
  • Acts 1 Kings 17:21Elijah throwing himself on the widow's son — the OT precedent for Paul's posture over Eutychus. Both involve physical contact, prayer, and restoration of life.

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
20:7

MacArthur on the Sunday gathering: this verse is one of the primary NT texts for Sunday as the Christian day of worship. The gathering to "break bread" on the first day of the week reflects the resurrection's reorientation of the weekly cycle — Sunday is the new creation's first day.

20:9–12

MacArthur on Eutychus: the raising demonstrates that the apostolic gift of raising the dead (cf. Peter and Tabitha, 9:36–41) continued beyond the Jerusalem ministry. MacArthur notes that Paul's response — 'don't be alarmed, he's alive' — is characteristically understated, deflecting attention from the miracle to the continuation of ministry.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:7

Calvin on breaking bread on the first day: this is the NT's clearest indication that Sunday had become the community's primary worship day, replacing the Jewish Sabbath. Calvin notes the theological rationale: the resurrection occurred on the first day, constituting a new creation event that reoriented the weekly calendar from the old creation's seventh day to the new creation's first day.

20:11

"Then he went upstairs again and broke bread and ate. After talking until daylight, he left" — Calvin on the persistence of the fellowship: even after the dramatic interruption of Eutychus's fall, the community resumes their meal and continues until dawn. Calvin sees this as the picture of the church's resilience: it continues its life together despite dramatic interruptions.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:7

The NET notes that "the first day of the week" is Sunday — the day after the Jewish Sabbath. The gathering is explicitly for "breaking bread" — an activity with eucharistic significance (cf. Luke 24:30–31, 35; Acts 2:42). This is the clearest NT evidence for Sunday as the primary Christian day of worship.

20:9

The NET notes that Eutychus was "picked up dead" (ērthē nekros) — not "thought to be dead" or "nearly dead." Luke presents this as a genuine death and genuine restoration to life. The subsequent "he's alive" (v.10) and the community's comfort (v.12) confirm that all parties understood a miraculous restoration had occurred.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:4

Keener notes the significance of the delegate list — men from Berea, Thessalonica, Derbe, and Asia. These represent the churches that have contributed to the Jerusalem collection. Their accompaniment of Paul is both a security measure against theft and a symbolic demonstration of Gentile solidarity with Jewish Christians.

20:7

Keener notes that 'breaking bread' on the first day of the week is consistent with early Christian practice attested in the Didache (chapter 14) and Justin Martyr's First Apology (chapter 67), both dating to c. AD 100–150. Acts 20:7 is the earliest datable reference to this practice.

Verses 13—38 — Journey to Miletus; Paul’s Farewell to the Ephesian Elders

Greek Word Study

ἐπίσκοπος episkopos — overseers: in v.28 Paul addresses the 'elders' (presbyterous, v.17) and calls them 'overseers' (episkopous) — the two terms are used interchangeably for the same office. The function is described as 'shepherding' (poimainein — to be shepherds) the church. Elder, overseer, and shepherd are three descriptions of one office, not three separate roles.

τὴν βουλὴν τοῦ θεοῦ tēn boulēn tou theou — the whole will of God: v.27 — Paul's defence of his ministry: 'I have not hesitated to proclaim to you the whole will of God' (boulē — the determinate counsel, plan, purpose of God). This is not merely 'what God wants' in a general sense but the complete redemptive purpose revealed in Scripture. Paul's ministry is defined by comprehensiveness: nothing held back, nothing added.

The weeping at departure (v.37) is the measure of what Paul and the community mean to each other.

Context

The Miletus speech is Paul's only address to Christian leaders in Acts and his most autobiographical speech. It functions as a pastoral testament — a dying man's (or departing man's) final instructions to those he leaves behind. The address moves through three phases: (1) Paul's past ministry model (vv.18–21: how I lived among you); (2) Paul's present situation (vv.22–24: where I am going and why); (3) Paul's future charge (vv.25–35: what I leave you to do). The conclusion — the quotation of Jesus (v.35: 'It is more blessed to give than to receive') — is the only Pauline quotation of a Jesus saying not found in the Gospels.

Cross-Reference

  • Ephesians 4:11"Christ himself gave the apostles, the prophets, the evangelists, the pastors and teachers" — Paul's letter to Ephesus elaborates the eldership structure he commissions in the Miletus speech.
  • 1 Peter 5:1–4"Be shepherds of God's flock... not lording it over those entrusted to you" — Peter's instructions to elders echo the Miletus speech's themes of shepherding and voluntary service.
  • Acts 20:35The only unrecorded Jesus saying in the NT: "It is more blessed to give than to receive" — not in any Gospel but preserved here by Paul.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
20:28

MacArthur on 'be shepherds of the church of God': the elder's primary responsibility is described with three verbs — watch over (v.28: prosechete), shepherd (v.28: poimainein), and guard (v.31: grēgorountes). MacArthur uses this as the definitive NT statement of pastoral responsibility: the elder's first duty is to the flock's health and protection, not to administration or programme.

20:29–30

MacArthur on the warning about false teachers: 'savage wolves will come in... even from your own number.' MacArthur notes that Paul's prediction was historically fulfilled within decades — the Ephesian letters in Revelation 2:2 commend the church for testing false apostles. The warning is both specific and universal: every church faces the dual threat of external wolves and internal distorters.

20:35

MacArthur on "it is more blessed to give than to receive": this is the only direct quotation of Jesus in Acts attributed to Paul. MacArthur notes that Paul's financial model — working with his hands to give to the weak — was itself a living embodiment of this principle. The quote is the theological foundation for the entire self-support argument.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:24

"However, I consider my life worth nothing to me; my only aim is to finish the race and complete the task" — Calvin on Paul's relationship to his own life: it is not that he does not value his life (that would be false) but that he holds it loosely, subordinating it entirely to the mission. Calvin uses this as the model for all Christian engagement with dangerous service: not carelessness about life but willingness to risk it for what matters more.

20:27

"For I have not hesitated to proclaim to you the whole will of God" — Calvin on the completeness of faithful ministry: Paul's defence of his innocence rests on comprehensiveness. He has proclaimed everything — nothing withheld from pastoral cowardice, nothing added from pastoral ambition. Calvin uses this as the standard for all preaching: the whole counsel of God, nothing more and nothing less.

20:32

"Now I commit you to God and to the word of his grace" — Calvin: the ultimate security of any congregation is not its leaders but God himself and his word. Paul is departing; the community is not orphaned because it has been committed to God. Calvin uses this as the consolation for all pastoral transitions: the shepherd changes but the flock's true Shepherd does not.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:28

The NET notes the textual variant: "church of God" or "church of the Lord" — the NET prefers "church of God" based on the best manuscript evidence. The phrase "which he bought with his own blood" then becomes a remarkable statement: God (in the person of his Son) shed his own blood. This is one of the few unambiguous NT references to the blood of God.

20:35

The NET notes this is an "agraphon" — an unwritten saying of Jesus preserved outside the canonical Gospels. The NET provides a footnote surveying other such sayings. This particular saying is consistent with the Synoptic tradition's emphasis on reversal (Matt 20:26–27; Mark 9:35; Luke 22:26) and is generally considered authentic.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
20:28

"Be shepherds of the church of God, which he bought with his own blood" — Robertson notes the remarkable Christological claim embedded here: the blood shed is God's own blood — an implicit statement of Christ's full divinity. Robertson uses this as one of Acts' clearest implicit Trinitarian statements.

20:35

Robertson on the unrecorded Jesus saying: "It is more blessed to give than to receive" — this is one of the agraphia (unwritten sayings of Jesus) preserved outside the Gospels. Robertson notes it is consistent with Jesus's teaching style and is probably preserved from oral tradition. Its authenticity is supported by its content's consistency with the Synoptic tradition.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:28

Keener notes the term episkopos (overseer/bishop) used here alongside presbyteros (elder) in v.17 — the earliest unambiguous NT use of both terms for the same office. The Miletus speech is one of Luke's most important ecclesiological passages: it defines the elder's office in terms of shepherding, guarding against false teaching, and financial integrity.

20:33–35

Keener provides background on Paul's financial self-support. Manual labour by a philosopher/teacher was unusual and regarded as undignified in the Greco-Roman world. Paul's insistence on supporting himself was a deliberate counter-cultural statement: his Gospel is free because it is not his means of income. This financial independence was both practically and rhetorically significant.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Paul↗ People
Apostle to the Gentiles; author of thirteen NT letters
Paul (Saul of Tarsus) is introduced as a persecutor at Stephen’s stoning (Acts 7:58) and is dramatically converted on the Damascus road (Acts 9). He dominates Acts 13–28 across three missionary journeys, planting churches from Cyprus to Greece to Rome. His final journey to Rome as a prisoner frames Acts’ theological climax: the gospel reaching the empire’s capital, "boldly and without hindrance" (Acts 28:31).
Peter↗ People
Lead apostle; spokesperson for the Jerusalem church
Peter (Simon bar Jonah) dominates Acts 1–12 as the church’s primary spokesman and miracle-worker. He preaches at Pentecost (Acts 2), heals at the Beautiful Gate (Acts 3), defends before the Sanhedrin (Acts 4–5), raises Tabitha (Acts 9:36–42), and receives the vision that opens the gospel to Gentiles (Acts 10). His release from Herod’s prison by an angel (Acts 12) closes his narrative in Acts; Paul’s story takes over from ch.13.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 20Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 20 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
