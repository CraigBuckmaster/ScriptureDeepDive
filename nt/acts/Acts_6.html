Acts 6

The Seven Chosen; Stephen Arrested

Verses 1–7 — The Appointment of the Seven Deacons
1234567

Greek Word Study

Ἑλληνιστάς / Ἑβραίους Hellēnistas / Hebraious — Hellenists / Hebrews: the Hellenists were Greek-speaking Diaspora Jews settled in Jerusalem; the Hebrews were Aramaic-speaking Palestinian Jews. This cultural-linguistic divide is the church's first internal tension — resolved not by favouritism but by structural wisdom that serves both communities.

διακονίᾳ diakonia — ministry/service: used both for the daily food distribution (v1) and the apostles' ministry of the word (v4). Luke deliberately uses the same word for both, showing that both are genuine ministries. The distinction is one of calling, not dignity.

The third apostolic hearing in Acts — but the first that will end not in flogging and release but in death. Luke marks the escalation with the unprecedented detail of Stephen's angelic face.

Context

The Seven's appointment is the functional origin of the later diaconal office (1 Tim 3:8–13). Their qualifications — Spirit-filled, full of wisdom, of good reputation — set the standard for servant leadership. Notably, all seven have Greek names, suggesting the appointment was deliberately sensitive to the Hellenistic community's representation. Luke places the growth summary of v7 immediately after the structural innovation — the organizational response was Spirit-blessed: 'the word of God spread.' The detail that 'a large number of priests became obedient' signals that the establishment itself is beginning to fracture.

Cross-Reference

  • Numbers 11:16–17Moses appoints seventy elders to share the burden of leadership — the OT precedent for delegated ministry. The Spirit given to Moses is distributed to the seventy.
  • 1 Timothy 3:8–13The qualifications for deacons — dignified, not double-tongued, tested — build on the Acts 6 precedent. The office is formally named and structured in the Pastoral Epistles.
  • Acts 6:7"The word of God spread" — Luke's growth summary immediately after the structural innovation, confirming the organizational response was Spirit-approved.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:2–4

MacArthur uses this passage to establish the NT pattern of ministry specialization. The apostles' commitment to "prayer and the ministry of the word" defines the primary calling of elders/pastors. The failure to guard this priority is, for MacArthur, the besetting sin of contemporary church leadership.

6:7

MacArthur highlights the significance of "priests" believing. The Jerusalem priestly families were Sadducean — the theological enemies of resurrection. Their conversion signals that the resurrection evidence was compelling even to those with the strongest institutional interest in denying it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:2–4

Calvin uses this passage as the foundation for the distinction between the offices of elder/pastor (ministry of the word) and deacon (ministry of mercy). Both are permanent offices; neither is superior in dignity. The distinction is functional, not hierarchical.

6:10

"They could not stand up against the wisdom he spoke or the Spirit by whom he spoke" — Stephen's opponents are defeated not by natural eloquence but by the Spirit's work through him. The fulfilment of Luke 21:15 is already happening.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:1

The NET notes that Ἑλληνιστάς ("Hellenists") are Greek-speaking Jewish Christians, not Gentiles. The Gentile mission has not yet formally begun; this is an intra-Jewish-Christian tension.

6:7

"A large number of priests" — the NET notes the Jerusalem Temple employed many lower-ranking priests who lived in the city and served in rotation (distinct from the high-priestly aristocracy). Their conversion represents a significant inroad into the religious establishment.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
6:2

"It would not be right for us to neglect the ministry of the word in order to wait on tables" — Robertson notes this is not a dismissal of table service but a recognition of calling. The Twelve were specifically called to be witnesses (1:8, 22); this calling constrains what they can devote time to.

6:6

The laying on of hands (epitithentes tas cheiras) — Robertson traces this from Numbers 27:18–23 (Moses investing Joshua). It is a gesture of identification and authorization, not the mechanical transfer of power.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:1

Keener notes that the Hellenistic/Hebrew division reflects real socio-cultural tensions. The widows being overlooked may reflect unconscious bias or simple administrative failure. The church's response is the model: structural change rather than denial or blame-shifting.

6:5

The appointment of seven men all with Greek names is almost certainly intentional: the Hellenistic community that raised the complaint receives exclusively Hellenistic representatives to address it. Cultural sensitivity is embedded in the structural response.

Verses 8–15 — Stephen Seized — Full of Grace and Power
89101112131415

Greek Word Study

ἀντέστησαν antestēsan — could not withstand: the aorist of anthistēmi — the same word Paul uses in Gal 2:11 ("I opposed him to his face") and Eph 6:13 ("stand your ground"). Stephen's opponents launch a full counter-attack and fail. The inability to refute his argument drives them to fabrication.

ὡσεὶ πρόσωπον ἀγγέλου hōsei prosōpon angelou — like the face of an angel: deliberately echoes Moses's shining face (Exod 34:29–35) and anticipates Stephen's vision of heaven open (7:55–56). His face reflects divine approval before he speaks a word — the heavenly court has already rendered its verdict.

Places

Synagogue of the Freedmen
31.7683° N, 35.2137° E
"Freedmen" (libertinoi — Latin loanword) were Jews or their descendants who had been enslaved and freed, probably from the Roman wars of 63 BC. Their synagogue drew Diaspora Jews who had returned to Jerusalem as a mark of piety — making them especially sensitive to perceived threats to Jewish identity.
The Sanhedrin Hall
31.7776° N, 35.2349° E
The Sanhedrin's Hall of Hewn Stone on the Temple Mount. For the fourth time in Acts the Sanhedrin is the venue for confrontation — but now the confrontation is capital: Stephen will not be released.

Context

The false witness pattern (v13) echoes Jesus's trial (Mark 14:55–59). Luke's parallel is deliberate: Stephen's passion narrative mirrors his Lord's. The charges — speaking against the Temple and the law — are the same charges brought against Jesus. This structural parallel signals that Stephen's martyrdom is patterned on and participates in the suffering of Christ. Stephen is becoming the first Christian whose life is shaped into the shape of the cross.

Cross-Reference

  • Mark 14:55–59False witnesses at Jesus's trial charged him with threatening the Temple — the same charge brought against Stephen. Luke frames Stephen's death as a repetition of Christ's passion in the life of the first martyr.
  • Exodus 34:29–35Moses's shining face after meeting God on Sinai — the OT precedent for Stephen's angelic appearance. Both Moses and Stephen are mediators of divine revelation being rejected by their own people.
  • Acts 7:55–56Stephen's face shines before his speech; his vision of the open heaven confirms God's approval after it. The framing brackets his entire defence with divine endorsement.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:13–14

MacArthur defends the charges as distortion rather than fabrication: Stephen probably did teach that Jesus had fulfilled and superseded both Temple worship (cf. John 4:21–24) and aspects of the Mosaic covenant. The witnesses' sin was misrepresenting the nuance and intention of what he said.

6:15

MacArthur connects Stephen's shining face to 2 Corinthians 3:18 — "being transformed into his image with ever-increasing glory." Stephen has spent himself in the Spirit's service; his face reflects the glory he has been contemplating.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:10

Calvin notes the sequence: unable to refute → secretly recruiting false witnesses → stirring up the crowd. This is the pattern of all bad-faith opposition: when argument fails, manipulation follows. The church should not be surprised when theological defeat of opponents is followed by personal attack.

6:15

Calvin interprets the angelic face as God's answer to the court's false proceedings: before the human verdict is reached, the divine verdict is displayed. Those who suffer for the Gospel are not abandoned.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:9

The NET follows the interpretation of a single synagogue with multiple ethnic constituencies, noting that a single definite article (tēs) governs the whole phrase.

6:14

"Customs Moses handed down to us" — the NET notes that "customs" (ethē) refers to the entire Jewish way of life derived from Moses. The charge anticipates the Jerusalem Council debate (Acts 15) about which customs are binding on believers.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
6:9

Robertson identifies the Synagogue of the Freedmen as a single synagogue with multiple ethnic constituencies, not five separate synagogues. The single definite article governs the whole phrase.

6:15

"Like the face of an angel" — Robertson connects the shining face to Moses (Exod 34) and the Transfiguration (Luke 9:29). The divine radiance is a sign of revelation about to be given and divine vindication already granted.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:11–14

Keener notes that the charges against Stephen reflect real tension in early Christianity. Jesus had spoken about the Temple's destruction (Luke 21:6) and obsolete customs (Mark 7:15). Stephen's crime was theological consistency — drawing out implications the establishment found intolerable.

6:15

"Like the face of an angel" was a recognized idiom in Jewish literature for extraordinary radiance or divine approval (cf. 1 Enoch; Josephus). The Sanhedrin members seeing this would have recognized it as a sign of prophetic authority — making their subsequent rejection all the more culpable.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 6Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 6 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
