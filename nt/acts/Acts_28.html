Verses 1—16 — Malta: The Viper; Publius’ Father Healed; Arrival in Rome

Greek Word Study

βάρβαροι barbaroi — islanders: v.2 — the Maltese are called barbaroi (non-Greek speakers) not as an insult but as a factual description. Luke immediately qualifies it: these 'barbarians' showed 'unusual kindness' (ou tēn tychousan philanthrōpian — extraordinary human kindness). The word philanthrōpia (love for humanity) is one of the NT's most positive terms.

πυρετοῖς καὶ δυσεντερίῳ pyretois kai dysenteriō — fever and dysentery: Luke the physician's precise medical diagnosis: Publius's father suffers from fever (pyretois) and dysentery (dysenteriō) — consistent with Malta fever (brucellosis), caused by goat's milk and endemic on Malta until the 20th century. Medical precision and eyewitness accuracy combined.

Places

Malta
35.9375° N, 14.3754° E
Three months on Malta — Paul heals Publius's father and many others; the islanders honour the group and supply them for the voyage. The island community receives more pastoral care in three months than many cities received in Paul's entire mission. Malta is an unremarkable stopping point that becomes a microcosm of the Gospel's reach.
Puteoli and Rome
40.8220° N, 14.1269° E
Puteoli (modern Pozzuoli) — the main Italian port for Alexandrian grain ships, near Naples. Finding believers there (v.14) shows the Gospel had reached Italy before Paul. The Rome church's welcoming delegation (v.15) — meeting Paul at the Forum of Appius (69 km from Rome) and the Three Taverns (53 km) — is the personal embodiment of the community Paul had written to years earlier (Romans).

Context

Paul's arrival in Rome (v.14: 'and so we came to Rome') is Acts' climactic geographical fulfilment of the 1:8 commission. The brothers and sisters who meet him on the Appian Way are the human expression of the church he had addressed in his letter years earlier without visiting. Paul's response — 'he thanked God and was encouraged' (v.15) — is the only recorded instance of Paul being encouraged by others in Acts. Even the apostle to the Gentiles needed the community of faith.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 1:8"To the ends of the earth" — Rome was the capital of the ends of the earth for Paul's world. His arrival fulfils the geographic scope of the commission given in Acts 1.
  • Romans 1:10–11"I long to see you so that I may impart to you some spiritual gift... that is, that you and I may be mutually encouraged by each other's faith" — Paul's letter-prayer now answered in person.
  • Acts 23:11"You must also testify in Rome" — the Lord's promise, made in Jerusalem after Paul's arrest, is now kept: Paul stands in Rome.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
28:8

MacArthur on Paul's healing of Publius's father: prayer plus laying on hands — the same apostolic practice throughout Acts. MacArthur notes that the subsequent healing of all the sick on the island (v.9) confirms that what happened to Publius's father was genuinely miraculous, not a medical coincidence.

28:15

MacArthur on 'Paul thanked God and was encouraged': the acknowledgment that Paul needed encouragement is one of Acts' most humanly honest moments. MacArthur uses this to argue against a superhuman view of apostolic ministry. Paul was as dependent on community as any believer — and as genuinely strengthened by it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
28:2

Calvin on the Maltese barbarians' unusual kindness: God preserves a seed of humanity even in those who have not received the Gospel. The kindness of pagans is not meritorious before God but it is genuine — and Calvin uses it to argue against a total-depravity reading that would deny all natural virtue to the unregenerate.

28:15

Calvin on Paul's encouragement: 'he thanked God and was encouraged.' The encouragement came from people; the thanks went to God. Calvin notes the proper order: human community is the instrument of divine encouragement. Paul neither bypasses the human community (as if he needed only God) nor credits it absolutely (as if God had no part in it).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
28:7

The NET notes that 'chief official of the island' (prōtos Melitaiōn) is the exact title attested on Maltese inscriptions. This precise title is one of Luke's most archaeologically verified details — the kind of specific local accuracy that characterises reliable ancient historiography.

28:14

The NET notes that "And so we came to Rome" (kai houtōs eis tēn Rhōmēn ēlthamen) is a climactic statement. The impersonal construction — "we came" — is Luke's understated announcement of one of history's most significant arrivals. The Gospel has reached the capital of the world.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
28:2

"The islanders showed us unusual kindness" — Robertson notes the word philanthrōpian (philanthropy — love for humanity) used of these pagan Maltese. Robertson: the Gospel finds unexpected hospitality in unexpected places. Divine providence precedes the apostle; kindness greets him before he arrives.

28:15

"At the sight of these people Paul thanked God and was encouraged" — Robertson notes the hapax legomenon tharsos labon (took courage, was encouraged): Paul was genuinely strengthened by the Roman church's welcome. The apostle who had encouraged so many was himself in need of encouragement and received it.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
28:7

Keener notes that 'the chief official of the island' (prōtos Melitaiōn — the first/chief of the Maltese) is confirmed by inscriptions found on Malta that use exactly this title (prōtos) for the island's leading official. Luke's specific title has been archaeologically verified.

28:13–14

Keener notes that Puteoli was the main Italian port for Alexandrian grain ships — the same route Paul was travelling. The presence of believers there before Paul's arrival confirms that the Gospel had reached Rome and Italian ports through unknown channels well before Paul's arrival (cf. Romans 1:8: 'your faith is being reported all over the world').

Verses 17—31 — Paul in Rome: Preaching the Kingdom of God Unhindered
171819202122232425262728293031

Greek Word Study

ἀκωλύτως akōlytōs — without any hindrance: the final word of Acts — akōlytōs (unhindered, without obstacle). The entire book has been building to this word. The Gospel that began in Jerusalem, survived opposition in every city, travelled through shipwreck and imprisonment, arrives in Rome and is proclaimed 'without any hindrance.' The book ends not with Paul's fate but with the word's freedom.

τῆς ἐλπίδος τοῦ Ἰσραήλ tēs elpidos tou Israēl — the hope of Israel: Paul's identification of his imprisonment's reason in v.20 — 'the hope of Israel.' The resurrection of the Messiah is not a departure from Israel's hope but its content. Paul is in chains not for abandoning Israel's heritage but for proclaiming its fulfilment.

Places

Rome — Paul's Rented House
41.8919° N, 12.5113° E
Paul's house arrest in Rome allows him to 'welcome all who came to see him' (v.30) — in the capital of the empire. The house becomes a mission centre: Roman citizens, guards, visitors from across the empire, and local Jewish leaders all come to Paul. Captivity becomes platform.
Rome — The Fulfilment of Acts 1:8
41.9028° N, 12.4964° E
Acts 1:8 promised witness 'to the ends of the earth'; Acts 28:31 delivers it: Paul proclaims 'with all boldness and without any hindrance' in Rome. The commission is complete. The book ends here — not because Paul's story is over but because the narrative's goal has been reached.

Context

Acts ends in a deliberate act of literary suspension. Paul is in Rome, preaching freely — the goal of the entire narrative is achieved. Luke does not tell us the outcome of Paul's trial because the trial's outcome is not his point. His point is that the word of God has arrived at the ends of the earth and is being proclaimed without hindrance. The Isaiah citation (vv.25–27) is the third and final statement of turning to the Gentiles (cf. 13:46–47; 18:6) — each time the pattern: Israel partially rejects, Gentiles receive. The 'without hindrance' of v.31 is Acts' theological thesis proven: no human power can finally stop the Gospel.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 1:8"You will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth" — the commission of Acts 1:8 is fulfilled in Acts 28:31. The book is structured as its own fulfilment.
  • Isaiah 6:9–10"You will be ever hearing but never understanding" — the third use of this Isaiah text in the NT (also Matt 13:14; John 12:40). Each use marks a decisive moment when Israel's hardening opens the door wider to the Gentiles.
  • Romans 11:11–15"Salvation has come to the Gentiles to make Israel envious... their loss means riches for the Gentiles" — Paul's theological account of the pattern Acts 28 narrates.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
28:28

"God's salvation has been sent to the Gentiles, and they will listen!" — MacArthur on the third and final turning to the Gentiles. The pattern of Acts: Israel receives the first offer; partial rejection; Gentile inclusion; repeat. MacArthur notes that Paul never abandons Israel — he meets the Roman Jewish leaders first even in Rome. But the repeated pattern confirms the Gentile mission's theological necessity.

28:31

"With all boldness and without any hindrance!" — MacArthur on the final verse: Luke ends Acts not with a legal verdict or a death report but with the proclamation of the Gospel. The biographical question (what happened to Paul?) is deliberately left unanswered. The theological answer is given: the word of God is not bound (2 Tim 2:9). MacArthur: "Acts ends in the middle of a sentence — the Gospel's sentence, which is still being spoken."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
28:23

Calvin on the all-day session: 'from morning till evening' — the duration is Calvin's illustration of apostolic diligence. Paul does not give a brief introduction and leave; he spends the entire day with those who come to hear. Calvin uses this as the model for pastoral instruction: the word deserves time, not efficiency.

28:31

Calvin on 'without any hindrance': this is, for Calvin, the triumph of the kingdom over every earthly opposition. Rome — the most powerful empire in history — could not finally stop the Gospel. Caesar's court became Paul's pulpit. Calvin uses this to encourage believers in every age: the powers that seem to suppress the Gospel are, in the end, its servants.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
28:29

The NET notes that v.29 ("After he said this, the Jews left, arguing vigorously among themselves") is absent from the best manuscripts and is considered a later addition to smooth the transition. The NET omits it from the main text with a footnote.

28:31

The NET notes that akōlytōs (without any hindrance) is the final word of Acts in Greek. Luke's choice to end on this adverb is deliberate: the word's freedom is the book's theological climax. Whatever happens to Paul (Luke does not say) the Gospel is unhindered. The narrative's goal — the ends of the earth — has been reached.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
28:23

"He witnessed to them from morning till evening, explaining and declaring the kingdom of God and trying to convince them about Jesus from the Law of Moses and from the Prophets" — Robertson on the all-day session: the same method Paul used in the synagogues throughout the Gentile mission, now applied in Rome. Robertson notes the three verbs: diamarturō (witnessed solemnly), ektithēmi (explained/expounded), peithō (sought to persuade) — a complete description of apostolic proclamation.

28:31

"With all boldness and without any hindrance!" — Robertson on the final word akōlytōs: this is not the end of Paul's story but the end of Luke's narrative goal. The Gospel has arrived in Rome, is being proclaimed freely, and no human power has ultimately been able to stop it. Robertson: "The book ends on a note of triumph — not Paul's triumph but the word's."

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
28:17–20

Keener notes that Paul's first action in Rome is to meet with the Jewish leaders — consistent with his 'to the Jew first' principle (Rom 1:16). The Roman Jewish community had no formal communication from Jerusalem about Paul (v.21), suggesting either that the Jerusalem case had collapsed or that the news had not yet arrived. Paul begins with a clean slate.

28:30–31

Keener notes that 'all who came to see him' (v.30 — tous eisporeuomenous pros auton) suggests Paul received a steady stream of visitors during his house arrest — soldiers, guards, local believers, curious visitors, official contacts. The Prison Epistles (Philippians, Colossians, Ephesians, Philemon) were written during this period, confirming that Paul's Roman imprisonment was one of his most productive periods.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Paul↗ People
Apostle to the Gentiles; author of thirteen NT letters
Paul (Saul of Tarsus) is introduced as a persecutor at Stephen’s stoning (Acts 7:58) and is dramatically converted on the Damascus road (Acts 9). He dominates Acts 13–28 across three missionary journeys, planting churches from Cyprus to Greece to Rome. His final journey to Rome as a prisoner frames Acts’ theological climax: the gospel reaching the empire’s capital, "boldly and without hindrance" (Acts 28:31).

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 28Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 28 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
