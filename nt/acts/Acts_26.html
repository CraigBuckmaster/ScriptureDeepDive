Acts 26

Paul's Defence Before Agrippa — The Damascus Commission

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Greek Word Study

σκληρόν σοι πρὸς κέντρα λακτίζειν sklēron soi pros kentra laktizein — It is hard for you to kick against the goads: v.14 — a Greek proverb (found in Euripides, Aeschylus, Pindar) meaning 'it is painful and futile to resist the force driving you.' Unique to the Acts 26 account, it suggests Paul was fighting against something already at work within him — the Spirit's conviction he was trying to suppress.

ἵνα ἀνοίξῃς ὀφθαλμοὺς αὐτῶν hina anoixēs ophthalmous autōn — to open their eyes: The commission in vv.17–18 is the most comprehensive statement of the Gentile mission's theological content in Acts: open eyes, turn from darkness to light, from Satan's power to God, receive forgiveness, receive inheritance among the sanctified, by faith in Christ — six dimensions of salvation in a single commission clause.

Context

The Agrippa speech is the climax of Paul's captivity apologetic — the most comprehensive statement of the Gospel in Acts. The audience is the most distinguished he has addressed, and the speech is the most theologically complete and personally vulnerable (v.11: 'I was so obsessed with persecuting them'). Paul's conclusion (v.29: 'I pray that all may become what I am, except for these chains') is the boldest evangelistic appeal in Acts — a prisoner inviting a king to share his faith.

  • Acts 9:1–19The first Damascus account — told by Luke as narrator. Acts 26 adds 'kicking against the goads,' the fullest commission statement, and Paul's immediate obedience.
  • Acts 13:47"I have made you a light for the Gentiles" — the Isaiah 49:6 quotation Paul applied at Pisidian Antioch. The Agrippa speech gives the full theological content behind that commission.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
26:8

"Why should any of you consider it incredible that God raises the dead?" — MacArthur notes Paul addresses the philosophical objection head-on. The question reframes the debate: the issue is not whether resurrection is possible but whether God exists. If God exists, resurrection is the natural consequence of his power over death.

26:19–20

"I was not disobedient to the vision from heaven. First to those in Damascus, then to those in Jerusalem... then to the Gentiles" — MacArthur: Paul summarises his entire missionary career in two sentences. The geographical progression mirrors Acts 1:8 — Paul has fulfilled the commission he received.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
26:6

"It is because of my hope in what God has promised our ancestors that I am on trial today" — Calvin: Paul's entire defence is that he is not departing from Israel's hope but fulfilling it. Christianity is not a replacement of Judaism but its consummation.

26:22

"I am saying nothing beyond what the prophets and Moses said would happen" — Calvin: Paul's Gospel is fully contained in the OT. He claims not new revelation but the correct interpretation of existing revelation. This is the Reformation principle that Christ is the key to the OT.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
26:14

The NET notes the Greek proverb about kicking against goads — sharp pointed sticks used to prod oxen — is the metaphor for the force already at work in Paul: possibly his encounters with Stephen's testimony, his knowledge of the OT, or his observation of Christian courage under persecution.

26:18

The NET notes "a place among those who are sanctified by faith in me" (klēron en tois hēgiasmenois) uses the OT language of inheritance/allotment. Gentiles receive not merely forgiveness but a full inheritance in God's people — the equal standing that was the theological heart of the Jerusalem Council decision.

Robertson — Word Pictures

26:14

"It is hard for you to kick against the goads" — Robertson notes this Greek proverb (Euripides' Bacchae 794–5) is unique to Acts 26. It suggests Paul's persecution was itself a form of resistance to something already at work within him. Robertson connects this to Paul's later description of his pre-conversion state as wretched (Rom 7:24).

26:18

Robertson on the six-dimensional commission: "open their eyes, turn from darkness to light, from the power of Satan to God, receive forgiveness of sins, a place among the sanctified, by faith in me." Robertson: the most theologically comprehensive statement of the Gospel's purpose in Acts.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
26:2–3

Keener notes Paul's opening is genuine, not merely rhetorical. Agrippa was genuinely 'well acquainted with all the Jewish customs and controversies' — as a Herodian king with authority over the high priesthood and intimate knowledge of Jewish politics, he was better equipped to understand Paul's case than any previous audience.

26:16–18

Keener notes the commission statement combines language from multiple Servant Songs (Isaiah 42:6–7, 16; 49:6; 61:1–2) and Ezekiel 2:1–3. Paul presents himself as the inheritor of the prophetic and servant tradition — his Gentile mission is not innovation but the fulfilment of what the whole prophetic corpus pointed toward.

Verses 19—32 — Paul’s Mission; Agrippa: Almost You Persuade Me

Greek Word Study

Ἐν ὀλίγῳ με πείθεις Χριστιανὸν ποιῆσαι En oligō me peitheis Christianon poiēsai — Do you think that in such a short time you can persuade me to be a Christian?: Agrippa's response in v.28 — 'en oligō' (in a little) could mean in a short time or with small effort. Paul's reply works regardless: 'Short time or long — I pray that all may become what I am.' Agrippa is engaged but deflects; Festus dismisses outright. Two responses to the same Gospel.

παρεκτὸς τῶν δεσμῶν τούτων parektos tōn desmōn toutōn — except for these chains: Paul's closing appeal in v.29 — the most daring close of any speech in Acts. He invites the king, the governor, and the entire audience to become what he is — minus the physical restraints. The chains are the measure of what he has paid for his faith; he would pay it again.

Context

The three responses to Paul's speech are Luke's typology of responses to the Gospel: Festus (intellectual dismissal — 'you are out of your mind'), Agrippa (engaged deflection — 'do you think you can persuade me?'), and the official verdict (legal but not personal acknowledgment — 'this man has done nothing wrong'). Paul's closing appeal — praying that all may become what he is except for the chains — is simultaneously the boldest and most humble evangelistic statement in the NT.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Corinthians 1:18"The message of the cross is foolishness to those who are perishing" — Festus's "you are out of your mind" is the Corinthian reaction to the resurrection, encountered at the highest political level.
  • Acts 17:32"When they heard about the resurrection of the dead, some of them sneered" — the Athens reaction mirrors Festus's. The resurrection is consistently the point of intellectual dismissal.
  • Acts 26:29"I pray to God that... all who are listening to me today may become what I am, except for these chains" — the most comprehensive evangelistic appeal in Acts.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
26:29

MacArthur on Paul's response to Agrippa: 'I pray that... all who are listening to me today may become what I am, except for these chains.' The prisoner-apostle prays for the freedom of his jailers. The chains become a mark of privilege — he would not trade his position except to spare others the physical restraint.

26:32

MacArthur on Agrippa's final verdict: Paul could have been freed — but his freedom would mean not reaching Rome. His chains are the providential mechanism of the mission's fulfilment. Divine sovereignty and human freedom operate simultaneously: Paul chose to appeal; God chose to use that choice to fulfil his purposes.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
26:24

Calvin on Festus's accusation: 'your great learning is driving you insane' — Calvin notes the irony: Festus attributes Paul's belief to intellectual excess. In fact, Paul's belief comes not from learning but from direct encounter with the risen Christ. Education and faith are not in tension; Festus simply cannot imagine a learned man sincerely believing in resurrection.

26:29

Calvin on 'except for these chains': the chains are Paul's ultimate credential — the cost he has paid for what he is inviting others to. The most credible evangelism is not argument but life: Paul's invitation carries weight because he has obviously counted the cost and found it worth paying.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
26:28

The NET Bible translates "In a short time you will persuade me to act like a Christian" — taking poiēsai as "to act like" rather than "to become." The NET footnote discusses the ambiguity: ironic dismissal, genuine near-conviction, or something in between.

26:32

The NET notes the irony: the statement simultaneously declares Paul's innocence (he could have been freed) and confirms the irrevocability of the appeal. Paul's chains are, in the deepest sense, voluntary — he chose the appeal that made them necessary.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
26:24

"You are out of your mind, Paul!" — Robertson notes mainē (you are raving/insane) was also used of the disciples at Pentecost (2:13) and Rhoda (12:15). The resurrection consistently produces the charge of insanity from those who cannot accept it.

26:28

Robertson on Agrippa's response: the Greek is ambiguous — 'in a little' could be irony or near-seriousness. Robertson prefers the ironic reading but notes Agrippa's engagement with Paul's speech was genuine — his is not Festus's dismissal but a more complex deflection.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
26:28

Keener notes the debate about Agrippa's response. The traditional reading 'almost thou persuadest me' (KJV) takes it as near-conviction; modern translations tend toward irony. Keener argues for genuine engagement with residual deflection: Agrippa was sufficiently troubled to ask a personal question but sufficiently politically aware to deflect rather than commit.

26:32

Keener notes the fifth innocence declaration and its qualification. 'This man could have been set free if he had not appealed to Caesar' — simultaneously declares Paul's innocence and confirms the irrevocability of the appeal. The Roman legal system has both vindicated Paul and locked him into the journey to Rome.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Paul↗ People
Apostle to the Gentiles; author of thirteen NT letters
Paul (Saul of Tarsus) is introduced as a persecutor at Stephen’s stoning (Acts 7:58) and is dramatically converted on the Damascus road (Acts 9). He dominates Acts 13–28 across three missionary journeys, planting churches from Cyprus to Greece to Rome. His final journey to Rome as a prisoner frames Acts’ theological climax: the gospel reaching the empire’s capital, "boldly and without hindrance" (Acts 28:31).
Festus↗ People
Felix’s successor as governor; sent Paul to Rome
Porcius Festus replaces Felix as governor (c.59–60 AD). He immediately confronts the Paul case, offering Paul a Jerusalem trial — which Paul refuses by appealing to Caesar (Acts 25:11). Festus consults Agrippa II before writing his report to Rome, acknowledging that Paul’s charges concern "their own religion and about a certain Jesus who had died, but whom Paul asserted to be alive" (Acts 25:19). He arranges Paul’s hearing before Agrippa (Acts 25:23–26:32).
Agrippa↗ People
Herod Agrippa II; king before whom Paul defends himself (Acts 26)
Herod Agrippa II is the last significant Herodian ruler, client-king of several territories and a man expert in Jewish customs and disputes (Acts 26:3). Paul’s defence before him in Acts 26 is the most polished forensic speech in Acts. Agrippa’s response — "In a short time you would persuade me to be a Christian?" (Acts 26:28) — is ambiguous: half-moved but unwilling to commit. He agrees with Festus that Paul could have been released had he not appealed to Caesar.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 26Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 26 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
