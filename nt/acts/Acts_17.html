Acts 17

Thessalonica, Berea, and Athens

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Verses 1–15 — Thessalonica and Berea
1When Paul and his companions had passed through Amphipolis and Apollonia, they came to Thessalonica, where there was a Jewish synagogue.2As was his custom, Paul went into the synagogue, and on three Sabbath days he reasoned with them from the Scriptures,3explaining and proving that the Messiah had to suffer and rise from the dead. "This Jesus I am proclaiming to you is the Messiah," he said.4Some of the Jews were persuaded and joined Paul and Silas, as did a large number of God-fearing Greeks and quite a few prominent women.5But other Jews were jealous; so they rounded up some bad characters from the marketplace, formed a mob and started a riot in the city. They rushed to Jason's house in search of Paul and Silas in order to bring them out to the crowd.6But when they did not find them, they dragged Jason and some other believers before the city officials, shouting: "These men who have caused trouble all over the world have come here,7and Jason has welcomed them into his house. They are all defying Caesar's decrees, saying that there is another king, one called Jesus."8When they heard this, the crowd and the city officials were thrown into turmoil.9Then they made Jason and the others post bond and let them go.10As soon as it was night, the believers sent Paul and Silas away to Berea. On arriving there, they went to the Jewish synagogue.11Now the Berean Jews were of more noble character than those in Thessalonica, for they received the message with great eagerness and examined the Scriptures every day to see if what Paul said was true.12As a result, many of them believed, as did also a number of prominent Greek women and many Greek men.13But when the Jews in Thessalonica learned that Paul was preaching the word of God at Berea, some of them went there too, agitating the crowds and stirring them up.14The believers immediately sent Paul to the coast, but Silas and Timothy stayed at Berea.15Those who escorted Paul brought him to Athens and then left with instructions for Silas and Timothy to join him as soon as possible.

Greek Word Study

εὐγενέστεροι eugenesteroi — more noble character: used of the Berean Jews in v.11 — nobility defined not by birth but by intellectual and spiritual posture: receiving with eagerness AND examining the Scriptures daily to verify. Both elements together constitute biblical nobility.

βασιλεύς ἕτερος basileus heteros — another king: the charge against Paul in v.7 — claiming allegiance to a basileus other than Caesar was potentially treasonous in the Roman world. The charge is technically accurate: Paul does proclaim Jesus as Lord of lords, though the kingdom he announces is not the political revolution the charge implies.

Context

The Thessalonica charge — 'these men who have caused trouble all over the world have come here' (v.6) — is Acts' most dramatic summary of the Gospel's reach. The accusers unwittingly describe the mission's global scope. The specific charge of 'another king' (v.7) reflects the Roman political sensitivity to any competing loyalty — a charge that will follow Paul all the way to Rome.

MacArthur on Paul's method: "reasoning from the Scriptures, explaining and proving" — dialegomai (reasoned), dianoigō (opened/explained), paratithēmi (placed alongside/proved). Paul's preaching is not emotive persuasion but rational argument from Scripture. The Gospel is defensible, not merely feelable.

MacArthur on the Berean model: the combination of eagerness (receptivity) and daily examination (critical engagement) is the NT model for receiving teaching. Neither eagerness without discernment nor discernment without openness constitutes biblical nobility. Both together do.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:3

"The Messiah had to suffer and rise from the dead" — Calvin on the dei (must) of necessity: Christ's suffering was not accidental but divinely ordained. The OT testimony to the necessity of the Messiah's suffering is Paul's primary apologetic tool throughout his synagogue ministry.

17:11

Calvin on Berean nobility: eagerness and examination are not opposites but companions. The truly humble person is both eager to receive God's word and careful to verify that what they're receiving is indeed God's word. This is the Reformed hermeneutic: receive Scripture with eagerness; test all teaching by Scripture.

17:7

The NET notes that "defying Caesar's decrees" refers to Roman imperial edicts — possibly against unauthorised religious associations or against predicting a change of rulers. The charge is politically sophisticated, designed to alarm Roman authorities rather than Jewish ones.

The NET notes that eugenesteroi (more noble) is a social term applied here to intellectual and spiritual virtue — nobility defined by openness to truth and diligence in examining it, not by birth or social standing.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
17:2

"As was his custom, Paul went into the synagogue" — Robertson notes the imperfect with customary force: this was Paul's consistent practice in every new city. The synagogue gave him a ready audience of Jewish monotheists and God-fearers — the ideal bridge between Jewish tradition and Gentile mission.

17:11

"They examined the Scriptures every day" — Robertson notes the imperfect anakrinō (ongoing, daily critical examination). The Bereans did not simply accept Paul's interpretation but tested it against the text. Robertson: "the model of all true biblical Christianity: receive with eagerness, verify with diligence."

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:6

"These men who have caused trouble all over the world" (anastatōsantes tēn oikoumenēn) — Keener notes the dramatic scope. The oikoumenē is the entire inhabited world. The accusers unwittingly testify to the mission's global reach — Acts' most ironic moment: opponents' exaggerated charge confirms what Luke has been describing.

17:11

Keener notes the Bereans' practice was unusual: most synagogues rejected Paul's interpretation immediately. The Bereans treated his argument as worthy of serious examination — a combination of openness and rigour that Luke explicitly commends.

Verses 16–34 — Paul at Athens — The Areopagus Speech
16While Paul was waiting for them in Athens, he was greatly distressed to see that the city was full of idols.17So he reasoned in the synagogue with both Jews and God-fearing Greeks, as well as in the marketplace day by day with those who happened to be there.18A group of Epicurean and Stoic philosophers began to debate with him. Some of them asked, "What is this babbler trying to say?" Others remarked, "He seems to be advocating foreign gods." They said this because Paul was preaching the good news about Jesus and the resurrection.19Then they took him and brought him to a meeting of the Areopagus, where they said, "May we know what this new teaching is that you are presenting?20You are bringing some strange ideas to our ears, and we would like to know what they mean."21(All the Athenians and the foreigners who lived there spent their time doing nothing but talking about and listening to the latest ideas.)22Paul then stood up in the meeting of the Areopagus and said: "People of Athens! I see that in every way you are very religious.23For as I walked around and looked carefully at your objects of worship, I even found an altar with this inscription: TO AN UNKNOWN GOD. So you are ignorant of the very thing you worship — and this is what I am going to proclaim to you.24"The God who made the world and everything in it is the Lord of heaven and earth and does not live in temples built by human hands.25And he is not served by human hands, as if he needed anything. Rather, he himself gives everyone life and breath and everything else.26From one man he made all the nations, that they should inhabit the whole earth; and he marked out their appointed times in history and the boundaries of their lands.27God did this so that they would seek him and perhaps reach out for him and find him, though he is not far from any one of us.28"'For in him we live and move and have our being.' As some of your own poets have said, 'We are his offspring.'29Therefore since we are God's offspring, we should not think that the divine being is like gold or silver or stone — an image made by human design and skill.30In the past God overlooked such ignorance, but now he commands all people everywhere to repent.31For he has set a day when he will judge the world in righteousness by the man he has appointed. He has given proof of this to everyone by raising him from the dead."32When they heard about the resurrection of the dead, some of them sneered, but others said, "We want to hear you again on this subject."33At that, Paul left the Council.34Some of the people became followers of Paul and believed. Among them was Dionysius, a member of the Areopagus, also a woman named Damaris, and a number of others.

Greek Word Study

κατείδωλον kateidōlon — full of idols: a hapax legomenon in the NT — kata (thoroughly) + eidōlon (idol). Athens was the most religiously dense city in the ancient world. Paul's response is parōxyneto — his spirit was provoked, inflamed — not merely saddened. The word suggests righteous indignation, not philosophical curiosity.

ἀγνοοῦντες agnoountes — ignorant / not knowing: the 'unknown God' altar (v.23) represents what Paul calls ignorance — agnoountes. The Athenians' religious thoroughness led them to hedge with an altar to any god they might have missed. Paul takes their unknowing and turns it into an introduction: the God you don't know is the one I'm here to declare.

The Areopagus ('Hill of Ares/Mars') was both a rocky hill northwest of the Acropolis and the ancient Athenian council that met there. Paul addresses this institution — the most prestigious intellectual and civic body in Athens. The setting is the equivalent of a modern nation's supreme philosophical forum.
Athens — The Agora
37.9752° N, 23.7219° E
The Athenian agora (marketplace) was the commercial, civic, and philosophical heart of Athens — where Socrates had debated. Paul debates 'day by day' in this same space, engaging Epicureans (pleasure/tranquility; no divine providence) and Stoics (reason/virtue; divine logos in all things).

Context

The Areopagus speech is the most carefully crafted address in Acts — designed for a Greek philosophical audience with no OT background. Paul builds on common ground: their religious thoroughness, the unknown God altar, Greek poets' statements about divine immanence (Aratus: 'We are his offspring'; Epimenides: 'In him we live and move and have our being'). He then uses these as stepping stones to the biblical God who made, sustains, and will judge all — climaxing with the resurrection. The Athens speech is the full elaboration of the Lystra natural theology argument (14:15–17).

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 14:15–17The Lystra natural theology sermon — the template for Athens. Both begin with the living God who made everything; Athens is the sophisticated elaboration.
  • Romans 1:18–23"What may be known about God is plain to them, because God has made it plain to them." Paul's theological account of natural revelation underpins the Areopagus argument.
  • 1 Corinthians 1:18–25"Greeks look for wisdom, but we preach Christ crucified... foolishness to Gentiles." Paul's later reflection on what the Athens experience taught him about proclamation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
17:22–23

MacArthur on the unknown God altar: it is not flattering bridge but diagnostic disclosure. The hedge exposes ignorance — and Paul takes that exposure as his starting point. He neither condemns their idolatry in the opening nor approves it. He starts where they are to bring them where they need to be.

17:30–31

"Now he commands all people everywhere to repent" — the universal command is the most confrontational element. MacArthur notes that Paul's address ends with exactly what his opponents feared: a universal claim that overrides all local religious and political loyalties.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:24–25

Calvin on 'he is not served by human hands, as if he needed anything' — the theological demolition of all forms of works-righteousness. God needs nothing from humanity; all religious service flows from his grace downward. We serve God by receiving what he gives, not by supplying what he lacks.

17:27

Calvin on 'so that they would seek him and perhaps reach out for him and find him' — God has placed himself within reach of all human beings through creation and providence. Their failure to find him is not due to God's hiddenness but to human sinfulness (Rom 1:18ff.). Natural revelation is real and sufficient to render humanity without excuse, but insufficient to produce saving knowledge.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:28

The NET notes Paul quotes two different Greek poets: 'In him we live and move and have our being' from Epimenides of Crete; 'We are his offspring' from Aratus, Phaenomena 5 (270 BC). Both poets meant their statements about Zeus; Paul redirects them to the God of Scripture.

17:31

"He has given proof of this to everyone by raising him from the dead" — the NET notes that pistis is used here in the unusual sense of "proof" or "assurance" (the only such usage in Acts). The resurrection is the public, verifiable evidence that God has appointed this man as judge.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
17:22

"In every way you are very religious" — Robertson notes that deisidaimōn could mean "very devout" (a compliment) or "very superstitious" (a mild rebuke). Paul uses the ambiguity deliberately: he begins with a compliment that contains a correction. Their religious thoroughness is both their virtue and their problem.

17:28

Robertson on the Greek poets: "In him we live and move and have our being" is from Epimenides of Crete (c. 6th century BC); "We are his offspring" is from Aratus's Phaenomena (c. 270 BC). Paul is not endorsing Greek pantheism — he is using points of contact to carry the biblical message into Greek minds.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:23

Keener notes that altars "To an Unknown God" (agnōstō theō) are attested in ancient sources — Pausanias and Philostratus both mention such altars at Athens. Paul turns their theological hedging into a declaration: the God they are covering themselves about is the God he can name.

17:28

Keener: quoting Aratus and Epimenides in a philosophical setting is cultural competence, not cultural capitulation — the kind of cross-cultural communication Paul describes in 1 Cor 9:19–23 ('I have become all things to all people'). Paul uses the best of Greek thought to carry the biblical message into Greek minds.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

Literary Structure

The Areopagus Speech — Bridge Structure — vv.22–31The Athens speech moves from common ground (religious thoroughness, unknown God altar) through natural theology (creator God, sustainer, sovereign over history) through Greek poetry (divine immanence) to the biblical punchline (judgment and resurrection). Each step uses Athens's own resources to go further than Athens's own thought can reach.
Berean vs Athenian Reception — vv.11,34The contrast between Berea (examined Scriptures daily, many believed) and Athens (sneered at resurrection, few believed) is Luke's implicit commentary. The Bereans had Scripture as a common reference point; the Athenians did not. The 'best intellectual audience' produced the smallest harvest — natural philosophy cannot replace scriptural preparation.
Acts 17 presents the Gospel's encounter with both Jewish Scripture-literacy (Berea) and Greek philosophical sophistication (Athens) — engaging both seriously while depending on neither. Only the resurrection produces the crisis of decision that genuine faith requires.
