Acts 10

Peter and Cornelius — The Gentile Pentecost

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Verses 1–23 — Cornelius's Vision and Peter's Trance
1At Caesarea there was a man named Cornelius, a centurion in what was known as the Italian Regiment.2He and all his family were devout and God-fearing; he gave generously to those in need and prayed to God regularly.3One day at about three in the afternoon he had a vision. He distinctly saw an angel of God, who came to him and said, "Cornelius!"4Cornelius stared at him in fear. "What is it, Lord?" he asked. The angel answered, "Your prayers and gifts to the poor have come up as a memorial offering before God.5Now send men to Joppa to bring back a man named Simon who is called Peter.6He is staying with Simon the tanner, whose house is by the sea."7When the angel who spoke to him had gone, Cornelius called two of his servants and a devout soldier who was one of his attendants.8He told them everything that had happened and sent them to Joppa.9About noon the following day as they were on their journey and approaching the city, Peter went up on the roof to pray.10He became hungry and wanted something to eat, and while the meal was being prepared, he fell into a trance.11He saw heaven opened and something like a large sheet being let down to earth by its four corners.12It contained all kinds of four-footed animals, as well as reptiles and birds.13Then a voice told him, "Get up, Peter. Kill and eat."14"Surely not, Lord!" Peter replied. "I have never eaten anything impure or unclean."15The voice spoke to him a second time, "Do not call anything impure that God has made clean."16This happened three times, and immediately the sheet was taken back to heaven.17While Peter was wondering about the meaning of the vision, the men sent by Cornelius found out where Simon's house was and stopped at the gate.18They called out, asking if Simon who was known as Peter was staying there.19While Peter was still thinking about the vision, the Spirit said to him, "Simon, three men are looking for you.20So get up and go downstairs. Do not hesitate to go with them, for I have sent them."21Peter went down and said to the men, "I'm the one you're looking for. Why have you come?"22The men replied, "We have come from Cornelius the centurion. He is a righteous and God-fearing man, who is respected by all the Jewish people. A holy angel told him to ask you to come to his house so that he could hear what you have to say."23Then Peter invited the men in and gave them lodging. The next day Peter started out with them, and some of the believers from Joppa went along.

Greek Word Study

εὐσεβής eusebēs — devout/God-fearing: used both of Cornelius (vv.2, 7) and of his devout soldier. Describes Gentiles who revered the God of Israel, attended synagogue, gave to the poor, and prayed — without formal conversion to Judaism. The 'God-fearer' category was documented in first-century synagogue inscriptions (notably the Aphrodisias inscription, discovered 1976).

ἐκστάσει ekstasei — trance: Peter's trance (ekstasis — a going-out of oneself) is distinct from Cornelius's vision (horama). Together they show divine orchestration converging from two directions on a single moment — neither man initiates; both respond to heaven's direction.

Places

Caesarea Maritima
32.5000° N, 34.9000° E
The Roman administrative capital of Judea, built by Herod the Great as a showcase Greco-Roman port city named for Caesar Augustus. Home of the Roman prefect and military garrison. Cornelius's presence as a centurion is historically plausible — Caesarea was the Roman military headquarters for the region.
Rooftop at Joppa
32.0543° N, 34.7510° E
Peter's trance occurs on a housetop (dōma — flat roof). Mediterranean houses commonly had flat rooftops used for prayer and rest. Peter's elevation — above street level — mirrors the vision's upward movement: the sheet comes down from heaven while Peter is raised above the ground.

Context

The 'God-fearers' (theosebeis, phoboumenoi ton theon) were Gentiles who had adopted Jewish monotheism and ethics without completing formal conversion. They attended synagogues, observed some Jewish practices, and gave to Jewish causes. They were a significant population across the Diaspora world and became a primary harvest field for Paul's Gentile mission. Peter's sheet vision (vv.11–16) is commonly read as abolishing food laws — but its primary application is stated explicitly in v.28: 'God has shown me that I should not call anyone impure or unclean.' The food imagery is the vehicle; the human application is the point.

Cross-Reference

  • Mark 7:18–19"He declared all foods clean" — Jesus's teaching that Luke applies practically in Acts 10. The abolition of food distinctions was theologically prepared for; Acts 10 is its social-missional application.
  • Isaiah 56:6–7"The foreigners who bind themselves to the Lord... these I will bring to my holy mountain." The OT promise of Gentile inclusion that Cornelius's reception fulfils.
  • Acts 1:8"To the ends of the earth" — Cornelius represents the first formal fulfilment of the Gentile stage of the commission.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
10:2

Robertson notes the precise categories for Cornelius: devout (eusebēs), God-fearing, generous to the poor, regular in prayer. These four qualities describe the ideal God-fearer — a Gentile who had come as close to the covenant community as Judaism's boundary markers permitted. The Gospel now comes to remove those barriers.

10:14

"Surely not, Lord!" — Robertson notes this is the fourth time Peter says "surely not, Lord" in the Gospels and Acts (cf. Matt 16:22; John 13:8). Peter's consistent pattern of well-intentioned resistance to divine direction makes the vision's three-fold repetition pedagogically necessary.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:1–2

Keener provides extensive background on Roman centurions in the NT. Centurions commanded approximately eighty soldiers and were the backbone of the Roman military — typically career soldiers of proven reliability. The NT consistently portrays centurions favourably (Luke 7:1–10; 23:47; Acts 27:43).

10:3–4

Keener notes that Cornelius's prayers and alms "have come up as a memorial offering before God" echoes the incense offering rising before the LORD in the Temple. Cornelius's non-Temple piety has been received by God as an offering — a remarkable statement about the reach of divine grace before formal Gospel proclamation.

Verses 24–48 — Peter Preaches to Cornelius; The Spirit Falls on Gentiles
24The following day he arrived in Caesarea. Cornelius was expecting them and had called together his relatives and close friends.25As Peter entered the house, Cornelius met him and fell at his feet in reverence.26But Peter made him get up. He is a guest in the home of Simon the tanner, who lives by the sea.'33So I sent for you immediately, and it was good of you to come. They killed him by hanging him on a cross,40but God raised him from the dead on the third day and caused him to be seen.41He was not seen by all the people, but by witnesses whom God had already chosen — by us who ate and drank with him after he rose from the dead.42He commanded us to preach to the people and to testify that he is the one whom God appointed as judge of the living and the dead.43All the prophets testify about him that everyone who believes in him receives forgiveness of sins through his name."44While Peter was still speaking these words, the Holy Spirit came on all those who heard the message.45The circumcised believers who had come with Peter were astonished that the gift of the Holy Spirit had been poured out even on Gentiles.46For they heard them speaking in tongues and praising God. Then Peter said,47"Surely no one can stand in the way of their being baptized with water. They have received the Holy Spirit just as we have."48So he ordered that they be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ. Then they asked Peter to stay with them for a few days.

Greek Word Study

οὐ λαμβάνει πρόσωπον ou lambanei prosōpon — does not show favoritism: literally 'does not receive a face.' The Hebrew idiom nasa panim (lift the face) meant to show partiality based on status. Peter's declaration in v.34 is a direct reversal of everything Jewish ethnic-religious boundary theology had maintained.

πάντων κύριος pantōn kyrios — Lord of all: this absolute title for Christ appears only here in Acts. Not Lord of Israel, not Lord of believers, but Lord of all peoples — the theological foundation for the universal Gentile mission. If Christ is Lord of all, the Gospel must reach all.

Places

32.5000° N, 34.9000° E
A substantial dwelling capable of hosting 'relatives and close friends' (v.24). Luke's precision about the gathering — family, friends, possibly servants — suggests a significant household of social standing. The Gospel's first formal Gentile reception is not a private meeting but a community event.
Caesarea — Roman Capital
32.5000° N, 34.9000° E
Caesarea Maritima was the seat of Roman power in Judea — home to the prefect and the military headquarters. That the Gentile Pentecost occurs here (in the Roman administrative centre) rather than in the Jewish capital carries programmatic significance for the mission's direction toward Rome.

Context

Peter's address to Cornelius's household (vv.34–43) is the most compact and complete summary of the Gospel kerygma in Acts — moving from John's baptism through Jesus's ministry, death, resurrection, and appearances to the commission to witness, ending with the offer of forgiveness. It is the template for apostolic preaching. The Spirit falls while Peter is still speaking — before baptism — demonstrating that the order is God's to determine. Peter's conclusion (v.47: 'Can anyone keep these people from being baptized? They have received the Holy Spirit just as we have') shows that baptism follows the Spirit's work as its public seal, not its prerequisite.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 2:38The Pentecost pattern: repent → be baptized → receive the Spirit. The Cornelius episode inverts the Spirit-baptism order, demonstrating that the sequence is flexible while the substance (faith, repentance, Spirit, baptism) remains constant.
  • Joel 2:28Quoted at Pentecost: "I will pour out my Spirit on all people" — the "all" is now visibly fulfilled as the Spirit falls on Gentiles.
  • Acts 15:7–11Peter's later testimony at the Jerusalem Council recalls the Cornelius episode as decisive evidence: 'God, who knows the heart, showed that he accepted them by giving the Holy Spirit to them, just as he did to us.'

10:34–35

MacArthur carefully distinguishes Peter's statement about God accepting those who fear him from universalism. Verse 36 immediately clarifies: the "message God sent" is the Gospel of peace through Jesus Christ. Cornelius's piety had not saved him — it had prepared him to receive the Gospel.

Where God has given the Spirit, the church must not withhold baptism on cultural or ethnic grounds.

10:34

Calvin on 'God does not show favoritism' — the demolition of all national, ethnic, and religious privilege before God. The Jew has no advantage over the Greek in the matter of divine acceptance. All stand equally before the grace that accepts those who fear God in every nation.

NET Bible Notes

10:36

The NET notes that "Lord of all" (pantōn Kyrios) is an unusual title for Christ in Acts, found only here. Its absolute scope is the theological foundation for the universal Gentile mission: if Christ is Lord of all peoples, the Gospel must reach all peoples.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
10:34–35

Robertson on Peter's summary: 'accepts from every nation the one who fears him and does what is right' is not universalism (salvation apart from Christ) but the summary of what genuine seeking looks like in every culture. The Cornelius episode shows God responding to genuine seeking by providing the full Gospel.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:36

The phrase "Jesus Christ, who is Lord of all" (Kyrios pantōn) is a direct challenge to the Roman imperial title Dominus et Deus noster claimed by Domitian and echoed in earlier imperial ideology. Luke's proclamation of Christ as "Lord of all" is politically charged in the Caesarean military headquarters context.

10:44–46

Keener notes that tongues at Cornelius's house function as the sign that the Gentile believers have received the same Spirit as the Jewish believers at Pentecost. Without a visible, audible sign, Peter and the circumcised believers would have had no external evidence. The tongues serve a specific ecclesiological function.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Peter
Apostle — first to baptise a Gentile household
Receives a vision of unclean animals and the divine command to "not call anything impure that God has made clean." When Cornelius's servants arrive, he goes willingly — and the Spirit falls on the Gentiles before baptism even happens. Peter cannot withhold the water.
Cornelius
Roman centurion; first Gentile convert
A "God-fearer" — a Gentile who worships Israel's God, gives generously, and prays regularly. His vision of an angel and his prayers reaching God demonstrate that Gentile devotion to YHWH was already being received. His household receives the Spirit in a Gentile Pentecost.

Literary Structure

Parallel Visions — Divine Orchestration — vv.3–20The simultaneous orchestration of Cornelius's vision (3 PM) and Peter's trance (about noon the following day, as the messengers approach) is Luke's literary demonstration of divine sovereignty over the Gentile mission. Neither man initiates; both respond to heavenly direction. The mission is God's, not theirs — engineered from heaven before either man took a step.
God Does Not Show Favouritism — v34 as Thesis — vv.34–48Peter's declaration 'I now realise how true it is that God does not show favoritism' (v34) is the theological thesis of the entire Cornelius narrative. Everything that follows — sermon, Spirit's falling, baptism — demonstrates this thesis. Luke structures the chapter as evidence for a proposition: the Cornelius story is an argument, not just an event.
Acts 10 is the theological fulcrum of the Gentile mission: the Spirit falls on Gentiles before baptism, establishing divine approval that no human tradition can override. The chapter becomes the decisive evidence at the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15) and the permanent theological charter for cross-cultural mission.
