Acts 22

Paul's Defence Before the Jerusalem Crowd

Verses 1—21 — Paul’s Defence: His Conversion and Call
123456789101112131415161718192021

Greek Word Study

Ἑβραΐδι διαλέκτῳ Hebraidi dialektō — in Aramaic: v.2 — the crowd becomes quiet when Paul speaks Aramaic (the common tongue of Palestinian Jews). Language choice is itself a rhetorical act: Paul establishes immediate common ground before saying a word of content. The silence is the first sign that the strategy is working.

Δίκαιον Dikaion — the Righteous One: Ananias's description of Jesus in v.14 — ton Dikaion (the Righteous One). This is one of the most concentrated Christological titles in Acts, linking Jesus to the OT figure of the Righteous Sufferer (Isaiah 53:11; Wisdom of Solomon 2:12–20). Paul's encounter with the Righteous One is his conversion's theological centre.

Context

This is the second of three Damascus road accounts in Acts (chs 9, 22, 26). Each retelling is tailored to its audience. Here, addressing a hostile Jewish crowd, Paul emphasises his Jewish credentials (Tarsus-born, Jerusalem-trained, Gamaliel-taught), Ananias's Jewish piety (v.12), and the Jerusalem temple vision (vv.17–21). He is establishing that his Gentile mission was not a personal preference or deviation from Judaism but a direct divine commission received in Jerusalem's most sacred space.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 9:1–19The first Damascus road account — told by Luke as narrator. Acts 22 adds: the Jerusalem temple vision (vv.17–21), Ananias's Jewish credentials (v.12), the 'Righteous One' title (v.14).
  • Galatians 1:13–16"You have heard of my previous way of life in Judaism, how intensely I persecuted the church of God... But God... was pleased to reveal his Son in me so that I might preach him among the Gentiles" — Paul's autobiographical account matches the Acts 22 version.
  • Acts 7:58"The witnesses laid their coats at the feet of a young man named Saul" — Paul now acknowledges his role at Stephen's stoning (v.20), closing the narrative arc that began in Acts 7.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:3

MacArthur on Paul's Jewish credentials: this is not mere rhetoric but genuine identity. Paul never ceased to be a Jew — he maintained Jewish practices throughout his ministry (Acts 18:18; 21:26). His Gentile mission was not a rejection of his Jewish heritage but its fulfilment. MacArthur uses this to argue against any anti-Semitic reading of Paul's theology.

22:17–21

MacArthur on the Jerusalem temple vision: Paul's commission to the Gentiles was given in Jerusalem's most sacred space — the Temple. MacArthur notes the irony: the very space the crowd is defending as holy is the space from which Paul was sent to the Gentiles. His mission was Jerusalem-rooted, not Jerusalem-abandoning.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:3

Calvin on 'brought up in this city': Paul's Jerusalem upbringing is part of his defense. He is not a foreign agitator but a Jerusalem man — trained in the city's most rigorous school. Calvin notes that Paul uses every legitimate credential to establish credibility before delivering the message that will make credibility irrelevant.

22:20

Calvin on 'the blood of your martyr Stephen was shed, I stood there giving my approval' — Paul's acknowledgment of his role at Stephen's death is, for Calvin, the most honest moment in the speech. He does not minimise his past; he displays it as the measure of what divine grace can transform. The persecutor's self-indictment becomes the most powerful testimony to conversion's reality.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:9

The NET notes the apparent tension between v.9 ("they did not understand the voice") and 9:7 ("they heard the sound but did not see anyone"). The NET harmonises: both use phōnē, which can mean a sound (general noise) or an intelligible voice. The companions heard a sound but not intelligible speech; Paul heard the voice clearly.

22:16

"Get up, be baptized and wash your sins away, calling on his name" — the NET notes that the Greek participle "calling on his name" (epikalesamenos) is the key: baptism is the outward act; calling on the name is the faith expression. The washing of sins is associated with calling on Christ's name, not with the water alone.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
22:3

"I studied under Gamaliel and was thoroughly trained in the law" — Robertson notes the precision: Paul claims both the content (law of our ancestors) and the method (thoroughly trained — kataeskeuasmenos — perfectly educated) of his Jewish formation. Gamaliel was the most respected teacher of the generation. Paul is establishing impeccable Jewish credentials before revealing his transformation.

22:14–15

"The God of our ancestors has chosen you to know his will and to see the Righteous One and to hear words from his mouth. You will be his witness to all people" — Robertson notes that Ananias's commission uses the language of the OT prophetic call (hear, see, witness). Paul's apostolic commission is framed as the fulfilment of a prophetic calling within the Jewish tradition.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:3

Keener provides background on Paul's education under Gamaliel. Gamaliel I (the Elder) was a Hillelite Pharisee — the grandson of Hillel, the most prominent rabbi of his generation. To study 'at the feet of Gamaliel' was the highest credential in first-century Jewish religious education. Paul is claiming the very best Jerusalem theological training.

22:12

Keener notes the deliberate contrast in how Ananias is described: in Acts 9:10 he is simply 'a disciple'; here he is 'a devout observer of the law and highly respected by all the Jews living there.' The description is tailored to the Jewish audience: Paul's conversion was mediated by someone whose Jewish credentials the crowd would recognise and respect.

Verses 22—30 — The Crowd’s Rage; Paul Claims Roman Citizenship
222324252627282930

Greek Word Study

Ῥωμαῖος Rhōmaios — Roman citizen: Paul's citizenship claim (v.25–28) is strategically deployed a second time (cf. 16:37). In Roman law, flogging a citizen without trial (the Valerian and Porcian laws) was illegal. Paul's claim is not a compromise of his willingness to suffer but a strategic use of legal protection for the church's benefit — establishing that the Gospel's spokesman has legal standing before Roman authority.

ἐγὼ δὲ καὶ γεγέννημαι egō de kai gegennēmai — But I was born a citizen: Paul's reply to the commander's purchased citizenship (v.28). Born citizenship was more prestigious than purchased citizenship in Roman society. Paul's birth citizenship in Tarsus (a Roman citizen city) gives him legal standing the commander had to buy. The contrast is Paul's quiet counter-punch.

The commander assembles the Sanhedrin to hear Paul's case — the fifth Sanhedrin confrontation in Acts (after chs 4, 5, 6, 7). But unlike the previous four, Paul is now in Roman custody rather than Jewish. The legal context has changed: Rome controls the proceedings.

Context

The trigger for the riot — 'Rid the earth of him!' (v.22) — is the word 'Gentiles.' Paul could say he was personally transformed, that he saw the Risen Christ, that he was commissioned in the Temple — the crowd endured all of this. But when Paul announces that his commission was to the Gentiles, the crowd erupts. The theological problem is not Paul's conversion but the implications of that conversion for Gentile inclusion. This is the Jerusalem Council controversy enacted at street level.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 21:36"Get rid of him!" — the same crowd cry at the Temple arrest. The verbal parallel to Jesus's passion (Luke 23:18: "Away with this man!") is deliberate.
  • Acts 16:37"They beat us publicly without a trial, even though we are Roman citizens" — the Philippi citizenship claim. Paul uses this right strategically, not routinely.
  • Romans 1:16"I am not ashamed of the gospel, because it is the power of God that brings salvation to everyone who believes: first to the Jew, then to the Gentile" — the theological principle behind Paul's Gentile commission, which the crowd rejected.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:22

MacArthur on the crowd's rage at 'Gentiles': this is the sharpest illustration in Acts of the Gospel's offence to Jewish exclusivism. Paul has established perfect Jewish credentials (v.3), described a genuine conversion (vv.6–16), and spoken of a temple vision (vv.17–21) — the crowd endured all of this. But when God's explicit commission includes Gentiles on equal terms, the crowd cannot bear it.

22:25

MacArthur on using legal rights: Paul does not invoke citizenship to avoid arrest or imprisonment (he went to Jerusalem knowing what awaited) but to avoid illegal treatment that would compromise his legal standing. MacArthur notes the distinction: Paul was willing to suffer what the law allowed; he was not willing to submit to what the law prohibited.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:22

Calvin on 'not fit to live': the crowd's verdict echoes every age's treatment of those who challenge religious exclusivism. Calvin notes that the most violent religious opposition is not to moral failure but to the inclusion of those who have been excluded. Paul's life is endangered not by what he did wrong but by what God declared right.

22:28

Calvin on Paul's birth citizenship: Calvin notes that God providentially arranged Paul's birth circumstances (Tarsus, Roman citizenship) decades before Paul needed those credentials. The legal protection that safeguards Paul's ministry to Rome was built into his biography at birth. Calvin uses this as a meditation on divine foresight in providential detail.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:22

The NET notes that "Rid the earth of him" (Aire apo tēs gēs) is literally "lift him from the earth" — a call for execution. The same phrase is used of Jesus in Luke 23:18 (in some manuscripts). Luke's linguistic parallel between the Jerusalem crowd's verdicts on Jesus and Paul is intentional.

22:29

"The commander himself was alarmed when he realized that he had put Paul, a Roman citizen, in chains" — the NET notes that the commander's alarm (ephobēthē) is the same word used of the Philippian magistrates who discovered they had beaten Roman citizens (16:38). The legal consequences of such an error were serious.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
22:22

"Rid the earth of him! He's not fit to live!" — Robertson notes this is the precise language used of Paul in Antioch Pisidia (13:50: "expel them from their region") and will be used again at Rome (28:19). The "he's not fit to live" is the same verdict the crowd rendered on Jesus (Luke 23:18–21). The parallel passion narrative continues.

22:25–28

Robertson on the citizenship exchange: Paul speaks at exactly the right moment — as they are stretching him out to flog him. The timing is both physically dramatic and legally precise. Robertson notes that claiming citizenship at the moment of illegal punishment (rather than before arrest) was a recognised legal procedure.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:22

Keener notes that the word 'Gentiles' (ta ethnē) triggered the riot because it implied equal standing — not merely that Paul preached to Gentiles (tolerable) but that God had sent him specifically to them (intolerable to Jewish nationalist sensibility). The crowd's reaction shows that the Jerusalem Council decision had not fully resolved the theological tension at the popular level.

22:28

Keener provides background on the two types of Roman citizenship. Purchased citizenship was available to wealthy non-Romans under certain conditions; it was nonetheless fully legal. Birth citizenship was inherited. Paul's birth citizenship in Tarsus was a consequence of Tarsus's status as a Roman citizen city — granted to Tarsus either by Pompey or Julius Caesar.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 22Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 22 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
