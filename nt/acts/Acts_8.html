Acts 8

The Church Scattered; Philip's Mission

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Greek Word Study

διασπείρω diaspeirō — scattered: from the same root as Diaspora — used three times in 8:1–4. The irony is precise: the very word for Jewish dispersion becomes the mechanism for gospel expansion. Persecution produces diaspora; diaspora produces mission. The enemies of the Gospel become its unwitting agents.

εὐαγγελίζομαι euangelizomai — preached the word / proclaimed the good news: used in v4 and v12. The scattered believers are not passive refugees but active evangelists. Luke's verb choice is deliberate: scattering produces not silence but speech.

Samaria — City

Context

The structural irony of Acts 8:1–4 is one of Luke's most important narrative moves: the persecution intended to silence the Gospel scatters its bearers, who then preach wherever they land. Luke makes this explicit in v4: 'those who had been scattered preached the word wherever they went.' Opposition becomes the mechanism of expansion — a principle that recurs throughout Acts. Simon Magus's desire to purchase the Spirit's power (vv.18–19) is the precise inversion of the Gospel's economics: the Spirit is given (v20: 'the gift of God'), not purchased. His name gives us the word 'simony' — the buying and selling of church offices.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:1

MacArthur notes the deliberate exception: "all except the apostles were scattered." The apostles remain in Jerusalem, providing a stable centre while the diaspora mission begins — not cowardice but calling. Their witness must be anchored in the city where the resurrection occurred.

8:20–23

"I see that you are full of bitterness and captive to sin" — Simon's baptism was not accompanied by genuine regeneration. MacArthur uses this as a cautionary lesson: formal profession and baptism do not guarantee salvation. The test is the heart's orientation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:4

Calvin on the scattered believers preaching: the proclamation of the Gospel is not confined to ordained ministers. Every believer scattered by persecution became a witness — not by usurping the ordained ministry but by fulfilling the basic calling of every Christian to confess Christ.

8:20–21

Calvin on "You have no part or share in this ministry" — the absolute language points to a heart entirely outside the covenant reality, despite external membership. This is Calvin's proof text for the distinction between the visible church (mixed congregation) and the invisible church (true believers only).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:16

The NET notes the theological difficulty of Samaritans baptized but not yet receiving the Spirit. The delay served a specific ecclesiological function: making the Samaritan community's equal reception of the Spirit public and verifiable before the Jerusalem community, preventing a divergent parallel church from forming.

8:20

"May your money perish with you" — the optative mood (eilē — may it be) is a formal curse formula. Peter is not merely criticising Simon but pronouncing a conditional judgment: if he does not repent, his money and his spiritual pretensions will both come to nothing.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
8:4

"Those who had been scattered preached the word wherever they went" — Robertson notes the imperfect tense (euangelizomenoi — were continually proclaiming). Every displaced believer became a mobile evangelist. Robertson calls this "the most effective missionary advance in the early church — carried out by laypeople, not apostles."

8:17

"Peter and John placed their hands on them, and they received the Holy Spirit" — the one case in Acts where the Spirit is given through apostolic hands rather than directly at baptism. The delay preserved the unity of the Samaritan church with Jerusalem at a critical juncture.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:9–11

Keener provides background on Simon's claim to be "the Great Power of God" (v10). In Gnostic traditions, a figure named Simon Magus became the founder of Gnosticism. What is certain is that claims to divine power through magical arts were common in the first-century Mediterranean, and Philip's Gospel outperformed them decisively.

8:16

"The Holy Spirit had not yet come on any of them" — Keener suggests the delay served to prevent the Samaritan church from developing as an independent movement. The apostolic confirmation in vv.14–17 united the Samaritan community to the Jerusalem church structurally before the two could diverge.

Verses 26—40 — Philip and the Ethiopian Eunuch: He Went on His Way Rejoicing

Greek Word Study

εὐνοῦχος eunouch — eunuch: a castrated male official, typically employed in royal courts. Eunuchs were excluded from the assembly of Israel (Deut 23:1) but Isaiah 56:3–5 promised them "a memorial and a name better than sons and daughters" in the new covenant community. Philip's baptism of this man is the enacted fulfilment of Isaiah's promise.

ἥρπασεν hērpasen — took away suddenly: the same verb (harpazō) used for Paul's heavenly vision (2 Cor 12:2) and for the eschatological gathering (1 Thess 4:17). Philip's transportation echoes Elijah's (1 Kings 18:12; 2 Kings 2:16) — the prophetic tradition of Spirit-directed movement.

Places

Gaza (Desert Road)
31.5017° N, 34.4667° E
The ancient city at the southern edge of Palestine, gateway to Egypt. The "desert road" (erēmos) was the less-travelled route — making the encounter all the more providential. Part of the ancient international trade route connecting the Mediterranean world with sub-Saharan Africa.
Azotus (Ashdod)
31.8028° N, 34.6499° E
Philip's arrival point after the Spirit's transportation (v40). The ancient Ashdod on the Philistine coastal plain. Philip preaches northward through the coastal towns to Caesarea, where he will later settle and raise a family of prophet-daughters (Acts 21:8–9).

Context

The Ethiopian eunuch represents two convergences: (1) geographically, he is from the 'ends of the earth' (Ethiopia was the edge of the known world — the implicit fulfilment of 1:8's final horizon); (2) theologically, he is one excluded from the assembly of Israel who is now included in the new covenant community (fulfilling Isaiah 56:3–5). That he was reading Isaiah 53 when Philip arrived is Luke's theological statement: the Scripture was already asking the question the Gospel answers. Philip does not impose the Gospel onto an unrelated text — he starts where the man is and shows how Isaiah 53 points to Jesus.

Cross-Reference

  • Isaiah 53:7–8The direct quotation (vv.32–33) — the Fourth Servant Song's description of vicarious suffering. Philip begins with this passage and proclaims "the good news about Jesus" — the apostolic hermeneutic in miniature.
  • Isaiah 56:3–5The promise to eunuchs of "a memorial and a name better than sons and daughters" — directly applicable to the Ethiopian eunuch's baptism. Philip enacted this promise without citing it.
  • Acts 1:8"To the ends of the earth" — the Ethiopian represents the geographic fulfilment of the commission's final horizon. Africa is reached before Europe.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:32–35

MacArthur uses this passage to establish the principle that all Scripture ultimately points to Christ. Philip starts with Isaiah 53 and "tells him the good news about Jesus" — not a separate message superimposed on the OT, but the OT's own meaning unpacked. Any Bible study that does not ultimately arrive at Christ has not yet arrived at the text's full meaning.

8:36–38

MacArthur notes the order: hearing the gospel (v35) → believing → requesting baptism (v36) → baptism (v38). The sequence is faith followed by baptism — not baptism producing faith.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:30–31

Calvin on "How can I, unless someone guides me?" — this is the argument for the preaching ministry. Private reading of Scripture is valuable; but the Holy Spirit works ordinarily through the ministry of explanation and application. The eunuch had the text; he lacked the interpreter. God provides Philip.

8:38–39

Calvin on the eunuch "going on his way rejoicing" — the joy that follows genuine encounter with the Gospel through Word and sacrament is the Spirit's work. Calvin notes the contrast with Simon Magus's unfounded confidence: the eunuch has the joy that comes from a right relationship with God.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:32–33

The NET notes that vv.32–33 quote Isaiah 53:7–8 from the LXX, which differs from the Hebrew in several details. Luke uses the LXX consistently; the theological point — the Servant's unjust suffering — is the same in both traditions.

8:37

Verse 37 ("I believe that Jesus Christ is the Son of God") is absent from the earliest manuscripts and is widely considered a later addition — reflecting early baptismal practice. The NET includes it with a full textual note.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
8:30

"Do you understand what you are reading?" — Robertson notes the Greek wordplay: ginōskeis ha anaginōskeis — "Do you know what you are reading back?" Philip asks if recognizing the letters is producing genuine understanding. The playful question opens the conversation naturally.

8:35

"Philip began with that very passage of Scripture and told him the good news about Jesus" — the imperfect ēuangelisato indicates Philip took time and developed his argument fully, starting from Isaiah 53 and moving through the full Gospel narrative.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:27

The "Kandake" (v27) is not a personal name but a title — the Meroitic word for queen mother of the Kushite royal court. Classical sources (Strabo, Pliny) confirm that the Kandake held governing authority. The eunuch's role as treasurer to the Kandake placed him at the highest level of royal administration.

8:38–39

The Spirit's transportation of Philip (v39) parallels Elijah's translation by the Spirit in 1 Kings 18:12 and 2 Kings 2:16. Luke presents this as a factual account of miraculous transportation, consistent with his historiographical method throughout Acts.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 8Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 8 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
