Acts 18

Corinth and Return

Verses 1—17 — Corinth: Aquila and Priscilla; A Year and a Half

Greek Word Study

σκηνοποιός skēnopoios — tentmaker: Paul, Aquila, and Priscilla share the same trade (v.3). His practice of supporting himself through manual labour while planting churches was deliberate (1 Cor 9:12–18) — preventing accusations of financial exploitation and modelling self-supporting ministry.

πολὺς λαός polys laos — many people: the Lord's reassurance in v.10 — 'I have many people in this city.' The Greek polys laos is OT covenant language for God's elect people. The Lord is telling Paul that Corinth has many elect still to be gathered — divine sovereignty in salvation grounds apostolic boldness in proclamation.

Places

Corinth
37.9080° N, 22.8785° E
The commercial capital of Roman Greece — a rebuilt Roman colony on the narrow isthmus connecting central Greece to the Peloponnese, controlling two ports. One of the wealthiest and most cosmopolitan cities in the Empire. Paul's eighteen months here plant one of his most complex and fruitful churches.
The Tribunal (Bema)
37.9080° N, 22.8785° E
The bema (judgment seat) at Corinth has been archaeologically confirmed — a large marble rostrum in the agora where the proconsul held court. This is the very place where Gallio dismisses the Jewish charges. The bema where Paul appeared before Gallio is the same word Paul uses for Christ's judgment seat (2 Cor 5:10).

Context

The Gallio ruling (vv.12–17) is Acts' most important legal precedent — and its most precisely dateable event. A Delphi inscription confirms Gallio's proconsulship at c. AD 51–52, anchoring the entire Pauline chronology. Gallio's ruling effectively classifies Christianity as a Jewish sect, protected under Rome's tolerance of Judaism. Paul's vision (vv.9–10) explains the eighteen-month stay: 'I have many people in this city' — divine sovereignty as the ground of sustained mission.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Corinthians 2:1–5"When I came to you, I did not come with eloquence or human wisdom... I resolved to know nothing while I was with you except Jesus Christ and him crucified." Paul's approach to Corinth after Athens.
  • Romans 16:3"Greet Priscilla and Aquila, my co-workers in Christ Jesus" — the couple Paul met at Corinth becomes among his most valued long-term partners.
  • 1 Corinthians 1:14"I am thankful that I did not baptize any of you except Crispus and Gaius" — Crispus, the synagogue ruler who believed in v.8, is named in Paul's letter.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
18:9–10

MacArthur: God does not say 'you will win many people' (depending on Paul's success) but 'I have many people' (depending on God's sovereign election). Paul's confidence is grounded in divine certainty, not human probability. This is the relationship between divine sovereignty and evangelistic boldness.

18:11

MacArthur on the eighteen-month stay: the combination of divine vision ('stay') and Gallio's legal protection creates conditions for sustained ministry. God uses both supernatural guidance and providential circumstances to direct his servants' ministries.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:9–10

Calvin: the command not to fear comes before the theological grounds are given. 'For I am with you, and I have many people in this city' — the promises are grounded in divine presence and sovereign election. Address the fear first; ground it in divine truth second.

18:14–15

Calvin notes the providential value of Gallio's decision: the Roman state's refusal to classify the Gospel as a crime gave the church breathing space during its earliest decades of growth. God uses even indifferent pagan authorities to protect his church's freedom.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
18:2

The NET notes the historical reference to Claudius's expulsion of Jews, dateable to c. AD 49 from Suetonius (Claudius 25.4) and Orosius (History 7.6.15). This providentially placed Aquila and Priscilla in Corinth exactly when Paul arrived.

18:17

The NET notes that v.17 is ambiguous about who beats Sosthenes and why. The most likely reading: the Gentile crowd, frustrated by Gallio's dismissal of the Jewish case, turns on the Jewish accusers.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
18:9–10

Robertson on the vision at Corinth: "I have many people in this city" — not "I want you to gather many" but "I have them already." The elect are already determined; Paul's preaching is the means of gathering them. Divine certainty grounds apostolic boldness.

18:14–15

Robertson on the Gallio ruling: it establishes the legal precedent that Christianity is a Jewish sect, protected under Rome's tolerance of Judaism. Robertson notes Luke records this carefully: it is the first official Roman legal determination that the Gospel is not illegal.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:2

Keener provides historical context: Suetonius (Claudius 25.4) mentions the expulsion of Jews from Rome "at the instigation of Chrestus" — most scholars interpret this as "Christus," suggesting the expulsion was triggered by disputes over Jesus in the Roman Jewish community. Dateable to c. AD 49.

18:12–17

Keener notes the significance of the Gallio inscription found at Delphi in 1905. It dates Gallio's proconsulship to AD 51–52 with reasonable precision, making it the most valuable external chronological anchor for the Pauline mission.

Verses 18—28 — Return Journey; Apollos Instructed at Ephesus
1819202122232425262728

Greek Word Study

λόγιος logios — learned: Apollos is logios — eloquent, learned, skilled in speech. His thorough Scripture knowledge (v.24), accurate teaching about Jesus (v.25), and subsequent instruction by Priscilla and Aquila (v.26) make him one of the most fully formed characters in Acts' latter half.

ἀκριβέστερον akribesteron — more adequately / more accurately: used in v.26 for Priscilla and Aquila's additional instruction. Akribesteron is the comparative of akribēs (precise, accurate) — they did not correct errors but completed what was incomplete. Apollos had accurate truth; they gave him more complete truth. Constructive supplementation, not hostile correction.

Places

Cenchreae
37.8833° N, 23.0000° E
The eastern port of Corinth — where Paul's ship departed for Syria. The vow he completes here (cutting his hair — a Nazirite-style vow) shows Paul maintaining Jewish practice even as he ministers to Gentiles.
Alexandria — Apollos's Origin
31.2001° N, 29.9187° E
The great Egyptian city — home to the largest Jewish Diaspora community, the Septuagint translation, and the philosophical tradition that produced Philo. Apollos's 'thorough knowledge of the Scriptures' and eloquence reflect the Alexandrian Jewish intellectual tradition.

Context

Apollos is one of Acts' most intriguing figures: learned, eloquent, scripturally equipped, accurate about Jesus — but knowing only John's baptism. Priscilla and Aquila do not contradict him publicly but invite him privately for supplementary instruction. The episode models constructive correction within the community: the more experienced teaching the gifted, in private, without public embarrassment. The result is Apollos more effective than before (v.28). Paul's Corinthian letters show he became a significant figure (1 Cor 1:12; 3:5–6).

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Corinthians 3:5–6"What, after all, is Apollos? And what is Paul? Only servants, through whom you came to believe... I planted the seed, Apollos watered it, but God has been making it grow."
  • 1 Corinthians 1:12"One of you says, 'I follow Apollos'" — Apollos became significant enough at Corinth to attract a following, reflecting his extraordinary ability.
  • Acts 19:1–7The disciples at Ephesus who knew only John's baptism — paralleling the Apollos gap. After Priscilla and Aquila instructed Apollos, Paul arrives to address a similar incomplete understanding in a larger group.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
18:24–26

MacArthur: Apollos had accurate truth but incomplete truth — real knowledge of Christ, real fervour, real accuracy in what he knew, but missing a crucial dimension. The answer is completion, not condemnation. Priscilla and Aquila model the ideal: private, gentle, supplementary instruction.

18:26

MacArthur notes the model for women's teaching ministry — private instruction of an individual in a household setting. Apollos's willingness to receive instruction from a tentmaking couple is part of his greatness, not a diminishment of it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:25

Calvin: genuine faith and genuine preaching can exist in incomplete form — God receives sincere, accurate-as-far-as-it-goes discipleship. But the church's responsibility is to complete what is incomplete, not merely validate what is partial.

18:26

Calvin: no Christian is so learned or gifted that they cannot receive further instruction from brothers and sisters. Apollos's willingness to be instructed by Priscilla and Aquila is itself a mark of true wisdom.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
18:25

The NET notes that "he knew only the baptism of John" probably means Apollos had received pre-Pentecost teaching — perhaps from early disciples who had encountered John's movement or the early Jerusalem church before the Spirit's coming was fully understood.

18:26

The NET notes Priscilla being named first is consistent with Romans 16:3 and 2 Timothy 4:19 — Luke's pattern of naming her first suggests deliberate acknowledgment of her prominent role in this partnership.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
18:26

"Priscilla and Aquila... explained to him the way of God more adequately" — Robertson notes Priscilla is named first (also in Rom 16:3; 2 Tim 4:19) — possibly indicating she was the more prominent teacher. The episode shows women exercising genuine teaching ministry in appropriate context.

18:28

"He vigorously refuted his Jewish opponents" — Robertson on diakatelencheto (vigorously refuted): the double compound intensifies the thoroughness. Apollos, now fully equipped, uses his natural eloquence in full service of the complete Gospel.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:24–25

Keener notes Apollos's Alexandrian background explains his scriptural sophistication. His 'accurate' teaching about Jesus (v.25) suggests he had learned the basic Gospel outline from travellers or letters from Palestine — but had not received post-Pentecost teaching about the Spirit's full role.

18:26

Keener notes the private instruction model preserves Apollos's public credibility while correcting his incomplete understanding. Correct in private what can be built on in public — pastoral wisdom confirmed by Apollos's subsequent effectiveness.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Paul↗ People
Apostle to the Gentiles; author of thirteen NT letters
Paul (Saul of Tarsus) is introduced as a persecutor at Stephen’s stoning (Acts 7:58) and is dramatically converted on the Damascus road (Acts 9). He dominates Acts 13–28 across three missionary journeys, planting churches from Cyprus to Greece to Rome. His final journey to Rome as a prisoner frames Acts’ theological climax: the gospel reaching the empire’s capital, "boldly and without hindrance" (Acts 28:31).
Apollos↗ People
Eloquent Alexandrian teacher; discipled by Priscilla and Aquila
Apollos is an Alexandrian Jew who arrives at Ephesus knowing the way of God accurately but only knowing John’s baptism (Acts 18:24–25). After Priscilla and Aquila complete his instruction, he becomes a powerful apologist in Achaia, "vigorously refuting his Jewish opponents in public debate" (Acts 18:28). Paul’s Corinthian correspondence shows Apollos became an unintended source of factionalism (1 Cor 1:12), though Paul regards him as a fellow worker.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 18Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 18 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
