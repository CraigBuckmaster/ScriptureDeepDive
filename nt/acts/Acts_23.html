Verses 1—11 — Before the Sanhedrin; The Lord Stands by Paul

Greek Word Study

ἀνάστασις νεκρῶν anastasis nekrōn — resurrection of the dead: Paul's tactical appeal in v.6 — 'I stand on trial because of the hope of the resurrection of the dead' — is both tactical and true. It is tactical because it splits the Sanhedrin along Pharisaic/Sadducean lines. It is true because the resurrection of Jesus is the theological ground of everything Paul proclaims. The tactic is not deception but precision.

κεκονιαμένε τοῖχε kekoniaméne toiche — whitewashed wall: Paul's rebuke of Ananias (v.3) — 'you whitewashed wall' — alludes to Ezekiel 13:10–16 (prophets who whitewash a dangerous wall) and anticipates Jesus's 'whitewashed tombs' (Matt 23:27). The image is of something that looks solid but is rotten inside. Paul recognises the high priest's judgment-seat hypocrisy.

Context

The Sanhedrin hearing (vv.1–10) is one of Acts' most dramatic scenes: Paul rebukes the high priest, is rebuked for it, then invokes the resurrection to split the council. The resulting riot saves him from coordinated opposition. Then the conspiracy of forty men (vv.12–22) reveals how desperate his enemies are — and how God providentially uses a nephew's overheard conversation to preserve his apostle. The Lord's vision (v.11: 'you must also testify in Rome') gives Paul's entire captivity narrative its theological direction: what looks like imprisonment is actually preparation for the mission's climax.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 18:9–10The Lord's night vision at Corinth: 'Do not be afraid; keep on speaking' — the same pattern of direct divine reassurance at the point of maximum danger.
  • Acts 9:15"This man is my chosen instrument to proclaim my name... to the Gentiles and their kings" — Paul's Rome mission was part of the Damascus road commission. The Sanhedrin hearing is the penultimate step toward its fulfilment.
  • Matthew 23:27"Woe to you, teachers of the law and Pharisees, you hypocrites! You are like whitewashed tombs" — Paul's "whitewashed wall" (v.3) uses the same imagery Jesus used of the religious establishment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
23:1

"I have fulfilled my duty to God in all good conscience to this day" — MacArthur on Paul's opening statement: this is not arrogance but the claim of integrity. Paul is not saying he has been perfect but that he has acted in genuine alignment with what he understood to be God's will — even his persecution of the church was done in good conscience, mistaken but sincere. MacArthur uses this to discuss the relationship between conscience and sin.

23:6

MacArthur on Paul's resurrection appeal: this is not a diversionary tactic but a substantive claim. The resurrection is the heart of the Gospel (1 Cor 15:14: 'if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile'). By framing the trial around resurrection, Paul correctly identifies the core theological issue. MacArthur notes that Paul never loses sight of what is actually at stake, even under pressure.

23:11

MacArthur on the Lord's night vision: this is the fifth direct divine communication to Paul in Acts (9:4–6; 16:9; 18:9–10; 22:17–21; 23:11; 27:23–24). MacArthur notes the pattern: God appears to Paul at moments of maximum danger and uncertainty. The visions are not for spiritual enhancement but for mission direction and courage in crisis.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:3

Calvin on Paul rebuking the high priest: the rebuke was just — striking someone who hasn't been tried is illegal. Calvin notes that Paul immediately acknowledged his error when told it was the high priest (v.5), citing Exodus 22:28. This is, for Calvin, the model of righteous boldness and humble correction: speak truth firmly, accept correction humbly.

23:6

Calvin on the resurrection appeal: Calvin notes that Paul's statement is simultaneously theological and tactical, and that there is no contradiction between these two dimensions. Wisdom in service of truth is not manipulation. Calvin uses this to argue that pastoral wisdom is legitimate — using the right argument at the right moment is not deception but discernment.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:5

"I did not realize that he was the high priest" — the NET notes several interpretations: (1) Paul genuinely did not recognise him (poor eyesight? unfamiliar with the current high priest after years away?); (2) Paul spoke sarcastically ("I did not recognise this as the behaviour of a high priest"); (3) Paul is using polite apology for his outburst. The NET favours the genuine non-recognition interpretation.

23:11

The NET notes the Lord "stood near" (epestē) Paul — the same verb used for angelic appearances in Acts (12:7; 27:23). The Lord's personal presence and the specific commission to Rome is the theological foundation for the entire remaining narrative of Acts.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
23:6

"I am a Pharisee, descended from Pharisees. I stand on trial because of the hope of the resurrection of the dead" — Robertson on Paul's tactical appeal: the statement is both accurate and strategic. Robertson notes that Paul is not manufacturing a false division — he genuinely is a Pharisee and the resurrection genuinely is the core of his Gospel. The tactic is precise, not deceptive.

23:11

"Take courage! As you have testified about me in Jerusalem, so you must also testify in Rome" — Robertson notes the dei (must) — the same word of divine necessity throughout Luke-Acts. Paul's Rome journey is not contingent but certain. The vision does not tell him how he will get to Rome; it tells him he will.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:2

Keener provides historical context for Ananias the high priest. Ananias ben Nedebaeus (high priest c. AD 47–59) was notorious for his greed and corruption — Josephus describes him as a violent man who appropriated the priestly tithes for himself (Antiquities 20.9.2). Paul's comparison to a 'whitewashed wall' was historically apt, not merely rhetorical.

23:12–15

Keener notes the conspiracy of forty men represents a significant organized resistance. The oath not to eat or drink until Paul was dead was a recognised form of vow — binding and extremely serious. The conspiracy's sophistication (involving both religious leaders and a planned ambush) shows that the opposition to Paul had moved beyond spontaneous riot to coordinated assassination planning.

Verses 12—35 — The Plot to Kill Paul; Transfer to Caesarea

Greek Word Study

κράτιστε Φήλικι kratiste Phēliki — Your Excellency, Governor Felix: v.26 — kratistos (most excellent, Your Excellency) is the formal address for Roman officials of equestrian rank. Felix (Marcus Antonius Felix) was a former slave who rose through imperial patronage to become governor of Judea (c. AD 52–59). The formal letter embeds Luke's legal argument: the Roman commander found no crime deserving death.

οὐδὲν ἄξιον θανάτου ouden axion thanatou — no charge deserving death: Claudius Lysias's verdict in the letter (v.29) is the Roman legal assessment of Paul's case: no capital crime. This is the third Roman innocence declaration in Acts (Gallio at Corinth, 18:14–15; Lysias here; Festus later, 25:25). Luke's cumulative legal argument: Roman authorities consistently found Paul innocent.

Places

Antipatris
32.1000° N, 34.9167° E
A city on the road from Jerusalem to Caesarea — roughly the midpoint of the 96 km journey. The infantry escort (400 soldiers) returns from Antipatris while the cavalry (70 horsemen) continues with Paul to Caesarea. The massive escort (470 soldiers for one prisoner!) reflects both Paul's Roman legal status and the seriousness of the assassination threat.
Caesarea — Herod's Palace
32.5000° N, 34.9000° E
The Roman governor's official residence in Caesarea — the praetorium (v.35). Paul will be imprisoned here for two years (24:27), under successive governors Felix and Festus. Caesarea is the administrative capital; here Paul's legal hearings will take place before eventually being appealed to Rome.

Context

The letter of Claudius Lysias (vv.25–30) is one of Acts' most carefully crafted literary devices. It functions as an official Roman document summarising Paul's case — and it advances Luke's legal argument: a Roman officer, acting in his official capacity, found no capital charge. The letter's slight inaccuracy (v.27: 'for I had learned that he is a Roman citizen' — actually Lysias discovered this after the arrest) is historically realistic: officials shade their reports to present themselves favourably. Luke preserves this small detail as evidence of verisimilitude.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 18:14–15Gallio's ruling at Corinth: 'no misdemeanor or serious crime' — the first Roman innocence declaration. The cumulative pattern: Roman officials consistently find Paul not guilty.
  • Acts 25:25"I found he had done nothing deserving death" — Festus's verdict continues the Roman innocence chain.
  • Acts 26:31–32"This man is not doing anything that deserves death or imprisonment... he could have been set free if he had not appealed to Caesar" — the climactic Roman innocence verdict.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
23:29

MacArthur on Lysias's letter: the commander's finding — 'no charge deserving death or imprisonment' — is the Roman legal system working as it should. MacArthur notes that God uses earthly legal structures to protect his servants. Paul is not above the law; he is protected by it. The providential use of Roman law is a recurring theme from Acts 18 onward.

23:34–35

MacArthur on Felix asking Paul's province: the governor's question determines jurisdiction. Paul's Cilician origin places him in a region under Felix's broader authority. MacArthur notes the almost bureaucratic precision with which Luke records Paul's legal processing — evidence that Luke is writing as an informed eyewitness of legal procedure.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:27

Calvin on the commander's letter: Lysias writes that he 'came with my troops and rescued him, for I had learned that he is a Roman citizen' — the citizenship discovery actually happened after the rescue (22:25–29). Calvin notes that official letters routinely present facts in a favourable sequence. He does not condemn Lysias for this but uses it to note that even official documents can be self-serving without being entirely false.

23:35

Calvin on Paul imprisoned in Herod's palace: the irony is not lost — Paul is imprisoned in the palace of the dynasty that killed James (12:2) and tried to kill Peter (12:4–5). Yet God's providential care operates even within the structures of hostile power. Calvin uses this to encourage believers: God's governance is not thwarted by the architecture of their opponents.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:27

The NET notes the chronological inaccuracy in Lysias's letter: he claims he 'had learned that he is a Roman citizen' before rescuing him — in fact he learned this after the arrest (22:25–29). The NET suggests Lysias may be covering himself from the liability of having arrested a Roman citizen. This kind of self-serving reportage is historically realistic and confirms Luke's accuracy in preserving the original document's wording.

23:35

The NET notes that "Herod's palace" (praitōrion) — the Latin praetorium — originally referred to the general's tent in a military camp, then to the official residence of a Roman governor. Herod's lavish palace at Caesarea had been converted into the governor's official residence, and Paul's imprisonment there gave him proximity to Felix and later Festus.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
23:29

"I found that the accusation had to do with questions about their law, but there was no charge against him that deserved death or imprisonment" — Robertson notes the significance of this official Roman verdict. It is the second such declaration (after Gallio, 18:14–15). Robertson argues that Luke is systematically building a legal brief: Roman officials who examined the evidence consistently found Paul not guilty.

23:23

"Get ready a detachment of two hundred soldiers, seventy horsemen and two hundred spearmen" — Robertson notes the extraordinary military response to protect one prisoner. Four hundred and seventy soldiers for Paul's safe transfer is both a measure of the real threat and of Paul's Roman legal standing.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:26

Keener provides historical context for Felix. Marcus Antonius Felix was the brother of Claudius's favourite freedman Pallas, which gave him political protection despite his low social origin. Tacitus describes him as exercising 'the power of a king with the mind of a slave' (Annals 12.54). Josephus confirms his brutal and corrupt governance (Jewish War 2.13.2; Antiquities 20.8.5).

23:35

"Herod's palace" (praitōrion) — Keener notes this was the former palace of Herod the Great, later converted to the Roman governor's official residence. Archaeological excavations at Caesarea have confirmed the existence of this Herodian structure. Luke's specific detail about Paul's imprisonment location is consistent with the archaeological record.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Paul↗ People
Apostle to the Gentiles; author of thirteen NT letters
Paul (Saul of Tarsus) is introduced as a persecutor at Stephen’s stoning (Acts 7:58) and is dramatically converted on the Damascus road (Acts 9). He dominates Acts 13–28 across three missionary journeys, planting churches from Cyprus to Greece to Rome. His final journey to Rome as a prisoner frames Acts’ theological climax: the gospel reaching the empire’s capital, "boldly and without hindrance" (Acts 28:31).
Felix↗ People
Roman governor of Judaea; kept Paul in custody for two years
Antonius Felix is governor of Judaea (52–59 AD) when Paul is transferred from Jerusalem to Caesarea (Acts 23:24). He gives Paul relative freedom and converses with him privately about faith and righteousness, but "became frightened" at Paul’s mention of judgment and deferred (Acts 24:25). He keeps Paul imprisoned hoping for a bribe (Acts 24:26) and leaves him in custody for two years as a political favour to the Jews.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 23Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 23 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
