Acts 24

Paul Before Felix

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Verses 1—21 — Paul Before Felix: Tertullus’ Accusation; Paul’s Defence

Greek Word Study

λοιμόν loimon — troublemaker / plague: Tertullus's first charge in v.5 — loimon (literally 'a pestilence, a plague'). This is the most contemptuous term available to describe a person — it is the word used for a public nuisance so serious that the entire social order is threatened. Luke preserves the precise legal vocabulary of the prosecution without softening it.

κατὰ τὴν ὁδόν kata tēn hodon — as a follower of the Way: Paul accepts the description of Christianity as 'the Way' (v.14) — the term his opponents use as a derogatory label. He neither apologises for the designation nor rejects it. His defence is that the Way is not a sect opposed to Judaism but its legitimate fulfilment: 'I believe everything that is in accordance with the Law and that is written in the Prophets.'

Places

Caesarea — Governor's Court
32.5000° N, 34.9000° E
Felix's official court in the praetorium. The formal hearing has the full apparatus of Roman legal procedure: advocates (Tertullus), governor as judge, defendant, witnesses. Luke presents Paul's defence as a formal legal speech — evidence that he is writing for an audience familiar with Roman legal process.
Jerusalem — Paul's Reason for Being There
31.7683° N, 35.2137° E
Paul's defence specifies the purpose of his Jerusalem visit: 'to bring my people gifts for the poor and to present offerings' (v.17). The collection for Jerusalem (Rom 15:25–28; 1 Cor 16:1–4) was the concrete expression of Gentile-Jewish solidarity. Paul's imprisonment comes in the course of a mission of solidarity.

Context

The Felix trial is Acts' first formal legal hearing before a Roman governor. Tertullus's prosecution speech (vv.2–8) uses the standard Roman oratorical form: captatio benevolentiae (flattery of the judge, vv.2–4), narratio (statement of facts, v.5), probatio (proof, vv.5–6), conclusio (request, v.8). Paul's defence (vv.10–21) strips away the flattery, denies the provable charges, and redirects to the real issue: resurrection. Felix's delay (v.22: 'when Lysias the commander comes down, I will decide your case') is not justice but procrastination — driven by hope of a bribe (v.26).

Cross-Reference

  • Romans 15:25–28"Now, however, I am on my way to Jerusalem in the service of the Lord's people there. For Macedonia and Achaia were pleased to make a contribution for the poor among the Lord's people in Jerusalem" — Paul's own account of what he was doing in Jerusalem (v.17).
  • Acts 23:29Claudius Lysias's verdict: 'nothing worthy of death or imprisonment' — Paul references the Sanhedrin hearing (vv.20–21) as his own evidence that no crime was found.
  • 2 Corinthians 1:12"Now this is our boast: Our conscience testifies that we have conducted ourselves... in holiness and sincerity that are from God" — Paul's consistent claim of clear conscience (Acts 23:1; 24:16) is his personal testimony throughout the captivity period.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
24:14

MacArthur on 'which they call a sect': Paul accepts the label but redefines its content. The Way is not a departure from Judaism's hope but its fulfilment: 'I believe everything that is in accordance with the Law and that is written in the Prophets.' MacArthur notes that Paul's defence throughout Acts consistently presents Christianity as the goal toward which the OT was always pointed.

24:16

"So I strive always to keep my conscience clear before God and man" — MacArthur on the relationship between conscience and conduct: Paul works (askō — to exercise, train) to maintain a clear conscience. Conscience is not automatic; it requires cultivation and obedience. MacArthur uses this to argue against both moral laxity (ignoring conscience) and moral perfectionism (treating conscience as infallible).

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:5

Calvin on Tertullus's charges: the formal rhetoric cannot disguise the vacuousness of the accusation. 'Troublemaker' is an epithet, not a crime. Calvin notes that throughout Acts, the Gospel's opponents consistently fail to articulate a genuine legal charge — they resort to labels because they cannot specify a law that has been broken.

24:15

Calvin on 'I have the same hope in God as these men themselves have, that there is going to be a resurrection of both the righteous and the wicked' — Calvin notes the universality: both righteous and wicked will be raised. Paul is not arguing for the resurrection of believers only but for the universal resurrection that was the common hope of Pharisaic Judaism and the specific claim of Christian proclamation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
24:6

The NET notes that most manuscripts omit vv.6b–8a ("and we would have judged him according to our own law. But Lysias the commander came and took him away from us by force. Then he ordered his accusers to come before you") — this longer reading appears in the Western text tradition. The NET follows the shorter text but provides the variant in a footnote.

24:15

The NET notes the phrase "resurrection of both the righteous and the wicked" (anastasin mellein esesthai dikaiōn te kai adikōn) — Paul affirms both Daniel 12:2 (resurrection to everlasting life and to everlasting contempt) and the broader Pharisaic teaching on universal resurrection. Paul uses common ground with his Pharisaic opponents to make his case.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
24:5

"A troublemaker, stirring up riots among the Jews all over the world" — Robertson notes the three charges: (1) public nuisance (loimon — plague/pestilence); (2) political agitator (kinōn staseis — stirring up dissension); (3) religious dissident (ringleader of the Nazarene sect). Robertson observes that none of these charges constitutes a capital crime under Roman law — which is why Felix delays rather than deciding.

24:14–15

Robertson on Paul's positive confession: "I worship the God of our ancestors as a follower of the Way" — Robertson notes that Paul never apologises for Christianity's distinctiveness but always grounds it in continuity with Israel's hope. The resurrection is not a new doctrine but the fulfilment of what "is written in the Prophets."

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:2–4

Keener provides background on Tertullus as a forensic advocate (rhētōr). Professional advocates were commonly hired for provincial legal proceedings. Tertullus's exaggerated flattery of Felix ('enjoyed a long period of peace under you, and your wise leadership has brought reforms') was conventional captatio benevolentiae — the opening flattery expected in Roman court oratory, not genuine praise.

24:17

Keener notes the significance of Paul's reference to 'gifts for the poor' (eleēmosynas). This is the only explicit reference in Acts to the Jerusalem collection, which was the central project of Paul's third journey (1 Cor 16; 2 Cor 8–9; Rom 15:25–28). The collection was both a practical act of solidarity and a theological statement about Gentile-Jewish unity in the body of Christ.

Verses 22—27 — Felix Procrastinates; Paul in Custody Two Years
222324252627

Greek Word Study

ἀνεβάλετο anebaleto — adjourned / postponed: Felix's tactical delay (v.22) is the first of two deliberate postponements. The second (v.25: 'when I find it convenient') reveals the pattern: Felix is not seeking justice but managing competing interests. The word anebaleto (put off, adjourn) is a legal term for deliberate postponement — Luke uses it precisely.

ἀκούσας δὲ περὶ δικαιοσύνης καὶ ἐγκρατείας καὶ τοῦ κρίματος τοῦ μέλλοντος akousas de peri dikaiosynēs kai enkrateias kai tou krimatos tou mellontos — righteousness, self-control, and the judgment to come: the three topics that trouble Felix (v.25). Paul does not preach what would make Felix comfortable; he preaches what Felix most needs to hear. Righteousness (the standard before God), self-control (the Stoic virtue Felix spectacularly lacked), and coming judgment (the consequence of failing both) — each topic cuts at Felix's actual life.

Places

Caesarea — Felix's Court
32.5000° N, 34.9000° E
Paul is held in Caesarea under relatively comfortable custody (v.23: 'some freedom... permit his friends to take care of his needs') for two years. The captivity is not the end of Paul's ministry: Luke implies he preached to Felix repeatedly, reaching the highest political level in the province.
Herod's Palace — Paul's Prison
32.5000° N, 34.9000° E
The praetorium that housed Paul also housed Felix's court. The proximity meant Felix could summon Paul easily (v.26: 'he sent for him frequently'). Paul's two years of imprisonment in Herod's palace is the period during which scholars believe he wrote the Prison Epistles.

Context

Felix's response to Paul's preaching is one of the most tragic vignettes in Acts: 'As Paul talked about righteousness, self-control and the judgment to come, Felix was troubled' — and postponed. Felix was 'well acquainted with the Way' (v.22), had a Jewish wife (Drusilla, Herod Agrippa I's daughter), heard Paul's Gospel repeatedly over two years — and left Paul imprisoned for the bribe that never came and to please his opponents. Felix had every advantage for conversion and chose the worse part.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 10:42"Mary has chosen what is better, and it will not be taken away from her" — the contrast with Felix: Mary chose to hear; Felix heard and postponed.
  • Hebrews 3:15"Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts" — Felix heard; he hardened.
  • Acts 28:31Paul preaching "without hindrance" in Rome — the eventual outcome of the journey that included two years imprisoned under Felix. The delay was not the end.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
24:25

MacArthur on Felix's postponement: 'when I find it convenient, I will send for you' — MacArthur calls this the saddest phrase in the captivity narrative. Felix was troubled — the Spirit was at work. But he postponed response to a more convenient time, and that convenient time apparently never arrived. MacArthur uses this as the warning against procrastination in responding to the Gospel: the 'convenient time' for those who keep postponing often never comes.

24:26–27

MacArthur on Felix leaving Paul imprisoned: the combination of bribe-seeking and political calculation is the anatomy of corrupt governance. Felix uses Paul as an instrument — for financial gain, then for political favour. MacArthur notes that the Gospel cannot be used; it can only be received or rejected. Felix tried to use it and left having neither received nor rejected it — simply avoided.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:25

Calvin on Felix's fear and postponement: 'Felix was troubled' — this is genuine, not feigned. The conviction was real. Calvin uses this to argue that genuine conviction of sin and genuine fear of judgment are not the same as genuine conversion. Many are troubled without being transformed. The missing element in Felix is not emotion (he had it) but the willingness to surrender.

24:27

Calvin on Felix keeping Paul imprisoned to please the Jews: this is the final verdict on Felix's character. He failed three times: (1) he did not acquit the innocent (justice); (2) he did not respond to the Gospel (faith); (3) he imprisoned the righteous to please his political opponents (integrity). Calvin uses Felix as a type of the politician who serves power rather than truth.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
24:25

The NET notes the three topics Paul addressed: "righteousness" (dikaiosynēs — the standard required by God), "self-control" (enkrateias — mastery of desires and impulses), and "the judgment to come" (tou krimatos tou mellontos — future accountability). The NET comments that each topic directly confronted Felix's known character: he was notorious for injustice, licentiousness, and impunity.

24:27

The NET notes that "to grant a favor to the Jews" (charita katathesthai tois Ioudaiois) was a calculated political move. Felix knew he would be recalled to Rome and tried for his misgovernance; he hoped that leaving Paul imprisoned would buy goodwill from the Jewish community who might otherwise testify against him. Josephus confirms that influential Jews did testify against Felix to Nero (Antiquities 20.8.9).

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
24:25

"Felix was troubled and said, 'That's enough for now!'" — Robertson on the Greek emphoros genomenon (having become afraid/troubled): Felix is emotionally moved, even frightened. Robertson notes that this is perhaps the nearest Felix came to conversion — genuine agitation at Paul's words. His response — "when I find it convenient, I will send for you" — is the perpetual answer of those who are moved but not converted.

24:26

"He was hoping that Paul would offer him a bribe" — Robertson notes that offering or receiving bribes in Roman legal proceedings was technically illegal (Lex Julia de repetundis), but the practice was widespread. Felix expected what was common; Paul refused to provide it. The irony is that Felix was enriching himself through corruption while Paul spoke to him of righteousness.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:22

Keener notes that Felix was 'well acquainted with the Way' — a significant detail. Felix had been governing Judea since c. AD 52, during which time the Christian movement had grown significantly, his wife Drusilla was Jewish, and he had presumably encountered the movement's claims through official channels. His knowledge was real, not superficial.

24:24

Keener provides background on Drusilla. She was Herod Agrippa I's youngest daughter, previously married to Azizus king of Emesa, whom she left to marry Felix at his instigation (Josephus, Antiquities 20.7.1–2). Her Jewish background made her potentially interested in Paul's claims about Jesus as Messiah. That Felix brought her to hear Paul suggests genuine curiosity, not merely courtesy.

Felix↗ People
Roman governor of Judaea; kept Paul in custody for two years
Antonius Felix is governor of Judaea (52–59 AD) when Paul is transferred from Jerusalem to Caesarea (Acts 23:24). He gives Paul relative freedom and converses with him privately about faith and righteousness, but "became frightened" at Paul’s mention of judgment and deferred (Acts 24:25). He keeps Paul imprisoned hoping for a bribe (Acts 24:26) and leaves him in custody for two years as a political favour to the Jews.
Agrippa↗ People
Herod Agrippa II; king before whom Paul defends himself (Acts 26)
Herod Agrippa II is the last significant Herodian ruler, client-king of several territories and a man expert in Jewish customs and disputes (Acts 26:3). Paul’s defence before him in Acts 26 is the most polished forensic speech in Acts. Agrippa’s response — "In a short time you would persuade me to be a Christian?" (Acts 26:28) — is ambiguous: half-moved but unwilling to commit. He agrees with Festus that Paul could have been released had he not appealed to Caesar.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 24Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 24 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
