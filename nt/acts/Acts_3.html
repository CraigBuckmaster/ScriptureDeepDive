Acts 3

The Lame Beggar Healed

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Greek Word Study

ἀρχηγόν archēgon — Author of life: also translated "Pioneer" or "Prince" — combines the ideas of originator, leader, and champion. The word recurs in Acts 5:31 and Hebrews 2:10; 12:2. The supreme irony: the one who is the source of all life was killed by those he gave life to.

τελεία ἰσχύς teleia ischys — complete healing: literally "complete strength." The man's restoration is total — not partial mobility but full strength in feet and ankles that had never functioned. Luke's medical precision (as a physician) is evident throughout.

Context

The healing of the lame man is Luke's first post-Pentecost miracle — and he describes it with three vivid verbs in v8 (jumping, walking, praising God) that enact Isaiah 35:6's messianic promise: 'then will the lame leap like a deer.' The miracle is not just a sign of power but a sign of the age: the eschatological restoration Isaiah promised has begun. Peter's immediate deflection (v12: 'Why do you stare at us as if by our own power?') models the apostolic pattern throughout Acts — miracles point beyond the apostle to the risen Christ.

Cross-Reference

  • Isaiah 35:6"Then will the lame leap like a deer" — the messianic promise that the healing enacts. Luke frames the miracle as a visible sign that the eschatological age of restoration has arrived.
  • Acts 4:12"There is no other name under heaven given to mankind by which we must be saved" — the "name" theology of ch.3 (vv.6, 16) reaches its doctrinal climax in ch.4.
  • John 5:8Jesus's healing of a paralytic with the command to "Get up! Pick up your mat and walk" is the direct parallel — Peter acts in the name and pattern of the same Jesus.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:6

The ability to heal in the apostolic period was a specific gift given to authenticate the apostles' message before the NT canon was complete. MacArthur distinguishes the apostolic sign-gifts from ongoing spiritual gifts — not to diminish the miracle but to explain why identical miracles are not normative expectations today.

3:12–13

Peter's immediate deflection — "Why do you stare at us as if by our own power or godliness we had made this man walk?" — is the anti-model of religious celebrity. The apostolic pattern is consistent: miracles point beyond the apostle to Christ.

3:19

"Times of refreshing" (kairoi anapsyxeōs) — a foretaste of the final restoration. Repentance now participates in eschatological renewal. The call is urgent: the refreshing is available but not permanent.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:13–15

Calvin notes the bold contrast: "You disowned the Holy and Righteous One" — "God raised him from the dead." The resurrection is the reversal of the unjust condemnation, the divine verdict overturning the human verdict.

3:17–19

The "ignorance" appeal (v17: "I know that you acted in ignorance") is pastoral, not exculpatory. Ignorance does not remove guilt but it does affect the nature of the appeal. Peter does not excuse; he explains — and then calls for repentance. Acknowledging ignorance opens the door to repentance.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:15

"Author of life" (archēgos tēs zōēs) — archēgos can mean originator, founder, or pioneer. The irony is maximised: the one who is the source and sustainer of all life was killed — but God raised him. The contradiction is the Gospel's scandal.

3:21

"Until the time comes for God to restore everything" (apokatastaseōs pantōn) — the only NT use of apokatastasis. The NET limits its scope to the eschatological fulfilment of the covenant promises made through the holy prophets, not universal salvation.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
3:6

Robertson notes that "In the name of Jesus Christ the Nazarene, walk" uses peripatei — the present imperative: keep on walking. Peter does not pray for healing but commands it — consistent with the authority delegated in the commission of Acts 1:8.

3:8

Three verbs in rapid succession: exallomenos (leaping up), periepātei (was walking), ēlthen (entered). Robertson savours Luke's vivid style: the imperfects capture the continuous action of a man who could not stop walking because walking was entirely new to him.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:2

Beggars at temple gates were well-attested in both Jewish and Greco-Roman sources — the socially marginal were drawn to temple precincts where charity was expected. The man's daily position at the Gate Beautiful made him a known fixture, meaning the healing would be recognised and verifiable by the entire Jerusalem community (Acts 4:16).

3:6

The economic contrast is embedded in Peter's words: no silver or gold (the currency of the Temple economy) but the authority of the name. The healing demonstrates that the church's power is not the power of wealth or institution but the power of the risen Christ.

Greek Word Study

μετανοήσατε metanoēsate — repent: aorist imperative — decisive, urgent turning. This is the fourth major repentance call in Acts (2:38; 3:19; later 17:30; 26:20). The pattern is consistent: the Gospel proclamation always concludes with a call for decision.

προκεχειρισμένον prokecheirismenon — appointed: designated beforehand — the appointment was before history, not merely after the resurrection. Jesus was divinely designated for this role from eternity; the Ascension publicly declares what was always true.

Context

Peter's second sermon constructs a deliberate typological argument: Abraham's covenant promise → Moses's prophet-like-me prophecy → Jesus. Each element points forward to its fulfilment. The Deuteronomy 18:15 citation ('a prophet like me') was a live messianic expectation in the first century (John 1:21; 6:14; 7:40). Peter applies it with full covenantal seriousness: Israel's history pointed to Jesus, whom they rejected — and yet, because of Israel's covenantal standing, the first offer of repentance is made to them.

Cross-Reference

  • Deuteronomy 18:15–19The prophet-like-Moses text applied to Jesus — also cited in John 1:21; 6:14; Stephen's speech (Acts 7:37); and Hebrews 3:1–6.
  • Genesis 22:18"Through your offspring all peoples on earth will be blessed" — the Abrahamic promise Paul develops in Galatians 3:16, 29. The Gentile mission is covenant fulfilment, not covenant abandonment.
  • Acts 7:37Stephen repeats the Deuteronomy 18 application to Jesus — confirming it was a standard piece of early Christian apologetic, not a one-off application.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:17–18

MacArthur notes that the acknowledgment of ignorance does not diminish responsibility; it explains why repentance is still available. Had Israel committed a fully informed, deliberate sin with no possible excuse, the situation would be hopeless. The ignorance means the door remains open.

3:21

MacArthur takes "restore everything" as referring to the prophesied kingdom restoration for Israel — the fulfilment of OT covenant promises. He connects this to the disciples' question of 1:6 (restoring the kingdom to Israel): Jesus said the timing was the Father's; Peter says it awaits the national repentance of Israel.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:19–21

Calvin interprets "restore everything" not as universal salvation or political restoration but as the completion of the redemptive plan — when all God's elect are gathered and his promises to the church are fully consummated.

3:25–26

Calvin uses this passage to press the priority of the Jewish mission: "first to you" shows that the Gospel is not the abandonment of Israel but the fulfilment of Israel's covenant destiny. The Gentile inclusion does not mean Jewish exclusion; it means the Abrahamic promise has reached its universal scope.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:20

"Who has been appointed for you — even Jesus" (ton prokecheirismenon hymin Christon Iēsoun) — prokecheirismenon means "designated beforehand." The appointment was pre-temporal: Jesus was divinely designated for this role from eternity, not merely appointed after the resurrection.

3:26

"Raised up his servant" (anastēsas) — this verb can mean either raise from the dead or raise up/appoint. In v26, "sending him to you" suggests appointment/commissioning rather than resurrection per se. Luke uses the same verb for the resurrection in v15.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
3:19

Robertson notes that "times of refreshing" (kairoi anapsyxeōs) is a phrase not found elsewhere in biblical Greek. He connects it to Jewish expectation of the messianic era as a time of rest and renewal — the call to repent is therefore not merely moral but eschatological.

3:22–23

The "must listen" (akousesthe) is an imperative of obligation. The "cutting off" echoes covenantal excommunication (karet — being cut off from the people of God). Peter applies this warning with full seriousness: rejecting Jesus is covenant-breaking.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:17–18

The "acting in ignorance" appeal fits Jewish penitential theology: ignorance (shegagah) was a recognized category affecting the nature of atonement required. Peter's pastoral move — acknowledging ignorance before calling for repentance — is consistent with Jewish homiletic practice: soften the accusation to open the door to repentance.

3:25–26

Peter's identification of his audience as "heirs of the prophets and of the covenant" gives them covenantal standing that makes their rejection of Jesus all the more urgent to address — but also makes their repentance all the more possible. The covenant membership can now lead them to receive the blessings Jesus brings.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Peter
Apostle — healer and preacher
Heals the lame man at the Beautiful Gate "in the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth" and uses the crowd's astonishment as the occasion for the second major sermon, identifying Jesus as the prophet of Deut 18.
John
Apostle — witness alongside Peter
Present with Peter at the gate and throughout the healing. The two function as a prophetic pair — Luke's first fulfillment of Jesus's "two witnesses" pattern (Luke 10:1).
The Lame Man
Recipient of apostolic healing
Lame from birth, carried daily to the temple gate. His leaping and praising God echoes Isaiah 35:6's vision of the messianic age. Becomes the visible proof that Jesus's resurrection power continues.

Literary Structure

Silver and Gold — Economic Reversal — v.6"Silver or gold I do not have, but what I do have I give you" contrasts the church's power with the Temple establishment's wealth. The healing economy operates on a completely different currency: authority in the name of the risen Christ rather than financial resources. This contrast will be pressed further in 4:13 — unschooled men with authority surpassing the authorities.
Prophet Typology — Moses to Jesus — vv.22–26Peter's sermon constructs a typological argument through Israel's history: Abraham's covenant → Moses's prophet-promise → Jesus. The argument builds relentlessly toward the conclusion: "when God raised up his servant, he sent him first to you to bless you." Israel's priority in the mission is not chauvinism but the logic of covenant fulfilment.
Acts 3 presents the first apostolic healing and the second public sermon — establishing the pattern of sign followed by proclamation that will characterise the entire Petrine mission.
