Verses 1—20 — Iconium; Lystra: Paul Stoned and Left for Dead
1234567891011121314151617181920

Greek Word Study

κατελιθάσθη katelithasthē — they stoned Paul: the aorist passive of kataithazō — a compound emphasising the completeness of the action ('thoroughly stoned'). Paul himself refers to this stoning in 2 Cor 11:25: 'once I was stoned.' That he got up and walked back into the city the same day either implies miraculous recovery or that the stoning was incomplete — both interpretations have patristic support.

ὁ θεὸς ζῶν ho theos zōn — the living God: Paul's address to pagans at Lystra (v.15) does not begin with Scripture (his Gentile audience has no Jewish background) but with natural theology: the living God who made everything and has shown his kindness through rain and harvests. This is Paul's first recorded sermon to a fully pagan audience — the template for the Athens speech (ch.17).

Places

Lystra
37.5833° N, 32.4833° E
A Roman colony in Lycaonia (southern Galatia), c.29 km south of Iconium. Its largely rural, Lycaonian-speaking population had little synagogue background — hence the misidentification of Paul and Barnabas as Hermes and Zeus. The local Zeus cult (v.13) was prominent enough to have a dedicated priest and temple outside the city.
Derbe
37.3500° N, 33.3833° E
The easternmost city of Paul's first journey — modern Kerti Hüyük in Turkey. A Roman colony on the border of Lycaonia and Cappadocia. Luke notes only that Paul and Barnabas 'won a large number of disciples' (v.21) — the lack of opposition suggests a more peaceful reception than the earlier cities.

Context

The Lystra episode is Paul's first encounter with purely pagan polytheism — a world without synagogue, Scripture, or Jewish monotheistic preparation. The crowd's identification of Barnabas as Zeus and Paul as Hermes (v.12) reflects a local legend (attested in Ovid, Metamorphoses 8) about gods visiting the region in human form. The apostles' response — tearing their clothes, rushing out, crying 'We are only human!' — is the absolute anti-thesis of the divine honours Herod Agrippa had accepted (12:22–23). Paul's sermon adjusts to his audience: no OT, no synagogue tradition, just creation, providence, and the call to turn to the living God.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 12:22–23Herod accepted divine acclamation and died immediately. Paul and Barnabas refuse divine acclamation vigorously (vv.14–15). The contrast is deliberate: the pattern of faithful response to divine honours is established by the apostles' own example.
  • Acts 17:22–31The Athens speech develops the Lystra natural theology argument much more fully: the unknown God, creation's testimony, human searching. Lystra is the template; Athens is the elaboration.
  • 2 Corinthians 11:25"Once I was stoned" — Paul's own reference to the Lystra stoning, confirming the Acts account.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:14–15

MacArthur on the apostles' response to divine honours: tearing clothes was the Jewish expression of horror at blasphemy. Paul and Barnabas do not politely deflect — they rush out, tear their garments, and shout their rejection. MacArthur uses this as the model for every leader who is over-honoured: the response must be immediate, unambiguous, and visceral.

14:19–20

MacArthur on Paul getting up after the stoning: whether miraculous or not, the apostolic commitment is extraordinary. The man who was stoned for preaching in Lystra walks back into Lystra the same evening and leaves for Derbe the next morning. The mission is more important than personal safety — a principle Paul will articulate in Phil 1:21: 'to live is Christ and to die is gain.'

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:15

Calvin on 'we are bringing you good news, telling you to turn from these worthless things to the living God': the Gentile mission begins with the repudiation of idols and the proclamation of the Creator. Calvin uses this to argue that natural theology is not sufficient for salvation but is the necessary starting point for preaching to those without Scripture — the living God of creation must be identified before the saving God of redemption can be proclaimed.

14:17

Calvin on "he has not left himself without testimony" — this is Calvin's key text for general revelation. God's kindness in rain, seasons, and food is a continuous testimony to his existence and benevolence — sufficient to render humanity without excuse (Rom 1:20) but insufficient to produce saving knowledge. The gospel does not replace natural revelation but completes it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:12

The NET notes that the identification of Barnabas as Zeus and Paul as Hermes reflects the ancient role of Hermes as the spokesman and messenger of the gods (hence "chief speaker" — v.12). Paul was evidently the main preacher; Barnabas, perhaps more imposing in appearance, was assigned the head of the pantheon.

14:19

The NET notes that the Jews "came from Antioch and Iconium" — a remarkable pursuit. The opposition that expelled Paul from Pisidian Antioch (13:50) and threatened him at Iconium (14:5) has now followed him to Lystra. Paul's later reference in Gal 4:13 to "a bodily illness" during his Galatian ministry may refer to the aftereffects of this stoning.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
14:3

Robertson on "speaking boldly for the Lord, who confirmed the message of his grace by enabling them to perform signs and wonders" — Robertson notes the causal structure: the signs and wonders confirm the message, not vice versa. The miracles do not create the Gospel; they authenticate it. The sequence is always: word → confirming signs, never: signs → word.

14:19–20

Robertson on Paul getting up and walking back into the city after the stoning: the recovery is rapid enough to be described as miraculous by many interpreters. Robertson notes that Paul's reference to Lystra in 2 Cor 11:25 ("once I was stoned") and his later catalogue of sufferings confirm the historical event without claiming miraculous recovery explicitly.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:11–12

Keener provides extensive background on the Zeus-Hermes identification. The Lycaonian region had a specific local tradition (attested in Ovid, Metamorphoses 8.611–724) that Zeus and Hermes had once visited the area disguised as mortals. The crowds' immediate identification of the apostles with these deities reflects a living mythological expectation, not mere flattery.

14:15–17

Keener notes that Paul's address to a pagan audience in Lystra has no OT citations — he begins with natural theology: the living God made everything and has not left himself without testimony through creation's providential gifts. This is Stoic-style natural theology deployed for Jewish-monotheist purposes — Paul meeting his audience where they are.

Verses 21—28 — Derbe; Strengthening the Churches; Return to Antioch
2122232425262728

Greek Word Study

πρεσβυτέρους presbyterous — elders: the first mention of appointed elders in Acts' record of Paul's church-planting work. Elders (from presbyteros — older, senior) are the governing officers of each local church. Paul and Barnabas do not simply preach and depart; they establish structure for ongoing pastoral care. The appointment is through prayer and fasting — not democratic election alone but Spirit-directed discernment.

θλίψεων thlipseōn — hardships: from thlibō — to press, to constrict, to afflict. The suffering Paul and Barnabas describe as the path to the kingdom (v.22) is not incidental but structural: the community must walk the same road as their Lord — through cross to crown, through suffering to glory (Rom 8:17).

Places

Attalia (Antalya)
36.8841° N, 30.7056° E
The main port city on the Pamphylian coast, from which Paul and Barnabas sailed back to Syrian Antioch to complete their report. Modern Antalya is Turkey's major resort city. The return by sea was the fastest route home.
Syrian Antioch — Home Base
36.2021° N, 36.1603° E
The church that commissioned the journey (13:1–3) receives the full report: 'all that God had done through them' (v.27). The mission is bookended by the sending community — the Antioch church is not just a launching pad but a receiving community that learns from and celebrates the mission's fruit.

Context

The return journey deliberately retraces the outbound route through the most dangerous cities (Lystra, Iconium, Pisidian Antioch — cities from which Paul had been expelled or nearly killed) to strengthen the young churches. This deliberate return is a statement: Paul does not abandon his converts once persecution drives him out. The appointment of elders (v.23) is the first recorded instance of Paul establishing formal local church leadership — the structure that will be elaborated in his Pastoral Epistles (Titus 1:5; 1 Tim 3:1–7).

Cross-Reference

  • Titus 1:5"I left you in Crete... so that you might put in order what was left unfinished and appoint elders in every town" — the Pastoral Epistles formalise what Acts 14:23 begins: elder appointment is the completion of church-planting, not optional afterwards.
  • Acts 13:1–3The commissioning scene at Antioch — the mission is bookended by the sending community's prayer and fasting. The report back (v.27) closes the loop: Antioch sent them; Antioch hears the results.
  • Acts 15:1–2The return to Antioch immediately precedes the Jerusalem Council controversy — the theological crisis that the success of the first journey provokes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:22

MacArthur on "many hardships to enter the kingdom of God" — this is Paul's foundational pastoral teaching to new converts: expect suffering. MacArthur uses this to argue that prosperity-gospel promises of ease are not just theologically wrong but pastorally cruel — they promise what Paul explicitly said would not be the disciples' experience.

14:23

MacArthur on elder appointment: the churches are not left as unstructured gatherings but given formal oversight. The elders are appointed (not merely affirmed) by the apostles, with prayer and fasting — showing that the appointment is both apostolic and Spirit-directed. MacArthur uses this to defend the perpetual necessity of formal church leadership.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:22

Calvin on 'remain true to the faith': the encouragement is not general optimism but specific doctrinal faithfulness. 'The faith' (tēn pistin with the article) refers to the body of apostolic teaching, not merely personal trust. Calvin uses this to argue for catechetical instruction alongside evangelism — converts must be taught the faith they have entered.

14:27

Calvin on 'all that God had done through them': Paul and Barnabas do not report what they accomplished but what God accomplished through them. Calvin reads this as the permanent apostolic posture toward ministry: the servant is the instrument; God is the agent. Faithfulness in the instrument is required; glory belongs to the agent alone.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:23

The NET notes that cheirotoneō (appointed) was used in classical Greek for election by show of hands in a public assembly. In later Christian usage it came to mean appointment by church leaders. The Acts 14 context — Paul and Barnabas appointing, with prayer and fasting — suggests apostolic appointment rather than democratic election, though the word's original democratic connotation may indicate community participation.

14:26

"Committed to the grace of God" — the NET notes that paradedomenoi (having been committed/entrusted) echoes the language of sacred deposit. The church had entrusted Paul and Barnabas to God's grace for the work; they now return having completed that entrusted task. The mission has a beginning, middle, and end — it is accountable and purposeful.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
14:22

"We must go through many hardships to enter the kingdom of God" — Robertson notes the dei (must) of divine necessity: suffering is not accidental but structural in God's plan for his people. Robertson connects this to Jesus's own prediction (Luke 24:26: "Did not the Messiah have to suffer these things?") and argues that Paul is teaching the disciples the theology of the cross.

14:27

"God had opened a door of faith to the Gentiles" — Robertson notes the metaphor: a door is an opportunity; faith is both the means of entry and what the door leads into. The passive "had opened" attributes the initiative entirely to God. Paul and Barnabas are not reporting their achievement but God's action through them.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:23

Keener notes that the appointment of elders by Paul and Barnabas (cheirotoneō — literally 'to stretch out the hand,' used for voting in Greek political context) represents a hybrid of Jewish synagogue practice (appointing elders) and Hellenistic civic practice (community selection). The prayer and fasting context places the ultimate appointment authority with God rather than with democratic process or apostolic fiat alone.

14:27

Keener notes the significance of the full congregational report: "they gathered the church together." The mission was not Paul's private enterprise or even the apostles' project — it was the Antioch church's mission. The report closes the accountability loop: the whole community that prayed them out hears the full account of what God did.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Paul↗ People
Apostle to the Gentiles; author of thirteen NT letters
Paul (Saul of Tarsus) is introduced as a persecutor at Stephen’s stoning (Acts 7:58) and is dramatically converted on the Damascus road (Acts 9). He dominates Acts 13–28 across three missionary journeys, planting churches from Cyprus to Greece to Rome. His final journey to Rome as a prisoner frames Acts’ theological climax: the gospel reaching the empire’s capital, "boldly and without hindrance" (Acts 28:31).
Barnabas↗ People
Paul’s first missionary partner; 'Son of Encouragement'
Barnabas (Joseph of Cyprus) is introduced as a generous land-seller in Acts 4:36–37 and is the one who vouches for the newly converted Paul to the suspicious Jerusalem church (Acts 9:27). He leads the Antioch church (Acts 11:22–24) and takes Paul as his partner for the first missionary journey (Acts 13–14). Their sharp disagreement over Mark (Acts 15:36–40) ends their partnership but doubles the missionary coverage.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 14Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 14 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
