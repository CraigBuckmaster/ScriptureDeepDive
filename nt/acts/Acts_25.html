Acts 25

Paul Before Festus; Appeal to Caesar

Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Verses 1–12 — Festus Arrives; Paul Appeals to Caesar
1Three days after arriving in the province, Festus went up from Caesarea to Jerusalem,2where the chief priests and the Jewish leaders appeared before him and presented the charges against Paul.3They requested Festus, as a favor to them, to have Paul transferred to Jerusalem, for they were preparing an ambush to kill him along the way.4Festus answered, "Paul is being held at Caesarea, and I myself am going there soon.5Let some of your leaders come with me, and if the man has done anything wrong, they can press charges against him there."6After spending eight or ten days with them, Festus went down to Caesarea. The next day he convened the court and ordered that Paul be brought before him.7When Paul came in, the Jews who had come down from Jerusalem stood around him. They brought many serious charges against him, but they could not prove them.8Then Paul made his defense: "I have done nothing wrong against the Jewish law or against the temple or against Caesar."9Festus, wishing to do the Jews a favor, said to Paul, "Are you willing to go up to Jerusalem and stand trial before me there on these charges?"10Paul answered: "I am now standing before Caesar's court, where I ought to be tried. I have not done any wrong to the Jews, as you yourself know very well.11If, however, I am guilty of doing anything deserving death, I do not refuse to die. But if the charges brought against me by these Jews are not true, no one has the right to hand me over to them. I appeal to Caesar!"12After Festus had conferred with his council, he declared: "You have appealed to Caesar. To Caesar you will go!"

Greek Word Study

Καίσαρα ἐπικαλοῦμαι Kaisara epikaloumai — I appeal to Caesar: Paul's appeal in v.11 is the formal legal term provocatio ad Caesarem — available to Roman citizens who believed they were being denied justice in the provinces. Once invoked before a magistrate, the appeal was irrevocable. Paul uses the one legal mechanism available to ensure he will reach Rome.

τῷ Καίσαρος βήματι tō Kaisaros bēmati — Caesar's court: v.10 — Paul insists he is already standing at Caesar's court (Felix and Festus govern as Caesar's representatives). The appeal is not an admission of guilt but an assertion of his right to a fair Roman trial rather than being handed over to his accusers' jurisdiction.

Context

Paul's appeal to Caesar is both a legal manoeuvre and a providential fulfilment. Legally it uses the provocatio ad Caesarem — the Roman citizen's right to appeal to the emperor when provincial justice fails. Providentially it fulfils Acts 23:11: 'you must also testify in Rome.' Paul does not passively wait for God to move him to Rome — he invokes the legal mechanism that will get him there. His active participation in the providential plan is characteristically Pauline.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 23:11"As you have testified about me in Jerusalem, so you must also testify in Rome" — the Lord's directive that Paul's appeal now fulfils.
  • Acts 19:21"After I have been there, I must also visit Rome" — Paul's own anticipation, now becoming reality.
  • Acts 26:32"This man could have been set free if he had not appealed to Caesar" — confirming the appeal was Paul's own decision.

Verses 13–27 — Agrippa and Bernice; Festus Describes Paul's Case
13A few days later King Agrippa and Bernice arrived at Caesarea to pay their respects to Festus.14Since they were spending many days there, Festus discussed Paul's case with Agrippa. He said: "There is a man here whom Felix left as a prisoner.15When I went to Jerusalem, the chief priests and the elders of the Jews brought charges against him and asked that he be condemned.16"I told them that it is not the Roman custom to hand over anyone before they have faced their accusers and have had an opportunity to defend themselves against the charges.17When they came here with me, I did not delay the case, but convened the court the next day and ordered the man to be brought in.18When his accusers got up to speak, they did not charge him with any of the crimes I had expected.19Instead, they had some points of dispute with him about their own religion and about a dead man named Jesus who Paul claimed was alive.20I was at a loss how to investigate such matters; so I asked if he would be willing to go to Jerusalem and stand trial there on these charges.21But when Paul made his appeal to be held over for the Emperor's decision, I ordered him held until I could send him to Caesar."22Then Agrippa said to Festus, "I would like to hear this man myself." He replied, "Tomorrow you will hear him."23The next day Agrippa and Bernice came with great pomp and entered the audience room with the high-ranking military officers and the prominent men of the city. At the command of Festus, Paul was brought in.24Festus said: "King Agrippa and all who are present with us, you see this man! The whole Jewish community has petitioned me about him in Jerusalem and here in Caesarea, shouting that he ought not to live any longer.25I found he had done nothing deserving death, but because he made his appeal to the Emperor, I decided to send him to Rome.26But I have nothing definite to write to His Majesty about him. Therefore I have brought him before all of you, and especially before you, King Agrippa, so that as a result of this investigation I may have something to write.27For I think it is unreasonable to send a prisoner to Rome without specifying the charges against him."

Greek Word Study

ἄνθρωπόν τινα τεθνηκότα anthrōpon tina tethnēkota — a dead man named Jesus: Festus's summary of Paul's case in v.19 — 'some points of dispute about their own religion and about a dead man named Jesus who Paul claimed was alive' — is the outsider's précis of the entire Gospel. The governor has accidentally identified the most important claim in human history as a legal technicality.

ἐξαιτεῖσθαι exaiteisthai — held over for the Emperor's decision: the technical term for Paul's appeal (v.21) — held over for the Augustus's examination. Luke uses precise Roman legal vocabulary throughout the captivity narrative, reflecting personal presence as an eyewitness of legal proceedings.

Context

Festus's description of Paul's case to Agrippa (vv.14–21) is Luke's masterstroke of irony. The governor — without intending to — gives the most concise and accurate summary of the Gospel in Acts: 'some points of dispute about their own religion and about a dead man named Jesus who Paul claimed was alive.' Festus is baffled; Paul is preaching the resurrection; and the reader understands that Festus has accidentally described the most important fact in history as a legal technicality.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 24:27"Felix wanted to grant a favor to the Jews" — the same political calculation that imprisoned Paul under Felix now threatens him under Festus (25:9). The pattern of justice compromised by political calculation recurs.
  • Acts 26:31–32"This man is not doing anything that deserves death or imprisonment" — Agrippa's verdict after the hearing this chapter sets up.
  • Luke 21:12–13"They will seize you and persecute you... you will stand before kings and governors, and all on account of my name. And so you will bear testimony to me" — Jesus's prediction being fulfilled precisely.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:13

Keener provides background on Herod Agrippa II (27–c.100 AD), son of Herod Agrippa I (who died in Acts 12:23). He was appointed by Rome as king over territories in northern Palestine and given authority over the high priesthood. His intimate knowledge of Jewish politics and religion made him uniquely qualified to advise Festus.

25:26

Keener notes the Roman legal requirement of the apostoli (written statement of charges) when transferring a prisoner to Rome. Without specifying charges, Festus could face legal consequences for an irregular transfer. The Agrippa hearing is both a courtesy to the royal visitor and a practical legal necessity.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

Literary Structure

The Outsider's Gospel Summary — v.19Festus's summary — 'about a dead man named Jesus who Paul claimed was alive' — is Acts' most compressed statement of the Gospel from an outsider's perspective. Festus intends it as a description of an inexplicable legal dispute; Luke presents it as the accurate identification of the most important claim in history. The chapter's irony reaches its peak in this single verse.
The Roman Innocence Chain — Fourth Link — v.25Acts 25 adds the fourth link in Luke's cumulative Roman innocence chain: Gallio (18:14–15), Claudius Lysias (23:29), Felix (implied), and Festus ('he had done nothing deserving death', v.25). The chain will be completed by Agrippa (26:31–32) — Luke's sustained legal argument that the Gospel is innocent before Roman authority.
Acts 25 advances Paul's legal journey to its decisive turn — the appeal to Caesar that will take him to Rome — while setting up the Agrippa hearing. Festus's bewildered summary of the resurrection dispute as a legal technicality is Luke's sharpest irony: the governor has accidentally identified the central claim of all history.
