Acts 27

The Voyage and Shipwreck

See on Map — Paul's Journey to Rome
Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Greek Word Study

Εὐρακύλων Eurakulōn — the Northeaster: the technical nautical term for the dreaded northeast Mediterranean storm (v.14). Ancient sailors knew this storm system by name: it blew from the northeast with hurricane force, making steering impossible. Luke's use of the precise technical term is one of many details confirming his eyewitness presence.

τοῦ θεοῦ οὗ εἰμι tou theou hou eimi — the God whose I am: Paul's identification in v.23 — 'the God whose I am and whom I serve.' The double claim of belonging and service is one of the most compact expressions of discipleship in the NT. Ownership precedes service; identity precedes activity.

Context

Acts 27 is one of the most detailed eyewitness voyage accounts in ancient literature — compared favourably by classicists to the best Greek and Roman seafaring narratives. But Luke's purpose is theological: the ship full of condemned prisoners is preserved because one man on board belongs to God and has an appointment in Rome. The angel's assurance (v.24: 'God has graciously given you the lives of all who sail with you') means 275 other people are kept alive by virtue of their association with Paul.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 23:11"You must stand trial before Caesar" — the Lord's promise that the angel echoes in v.24. The shipwreck is part of the route to Rome, not a setback to it.
  • Jonah 1:1–16The storm, the prophet on board, the endangered crew — the structural parallel with Jonah is intentional. Paul is the anti-Jonah: where Jonah fled his commission, Paul is fulfilling his; where Jonah endangered the ship, Paul's presence saves it.
  • Psalm 107:23–30"Some went out on the sea in ships... He stilled the storm... and he guided them to their desired haven" — the psalm of sea rescue that provides the theological framework.

The entire ship is preserved because of one man's divine mission — an illustration of how God's purposes for one person can overflow into blessing all who share their circumstances.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
27:22–24

Calvin on Paul's assurance during the storm: the contrast is deliberate — the crew threw cargo overboard in despair; the apostle delivers a divine promise with complete calm. Paul's calm is not temperament but faith: he has a specific promise and states it as settled fact. Faith-based courage is not the absence of fear but the presence of a better certainty.

27:9–10

Calvin on Paul's ignored advice: the one man on board with divine knowledge is the one whose judgment is disregarded. The word of God is routinely set aside in favour of the world's practical expertise — with predictable results.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
27:9

The NET notes that "the Fast" refers to Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) — the only day in the Jewish calendar designated as a fast. It falls in late September or October, establishing the timing precisely and confirming Luke's attention to calendar detail.

27:17

The NET notes that "they passed ropes under the ship" (hypozōnnyntes to ploion) is frapping — a maritime practice of wrapping the hull with ropes to prevent planks from splitting under storm stress. The technical detail confirms Luke's eyewitness knowledge.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
27:10

"Men, I can see that our voyage is going to be disastrous" — Robertson notes Paul says "I can see," not "the Spirit tells me" — this is practical judgment from an experienced traveller who had already been shipwrecked three times (2 Cor 11:25). The advice is ignored, making his subsequent angelic assurance both more dramatic and more credible.

27:22

"Not one of you will be lost; only the ship will be destroyed" — Robertson notes the precision: lives preserved, ship lost. God's promises are specific, not general. Robertson: "Paul states it as settled fact — this is faith, not optimism."

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:9

Keener notes the navigation significance of 'the Day of Atonement': Yom Kippur falls in September/October — the period when the ancients considered Mediterranean sailing dangerous (approximately September 14 to November 11 was increasing-risk season). Paul's warning was maritimely sound as well as spiritually grounded.

27:14

Keener provides background on the Eurakulos (Northeaster). Pliny the Elder and other ancient writers describe this storm system. The storm's route — taking the ship from Crete toward Malta — is consistent with actual Mediterranean storm patterns, confirming the voyage's historicity.

Greek Word Study

εὐχαριστήσας τῷ θεῷ ἐνώπιον πάντων eucharistēsas tō theō enōpion pantōn — gave thanks to God in front of them all: Paul's bread-breaking in vv.35–36 — taking bread, giving thanks, breaking it — uses the same fourfold eucharistic action as the Last Supper (Luke 22:19) and Emmaus (Luke 24:30). The Gospel's table practice appears in the middle of a shipwreck, in front of 275 non-Christians.

διεσώθησαν πάντες diesōthēsan pantes — everyone reached land safely: v.44 — the fulfilment of the angel's promise (v.24). The passive 'were saved' (diesōthēsan) is Luke's theological signal: this was God's act. Every one of the 276 people on board survived — not one was lost.

The 'bay with a sandy beach' (v.39) where the ship ran aground. The description is consistent with Malta's northern coast. The centurion Julius — who protected Paul throughout the voyage (v.43) — here again uses his authority to ensure Paul's survival.

Context

The shipwreck narrative reaches its climax in Paul's eucharistic bread-breaking (vv.33–36) and the grounding that fulfils the angel's promise exactly (vv.41–44). The bread-breaking in front of all 276 people is the Gospel's table in the midst of disaster — the community of faith sharing food with those who do not yet share their faith. Every one of the 276 reaches land safely: the angel's specific promise is specifically kept.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 22:19"He took bread, gave thanks and broke it" — the Last Supper action that Paul replicates in vv.35–36.
  • Luke 24:30–31"He took bread, gave thanks, broke it and began to give it to them. Then their eyes were opened" — Luke consistently uses the eucharistic action as the moment of spiritual recognition.
  • Acts 27:24"God has graciously given you the lives of all who sail with you" — the promise fulfilled exactly in v.44: "everyone reached land safely."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:33–36

MacArthur on Paul's bread-breaking: the eucharistic action in front of 275 non-Christians is a public act of faith. Paul visibly demonstrates that he trusts God by eating, inviting others to follow. The crew follows his lead (v.36). Faith is contagious when visibly demonstrated.

27:43–44

MacArthur on Julius's protection: Julius's repeated protection of Paul (v.3; v.43) is the human instrument of divine preservation. God's providential protection regularly operates through the actions of non-Christians who have no awareness of their role in his plan.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
27:34

"Not one of you will lose a single hair from his head" — Calvin: the divine promise is as specific as humanly possible. "Not a hair" echoes Luke 21:18. God's promises are not vague reassurances but specific commitments — and their fulfilment is equally specific.

27:44

Calvin on 'everyone reached land safely': the narrative proof of Paul's faith (v.25: 'I have faith in God that it will happen just as he told me'). Not faith that avoids danger but faith that walks through danger holding specific divine promises.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
27:35

The NET notes the debate about whether this is a formal Eucharist. The language parallels the Last Supper (Luke 22:19) and Emmaus meal (Luke 24:30). The NET suggests that while this may not be the formal sacrament, Luke deliberately uses eucharistic language — the meal carries the theology of the Lord's Table even if it is not the formal sacrament.

27:37

The NET notes the textual variant: most manuscripts read 276; a few read 76. The NET follows the majority reading of 276 as the harder reading — more likely to be accidentally reduced than inflated.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
27:35

"He took some bread and gave thanks to God in front of them all. Then he broke it" — Robertson notes the deliberate eucharistic language: the four verbs (took, gave thanks, broke, began to eat) are precisely the Last Supper verbs. Paul acts as the host of a meal of thanksgiving in the middle of a crisis — a visible demonstration of faith to 275 unbelievers.

27:44

"Everyone reached land safely" — Robertson on hapas (all, everyone) completing the promise of v.24. The angel's specific promise has been kept exactly. God's specific promises are specifically fulfilled.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:28

Keener notes the navigational precision: the soundings of 120 feet, then 90 feet are consistent with the approach to Malta's northern coast. Modern hydrographic surveys of St. Paul's Bay confirm the depth contours match Luke's account exactly — powerful evidence of eyewitness accuracy.

27:37

Keener notes that 276 people on a grain ship is historically plausible. Josephus (Life 3.15) mentions a grain ship with 600 on board. The Alexandria-to-Rome grain route routinely carried large numbers; 276 is not exaggerated.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Paul↗ People
Apostle to the Gentiles; author of thirteen NT letters
Paul (Saul of Tarsus) is introduced as a persecutor at Stephen’s stoning (Acts 7:58) and is dramatically converted on the Damascus road (Acts 9). He dominates Acts 13–28 across three missionary journeys, planting churches from Cyprus to Greece to Rome. His final journey to Rome as a prisoner frames Acts’ theological climax: the gospel reaching the empire’s capital, "boldly and without hindrance" (Acts 28:31).

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 27Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 27 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
