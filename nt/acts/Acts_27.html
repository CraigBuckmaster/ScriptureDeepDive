Acts 27

The Voyage and Shipwreck

See on Map — Paul's Journey to Rome
Author

Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a Gentile convert and travelling companion of Paul. Acts is the second volume of a two-part work addressed to Theophilus (Luke 1:3; Acts 1:1). The “we” sections (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) place Luke as an eyewitness participant in significant portions of the Pauline mission. Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, and the Muratorian Canon (c. AD 170) unanimously identify Luke as author.

When written

c. AD 62–64, most likely during Paul's Roman imprisonment (28:30–31). The abrupt ending — Paul awaiting trial — is most naturally explained if Luke wrote before the outcome was known. The silence about Jerusalem’s destruction (AD 70) and Paul’s death (c. AD 67) supports an early 60s date.

What prompted it

Luke traces the Gospel from Jerusalem to Rome, fulfilling the commission of 1:8 (“you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth”). Acts defends the legitimacy of the Gentile mission, demonstrates the continuity of the church with Israel, and shows that the gospel’s advance is providentially directed through opposition, imprisonment, and persecution. It is the indispensable bridge between the Gospels and the Epistles.

Greek Word Study

Εὐρακύλων Eurakulōn — the Northeaster: the technical nautical term for the dreaded northeast Mediterranean storm (v.14). Ancient sailors knew this storm system by name: it blew from the northeast with hurricane force, making steering impossible. Luke's use of the precise technical term is one of many details confirming his eyewitness presence.

τοῦ θεοῦ οὗ εἰμι tou theou hou eimi — the God whose I am: Paul's identification in v.23 — 'the God whose I am and whom I serve.' The double claim of belonging and service is one of the most compact expressions of discipleship in the NT. Ownership precedes service; identity precedes activity.

Context

Acts 27 is one of the most detailed eyewitness voyage accounts in ancient literature — compared favourably by classicists to the best Greek and Roman seafaring narratives. But Luke's purpose is theological: the ship full of condemned prisoners is preserved because one man on board belongs to God and has an appointment in Rome. The angel's assurance (v.24: 'God has graciously given you the lives of all who sail with you') means 275 other people are kept alive by virtue of their association with Paul.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 23:11"You must stand trial before Caesar" — the Lord's promise that the angel echoes in v.24. The shipwreck is part of the route to Rome, not a setback to it.
  • Jonah 1:1–16The storm, the prophet on board, the endangered crew — the structural parallel with Jonah is intentional. Paul is the anti-Jonah: where Jonah fled his commission, Paul is fulfilling his; where Jonah endangered the ship, Paul's presence saves it.
  • Psalm 107:23–30"Some went out on the sea in ships... He stilled the storm... and he guided them to their desired haven" — the psalm of sea rescue that provides the theological framework.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
27:22–24

Calvin on Paul's assurance during the storm: the contrast is deliberate — the crew threw cargo overboard in despair; the apostle delivers a divine promise with complete calm. Paul's calm is not temperament but faith: he has a specific promise and states it as settled fact. Faith-based courage is not the absence of fear but the presence of a better certainty.

27:9–10

Calvin on Paul's ignored advice: the one man on board with divine knowledge is the one whose judgment is disregarded. The word of God is routinely set aside in favour of the world's practical expertise — with predictable results.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
27:9

The NET notes that "the Fast" refers to Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) — the only day in the Jewish calendar designated as a fast. It falls in late September or October, establishing the timing precisely and confirming Luke's attention to calendar detail.

27:17

The NET notes that "they passed ropes under the ship" (hypozōnnyntes to ploion) is frapping — a maritime practice of wrapping the hull with ropes to prevent planks from splitting under storm stress. The technical detail confirms Luke's eyewitness knowledge.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
27:10

"Men, I can see that our voyage is going to be disastrous" — Robertson notes Paul says "I can see," not "the Spirit tells me" — this is practical judgment from an experienced traveller who had already been shipwrecked three times (2 Cor 11:25). The advice is ignored, making his subsequent angelic assurance both more dramatic and more credible.

27:22

"Not one of you will be lost; only the ship will be destroyed" — Robertson notes the precision: lives preserved, ship lost. God's promises are specific, not general. Robertson: "Paul states it as settled fact — this is faith, not optimism."

Keener — Acts Commentary

27:9

Keener notes the navigation significance of 'the Day of Atonement': Yom Kippur falls in September/October — the period when the ancients considered Mediterranean sailing dangerous (approximately September 14 to November 11 was increasing-risk season). Keener provides background on the Eurakulos (Northeaster). Pliny the Elder and other ancient writers describe this storm system. The storm's route — taking the ship from Crete toward Malta — is consistent with actual Mediterranean storm patterns, confirming the voyage's historicity.

Greek Word Study

εὐχαριστήσας τῷ θεῷ ἐνώπιον πάντων eucharistēsas tō theō enōpion pantōn — gave thanks to God in front of them all: Paul's bread-breaking in vv.35–36 — taking bread, giving thanks, breaking it — uses the same fourfold eucharistic action as the Last Supper (Luke 22:19) and Emmaus (Luke 24:30). The Gospel's table practice appears in the middle of a shipwreck, in front of 275 non-Christians.

διεσώθησαν πάντες diesōthēsan pantes — everyone reached land safely: v.44 — the fulfilment of the angel's promise (v.24). The passive 'were saved' (diesōthēsan) is Luke's theological signal: this was God's act. Every one of the 276 people on board survived — not one was lost.

Context

The shipwreck narrative reaches its climax in Paul's eucharistic bread-breaking (vv.33–36) and the grounding that fulfils the angel's promise exactly (vv.41–44). The bread-breaking in front of all 276 people is the Gospel's table in the midst of disaster — the community of faith sharing food with those who do not yet share their faith. Every one of the 276 reaches land safely: the angel's specific promise is specifically kept.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 22:19"He took bread, gave thanks and broke it" — the Last Supper action that Paul replicates in vv.35–36.
  • Luke 24:30–31"He took bread, gave thanks, broke it and began to give it to them. Then their eyes were opened" — Luke consistently uses the eucharistic action as the moment of spiritual recognition.
  • Acts 27:24"God has graciously given you the lives of all who sail with you" — the promise fulfilled exactly in v.44: "everyone reached land safely."

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:28

Keener notes the navigational precision: the soundings of 120 feet, then 90 feet are consistent with the approach to Malta's northern coast. Modern hydrographic surveys of St. Paul's Bay confirm the depth contours match Luke's account exactly — powerful evidence of eyewitness accuracy.

27:37

Keener notes that 276 people on a grain ship is historically plausible. Josephus (Life 3.15) mentions a grain ship with 600 on board. The Alexandria-to-Rome grain route routinely carried large numbers; 276 is not exaggerated.

"I urge you to keep up your courage, because not one of you will be lost." Standing before 275 terrified sailors and soldiers, Paul is the only calm figure on the ship. An angel has told him he will stand before Caesar; God has given him everyone on board. 276 people owe their lives to one man's mission.

Roman officer — protector of Paul throughout
In charge of Paul's transfer to Rome. Consistently extends kindness: allows Paul to visit friends at Sidon; protects him from the soldiers' plan to kill prisoners after the shipwreck. The human instrument of Paul's providential protection.

Literary Structure

The Anti-Jonah Narrative — ch.27Acts 27 is constructed in deliberate parallel and contrast with Jonah: both involve a prophet on a ship in a storm; both involve endangered sailors; both are resolved by divine preservation. But Paul is the anti-Jonah: where Jonah fled his commission and endangered the ship, Paul is fulfilling his commission and his presence saves the ship. Where Jonah was thrown overboard, Paul's presence on board preserves all 276.
The Eucharist in the Storm — vv.35–36Paul's bread-breaking in front of 275 non-Christians during a two-week storm is Luke's most daring eucharistic moment. The four eucharistic verbs appear in the midst of the most secular narrative in Acts — surrounding sailors, soldiers, and prisoners in a broken ship at dawn. The Gospel's table does not wait for a sanctuary; it appears wherever one believer gives thanks to God in front of all.
Acts 27 is simultaneously the most detailed eyewitness voyage narrative in ancient literature and Luke's most sustained meditation on what it looks like for one person's relationship with God to overflow into the preservation of everyone around them. 276 lives are kept because one man on the ship belongs to God and has an appointment in Rome.
