Verses 1–19 — John’s Question from Prison; Jesus’ Testimony to John
12345678910111213141516171819

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

John's question from prison (v.3) is theologically honest: he had proclaimed a coming judge; what he hears is healings and proclamation to the poor. Jesus's answer is Isaiah-saturated: the messianic era looks like Isaiah 35 and 61, not like a fire-judgment. The challenge is not whether Jesus is fulfilling Scripture but whether John (and the reader) can accept the shape of the fulfilment. Jesus then defends John's greatness while redrawing the boundary of greatness: the least in the kingdom exceeds the greatest prophet.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 35:5–6; 61:1Blind see, lame walk, deaf hear, dead raised, poor hear good news — the portrait of the messianic era. Jesus's answer to John is a direct application of these texts to his own ministry.
  • Mal 3:1; 4:5–6My messenger who prepares the way; the return of Elijah. Jesus identifies John as both — the messenger of Malachi 3 and the Elijah of Malachi 4.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:11

Calvin on John being the greatest among those born of women yet the least in the kingdom: This is not a diminishment of John but a statement about the progressive nature of revelation. John stands at the threshold; those who enter the kingdom receive what John could only see from the outside. The greater revelation produces the greater privilege -- even the least in the kingdom stands inside what John saw only from the border.

11:28-30

Calvin on 'learn from me, for I am gentle and humble in heart': This is the description of the Saviour who receives weary sinners. The divine Lawgiver who could demand and condemn presents himself as gentle and humble -- the qualities that make the yoke bearable. The person who comes to Christ for rest finds not a demanding judge but a servant master who bears the yoke alongside them.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:2-6

The NET note on the messianic evidence Jesus cites traces each element to its prophetic source: blind see (Isa 29:18; 35:5), lame walk (35:6), lepers cleansed (implicitly through the purity-restoration theme), deaf hear (35:5), dead raised (26:19), poor have good news (61:1). The cumulative citation establishes that Jesus' ministry fulfils the eschatological programme Isaiah described.

11:25-27

The NET note on 'no one knows the Son except the Father, and no one knows the Father except the Son' discusses the mutual knowledge formula. The note identifies this as the highest Christological claim in Matthew: the Son has the same exclusive, direct knowledge of the Father that the Father has of the Son -- the mutual knowledge of divine persons.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
11:2-6

Robertson notes John's question from prison is not doubt but a request for clarification -- or possibly a pastoral prompt to his own disciples to hear Jesus directly. The response lists six types of healing (blind see, lame walk, lepers cleansed, deaf hear, dead raised, poor have good news proclaimed) that correspond to Isaiah's messianic programme (Isa 35:5-6; 61:1). Jesus answers the question with evidence rather than assertion.

11:28-30

Robertson identifies the yoke metaphor as drawn from Jewish wisdom tradition (Sirach 51:23-27) where wisdom invites students to take up her yoke. Jesus presents himself as the embodiment of wisdom -- taking his yoke is learning from him directly. The paradox (my yoke is easy, my burden is light) is answered by the qualifier: I am gentle and humble in heart. The burden is heavy if the master is harsh; it is light if the master bears it alongside you.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
11:2-6

Chrysostom: John did not doubt -- a man who had seen the Spirit descend like a dove and heard the voice from heaven could not have doubted. He sent his disciples to Christ for their own benefit, not his. He was preparing them to leave him and follow Christ. The answer Jesus gave was not to rebuke John but to instruct his disciples by the evidence.

11:28-30

Augustine: Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest -- this is the gospel in miniature. The weary are those exhausted by the law's demands without the law's grace. The burdened are those carrying guilt, shame, and the weight of unforgiven sin. Christ offers what the law requires but cannot supply: rest for the soul.

Places

Chorazin
c.3km north of Capernaum
Chorazin is singled out for most of Jesus's miracles — yet it did not repent. The site is archaeologically identified; a basalt-stone synagogue has been excavated there. Despite being the site of extraordinary works, Chorazin's rejection makes it more culpable than Tyre and Sidon.
Bethsaida
Northeast shore of Sea of Galilee
Bethsaida is the hometown of Peter, Andrew, and Philip (John 1:44). Its proximity to Jesus's ministry base makes its unrepentance particularly striking. It will face judgment greater than Tyre and Sidon — the Phoenician cities that saw no miracles at all.
Capernaum
Northwest shore of Sea of Galilee
"Will you be lifted to the heavens? No, you will go down to Hades." Capernaum is the worst case: "exalted to the heavens" because of the extraordinary privileges it received, yet about to be condemned. The most favoured geography faces the most severe judgment.

11:1-19

MacArthur: John the Baptist's question from prison — are you the one who is to come, or should we expect someone else? — is not a failure of faith but an honest question from a man in a context that did not match his expectation. Verses 20–24 — Woes on Unrepentant Cities: Chorazin, Bethsaida, Capernaum
2021222324

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The chapter's second movement reverses the emotional register: from the woes on unrepentant cities (vv.20–24) to the Father's doxology (vv.25–26) to the most intimate invitation in Matthew (vv.28–30). The woes are the necessary context for the invitation — the cities that rejected the evidence have nowhere to go. But Jesus turns immediately to the weary and burdened with the most open-handed offer in the gospel: come, and I will give you rest.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 14:13–15I will raise myself above the stars of God — the pride of Babylon, now applied to Capernaum in v.23. To be "lifted to the heavens" in pride is to go down to Hades in judgment.
  • Jer 6:16Stand at the crossroads and ask for the ancient paths... you will find rest for your souls. Jesus's invitation in v.29 echoes Jeremiah's language but replaces "the ancient paths" with himself: "learn from me."
  • Sir 51:23–27Ben Sira's invitation to take wisdom's yoke. Jesus takes the Wisdom tradition's invitation to Torah-study and replaces Torah with himself — he is the Wisdom of God embodied.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:20-24

Calvin on the cities' unrepentance: The greater the gifts, the greater the guilt if they are spurned. The people of Chorazin and Bethsaida were not ignorant -- they were privileged beyond any generation in Israel's history and chose to ignore what was offered. Their judgment will be correspondingly severe. This teaching is a solemn warning to every generation that has received the gospel and neglected it.

11:25-27

Calvin on 'you have hidden these things from the wise and learned and revealed them to little children': This is a statement of sovereign grace. God does not owe the revelation to the wise; he gives it to whom he will. The little children (nepioi -- infants, the simple) receive what the sophisticates miss -- not because simplicity merits it but because God is pleased to give it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:16-19

The NET note on wisdom justified by her deeds (v.19) discusses the textual variant: Luke 7:35 has 'all her children' where Matthew has 'her deeds.' The note identifies the Matthean version as focusing on the validation of the wisdom approach by its outcomes. The deeds of the kingdom -- healings, proclamation, transformed lives -- justify the wisdom of both John's approach and Jesus' approach.

11:28-30

The NET note on 'easy' (chrestos) and 'light' (elaphros) in v.30 discusses the apparent paradox: discipleship involves significant demands and suffering. The note identifies the resolution: the yoke is easy and the burden light not because they are objectively small but because the master is gentle and humble -- he bears the burden alongside the disciple.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
11:16-19

Robertson notes the children's game image (piping/mourning) is a brilliant piece of social observation: the current generation is like children who criticise every overture -- John is too ascetic (has a demon), Jesus is too sociable (a glutton and drunkard). No presentation of the gospel will satisfy those who have determined not to be satisfied. Robertson notes wisdom is justified by her deeds (v.19b) -- the validation of the approach comes from its fruit.

11:20-24

Robertson observes that Chorazin and Bethsaida are condemned more severely than Tyre and Sidon -- the cities of Gentile corruption -- because greater privilege produces greater accountability. The unrepentant response to Jesus' miracles is more culpable than the ignorance of those who never saw them. The greater the revelation, the greater the responsibility.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
11:20-24

Jerome: Woe to you, Chorazin and Bethsaida -- these are the cities where most of his miracles were performed, yet they did not repent. The severity of the judgment is proportional to the privilege received. Tyre and Sidon, which received no direct ministry, would have repented with less evidence. The unrepentance of Chorazin and Bethsaida is therefore more culpable, not less.

11:25-27

Chrysostom on the mutual knowledge of Father and Son: No one knows the Father except the Son -- this statement puts the Son in a category shared with no creature. Not even the angels know the Father as the Son knows him. The mutual knowledge is the knowledge of co-equal persons in the eternal life of the Trinity.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
11:20-30

MacArthur: Woe to you, Chorazin! Woe to you, Bethsaida! The cities of privilege — where most of Jesus's miracles had been performed — receive the judgment. Greater revelation creates greater responsibility. Tyre and Sidon, pagan cities, would have responded to lesser evidence. The most privileged receive the harshest words.

MacArthur: Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest. My yoke is easy and my burden is light. Ancient Sources

Chorazin and Bethsaida
Both towns on the northern shore of the Sea of Galilee, near Capernaum. Chorazin is attested archaeologically but no miracles there are recorded in Matthew — suggesting a tradition Matthew did not preserve.
The woes imply miracles not recorded in the gospel.
The easy yoke
Rabbi Akiva's disciples were said to take upon themselves "the yoke of Torah." Jesus's invitation redeploys this established Jewish metaphor but substitutes himself for Torah.
The yoke metaphor was immediately recognisable in its Jewish context.

Reception History

11:28 in Christian spirituality
The "Come to me" invitation is among the most-quoted verses in Christian devotional literature — cited by Augustine, Thomas Aquinas, Julian of Norwich, Spurgeon. It grounds the entire tradition of approaching God in prayer with the certainty of welcome.
11:27 and Trinitarian theology
The mutual knowledge of Father and Son in v.27 was a key text in the Arian controversy — Athanasius cited it to argue for the Son's unique divine knowledge, not a subordinate's derived knowledge.

Textual Notes

11:12The kingdom of heaven has been subjected to violence, and violent people are raiding it
MT: apo de ton hemeronIoannou tou baptistou heos arti he basileia ton ouranon biazetai. The passive biazetai: is it forcefully advancing or being forcefully attacked?
The two readings produce opposite meanings: the kingdom is advancing powerfully, or the kingdom is being violently resisted. The context (John in prison, violence against the prophets) slightly favours the second reading: the kingdom's coming is being opposed by force. Its citizens enter through the same violence they face.

Scholarly Debates

Matthew 11:28-30 -- Does 'Rest' Mean Salvation or Discipleship?
Rest as salvation -- the relief of forgiveness
Most evangelical commentators
The weary and burdened are those exhausted by sin and law; the rest is the relief of forgiveness and acceptance. The gospel invitation is primarily soteriological.
Rest as discipleship -- the peace of learning from Christ
Some commentators
Take my yoke and learn from me describes ongoing discipleship. The rest is the settled peace of a life governed by a gentle master rather than a harsh taskmaster.
The two readings are complementary: initial rest (the relief of forgiveness) leads to ongoing rest (the peace of discipleship). Both are present in the invitation.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 11 moves from the validation of the messianic ministry (John's question answered) to the condemnation of unrepentance (the cities warned) to the gospel invitation (come to me). The chapter's governing tension: the very one who pronounces judgment on unrepentance is the one who invites the weary.
