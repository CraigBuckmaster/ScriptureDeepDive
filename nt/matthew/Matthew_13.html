Matthew 13

The Parable Discourse: The Sower; Mustard Seed; Hidden Treasure; Pearl

Verses 1–23 — The Sower: The Parable and Its Explanation
1234567891011121314151617181920212223

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

Matthew 13 is the third of the five great discourses — the parable discourse. It is given from a boat, to crowds on the shore, in the middle of ch.12's escalating opposition. The parable of the sower explains the mixed response Jesus has received throughout chs.11–12: the same word, sown in the same field, produces radically different results depending on the soil. The disciples' question (v.10) triggers the most important explanation of the parable method in the NT: Jesus speaks in parables because of the hardness of the crowd, and teaches their meaning to the disciples in private.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 6:9–10Hear but not understand; see but not perceive — the hardening oracle from Isaiah's call. Jesus applies it to the crowd's response to the parables. The parables are both judgment (hardening the resistant) and mercy (revealing to the receptive).
  • Ps 78:2I will open my mouth in parables — cited in v.35 as the introduction to the second parable series. The Psalter anticipated the parabolic method of the Messiah.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
13:1-23

MacArthur: The parable of the sower is the parable of all parables — the interpretive key to the rest. Four soils, one seed: the variable is not the gospel's power but the soil's receptivity. The explanations Jesus gives his disciples (vv.18-23) establish that kingdom parables are not merely illustrative but revelatory — they reveal and conceal simultaneously.

13:11-13

MacArthur: The knowledge of the secrets of the kingdom of heaven has been given to you but not to them. The parables are not dumbed-down explanations for the simple — they are revelations that require spiritual receptivity to penetrate. The same words that illuminate the disciple's understanding confirm the unbeliever's blindness.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:1-9

Calvin on the parable of the soils: The word of God is the same seed in all cases -- the preacher's responsibility is to sow faithfully. The various soils represent the various conditions of human hearts. The lesson for the preacher: do not be discouraged by the hard soil, the rocky soil, or the thorny soil. The harvest in the good soil compensates for all the seed that did not produce.

13:10-17

Calvin on the purpose of parables: The hardening of those who will not see is judicial -- God uses the parables to confirm them in the blindness they have chosen. This is not arbitrary but is the consistent pattern of divine justice: those who refuse available light receive less. The disciples' privilege of understanding is grace; the crowds' inability is judgment on their prior refusal.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:1-9

The NET note on the parable of the sower discusses the agricultural background: broadcast sowing in Palestine preceded ploughing, which is why seed fell on the path (unploughed path through the field), the rocky ground (limestone shelf just under the surface), and among thorns (unploughed weedy soil). The note also discusses the spectacular yield: thirty, sixty, hundredfold is far above the typical ancient Palestinian average of ten to fifteenfold.

13:10-17

The NET note on the purpose of parables discusses the Isaianic citation (Isa 6:9-10) and its theological function. The note traces the hardening theme through the OT: judicial hardening is the confirmation in the blindness of those who have already refused to see. The disciples receive explanation; the crowds receive concealment.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
13:1-9

Robertson notes that the parable of the sower is more accurately the parable of the four soils -- the sower is the same in all four cases; what differs is the reception. The fourfold soil (path, rocky, thorny, good) represents four different responses to the same proclamation. Robertson observes that only one in four produces a harvest -- but that harvest is spectacular: thirty, sixty, or a hundredfold.

13:18-23

Robertson identifies the interpretive keys: the birds are the evil one (v.19); the rocky soil is the person who receives with joy but has no root (v.20-21); the thorny soil is the person distracted by the anxieties of life and deceitfulness of wealth (v.22); the good soil is the person who hears, understands, and produces fruit (v.23). Robertson notes understanding (syniemi) is the key verb -- genuine reception requires genuine comprehension.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
13:1-9

Chrysostom: Why did he speak in parables? Because the multitude was gross and sensual and needed to be drawn by the pleasure of a story before they could receive the instruction. But also because the parables concealed from those who would not receive and revealed to those who would. The parable is both invitation and filter.

13:10-17

Augustine on the reason for parables: He speaks in parables not to obscure the truth but to reveal it to those with ears to hear while leaving it hidden from those who have hardened their hearts. The parable is judgment on those who will not see and grace to those who will. The same words accomplish both.

Verses 24–43 — Weeds; Mustard Seed; Yeast: Kingdom Growth and Patience

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The second section of the parable discourse addresses the kingdom's mixed composition (weeds and wheat), its unexpected growth pattern (mustard seed, yeast), and the eschatological patience required by both. The weeds parable is the most explicitly eschatological in ch.13 — the Son of Man's angels will separate at the harvest. The mustard seed and yeast parables counter the discouragement that arises from the kingdom's small beginnings: the disproportion between seed and tree, pinch and loaf, is the characteristic of God's work in history.

Cross-Reference

  • Dan 4:12; Ezek 17:23A great tree where birds nest — the OT image for a kingdom that shelters all nations. The mustard seed's tree echoes these texts: the kingdom that begins small will become the shelter of the nations.
  • Dan 12:3The righteous will shine like the stars — v.43 ("the righteous will shine like the sun") echoes Daniel's resurrection promise. The harvest-judgment produces this eschatological glory.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
13:24-43

MacArthur: The wheat and tares parable explains the co-existence of kingdom citizens and kingdom opponents in the present age. The servants want immediate separation; the master restrains them. Premature separation would destroy wheat along with weeds. The eschatological separation is real and coming — but it belongs to God, not to the servants.

13:31-33

MacArthur: The mustard seed and the leaven both describe the kingdom's hidden, organic growth from small beginnings to large presence. The kingdom does not arrive through revolutionary imposition but through the patient, invisible process of leaven working through dough. The parables rebuke the expectation of immediate, visible, triumphant establishment.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:36-43

Calvin on the tares parable's interpretation: The wheat and tares growing together until the harvest describes the mixed church -- genuine believers and pretenders coexisting until the final judgment. This is not a charter for laxity but a warning against premature separation: the attempt to uproot the tares before the harvest would destroy the wheat. The church's purity is eschatological; its present task is patient witness.

13:51-52

Calvin on the scribe trained for the kingdom: The scribe brings out of his treasure new things and old things -- the properly trained teacher of the kingdom uses both the OT ('old') and the new covenant revelation ('new'). The kingdom's teacher is not one who abandons the old for the new or the new for the old but who reads both in their proper relationship.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:31-33

The NET note on the mustard seed discusses the apparent discrepancy: mustard is not literally the smallest of all seeds (orchid seeds are smaller) nor does it literally become a tree. The note identifies the hyperbolic register: Jesus is using the cultural idiom for small seed (mustard was the standard example) and large growth (a garden plant large enough for birds). The point is the kingdom's disproportionate growth from small beginnings.

13:47-50

The NET note on the dragnet parable discusses the universal gathering followed by separation at the judgment. The parable parallels the tares: the same mixture characterises the present age; the same separation characterises the end. The note identifies the implication for mission: the gospel net is cast broadly -- the judgment's sorting is not the church's task.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
13:31-33

Robertson notes the mustard seed and leaven parables both concern the kingdom's growth from small beginnings to large outcomes. The mustard seed's growth from the smallest seed to the largest garden plant describes the kingdom's external expansion; the leaven's permeation of the whole lump describes the kingdom's internal transformation. Together they describe a kingdom that grows both extensively and intensively.

13:44-46

Robertson identifies the hidden treasure and pearl parables as uniquely focused on the joy of discovery. The man who finds the treasure hides it and sells everything with joy (chara) -- the sacrifice is not reluctant. Robertson notes this describes the response to the kingdom's discovery: when the kingdom is genuinely found, all other things lose their comparative value not through grim renunciation but through joyful revaluation.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
13:31-33

Chrysostom on the mustard seed: From the smallest beginning -- twelve uneducated men -- Christ's kingdom has grown to fill the world. The birds who lodge in its branches are the Gentile nations that have found shelter in the kingdom's expansion. The obscure beginning is the guarantee of the glorious outcome.

13:44-46

Augustine on the hidden treasure and the pearl: These two parables describe the discovery of the kingdom from two directions -- the accidental finding (the treasure) and the deliberate seeking (the merchant seeking pearls). Both produce the same response: everything is sold with joy. The kingdom is not found without cost; but when it is found, the cost becomes irrelevant in light of what is gained.

Verses 44–58 — Treasure, Pearl, Net; New and Old; Rejected at Nazareth
444546474849505152535455565758

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The final parables (treasure, pearl, net) bring the kingdom discourse to its personal application: what would you sell everything for? The treasure and pearl parables are the most personally challenging in ch.13 — they ask not about the kingdom's growth or composition but about the hearer's response to it. The net parable returns to eschatological separation (echoing the weeds). The householder saying (v.52) is Matthew's own portrait of the scribe-become-disciple. The Nazareth rejection then provides the lived illustration: the crowd's familiarity prevents them from receiving the treasure they have.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 6:21Where your treasure is, there your heart will be also. The hidden-treasure parable enacts this: the man's joy-driven sale of everything is the natural consequence of having found where his treasure is.
  • Matt 11:6Blessed is anyone who does not stumble on account of me. Nazareth fails this test — skandalizō at the familiar Jesus. The beatitude that addressed John's doubt is now denied by Jesus's hometown.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
13:44-52

MacArthur: The treasure and the pearl are paired because they approach the same conclusion from opposite directions: one man stumbles on the treasure accidentally; the other seeks deliberately. In both cases, the finding produces immediate, complete, joyful surrender of everything else. The cost is not reluctant sacrifice but the rational response to supreme value discovered.

13:54-58

MacArthur: He did not do many miracles there because of their lack of faith. The restraint of miraculous power in the face of unbelief is not inability but covenant consistency: faith is the necessary posture for receiving what God gives. The people of Nazareth received less not because Jesus could give less but because their unbelief closed the channel of reception.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:53-58

Calvin on the Nazareth rejection: The people stumbled at the lowliness of his origin -- they could not reconcile carpenter's son with Son of God. This teaches that the knowledge of Christ's earthly origins is no obstacle to faith when faith is present, but when faith is absent, even legitimate knowledge becomes a stumbling block. The problem was not what they knew but their unwillingness to receive what the evidence pointed to.

13:54-58

Calvin on the rejection at Nazareth: the townspeople's stumbling at Jesus is the scandal of the incarnation precisely — they reason from familiar humanity to disqualified Messiahship. Calvin observes that familiarity can harden where it should soften: the people of Nazareth had the most exposure and the least faith.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:53-58

The NET note on 'he did not do many miracles there because of their unbelief' discusses whether unbelief limited Jesus' ability or only his willingness to do miracles. The note identifies the theological logic: miracles in Matthew function as signs that require receptive soil. Unbelief does not prevent miracles mechanically but removes the context in which they serve their function.

13:44-52

NET note on the kingdom parables: the cluster of seven parables in ch.13 is the largest parabolic discourse in Matthew. The final parable (the net, vv.47-50) completes the set by returning to the eschatological theme of the wheat and tares — the separation at the end of the age. Matthew's arrangement is deliberate: kingdom mixed in the present, sorted in the future.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
13:53-58

Robertson notes that Jesus' reception in Nazareth illustrates the parable principle just discussed: the same word produces different responses in different soils. The hometown crowd's familiarity becomes a barrier: 'Is this not the carpenter's son?' -- they know the origin and therefore cannot accept the significance. Robertson notes 'he did not do many miracles there because of their unbelief' -- unbelief does not make miracles impossible but removes the receptive context in which Jesus characteristically worked.

13:44-46

Robertson on the two parables of treasure and pearl: both teach the same point from opposite directions — one man stumbles on the treasure accidentally, the other seeks the pearl deliberately. Robertson notes that the joy in both cases precedes the cost: the selling of all is not sacrifice but the rational response to discovered supreme value.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
13:53-58

Chrysostom: A prophet is not without honour except in his hometown. The familiarity of Nazareth became the obstacle to faith -- they thought they knew him too well to receive him as Lord. This is the perennial danger of religious familiarity: knowing about Christ without encountering him. The hometown crowd had all the biographical facts and none of the faith.

13:44-46

Robertson on the twin parables of the hidden treasure and the pearl: both describe someone who finds the kingdom and then sells everything with joy. Robertson notes that the joy precedes the cost -- the man does not reluctantly sell; he sells because the value discovered makes everything else secondary.

13:47-50

The Catena records the patristic treatment of the dragnet parable as the church's mixed composition. Chrysostom: the net gathers fish of every kind -- the visible church contains both righteous and wicked until the separation at the end. The parable teaches patience with the church's imperfection and confidence in the final judgment's completeness.

13:44-46

Calvin on the hidden treasure and the pearl: in both parables the person sells everything with joy. Calvin notes that the selling is not reluctant sacrifice but the rational response of someone who has discovered what everything else is worth in comparison. The kingdom reorders all values by revealing the supreme value.

13:47-50

The NET note on the dragnet (sagene) parable discusses its distinction from the hidden treasure and pearl: those parables address individual discovery; this one addresses the community's mixed composition. The note traces the eschatological separation as the complement to the planting parables' growth theme.

Ancient Sources

Palestinian agriculture
Fields were commonly used to hide valuables in times of invasion. A landless labourer ploughing a rented field who found buried treasure had a legal claim if he first purchased the field.
The parable's legal realism would be immediately recognised.
Darnel
Lolium temulentum — a weed that closely mimics wheat until the grain develops. Eating darnel causes intoxication (the name may derive from French "ivre" — drunk). The agricultural precision of the weeds parable is exact.
The realism confirms that Jesus or Matthew had agricultural knowledge.

Reception History

The parable chapter and allegory
Matthew 13 generated enormous allegorical interpretation from Origen onward. The weeds parable's explanation (vv.37–43) encouraged readers to find allegorical correspondences in every detail of every parable — an approach later scholarship has questioned.
The householder saying (v.52)
Widely read as Matthew's self-portrait — the Jewish scribe who has become a disciple. The saying has been used to argue for Matthew's authorship and his scribal background.

Textual Notes

13:35I will open my mouth in parables, I will utter things hidden since the creation (Ps 78:2)
Matthew cites Ps 78:2 as the prophetic warrant for Jesus' parabolic teaching. Psalm 78:2 is by Asaph, not a prophet in the technical sense.
Matthew's broad use of prophetic category encompasses the Psalms as inspired Scripture. The note also identifies the theological significance: the kingdom's mysteries were hidden from the creation and are now being disclosed in the parables -- the parables are not mere illustrations but disclosure of what was previously concealed.

Scholarly Debates

Why Did Jesus Teach in Parables? Revelation or Concealment?
Revelation -- to make truth accessible
Common popular understanding
Parables make abstract truth concrete and memorable. They are pedagogical tools for illumination.
Both revelation and concealment -- depending on the hearer
Most careful commentators
Matthew 13:11-17 explicitly states that parables reveal to disciples and conceal from those outside. The Isaianic citation (Isa 6:9-10) makes the concealing function intentional.
The both/and reading is required by the text. Parables reveal to those with ears to hear and conceal from those who have hardened their hearts. The same parable accomplishes both simultaneously.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 13 is the parable discourse -- Jesus' sustained teaching about the kingdom's nature, growth, and value. The governing question: what is the kingdom? It grows from small beginnings, contains mixture until the harvest, is worth everything, and produces understanding in those who receive it.
