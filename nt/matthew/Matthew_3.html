Matthew 3

John the Baptist: Preparation and Proclamation; The Baptism of Jesus

Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, a tax collector called by Jesus at his customs post in Capernaum (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles and an eyewitness to the ministry of Jesus. He would have been literate and numerate by profession — uniquely equipped to compose a carefully structured written account. Unanimous early tradition attributes the Gospel to him: Papias (c.AD 125) records that "Matthew compiled the oracles in the Hebrew language, and everyone interpreted them as best he could." The Gospel as we have it is in polished literary Greek, suggesting either a Greek composition drawing on Aramaic sources, or a Greek original.

When written

c.AD 80–90, traditionally associated with Antioch of Syria, the first great Gentile church and a centre of Jewish-Christian interaction. The Gospel presupposes the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) in its apocalyptic passages (Matt 22:7; 24:15-22), suggesting composition after that date. It draws on Mark's Gospel (c.AD 65-70) and on a sayings source shared with Luke (conventionally called Q). Matthew shapes his sources with a more formal, structured literary hand than any of the other evangelists.

What prompted it

To present Jesus of Nazareth as the fulfilment of Israel's entire scriptural heritage — the promised Messiah, the new Moses, the true Israel, and the Son of God. Matthew writes primarily for a Jewish-Christian community navigating its relationship to the synagogue after AD 70. His ten fulfilment-formula citations ("all this took place to fulfil what was spoken through the prophet") are the theological spine of the work: Israel's Scripture is not superseded but completed in Jesus. The Great Commission (28:18-20) shows the universal horizon of a Gospel that begins with Abraham and ends with all nations.

Verses 1–12 — John the Baptist: Preparation and Proclamation
1In those days John the Baptist came, preaching in the wilderness of Judea2and saying, "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven has come near."3This is he who was spoken of through the prophet Isaiah: "A voice of one calling in the wilderness, 'Prepare the way for the Lord, make straight paths for him.'"4John's clothes were made of camel's hair, and he had a leather belt around his waist. His food was locusts and wild honey.5People went out to him from Jerusalem and all Judea and the whole region of the Jordan.6Confessing their sins, they were baptized by him in the Jordan River.7But when he saw many of the Pharisees and Sadducees coming to where he was baptizing, he said to them: "You brood of vipers! Who warned you to flee from the coming wrath?8Produce fruit in keeping with repentance.9And do not think you can say to yourselves, 'We have Abraham as our father.' I tell you that out of these stones God can raise up children for Abraham.10The ax is already at the root of the trees, and every tree that does not produce good fruit will be cut down and thrown into the fire.11"I baptize you with water for repentance. But after me comes one who is more powerful than I, whose sandals I am not worthy to carry. He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and fire.12His winnowing fork is in his hand, and he will clear his threshing floor, gathering his wheat into the barn and burning up the chaff with unquenchable fire."

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

John the Baptist is simultaneously the last OT prophet and the first NT herald. His clothing (camel's hair, leather belt) deliberately echoes Elijah (2 Kgs 1:8). His message is pure OT prophetic tradition — repentance, fruit, judgment. But his message points forward to one who is qualitatively greater. The contrast between water baptism (v.11) and Spirit-and-fire baptism announces that something unprecedented is about to occur.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 40:3A voice calling in the wilderness — the opening of the great consolation section of Isaiah. John's ministry inaugurates the new Exodus: prepare the way, the LORD is coming.
  • Mal 3:1; 4:5–6The messenger who prepares the way; the return of Elijah before the great day of the LORD. Matthew identifies John as this Elijah-figure explicitly in 11:14 and 17:12.
  • 2 Kgs 1:8Elijah wore a garment of hair with a leather belt. John's clothing is a deliberate Elijah allusion, legible to any Jewish reader.

Places

Desert of Judea
Wilderness area between Jerusalem and the Dead Sea
John preaches in the Desert of Judea — the traditional space of prophetic calling. Elijah lived in the wilderness; Isaiah's voice cries in the wilderness (40:3). The desert geography signals that John is in the prophetic tradition of those who speak before major covenant acts.
Jordan River region
Jordan Valley; John's baptismal site
People from Jerusalem, all Judea, and the whole Jordan region come out to John. The Jordan is Israel's boundary river — crossed by Joshua, associated with Elijah's departure. John at the Jordan is positioned at the covenant's geographical entry point into the land.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
3:1-12

MacArthur: John's message — Repent, for the kingdom of heaven has come near — is not novel but the reapplication of the covenant demand that has always preceded covenant renewal. The fruit language (v.8) grounds the call to repentance in visible transformation: genuine repentance produces observable change in behavior.

3:7-10

MacArthur: John's challenge to the Pharisees and Sadducees — do not think you can say to yourselves, we have Abraham as our father — strikes at the deepest assumption of first-century Judaism. Genealogical membership in the covenant people does not exempt anyone from the covenant's ethical demands. The ax is already at the root.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:3

Calvin: John fulfilled the role of preparatory herald with precision that made the Isaiah 40:3 connection unmistakable. The wilderness setting is theological as well as geographical — John stands outside the established religious structures as a prophetic voice calling Israel to the covenant faithfulness the institutions had abandoned.

3:11-12

Calvin on the two baptisms: John's water baptism is the sign; Christ's Spirit baptism is the substance. The outward washing points to the inward cleansing that only the Spirit effects. This is the consistent relationship between sacrament and Spirit: the sign accompanies and points to but does not produce the reality.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
3:2

Robertson notes that 'has come near' (engiken) is a perfect tense — the kingdom has arrived and its presence continues. This is not a future promise but a present announcement. John's repentance demand is the appropriate response to a present reality.

3:11-12

Robertson notes the baptism with Holy Spirit and fire is a single baptism with two effects. The winnowing fan (ptyon) is the familiar agricultural tool for eschatological separation — the coming one does what John announces but cannot accomplish.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
3:2

Chrysostom: John began with the same preaching as Christ himself — repent, for the kingdom is at hand. He was the voice preparing for the Word, the lamp heralding the Sun. He preached repentance with greater urgency because the kingdom's arrival was imminent.

3:11-12

Augustine: He who baptizes with Holy Spirit and fire — the Spirit as purifying flame, the fire as consuming judgment. The same fire that refines gold destroys chaff. The dual meaning names the dual destiny that the kingdom's arrival creates.

Verses 13–17 — The Baptism: The Trinity at the Jordan
13Then Jesus came from Galilee to the Jordan to be baptized by John.14But John tried to deter him, saying, "I need to be baptized by you, and do you come to me?"15Jesus replied, "Let it be so now; it is proper for us to do this to fulfil all righteousness." Then John consented.16As soon as Jesus was baptized, he went up out of the water. At that moment heaven was opened, and he saw the Spirit of God descending like a dove and alighting on him.17And a voice from heaven said, "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased."

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The baptism of Jesus is the most theologically concentrated event of Matthew 3. John's protest (v.14) acknowledges the anomaly: the one without sin submitting to a repentance-baptism. Jesus's answer — "to fulfil all righteousness" — points to his representative solidarity with humanity. The divine response is triadic: the Spirit descends, the Father speaks, the Son is designated. This is the first fully Trinitarian moment in Matthew, anticipating the baptism formula of 28:19.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 2:7You are my Son, today I have become your Father — the royal coronation psalm. The Father's words at the baptism are a coronation declaration: Jesus is installed as the Davidic King.
  • Isa 42:1Here is my servant whom I uphold, my chosen one in whom I delight — the first Servant Song. The Father's words also echo this: "with him I am well pleased." Jesus is simultaneously Davidic King and Isaianic Servant.
  • Gen 22:2Take your only (agapētos) son... Isaac, whom you love. The language of the baptismal declaration echoes the Aqedah — the offering of the beloved son. The cross is already in view.

Places

Jordan River
Traditional crossing point; possibly near Jericho
Jesus comes from Galilee to the Jordan to be baptized by John. The journey from Galilee south to the Jordan is roughly 100km. Jesus submits to a Judean prophet's rite, identifying with the people he has come to save — the geography of covenant solidarity.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
3:13-17

MacArthur: Jesus's baptism is not for repentance — he has no sin to repent of — but for identification with Israel and for public commissioning. Fulfill all righteousness means to complete everything the covenant requires. The Trinitarian theophany (Father speaking, Spirit descending, Son standing) is the Trinity's simultaneous self-disclosure.

3:17

MacArthur: This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased. The words combine Psalm 2:7 (the royal Messiah) and Isaiah 42:1 (the Suffering Servant). From the beginning of his public ministry, Jesus is identified as both the royal Messiah who reigns and the servant who suffers. The two roles Matthew will spend his Gospel holding together are announced at the baptism.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:13-15

Calvin: Christ was not baptized for his own benefit but for ours. He submitted to the form appointed for sinners to show that he was taking our place. The sinless one enters the sinner's waters — this is the beginning of his substitutionary identification with those he came to save.

3:16-17

Calvin on the Trinitarian theophany: Here is a noble testimony to the undivided Trinity — the Son in the water, the Spirit as a dove, the Father in the voice. God solemnly presents his Son as entering on his prophetic office. Three persons distinct but not divided.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:13-15

The NET note on 'to fulfil all righteousness' surveys the interpretive options: fulfilling the law's requirements, identifying with sinful humanity, inaugurating the new covenant. In Matthew, 'righteousness' typically refers to covenant-required conduct, not legal imputation.

3:16-17

The NET note on 'this is my beloved Son' identifies the allusion to Ps 2:7 and Isa 42:1 and discusses the text-critical issue: 'well pleased' (eudokēsa, aorist) may point to a specific moment of divine commitment — the Father's eternal pleasure in the Son expressed at this consecration.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
3:13-15

Robertson identifies 'to fulfil all righteousness' (plērosai pasan dikaiosynēn) as Jesus' self-identification with the covenant community he came to redeem. He enters the waters of repentance not because he needs repentance but because he is taking his place among those who do.

3:16-17

Robertson notes the schizō (torn open/split) vocabulary in Mark's parallel — heaven is not merely opened but torn apart. The heavenly affirmation echoes both Ps 2:7 (royal investiture) and Isa 42:1 (servant commissioning). The baptism is simultaneously royal inauguration and servant commissioning.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
3:13-15

Chrysostom: Why was he baptized having no need of baptism? To fulfil the law, to sanctify the water, to give us an example, and to honour John. He who came to take away the sins of the world needed no washing — he came to sanctify the waters for us and to leave in them a power of sanctification.

3:16-17

Jerome: The Spirit descended not to enlighten him who needed no enlightenment but to show the world that the Spirit rests upon the meek and humble. The dove is the fitting symbol of the Spirit of peace.

Ancient Sources

Jewish baptism
Proselyte baptism (immersion for converts to Judaism) pre-dates John. John's baptism is distinctive: for Jews, as a sign of repentance, in anticipation of the coming one.
John's practice was unprecedented enough to give him his name: "the Baptizer."
Josephus on John
Josephus describes John as a "good man" who "commanded the Jews to exercise virtue, both as to righteousness towards one another, and piety towards God" (Antiquities 18.5.2).
Josephus's account confirms John's historical existence and public prominence.

Reception History

Christian baptism
The baptism of Jesus as the pattern for Christian baptism: the rite of entry into the new covenant community. The Trinitarian formula of 28:19 derives from the Trinitarian event of 3:16–17.
The dove in iconography
The dove descending on Jesus has been the primary symbol of the Holy Spirit in Christian art from the early church through the Renaissance.

Textual Notes

3:11'Will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and fire'
Matthew uses 'and fire' (kai pyri); some argue this is a single baptism with dual effect, others two separate baptisms.
The single-baptism reading fits the grammar and the prophetic background (Ezek 36:25-27; Mal 3:2-3). The fire purifies the repentant and judges the impenitent.

Scholarly Debates

Was John's Baptism Christian Baptism?
John's baptism was preparatory; Christian baptism is distinct
Most Protestant commentators
John baptized in water for repentance; Christian baptism is in the name of the Trinity and linked to the Spirit's work (Acts 2:38; 19:1-6).
Continuity of covenant sign
Some Reformed scholars
Both point to cleansing and covenant membership; the difference is degree of fulfilment, not essential nature.
Acts 19:1-6 establishes the distinction most clearly. John's baptism was preparatory; Christian baptism is fulfilment baptism administered in the name of the triune God.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 3 establishes three foundational realities: the kingdom has arrived and demands repentance; the sinless Son identifies with sinners from his ministry's first moment; and the triune God publicly consecrates the Son for his redemptive work.
