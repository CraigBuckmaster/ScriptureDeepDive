Matthew 17

The Transfiguration; Elijah Has Come; Failed Exorcism; Second Passion Prediction; Temple Tax

Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, a tax collector called by Jesus at his customs post in Capernaum (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles and an eyewitness to the ministry of Jesus. He would have been literate and numerate by profession — uniquely equipped to compose a carefully structured written account. Unanimous early tradition attributes the Gospel to him: Papias (c.AD 125) records that "Matthew compiled the oracles in the Hebrew language, and everyone interpreted them as best he could." The Gospel as we have it is in polished literary Greek, suggesting either a Greek composition drawing on Aramaic sources, or a Greek original.

When written

c.AD 80–90, traditionally associated with Antioch of Syria, the first great Gentile church and a centre of Jewish-Christian interaction. The Gospel presupposes the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) in its apocalyptic passages (Matt 22:7; 24:15-22), suggesting composition after that date. It draws on Mark's Gospel (c.AD 65-70) and on a sayings source shared with Luke (conventionally called Q). Matthew shapes his sources with a more formal, structured literary hand than any of the other evangelists.

What prompted it

To present Jesus of Nazareth as the fulfilment of Israel's entire scriptural heritage — the promised Messiah, the new Moses, the true Israel, and the Son of God. Matthew writes primarily for a Jewish-Christian community navigating its relationship to the synagogue after AD 70. His ten fulfilment-formula citations ("all this took place to fulfil what was spoken through the prophet") are the theological spine of the work: Israel's Scripture is not superseded but completed in Jesus. The Great Commission (28:18-20) shows the universal horizon of a Gospel that begins with Abraham and ends with all nations.

Verses 1–13 — The Transfiguration; Elijah Has Already Come
12345678910111213

Greek Word Study

Context

The transfiguration (vv.1–9) is the visual confirmation of Peter's confession (16:16) and the counterweight to the passion announcement (16:21): the one who must suffer is the one whose face shines like the sun. Moses and Elijah represent Torah and the Prophets — the whole of the OT — in conversation with Jesus, whose presence supersedes them (v.8: "they saw no one except Jesus"). The cloud and voice repeat the baptism's theophany with the crucial addition: "Listen to him." The question about Elijah (vv.10–13) then confirms that the Elijah of Malachi 4 was John the Baptist — and that John's fate was the pattern for the Son of Man's.

Six days after Caesarea Philippi, Jesus takes Peter, James, and John up a high mountain. The traditional identification is Mount Tabor (Jezreel Valley) or Mount Hermon (near Caesarea Philippi, which is more geographically consistent with "six days later"). Cross-Reference

  • Exod 24:15–16; 34:29–30The cloud of divine presence on Sinai; Moses's face shining. The transfiguration echoes both: the glory-cloud of God's presence; the shining face of the one who stands in God's presence. Jesus is the new Moses — and more.
  • Mal 4:5–6I will send you the prophet Elijah before the great and dreadful day of the LORD. Jesus identifies John as this Elijah — and immediately applies the pattern to himself: they did to him what they wished, and so with the Son of Man.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
17:2

His face shone like the sun. The superlative brightness in the NT: brighter than Sinai, brighter than any comparison the disciples had seen. This is the glory of the pre-existent Son becoming visible — the Shekinah-glory that the temple was meant to house, now resident in a person. The disciples fall on their faces (v.6) in the appropriate theophanic response.

17:5

Listen to him. The three-word command added to the baptismal declaration. In the immediate context of ch.16's passion announcement and Peter's resistance, "listen to him" means: hear what he said about the cross and believe it, even though it violates your expectations. The voice from the cloud is answering Peter's "this shall never happen to you" with divine authority.

17:12

Elijah has already come, and they did not recognise him. The pattern of rejection is now traceable: John (Elijah) → killed; the Son of Man → to suffer in the same way. The transfiguration's glory and the passion's suffering are not contradictions but the two dimensions of the same identity.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:1-5

Calvin on the Transfiguration: 'Christ was transfigured not to display his glory permanently during the earthly ministry but to give his disciples a momentary glimpse so that they might endure the scandal of the cross. The glory was real but brief -- a down-payment, not the final possession. Faith must hold the glimpsed glory through the long darkness that follows.'

17:9-13

Calvin on John as Elijah: 'John was not literally Elijah, but he came in the spirit and power of Elijah (Luke 1:17). This fulfils the prophecy without requiring a literal reincarnation. The fulfilment is real and substantial; the literalism that demands the exact person misunderstands the nature of prophetic typology.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:2-5

The NET note on the cloud (nephele) in v.5 traces the Shekinah cloud of divine presence through the OT: the cloud at Sinai (Exod 24:15-18), the cloud in the wilderness tabernacle (Exod 40:34-38), the cloud filling Solomon's temple (1 Kgs 8:10-11). The cloud at the Transfiguration places Jesus within the canonical sequence of divine-presence manifestations.

17:10-13

The NET note on the Elijah-who-must-come-first (Mal 4:5) discusses the two-Elijah pattern in Jewish expectation: a literal return of Elijah versus an Elijah-like figure. Jesus resolves the ambiguity by identifying John the Baptist as the Elijah who has already come -- and been rejected. The note traces the implications for understanding the Messiah's rejection that follows.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
17:1-8

Robertson on metamorphothe (he was transfigured, v.2): the same word as Rom 12:2 and 2 Cor 3:18 for spiritual transformation. The physical shining of Jesus's face and clothes is an uncloaking of the divine glory that the Incarnation veiled. Robertson notes that Moses and Elijah represent the Law and the Prophets bearing witness to Jesus -- the very authorities the Pharisees claim to follow.

17:9-13

Robertson traces the Elijah identification (v.13: 'then the disciples understood that he was speaking to them about John the Baptist'). The disciples finally grasp what Jesus has been implying: Malachi's Elijah who must come first (Mal 4:5) is John the Baptist, whose ministry of preparation has been completed -- and rejected -- before Jesus's own ministry.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
17:1-8

The Catena records the patristic reading of the Transfiguration as the preview of the resurrection glory. Chrysostom: 'Before the cross, Christ shows the resurrection, so that the disciples may know that the suffering is not defeat but the path to the glory.' The three witnesses (Peter, James, John) are the same three who will witness the agony in Gethsemane -- glory and suffering have the same witnesses.

17:9-13

The Catena notes Origen's reading of the 'until the Son of Man is raised from the dead' instruction (v.9): the Transfiguration vision is sealed until the resurrection interprets it. The resurrection is the hermeneutical key to the Transfiguration. The disciples' confusion (v.10: but why do the teachers of the law say Elijah must come first?) shows they still cannot connect the suffering to the glory.

Verses 14–23 — The Epileptic Boy; Second Passion Prediction
141516171819202223

Greek Word Study

Context

The descent from the transfiguration into the failed exorcism (vv.14–20) is a deliberate contrast. The disciples who were not on the mountain have failed; faith the size of a mustard seed would have been enough, but their faith was insufficient. The second passion prediction (vv.22–23) follows — the disciples grieve, but do not yet understand. The temple tax episode (vv.24–27) closes the chapter on a surprising note: Jesus the Son, exempt from the Father's house-tax, pays it anyway to avoid causing offense. Sovereignty and servanthood coexist.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 30:13–16The two-drachma temple tax — half a shekel per adult male Israelite, collected annually for the upkeep of the sanctuary. Jesus's exemption claim is based on the Son's relationship to the Father: he would not be taxed for his own Father's house.
  • Matt 13:31Mustard seed faith — the parable used positively (kingdom growth) and now diagnostically (faith sufficient for the impossible). The same image carries both the promise and the rebuke.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
17:17

You unbelieving and perverse generation. The rebuke is addressed to the crowd and the disciples — but then privately the disciples ask Jesus to diagnose them specifically (v.19). Jesus does: little faith. The sharpness of v.17 is the sharpness of the teacher who knows what is possible and sees what is being missed.

17:20

Mustard seed faith. The smallest possible measure of genuine faith is sufficient for the impossible. The disciples' failure was not that their faith was small but that it was insufficient — perhaps performative, perhaps dependent on method rather than trust. Even a tiny amount of genuine, living faith is enough. The mountain-moving promise is not hyperbole but the description of what genuine trust in the living God produces.

17:27

So that we may not cause offense. The temple tax episode shows Jesus balancing his identity (Son, therefore exempt) with his mission (servant, therefore pays). The coin from the fish is not a parlour trick but a sign: the Son for whom the whole creation is ordered provides for his community's obligations through creation's resources.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:17-21

Calvin on the disciples' failure: 'Their faith was genuine but its roots were not yet deep enough to produce the particular fruit of this particular demand. A faith that can move mountains is not a quantity of faith but a quality -- the faith that knows it relies entirely on God and acts accordingly. The disciples were relying partly on their commission and partly on their faith; neither alone is sufficient.'

17:24-27

Calvin on the temple tax: 'Christ pays not because he owes the tax but to avoid giving unnecessary offence. This teaches the principle of Christian liberty in indifferent matters: where a practice is not sinful and its refusal would cause stumbling, the Christian should comply -- not from obligation but from love. Principle is not surrendered; offence is avoided.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:20

The NET note on 'faith as small as a mustard seed' discusses the apparent contradiction with v.17's 'little faith' diagnosis. The note explains: the disciples had some faith (v.20a) but not the particular quality of faith -- the wholehearted, undivided dependence -- required for this exorcism. The mustard seed is about quality (pure, undivided), not quantity.

17:24-27

The NET note on the coin in the fish's mouth (stater, v.27) discusses the practicalities: a stater was worth four drachmas, exactly enough for two temple taxes (one for Jesus, one for Peter). The note observes that Jesus did not work miracles for his own convenience but here works one specifically to honour an obligation while teaching the deeper principle of kingdom sonship.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
17:14-21

Robertson on the disciples' failure to exorcise the epileptic boy: Jesus attributes it to their oligopistia (little faith, v.20 -- the abstract noun form of oligopistos). Robertson notes the contrast: the disciples who have already been given authority to cast out demons (10:1) fail here. The failure is not of the authority conferred but of the faith exercised.

17:24-27

Robertson on the temple tax (didrachmon, the two-drachma coin, v.24): the annual half-shekel temple tax of Exod 30:13, collected from every adult Jewish male. Robertson traces Jesus's argument: sons of the king don't pay the king's taxes -- but to avoid scandal, he provides the tax anyway. The miracle of the coin in the fish's mouth is understated; Robertson notes Jesus simply says 'give it to them for me and you.'

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
17:14-21

The Catena records Chrysostom's treatment of the disciples' failure: they had grown overconfident after the Transfiguration, relying on their authority rather than their dependence on God. Chrysostom: 'They had begun to trust in themselves; Christ corrects them by allowing the failure. The lesson is that miracle-working requires not merely the authority conferred but the faith that remains consciously dependent on the one who conferred it.'

17:22-23

The Catena notes that the second passion prediction (v.22-23) produces grief in the disciples -- they understand more than before, even if not fully. Chrysostom: 'They were going to be exceedingly grieved -- this is the mark of their love for him. They cannot yet understand resurrection, but they understand death. The grief is the fruit of attachment.'

Ancient Sources

Mount Hermon
The most probable transfiguration site — a 2,800m peak north of Caesarea Philippi. The six days connect to Exod 24:16 (the cloud covered Sinai for six days before God spoke on the seventh).
The Sinai typology is reinforced by the timing.
The temple tax
Two drachmas = half a shekel. Collected by Jewish communities in the diaspora as well as in Palestine. Josephus records disputes about its collection. The Qumran community paid it once in a lifetime rather than annually.
The tax was a significant communal institution whose obligation Jesus acknowledges while claiming exemption.

Reception History

The Transfiguration in Eastern Orthodoxy
The Transfiguration (August 6) is one of the twelve Great Feasts of the Eastern Orthodox calendar — the visible disclosure of Christ's divine nature, the goal of theosis (participation in God's nature).
Mustard seed faith in preaching
17:20 has been the text for countless sermons on the sufficiency of small, genuine faith. The danger of over-literalism is real — the point is not quantity but quality and genuineness of trust.

Textual Notes

17:21This kind only comes out by prayer (and fasting)
Many manuscripts omit v.21. The shorter text (omitting v.21) is supported by the best manuscripts (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus). The verse appears to have been added from Mark 9:29.
Even if v.21 is secondary, the principle it articulates is consistent with Jesus's teaching on prayer throughout Matthew.

Scholarly Debates

What Caused the Disciples' Failure? (17:19-21)
Insufficient faith
Calvin, most evangelical interpreters
The disciples had faith but not the quality of undivided, dependent faith that this exorcism required. The mustard seed saying is about the purity of faith, not its quantity.
Lack of prayer and fasting (v.21)
Interpreters following the longer text
The failure was a failure of spiritual preparation -- prayer and fasting are the disciplines that develop the faith capable of this exorcism.
The two answers converge: the undivided, dependent faith (mustard seed) is precisely the faith that prayer and fasting cultivate. The failure is both a faith failure and a prayer failure; the two are the same thing viewed from different angles.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 17 moves from the Transfiguration's preview of glory to the disciples' failure at the foot of the mountain. The chapter establishes the pattern: glimpsed glory, returned to ordinary reality, and the ongoing demands of dependent faith. The disciples cannot yet hold what they have seen.
