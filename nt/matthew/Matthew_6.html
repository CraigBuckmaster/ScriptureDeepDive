Matthew 6

The Lord's Prayer; Fasting and Rewards; Anxiety and Trust; Kingdom Priorities

Verses 1–18 — Almsgiving, Prayer, and Fasting: The Sermon Continues
123456789101112131415161718

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The three practices of Jewish piety — almsgiving, prayer, fasting — are structured by a single repeated diagnosis (don't perform for human approval) and a single repeated promise (your Father who sees in secret will reward you). The Lord's Prayer (vv.9–13) is set within the teaching on prayer as the positive model: brief, God-centred, community-shaped ("our Father," "our daily bread," "our debts"). It is not a formula to be recited but a pattern to be followed.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezek 36:23Let me show the holiness of my great name — "hallowed be your name" in the Lord's Prayer echoes Ezekiel's new covenant promise that God will hallow his own name through Israel's restoration.
  • Isa 58:3–7Why have we fasted and you have not seen it? — God's critique of fasting for show. Matthew 6:16–18 echoes Isaiah's critique of performative religiosity.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:5-8

Calvin: Christ does not abolish public prayer but condemns the love of display that uses prayer as a vehicle for self-promotion. The person who prays to be seen has received their full reward -- human praise. The one who prays in secret receives what human praise can never provide: the Father's own attention and response.

6:9-13

Calvin on 'your kingdom come': This petition encompasses the destruction of Satan's kingdom and the establishment of God's reign in human hearts. It is not a prayer for geographical or political power but for the progressive realisation of covenant rule wherever human beings are brought under God's governance by the Spirit.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:1-4

The NET note on 'they have received their reward in full' explains apechein as commercial receipt language -- the transaction is complete, no further payment is due. The performer who gives for praise has been paid in full by the praise received. No further divine reward remains.

6:9-13

The NET note on epiousion (daily) surveys three interpretations: bread for today, bread for tomorrow, or bread that is sufficient/necessary. The petition is most naturally a prayer for present-moment provision, not eschatological bread -- the manna-provision theology of the wilderness.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
6:1-4

Robertson notes that hypokritas originally meant an actor playing a role. The alms-trumpeting in synagogues and streets is performance religion: the audience is human, the reward is human praise. All three practices (alms, prayer, fasting) come with a divine reward for the genuine practitioner and a human reward for the performer.

6:9-13

Robertson provides detailed lexical notes on the Lord's Prayer: hallowed (hagiastheto, aorist passive imperative); your kingdom come (eltheto, aorist imperative); daily bread (epiousion, a rare word, possibly 'for today'); forgive us our debts (opheilemata, literally financial debts used metaphorically for moral obligations).

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
6:5-8

Chrysostom: Enter your inner room and shut your door -- this does not forbid public prayer but private posturing. The one who seeks human attention in prayer has received their reward. Prayer in the inner room is seen by the Father who sees in secret and rewards openly.

6:9-13

Augustine on the Lord's Prayer: Seven petitions -- three for eternal goods (hallowed be your name, your kingdom come, your will be done) and four for temporal goods (daily bread, forgiveness, deliverance from temptation, rescue from evil). The eternal goods are the foundation; the temporal goods are the scaffolding of our earthly journey.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
6:1-18

MacArthur: The three acts of righteousness — giving, prayer, fasting — are structured identically: do not do them to be seen by others, but do them in secret and your Father who sees in secret will reward you. The public performance of private devotion is the target, not the devotion itself. Audience determines authenticity.

6:9-13

MacArthur: The Lord's Prayer is a model, not a formula. Your kingdom come before give us this day our daily bread is the structural key: kingdom priority before personal need. The prayer disciplines the disciple to seek first the kingdom (v.33) rather than treating God as a means to personal ends.

Verses 19–34 — Treasures, the Eye, Two Masters, Do Not Worry
19202122232425262728293031323334

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The second section of ch.6 moves from the three piety practices to the three anxieties: treasure (vv.19–21), sight (vv.22–23), and service (v.24) provide the theological diagnosis; the birds and flowers (vv.26–30) provide the analogical argument; and vv.31–34 provide the positive alternative — seek first the kingdom. The logic is: worry is the symptom of a heart whose treasure is on earth (v.21) and whose eye is single toward money (v.24). Re-order the heart and the worry is resolved.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 10:1–7Solomon in all his splendour (v.29). The flowers of the field exceed even the peak of human royal magnificence — God's artistry in creation exceeds human achievement.
  • Ps 55:22Cast your burden on the LORD and he will sustain you — the OT basis for the anxiety teaching. Jesus is extending the Psalmist's invitation into a sustained argument from God's faithfulness in creation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:24

Calvin on serving two masters: Wealth always wants to be the supreme good. The person who tries to serve both will inevitably find that one governs when interests conflict. The exclusive claim of covenant allegiance requires that money remain a servant, never a master.

6:25-34

Calvin on anxiety: Christ does not forbid prudent care for the future -- he forbids the anxious concern that treats earthly provisions as the foundation of security rather than the Father's provision. The distinguishing mark: does planning proceed from trust in the Father, or from the belief that security depends entirely on your own provision?

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:24

The NET note on mammon traces the word to Aramaic mamon -- property, possessions, wealth. Mammon is treated as a quasi-divine power: a rival claimant to the loyalty that belongs to God alone. The two-masters structure identifies wealth as a rival deity, not merely a competing priority.

6:33

The NET note on 'seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness' discusses whether dikaiosyne here is covenant righteousness (the character required of kingdom citizens) or imputed righteousness. In Matthew's usage, dikaiosyne consistently refers to covenantal conduct.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
6:19-21

Robertson notes 'rust' (brosis, literally eating/consuming) includes corrosion and the damage done by insects and rodents to stored goods. The treasure in heaven is not subject to any form of entropy. The heart-treasure link (v.21) is the chapter's psychological insight: where you invest is where you live.

6:25-34

Robertson observes that 'do not be anxious' (me merimnate) is a present imperative with the negative -- stop the ongoing practice of anxiety. The birds and lilies are providential arguments from the lesser to the greater: if God cares for them, how much more for those whose value infinitely exceeds theirs.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
6:19-21

Chrysostom: Where your treasure is, there your heart will be also -- this is the law of the spiritual life. The person invested in earthly things has an earthbound heart. The person invested in heaven has a heart that rises with their treasure. The command to store heavenly treasure is simultaneously a command about where your heart will live.

6:33

Augustine: Seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness -- when the kingdom is first, all other things are received in their proper order. When anything else is first, even the other things are disordered. The right ordering of desire is the foundation of all Christian ethics.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
6:19-34

MacArthur: Do not store up treasures on earth... but store up treasures in heaven. The command is not anti-wealth but anti-mammon — the prohibition is on the anxiety-producing, heart-captivating attachment to material accumulation. Where your treasure is, there your heart will be also is a description of how the heart works, not a command.

6:33

MacArthur: Seek first his kingdom and his righteousness, and all these things will be given to you as well. The promise is not that the kingdom-seeker gets everything they want but that the One who governs the kingdom also governs the provision of what is needed. Priority reordering is the antidote to anxiety.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The disciples
Addressed as "you" throughout
The Sermon is addressed to the formed community — those who have left nets and followed. The demands presuppose the prior act of discipleship.

Ancient Sources

The Lord's Prayer in the early church
The Didache (c.AD 100) directs Christians to pray the Lord's Prayer three times daily — the earliest evidence of its liturgical use.
The Prayer was embedded in worship practice within a generation of the Sermon.
Mammon
An Aramaic loanword for wealth or property. Not inherently evil — the problem is making it a master. Paul's "the love of money is a root of all kinds of evil" (1 Tim 6:10) echoes the same concern.
The Aramaic word shows the Sermon's Jewish oral tradition roots.

Reception History

The Lord's Prayer across traditions
The most universally memorised Christian text. Used in Eastern and Western liturgy, in private devotion, and as the model for Christian prayer since the first century.
Seek first the kingdom
6:33 has been the foundational text for Christian social ethics and mission — the priority of God's kingdom over every other concern, and the promise that basic needs are within the Father's provision.

Textual Notes

6:12Forgive us our debts as we have forgiven our debtors
Matthew uses opheilemata (debts) where Luke uses hamartias (sins). The debt metaphor treats sin as a moral obligation unpaid.
The reception of forgiveness is structurally connected to its extension. Neither is a condition for salvation; both are characteristics of the same transformed character.

Scholarly Debates

The Lord's Prayer: Model or Formula?
A model for the structure and content of prayer
Most Protestants
Pray like this indicates a model, not a verbatim formula. The prayer teaches priorities, posture, and petitions appropriate to kingdom citizens.
A specific formula to be repeated
Catholic and Anglican tradition
Pray like this combined with Luke's parallel ('when you pray, say') supports regular verbatim use as a liturgical prayer.
The two readings are complementary rather than contradictory. The prayer is both a model (structuring all prayer) and a liturgical formula (repeated in worship as a summary of Christian prayer).

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 6 governs the interior life of kingdom citizens. The three practices must be directed toward the Father rather than performed for human approval. The kingdom-first orientation is the antidote to anxiety.
