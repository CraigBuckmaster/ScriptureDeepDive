Verses 1–12 — Sign of Jonah; Yeast of the Pharisees
123456789101112

Greek Word Study

Context

The sign demand (vv.1–4) from the combined Pharisees and Sadducees — an unusual alliance — repeats the 12:38 request and receives the same answer: the sign of Jonah. The yeast warning (vv.5–12) applies the metaphor from 13:33 negatively: as a small amount of yeast works through the whole dough, the teaching of the religious establishment corrupts everything it touches. The disciples' literalism (thinking about bread) reveals how little they have absorbed from the two wilderness feedings.

Places

Magadan / Dalmanutha
Western shore of Sea of Galilee
The Pharisees and Sadducees come to test Jesus at Magadan (Mark's Dalmanutha). This is the last Galilean controversy before the journey northward to Caesarea Philippi. The demand for a sign from heaven at this location is followed immediately by the boat crossing and the leaven warning.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 12:38–39The sign of Jonah — first occurrence. The second repetition here signals the finality of the refusal: no other sign will be given.
  • Matt 13:33Yeast that works through the whole batch — used positively of the kingdom. Here used negatively of corrupting teaching. The same mechanism, two applications: kingdom growth and doctrinal corruption both work invisibly and pervasively.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:6

Be on your guard against the yeast of the Pharisees and Sadducees. The disciples misunderstand and think about literal bread — the same misunderstanding Peter will show in v.22 (taking Jesus's passion prediction literally and wrongly). Matthew uses these literalism moments to show how much the disciples still have to learn before the resurrection re-educates them.

16:9–10

Do you not remember? The two feedings are not merely compassion stories — they are object lessons in the sufficiency of Jesus's provision. The disciples have witnessed the feeding of 9,000 people from 12 loaves and are worried about not having brought bread. The failure to connect the miracles to present trust is the diagnosis of "little faith."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:1-4

Calvin on the sign demand: they ask for a sign not because they are genuinely seeking but to use his refusal as evidence of inadequacy. Christ refuses to be manipulated by those who have already decided against him. The sign of Jonah will be given, but those who harden their hearts against it will not benefit from it.

16:11-12

Calvin on the yeast teaching: Christ does not mean their teaching is wholly corrupt but that it is mixed with errors that spread like yeast through the whole lump. A small error in the foundation corrupts the entire building. The Pharisees had made the human addition so large it had consumed the original divine commandment.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:4

The NET note on 'an evil and adulterous generation' traces the OT background: Israel's unfaithfulness to YHWH is consistently described as spiritual adultery (Jer 3:8; Ezek 16:15-58; Hos 1-3). The sign demand is itself evidence of the adulterous generation: those genuinely seeking God do not demand signs on their own terms.

16:11-12

The NET note on 'the yeast of the Pharisees and Sadducees' explains the disciples' failure to understand as a recurring Matthean theme: they understand more than outsiders but less than the full revelation requires. Comprehension is gradual and requires the Spirit's illumination.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
16:1-4

Robertson on 'the sign of Jonah': the Pharisees and Sadducees (unusual alliance) demand a semeion from heaven. Jesus's refusal and counter-reference to Jonah points to his death and resurrection as the only sign that will be given. The genitive semeion Iona (sign of Jonah) is the same phrase as 12:39-40 -- the repetition emphasises the single authenticating event that is coming.

16:5-12

Robertson on 'the yeast of the Pharisees and Sadducees': the disciples think Jesus is rebuking them for forgetting bread. Robertson traces the progression from literal (bread) to metaphorical (teaching/influence). The yeast metaphor for corrupting influence appears in 1 Cor 5:6-7 -- Robertson notes Paul may be drawing on this dominical saying.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
16:1-4

The Catena records Chrysostom's treatment of the sign demand: the Pharisees and Sadducees who oppose each other on every theological question are united in opposition to Jesus. Their coalition is the sign that Jesus represents a threat transcending sectarian divisions. The only sign offered is death and resurrection -- which they will refuse to see even when given it.

16:6-12

The Catena traces the patristic consensus that the yeast of the Pharisees is specifically their hypocritical teaching -- outwardly pious, inwardly corrupt. Chrysostom notes that yeast works invisibly and pervasively: the most dangerous false teaching appears orthodox on the surface. The disciples' misunderstanding of the bread reference is a failure of spiritual perception.

Verses 13–20 — Peter’s Confession: You Are the Christ
1314151617181920

Greek Word Study

Context

Matthew 16:13–28 is the structural pivot of the gospel — the second of Matthew's two great turning points (4:17 — "from that time on Jesus began to preach"; 16:21 — "from that time on Jesus began to explain that he must go to Jerusalem"). Peter's confession produces the first explicit disclosure of the Passion (v.21) and the immediate Satanic resistance (vv.22–23) which demonstrates how precisely the path to the cross must be walked. The section closes with the cross-carrying call repeated from 10:38.

Places

Caesarea Philippi
Northern Israel; foot of Mount Hermon
Peter's confession occurs at Caesarea Philippi — the most remote location in Matthew's Galilean ministry, 40km north of the Sea of Galilee at the foot of Mount Hermon. The city was built by Philip, son of Herod, beside the spring cave dedicated to Pan (a pagan site). Peter identifies Jesus as Messiah in a city named after Caesar, near a pagan shrine — the geography of bold confession.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 22:22The key of the house of David — the authority to open and shut given to Eliakim. Jesus gives the keys of the kingdom to Peter/the church, drawing on the same image of steward-authority.
  • Dan 2:34–35; 7:13–14The rock that crushed the kingdoms (Dan 2) and the Son of Man receiving the kingdom (Dan 7). The church built on the rock-confession, given the kingdom's keys, is the fulfilment of both Danielic visions.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:16

You are the Messiah, the Son of the living God. The fullest confession in Matthew to this point. Peter adds "the living God" — the God who is active, present, and sovereign, contrasting with the dead idols. The confession is revelatory in origin (v.17: revealed by my Father) — a divine act producing a human declaration.

16:21

From that time on Jesus began to explain that he must go to Jerusalem and suffer. The gospel's second great pivot (the first is 4:17). The dei — "must" — is the constraint of divine necessity. Not fate but purpose: the cross is not a defeat to be avoided but the goal toward which everything has been moving. Peter's immediate resistance (v.22) shows how foreign this is to messianic expectation.

16:23

Get behind me, Satan! The sharpest rebuke in Matthew, directed at Peter who had just received the highest commendation. The distance between "blessed are you, Simon" (v.17) and "get behind me, Satan" (v.23) is five verses. The man who received divine revelation immediately becomes the instrument of satanic temptation — offering Jesus the cross-less route the desert temptation offered (4:8–10). The pattern repeats.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:13-20

Calvin on the keys of the kingdom: the power of the keys is not judicial authority over souls but the ministry of the word -- the gospel that opens the kingdom to believers and closes it to unbelievers. When the church proclaims the gospel faithfully, heaven ratifies the proclamation. The binding and loosing is the church's gospel preaching, not an individual priest's absolution.

16:21-23

Calvin on 'get behind me, Satan': Peter here becomes the instrument of the devil because he opposes the salvation of the human race. The best-intentioned human advice, when it contradicts God's plan, becomes the adversary's voice. Peter is not wicked but misguided -- and the misguided friend who discourages the cross is more dangerous than the open enemy.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:18-19

The NET note on petros/petra (Peter/rock) discusses the Aramaic background where a single word kepha underlies both -- the Greek distinction may be grammatical rather than semantic. The note surveys the major interpretive options and traces the binding-and-loosing language through rabbinic usage for authoritative legal decision-making.

16:27-28

The NET note on 'some standing here will not taste death before they see the Son of Man coming in his kingdom' discusses the major interpretive options: the Transfiguration (immediately follows in ch.17), Pentecost, the fall of Jerusalem (AD 70), or the final parousia. The Transfiguration connection is the most contextually supported.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
16:13-20

Robertson on Peter's confession: 'Thou art the Christ, the Son of the living God' (v.16). The full form is uniquely Matthean -- Mark and Luke have shorter versions. The 'Son of the living God' addition emphasises divine sonship as distinct from Messianic office. Jesus calling Peter 'blessed' and attributing the confession to divine revelation is the foundation of the rock-church-keys passage.

16:21-28

Robertson on 'get behind me, Satan' (v.23): the same words used at the temptation (4:10), now addressed to Peter. Peter, who just confessed Jesus as Messiah, is now -- in his misunderstanding of the messianic mission -- speaking with the adversary's voice. The cross is not a failure of the messianic plan; it is the messianic plan.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
16:13-20

The Catena presents the patristic debate on the 'rock' of v.18. Chrysostom and most Eastern Fathers understand the rock as Peter's faith/confession rather than Peter himself. Augustine's famous oscillation between the two readings is noted. The Catena's consensus: the church is built on the confession of Christ as Son of God.

16:24-28

The Catena traces the patristic reading of cross-bearing as the definition of discipleship. Chrysostom: 'If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross.' The cross is the specific shape of self-abandonment that following Jesus requires. The soul's worth exceeds the world's worth -- the man who loses his soul to gain the world has made the worst trade in human history.

Translation Comparison

Matt 16 -- Key Terms

VersionTranslation
NIV"You are the Messiah, the Son of the living God."
NoteThe fullest human confession in Matthew — Messiah (royal) and Son of the living God (divine). Revealed by the Father, not reasoned by Peter. The confession produces the first explicit passion prediction five verses later.

Ancient Sources

Caesarea Philippi
A Gentile city at the foot of Mount Hermon, dedicated to Pan and to Caesar. The confession of Jesus as Messiah and Son of God is made in the shadow of the empire's divine claims. The location is not accidental.
The political and religious backdrop intensifies the confession's significance.
The rock and Roman Catholicism
Matthew 16:18 is the primary NT text for the doctrine of papal primacy. Protestant exegesis has generally identified the rock as Peter's confession rather than Peter's person; Catholic exegesis as Peter's apostolic role; both are possible in context.
The debate has been sustained for sixteen centuries.

Reception History

The keys in church history
The keys of the kingdom (v.19) have been interpreted as: the authority to preach the gospel (Lutheran), the authority of church discipline (Calvinist), and the authority of Peter and his successors (Catholic). All three are exegetically possible; the immediate context (binding and loosing) favours a disciplinary/doctrinal reading.
16:21 as structural pivot
Virtually all Matthew scholars identify 16:21 ("from that time on") as the gospel's second structural hinge — the transition from the Galilean ministry to the passion journey. The formula exactly mirrors 4:17.

Literary Structure

Textual Notes

16:18Petros and Petra: rock wordplay
Greek: sy ei Petros, kai epi taute te petra. Aramaic original: kepha...kepha -- no gender distinction. The Greek distinction may be grammatical only.
The most natural reading: the church is built on the confession Peter has made, with Peter as its first representative voice.

Scholarly Debates

What Is the Rock in Matthew 16:18?
Peter himself
Roman Catholic tradition
Peter is the rock; his successors (popes) inherit his authority. The Aramaic background (kepha) supports identifying Peter directly.
Peter's confession of Christ
Most Protestant and Eastern Orthodox interpreters
The church is built on the truth Peter confessed. Peter is the representative confessor, not the exclusive foundation.
Christ himself
Some Reformed interpreters
1 Cor 3:11: no one can lay any foundation other than Jesus Christ.
All three readings have support. The Matthean context most supports the confession-as-rock reading: Jesus attributes the confession to divine revelation (v.17) and then builds his church on it.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 16 is the gospel's Christological summit: the confession of Jesus as Messiah and Son of God produces the church's charter, the passion prediction, and the discipleship cost. The chapter pivots from growing understanding to the first explicit passion teaching.
