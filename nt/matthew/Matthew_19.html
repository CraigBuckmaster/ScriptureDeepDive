Matthew 19

Divorce and Creational Marriage; Blessing the Children; The Rich Young Man; First and Last

Verses 1–12 — Teaching on Divorce
123456789101112

Greek Word Study

Context

The divorce question (vv.3–12) and the children episode (vv.13–15) are thematically linked through the theme of vulnerability and covenant. The divorce debate restores the creational standard from Gen 2 — marriage as the one-flesh union that only death (not convenience) ends. The disciples' reaction (better not to marry, v.10) leads to the teaching on celibacy as a kingdom gift. The children episode immediately follows — those the disciples try to exclude are exactly the ones the kingdom belongs to (v.14). The pattern from ch.18 continues: the kingdom inverts normal power dynamics.

Places

Region of Judea beyond the Jordan
Transjordan / Perea
"Jesus left Galilee and went into the region of Judea beyond the Jordan." This is the beginning of the Jerusalem journey — the sustained movement south that will culminate in the Triumphal Entry (ch.21). Matthew marks the geographical turning point here: the Galilean ministry is ending.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 1:27; 2:24Male and female; one flesh — Jesus goes behind Deuteronomy's divorce provision to Genesis's creation order. The creational intent is the standard; Mosaic accommodation is the exception.
  • Deut 24:1The certificate of divorce — Moses's provision. Jesus places it in the context of hard-heartedness: not God's design but human failure accommodated.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
19:4–6

Haven't you read that at the beginning the Creator made them male and female? Jesus grounds marriage ethics in creation theology, not Mosaic legislation. The marriage standard is "from the beginning" — before the fall, before the hardness of heart, before the need for divorce provisions. The restoration of all things that Jesus brings includes the restoration of creational marriage.

19:14

Let the little children come to me, and do not hinder them, for the kingdom of heaven belongs to such as these. The disciples' rebuke is the opposite of the ch.18 command to welcome the little ones. They are doing exactly what Jesus warned against — treating the insignificant as a distraction. His "do not hinder them" corrects the rebuke and defines the kingdom's membership: those who belong to the kingdom are those who receive it as children receive a gift.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
19:4-6

Calvin on creational marriage: 'Christ goes back to the original institution to show that God's intention from the beginning was the permanent union of one man and one woman. Divorce is a concession to human hardness of heart (v.8), not a positive provision. The original order is the norm; the Mosaic allowance is the accommodation to sin that sin made necessary.'

19:16-22

Calvin on the rich young man: 'His question -- what good thing must I do to inherit eternal life? -- reveals the fundamental error: he conceives of eternal life as the reward of accumulated merit. Jesus's answer redirects him to the commandments; when he claims to have kept them, Jesus exposes the one thing lacking. The rich man's face falls: he had not bargained for total surrender.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
19:9

The NET note on the exception clause (porneia) surveys the major interpretive positions: (1) any sexual immorality permits divorce and remarriage (evangelical consensus); (2) premarital unchastity discovered after marriage (betrothal interpretation); (3) incestuous marriages invalid from the start (Acts 15:20 application). The note also discusses the 'no remarriage' reading held by many early church fathers.

19:16-22

The NET note on 'what good thing must I do?' discusses the man's moral framework: he assumes eternal life is earned by moral achievement. The note traces Jesus's engagement -- confirming the commandments, then exposing the one remaining deficiency -- as the Socratic method applied to self-assessment. The man's claim to have kept all the commandments is challenged not by disputing it but by pushing one step further.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
19:1-12

Robertson on the Pharisees' divorce test (v.3): 'for any cause' (kata pasan aitian) -- the debate between the Shammai school (adultery only) and Hillel school (any displeasure) is the background. Jesus refuses both options and grounds marriage in the creational order (Gen 1:27; 2:24), before Moses, before any rabbinic school. The Pharisees are debating the terms of divorce; Jesus is questioning whether divorce is the right category.

19:10-12

Robertson on the disciples' startled response ('it is better not to marry') and Jesus's eunuch-for-the-kingdom teaching (v.12): Robertson notes three categories of eunuchs are distinguished -- born eunuchs, made eunuchs by others, and those who choose celibacy for the kingdom. The third category is a genuine calling, not a requirement. 'Let the one who can accept this accept it' (v.12b) makes the calling self-selecting.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
19:3-9

The Catena records the patristic debate on the exception clause (porneia, v.9). Most Eastern Fathers (Origen, Chrysostom) read porneia narrowly as sexual immorality and permit remarriage after such divorce. The Western tradition generally permits separation but not remarriage. The Catena notes the exception clause is unique to Matthew (both occurrences: 5:32 and 19:9) and absent from Mark and Luke, producing the long history of exegetical debate.

19:13-15

The Catena records Chrysostom's reading of the children being brought to Jesus: the disciples who rebuke those bringing children have forgotten the lesson of ch.18. Jesus's anger at the disciples (Mark 10:14 records this; Matthew omits it) and his welcome of the children is the enacted repetition of the kingdom-belongs-to-such-as-these teaching.

Verses 13–22 — Children Brought to Jesus; The Rich Young Man
13141516171819202122

Greek Word Study

Context

The rich young man episode (vv.16–22) is the most poignant refusal in Matthew — a man who has genuinely kept the commandments, asks the right question, and walks away sad because he has great wealth. The episode produces the camel-needle saying (v.24) and the disciples' astonishment ("who then can be saved?"). Jesus's answer is the theological hinge: "With man this is impossible, but with God all things are possible" (v.26). The first/last saying closes the chapter and introduces the parable of the workers that opens ch.20.

Places

Region of Judea beyond the Jordan
Transjordan / Perea
"Jesus left Galilee and went into the region of Judea beyond the Jordan." This is the beginning of the Jerusalem journey — the sustained movement south that will culminate in the Triumphal Entry (ch.21). Matthew marks the geographical turning point here: the Galilean ministry is ending.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 6:21,24Where your treasure is; you cannot serve both God and money. The rich young man is the concrete illustration of these Sermon principles: his treasure is his wealth, and it has become his master.
  • Isa 65:17I will create new heavens and a new earth — the palingenesia of v.28 echoes Isaiah's new creation. The disciples' thrones in the renewed creation are the restoration of the twelve-tribe structure of Israel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
19:21

If you want to be perfect, go, sell your possessions. The command is specific to this man — it targets the precise thing that has become his master. Jesus does not give this command to everyone, but he gives it to everyone for whom "one thing" stands between them and complete loyalty. The question is not whether you have wealth but whether wealth has you.

19:24

A camel through the eye of a needle. The most extreme impossibility image in ancient rhetoric. Various interpretations have softened it (a narrow gate called "the needle's eye"; a rope, not a camel). But the disciples' astonished response ("who then can be saved?") confirms that the original hearers understood it as absolute impossibility — which is exactly what v.26 then addresses: "with God all things are possible."

19:26

With man this is impossible, but with God all things are possible. The answer to the disciples' soteriological panic. Salvation is impossible for humans to achieve — the camel-and-needle is precisely true if salvation is an achievement. But salvation is God's act, not human attainment. The disciples' "we have left everything" (v.27) shows they still haven't quite understood this — Peter is still calculating the reward.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
19:23-26

Calvin on the camel and the needle: 'The wealthy person who enters the kingdom does so not because wealth is no obstacle but because God removes the obstacle that wealth creates. With God all things are possible -- including the salvation of those whom their possessions would otherwise prevent. The grace that saves the rich is the same grace that saves everyone; wealth simply makes its necessity more visible.'

19:27-30

Calvin on 'everyone who has left houses or brothers or sisters...will receive a hundred times as much' (v.29): 'The promise is not that material renunciation will produce material return in this life. The hundred times more is the fellowship of the kingdom, the spiritual family that replaces the biological one, and the inheritance of eternal life. The accounting of the kingdom is entirely different from the accounting of the world.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
19:23-24

The NET note on the camel and the needle's eye surveys the interpretive options: (1) literal absurdity as hyperbole (most commentators); (2) the 'needle gate' (no archaeological support); (3) kamelos (camel) as a scribal error for kamilos (rope) -- weakly supported. The note concludes that the literal hyperbolic reading best serves Jesus's point: the salvation of the wealthy is humanly impossible and requires divine intervention.

19:28-30

The NET note on 'the renewal of all things' (palingenesia, v.28) discusses this as one of the NT's most concentrated eschatological terms -- used elsewhere only in Titus 3:5. The note traces its Stoic background (world renewal through fire) and its Jewish application (the age to come, the resurrection). Jesus uses it to describe the eschatological state the disciples will share in, judging the twelve tribes of Israel.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
19:16-22

Robertson on the rich young man: he came to Jesus 'running' (Mark 10:17) and asked what good thing he must do. Robertson notes the three qualifiers Matthew preserves: he is young (v.20: 'all these I have kept since I was a boy'), he is rich (v.22: 'he had great wealth'), and he goes away grieving -- all three together characterise a person whose moral attainment and material security have closed the space where need should be.

19:23-30

Robertson on 'it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for someone who is rich to enter the kingdom' (v.24): Robertson rejects the medieval interpretation ('needle gate' in Jerusalem) as unsupported and unnecessary. The image is deliberately hyperbolic: an impossibility. Robertson on the disciples' astonishment: 'who then can be saved?' reveals that wealth was widely understood as a sign of divine blessing -- Jesus has overturned that assumption.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
19:16-22

The Catena records Origen's treatment of 'one thing you lack': Origen identifies this as the perfection of love (v.21: 'if you wish to be perfect'). The man has observed the commandments in their minimal legal sense but has not yet arrived at the love that gives everything. Chrysostom adds: 'The commandments he has kept tell us he is not a bad man; the one thing he lacks tells us he has not yet become a good one.'

19:23-30

The Catena notes that the disciples' question 'who then can be saved?' receives the answer that grounds all salvation: 'with God all things are possible' (v.26). The Fathers consistently read this as the doctrine of grace: what is impossible for human moral achievement is possible for divine grace. The impossible camel passage is the setup for the salvation-by-grace affirmation.

Ancient Sources

Divorce schools
The Pharisaic debate was between Shammai (divorce only for sexual immorality) and Hillel (divorce for any reason, including bad cooking). Jesus addresses the test by going behind both schools to Genesis — the creation standard supersedes both.
Jesus's answer bypasses the schools entirely.
Celibacy in Judaism
Voluntary celibacy was extremely rare and generally disapproved in Judaism — children were a covenantal obligation (Gen 1:28). Jesus's commendation of kingdom-celibacy (v.12) is without precedent in Jewish tradition.
The teaching would have been striking to a Jewish audience.

Reception History

19:14 and infant baptism
Let the children come to me — the primary NT text cited in debates about infant baptism. The context is blessing, not baptism; but the theology of kingdom-belonging for the young is foundational for paedo-baptist traditions.
19:24 in Christian ethics
The camel-needle image has been the primary text for Christian engagement with wealth across history — from the desert fathers who took it literally through Francis of Assisi to liberation theology. The question of whether it applies universally or situationally remains contested.

Textual Notes

19:9The exception clause: porneia
Greek: me epi porneia -- 'except for porneia (sexual immorality).' Mark (10:11-12) and Luke (16:18) lack the exception clause. Matthew includes it twice (5:32 and 19:9).
The exception clause's meaning has been debated for centuries. The most natural Greek reading: Jesus permits divorce (and implicitly remarriage) in cases of sexual immorality. The theological debate is whether this is a permission, a concession, or a recognition of pre-existing dissolution.

Scholarly Debates

What Does the Exception Clause (porneia) Permit?
Divorce and remarriage permitted for sexual immorality
Evangelical consensus, most Protestant interpreters
Porneia = sexual immorality; the innocent party may divorce and remarry. This is the standard evangelical position, consistent with the plain reading of the Greek.
Divorce permitted but not remarriage
Many early church fathers, some Catholic scholars
The exception permits divorce (separation) but remarriage is not explicitly authorized. The 'no adultery' clause in v.9b applies to the divorced person who remarries.
Betrothal unchastity only
Some scholars, explains Matt-only occurrence
Porneia refers to unchastity discovered during the betrothal period (Joseph's situation in 1:18-19). After consummation, there is no exception.
The evangelical consensus reading (divorce and remarriage permitted for sexual immorality) is most consistent with the Greek. However, the weight of the early church and the Catholic tradition against remarriage deserves serious engagement rather than dismissal.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 19 addresses three groups who come to Jesus with questions about entrance to the kingdom: the Pharisees (marriage/divorce), the parents bringing children (blessing), and the rich young man (moral requirements). In each case Jesus reframes the question: the kingdom's terms are not the world's terms.
