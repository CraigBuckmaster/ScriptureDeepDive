Matthew 14

John Beheaded; Feeding the Five Thousand; Walking on Water

Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, a tax collector called by Jesus at his customs post in Capernaum (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles and an eyewitness to the ministry of Jesus. He would have been literate and numerate by profession — uniquely equipped to compose a carefully structured written account. Unanimous early tradition attributes the Gospel to him: Papias (c.AD 125) records that "Matthew compiled the oracles in the Hebrew language, and everyone interpreted them as best he could." The Gospel as we have it is in polished literary Greek, suggesting either a Greek composition drawing on Aramaic sources, or a Greek original.

When written

c.AD 80–90, traditionally associated with Antioch of Syria, the first great Gentile church and a centre of Jewish-Christian interaction. The Gospel presupposes the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) in its apocalyptic passages (Matt 22:7; 24:15-22), suggesting composition after that date. It draws on Mark's Gospel (c.AD 65-70) and on a sayings source shared with Luke (conventionally called Q). Matthew shapes his sources with a more formal, structured literary hand than any of the other evangelists.

What prompted it

To present Jesus of Nazareth as the fulfilment of Israel's entire scriptural heritage — the promised Messiah, the new Moses, the true Israel, and the Son of God. Matthew writes primarily for a Jewish-Christian community navigating its relationship to the synagogue after AD 70. His ten fulfilment-formula citations ("all this took place to fulfil what was spoken through the prophet") are the theological spine of the work: Israel's Scripture is not superseded but completed in Jesus. The Great Commission (28:18-20) shows the universal horizon of a Gospel that begins with Abraham and ends with all nations.

Verses 1–12 — John the Baptist Beheaded
123456789101112

Greek Word Study

Context

The death of John is recounted as a flashback triggered by Herod's superstitious fear (v.2). Its placement here is structural: the forerunner who prepared the way has been killed; the one whose way he prepared is about to demonstrate who he is in the feeding of the five thousand. The pattern of prophet-rejection and murder is the context in which Matthew 14–28 operates. John's disciples report to Jesus — and Jesus withdraws, but then the crowds find him anyway.

Places

Herod's palace / court
Tiberias or Machaerus; Herod Antipas's territory
The beheading of John occurs at Herod Antipas's birthday banquet — either at his Galilean capital Tiberias or at the fortress Machaerus east of the Dead Sea (Josephus locates John's imprisonment at Machaerus). The geography of power executes the one who challenged it.

Cross-Reference

  • Mark 6:14–29Mark's fuller account includes the name Salome for the daughter of Herodias (from Josephus). Matthew's abbreviated version focuses the irony: a king bound by his own rash oath.
  • Matt 11:2–14John's death fulfils what Jesus implied — the forerunner suffers the Elijah's fate (17:12) before the Son of Man suffers his.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:3–4

John had been saying to him: "It is not lawful for you to have her." John's imprisonment is the direct consequence of speaking truth to power. The pattern is the same as the prophets before him (Matt 5:12) and the disciples after him (10:17–22). Faithfulness to God's word generates opposition from those with something to lose.

14:9

The king was distressed. Herod didn't want to kill John — he was afraid of him (Mark 6:20: "he protected John, knowing him to be a righteous and holy man"). But political calculation overcame his conscience. He is the portrait of the person who knows the right thing and does the wrong one for social reasons.

14:12

They went and told Jesus. The disciples' report to Jesus is both an act of discipleship (bringing the loss to the teacher) and the narrative hinge: it triggers Jesus's withdrawal, which is interrupted by the crowds, which produces the feeding of the five thousand. John's death is the shadow over the whole chapter.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:1-5

Calvin: Herod feared John yet imprisoned him -- conviction without conversion. He knew John was righteous but was not willing to pay the price of repentance. Conviction that stops short of action is worse than ignorance: it leaves the person responsible for the light they refused.

14:9-11

Calvin on Herod's oath: he swore rashly and kept his oath rashly. A rash oath made to commit wickedness should not be kept -- the sin of oath-breaking would have been far less than the sin of murder. But Herod, more afraid of losing face before his guests than of murdering the innocent, adds one sin to another.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:3-5

The NET note on Herodias as Herod Philip's wife discusses the complex Herodian family relationships documented by Josephus. Antipas met Herodias while visiting his half-brother in Rome, divorced his own wife (daughter of the Nabataean king Aretas IV), and married Herodias -- which John condemned as unlawful under Lev 18:16.

14:12-13

The NET note on Jesus withdrawing to a solitary place when he heard of John's death discusses the theological significance. It is not panic but prayerful retreat -- the same pattern seen at Gethsemane. Jesus processes the forerunner's death in solitude before continuing his ministry.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
14:3-12

Robertson notes Herod Antipas the tetrarch is distinguished from Herod the Great throughout Matthew. The imprisonment of John for rebuking the marriage is the political backstory explaining why Jesus withdraws when he hears the news (v.13). Robertson traces the political geography: Herod Antipas ruled Galilee and Perea, where John was baptising when arrested.

14:10-12

Robertson on apekephalisen (he beheaded him) -- aorist, a single decisive act. The disciples' burial of John's body and their reporting to Jesus connects the Baptist's death directly to Jesus's ministry: the forerunner's fate foreshadows the messianic fate.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
14:1-12

The patristic commentators consistently read John's beheading as a martyrdom typology. Chrysostom notes John is the last OT prophet who died for speaking truth to power. His willingness to rebuke the king at personal cost is the prophetic model Jesus himself will follow.

14:9-12

The Catena notes Origen's reading of Herodias's daughter as a figure of pleasure that destroys wisdom. The dance at a birthday banquet -- a context of celebration and indulgence -- produces the death of the greatest prophet. Unchecked pleasure produces moral catastrophe.

Verses 13–21 — Feeding the Five Thousand
131415161718192021

Greek Word Study

Context

The feeding of the five thousand (vv.13–21) is the only miracle recorded in all four gospels. Its eucharistic resonance is unmistakable: took, blessed, broke, gave — the same four verbs as the Last Supper (26:26). Jesus begins the gesture that will be completed in the upper room. The walking on water (vv.22–33) follows immediately — both miracles occurring after John's death, as if the forerunner's death triggers a new level of self-disclosure. The disciples' "Truly you are the Son of God" (v.33) is the first corporate confession of his divine identity in Matthew.

Places

Desolate place near Bethsaida
Northeast shore of Sea of Galilee
Jesus withdraws to a desolate place after hearing of John's death — but the crowd follows on foot from the towns. The withdrawal becomes the feeding: the desolate geography is transformed into the site of abundant provision, echoing the wilderness manna.
Sea of Galilee — on water
The lake itself
Jesus walks on the Sea of Galilee and Peter briefly does too. The sea geography is theologically loaded: the sea is the chaos symbol of the ancient world. Jesus walking on the sea is the Creator ordering chaos — the same act as the Exodus sea crossing and the creation separation of waters.
Gennesaret
Northwest shore of Sea of Galilee
The landing at Gennesaret triggers another wave of healings. Gennesaret is a fertile plain on the northwest shore — a prosperous agricultural area. Even landing in Gennesaret produces healing: Jesus's geographical presence generates covenant restoration.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 4:42–44Elisha feeding a hundred with twenty loaves, with food left over. Jesus feeds five thousand with five loaves — the eschatological multiplication exceeds the prophetic precedent.
  • Job 9:8; Ps 77:19Who alone treads on the waves of the sea; your path led through the sea. The language of God's sea-walking applied to Jesus. The disciples' terror is the appropriate theophanic response.
  • Matt 26:26Took bread, gave thanks, broke it, gave it to the disciples. The fourfold action of the feeding is the fourfold action of the Last Supper — the wilderness feeding anticipates the covenant meal.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:19

He gave thanks and broke the loaves and gave them to the disciples. The eucharistic structure (took/blessed/broke/gave) is the same in 14:19, 15:36, and 26:26–27. Matthew is drawing a deliberate line from the wilderness feeding through the second feeding to the Last Supper. Every time Jesus feeds a crowd, it anticipates the meal that will interpret his death.

14:27

It is I — egō eimi. The divine name from Exod 3:14 and the "I am" language of Isa 43:10–13 ("I am he"). The disciples who saw Jesus calm a storm (8:27: "what kind of man is this?") now have their answer confirmed: the Son of God who walks on water is the one whom the OT reserves this language for — the LORD himself.

14:33

Truly you are the Son of God. The boat-worship is Matthew's first corporate confession of Jesus's divine sonship. The voice from heaven said it at the baptism (3:17); the demons said it at Gadara (8:29); now the disciples say it. The growing clarity of confession mirrors the escalating self-disclosure of the miracles.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:13-21

Calvin on the five loaves: Christ does not wait until they are utterly famished before he relieves them. He provides before the distress is extreme. This teaches us that God anticipates our needs and does not delay relief until we have exhausted all human resources.

14:28-32

Calvin on Peter: his faith is sincere but not yet perfect. His walking on water is genuine; his sinking is real but not punished. The rebuke 'you of little faith' is pastoral correction, not condemnation: it identifies precisely what failed and what needed to grow. The Lord does not discard the doubter; he corrects him and brings him into the boat.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:13-21

The NET note on the feeding discusses kophinos (basket, v.20) -- a specific Jewish travelling basket, distinct from the spuridas used in the feeding of the four thousand (15:37). This detail is evidence of two distinct events rather than duplicate traditions.

14:28-31

The NET note on Peter walking on water discusses distazō (to doubt, v.31) -- appearing only twice in Matthew (here and 28:17). The note distinguishes distazō from apistia (unbelief): doubt is wavering faith, not absent faith. Peter's faith is present but insufficient to sustain under pressure without fixing attention on Christ.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
14:13-21

Robertson notes the feeding of the five thousand is the only miracle recorded in all four Gospels. His word-picture of anaklinai (to recline, v.19) emphasises the formal table posture: Jesus has the crowd recline as at a proper meal. The Eucharistic overtones -- took, blessed, broke, gave -- are intentional and Matthean.

14:28-31

Robertson on Peter's walking on water: epetrepsen (he commanded/permitted, v.29) implies a positive command, not just permission. Peter is not acting presumptuously. His failure is distraction: blepon ton anemon (seeing the wind, v.30) -- the participle shows attention shifted from Jesus to the storm.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
14:19-21

The Catena traces the patristic eucharistic reading of the multiplication. Chrysostom notes the five loaves and two fish become sufficient for five thousand men plus women and children, with twelve baskets remaining -- one for each apostle. The kingdom's provision always exceeds the need.

14:25-33

The Catena presents Peter's walking-and-sinking as the church's experience of faith and doubt. Chrysostom: Peter's bold faith brings him to the water; his shifting attention to circumstances brings him under it. The Lord's immediate response -- immediately Jesus reached out his hand -- is the pastoral promise: the hand extends before the cry is fully formed.

Ancient Sources

Josephus on John's death
Josephus (Ant. 18.5.2) records John's imprisonment and execution by Herod Antipas for political reasons — fear of John's influence. Matthew's account centres on the dance and oath; Josephus focuses on political threat. Both agree on the essential facts.
The two accounts are complementary, not contradictory.
The feeding and Eucharist
The four verbs — took, blessed/gave thanks, broke, gave — appear verbatim in 14:19, 15:36, and 26:26. Matthew's repetition is deliberate: the wilderness feedings are pre-enactments of the Last Supper.
The parallel structure is the strongest argument for eucharistic intent.

Reception History

The feeding in early Christianity
The feeding miracle was one of the earliest depicted in Christian catacomb art — loaves and fish as symbols of the Eucharist and resurrection. The connection between feeding and resurrection was made from the beginning.
Peter's sinking
Peter's walk-and-sink has been the pattern text for understanding discipleship as real but insufficient faith — Augustine, Luther, and contemporary preachers all use it to describe the disciple who acts on faith and needs rescue when fear takes over.

Literary Structure

The Feeding: Compassion Before Theology — 14:13-21Jesus saw the crowd, had compassion, healed their sick, then fed them. The sequence -- compassion, healing, provision -- is Matthew's portrait of kingdom priorities.
Walking on Water: Faith Under Pressure14:28-31Peter walked on the water -- and then looked at the wind. The distraction from Jesus to the storm is the narrative's moral anatomy of wavering faith. The hand extends before the cry is fully formed.
Peter's bold step matched by his sinking when attention shifts to the storm. The Lord's hand extends before the cry is fully formed.

Textual Notes

14:36The fringe of his garment
Greek kraspedou -- the tassel/fringe worn per Num 15:38. All who touched it were healed.
The healing power resides in Jesus, not the garment. The fringe is the point of physical contact for those who cannot reach Jesus directly.

Scholarly Debates

Was the Feeding of the Five Thousand Eucharistic?
Yes -- deliberate foreshadowing
Most patristic and many modern commentators
The fourfold action (took, blessed, broke, gave) exactly mirrors the institution of the Eucharist (26:26).
No -- provision only
Some Reformed interpreters
The eucharistic reading imports later theology. The miracle is about compassionate physical provision.
Both dimensions are present. Matthew's literary shaping makes the eucharistic echo unmistakable. The miracle of provision is real; the sacramental foreshadowing is equally real.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 14 moves from martyrdom to miracle. The forerunner's death occasions Jesus's withdrawal; the crowd follows him into solitude; compassion produces the feeding; the disciples' boat-crossing produces the walking-on-water. Compassion that exceeds what is needed is the chapter's governing pattern.
