Matthew 2

The Magi; Flight to Egypt; The Slaughter of the Innocents; Return to Nazareth

See on Timeline — Jesus Born See on Map — The Nativity
Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, a tax collector called by Jesus at his customs post in Capernaum (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles and an eyewitness to the ministry of Jesus. He would have been literate and numerate by profession — uniquely equipped to compose a carefully structured written account. Unanimous early tradition attributes the Gospel to him: Papias (c.AD 125) records that "Matthew compiled the oracles in the Hebrew language, and everyone interpreted them as best he could." The Gospel as we have it is in polished literary Greek, suggesting either a Greek composition drawing on Aramaic sources, or a Greek original.

When written

c.AD 80–90, traditionally associated with Antioch of Syria, the first great Gentile church and a centre of Jewish-Christian interaction. The Gospel presupposes the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) in its apocalyptic passages (Matt 22:7; 24:15-22), suggesting composition after that date. It draws on Mark's Gospel (c.AD 65-70) and on a sayings source shared with Luke (conventionally called Q). Matthew shapes his sources with a more formal, structured literary hand than any of the other evangelists.

What prompted it

To present Jesus of Nazareth as the fulfilment of Israel's entire scriptural heritage — the promised Messiah, the new Moses, the true Israel, and the Son of God. Matthew writes primarily for a Jewish-Christian community navigating its relationship to the synagogue after AD 70. His ten fulfilment-formula citations ("all this took place to fulfil what was spoken through the prophet") are the theological spine of the work: Israel's Scripture is not superseded but completed in Jesus. The Great Commission (28:18-20) shows the universal horizon of a Gospel that begins with Abraham and ends with all nations.

Verses 1–12 — The Magi: Star, Worship, and Herod's Plot
123456789101112

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

Matthew 2 is built around four OT fulfilment quotations — more than any other chapter. The Magi scene (vv.1–12) establishes the central irony of the chapter: Gentile outsiders from the east recognise and worship the King of the Jews, while the official Jewish establishment in Jerusalem is "disturbed" (v.3). Herod knows where the Messiah is to be born (the scholars can tell him precisely) but uses that knowledge to plot murder rather than worship.

Cross-Reference

  • Mic 5:2Bethlehem as the birthplace of the ruler who will shepherd Israel — quoted v.6. Matthew adds a phrase from 2 Sam 5:2 ("who will shepherd my people Israel"), combining both texts.
  • Num 24:17A star shall come out of Jacob — Balaam's oracle. The Magi's star may be the fulfilment of this prophecy, connecting Israel's Gentile opponents in Numbers to the Gentile worshippers in Matthew.
  • Isa 60:6Gold and frankincense from the nations — Isaiah's vision of Gentiles bringing tribute to Zion. Matthew's Magi enact this vision.

Places

Jerusalem
Capital of Judea; Herod's court
The Magi come to Jerusalem first — the obvious place to find a king. Herod's court is in Jerusalem; the chief priests and scribes are there. The city that should welcome the Messiah is instead the site of the plot against him.
Bethlehem
Judean hill country, c.8km south of Jerusalem
Bethlehem is identified from Micah 5:2 by the Jerusalem scribes. The Magi travel the short distance south. The Messiah is born not in the capital but in the village — a geographical humility that the whole narrative of Matthew will sustain.
Star's direction
From east; possibly Persia, Babylon, or Arabia
The Magi come from "the east" — the direction of the Babylonian diaspora, Persia, and Arabia. Their journey reverses the direction of the Exile: the east sends wise men westward to worship the one born King of the Jews.

Verses 13–18 — Flight to Egypt; The Massacre of the Innocents
131415161718

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The flight to Egypt (vv.13–15) and the massacre of the innocents (vv.16–18) are Matthew's two darkest scenes — and both serve typological purposes. The flight recapitulates the Exodus (Jesus = Israel / Moses). The massacre echoes Pharaoh's killing of Hebrew infants (Exod 1:16) and the Exile's grief (Jer 31:15). Matthew is reading the birth of Jesus as the convergence point of Israel's entire history.

Cross-Reference

  • Hos 11:1Out of Egypt I called my son — originally Israel at the Exodus. Jesus recapitulates Israel's story as the true, faithful Israel.
  • Jer 31:15Rachel weeping for her children — Jeremiah's lament over the Exile. Matthew applies it to the Bethlehem massacre, reading the massacre as a new Exile-level catastrophe.
  • Exod 1:15–22Pharaoh's massacre of Hebrew infant boys. Herod mirrors Pharaoh; Jesus mirrors Moses — the one who escapes to bring liberation.

Places

Egypt
Northeastern Africa; place of Exile and refuge
The flight to Egypt reverses Israel's Exodus and recapitulates it: Jesus goes down to Egypt as Israel went down to Egypt, then comes up again (Hos 11:1). Egypt is the place of danger (Herod), refuge (God's protection), and divine calling ("out of Egypt I called my son").
Bethlehem — massacre
Judean hill country
The massacre is localized to Bethlehem and its vicinity — the same geography as the birth. The place of birth becomes the place of slaughter. Rachel weeping (Jer 31:15) was associated with Ramah near Jerusalem — Matthew applies it to Bethlehem by typological extension.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
2:13-18

MacArthur: The flight to Egypt evokes Israel's sojourn in Egypt and the subsequent exodus. As YHWH called Israel out of Egypt (Hos 11:1), so the Father calls the Son. The massacre of the innocents parallels Pharaoh's slaughter of Hebrew boys. Jesus's infancy is Israel's history in miniature — the pattern of the nation compressed into one life.

MacArthur: The weeping at Ramah (Jer 31:15) comes from a passage that moves immediately to consolation and covenant renewal (Jer 31:16-34). Matthew cites the grief to invoke the whole chapter's movement: the new covenant consolation that follows. The massacre is not the end of the story but the darkness before the covenant dawn.

Calvin's Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:13-15

Calvin: God directs the flight with the same precision as the fulfilment. The same divine hand that moved Israel out of Egypt now moves the Son into Egypt. The Hosea citation is not allegory but typological completion: what Egypt signified in Israel's experience, it now fulfils in the Messiah's.

2:16-18

Calvin: The weeping of Rachel is not mere narrative pathos but theological testimony. Matthew's citation of Jeremiah shows that the consolation promised after exile (Jer 31) passes through grief. The innocents' death is the first instalment of the cost the Messiah's coming would impose.

Verses 19–23 — Return: Out of Egypt; Settled in Nazareth
1920212223

Places

Israel / Judea vs Galilee
Judea under Archelaus vs Galilee under Antipas
Joseph is warned not to return to Judea (under the brutal Archelaus) and is directed to Galilee instead. The political geography of Herod's divided kingdom shapes the Messiah's location: Galilee's Antipas is less dangerous than Archelaus's Judea.
Nazareth
Lower Galilee
"He will be called a Nazarene" — the settlement in Nazareth fulfils the prophetic theme of the Messiah's lowly origins. Nazareth was an obscure Galilean village with no prophetic significance of its own; its very obscurity embodies the incarnation's pattern.

Human plans (Herod's, the Magi's route) are redirected by God's direct communication. The infancy narrative presents a world where the seemingly secure plans of the powerful are overruled by divine message.

Nazareth was a nothing-town; the Messiah from Nazareth embodied the incarnation's consistent pattern of choosing the low.

Historical Context

Context

The return from Egypt (vv.19–23) completes the Exodus typology: as Israel came up out of Egypt into the promised land, so Jesus comes up from Egypt into Israel. But the destination is Galilee, not Judea — a detail Matthew uses to fulfil "he will be called a Nazarene." The move to Galilee also sets up 4:12–17, where Jesus's Galilean ministry fulfils Isaiah's "Galilee of the Gentiles."

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 4:19Go back to Egypt, for all those who were trying to take your life are dead — God's words to Moses almost verbatim. The angel's words to Joseph in v.20 echo this precisely, underlining the Moses typology.
  • Isa 11:1A shoot from the stump of Jesse / a Branch (nēṣer) from his roots. The wordplay on Nazareth/Nazarene may connect to this — the "Branch" prophecy pointing to the Davidic king from Jesse's line.

Ancient Sources

Herod the Great
Roman client-king of Judea, known for grandiose building projects and violent paranoia. He executed several of his own sons. The massacre of Bethlehem's infant boys fits his documented character exactly.
Josephus confirms Herod's murderous paranoia though doesn't record this specific event.
The Magi's star
Various proposals: conjunction of Jupiter and Saturn (7 BC), a nova, Halley's Comet (12 BC), or a supernatural phenomenon. Matthew does not identify it astronomically — its function in the narrative is to lead and to stop.
The star's movement (going ahead of them, stopping) is not astronomical behaviour.

Reception History

Epiphany
The Magi's visit has been celebrated as Epiphany (6 January) since at least the 4th century — the manifestation of Christ to the Gentiles.
The Innocents
The massacre of the Holy Innocents (28 December in the Western calendar) is among the oldest commemorations in the Christian calendar.
