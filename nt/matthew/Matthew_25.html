Matthew 25

Ten Virgins; The Talents; Sheep and Goats: Whatever You Did for the Least

Verses 1–13 — The Ten Virgins: Keep Watch
12345678910111213

Greek Word Study

Context

The two parables address watchful readiness and productive faithfulness -- two dimensions of eschatological posture. The virgins parable: are you ready when the moment comes? The talents parable: are you faithful in the interval? Together they answer "what does keeping watch look like?" -- not passive waiting but active, risk-taking faithfulness.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 7:21-23"Lord, Lord" -- the virgins' cry (v.11) echoes the Sermon's warning. Formal invocation without real relationship is excluded at judgment.
  • Matt 24:42-44Keep watch; be ready -- the virgins and talents parables are the concrete illustrations of the watchfulness commanded at the end of ch.24.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
25:4

The wise virgins had taken oil in jars. The oil cannot be shared -- whatever it represents (genuine readiness, formed character, real relationship with Christ) is irreversibly personal. Preparation must be done before the moment of reckoning; it cannot be borrowed at the last minute.

25:21

Well done, good and faithful servant. The most desired words in the gospel -- addressed to servants faithful with what they were given. Five-talent and two-talent servants receive identical commendation. Faithfulness is the criterion, not giftedness.

25:25

I was afraid and hid your gold. The unfaithful servant's motive is fear -- a wrong theology of God producing paralysis rather than risk-taking trust. Wrong theology of God always produces unfaithful stewardship.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:1-13

Calvin: the chief thing required of Christ's servants is perseverance -- the readiness that survives an unexpected delay. The foolish virgins failed not because they slept but because they did not provision for the night. What we must be is provisioned for the long watch.

25:31-46

Calvin on the sheep-and-goats: Christ does not teach that we earn salvation by works of charity but that the charitable life is the evidence of the faith that saves. Those who are truly the Father's children show it by caring for their siblings. Works are the fruit, not the root, of salvation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:1-13

The NET note on the ten virgins discusses the ancient Jewish wedding custom: the bridegroom came at an unpredictable time, often late at night. The parable addresses the community that must maintain readiness through an indefinite delay.

25:31-46

The NET note on 'the least of these brothers and sisters of mine' (v.40) surveys the debate: does it refer to all the poor universally or specifically to Jesus's disciples? The note concludes that the specific reference to Jesus's disciples (missionaries) is most contextually supported, though the universal application is a legitimate secondary extension.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
25:1-13

Robertson on the ten virgins: all ten fall asleep (v.5) -- the failure is not sleeping but arriving without oil. The wise virgins prepared for a delay; the foolish presumed prompt arrival. Robertson notes the shut door (he kleithe he thyra) as an aorist passive -- the door was shut permanently, not temporarily.

25:14-30

Robertson on the talents: the first servant receives five, the second two, the third one -- each kata ten idian dynamin (according to his own ability, v.15). The condemnation of the third servant is not for the small amount received but for burying it. The master's rebuke establishes that the minimum required was some use.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
25:1-13

Chrysostom: the oil of good works cannot be borrowed -- the interior life of faith and charity cannot be transferred. The virgins who have oil cannot share it not from selfishness but because what has been accumulated through living cannot be handed over at the last moment.

25:31-46

The Catena traces the patristic importance of the sheep-and-goats for social ethics. Chrysostom's longest homilies develop this passage: whatever you did for the least of these makes Christ present in the hungry, thirsty, stranger, naked, sick, and imprisoned.

Verses 14–30 — The Parable of the Talents
1415161718192021222324252627282930

Greek Word Study

Context

The sheep and goats parable is the final parable of Jesus's public ministry in Matthew -- and the most concrete account of what God values. The criterion of judgment is entirely embodied: what you did with your hands and resources when you encountered the hungry, the stranger, the naked, the sick, the imprisoned. The surprise of both groups is the key: neither performed their care (or non-care) for divine approval.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezek 34:17God judging between sheep and goats -- the divine shepherd's act. Jesus takes on this role in the judgment scene.
  • Prov 19:17Whoever is kind to the poor lends to the LORD -- the theological basis for the King's identification with the vulnerable. Jesus radicalises it: not "as if" but actually.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
25:40

Whatever you did for the least of these, you did for me. The most radical identification in the gospel. The encounter with the vulnerable is the encounter with Christ. Service to the powerless is service to him -- not metaphorically but actually.

25:44

When did we see you and not help? The goats' question confirms their failure was inattention, not deliberate rejection. The judgment is not for active persecution but for passive neglect of those in whom Christ was present.

25:46

Eternal punishment; eternal life. Both outcomes are real and permanent. The criterion throughout has been thoroughly embodied -- how you treated the powerless in ordinary life. The judgment is a direct audit of daily practice.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:14-30

Calvin on the buried talent: the third servant does not hate his master but fears him -- an unworthy fear that produces paralysis rather than action. The demanding master is the reason to act, not the reason to hide. Knowledge that the master is demanding should produce urgency.

25:40

Calvin: Christ honours his members with his own dignity -- he so identifies with them that what is done to them is done to him. We do not earn by charitable works; we recognise Christ and respond.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:14-30

The NET note on the talent (talanton, v.15) discusses its monetary value: one talent was approximately 6,000 denarii (roughly 20 years of a labourer's wages). The five-talent servant's return represents approximately 100 years of wages. The talents represent the resources of the kingdom entrusted to servants for the period of the master's absence.

25:46

The NET note on 'eternal punishment' (kolasin aionion) and 'eternal life' (zoen aionion) discusses the parallel structure: both outcomes use the same adjective. In Matthew's eschatological context, eternal duration is the primary meaning.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
25:31-46

Robertson on the sheep-and-goats: the criterion of judgment -- feeding the hungry, clothing the naked, visiting the sick and imprisoned -- is entirely practical. The surprise of both groups ('Lord, when did we see you?') shows the judgment is revealing what was already real, not imposing new requirements.

25:40-46

Robertson on 'the least of these brothers and sisters of mine' (ton adelphon mou touton ton elachiston): adelphon (brothers) in Matthew consistently refers to disciples/fellow believers. The vulnerable missionary is the one in whom Christ is present. The judgment reveals that care for the suffering disciple is care for Christ.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
25:14-30

Chrysostom: the servant who buried his talent was not wrong to fear its loss -- he was wrong to think burial was the safe choice. What is entrusted for use cannot be kept safe by not using it; it is lost precisely by the non-use that was meant to preserve it.

25:40

Chrysostom: do you wish to honour the body of Christ? Do not despise him in his nakedness and then honour him with silk in the church. There is more value in a warm cloak given to the shivering poor person than in gold poured on the altar. The Christ of the Eucharist and the Christ of the poor are the same Christ.

Ancient Sources

Wedding customs
Jewish wedding processions could last until midnight. Bridesmaids waited to escort the bride. Their oil was their personal responsibility.
Immediately recognisable to the audience.
Talents and banking
V.27: "put my money on deposit with the bankers." Even minimal risk would have been better than paralysis. The economic realism grounds the judgment.
Ancient banking offered interest on deposits.

Reception History

Matthew 25 and social ethics
The sheep and goats parable has been foundational for Christian social ethics -- from the Didache through Francis of Assisi to liberation theology.
"Talent" in English
The word entered English as a synonym for natural ability through this parable.

Textual Notes

25:31Son of Man in his glory
Matt 25:31 is the only Matthean text combining 'Son of Man', 'his glory', 'his angels', and 'his throne' -- the fullest eschatological picture in the gospel.
The accumulated titles place the final judgment entirely in Jesus's hands: he is simultaneously the shepherd separating the flocks and the king pronouncing the eternal verdicts.

Scholarly Debates

Who Are 'the Least of These' in Matthew 25:40?
All the poor universally
Patristic consensus (especially Chrysostom), most modern readers
Christ is present in all suffering humanity. The judgment is on universal charity.
Specifically Jesus's disciples/missionaries
Most modern exegetes (Davies, France, Nolland)
Matthew's use of 'brothers' consistently refers to disciples. The vulnerable missionaries sent out in ch.10 are 'the least of these.'
Both, in concentric circles
Some commentators
The immediate reference is to disciples; the principle extends to all the vulnerable because the disciples represent them.
The missionary-disciples reading is most contextually supported in Matthew. The universal extension is a legitimate theological application that Christians have rightly made throughout church history.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 25 is the eschatological culmination of the Olivet Discourse: three parables of the end that address readiness, faithfulness, and solidarity with the vulnerable. The sheep-and-goats final image is the gospel's last word before the passion narrative begins.
