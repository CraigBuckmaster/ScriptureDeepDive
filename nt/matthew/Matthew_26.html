Matthew 26

Anointing; Conspiracy; Last Supper; Gethsemane; Arrest; Trial; Denial

See on Timeline — Last Supper
Author

Matthew (Levi) ben Alphaeus, a tax collector called by Jesus at his customs post in Capernaum (Matt 9:9; Mark 2:14). One of the twelve apostles and an eyewitness to the ministry of Jesus. He would have been literate and numerate by profession — uniquely equipped to compose a carefully structured written account. Unanimous early tradition attributes the Gospel to him: Papias (c.AD 125) records that "Matthew compiled the oracles in the Hebrew language, and everyone interpreted them as best he could." The Gospel as we have it is in polished literary Greek, suggesting either a Greek composition drawing on Aramaic sources, or a Greek original.

When written

c.AD 80–90, traditionally associated with Antioch of Syria, the first great Gentile church and a centre of Jewish-Christian interaction. The Gospel presupposes the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) in its apocalyptic passages (Matt 22:7; 24:15-22), suggesting composition after that date. It draws on Mark's Gospel (c.AD 65-70) and on a sayings source shared with Luke (conventionally called Q). Matthew shapes his sources with a more formal, structured literary hand than any of the other evangelists.

What prompted it

To present Jesus of Nazareth as the fulfilment of Israel's entire scriptural heritage — the promised Messiah, the new Moses, the true Israel, and the Son of God. Matthew writes primarily for a Jewish-Christian community navigating its relationship to the synagogue after AD 70. His ten fulfilment-formula citations ("all this took place to fulfil what was spoken through the prophet") are the theological spine of the work: Israel's Scripture is not superseded but completed in Jesus. The Great Commission (28:18-20) shows the universal horizon of a Gospel that begins with Abraham and ends with all nations.

Verses 1–16 — Anointing at Bethany; Judas Agrees to Betray

Greek Word Study

Context

The first half moves through six scenes: the plot (vv.1-5), the anointing (vv.6-13), Judas's bargain (vv.14-16), the Last Supper (vv.17-29), prediction of scattering (vv.30-32), and Peter's boast (vv.33-35). The chapter's deepest irony: the highest act of worship (the unnamed woman's anointing) and the deepest betrayal (Judas's bargain) are placed side by side.

Places

Bethany — Simon's house
Village east of Jerusalem
The anointing happens at Bethany in Simon the leper's house — two days before Passover. Bethany is the final refuge before the Passion begins. The woman's extravagance ("the whole house was filled with the fragrance") contrasts with the disciples' calculation.
Jerusalem — Passover preparations
City where the Last Supper occurs
"Where do you want us to make preparations for you to eat the Passover?" Jerusalem is the required location for the Passover. The upper room is in Jerusalem. The Last Supper, Gethsemane, the arrest, and the trials all occur within walking distance of each other.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
26:7-13

The anointing woman. Her act is extravagant and prophetic -- she does not know she is preparing Jesus for burial. The disciples' objection (waste) is met by commendation: she has done something beautiful and memorial. Her story has been told everywhere the gospel has gone -- for two thousand years.

26:28

Blood of the covenant, poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins. Three prepositional phrases: covenant ratification, substitutionary scope, redemptive purpose. The theology of atonement in miniature.

26:35

Even if I have to die with you, I will never disown you. Peter's boast is sincere and wrong. He will disown Jesus three times before dawn. Matthew's portrait of the disciples at table: confident, ignorant of what they are about to do.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
26:26-29

Calvin on 'this is my body': Christ does not say the bread becomes his body but that the bread is the sign and pledge of his body. The presence is real -- Christ is truly present in the Supper -- but the presence is spiritual, received by faith. The Catholic doctrine of transubstantiation confounds the sign with the thing signified.

26:36-46

Calvin on Gethsemane: his horror at death was the genuine experience of the wrath of God against sin. He was not merely afraid of physical death but of the spiritual death -- separation from the Father -- that the cup contained. This is what makes the cross a real atonement: he bore the punishment sin deserved, including its darkest dimension.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
26:26-28

The NET note on 'this is my body / blood of the covenant' surveys the eucharistic interpretation debate: real presence (Lutheran, Catholic), spiritual presence (Reformed), memorial (Zwinglian). The note traces the OT covenant-blood background (Exod 24:8) and the new covenant promise (Jer 31:31-34), concluding that Jesus identifies his death as the ratifying event of the new covenant.

26:36-46

The NET note on Gethsemane traces the cup (to poterion) as the OT image of divine judgment (Isa 51:17-23; Jer 25:15-29; Ps 75:8). Jesus's prayer asks whether the judgment-cup can pass -- the weight is the genuine bearing of God's judgment against sin, not physical cowardice.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
26:1-16

Robertson on the anointing at Bethany: the woman's act is described as eis mnemosynon autes (for her memorial, v.13) -- the only case in the gospels where Jesus guarantees that someone's act will be remembered wherever the gospel is proclaimed. The act of wasteful devotion is permanently enshrined in the story of the kingdom.

26:17-35

Robertson on the Last Supper's eucharistic formula: touto estin to soma mou (this is my body) and touto estin to haima mou tes diathekes (this is my blood of the covenant). Robertson traces diatheke (covenant) through LXX usage: Jesus identifies his blood as the new covenant's ratifying blood (cf. Jer 31:31-34; Exod 24:8).

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
26:6-13

Chrysostom: while the disciples debate waste (it could have been sold and given to the poor), she performs an act of devotion that transcends their calculus. True worship cannot always be reduced to its most efficient alternative use. The kingdom includes the extravagant act done purely for love.

26:36-56

The Catena traces the theology of Gethsemane: Jesus's prayer ('let this cup pass, yet not as I will but as you will', v.39) is the text for the doctrine of Christ's human will. Maximus the Confessor developed the Gethsemane prayer as the foundation for asserting Christ's real human will genuinely submitted to the divine will. Monotheletism is refuted by this prayer.

Verses 17–35 — The Last Supper; Peter’s Denial Predicted
17181920212223242526272829303132333435

Greek Word Study

Context

The second half holds together Gethsemane (vv.36-46), the arrest (vv.47-56), the Sanhedrin trial (vv.57-68), and Peter's denial (vv.69-75). The structural parallel is precise: Jesus prays "not my will" three times; Peter denies "I don't know the man" three times. Perfect obedience and perfect failure are simultaneous.

Places

Gethsemane
Garden at the foot of the Mount of Olives
Gethsemane ("olive press") is at the base of the Mount of Olives — the route Jesus walks after the Last Supper. The garden is where the will is submitted: "not as I will, but as you will." The geography of the prayer is intimate and private; the geography of the arrest is public and violent.
Caiaphas's house
Jerusalem; high priest's residence
After the arrest, Jesus is taken to Caiaphas's house for the night trial. Peter follows and denies three times in the courtyard. The high priest's domestic space becomes the site of both the covenant's condemnation and its witness's failure.

  • Ps 110:1; Dan 7:13Son of Man at the right hand of the Mighty One, coming on clouds -- Jesus combines both texts under oath. Either blasphemy or truth; no middle option.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
26:39

Not as I will, but as you will. The most transparent moment in Matthew's portrayal of Jesus. The agony is real, not theatrical. He knows what the cup contains. The "yet" is the hinge of salvation history.

26:64

From now on you will see the Son of Man sitting at the right hand of the Mighty One. The most explicit Christological claim in the gospel, made under oath before the Sanhedrin. Combines Ps 110:1 and Dan 7:13. Either blasphemy or truth -- no middle option.

26:75

Peter wept bitterly. The most devastating sentence in ch.26. The bitter weeping is contrition, not despair. It is the beginning of the road back. Peter's failure and his tears are the portrait of every disciple.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
26:63-65

Calvin on the blasphemy charge: when the high priest asks if Jesus is the Christ the Son of God, the Sanhedrin is right that a mere man making this claim would be guilty of blasphemy. They are wrong only in their assumption that he is a mere man. This claim is either blasphemy or it is the gospel.

26:69-75

Calvin on Peter's denial: it was permitted that Peter should fall deeply so that he should rise more firmly. His fall revealed the self-confidence that needed to be broken. The weeping that followed is the beginning of genuine humility -- the apostle who would lead the church was first made to know what he was without divine support.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
26:57-67

The NET note on the Sanhedrin trial discusses the legal problems: night trials were prohibited by the Mishnah; witnesses had to agree; the accused could not condemn himself. The note surveys whether these Mishnaic prohibitions were in force in the first century.

26:69-75

The NET note on Peter's denial traces his geographic movement: from inside the courtyard to the gateway (v.71) to outside (v.75). The outward movement corresponds to the escalation of the denial.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
26:47-75

Robertson on Peter's denial: the threefold denial is marked by escalation -- first a simple denial, then with an oath, then with a curse and an oath. Robertson notes anemnesthes (he remembered) is an aorist -- the memory was sudden. Peter went outside and wept bitterly (pikros eklaien) -- the adverb pikros marks the grief of genuine remorse.

26:57-68

Robertson on the trial: the blasphemy charge follows Jesus's self-identification as the Son of Man seated at the right hand and coming on the clouds (v.64, citing Ps 110:1 and Dan 7:13). This is the culmination of Jesus's identity disclosure: before the Sanhedrin, under oath, he makes the claim the entire gospel has been building toward.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
26:57-68

The Catena: the false witnesses are inconsistent and the high priest cannot build a case from them. Only when Jesus speaks on oath does the trial resolve. Chrysostom: he is condemned for telling the truth about himself -- the ultimate irony of the passion.

26:69-75

Chrysostom on Peter's denial: the one who denied three times with oaths and curses became the one who preached three thousand to baptism at Pentecost. The Lord's foreknowledge of the denial and his prayer for Peter (Luke 22:32) transformed the failure into the foundation for the church's pastor.

Scholarly Debates

Real Presence or Memorial in the Lord's Supper?
Transubstantiation: substance becomes body and blood
Roman Catholic
This is my body is a literal identity statement; the substance of the elements is changed.
Consubstantiation: body and blood present in, with, under the elements
Lutheran
The body and blood are truly present alongside the bread and wine.
Spiritual presence received by faith
Reformed / Calvinist
Christ is truly present but spiritually -- received by faith. The bread and wine are signs and pledges.
Memorial only
Zwinglian
This is my body means this represents my body. The Supper is a commemoration.
The Reformed reading (true spiritual presence received by faith) is most consistent with the biblical evidence, preserving the reality of Christ's presence while avoiding physicalism that the NT's teaching on Christ's ascended body makes problematic.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 26 is the passion's opening movement: anointing, conspiracy, supper, garden, arrest, trial, denial. The chapter establishes the passion's theological framework: new covenant ratified in blood, human will submitted to divine will, and the disciple who relied on himself broken down to the weeping that precedes genuine apostolic leadership.
