Verses 1–16 — Labourers in the Vineyard: The Last Will Be First
12345678910111213141516

Greek Word Study

Context

The workers-in-the-vineyard parable (vv.1–16) directly answers 19:27–30's first/last reversal and Peter's reward-calculation. The parable teaches not that later-arriving workers are better but that the owner's generosity is sovereign — he has the right to give the last the same as the first. The third passion prediction (vv.17–19) is Matthew's most detailed — explicitly including the Gentiles, the mocking, the flogging, the crucifixion, and the resurrection. This is the clearest announcement of the cross before Jerusalem.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
20:15

Don't I have the right to do what I want with my own money? Or are you envious because I am generous? The most pointed question about grace in the parables. The parable's scandal is that equal pay for unequal work feels unjust — which is exactly how grace feels to those who think they have earned it. The landowner is not unjust (he paid what was agreed) but he is generous beyond merit. Envy resents what grace gives to others.

20:18–19

The Son of Man will be delivered to the Gentiles to be mocked and flogged and crucified. The third and most specific passion prediction — now including the Gentile involvement, the specific tortures, and crucifixion by name. The disciples have heard this twice before and still do not understand (v.22 will confirm it). The prediction is a gift of preparation that they are not yet able to receive.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:1-16

Calvin on the parable: 'The landowner is not unjust to those who worked the whole day -- they received what was agreed. He is generous to those who worked less. The complaint arises from comparing oneself with others rather than being grateful for what one has received. The kingdom's economy destroys the comparative resentment that calculates one's share by measuring another's.'

20:20-28

Calvin on the ransom saying (v.28: 'to give his life as a ransom for many'): 'The word ransom (lytron) is the key to the atonement theology of the gospel. Christ gave his life antilytron -- a price paid in exchange for others. The preposition anti (in exchange for, in the place of) is the substitutionary formula: he died in the place of the many. This is not metaphor but transaction.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:1-16

The NET note on 'the last will be first and the first will be last' (v.16) discusses the relationship to the same statement in 19:30: the parable is the explanation of the principle. The note traces the reversal theme through the Magnificat (Luke 1:52-53) and identifies it as the kingdom's structural inversion: the world's first (those who calculate their entitlement) become last in the kingdom's economy.

20:17-19

The NET note on the third passion prediction traces its growing specificity: the first (16:21) mentions suffering, death, and resurrection; the second (17:22-23) adds the betrayal; the third adds handing over to Gentiles, mocking, flogging, and crucifying. The increasing specificity as Jerusalem approaches is Matthew's narrative signal that the passion is now the story.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
20:1-16

Robertson on the parable of the workers in the vineyard: the denarius (a day's wage) given to all workers regardless of hours reveals that the kingdom's justice is the landowner's generosity, not the workers' calculation of equity. Robertson notes that the workers hired first agreed to the denarius (v.2: suphonesei) -- they entered a contract. The later workers simply trusted the landowner's what-is-right promise (v.4). The grumbling (v.11) comes from those who substituted their calculation for the landowner's generosity.

20:17-19

Robertson on the third passion prediction (vv.17-19): the most specific of the three -- handing over to the Gentiles, mocking, flogging, crucifying, and rising on the third day. Robertson notes that as Jerusalem approaches, the predictions become more specific. The disciples still do not understand (cf. the immediately following request of James and John's mother), revealing the gap between Jesus's clarity about what is coming and the disciples' continued confusion.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
20:1-16

The Catena traces multiple patristic readings of the vineyard parable. Chrysostom reads the hours as the hours of the day at which different people come to faith: from childhood (first hour) to deathbed (eleventh hour). All receive the same eternal life because eternal life is not proportional to the length of the work but to the generosity of the one who gives it.

20:20-23

The Catena records the patristic reading of the mother of Zebedee's sons as representing the misunderstanding of the disciples: even those closest to Jesus share the confusion about the kingdom's nature. Chrysostom notes that the request -- the best seats in the kingdom -- reveals that they still conceive the kingdom as a political hierarchy where proximity to the king confers status.

Verses 17–28 — Third Passion Prediction; James and John’s Request
171819202122232425262728

Greek Word Study

Context

The request of James and John (vv.20–28) is the most poignant misunderstanding in Matthew — the third passion prediction has just been given (vv.17–19) and the response is an immediate jockeying for seats of honour. The request produces Jesus's most concentrated statement of kingdom leadership: greatness means servanthood; first means slave; and the Son of Man himself is the model and the reason (v.28 — "just as the Son of Man"). The two blind men at Jericho (vv.29–34) close the chapter with a healing that dramatises the whole section: those who cannot see but cry out persistently are given sight and "follow him" — the definition of discipleship.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
20:22

You don't know what you are asking. The gentlest possible rebuke — addressed to those who want to be great without knowing what greatness costs. The cup they are so confidently claiming is the cup of judgment that Jesus will ask to be taken from him in Gethsemane. Their "we can" is sincere and wrong.

20:28

The Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many. The most theologically concentrated verse in Matthew 20. It answers the status-competition that has run through chs.18–20 with the ultimate example: the Son of Man himself, who had every right to be served, came to serve and to die in the place of others. The ransom-for-many is both the means of salvation and the model of discipleship.

20:34

Jesus had compassion on them and touched their eyes. The chapter that opened with a vineyard parable about equal grace closes with two blind men healed by compassion. They receive sight and follow him. The trajectory of the chapter — from entitlement (workers' grumbling) through ambition (James and John) to persistent, receiving faith (the blind men) — is the trajectory the disciples must travel.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:24-28

Calvin on servanthood as leadership: 'Christ is not giving an external rule for hierarchical organisation but revealing the nature of authority in the kingdom. The Gentile rulers lord it over others; the kingdom's leaders give themselves for others. The difference is not in the structure but in the direction of energy: outward (for others) rather than inward (for self-preservation).'

20:29-34

Calvin on the blind men's persistence: 'The more the crowd attempted to silence them, the more fervently they cried out. This teaches us that the obstacles the world places between us and Christ should increase our urgency, not reduce it. The one who is silenced by the crowd's rebuke will not receive what the one who persists obtains.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:28

The NET note on antilytron (ransom, v.28) traces the term through its OT background (Isa 43:3-4; 53:10-12) and its NT uses (1 Tim 2:6: antilytron hyper panton). The note identifies the Isaianic Servant connection: the 'many' of v.28 echoes Isa 53:11-12. Jesus's ransom saying is the most concentrated self-identification with the suffering Servant in the gospels.

20:29-34

The NET note on the two blind men discusses the apparent tension with Mark (one blind man named Bartimaeus). The note surveys the major explanations: Matthew's characteristic doubling, the possibility that Bartimaeus was the more prominent of two healed, or the independent tradition. The note observes that the historical tension does not affect the theological point: the Son of David heals those whom the crowd ignores.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
20:20-28

Robertson on the ransom saying (v.28: antilytron peri pollon, ransom for many): Robertson notes anti (in exchange for) is the substitutionary preposition -- not 'for the benefit of' but 'in the place of.' Robertson traces lytron (ransom) through 1 Tim 2:6 (antilytron, a ransom in exchange) as the NT's clearest substitutionary atonement language. The many (pollon) is the OT Isaianic Servant language of Isa 53:12 -- Robertson identifies the ransom saying as Jesus's most direct self-identification with the Servant.

20:29-34

Robertson on the healing of two blind men at Jericho: Matthew has two; Mark and Luke have one (Bartimaeus). Robertson notes Matthew's characteristic doubling of healing recipients (cf. 8:28-34: two demoniacs; 9:27-31: two blind men). The double witness may serve the Mosaic legal standard of two witnesses. The cry 'Son of David' is the messianic title that the previous chapters have been building toward.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
20:20-28

The Catena traces the patristic reading of 'the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve' (v.28) as the charter of Christian leadership. Chrysostom: 'Among the pagans the greatest person commands the others; among you, the greatest is the servant of the others. This is not merely a rule for conduct but a description of the kingdom's nature: power in the kingdom is exercised through self-giving, not self-assertion.'

20:29-34

The Catena notes that the two blind men who cry 'Lord, Son of David, have mercy on us!' are commended by the Fathers as models of persistent prayer: the crowd rebukes them and they cry out all the louder (v.31). Chrysostom: 'They would not be silenced by the crowd's rebuke, and Jesus stopped. The prayer that persists past the crowd's rebuke reaches the Lord who stops for those the crowd ignores.'

NoteLytron anti pollōn — ransom in place of the many. Matthew's most explicit atonement statement. The Son of Man's service is simultaneously the model of discipleship and the ground of salvation.

Ancient Sources

The denarius
A Roman silver coin = one day's wage for a labourer. The agreed amount (v.2) was fair for a full day's work. The parable's economy is realistic: the landowner pays the contractual amount to the first workers and more than the contractual amount to the last.
The economic realism makes the grace visible.
Jericho
A wealthy oasis city 24km northeast of Jerusalem, the last major settlement before the climb to Jerusalem. Bartimaeus and his companion sit at the road's edge as the Passover pilgrims stream past.
The location makes Jerusalem's proximity felt.

Reception History

20:28 and atonement theology
The ransom-for-many has been the foundational text for ransom theories of atonement (Origen) and substitutionary atonement (Anselm, Calvin) alike. The preposition anti (in place of) is the strongest substitutionary term available.
The vineyard parable and grace
The parable has been cited in disputes about merit and grace across church history — whether salvation is entirely God's free gift (Augustine, Luther) or partly responsive to human effort (Pelagius, Arminius). The landowner's final question ("are you envious because I am generous?") is the definitive answer.

Textual Notes

20:28Ransom for many: anti peri pollon
Greek: anti as the substitutionary preposition ('in exchange for') vs peri ('for the benefit of'). Matthew and Mark both use anti; 1 Tim 2:6 uses antilytron hyper panton.
The substitutionary reading (anti = in the place of) is the most exegetically grounded. The ransom metaphor requires an exchange: the one who pays is freed from what previously held them.

Scholarly Debates

Are the 'Many' in the Ransom Saying Limited?
Many = all humanity (universal scope)
Most evangelical interpreters
The OT background (Isa 53:11-12) uses 'many' to mean 'the great multitude' -- the all-encompassing extent of the Servant's work. In context it means all without restriction.
Many = the elect (limited scope)
Reformed interpreters following definite atonement
The many are those for whom the ransom is applied effectively -- the elect. The 'ransom for many' is distinguished from 'for all' to preserve the particularity of redemption.
The exegetical debate is whether 'many' (pollon) is inclusive-extensive (all) or selective-limited. The OT background strongly supports the inclusive reading. The application of the ransom's benefits is a separate theological question.

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 20 descends from the Transfiguration mountain to the Jerusalem road. The workers parable establishes the kingdom's grace-economy; the passion prediction clarifies what the Jerusalem journey means; the servanthood charter redefines greatness; and the ransom saying names the mechanism of the passion. The chapter is the atonement's theological preparation.
