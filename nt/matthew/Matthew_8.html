Matthew 8

Healing Ministry: The Leper, the Centurion, the Storm; Gadarene Demoniacs

Verses 1–17 — The Leper; the Centurion’s Servant; Peter’s Mother-in-Law
1234567891011121314151617

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

Matthew 8–9 is the miracle section of the first great block — the practised summary of 4:23 ("healing every disease and sickness") now filled with specific stories. The three healings of vv.1–17 are arranged to maximise social contrast: a Jewish leper (excluded by purity law), a Gentile centurion's servant (excluded by ethnicity), and Peter's female relative (excluded by gender norms). Jesus heals all three and touches the untouchable. The section closes with Isaiah 53:4, interpreting the healings as Servant fulfilment.

Places

Capernaum
Northwest shore of Sea of Galilee
After the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus enters Capernaum. The leper approaches "while Jesus was coming down the mountain" — the movement from mountain (teaching) to town (healing) is Matthew's structure for chs.5-9: authoritative word followed by authoritative deed.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 53:4He took up our infirmities and bore our diseases — quoted in v.17. The healings are not merely compassion; they are Servant-work, bearing human suffering rather than merely removing it.
  • Matt 28:19Many will come from east and west (v.11) — the eschatological table with Abraham anticipates the commission to all nations. The centurion's faith is the first instalment of the Gentile harvest.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:3

Calvin on Jesus touching the leper: Under the law, touching a leper communicated defilement. Christ reverses this by the power of his holiness -- he touches what is unclean and the unclean becomes clean. This is the pattern of the incarnation: he enters the domain of human sin without being contaminated; instead he purifies.

8:10-12

Calvin on the centurion's faith: This pagan soldier's faith shames Israel -- he who has received no covenant promise believes; they who have received all the promises do not. This is the mystery of grace: it operates where it will, not where it is expected.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:1-4

The NET note on 'show yourself to the priest' discusses the legal significance of the Leviticus 14 requirement. Jesus sends the healed leper to fulfil Torah's requirements -- the healing is real and publicly certified.

8:5-13

The NET note on 'I will come and heal him' discusses whether this is a statement or a question ('Shall I come and heal him?'). The interrogative reading heightens the Gentile-inclusion significance: Jesus offers to cross Jewish-Gentile social boundaries.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
8:1-4

Robertson notes the leper's address 'Lord, if you are willing' is the most perfect expression of faith in the Gospels -- total confidence in ability combined with total submission to will. The touching of the leper is the drama's peak: a leper was untouchable by law; Jesus touches him. The touch does not make Jesus unclean; it makes the leper clean.

8:5-13

Robertson draws attention to the centurion's theology of authority: he understands Jesus' word as having the same relationship to reality that a military command has to soldiers -- it is effective by its own authority. Robertson notes 'I have not found such great faith in anyone in Israel' is among Jesus' most astonishing statements.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
8:1-4

Chrysostom: Christ stretched out his hand and touched what was not to be touched, showing that holiness is stronger than uncleanness. By touching the impure he purified it -- this is the pattern of the incarnation itself: he enters the domain of human sin and death without being contaminated; instead he purifies.

8:5-13

Augustine on the centurion: He confessed himself unworthy that Christ should enter under his roof -- greater humility has never been spoken. His faith was measured not by years of instruction but by the depth of his recognition: this man's word is sovereign over disease as a military command is sovereign over soldiers.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
8:1-17

MacArthur: The healing narrative of chs.8-9 is Matthew's demonstration of the kingdom power proclaimed in chs.5-7. The triad of healed persons — leper (social outcast), centurion's servant (Gentile), Peter's mother-in-law (woman) — suggests the kingdom's reach: across purity boundaries, ethnic boundaries, and gender boundaries.

8:16-17

MacArthur: He drove out the spirits with a word and healed all the sick. This was to fulfill what was spoken through the prophet Isaiah: he took up our infirmities and bore our diseases. Isaiah 53 is applied to Jesus's healing ministry before it is applied to his death — the Servant's bearing of human pain encompasses both.

Verses 18–27 — The Cost of Following; Stilling the Storm
18192021222324252627

Greek Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The second half of ch.8 balances the miracle sequence with two discipleship sayings (vv.19–22) that set the cost before two nature miracles (vv.23–27, 28–34). The stilling of the storm demonstrates authority over creation — completing the Servant's authority portfolio (over disease, ethnicity, gender, demons, and now nature). The disciples' question ("what kind of man is this?") is the chapter's theological hinge, demanding an answer the rest of Matthew will provide.

Places

Sea of Galilee
The lake itself during crossing
The disciples and Jesus get into a boat to cross the sea. The storm on the Sea of Galilee is not metaphor but geography: the lake is known for sudden violent storms caused by cold air descending from the surrounding hills into the warmer lake basin.
Region of the Gadarenes / Gergesenes
Eastern shore of the Sea of Galilee; Decapolis territory
The far shore is Gentile territory — the Decapolis, Greek cities east of the Jordan. The healing of the Gadarene demoniacs is Jesus's first extended engagement in Gentile geography. Pigs (unclean animals for Jews) grazing here marks the territory as outside the covenant's ethnic boundary.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 107:23–30He stilled the storm to a whisper — the Psalter's language of God calming the sea applied to Jesus's act. The disciples' amazement is the appropriate response to a theophany.
  • Job 38:8–11Who shut up the sea behind doors? — God's speech from the whirlwind. Jesus's authority over wind and waves is presented in language reserved for God's creative sovereignty.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:25-26

Calvin on 'you of little faith, why are you so afraid?': The rebuke is not for calling out -- that was right. The rebuke is for the panic implying he was unable to save them. Little faith calls to God in crisis but does not trust his ability or willingness. Great faith calls with confidence that his power and care are both sufficient and certain.

8:29

Calvin on the demons' question: The demons know the appointed day of judgment. They are not ignorant of their destiny; they seek only a temporary reprieve. Their acknowledgment of Christ as Son of God is forced confession, not worship.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:23-27

The NET note on 'what kind of man is this?' traces the disciples' growing Christological question through Matthew. The storm stilling is the first incident forcing it directly: no human being commands wind and sea.

8:28-34

The NET note on 'the appointed time' (kairos) that the demons reference discusses the eschatological dimension: the demons understand that the final judgment has a set time and that Jesus' arrival has not yet inaugurated it.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
8:23-27

Robertson notes the storm on the Galilean lake was a sudden, violent squall typical of the geography. The rebuke of the wind and sea uses epitimao -- the same word used for rebuking demons. The storm is treated as a personal hostile force.

8:28-34

Robertson identifies the demons' question -- 'Have you come to torment us before the appointed time?' -- as an acknowledgment of their defeat in advance. They have theological knowledge of who Jesus is and what the Judgment will mean for them.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
8:23-27

Chrysostom: Why did he allow them to be in danger? To exercise their faith. He permitted the trial so the disciples might cry out to him in helplessness and learn that their safety rests in him alone, not in their own seamanship.

8:28-34

Jerome on the demoniacs: The demons know who Jesus is before his disciples fully understand it. They call him Son of God and acknowledge the appointed time will bring their torment. This supernatural knowledge is not worship but terror.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
8:18-34

MacArthur: The would-be disciples of vv.18-22 frame the storm miracle: the first wants to follow with conditions (I will follow you wherever you go — but first let me...); the second hedges with a prior obligation. The storm-stilling follows: Jesus has authority over the chaos of creation. The question is whether the disciples will trust that authority without conditions.

8:26

MacArthur: You of little faith, why are you so afraid? The disciples have witnessed the teaching of chs.5-7 and the healings of 8:1-17. They know who Jesus is. Their fear in the storm is not ignorance but the failure to apply what they know to the present circumstance. Little faith is not no faith but faith that doesn't reach far enough.

Matt 8 — Key Terms

VersionTranslation

NoteThe centurion's authority-logic: commands are obeyed because of the rank behind them. He applies this to Jesus — confidence in his word, not in his physical presence. This is the definition of faith in Matthew 8.

Ancient Sources

Roman centurion
Commander of 80–100 soldiers, the backbone of the Roman army. That a centurion would appeal to a Jewish healer reflects both desperation and unusual openness.
Centurions appear positively in Matthew (8:5–13) and Luke (7:1–10; 23:47) — their institutional role in the crucifixion and their positive personal responses create a studied contrast.
The Sea of Galilee
Below sea level, surrounded by hills that funnel sudden storms down onto the lake. Furious squalls are historically documented.
The geographical setting makes the storm narrative historically plausible.

Reception History

Isa 53:4 in Matthew
Matthew's use of Isa 53:4 for physical healing (v.17) diverges from Paul's spiritual use (1 Pet 2:24). The difference reflects two dimensions of the Servant's work: physical restoration and spiritual atonement are both aspects of the one redemption.
The centurion in liturgy
The centurion's words in v.8 are the basis for the Agnus Dei in Catholic/Anglican liturgy: "Lord, I am not worthy that thou shouldst come under my roof" — spoken before communion since at least the 8th century.

Literary Structure

The leper is touched; the centurion's servant healed at a distance; Peter's mother-in-law healed by touch. Every mode of healing demonstrates the comprehensive authority.

Textual Notes

8:17He took up our infirmities and bore our diseases (Isa 53:4)
Matthew applies Isa 53:4 to the healing ministry. Isaiah 53:4 in MT: aken cholayenu hu nasa.
Matthew extends the Servant's bearing to physical disease as well as sin. The immediate fulfilment is the healing ministry; the ultimate fulfilment is the atonement addressing the root cause of all disease.

Scholarly Debates

Does Matthew 8:17 Teach Physical Healing in the Atonement?
Yes -- Isaiah 53:4 applies to physical healing
Word of Faith theology, some Pentecostals
Matthew's application of Isa 53:4 to physical healing establishes it as part of the atonement's provision.
No -- Matthew describes the ministry, not the atonement mechanism
Reformed and most evangelical commentators
Matthew applies Isa 53:4 to the healing ministry as its own fulfilment. The atonement (Isa 53:5) addresses sin; the healing ministry (53:4) demonstrates compassionate bearing of human suffering.
The Reformed reading is most consistent with Matthew's context and with Peter's use of Isa 53:5 (not 53:4) for the atonement (1 Pet 2:24).

Theological Themes Tracker

Matthew 8 presents Jesus' authority across every domain: physical disease, distance, natural forces, and demonic power. The chapter's governing Christological question -- who is this man? -- is answered cumulatively by the evidence of authority that no human being possesses.
