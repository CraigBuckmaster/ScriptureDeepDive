John 15

The True Vine

Author

John the son of Zebedee — fisherman, apostle, and “the disciple whom Jesus loved” (John 13:23; 19:26; 20:2; 21:7, 20). The fourth Gospel never names its author directly, but the evidence converges on John: (1) The “beloved disciple” is an eyewitness (19:35; 21:24) present at the Last Supper, the cross, and the empty tomb. (2) Early and unanimous tradition: Irenaeus (c. AD 180), Clement of Alexandria, Origen, and Eusebius all identify the author as John the apostle writing from Ephesus in old age. (3) The Gospel’s intimate knowledge of Jerusalem topography (Pool of Bethesda, Pool of Siloam, the Pavement/Gabbatha) is confirmed by archaeology. (4) The eyewitness claim of 19:35 and the closing verse of 21:24 point to direct authorial witness. Liberal scholarship has proposed an anonymous Johannine community or the Elder John of Ephesus; the traditional identification with the apostle remains the best-attested position.

When written

c. AD 85–95, probably from Ephesus, making it the latest of the four Gospels. The Rylands Papyrus (P52, c. AD 125) — the earliest surviving NT manuscript fragment — contains John 18:31-33, 37-38, confirming the Gospel was in circulation in Egypt by the early second century. A pre-AD 70 date has been argued from the Temple language in chs 2, 5, and 10, which reads as description of a standing building; the majority of scholars favour a post-70 date for the final composition. The three Johannine letters and Revelation share vocabulary and theological concerns with the Gospel, suggesting a common Ephesian milieu.

What prompted it

John states his purpose explicitly — a rarity in the Gospels: “These are written that you may believe that Jesus is the Messiah, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name” (20:31). The Gospel was written to produce and sustain faith in the person of Jesus as divine Son. It supplements the Synoptics rather than duplicates them: John omits the Baptism, the Transfiguration, the Olivet Discourse, and the institution of the Lord’s Supper as a formal narrative, while providing material found nowhere else — the wedding at Cana, Nicodemus, the Samaritan woman, the raising of Lazarus, the Farewell Discourse (chs 14–17), and the resurrection appearances to Mary Magdalene and Thomas. Its elevated Christology (“In the beginning was the Word”) addresses late first-century challenges to the full divinity of Christ and lays the foundation for all subsequent trinitarian theology.

Verses 1–17 — I Am the True Vine: Abide in Me

Greek Word Study

menō ME-no — remain / abide / dwell: The verb appears ten times in vv.4-10 — the most concentrated use in the Gospel. Menō is John's signature word for the quality of relationship with Christ that produces life and fruitfulness. It is not passive (staying put) but active (maintaining the connection): remaining in Jesus means continuously drawing life from him, as a branch draws sap from the vine. The command "remain in me" (v.4) is the practical application of all the mutual-indwelling theology of ch.14.

ho ampelos hē alēthinē ho am-PE-los hē a-LE-thi-nē — the true vine: The seventh and final I am saying with predicate (v.1). "True" (alēthinē) echoes the "true light" (1:9), "true bread" (6:32), and "true worshippers" (4:23) — the authentic, genuine, original of which all other examples are copies. Israel was God's vine (Ps 80:8-16; Isa 5:1-7; Jer 2:21), but a vine that failed. Jesus is the true vine — the faithful Israel who does and is what Israel was called to be and failed to be.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
15:2

"He cuts off every branch in me that bears no fruit" — the cutting off of unfruitful branches raises the question of whether these are genuine believers who fall away or false professors who were never truly connected. The phrase "in me" (en emoi) suggests some form of connection; the fruitlessness and cutting suggest the connection was superficial. The Farewell Discourse is addressed to the eleven (Judas having left in ch.13) — yet it contains this warning. The warning is real; its application to any individual requires the whole Gospel's framework of genuine vs. superficial faith.

15:4-5

"No branch can bear fruit by itself; it must remain in the vine... apart from me you can do nothing" — the most radical statement of Christian dependence in the Gospel. "Nothing" (ouden) is absolute. Every spiritual capacity, every fruitful act, every word of witness — none of it can come from the disciple independently. The moment of confidence in one's own spiritual resources is the moment of disconnect from the vine.

15:13-15

"Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends" — the transition from "servants" to "friends" is marked by the kind of love that characterises the relationship. A servant obeys without knowing the master's purposes; a friend is let into the master's confidence. Jesus has done both: laid down his life and disclosed everything the Father told him. The friendship is not sentimental familiarity but a covenantal relationship of shared purpose.

15:16

"You did not choose me, but I chose you" — the election language is unambiguous. The disciples' relationship with Jesus is grounded in his prior choice, not their prior seeking. The appointment is purposive: "so that you might go and bear fruit." The election is not for privilege alone but for mission. Those who are chosen are chosen for fruitfulness.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:2

Calvin uses the pruning passage to argue for the necessity of affliction in the Christian life: the Father prunes the fruitful branches so that they may bear more fruit. This is not punishment but cultivation — the Father's direct engagement with the believer's life to remove what prevents greater fruitfulness. The suffering that feels like cutting is often the Father's pruning.

15:16

"You did not choose me, but I chose you" — Calvin's strongest text for the doctrine of election in John's Gospel. The disciples did not seek Jesus; he called them (1:43). Their fruitfulness is grounded not in their will but in his sovereign appointment. This does not make human response irrelevant (they did respond, they did follow) but it grounds the response in the prior divine initiative.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:2

"He cuts off" (airō) — the verb can also mean "lifts up" (as in 5:8-12 — pick up your mat). Some interpreters suggest the Father "lifts up" (nurtures/supports) unfruitful branches rather than removes them — a reading that removes the severity of the warning. However, the parallel with v.6 (branches thrown away and burned) strongly supports the "removes/cuts off" reading.

15:6

"Thrown into the fire and burned" — the fate of the unfruitful branch. This has been read as: (1) loss of salvation (branches that were truly connected can be permanently severed); (2) loss of reward but not salvation (the branch represents works, not the person); (3) false professors who were never truly connected. The passage does not resolve the question; it was probably designed to create urgency about the seriousness of the abiding relationship.

Augustine: "Apart from me you can do nothing. And therefore he says 'I am the vine, you are the branches.' The branch does not give life to the vine; it receives life from the vine. So Christ does not receive life from the church; the church receives life from Christ. The bishop, the presbyter, the laity — all branches, all dependent on the one vine."

15:13

Chrysostom: "Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends. He did not say 'for the righteous' or 'for the worthy' — for his friends. And who were his friends? Those who had denied him, abandoned him, would betray him. He calls them friends in the act of dying for them — and in dying for them makes them worthy to be called friends."

Verses 18–27 — The World’s Hatred; The Spirit Will Testify
18192021222324252627

Greek Word Study

misei mi-SEI — hates: The word appears seven times in vv.18-25, escalating from "hates you" to "hated me" to "hated my Father" to "hated without reason." The world's hatred of the disciples is not personal — it is derivative and theological: it is the hatred of Jesus transferred to his followers because they bear his name, and it is ultimately the hatred of the Father expressed through rejection of the Son.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
15:18-19

"If the world hates you, keep in mind that it hated me first... I have chosen you out of the world. That is why the world hates you" — the logic is precise: (1) you were chosen out of the world; (2) the world hates what is not of itself; (3) therefore the world hates you because you no longer belong to it. Persecution is not an anomaly in the Christian life; it is the expected response of a world that hated Christ.

15:22

"If I had not come and spoken to them, they would not be guilty of sin; but now they have no excuse for their sin" — the Incarnation and ministry of Jesus has changed the moral situation of the world irreversibly. Ignorance might have partially excused rejection of God before Christ came; after the Incarnation, the rejection of Jesus is rejection of the Father with full light. The greater the revelation, the greater the responsibility.

15:26

"The Spirit of truth who goes out from the Father... will testify about me" — the Paraclete's testimony is specifically about Jesus. All genuine Spirit-work points to Christ; any spiritual movement that does not testify to Jesus is not the Spirit Jesus promised. The criterion for discerning the Spirit's work is Christocentric: does it magnify Jesus?

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:18-19

Calvin warns against the two errors: (1) being surprised by persecution, as if it were unexpected; (2) being crushed by persecution, as if it were evidence of abandonment. The hatred of the world is actually evidence of belonging to Christ — it is the price paid for bearing his name. The Christian who is universally loved by the world should examine whether they have failed to bear that name clearly.

15:26

Calvin notes the trinitarian structure of v.26: the Advocate whom "I will send to you from the Father... the Spirit of truth who goes out from the Father." The Spirit is sent by the Son but proceeds from the Father — the Western church's Filioque controversy (whether the Spirit proceeds from the Father "and the Son") finds its primary Johannine basis here, where the Spirit is "sent by the Son from the Father."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:26

"The Spirit of truth who goes out from the Father" — the Greek ekporeuetai (goes out / proceeds) is the verb used in the Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed: "who proceeds from the Father." The Western addition of "and the Son" (Filioque) is based partly on this verse ("whom I will send to you from the Father") and partly on 16:7 ("I will send him"). The Eastern Orthodox church rejects the Filioque on the grounds that the creed uses only "from the Father" — a debate that has lasted from AD 589 to the present.

15:22

"They would not be guilty of sin" — Jesus is not saying they would be sinless, but that their specific guilt of rejecting the revelation of God would not exist. The Incarnation creates a new moral situation: the guilt of rejecting the Light is incurred only because the Light has come. This is the logic of greater revelation = greater responsibility.

Catena Aurea

Augustine: "A servant is not greater than his master. You who are servants must expect what your Master received. He was treated shamefully, dishonoured, persecuted, crucified. And the Son does not testify about himself but about the Father. This is the order of divine love: each testifying to the other, each glorifying the other, the whole Trinity in mutual self-giving."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The Father
The Gardener
The divine husbandman who both cuts off unfruitful branches and prunes fruitful ones. The Father's active engagement with the disciples' lives — cutting, pruning, cultivating — is the practical expression of the love Jesus describes as the standard (v.9).
Jesus
The True Vine
The vine who is himself Israel-fulfilled. The disciples' entire capacity for fruitfulness is derived from their connection to him. "Apart from me you can do nothing" (v.5) is the most radical statement of Christian dependence in the Gospel.

Reception History

"Abide in Me" in Christian Mystical Tradition
The "abiding" language of John 15 has shaped Christian mystical and contemplative theology from the earliest centuries. The Cloud of Unknowing, the Julian of Norwich's Revelations of Divine Love, Thomas à Kempis's Imitation of Christ, and the 19th-century "Higher Life" movement (Hannah Whitall Smith's The Christian's Secret of a Happy Life) all draw on the abiding metaphor as the grammar of the contemplative life. The vine and branches is the most sustained image of union with Christ in the NT.
"Greater Love Has No One Than This" in Christian Ethics
John 15:13 has been the foundational text for Christian sacrifice in service of others. It is inscribed on war memorials, used in eulogies for those who died in service, and cited in every tradition of Christian martyrology. The verse's specific context (Jesus's own death as the paradigm) means it describes not merely a moral ideal but the act that makes all subsequent self-giving possible.

Literary Structure

Abiding as Structural Principle — vv.4-10Menō appears ten times in seven verses — the most concentrated verbal repetition in John. The structure is concentric: remain in me (v.4) → bear fruit (v.5) → ask whatever you wish (v.7) → Father glorified (v.8) → remain in my love (v.9) → keep my commands (v.10). The abiding produces fruit; the fruit glorifies the Father; the love of the Father is the context of the abiding.
True Vine vs. False Israel — v.1The seventh I am saying with predicate closes the series. The series has moved from the most cosmological (I am the light of the world) through the most pastoral (I am the good shepherd) to the most relational (I am the vine, you are the branches). The final saying is not a solitary claim but a communal one — Jesus as vine is incomplete without branches.
Three-Part Vine Argument — vv.1-17The Vine discourse has a clear three-movement structure: (1) vv.1-8 — relationship between vine and branches (abiding/fruitfulness); (2) vv.9-11 — relationship between love and joy; (3) vv.12-17 — commandment to love as the fruit of abiding. Each movement deepens the prior one.

Textual Notes

15:8"Showing yourselves to be my disciples" vs "you are my disciples"
Some MSS read hina genēsthe (become) rather than kai genēsthe (and you will become / showing that you are). NA28 follows a reading that can be translated either "so that you may become my disciples" (becoming) or "thus showing/proving yourselves to be disciples" (demonstrating). The difference is between ongoing growth into discipleship and demonstrating a pre-existing status.
The textual evidence is divided; both readings make theological sense. The fruit-bearing that glorifies the Father is simultaneously what constitutes genuine discipleship.

Scholarly Debates

What happens to branches that are "cut off" in 15:2, 6?
Genuine believers who fall away (arminian)
Wesley, Arminius, many Pentecostals
The branches are genuinely "in" Christ — truly saved — but can be cut off through persistent unfruitfulness or apostasy. The warning is genuine and addressed to real believers.
False professors who were never truly saved (calvinist)
Calvin, MacArthur, most Reformed scholars
The fruitless branches had an external connection to Jesus (in the covenant community, in the church) but were never genuinely regenerate. Their removal reveals that they were never truly his (cf. 1 John 2:19).
Loss of reward, not salvation (some dispensationalists)
Chafer, some Dallas Seminary theologians
The branches represent the believer's works, not the believer's person. Unfruitful branches lose rewards at the judgment but not salvation.
No consensus. The Calvinist reading is consistent with John's wider emphasis on the perseverance of the elect (10:27-29). The Arminian reading takes the warning language more literally. The dispensationalist reading requires a non-natural reading of "branch." The warning functions regardless of which interpretation is adopted.

Theological Themes Tracker

John 15 develops two themes in sequence and connection: abiding produces fruit (vv.1-17), and fruitfulness produces hostility (vv.18-27). The vine metaphor is the most relational of Jesus's I am sayings — it describes a community, not merely an individual. The disciples' entire life (fruitfulness, prayer, joy, love) flows from the single source of abiding in Christ. And the fruitfulness that flows from abiding is precisely what makes the disciples bearers of Jesus's name in a hostile world — the world that hated him will hate them, and the Spirit will testify alongside them.
