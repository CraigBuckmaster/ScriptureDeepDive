John 1

The Word Became Flesh

Author

John the son of Zebedee — fisherman, apostle, and “the disciple whom Jesus loved” (John 13:23; 19:26; 20:2; 21:7, 20). The fourth Gospel never names its author directly, but the evidence converges on John: (1) The “beloved disciple” is an eyewitness (19:35; 21:24) present at the Last Supper, the cross, and the empty tomb. (2) Early and unanimous tradition: Irenaeus (c. AD 180), Clement of Alexandria, Origen, and Eusebius all identify the author as John the apostle writing from Ephesus in old age. (3) The Gospel’s intimate knowledge of Jerusalem topography (Pool of Bethesda, Pool of Siloam, the Pavement/Gabbatha) is confirmed by archaeology. (4) The eyewitness claim of 19:35 and the closing verse of 21:24 point to direct authorial witness. Liberal scholarship has proposed an anonymous Johannine community or the Elder John of Ephesus; the traditional identification with the apostle remains the best-attested position.

When written

c. AD 85–95, probably from Ephesus, making it the latest of the four Gospels. The Rylands Papyrus (P52, c. AD 125) — the earliest surviving NT manuscript fragment — contains John 18:31-33, 37-38, confirming the Gospel was in circulation in Egypt by the early second century. A pre-AD 70 date has been argued from the Temple language in chs 2, 5, and 10, which reads as description of a standing building; the majority of scholars favour a post-70 date for the final composition. The three Johannine letters and Revelation share vocabulary and theological concerns with the Gospel, suggesting a common Ephesian milieu.

What prompted it

John states his purpose explicitly — a rarity in the Gospels: “These are written that you may believe that Jesus is the Messiah, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name” (20:31). The Gospel was written to produce and sustain faith in the person of Jesus as divine Son. It supplements the Synoptics rather than duplicates them: John omits the Baptism, the Transfiguration, the Olivet Discourse, and the institution of the Lord’s Supper as a formal narrative, while providing material found nowhere else — the wedding at Cana, Nicodemus, the Samaritan woman, the raising of Lazarus, the Farewell Discourse (chs 14–17), and the resurrection appearances to Mary Magdalene and Thomas. Its elevated Christology (“In the beginning was the Word”) addresses late first-century challenges to the full divinity of Christ and lays the foundation for all subsequent trinitarian theology.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Jesus
The Word Made Flesh
The eternal Word (Logos) who was with God and was God, who tabernacled among humanity in the Incarnation. John's Gospel opens with the highest Christology in the NT and builds from there. Jesus's first words in John are a question: "What are you seeking?" His first self-disclosure is to Nathanael — omniscient and yet intimate.
John the Baptist
Witness to the Light
In John's Gospel, the Baptist appears not to baptise (the actual baptism is not narrated) but to testify. His function is pure witness: he is the voice, not the Word; the lamp, not the light; the friend of the bridegroom, not the bridegroom. His two-fold "Look, the Lamb of God!" (1:29, 36) is the Gospel's first and foundational Christological declaration.
Andrew
First Called; Connector
The first disciple called in John's Gospel — one of the two who heard the Baptist's testimony and followed Jesus. His immediate response to finding Jesus was to find his brother Simon: "We have found the Messiah." Andrew is consistently in John the one who brings others to Jesus (6:8-9; 12:22).
Peter (Simon)
Renamed at First Meeting
Jesus renames Simon "Cephas/Peter" at their very first meeting — a declaration of destiny before any test of character. Peter's rock-like quality is something Jesus will create, not something he already possesses.
Philip
Called Directly by Jesus
Unlike Andrew and Peter who came via the Baptist's testimony, Philip is called directly by Jesus ("Follow me," v.43) — representing the divine initiative in discipleship that does not require human mediation.
Nathanael
Israelite of Integrity
Characterised by Jesus as "an Israelite in whom there is no deceit" — the authentic Israel that the name "Jacob" (supplanter/deceiver) aspired to be. His initial scepticism ("Can anything good come from Nazareth?") gives way to the Gospel's most explicit early confession: "Son of God... King of Israel" (v.49).

Translation Comparison

1:1, 14, 29

VersionTranslation
logos (1:1)NIV: "Word"; KJV: "Word"; ESV: "Word"; NRSV: "Word". Unanimous. Translating logos as "Word" is a conventional but inadequate rendering — the Greek encompasses "Reason, Discourse, Logic, Meaning." Some translators have proposed "Meaning" (Eugene Peterson: "the Word") or "the eternal Word" (NLT).
eskēnōsen (1:14)NIV: "made his dwelling"; KJV: "dwelt"; ESV: "dwelt"; NRSV: "lived among". The Tabernacle resonance (tabernacled/pitched his tent) is lost in most translations. The NEB "came to dwell among us" and the NIV's "made his dwelling" both capture the idea of temporary residence without the Tabernacle allusion.
monogenēs (1:14, 18)NIV: "one and only"; KJV: "only begotten"; ESV: "only"; NRSV: "only". KJV's "only begotten" preserves the birth/generation nuance; modern translations prefer "only" or "unique." The theological weight is the Son's unique relationship to the Father.

Ancient Sources

Philo of Alexandria, On the Creation
"God, being good, did not grudge a share in his own excellent nature to a substance which had of itself nothing fair and lovely" — Philo's Logos mediates between the transcendent God and the created world. John takes up the Logos concept and radically personalises it: the Logos is not a principle or a heavenly intermediary but a person who became flesh.
John's use of Logos engages the most sophisticated philosophical vocabulary of his day — Hellenistic Judaism and Stoic philosophy — to make the claim that the universal Reason behind all things is a person who can be known personally.
Targum Onkelos on Genesis 1
The Aramaic translation of Genesis 1 uses "the Word of the Lord" (Memra de-YHWH) as a reverential circumlocution for God's direct action in the world. "And the Word of the Lord said, Let there be light." This Aramaic tradition provides direct Jewish background for John's "In the beginning was the Word."
The Memra tradition shows that the Logos concept was not merely Hellenistic but deeply rooted in Jewish interpretive practice, making John's prologue a claim intelligible across both cultural worlds.

Reception History

The Prologue in Christian Worship
John 1:1-14 has been read as the concluding Gospel in the Latin Mass since the early medieval period — the "Last Gospel" read at the end of every Eucharist in the extraordinary form of the Roman Rite. Luther ordered it read at the end of every German Mass. The prologue's liturgical position at the conclusion of the Mass (rather than the beginning) reflects its theological function: it is the interpretation of everything that has just happened.
"The Word Became Flesh" in Theology of the Incarnation
John 1:14 is the single most important verse in the history of Christology. The Council of Nicaea (AD 325), Chalcedon (AD 451), and every subsequent creedal formulation depends on it. The debates of the first four centuries — against Docetism (the Word only appeared to be flesh), Arianism (the Word was a created being), Apollinarianism (the Word replaced the human soul in Christ), and Nestorianism (two persons in Christ) — all turn on the precise meaning of "the Word became flesh."

Literary Structure

Inclusio — vv.1-18 / 20:28The Gospel opens with "the Word was God" (1:1) and reaches its confessional climax with Thomas's "My Lord and my God!" (20:28). The whole Gospel is bracketed by divine identity declarations.
The Seven Days — vv.19, 29, 35, 43The Baptist section is structured as a series of "next day" sequences (1:19, 29, 35, 43) and the wedding at Cana occurs "on the third day" (2:1) — possibly mapping the opening chapter onto a week-long sequence echoing the creation week.
Chiasm — vv.1-18The Prologue is structured as a descending/ascending arch: the eternal Word → incarnation → witness of John → reception/rejection → the glory revealed. The phrase "and the Word was God" (v1) answers to "we have seen his glory" (v14), framing all in between.

Textual Notes

1:18"The one and only Son" vs "God the one and only"
Significant MSS (P66, P75, Sinaiticus, Vaticanus) read monogenēs theos ("God the only-begotten" or "the one and only, who is himself God"). Later MSS read ho monogenēs huios ("the one and only Son"). NA28 follows the earlier witnesses: "the one and only Son, who is himself God." This is one of the strongest textual statements of the Son's deity in the NT.
The reading monogenēs theos is almost certainly original — it is harder (scribes would be more likely to soften it toward "Son") and better attested. The reading "Son" may have been introduced to harmonise with 3:16.
1:34"Son of God" vs "Chosen One of God"
P5, P106, Sinaiticus, and the Old Latin read ho eklektos ("the Chosen One") rather than ho huios ("the Son"). NA28 adopts "Chosen One" as the harder reading. If original, it connects to Isa 42:1 ("my chosen one") and provides a Servant-Song background for the baptism testimony.
The "Chosen One" reading is probably original; "Son of God" is a very common Johannine phrase that scribes would naturally introduce.

Scholarly Debates

Is the Prologue a Pre-existing Hymn?
Yes: hymnic adaptation
Bultmann, Brown, Schnackenburg
The prologue has a poetic, rhythmic structure distinct from the surrounding narrative, with prose insertions about John the Baptist (vv.6-8, 15). The original hymn may have been a Johannine community hymn to the Logos that the evangelist adapted.
No: unified composition
Carson, Keener, Bauckham
The supposed hymnic structure is not consistent enough to identify a discrete pre-existing hymn. The prologue is the evangelist's own introduction, written in elevated prose that reflects the content's grandeur.
The question remains open. The presence of the Baptist insertions and the poetic character of certain verses make a hymnic source plausible, but the evidence for a complete pre-existing hymn is circumstantial.

Theological Themes Tracker

John 1 is the Gospel's theological overture. The prologue (1:1-18) establishes every major theme in miniature: the divine identity of the Son, the creation through him, the entrance of light into darkness, the rejection by his own, and the gift of new birth to those who receive him. The narrative then enacts these themes immediately: the Baptist witnesses, the disciples believe, Nathanael is an Israelite without deceit — the Gospel's ideal receptor of the light. The chapter closes with the Jacob's ladder image, declaring that in Christ the permanent connection between heaven and earth has been established.
