John 10

The Good Shepherd

Author

John the son of Zebedee — fisherman, apostle, and “the disciple whom Jesus loved” (John 13:23; 19:26; 20:2; 21:7, 20). The fourth Gospel never names its author directly, but the evidence converges on John: (1) The “beloved disciple” is an eyewitness (19:35; 21:24) present at the Last Supper, the cross, and the empty tomb. (2) Early and unanimous tradition: Irenaeus (c. AD 180), Clement of Alexandria, Origen, and Eusebius all identify the author as John the apostle writing from Ephesus in old age. (3) The Gospel’s intimate knowledge of Jerusalem topography (Pool of Bethesda, Pool of Siloam, the Pavement/Gabbatha) is confirmed by archaeology. (4) The eyewitness claim of 19:35 and the closing verse of 21:24 point to direct authorial witness. Liberal scholarship has proposed an anonymous Johannine community or the Elder John of Ephesus; the traditional identification with the apostle remains the best-attested position.

When written

c. AD 85–95, probably from Ephesus, making it the latest of the four Gospels. The Rylands Papyrus (P52, c. AD 125) — the earliest surviving NT manuscript fragment — contains John 18:31-33, 37-38, confirming the Gospel was in circulation in Egypt by the early second century. A pre-AD 70 date has been argued from the Temple language in chs 2, 5, and 10, which reads as description of a standing building; the majority of scholars favour a post-70 date for the final composition. The three Johannine letters and Revelation share vocabulary and theological concerns with the Gospel, suggesting a common Ephesian milieu.

What prompted it

John states his purpose explicitly — a rarity in the Gospels: “These are written that you may believe that Jesus is the Messiah, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name” (20:31). The Gospel was written to produce and sustain faith in the person of Jesus as divine Son. It supplements the Synoptics rather than duplicates them: John omits the Baptism, the Transfiguration, the Olivet Discourse, and the institution of the Lord’s Supper as a formal narrative, while providing material found nowhere else — the wedding at Cana, Nicodemus, the Samaritan woman, the raising of Lazarus, the Farewell Discourse (chs 14–17), and the resurrection appearances to Mary Magdalene and Thomas. Its elevated Christology (“In the beginning was the Word”) addresses late first-century challenges to the full divinity of Christ and lays the foundation for all subsequent trinitarian theology.

Verses 1–21 — The Good Shepherd Discourse

Greek Word Study

ho poimēn ho kalos ho poi-MEN ho ka-LOS — the good shepherd: Kalos means not merely morally good (agathos) but beautiful, noble, genuine, ideal — the shepherd who perfectly embodies what a shepherd should be. The OT is saturated with shepherd imagery for God (Ps 23; Ezek 34), Israel's leaders (Jer 23:1-4; Ezek 34:2-10), and the coming Messianic King (Ezek 34:23-24; Mic 5:4). Jesus claims this entire tradition for himself.

tithēmi tēn psychēn ti-THE-mi tēn psy-CHEN — lay down my life: The phrase occurs five times in the discourse (vv.11, 15, 17, 18 × 2). Tithēmi means to place, set down, deposit — a deliberate, voluntary act. Jesus does not say "lose" or "forfeit" his life but "lay it down" — as a deposit, with the intention of taking it up again (v.18). The voluntary, purposive character of the death is the discourse's central theological claim.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:10

"The thief comes only to steal and kill and destroy; I have come that they may have life, and have it to the full" — the contrast between the thieves and the good shepherd is made in terms of purpose and outcome. The thief's purpose is extraction and destruction; Jesus's purpose is life in abundance (perissōs — exceeding, overflowing). This is John's most explicit statement of the positive purpose of the Incarnation: not merely rescue from destruction but the gift of overflowing life.

10:11

"The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep" — hyper tōn probatōn: on behalf of, in place of the sheep. The preposition hyper carries substitutionary weight: the shepherd dies so the sheep may live. This is the atonement stated in shepherd language, consistent with "Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world" (1:29).

10:17-18

"I lay down my life — only to take it up again... I have authority to lay it down and authority to take it up again" — the sovereign freedom of the cross. Jesus is not a victim of circumstances; he is the sovereign agent of his own death. His authority over his death is simultaneously authority over his resurrection. The Father's love (v.17) is provoked by this voluntary self-giving — not as its condition but as its response.

10:16

"There shall be one flock and one shepherd" — the eschatological gathering of all peoples under the one shepherd. This is not the abolition of ethnic identity but its transcendence. The "one flock" is not uniformity but unity — the diverse peoples gathered by the one voice into the one community.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:11

Calvin develops the substitutionary dimension of "laying down his life for the sheep": the shepherd's death is not merely exemplary (showing how to be self-sacrificing) but vicarious (dying in place of those who deserve to die). The shepherd enters the danger zone and takes the wolf's attack on himself so that the sheep may escape.

10:27-28

Though this passage is in the second section, Calvin's treatment of the whole discourse emphasises the security of the elect: those whom the Father gives to the Son will come; those who come will not be cast out; none of those the Father gives will be lost. The triple security (the Father gives, the Son receives, the Son loses none) is the foundation of assurance.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:8

"All who have ever come before me are thieves and robbers" — this does not mean the OT prophets and leaders were thieves. The context (the Pharisees who just expelled the blind man) and the subsequent development (the hired hand) suggest "those who come without proper authority," specifically the false shepherds of Ezek 34 and any would-be messiahs who do not enter through the proper gate (divine commission).

10:16

"One flock and one shepherd" — NIV correctly reads "one flock" (mia poimnē); KJV "one fold" reflects a Latin translation error (una ovile = one sheepfold, rather than unum grex = one flock). The distinction matters: one flock suggests a single community of diverse people; one fold suggests a single institutional enclosure.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
10:11

Chrysostom: "The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep. What shepherd ever did this? No human shepherd, however devoted, died for his sheep. But this shepherd, to save not sheep but people — who were worth more and who had wandered further — gave his own life. This is love beyond all human precedent."

10:16

Augustine: "Other sheep I have, not of this fold. The Jewish people were this fold; the Gentiles are the other sheep. When will they become one flock? When the wall of partition is broken down (Eph 2:14). The shepherd is one; his voice is one; the pasture is one. The fold is different; the flock is one."

Verses 22–42 — Feast of Dedication; I and the Father Are One

Greek Word Study

hen esmen hen ES-men — we are one: The neuter hen (one thing, one reality) rather than the masculine heis (one person) — Jesus is not claiming to be the Father but claiming a unity of essence, purpose, and will with the Father. The opponents' response (stoning for blasphemy, v.31; "you claim to be God," v.33) confirms they heard a claim to divine nature, not mere agreement of purpose. The statement is the ontological basis for all the "I do what the Father does" language of ch.5.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 82:6"I said, 'You are gods; you are all sons of the Most High'" — the Psalm Jesus cites in vv.34-35, arguing a fortiori: if judges exercising divine authority were called gods, how much more the one the Father actually consecrated and sent into the world.
  • Deut 6:4"Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one" — the Shema, the foundational Jewish monotheism statement, which Jesus's "I and the Father are one" simultaneously affirms and complicates.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:27-28

"My sheep listen to my voice; I know them, and they follow me. I give them eternal life, and they shall never perish; no one will snatch them out of my hand" — the basis of assurance in John. Three dimensions: (1) identification — "I know them"; (2) security — "they shall never perish"; (3) protection — "no one will snatch them." The promise is absolute: ou mē apolōntai (double negative — they will absolutely not perish, ever).

10:29-30

The double security — no one can snatch from the Son's hand (v.28) AND no one can snatch from the Father's hand (v.29) — followed by "I and the Father are one" (v.30) reveals the logic: the double security is grounded in the unity. Because the Son and the Father are one, the one who is held by the Son is simultaneously held by the Father. The security is trinitarian.

10:33-36

Jesus's response to the blasphemy charge uses a qal wa-chomer (lesser to greater) argument from Ps 82:6. If the OT called human judges "gods" in a limited sense, and if "Scripture cannot be set aside" (v.35 — a remarkable aside affirming biblical authority), then the one uniquely consecrated and sent by God can certainly be called "Son of God" without blasphemy. The argument is not retracting the claim — it is demonstrating its consistency with Scripture.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:30

Calvin uses "I and the Father are one" as the foundational verse for trinitarian theology: the unity of substance (one) alongside the distinction of persons (I... the Father). He argues against both Sabellian confusion of persons (they are not the same person) and Arian subordination of substance (they share the same divine essence). The statement is the most economical expression of orthodox Trinitarianism in the NT.

10:34-36

Calvin notes that Jesus's appeal to Scripture ("Scripture cannot be set aside," v.35) is itself a significant authority claim: Jesus treats Scripture as self-evidently binding even on his opponents. The argument from Ps 82 is not a retreat from the divine claim but a demonstration that the Jewish Scripture itself used divine language for lesser figures — making the claim for the uniquely consecrated Son entirely consistent.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:22

The Festival of Dedication (Hanukkah) occurs in November/December — hence "it was winter" (v.22). This places the discourse roughly two months after the Feast of Tabernacles discourses of chs 7-9. The setting in Solomon's Colonnade on the eastern side of the Temple Mount is mentioned twice (10:23; Acts 3:11; 5:12) — a well-known gathering place in first-century Jerusalem.

10:36

"The one whom the Father set apart as his very own" — hēgiasen, from hagiazō: to consecrate, sanctify, set apart. The word is related to hagios (holy/saint) and to the concept of dedication/consecration behind Hanukkah itself. Jesus claims to be the one truly "dedicated" — the fulfilment of the feast's theme of Temple rededication.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
10:30

Augustine: "I and the Father are one. One what? One God, one substance, one nature. Not one person — for the Father is the Father and the Son is the Son. But one God, one divine substance, one omnipotence. And this is why those who tried to stone him were not wrong to understand it as a claim to be God — they were wrong only to reject it."

10:28

Chrysostom: "They shall never perish — not merely 'it is unlikely they will perish' or 'it is difficult for them to perish' — but 'they shall never perish.' This is certainty, not probability. And why? Because I give them eternal life — the life of God himself. Can God's life be taken away?"

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Jesus
The Good Shepherd, Gate, and Son
Three self-descriptions in one discourse: the Gate (v.7, 9), the Good Shepherd (v.11, 14), and the one who is one with the Father (v.30). Each deepens the previous: the gate provides access; the shepherd provides life; the unity with the Father grounds the eternal security of those who enter and are gathered.

Reception History

The Good Shepherd in Christian Art and Devotion
The Good Shepherd is the most common image of Jesus in the early church's art — appearing in the Roman catacombs from the 2nd century onward, always depicted carrying a sheep on his shoulders. The image was preferred over crucifixion imagery in the early centuries because it conveyed grace and salvation without the scandal of the cross to pagan onlookers. The shepherd image has shaped Christian pastoral theology: the word "pastor" (Latin: shepherd) derives directly from this discourse.
"I and the Father Are One" in Trinitarian Theology
John 10:30 is the single most important verse in the NT for the Council of Nicaea's formulation of trinitarian theology. Athanasius's argument that the Son is homoousios (of one substance/essence) with the Father rests heavily on this verse. The Arian alternative (the Son is homoiousios — of similar but not identical substance) was rejected at Nicaea as failing to account for the full force of hen esmen.

Literary Structure

Gate and Shepherd — vv.7-11Jesus offers two consecutive I am sayings: I am the gate (v.7, 9) and I am the good shepherd (v.11, 14). The two images are complementary: the gate controls access; the shepherd provides care. Together they describe the complete provision of the messianic community: entry and sustenance, protection and life.
Door/Shepherd Duality — vv.7-18Jesus applies two distinct metaphors: Gate (vv.7-10) emphasizes access and security; Shepherd (vv.11-18) emphasizes sacrifice and intimacy. The movement from gate to shepherd mirrors John's broader pattern of Jesus first as the threshold (the way in) and then as the guide within.

Textual Notes

10:29"My Father who has given them to me is greater than all"
Significant textual variant: some MSS read "what my Father has given me is greater than all" (neuter ho, referring to the gift); others read "my Father who has given them to me is greater than all" (masculine hos, referring to the Father). NA28 follows the latter as better attested. Either reading supports the ultimate security argument.
The Father-as-subject reading (masculine relative pronoun) is better attested and makes the security argument more explicitly trinitarian.

Scholarly Debates

"I and the Father are one" — unity of essence or unity of purpose?
Unity of essence/nature (orthodox Trinitarianism)
Athanasius, Calvin, MacArthur, most orthodox interpreters
The neuter hen asserts a unity of divine substance. The opponents' immediate response (stoning for blasphemy, "you claim to be God," v.33) confirms a divine nature claim. Jesus does not deny the charge; he defends the claim's consistency with Scripture.
Unity of purpose/will only
Arianism, Jehovah's Witnesses, some liberal scholars
Hen refers to unity of purpose and mission, not ontological identity. The Father and Son work together as one. This reading cannot account for the stoning response or for John's prologue ("the Word was God").
Both — inseparable
Most mainstream NT scholars
The unity of purpose (everything the Son does the Father does, ch.5) and the unity of essence are not separable in John's theology. The hen statement encompasses both, grounded in the ontological.
The ontological reading is overwhelmingly supported by the context (stoning for blasphemy), the grammar (neuter asserts quality), and the prologue's "the Word was God." The purpose-only reading cannot adequately explain the opponents' response.

Theological Themes Tracker

John 10 gives Jesus his most pastoral self-description (Good Shepherd) and his most ontologically dense claim (I and the Father are one). The two are inseparable: the shepherd's ability to give unloseable eternal life (vv.27-29) is grounded in his unity with the Father (v.30). The discourse moves from the image of the shepherd's care to the metaphysical ground of that care: the sheep are secure because the Shepherd is one with God.
