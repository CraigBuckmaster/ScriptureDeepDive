John 5

The Healing at the Pool and the Son's Authority

Author

John the son of Zebedee — fisherman, apostle, and “the disciple whom Jesus loved” (John 13:23; 19:26; 20:2; 21:7, 20). The fourth Gospel never names its author directly, but the evidence converges on John: (1) The “beloved disciple” is an eyewitness (19:35; 21:24) present at the Last Supper, the cross, and the empty tomb. (2) Early and unanimous tradition: Irenaeus (c. AD 180), Clement of Alexandria, Origen, and Eusebius all identify the author as John the apostle writing from Ephesus in old age. (3) The Gospel’s intimate knowledge of Jerusalem topography (Pool of Bethesda, Pool of Siloam, the Pavement/Gabbatha) is confirmed by archaeology. (4) The eyewitness claim of 19:35 and the closing verse of 21:24 point to direct authorial witness. Liberal scholarship has proposed an anonymous Johannine community or the Elder John of Ephesus; the traditional identification with the apostle remains the best-attested position.

When written

c. AD 85–95, probably from Ephesus, making it the latest of the four Gospels. The Rylands Papyrus (P52, c. AD 125) — the earliest surviving NT manuscript fragment — contains John 18:31-33, 37-38, confirming the Gospel was in circulation in Egypt by the early second century. A pre-AD 70 date has been argued from the Temple language in chs 2, 5, and 10, which reads as description of a standing building; the majority of scholars favour a post-70 date for the final composition. The three Johannine letters and Revelation share vocabulary and theological concerns with the Gospel, suggesting a common Ephesian milieu.

What prompted it

John states his purpose explicitly — a rarity in the Gospels: “These are written that you may believe that Jesus is the Messiah, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name” (20:31). The Gospel was written to produce and sustain faith in the person of Jesus as divine Son. It supplements the Synoptics rather than duplicates them: John omits the Baptism, the Transfiguration, the Olivet Discourse, and the institution of the Lord’s Supper as a formal narrative, while providing material found nowhere else — the wedding at Cana, Nicodemus, the Samaritan woman, the raising of Lazarus, the Farewell Discourse (chs 14–17), and the resurrection appearances to Mary Magdalene and Thomas. Its elevated Christology (“In the beginning was the Word”) addresses late first-century challenges to the full divinity of Christ and lays the foundation for all subsequent trinitarian theology.

Verses 1–15 — The Pool of Bethesda: Rise and Walk

Greek Word Study

theleis hygiēs genesthai THE-leis hy-GI-ēs ge-NES-thai — Do you want to get well?: Jesus's question to the man (v.6) is not therapeutic conversation — it is a theological penetration. Thirty-eight years of illness shapes identity; the question asks whether the man wants the identity change that healing brings. The man's answer reveals he does not yet understand: he thinks in terms of getting into the pool faster, not of a word that can heal without the pool.

ison heautou poiōn tō theō I-son heau-TOU poi-ON to the-O — making himself equal with God: The Jewish leaders' charge (v.18) is not a misunderstanding — it is the correct inference from Jesus's claim. To call God "my Father" in the specific sense Jesus means (v.17 — doing what the Father does, working as the Father works) is to claim a unique divine Sonship that constitutes equality with God. The charge that would eventually condemn Jesus (19:7) is first formulated here.

Places

Pool of Bethesda
31.7810° N, 35.2367° E
Near the Sheep Gate (northeast of the Temple Mount), the Pool of Bethesda was a large double-pool complex used for ritual purification. Excavations since 1888 have confirmed John's description precisely: two pools separated by a rock-cut partition, with four outer colonnades and one central colonnade producing exactly five porticoes. The pool is now visible beneath the Church of Saint Anne in Jerusalem. This is one of the most important archaeological confirmations of John's Gospel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:6

"Do you want to get well?" — the question exposes the man's helplessness but also tests his orientation. His answer reveals he is focused on method (the pool, being helped in) rather than person. He does not ask Jesus for help — he explains why the usual method hasn't worked. Jesus bypasses the method entirely and addresses the man directly. Salvation works the same way: it comes not through our performance of the right ritual at the right moment but through direct encounter with the person of Christ.

5:14

"Stop sinning or something worse may happen to you" — Jesus does not teach here that illness is always the consequence of specific sin (he explicitly rejects that in 9:3). But he recognises that in this man's case there was a connection, and the healing requires moral as well as physical response. The warning is pastoral, not juridical.

5:17

"My Father is always at his work to this very day, and I too am working" — a stunning Sabbath claim. Jewish theology held that God continued to sustain creation on the Sabbath (providential upholding is not work that violates the Sabbath). Jesus claims to do exactly what the Father does — including on the Sabbath. The logic of his opponents is correct: this is equality with God. Jesus does not deny it; he develops it in the discourse that follows.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:14

Calvin uses "stop sinning or something worse may happen to you" to argue that physical suffering can serve as divine chastisement in individual cases, without being a universal principle (as Jesus explicitly denies in 9:3). The pastoral wisdom is: when suffering comes, it is worth examining one's life — not because all suffering is punitive but because sometimes it is, and the examination has value regardless.

5:17-18

The Jewish leaders correctly infer that Jesus is claiming equality with God — and they correctly identify this as the logical consequence of his "my Father" language. Calvin notes that Jesus does not dispute their inference; he confirms it and develops it in the discourse. The equality is real, not metaphorical.

Verses 16–30 — The Son’s Authority: Equal with the Father

Greek Word Study

zōopoieō zo-o-poi-EH-o — give life / make alive: The verb (v.21) appears six times in John 5 and is one of John's most theologically concentrated words. The exclusive divine prerogative to give life — exercised by the Father in resurrection — is now delegated to the Son. This is not a general claim to healing but a claim to the same life-giving power that reverses death itself.

martyia mar-ty-RI-a — testimony / witness: The word and its cognates appear 9 times in vv.31-40, structuring the passage as a formal legal argument. Jesus presents four witnesses to his identity: (1) John the Baptist (vv.33-35); (2) his works (v.36); (3) the Father's own testimony (vv.37-38); (4) the Scriptures/Moses (vv.39-47). The passage is structured as a legal deposition responding to the charge of making himself equal with God.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:19-20

"The Son can do nothing by himself; he can do only what he sees his Father doing." This is not a statement of limitation but of perfect unity of will and action. The Son does not act independently because he does not want to: his will is perfectly aligned with the Father's. The analogy is of an apprentice who learns by watching the master — except that the apprentice here has perfect knowledge and perfect conformity. The relationship is one of love (v.20), not merely hierarchy.

5:24

"Whoever hears my word and believes him who sent me has eternal life and will not be judged but has crossed over from death to life" — the perfect tense "has crossed over" (metabebēken) indicates a completed transition: the believer is already on the life side of the death/life divide. Judgment is not a future threat for the one who believes; the verdict has already been given. This is John's consistent realised eschatology.

5:39-40

"You study the Scriptures diligently because you think that in them you have eternal life. These are the very Scriptures that testify about me, yet you refuse to come to me to have life." One of the most searching statements in the Gospel: the Bible does not itself give life — it points to the one who does. The Pharisees had the Scriptures and missed the person the Scriptures pointed to. Scripture is the signpost; Jesus is the destination.

5:45-47

"Your accuser is Moses" — the irony is devastating. The religious leaders claim Moses as their authority for rejecting Jesus; Jesus says Moses is their accuser because Moses wrote about him. The Scriptures they weaponise against Jesus are the Scriptures that, rightly read, convict them of rejecting the one Moses anticipated.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:19

Calvin uses the Son's dependence on the Father ("can do nothing by himself") to argue against any subordinationist reading: the Son's unity of action with the Father is not the obedience of an inferior but the perfect harmony of equal persons in the Godhead. The Son does what he sees the Father doing not because he must but because he wills exactly what the Father wills.

5:39-40

Calvin's most important use of this passage: Scripture is not itself the final authority but the witness to the final authority. Those who make Scripture itself the object of faith have committed the same error as the Pharisees. Scripture's purpose is to lead to Christ; when it is treated as an end in itself, it fails its own purpose.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:28-29

"All who are in their graves will hear his voice" — a future resurrection of all, both righteous and wicked. This is one of the clearest statements of a general bodily resurrection in John, alongside an explicit final judgment based on deeds. It stands alongside the realised eschatology of v.24-25, both being true: eternal life is present now for believers (realised); the final resurrection is still future (futurist).

5:44

"How can you believe since you accept glory from one another but do not seek the glory that comes from the only God?" — the social and psychological analysis of unbelief: the desire for human approval creates a structural barrier to faith. This analysis anticipates the secret believers among the Pharisees who would not confess Jesus "for fear they would be put out of the synagogue; for they loved human praise more than praise from God" (12:42-43).

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas (compiler), Catena Aurea — Patristic Commentary, Public Domain
5:39-40

Augustine: "They had the Scriptures and in the Scriptures they had the testimony to Christ. But they would not come to him. Why? Because they loved the praise of men more than the glory of God (5:44). The Scripture is the letter; Christ is the spirit. To read Scripture without coming to Christ is to handle the lamp without seeing the light."

5:24

Chrysostom: "He has crossed over from death to life — past tense, completed action. Not 'will cross over' but 'has crossed over.' The transition is not at death but at faith. From the moment of believing, eternal life is possessed; the judgment that all fear has already been passed for the one who believes.

Reception History

"You Search the Scriptures" in Hermeneutical Debate
John 5:39-40 has been a touchstone in debates about the proper use of Scripture. The Reformation principle of sola scriptura has sometimes been misread as making Scripture itself the final object of faith; 5:39-40 corrects this by insisting that Scripture points beyond itself to Christ. Karl Barth's theology of revelation — Scripture as witness to revelation rather than revelation itself — draws heavily on this Johannine logic.

Literary Structure

Legal Proceeding Structure — vv.31-47The section is structured as a formal legal defence: charge (v.18: making himself equal with God), Jesus's response to the charge (vv.19-30), presentation of witnesses (vv.31-47). John consistently frames controversy as courtroom drama — the whole Gospel can be read as a trial in which Jesus is simultaneously defendant, witness, and judge.
Sabbath Frame — vv.1-18 and the LawThe healing on the Sabbath is not incidental but structural: it triggers the legal dispute that dominates the chapter. John frames Jesus's defense (vv.17-47) as a formal legal proceeding with witnesses (the Father, John, works, Scripture) — patterned after Hebrew trial rhetoric.

Scholarly Debates

Realised vs. Future Eschatology in John 5
Primarily realised
Dodd, Bultmann
The emphasis in John is on present possession: "has eternal life," "has crossed over from death to life" (v.24). Future resurrection language (vv.28-29) may be a later ecclesiastical addition to John's original text.
Both present and future, complementary
Carson, Ridderbos, Keener
Both dimensions are authentically Johannine and authentically dominical. Present realisation of eternal life (v.24-25) and future bodily resurrection (v.28-29) are both affirmed; they describe different aspects of the same salvation. The Gospel maintains this both/and throughout.
The two-eschatologies view (both realised and futurist) is strongly preferred by mainstream scholarship. Attempts to excise vv.28-29 as secondary have no manuscript support. Both dimensions of eschatology are integral to John's theology.

Theological Themes Tracker

John 5 develops the "equal with God" charge of 5:18 into the most explicit Christological discourse in the Gospel so far. The Son gives life (as the Father gives life), exercises judgment (as the Father exercises judgment), and receives honor (as the Father receives honor). These three divine prerogatives — life, judgment, honor — constitute the argument: Jesus is not merely a great teacher or prophet but the one who does what only God does. The chapter ends with a devastating reversal: the accusers' own scriptures and their own Moses accuse them.
