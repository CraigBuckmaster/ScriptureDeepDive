The Olivet Discourse: Signs, Tribulation, and the Son of Man

Verses 1–13 — The Temple’s Destruction; Signs of the End; Persecution

Greek Word Study

bdelygma erēmōseōs bdel-YG-ma er-ay-MO-se-os — abomination that causes desolation: When you see the abomination that causes desolation — the phrase from Dan 9:27; 11:31; 12:11. Originally referring to Antiochus Epiphanes’ desecration of the Temple in 167 BC. Jesus applies it to a coming desecration that will trigger the decisive crisis. Context

The Olivet Discourse is Jesus’s extended eschatological teaching, prompted by the disciples’ amazement at the Temple’s grandeur. Jesus’s prediction that not one stone will be left (v.2) was fulfilled in AD 70 when Titus destroyed Jerusalem. The discourse weaves together near-term events (the Jerusalem siege) and far-term events (the Son of Man’s coming) in a way that has produced sustained interpretive disagreement.

Cross-Reference

  • Dan 9:27; 11:31The abomination that causes desolation — Daniel’s language for Antiochus Epiphanes’ desecration, re-applied by Jesus to a future event.
  • Dan 12:1A time of distress such as has not happened from the beginning of nations — the language Jesus echoes in v.19.
  • Deut 13:1-3If a prophet performs signs and wonders… do not listen — the basis for Jesus’s warning about false prophets who perform miracles.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:2

Marcus: "Not one stone will be left on another" — Josephus confirms that Titus ordered the Temple stones thrown down to recover the gold that had melted between them in the fire of AD 70 (Jewish War 7.1-3). The prediction is thus precisely fulfilled. Marcus argues that the discourse is not a post-eventum prophecy inserted after AD 70 but an authentic prediction: the general expectation of Temple destruction (cf. Micah 3:12, which already predicted it once) was available to any prophet in the Second Temple period.

13:14

Marcus: "The abomination that causes desolation" (bdelygma tēs erēmōseōs) is cited from Dan 9:27 and 11:31. In Daniel the phrase refers to Antiochus IV Epiphanes' desecration in 167 BC. Jesus applies the same Danielic language to a future desecration. Marcus argues that the masculine participle "standing" (hestēkota) points to a person rather than an object — possibly the Roman standards (which carried divine images) entering the Temple courts in AD 70, or the emperor Caligula's attempted statue installation in AD 40.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:1-23

Rhoads: The Olivet Discourse begins with an architectural observation by an unnamed disciple ("What massive stones!") and ends with a command to all ("So be on your guard"). The narrative moves from wonder at the visible to urgency about the invisible. Rhoads notes that the disciples' four private questions (Peter, James, John, Andrew — the first four called) frame the discourse as insider teaching: the Olivet Discourse is the final installment of the private explanation pattern that began with the parable explanation in 4:10-20.

13:9-13

Rhoads: "You will be handed over to local councils... you will stand before governors and kings as witnesses to them" — the narrator's Jesus here speaks prophetically into the future of the narrative's implied audience. The verb "handed over" (paradidōmi) links the disciples' fate to Jesus's own fate (9:31; 14:10, 11, 18, 21, 41, 42, 44). The disciples' mission and suffering will be a continuation and extension of Jesus's own delivery into hostile hands. The audience is told this before experiencing it.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
13:1-13

The Olivet Discourse begins with the disciples' admiration of the Temple stones — and Jesus's prediction of their total destruction. Chrysostom notes that the question "When will these things happen?" leads to a discourse that does not primarily answer it, but redirects attention to how to live in the uncertainty between present and consummation. The birth pains metaphor (v8) is, for Origen, an image of creative suffering: the end is not destruction but a new birth. The promise "the one who stands firm to the end will be saved" (v13) is, for Bede, the thesis of the entire discourse.

13:14-23

The "abomination of desolation" (v14) is interpreted in the Catena tradition through Daniel 9:27, 11:31, 12:11. Chrysostom reads the immediate reference as the Roman destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) and the ultimate reference as the end-time desecration. The instruction to flee to the mountains without returning for possessions is, for Origen, both a practical warning to the Jerusalem church (historically heeded in the flight to Pella) and a spiritual instruction: when the final crisis comes, no earthly attachment must delay escape. The warning against false messiahs (vv.21-22) is, for Bede, the permanent pastoral application.

13:7

Such things must happen — Calvin: dei (must) is the language of divine sovereignty. The wars, famines, and earthquakes are not accidents but are within the providential order. This does not make them good but makes them purposeful.

grēgoreō greh-go-REH-oh — keep watch/be alert: Watch! — grēgoreite. The final command of the discourse, addressed to everyone (v.37). Context

The second half of the Olivet Discourse moves from near-term signs (Jerusalem’s destruction) to the ultimate coming of the Son of Man. The crucial verse is 13:32 — no one knows the day or hour, not even the Son. The response to this uncertainty is not calculation but watchfulness. The four watch-times in v.35 correspond to the four watches of the night — and to the four watch-times of the Passion narrative (evening, midnight, cock-crow, dawn).

Cross-Reference

  • Dan 7:13-14The Son of Man coming with the clouds of heaven — the Danielic parousia that Jesus describes in v.26.
  • Isa 13:10The sun will be darkened, the moon will not give its light — the OT cosmic-sign language Jesus uses for the eschatological event.
  • Zech 2:6I scattered you to the four winds of heaven — the gathering of the elect from the four winds (v.27) reverses the scatter.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:26

Marcus: "The Son of Man coming in clouds with great power and glory" is the fullest citation of Dan 7:13-14 in the Synoptics. Marcus argues that in its Danielic context, the Son of Man's "coming" is a coming to the Ancient of Days to receive dominion — an ascent to the divine throne, not a descent to earth. The clouds are the vehicle of divine transport in both directions. Mark may intend the language to describe the vindication/exaltation of the Son of Man (visible at the resurrection and Pentecost) rather than a singular end-of-history Parousia.

13:32

Marcus: "Nor the Son" — Marcus identifies this as the strongest kenosis statement in the Synoptic tradition. The gradation (angels, then the Son) emphasizes that even the Son, in his incarnate state, has a limit of eschatological knowledge. Marcus connects this to Phil 2:7 (the self-emptying) and argues it is evidence of authenticity rather than a problem to explain away: no early church theologian would have invented a saying that appeared to limit the Son's knowledge.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:24-37

Rhoads: The second half of the discourse shifts register from description to command: "Watch out" (blepete, 13:5, 9, 23, 33). The discourse ends with the word "Watch!" (grēgoreite, 13:37) addressed not to the four disciples but to all — the only place in the Gospel where Jesus breaks out of the story-world to address the narrative audience directly. Rhoads argues this is the narrator's most transparent intrusion: Jesus's command to watch becomes the narrator's command to the reader.

13:35

Rhoads: The four watch-times (evening, midnight, cock-crow, dawn) correspond structurally to the four stages of the Passion narrative that immediately follows: Last Supper (evening), Gethsemane (midnight), Peter's denial (cock-crow), morning trial (dawn). Rhoads identifies this as anticipatory foreshadowing: the disciples are told to watch at precisely the hours when the narrative will show them failing to watch. The Olivet Discourse is a double-coded text — prediction of cosmic events and prediction of the disciples' own immediate failure.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
13:24-31

The cosmic signs following the tribulation are, for Chrysostom, not astronomical events but prophetic images of total disruption drawn from Isaiah 13:10 and 34:4 — the "stars falling" is the language of empire collapse and divine judgment on world-powers. The coming of the Son of Man in clouds (v26) is, for Origen, the fulfilment of Daniel 7:13-14: the vindication of the persecuted and the establishment of the eternal kingdom. The parable of the fig tree is, for Bede, a comfort not a threat — when the signs begin, the end is near, which means the redemption is near.

13:32-37

The saying "about that day or hour no one knows, not even the angels in heaven, nor the Son, but only the Father" has generated extensive Catena commentary. Chrysostom and Ambrose both interpret it as a statement about the Son in his incarnate, kenotic condition — in his capacity as the one sent, not the sender. Origen adds that this saying is the foundation of Christian watchfulness: the ignorance is not an invitation to apathy but the ground of perpetual readiness. The parable of the absent master (vv.34-36) closes the discourse with a single imperative that applies to all generations: Watch.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:32

No one knows, not even the Son — the most theologically challenging statement in the discourse. Jesus subordinates his own knowledge to the Father’s. This is not a denial of omniscience but a statement about the kenotic limitations of the incarnate Son’s knowledge in his earthly state.

13:37

What I say to you, I say to everyone: Watch! — the discourse that began with a private question from four disciples ends with a universal command. The watchfulness required in the last days is the posture of every Christian in every generation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:32

No one knows, not even the Son — Calvin: the Son’s not knowing is appropriate to his role as mediator in his humiliated state. It is not a denial of his divine nature but a statement about his incarnate condition. Calvin reads it as an expression of the servant-form (Phil 2:7) that defined his earthly ministry.

13:37

Watch! — Calvin: the command is not merely about the Second Coming but about the posture of the Christian life. Every moment has the character of the last days. The watching required for Christ’s return is the same attentiveness required for all faithful living.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:30

This generation (genea hautē) — the most debated phrase in the discourse. In Mark genea consistently refers to the contemporary generation (8:12, 38; 9:19). Most of the signs in vv.5-23 fit the events of AD 66-70. The parousia signs in vv.24-27 are on a different scale.

13:35

Evening, midnight, cock-crow, dawn — the four Roman night watches. The four watches correspond structurally to the four stages of the Passion narrative: the Last Supper (evening), Gethsemane (midnight), Peter’s denial (cock-crow), the morning trial (dawn). Mark’s structural artistry is at work here.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Jewish War 6.312-313
Josephus describes the signs and omens that preceded the fall of Jerusalem in AD 70, including a comet, apparitions of armies in the sky, and the opening of the Temple gates at night.
Confirms the apocalyptic atmosphere surrounding the AD 70 events and provides historical context for Mark 13’s predictions.
1 Enoch 62:5-7
The Son of Man seated on the throne of glory, judging the nations — a Second Temple text using Danielic Son of Man imagery.
Shows that Jesus’s Son of Man coming language drew on an existing tradition of eschatological expectation that his audience would have recognized.

Reception History

Albert Schweitzer, The Quest of the Historical Jesus (1906)
Schweitzer argued that Mark 13 shows Jesus was a failed apocalyptic prophet who expected the immediate end of the world. His prediction was not fulfilled.
N.T. Wright, Jesus and the Victory of God (1996)
Wright argued that Mark 13’s “coming of the Son of Man” refers not to the Second Coming but to the vindication of Jesus and the fall of Jerusalem — fulfilled in AD 70. The cosmic language is Jewish apocalyptic rhetoric, not literal cosmology.

Textual Notes

13:2"These great buildings" — and the Temple mount
No significant textual variation in v.2; the prediction is stable across all manuscript traditions.
The absence of textual dispute is remarkable given the prediction's political sensitivity and the date of fulfilment (AD 70). It was not softened or clarified by copyists.
13:14"Standing where it does not belong" — masculine or neuter participle
NA28/UBS5 reads hestēkota (masculine: standing — referring to a person); some MSS read hestēkos (neuter: standing, referring to an object). Matthew 24:15 has the neuter with the explicit mention of Daniel.
The masculine participle (hestēkota) is the more difficult reading and likely original to Mark: it points to a person, making the identification with the Roman general or emperor more plausible than a mere object.

Scholarly Debates

Does “this generation” refer to AD 70 or to a future generation?
AD 70 view
Genea consistently means the contemporary generation in Mark. The signs of vv.5-23 fit the Jewish War of AD 66-70. The prediction was fulfilled within a generation of Jesus’s lifetime.
Near-term fulfilment
Future generation view
“This generation” refers to the generation that sees the specific signs of vv.14-22 begin. The ultimate parousia (vv.24-27) was not fulfilled in AD 70.
Future eschatological generation
"This generation will certainly not pass away until all these things have happened" requires that at least some of “these things” occurred within a generation. The fall of Jerusalem provides partial fulfilment; the parousia awaits ultimate fulfilment.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 13 is the eschatology chapter: the Temple’s destruction and the Son of Man’s coming are two horizons of the same divine judgment. The disciple’s response to both is watchfulness.
