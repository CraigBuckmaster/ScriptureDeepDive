Mark 11

Triumphal Entry; Fig Tree; Temple Cleansed; Authority Questioned

Verses 1–11 — The Triumphal Entry into Jerusalem
1234567891011

Greek Word Study

Hōsanna hoh-SAN-nah — save now! / please save: Hosanna — from the Hebrew hoshiʻa-na: “save now!” It is a cry from Psalm 118:25. What began as a liturgical plea for divine rescue became a shout of messianic welcome. The crowd is greeting the one they believe will bring salvation — but their understanding of what salvation means will be exposed within a week.

Places

Bethphage and Bethany
Villages on the eastern slope of the Mount of Olives, c.3km from Jerusalem
The entry begins on the eastern slope of the Mount of Olives — the traditional approach to Jerusalem from Jericho. Zechariah 14:4 locates the Messiah’s coming on the Mount of Olives. Jesus’s deliberate use of this route is a geographical claim.
Mount of Olives
Hill east of Jerusalem, overlooking the Temple mount across the Kidron Valley
The Mount of Olives commands a view of the Temple courts. Jesus’s approach from the east, down the olive-covered slope, into the Kidron Valley and up to the Temple, is the route of royal processional. The geography makes the claim visible.
Jerusalem — Temple courts
The city and its central sanctuary
Jesus enters the city and goes directly to the Temple — not to Herod’s palace, not to Pilate’s praetorium, but to the house of prayer. The Temple is his destination and his challenge.

Context

Mark uses his signature sandwich structure: fig tree cursed (Monday morning) / Temple cleansed (Monday) / fig tree withered (Tuesday morning). The authority question is the Temple establishment’s challenge to the one who has just overturned their tables.

Marcus Commentary

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8; Mark 8–16 (Word Biblical Commentary) — Faithful Paraphrase
11:1-6

Joel Marcus (Word Biblical Commentary) notes that the preparation for the entry is structured as a royal requisition — the disciples' password-like exchange ("The Lord needs it") echoes the right of a king to commandeer property for state use (cf. 1 Sam 8:11-18). This is not simply logistical but theological: Jesus approaches Jerusalem as a king approaches his capital, and his arrival cannot be refused. The detail that the colt has never been ridden (v2) marks it as ritually fit for sacred use, parallel to the unbroken heifer of Numbers 19.

11:7-11

Marcus notes the deliberate restraint of Mark's entry narrative compared to later Christian readings: the crowd acclaims Jesus using Psalm 118 language, but the psalm is a pilgrimage song, not necessarily a messianic proclamation. The cry "Blessed is the coming kingdom of our father David!" (v10) is Mark's unique phrasing — the kingdom, not just the king, is announced. Marcus argues that the entry is best read as a prophetic sign-act, not a triumphal march: its significance is enacted, not explained, and the explanation comes only in retrospect through the passion.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:12-25

Rhoads: The fig tree / Temple / fig tree intercalation is the most explicitly structural of Mark's sandwiches. The two halves of the fig tree story (11:12-14, 20-25) bracket the Temple cleansing (11:15-19). Rhoads's interpretive principle: the two parts of the sandwich are meant to be read through each other. The fig tree's judgment (withering from the roots) is the Temple's judgment. The Temple cleansing is the fig tree's leaves being stripped. The withering visible the next morning is the Temple's visible coming fall.

11:27-33

Rhoads: The authority challenge is the first of five controversy exchanges in chs.11-12. The narrator characterizes the chief priests, scribes, and elders as a bloc rather than as individuals — they speak with a collective voice, deliberate collectively, and are collectively silenced. This group characterization distances them from the "one of the teachers of the law" in 12:28-34 who is treated as an individual and commended. The narrative distinguishes between the institutional establishment and the person within it who seeks honestly.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
11:1-6

The instruction about the colt (vv.1-6) is, for Chrysostom, a display of divine foreknowledge: the animal has never been ridden and awaits precisely this use. Origen notes that the disciples' readiness to answer "The Lord needs it" is itself a model of apostolic obedience — no extended explanation, just the word of the Lord and the expectation of compliance. The literal fulfilment of Zechariah 9:9 is, for Bede, the first public signal that Jesus is acting with full messianic intentionality.

11:7-11

The spreading of cloaks on the road is, for Theophylact, a royal carpet ceremony (cf. 2 Kings 9:13 for Jehu). The Hosanna cry from Psalm 118:25-26 is, for Chrysostom, both a petition and an acclamation — the crowd is crying for salvation to the very one who is bringing it. The anti-climactic close — Jesus looked around at everything and left for Bethany because it was late — is, for Bede, Mark's characteristic restraint: no triumphal occupation, no immediate revolution, just the measured advance of the servant-king.

Greek Word Study

proseukhs pros-oo-KHAY — prayer/house of prayer: My house will be called a house of prayer for all nations — oikos proseukhs. The Temple’s primary purpose — prayer for all nations, not just Israel — is being subverted by the commercial activity in the court of the Gentiles. Jesus’s action restores the space where Gentiles were supposed to pray.

Places

Bethany
Eastern slope of the Mount of Olives; Jesus’s Passion Week base
Jesus leaves Bethany each morning and returns each evening. The Mount of Olives is both his sleeping ground and the vantage point from which he approaches Jerusalem. The geography of withdrawal and return structures the entire week.
The Temple courts
Jerusalem Temple; specifically the Court of the Gentiles
The Court of the Gentiles — the outermost court where Gentiles were permitted to pray — had become occupied by the money-changing and dove-selling operations. Jesus’s act restores the Gentile prayer space that the commercial activity had colonized.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:13

Marcus: "It was not the season for figs" — Mark's parenthetical note has generated sustained comment. Marcus argues that the fig tree, like the Temple, is being judged not for failure to fulfil a natural calendar but for false advertising: the leaves signalled the presence of early figs (pāgîm in Hebrew, the small precursor figs that appear with the leaves before the main harvest). A leaf-bearing tree with no early figs was promising what it did not have. The judgment is on the gap between appearance and reality — precisely the Temple's condition.

11:17

Marcus: The combination of Isa 56:7 ("house of prayer for all nations") and Jer 7:11 ("den of robbers") is a deliberate double citation. Marcus argues that both texts are being fulfilled simultaneously: Jesus restores the Gentile prayer space (Isa 56's promise) by removing the obstruction (Jer 7's condemnation). The Jer 7 context is critical — Jeremiah's Temple sermon preceded the destruction of 587 BC. The citation imports the full context of coming judgment.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
11:27-33

The question about John's baptism is, for Chrysostom, a political trap that Jesus turns into a theological mirror. The chief priests and scribes cannot answer because they are not seeking truth — they are seeking rhetorical advantage. Jesus's refusal to answer their question with his own is not evasion but exposure: their silence condemns them. Bede notes that the crowd's opinion ("They feared the people, for everyone held that John really was a prophet") becomes the authorities' constraint — their leadership is entirely reactive to popular sentiment, which is itself a judgment on their fitness to govern.

11:27-33

The authorities' inability to answer Jesus about John's baptism is, for Chrysostom, the definitive exposure of their motivation: they are not seeking truth but managing reputation. Their deliberation — "If we say 'from heaven,' he will ask why we didn't believe him; if we say 'from men'..." — is, for Origen, the anatomy of bad-faith inquiry. Jesus's refusal to answer is not evasion but justice: those who will not answer honestly have no claim on honest answers. Bede notes that this exchange immediately precedes the Parable of the Tenants — the vineyard owners who refuse accountability are already on display in the authorities' non-answer.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:13-14

Calvin: The fig tree cursed for having leaves but no fruit is the parable of all false religion. The leaves are the external show of piety; the fruit is the genuine transformation of heart and life. The Temple had become all leaves.

11:28

By what authority? — Calvin: the establishment’s question is procedurally correct but morally hollow. They want authorization papers, not revelation. Jesus’s counter-question about John’s baptism exposes their proceduralism: they would not accept authority they could not manage.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:15

The court of the Gentiles — not named explicitly in Mark but implied by the Isa 56:7 quotation (“for all nations”). The commercial operations occupied the space designated for Gentile prayer, effectively excluding them from the Temple’s universal purpose.

11:30

John’s baptism — from heaven or of human origin? Jesus’s counter-question is not evasion but revelation. If they accepted John’s authority (divine origin), they would recognize Jesus, whom John pointed to. Their refusal to answer exposes their bad faith.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities 15.380-425
Josephus describes Herod’s Temple complex in detail, including the Court of the Gentiles and the commercial activity associated with the festival crowds.
Confirms the historical context of the money-changing and animal-selling operations that Jesus confronted.
Mishnah Sheqalim 1:3
Rabbinic text describing the money-changing operations at the Temple — changing foreign currency into Tyrian shekels (required for the Temple tax).
Confirms that the money-changing was an established, authorized institution, making Jesus’s disruption of it a challenge to official Temple practice.

Reception History

E.P. Sanders, Jesus and Judaism (1985)
Sanders argued that the Temple action was not a “cleansing” but a symbolic act of destruction — a prophetic sign that the Temple was under divine judgment. The overturned tables signified the Temple’s coming overthrow (13:2).
N.T. Wright, Jesus and the Victory of God (1996)
Wright argued that Jesus was acting as the true Messiah claiming his Father’s house, enacting Mal 3:1-3 and Isa 56:7 simultaneously. The action was both prophetic judgment and messianic claim.

Textual Notes

11:3"The Lord needs it" — kyrios as Jesus or as God
Kyrios (Lord) could refer to Jesus himself — an early christological usage — or could mean "its owner/master" in a secular sense. No textual variant; the ambiguity is semantic.
The deliberate ambiguity between divine title and ordinary owner designation is characteristic of Mark's understated Christology. Both readings are simultaneously possible.
11:26Verse 11:26 — omitted in modern editions
Sinaiticus, Vaticanus omit 11:26 entirely; later MSS add "But if you do not forgive, neither will your Father in heaven forgive your trespasses" (harmonising with Matt 6:15).
Almost certainly a scribal addition. No reliable early MS includes it. The NIV omits it or brackets it in a footnote.

Scholarly Debates

Was the Temple action a cleansing or a judgment?
Cleansing view
Jesus restored the Temple’s proper function by removing commercial corruption from the Court of the Gentiles. The action is reformist.
Reformist cleansing
Judgment view
Jesus enacted the Temple’s coming destruction. The overturned tables are a prophetic sign, not a reform measure. E.P. Sanders argues this view.
Prophetic judgment
"You have made it a den of robbers" (Jer 7:11) is from a sermon that preceded Jerusalem’s destruction in 587 BC. The allusion suggests Jesus is the new Jeremiah announcing new judgment, not a temple administrator restoring order. Both dimensions — prophetic sign and reformist challenge — may be simultaneously present.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 11 is the Temple chapter: Jesus enters, surveys, cleanses, and provokes the authority challenge. Every element of the chapter orbits the Temple as the site of the covenant’s climactic crisis.
