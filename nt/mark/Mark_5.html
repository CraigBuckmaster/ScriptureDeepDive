Mark 5

Three Restorations: Demoniac, Bleeding Woman, Jairus's Daughter

Verses 1–20 — The Gerasene Demoniac: Legion into the Pigs
1234567891011121314151617181920

Greek Word Study

legiōn leh-gi-OHN — legion (Latin loan-word): My name is Legion — a Roman military unit of 3,000–6,000 soldiers. The name is both descriptive (many demons) and politically charged: Rome’s military power and demonic oppression use the same vocabulary. The exorcism in Gentile territory that destroys the pigs reads, for Mark’s Roman audience, as a cosmic challenge to Roman imperial power.

Places

Region of the Gerasenes
Eastern shore of the Sea of Galilee; Decapolis territory
This is Gentile land — the pigs confirm it. The Decapolis (ten Greek cities) was a region of Hellenistic culture east of the Jordan. Jesus’s first extended Gentile engagement happens at the far edge of Jewish geography, in a cemetery, among unclean animals.
The Sea / Steep Bank
Sea of Galilee eastern shore
The herd of 2,000 pigs rushing down the steep bank into the sea dramatizes the exorcism’s scale and irrevocability. The sea, which Jesus crossed in the storm, now becomes the grave of Legion.

Context

The Gerasene exorcism is the longest and most dramatic in Mark. Three elements make it distinctive: the man’s extreme condition (tombs, chains, self-harm), the demons’ naming as Legion, and the first explicit mission to Gentiles — Jesus tells the restored man to go home and testify in the Decapolis, breaking the Messianic Secret pattern that governs Jewish territory.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 65:3-4People who sit among the graves and spend their nights keeping secret vigil — the tomb-dweller is an OT figure of uncleanness and idolatry. Jesus enters this extreme uncleanness without contamination.
  • Deut 5:9The God who judges to the third and fourth generation — the demonic claim over territory (v.10) reflects the ancient notion of territorial spirits.
  • Luke 8:38-39The parallel account confirms the Decapolis mission and adds that the man proclaimed what God had done — equating Jesus with God.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:9

Marcus: "Legion, for we are many" — the Roman military legion (3,000-6,000 troops) was the symbol of Roman imperial violence in the occupied provinces. Marcus's influential reading sees the exorcism as a covert anti-imperial story: the "Legion" driven into pigs (unclean animals, associated with Rome) and drowned in the sea reverses the Exodus — this time Roman military power is swallowed by the sea. The story's original audience in Roman Galilee would have heard the political resonance.

5:19

Marcus: "Go home to your own people and tell them how much the Lord has done for you" — the first explicit evangelistic commission in Mark, given to a Gentile, in Gentile territory. The Decapolis mission anticipates the Great Commission. That the command breaks the Messianic Secret (operative in Jewish territory) and is given in Gentile space suggests that the Secret's function is not about Christology per se but about the management of Jewish messianic expectations.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:1-20

Rhoads: The Gerasene demoniac is the most fully characterized minor figure in Mark. He has a habitat (tombs), a condition (superhuman strength, self-harm, cries), a name (Legion), a location-preference (v.10: begging not to be sent out of the area), a request (the pigs), and a post-healing description (clothed, in his right mind). The detailed characterization invites reader sympathy before the miracle and makes the village's rejection of Jesus after the healing — preferring the loss of their pigs — all the more striking.

5:15

Rhoads: "Sitting, clothed, and in his right mind" — the three details reverse the three elements of the possessed state (roaming naked among tombs, breaking chains, crying out). Rhoads's narrative criticism highlights how Mark uses this reversal-pattern to signal restoration: the character's new state is described by the exact negation of their former state. The village's fear in the face of this restoration is one of Mark's sharpest ironies.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1-13

Origen devotes extended attention to the Gerasene demoniac in the Catena tradition. The possessed man living among the tombs is, for him, the image of a soul in complete disorder: its natural home (the body) turned to a place of death. The demons' request to enter the pigs is permitted, says Chrysostom, both to demonstrate the number and malice of the spirits and to give the man unambiguous evidence of his deliverance. Bede adds that the pigs rushing into the lake is a figure of the self-destructive nature of demonic influence when unleashed from a human host.

5:14-20

The townspeople's fear at seeing the demoniac "sitting there, dressed and in his mind" is, for Chrysostom, a profound comment on human perversity: they are more comfortable with a known disorder than an unexpected healing. The request that Jesus leave their region is, for Origen, a type of those who love their property more than the presence of God. The man's request to accompany Jesus is refused — he is instead sent back as a missionary to his own people. Chrysostom notes that he is the first Gentile missionary in Mark, commissioned not from Jerusalem but from Decapolis.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:9

Legion — for we are many. The plural within the singular (“my name is Legion / for we are many”) reflects demonic fragmentation: the possessed man has no single self left. The restoration in v.15 — sitting, clothed, in his right mind — is the reversal of every element of the possession.

5:19

Go home to your own people — the first explicit mission. Jesus breaks the Messianic Secret here because this is Gentile territory where there is no messianic expectation to be managed. The restored man becomes Mark’s first evangelist.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:9

Calvin: The naming of the demons as Legion is not merely descriptive but a confession of defeat. When demons name themselves to Jesus they are acknowledging his authority to command them. The name is given in response to Jesus’s question — he has power to compel even the identification of his enemies.

5:19-20

The mission to the Decapolis is the first deliberate Gentile outreach. Calvin notes that Jesus employs a man who is not one of the Twelve — the Spirit’s mission is not confined to official channels.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:13

The herd rushed down the bank into the lake — the verb hormēsen (rushed/charged) is violent and directional. The demons’ destination is the sea, which in apocalyptic tradition is the domain of chaos and the Abyss. Their request to enter the pigs rather than be sent to the Abyss (v.10) is ultimately futile — they end in the sea anyway.

5:20

He began to tell in the Decapolis — the ten Greek cities of the Transjordan region. The Gentile mission, barely visible in Mark, is planted here. By the time of Acts 9 there are disciples throughout the region.

Verses 21–34 — Jairus’s Plea; The Woman Who Touched His Cloak
2122232425262728293031323334

Greek Word Study

talitha koum TA-li-tha KOOM — little girl, get up (Aramaic): Talitha koum is one of the few places where Mark preserves Jesus’s actual Aramaic words, untranslated — then immediately translates them. The preservation of the original Aramaic is a mark of eyewitness transmission. Peter would have remembered the exact words in the room.

Places

Region of the Gerasenes
Eastern shore of the Sea of Galilee; Decapolis territory
This is Gentile land — the pigs confirm it. The Decapolis (ten Greek cities) was a region of Hellenistic culture east of the Jordan. Jesus’s first extended Gentile engagement happens at the far edge of Jewish geography, in a cemetery, among unclean animals.
The Sea / Steep Bank
Sea of Galilee eastern shore
The herd of 2,000 pigs rushing down the steep bank into the sea dramatizes the exorcism’s scale and irrevocability. The sea, which Jesus crossed in the storm, now becomes the grave of Legion.

Context

Mark interweaves two restoration stories — the technique known as a Markan sandwich. Jairus’s dying daughter provides the frame; the bleeding woman is inserted into the middle. The structural connection is the number twelve: the woman bled for twelve years; the girl is twelve years old. Both are daughters (v.23, v.34), both are restored to full life.

Cross-Reference

  • Lev 15:25-30A woman with an abnormal discharge — the Levitical law that the bleeding woman has been under. She has been ceremonially unclean for twelve years, excluded from worship and touch.
  • 1 Kings 17:17-24Elijah raises the widow’s son — the closest OT parallel to a resurrection. Jesus does what the prophet did, but by command rather than prayer.
  • Ps 30:3You brought me up from the realm of the dead — the psalm of resurrection that frames the raising of Jairus’s daughter.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:25-34

Marcus: The woman's twelve-year hemorrhage made her ritually impure under Lev 15:25-30 — she would have contaminated anyone she touched. Her touching Jesus's cloak is an act of ritual transgression driven by desperation. Marcus notes that Jesus does not rebuke her for the transgression but commends her faith. The legal contamination-logic is simply overridden: in Jesus's presence the flow of uncleanness is reversed to a flow of power (dynamis, v.30).

5:36

Marcus: "Don't be afraid; just believe" — Marcus reads this as a summary of Mark's entire theology of faith. In Mark's narrative, faith is not intellectual assent but the act of coming to Jesus despite every obstacle (the woman pushing through the crowd; Jairus maintaining hope after hearing his daughter is dead). Marcus connects this to the Habakkuk pesher at Qumran (1QpHab 8:1-3), where "the righteous lives by faith" is interpreted as trusting the Teacher of Righteousness despite apparent delay of fulfilment.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:21-43

Rhoads: The Jairus / bleeding woman sandwich is the most fully developed intercalation in Mark. The structural principle: the two stories interpret each other. The woman's twelve-year illness and the girl's twelve years of life are numerically linked. The woman's healing "on the way" creates the time-delay that allows Jairus's daughter to die. Faith is tested in the interval (5:35-36) and rewarded by resurrection. Rhoads argues that Mark's intercalation is not merely a compositional device but a theological statement: interruptions do not cancel God's action — they create the space in which faith is deepened.

5:41

Rhoads: "Talitha koum" — the Aramaic preserved in the narrator's voice is one of several places where Mark includes an untranslated saying and then translates it. The effect is to give the reader access to two layers: the reported original voice of Jesus in his mother tongue, and the narrator's explanation for non-Aramaic readers. This dual layer implies an audience distant from the original setting — confirming the Gospel's self-presentation as translation of an eyewitness tradition (cf. Papias on Mark as Peter's interpreter).

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
5:21-34

The interlocking of Jairus's daughter and the bleeding woman is, for Ambrose, deliberate literary theology: the interruption is not a delay but a demonstration. Both sufferers are female; one is twelve years old, the other has been ill twelve years — Bede and Theophylact both note this parallel. The woman's touch of the hem of Jesus's garment is, for Chrysostom, a picture of faith that needs no formal request — she does not ask, she reaches, and power goes out. Jesus's question "Who touched me?" is not ignorance but invitation: he wishes to make her healing public and complete.

5:35-43

The raising of Jairus's daughter begins with the most devastating news imaginable — "your daughter is dead" — and Jesus's response is, for Chrysostom, the paradigm of faith under extreme testing: "Don't be afraid; just believe." The exclusion of the mourners and the admission of only three disciples and the parents is, for Origen, a principle of spiritual economy — the mystery of resurrection is not for public exhibition but for intimate witness. The command "Talitha koum" is preserved in Aramaic by Mark alone, which Bede says is because it carries its power in the original sound.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:34

Your faith has healed you — the woman’s touch was an act of desperate, theologically imperfect faith. She hoped for a quasi-magical transfer of power. Jesus does not correct her theology in public but affirms the faith and converts the transaction into a covenant encounter: “Daughter, go in peace.”

5:41

Talitha koum — the Aramaic command is preserved because Peter was in the room. The intimacy of the small word — “little girl” — is as characteristic of Jesus as the power behind it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:36

Don’t be afraid; just believe — the two-word command is the Gospel in miniature. Fear and faith are mutually exclusive postures. Jairus is at the exact moment where faith either grows or collapses under the weight of news. Jesus’s command does not deny the news; it relocates its significance.

5:43

He told them to give her something to eat — the practical concern after the miracle. Calvin: Jesus demonstrates that the raised girl is genuinely alive, not a ghost or vision. The request for food is theological: the resurrection is bodily, not merely spiritual.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:26

She had suffered a great deal under the care of many doctors — a realistic detail that was softened in Luke’s parallel (Luke 8:43, which omits the doctors’ failure). Mark’s version preserves the social reality: twelve years of medical failure and financial ruin.

5:43

He gave strict orders not to let anyone know — the Messianic Secret applied to a resurrection. The crowds already know the girl was dead (v.40: they laughed at Jesus). The command is not to suppress the resurrection but to control the interpretation of who Jesus is.

Reception History

Augustine, Sermon 98
Augustine used the two stories (5:21-43) to argue that faith in Jesus overcomes both chronic suffering (twelve years) and death. The woman represents those whose faith is small and grasping; Jairus represents those whose faith is tested at the extreme.
Karl Barth, Church Dogmatics IV/2
Barth identified the Jairus story as the paradigm of “the awakening of the dead” — the eschatological pattern made visible in a specific historical act. Every resurrection follows the Talitha koum pattern: a word that overcomes death.

Greek-Rooted Reading

Greek-Rooted Reading

5:9 Legiōn — Latin loan-word for a Roman army unit. The demonic name uses the vocabulary of imperial military power.
5:30 Dynamis — power. Jesus is aware of its outward movement. The healing is not passive but a positive expenditure.
5:41 Talitha koum — Aramaic preserved verbatim. One of a handful of places where we hear the actual words Jesus spoke.

Textual Notes

5:1Gerasenes, Gadarenes, or Gergesenes
Sinaiticus, Alexandrinus read Gerasēnōn; other MSS Gadarēnōn (Matthew's parallel) or Gergesēnōn (Origen's preferred reading based on topography).
Gerasa is 37km from the lake — geographically problematic. Gadara is closer but still several kilometres. "Gergesenes" (Origen) may be a scholarly correction. No reading is free of difficulty; Gerasenes is the best-attested.
5:36"Overhearing" or "ignoring"
NA28 reads parakoysas (overhearing/ignoring); some MSS read akousas (having heard).
Parakoysas is the harder reading (ambiguous: overhear or disregard) and likely original; it fits the urgency of the scene.

Scholarly Debates

Did Jesus raise the dead or was she sleeping?
Resurrection view
Jesus’s statement “she is not dead but asleep” is a metaphor for resurrection, not a medical diagnosis. The crowd knew she was dead (v.35). Sleeping is Jesus’s frame for what death looks like from his perspective.
Resurrection
Resuscitation view
Some suggest the girl was in a deep coma and Jesus correctly diagnosed it. The crowd’s laughter (v.40) supports the possibility that her death was uncertain.
Resuscitation
"Your daughter is dead" (v.35) is the messenger’s report. Jesus’s response is not a medical correction but a theological reframe: from the perspective of resurrection power, death is temporary sleep. The miracle is real whatever the precise medical state.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 5 is the authority chapter par excellence: three demonstrations of Jesus's power over demonic forces, chronic disease, and death itself. Each restoration is more extreme than the last.
