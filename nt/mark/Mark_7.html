Mark 7

What Defiles; Gentile Faith; Healing

Author

John Mark, son of Mary of Jerusalem (Acts 12:12), cousin of Barnabas (Col 4:10), and close associate of both Paul and Peter. Early and unanimous tradition identifies him as “the interpreter of Peter”: Papias of Hierapolis (c. AD 125, preserved in Eusebius, HE 3.39.15) records that Mark “wrote down accurately, though not in order, as much as he remembered of the things said or done by the Lord” — drawing entirely on Peter’s teaching. Irenaeus (Against Heresies 3.1.1), Clement of Alexandria (Hypotyposeis, in Eusebius HE 6.14.6), and the Anti-Marcionite Prologue all confirm the Petrine connection. Mark himself is mentioned in Acts 12:12, 25; 13:13; 15:37–39; Col 4:10; Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11; and 1 Pet 5:13 (“my son Mark”). He was present at some events he records (the young man fleeing naked, 14:51–52, is widely identified as a Markan self-cameo), and his vividness throughout reflects the eyewitness testimony he received directly from Peter in Rome.

When written

c. AD 55–65, almost certainly in Rome, most likely before the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 — which Mark 13 predicts but nowhere records as fulfilled. Clement of Alexandria states the Gospel was written “while Peter was still preaching publicly in Rome.” If so, the most probable window is the late 50s to mid-60s, during Paul’s Roman imprisonment (Acts 28) and Peter’s residence in the capital. Mark is the earliest of the four Gospels (the Markan Priority hypothesis, held by the majority of NT scholars), and both Matthew and Luke appear to have used it as a primary source. The Gospel’s Latin loanwords (legiōn, denarius, praetorium, centurio), its explanation of Jewish customs for non-Jewish readers (7:3–4), and its Latinisms of idiom all point to a Roman Gentile audience.

What prompted it

To preserve Peter’s eyewitness testimony in permanent written form for a church facing persecution under Nero (AD 64), and to answer the most urgent question that question raised: who is Jesus, and why did he die? Mark’s opening declaration — “The beginning of the good news about Jesus the Messiah, the Son of God” (1:1) — is simultaneously title, thesis, and answer. The Gospel is structured around the progressive revelation of that identity: demons recognise Jesus before disciples do (1:24); Peter confesses “You are the Messiah” at the midpoint (8:29); a Roman centurion declares “Surely this man was the Son of God” at the cross (15:39). The relentless pace — euthys (“immediately”) appears 41 times — reflects both Peter’s preaching style and the urgency of a community for whom the Passion narrative was not ancient history but recent, costly, and still unfolding.

Verses 1–23 — Clean and Unclean: Tradition vs the Command of God
12345678910111213141517181920212223

Greek Word Study

korban KOR-ban — offering/gift dedicated to God: Corban — a vow dedicating property to the Temple. The Pharisees allowed a son to declare his assets Corban, effectively making them unavailable to help aging parents while still retaining personal use. Jesus exposes this as a mechanism for legally violating the fifth commandment while appearing pious.

Context

The clean/unclean controversy is the theological centre of Mark’s Galilean section. Jesus makes two claims: (1) external ritual cannot defile the heart — the oral tradition overrides the written Torah; (2) only internal evil defiles — overturning the Levitical purity system entirely. Mark’s editorial note (v.19: “In saying this, Jesus declared all foods clean”) is one of the most significant theological parentheses in the NT.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 29:13These people honour me with their lips but their hearts are far from me — the text Jesus quotes directly. Isaiah’s charge against eighth-century Israel becomes Jesus’s charge against first-century Pharisaism.
  • Exod 20:12Honour your father and your mother — the command the Corban tradition subverts.
  • Ezek 36:25-27I will give you a new heart — the prophetic promise of internal transformation that Jesus’s teaching about internal defilement anticipates.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:3-4

Marcus: The parenthetical explanation of Jewish purity customs for non-Jewish readers is among the strongest internal evidence for Mark's Gentile-Roman audience. Marcus notes that Mark is the only Gospel that explains these customs, suggesting a community not observant of or familiar with Second Temple Jewish practice. The Mishnah (tractate Yadayim) provides extensive discussion of hand-washing before meals, confirming this was a genuine Pharisaic practice, not a Markan caricature.

7:11

Marcus: The Corban practice is attested in Josephus (Against Apion 1.167) and in a Qumran fragment (4Q270). Marcus argues that the rabbinic sources (Mishnah Nedarim) show the sages were themselves uncomfortable with vows that disadvantaged parents — suggesting Jesus's critique was an internal Jewish debate, not an attack on Torah observance per se. The force of the criticism is not that the tradition is Jewish but that it has been used to nullify the higher principle it claimed to honour.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:1-23

Rhoads: The clean/unclean controversy has a three-part structure: dispute (7:1-13), public teaching (7:14-16), private explanation (7:17-23). This structure mirrors the parable sections of ch.4: public pronouncement that confuses, followed by private clarification for insiders. The narrator's interjection in 7:19 ("In saying this, Jesus declared all foods clean") is the narrator speaking directly to the reader, interpreting Jesus's teaching for a community apparently living under the implications of that teaching.

7:24

Rhoads: "Jesus left that place and went to the vicinity of Tyre. He entered a house and did not want anyone to know it; yet he could not keep his presence secret." The tension between Jesus's desire for hiddenness and his unavoidable notoriety is a recurring narrative pattern in Mark. Rhoads identifies this as a characterization technique: the Messianic Secret operates as an element of plot tension, not just theology. Every time Jesus withdraws, he is found; every time he silences testimony, it spreads. The character cannot be contained.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1-13

Chrysostom notes that the Pharisees' challenge about unwashed hands is not about hygiene but about the authority of oral tradition. Jesus's response cuts to the root: the tradition has been used to nullify the command. The Corban example is, for Aquinas, an instance of legal ingenuity deployed against moral obligation — a person could declare property dedicated to God and thereby escape the duty to support parents. Bede observes that Jesus quotes Isaiah 29:13 precisely: "their hearts are far from me" — the critique is not of ritual practice as such but of ritual practice divorced from inward reality.

7:14-23

The teaching on defilement — "Nothing outside a person can defile them" — is, for Origen, the most radical dietary pronouncement in the Synoptics: Mark notes parenthetically that Jesus "declared all foods clean" (v19). Chrysostom says this is not the abolition of the law but its fulfilment: the law's food codes were pedagogical, pointing toward the interior purity that alone makes a person acceptable to God. The catalogue of vices in vv.21-22 is, for Theophylact, a comprehensive anatomy of the sinful heart — beginning with evil thoughts and ending with foolishness, showing that moral failure begins in the mind.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:8

You have let go of the commands of God and are holding on to human traditions — the reversal is the charge: what was meant to protect the Torah has replaced the Torah. Tradition has swallowed the text it claimed to honour.

7:19

Jesus declared all foods clean — Mark’s editorial comment, not part of Jesus’s direct speech. This parenthetical interpretation by the narrator is the most significant food-law statement in the Synoptics and becomes the theological basis for Peter’s Cornelius vision (Acts 10).

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:9

You have a fine way of setting aside the commands of God — Calvin reads this as the defining critique of all religion that substitutes human invention for divine command. The Reformation’s critique of tradition-over-Scripture is exegetically grounded here.

7:19

All foods clean — Calvin notes that this anticipates the abolition of the ceremonial law in Christ. The dietary laws were shadows of the covenant reality. Their fulfilment in Christ releases their binding force.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:11

Corban (korbanas) — a loanword from Hebrew qorban (offering). The practice of corban vows is attested in Mishnah Nedarim 9:1, which shows that rabbis were themselves uncomfortable with vows that disadvantaged parents. Jesus’s criticism reflects a genuine abuse within the tradition.

7:19

“In saying this” — the phrase is a participial clause describing Jesus’s action: as he said this, he was pronouncing all foods clean. Mark’s editorial recognition of the implication is confirmed in Acts 10:9-16 (Peter’s vision) and Acts 15:28-29 (Jerusalem council decision).

Verses 24–37 — The Syrophoenician Woman; Healing a Deaf Man
2425262728293031323334353637

Greek Word Study

ephphatha ef-FA-tha — be opened (Aramaic): Ephphatha is the second Aramaic word preserved verbatim in Mark (after Talitha koum in 5:41). The opening of ears and tongue in a Gentile region fulfils Isaiah 35:5-6 — the eschatological opening of the deaf and the mute is happening, and it is happening in Gentile territory.

Places

Region of Tyre and Sidon
Phoenician coast; modern Lebanon, c.50km from the Sea of Galilee
Tyre and Sidon are Israel’s ancient Phoenician neighbours — the territory of Jezebel’s origin (1 Kings 16:31). Jesus enters the homeland of the OT’s most notorious pagan queen and finds a Gentile woman of extraordinary faith there.
Decapolis
Ten Greek cities east of the Jordan and around the Sea of Galilee
The circular route — Tyre, Sidon, down through the Decapolis — keeps Jesus in Gentile territory for the entire section. Both healings are in non-Jewish space.

Context

The movement into Tyre and Sidon is the deepest penetration of Gentile territory in Mark’s Gospel. The Syrophoenician woman’s exchange with Jesus is among the most theologically charged conversations in the NT: Jesus’s “dogs” remark and her brilliant riposte turn on the question of the covenant’s scope. Her persistence wins a Gentile healing and a commendation of her faith. The deaf man’s healing in the Decapolis then fulfils Isaiah 35’s eschatological vision.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 35:5-6Then will the ears of the deaf be unstopped…and the mute tongue shout for joy — the text fulfilled in v.35 is the eschatological healing of the new creation.
  • Matt 15:21-28Matthew’s parallel explicitly commends the woman’s great faith — Mark omits this but it is implicit in Jesus’s response.
  • Isa 56:6-7My house will be called a house of prayer for all nations — the inclusion of Gentiles in the covenant that the Syrophoenician episode inaugurates.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:24

Marcus: The withdrawal to Tyre and Sidon is the deepest penetration of Gentile territory in Mark's narrative. Tyre was notorious in OT tradition as the exemplar of pagan wealth and pride (Ezek 26-28; Isa 23; Joel 3:4-8). That Jesus goes there and heals a Gentile woman in her homeland represents the fulfilment of the OT promises that even Israel's most notorious enemies would eventually receive divine grace (Isa 23:17-18; Zech 9:3-4).

7:34

Marcus: "Ephphatha" (be opened) — the Aramaic preserved in the healing command echoes Isa 35:5 ("the ears of the deaf will be unstopped"). Crucially, Isa 35 is addressed to Zion in the context of the new exodus: the deaf will hear as part of the eschatological restoration. That this healing happens in Gentile territory (the Decapolis) is Mark's implicit claim that the eschatological healing of Israel has been extended to include the nations — fulfilling the prophetic horizon that Isa 35 itself anticipated (cf. Isa 35:1-2, the wilderness flourishing).

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:24-30

Rhoads: The Syrophoenician woman is characterized through dialogue alone — the most dialogue-intensive exchange with a minor character in Mark. Her speech is economical, theologically precise, and rhetorically skilled. She accepts the premise of Jesus's statement (children first) and finds the logical room within it (crumbs remain for dogs). Rhoads argues this is the narrative's clearest example of a character who "out-argues" Jesus — and in doing so, demonstrates the faith that the disciples consistently fail to show.

7:31

Rhoads: The circuitous route from Tyre through Sidon to the Sea of Galilee via the Decapolis is geographically odd — Sidon is north of Tyre, Decapolis is south of Galilee — but narratively intentional. Mark keeps Jesus in Gentile territory for an extended period before returning to Jewish space. The route signals that the Gentile mission is not a brief excursion but a sustained engagement. Rhoads's narrative geography argument: the map of Mark's narrative is the map of the kingdom's expansion.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
7:24-30

The Syrophoenician woman's encounter is, for Chrysostom, the most theologically concentrated dialogue in Mark. Jesus's initial refusal ("It is not right to take the children's bread and toss it to the dogs") is not, for Origen and Ambrose, a statement of prejudice but a test of faith and a teachable moment about the order of redemptive history — Israel first. The woman's response accepts the metaphor and turns it: dogs under the table eat the children's crumbs. This, says Chrysostom, is the highest form of faith — to hold on to hope even when the apparent word is discouragement.

7:31-37

The healing of the deaf-mute involves a series of physical gestures — fingers in the ears, touching the tongue, spitting, looking up to heaven — which Bede says are not magical acts but signs of condescension: Jesus works through material means to meet a person whose faith is in its infancy. The Aramaic command "Ephphatha" (Be opened) is preserved by Mark with special weight — Origen notes that the same word applies to the ears of spiritual hearing. The crowd's reaction "He has done everything well" is, for Chrysostom, an unwitting echo of Genesis 1: the Creator's work is good.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:27

It is not right to take the children’s bread and toss it to the dogs — the remark appears harsh but the diminutive “dogs” (kynarion — household dogs, not street curs) is less harsh than it sounds. The priority of Israel is real (Rom 1:16: first to the Jew); the woman’s reply accepts the priority and claims the overflow.

7:34

He looked up to heaven with a deep sigh — the sigh (stenaxas) is the same root as the groaning of creation in Rom 8:22-23. Jesus groans in solidarity with the disorder he is about to reverse.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:27

Calvin: The answer about dogs and children is a test rather than a refusal. Jesus is eliciting the faith he knows is there. The woman’s reply shows that she understands the covenant order (children first) and trusts in the covenant’s generosity (crumbs are enough). Her theology is sounder than many of Israel’s insiders.

7:34

Ephphatha — Calvin: the Aramaic command is preserved to show that the healing is by authority, not formula. The word effects what it says. The deaf hear not because of the ritual (fingers, spittle, sigh) but because the word of the Lord is irresistible.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:26

A Greek, born in Syrian Phoenicia — the double designation is precise. She is ethnically Greek (Hellēnis: culturally Hellenistic) and racially Syrophoenician. She represents the Gentile world at its most culturally sophisticated — not a barbarian but a Hellenized woman of the coastal trading cities.

7:34

Deep sigh (stenaxas) — the verb indicates emotional intensity. Jesus’s healings in Mark are not clinical but emotionally engaged. He is not a dispassionate miracle machine but a person who shares in the weight of the condition he is removing.

Reception History

Augustine, Reply to Faustus 12.14
Augustine used the Syrophoenician woman’s exchange to argue that the covenant’s overflow to Gentiles was always its inner logic — the priority of Israel was temporal and pedagogical, not exclusionary.
Martin Luther, Commentary on the Gospels
Luther used the Syrophoenician woman as his primary example of what he called “struggling faith” — faith that persists against apparent refusal and holds God to his own promise.

Mark 7 presents the theoretical principle (internal defilement, not external) then its practical application (Gentile mission is possible). The clean/unclean controversy is the gateway that opens the door of the Gentile mission.

Greek-Rooted Reading

Greek-Rooted Reading

7:11 Korban — Hebrew qorban: “gift dedicated to God.” A technically pious mechanism for avoiding the fifth commandment.
7:19 Katharizōn — cleansing/declaring clean. Mark’s participial aside is one of the most significant editorial comments in the NT: Jesus is the one who declares all foods clean.
7:34 Ephphatha — Aramaic: be opened. Third preserved Aramaic word in Mark (after 5:41). The eschatological opening of Isa 35 in a Gentile mouth.

Textual Notes

7:3"With a fist" or "carefully/up to the elbow"
NA28 reads pugmē (with a fist — a specific washing gesture); some interpret as "carefully" or "frequently." The term is unclear and unique in the NT.
The hapax legomenon pugmē is almost certainly original (no scribe would insert an obscure term); the meaning is debated but the ritual washing context is secure.
7:16"Let anyone with ears to hear, listen"
Sinaiticus, Vaticanus omit 7:16 entirely; it appears in later MSS as a scribal addition harmonising with 4:9.
The verse is almost certainly secondary — a scribe imported 4:9's refrain. The NIV brackets it or omits it. Its absence does not affect the argument of 7:14-15.

Scholarly Debates

Was Jesus’s “dogs” remark genuinely harsh?
Test-of-faith view
Jesus knew the woman’s faith and was drawing it out. The diminutive kynarion (household dogs) is less insulting than the street-dog slur.
Pedagogical test
Priority-of-Israel view
Jesus genuinely maintained the covenant priority (Israel first) and only extended healing after the woman’s persistent faith demonstrated Gentile receptivity.
Real covenant order
"For such a reply" (7:29) suggests the remark was a genuine test that her answer successfully navigated. The interaction is one of the clearest examples of faith demonstrated through persistent engagement.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 7 is the purity and mission chapter: the clean/unclean controversy dissolves the barrier between Jewish and Gentile space, enabling the Syrophoenician and Decapolis healings.
