Mark 1

The Beginning of the Good News; Authority Demonstrated

See on Timeline — Jesus Baptism See on Map — Galilean Ministry
Verses 1–8 — John the Baptist Prepares the Way
12345678

Greek Word Study

arche AR-khay — beginning/origin: Mark’s single-word opening echoes Genesis 1:1 in the Septuagint (LXX). He is announcing a new creation, not merely a biography.

Places

Wilderness of Judea
Barren region between Jerusalem and the Dead Sea
The wilderness is both geographical and theological — the space of the new exodus. Isaiah promised that the divine highway home would cut through the wilderness (Isa 40:3). John’s location signals that something at the scale of the Exodus is beginning.
Jordan River
Jordan Valley; traditional baptism site at Qasr el-Yahud
The Jordan is Israel’s entry river into the Promised Land under Joshua. Being baptized in the Jordan re-enacts the covenant crossing — confessing that Israel needs to enter the land again, renewed.

Context

Mark opens without genealogy or birth narrative. His thesis is the single sentence of v.1. John the Baptist is immediately introduced as the fulfilment of two OT texts — Malachi 3:1 and Isaiah 40:3 combined — anchoring the story in covenant history from verse 2.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 40:3A voice of one calling in the wilderness — the new-exodus promise applied to John as the forerunner.
  • Mal 3:1I will send my messenger ahead of you — the promised forerunner before the Day of the LORD.
  • John 1:6-8John came as a witness to testify — the Fourth Gospel confirms John’s role as witness, not the light.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:2-3

Marcus: The conflation of Mal 3:1 and Isa 40:3 under "Isaiah the prophet" reflects a technique called "catchword composition" well attested in Second Temple Jewish midrash. The Qumran community (1QS 8:12-14) also applied Isa 40:3 to their own wilderness mission, suggesting that this text was a live messianic-preparatory image in Jesus's era.

1:4-5

Marcus: The Jordan baptism invokes Israel's wilderness-to-land narrative. John's desert location recalls Elijah's wilderness sojourn and the Qumran community's own desert retreat. The "whole Judean countryside" reflects the same hyperbolic summation found in Josephus's description of popular prophetic movements — Mark is situating John within a recognizable social type.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:1-8

Rhoads: Mark's narrator establishes a reliable inside view immediately: "The beginning of the good news about Jesus the Messiah, the Son of God." The narrator tells the reader what no character in the story yet knows. This creates a sustained dramatic irony — the audience watches characters struggle with a question (who is Jesus?) whose answer has been given in verse 1.

1:4-8

Rhoads: The narrator's characterization of John through appearance (camel hair, leather belt) and diet (locusts, wild honey) economically identifies him as a prophet-type without editorial comment. Mark's narrator consistently characterizes through action and appearance rather than interior monologue — showing rather than telling.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1-3

Chrysostom notes that Mark begins not with genealogy or nativity but with the herald — John the Baptist — because Mark's Gospel is the Gospel of immediate action. The quotation from Isaiah and Malachi (vv.2-3) in one breath is deliberate: Aquinas, following Bede, says it shows the two offices of the Forerunner — as the messenger of the covenant (Malachi) and as the voice in the wilderness (Isaiah). The prophecy precedes the person; the word precedes the Word.

1:6-8

Bede observes that John's clothing of camel hair and leather belt deliberately recalls Elijah (2 Kings 1:8), signalling that John is the promised Elijah who was to come. Chrysostom adds that the locusts and wild honey of his diet signify both his mortification of the flesh and his contentment — the prophet neither enriches himself nor requires hospitality. His declaration "I am not worthy to stoop down and untie his sandals" is, for Ambrose, a statement not of inferiority but of office: the herald cannot also be the bridegroom.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

The two titles — Messiah (anointed covenant king) and Son of God — are the twin pillars of Mark’s Gospel. Peter’s confession in 8:29 and the centurion’s declaration in 15:39 form an inclusio around the whole book.

1:2-3

Mark conflates Mal 3:1 and Isa 40:3 under “Isaiah the prophet” — ancient practice attributed composite quotes to the most prominent source. The Isaiah citation is primary because it frames the new-exodus theme.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1-3

Calvin: Mark begins not with the birth but with the beginning of the preaching — because the gospel is primarily the announcement of salvation. The prophetic testimony grounds it in ancient covenant fulfilment, not new invention.

1:6

The camel-hair garment and desert diet are deliberately Elijah-like (2 Kings 1:8). The new Elijah has come, which means the Day of the LORD is near.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:1

“Son of God” is disputed textually but the weight of external evidence supports inclusion. The title is programmatic: Mark’s whole narrative reveals this identity, culminating at the cross (15:39).

1:4

John’s “baptism of repentance” (baptisma metanoias) — a genitive of purpose: baptism that expresses and seals repentance. It anticipates Christian baptism but differs in not being in Jesus’s name.

Verses 9–13 — Baptism and Temptation
910111213

Greek Word Study

schizo SKHI-zo — torn/ripped: Mark uses schizomenous — “torn open” — for the heavens here (1:10) and the same root for the Temple veil at the crucifixion (15:38). Two tearings frame the Gospel: heaven opens to commission the Son; the veil tears to grant access at his death.

Places

Nazareth
Lower Galilee, c.100km north of the Jordan baptism site
Jesus comes from Nazareth — the obscure village of his upbringing — to the Jordan for public commissioning. The ministry will begin in Galilee (1:14) and end in Jerusalem (11:1). The geographical arc mirrors the theological movement.
Jordan River
Traditional baptism site; Jordan Valley
The Jordan is Israel’s covenant crossing point. Jesus’s identification with Israel at the Jordan grounds his representative role.
Wilderness
Desert of Judea; forty-day retreat
Forty days echo Israel’s forty years (Num 14) and Moses’s forty days on Sinai (Exod 24). Jesus recapitulates and completes what Israel failed to do.

Context

Mark compresses baptism and temptation into five verses. The baptism is a Trinitarian event: Son baptized, Spirit descends, Father speaks. The temptation follows immediately — “at once the Spirit sent him out.” The forty days echo Israel’s forty years in the wilderness; Jesus recapitulates Israel’s story and succeeds where Israel failed.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 2:7You are my Son — the royal coronation Psalm. The Father’s declaration installs Jesus as the Davidic king.
  • Isa 42:1Here is my servant in whom I delight — the Servant Song combined with Ps 2 at the baptism: Jesus is both Davidic king and Suffering Servant.
  • Isa 40:3The same wilderness where John prepares the way is where Jesus is tested. The new exodus passes through wilderness before the Promised Land.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:10

Marcus: The "tearing" (schizomenous) of the heavens echoes Isa 64:1 ("Oh, that you would rend the heavens and come down!"). This is a theophany of the new exodus type: as the Red Sea was split (schizō in the LXX of certain traditions) to allow Israel passage, so the heavens split to allow the Spirit's descent. The dove echoes the Noah story (Gen 8:8-12) where a dove signals the end of judgment and the beginning of a new world order.

1:12-13

Marcus: The forty days in the wilderness parallel not only Israel's forty years but Moses's forty days on Sinai (Exod 24:18; 34:28) and Elijah's forty days to Horeb (1 Kings 19:8). The "wild animals" (thēria) may reflect the eschatological peace of Isa 11:6-9 or the dangerous beasts of the desert (Isa 34; Ps 91:13). Either way Jesus is being presented as the last Adam in restored creation.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:9-11

Rhoads: The baptism scene is a private theophany — only Jesus sees the torn sky and hears the voice (note: "he saw," "you are my Son"). The reader shares this privileged inside view; the crowds do not. This establishes the pattern of divided knowledge: the reader knows what the characters do not, and vice versa throughout the Gospel.

1:12-13

Rhoads: "At once the Spirit sent him out into the wilderness" — euthys, immediately. Mark's narrator moves at the speed of divine urgency. The compressed temptation account (two verses vs Matthew's eleven) reflects the narrator's characteristic withholding of dialogue and interior reflection: the reader is given the event without the deliberation. What Jesus felt or thought during forty days is entirely absent.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
1:9-11

Chrysostom says the heavens being "torn open" (schizo) at the baptism — rather than merely opened — signals the permanent accessibility of heaven to humanity after the incarnation. The Spirit descending as a dove is, for Basil of Caesarea, a foreshadowing of the new creation: the dove over the waters of baptism echoes the Spirit hovering over the waters of Genesis 1. The Father's voice "You are my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased" declares what was always true but now made manifest in history.

1:12-13

Origen observes that Mark says the Spirit "drove" (ekballo) Jesus into the wilderness — a word used elsewhere for expelling demons — to show that the Spirit's impulsion is irresistible and that Jesus entered the temptation not reluctantly but as a warrior entering battle. The forty days echo Moses and Elijah; the wild animals are, for Jerome, either a sign of menace (the danger of the wilderness) or of harmony (the new Adam ruling creation peacefully). Both readings were preserved in the Catena tradition.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:9-11

Jesus had no sin to repent of — he identifies with sinners in baptism as their representative, not as one of them (2 Cor 5:21). The baptism is public installation: dove, voice, and Trinitarian commissioning begin the messianic mission.

1:12-13

Mark skips the three specific temptations entirely. He notes only duration (40 days), adversary (Satan), company (wild animals), and provision (angels). The wild animals may echo the Edenic ideal of Isa 11:6-9: the Last Adam in restored creation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:9

Why was Jesus baptized if sinless? He was baptized as the representative of his people, taking their place before God. His baptism is not confession but substitution.

1:12-13

The brevity highlights the essentials: Jesus was genuinely tempted, he endured the full 40 days, and he overcame. The wild animals and angel ministry frame the temptation as restoration of the Edenic condition.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:10

“Just as he was coming up out of the water” confirms immersion. The descent “like a dove” — the simile is functional: the manner was dove-like, gentle and visible.

1:13

“Being tempted” — present participle: ongoing throughout the 40 days, not a single event at the end. The wild animals are unique to Mark — possibly Edenic restoration imagery or Markan geographical realism about the desert terrain.

Verses 14–20 — The Kingdom Proclaimed; First Disciples Called
14151617181920

Greek Word Study

kairos KY-ros — appointed time/decisive moment: The time has come — ho kairos peplerōtai. Kairos is not chronological time (chronos) but the decisive moment in God’s redemptive calendar. The perfect tense: the appointed season has reached its completion-point.

Places

Galilee
Northern Israel region; “Galilee of the Gentiles” (Isa 9:1)
Jesus deliberately begins in the marginal north, not Jerusalem. The geographical choice is theological: the kingdom comes first to the periphery, fulfilling Isaiah’s promise of light in the land of Zebulun and Naphtali.
Sea of Galilee
Freshwater lake, 21km x 13km; working fishing economy
The first disciples are called in their actual working environment. The Sea of Galilee’s fishing industry was substantial. Their immediate abandonment of nets and boat measures the authority of the call.

Context

John’s imprisonment triggers Jesus’s public ministry — the forerunner’s silencing signals that the one he announced has arrived. Mark’s kingdom proclamation in v.15 is the most compressed gospel summary in the synoptics: time fulfilled, kingdom near, repent, believe. The calling of the first four disciples requires no prior acquaintance and gives no explanation — the authority is in the call itself.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 52:7How beautiful on the mountains are the feet of those who bring good news — the proclamation of the kingdom fulfils Isaiah’s new-exodus announcement.
  • Dan 7:13-14The kingdom of God in Mark is Danielic: the rule of the Ancient of Days breaking in through the Son of Man.
  • Matt 4:17From that time on Jesus began to preach: Repent, for the kingdom of heaven has come near — Matthew’s parallel shows the programmatic nature of this proclamation.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:14-15

Marcus: "The time has come" (peplerōtai ho kairos) — the perfect passive signals that an eschatological clock has run its course. The kingdom of God announcement would have resonated with multiple strands of Second Temple expectation: the Psalms of Solomon's expectation of a Davidic king (17:21), the Qumran community's anticipation of a new covenant, and the Zealot hope of political restoration.

1:16-20

Marcus: The immediate response of the disciples is sociologically striking. The fishermen of Galilee were not subsistence peasants but working tradesmen with property (boats, nets, hired workers). Their abandonment of this economic position is more dramatic than it might first appear. The calling narrative may deliberately echo Elijah's call of Elisha in 1 Kings 19:19-21, where the plowing Elisha also leaves everything.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:14-15

Rhoads: Jesus's first speech in Mark is a four-part proclamation: time fulfilled, kingdom near, repent, believe. Mark's Jesus characteristically speaks in direct, imperative speech — not extended discourse. This opening speech establishes his rhetorical character: authoritative, compressed, declarative. The characters around him will consistently fail to understand; the reader, positioned by the narrator in 1:1, already knows the framework.

1:16-20

Rhoads: The calling scene is pure action: "Come, follow me" — immediate response — "they left." No dialogue, no deliberation, no background provided. Mark's narrator withholds interior life as a systematic technique. The effect is to characterize Jesus as one whose call produces immediate transformation, and to characterize the disciples initially as fully responsive — a portrait that will increasingly complicate across the narrative.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
1:14-15

Theophylact notes that Jesus began his preaching only after John's arrest — not because he depended on John, but to show that the preparatory work was complete. The proclamation "the kingdom of God has come near" is, for Chrysostom, not merely future but present: the king's arrival is the kingdom's arrival. Repentance and belief are inseparable in Mark's summary — Origen says you cannot receive a king without first clearing the throne room.

1:16-20

The calling of the fishermen is, for Ambrose, a deliberate inversion of social expectation: God does not choose philosophers or lawyers but working men, to show that wisdom and power come from above, not from human credentials. Chrysostom notes the immediacy — "at once they left their nets" — as the model of true discipleship: no negotiation, no conditional response. Bede adds that the detail about Zebedee being left with the hired men shows the disciples did not abandon their family to destitution; they provided for those left behind.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:14-15

The kingdom of God has come near (engiken) — the perfect tense indicates arrival at the threshold. The double call to repentance and faith is the gospel’s basic demand: turn from self-direction and trust in the good news.

1:16-20

Mark emphasizes speed and completeness: “at once,” “without delay,” “they left their nets,” “they left their father.” The authority of the call produces immediate obedience.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:14-15

The kingdom is announced before any miracles. Mark’s order is deliberate: the word precedes the signs. The miracles authenticate the word; they do not replace it.

1:16-20

The immediate response demonstrates divine election, not human decision. Their ability to leave everything immediately was itself the mark of divine enablement.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:14

John was “delivered up” (paradothēnai) — the same word used for Jesus’s own betrayal later. John’s fate prefigures Jesus’s. The forerunner’s imprisonment is not a setback but a signal.

1:15

Engiken — debated between “has arrived” and “is at hand.” The perfect tense with a verb of approach most naturally means arrival at the threshold. The kingdom is present in the person of Jesus.

Verses 21–45 — A Day in Capernaum: Authority Over Teaching, Demons, and Disease
21222324252627282930313233343536373839404142434445

Greek Word Study

exousia ex-OO-sia — authority/power: He taught as one who had exousia. The same word covers his authority over unclean spirits (v.27), over sickness, and later over sins (2:10). Exousia is the signature of the kingdom’s presence. Scribes had derived authority from tradition; Jesus has intrinsic authority from himself.

Places

Capernaum synagogue
Fishing town on northwest shore of Sea of Galilee; synagogue site excavated
Capernaum is Jesus’s ministry base — Mark calls it “his own town” (2:1). The synagogue is the first public venue: Jesus enters the heart of Jewish communal life. The basalt and limestone synagogue remains at Capernaum preserve the site.
Simon’s house, Capernaum
Domestic space near the synagogue
The kingdom enters the home as naturally as the synagogue. Archaeological work at Capernaum has identified a first-century domestic structure beneath the later octagonal church as a plausible candidate for Peter’s house.
Solitary place outside Capernaum
Hillside or desert area north of the town
Jesus retreats before dawn to pray alone — the pattern of withdrawal and return that runs through Mark. The solitary place is the space of renewal between public ministry episodes.

Context

Mark 1:21-45 is a programmatic day in Capernaum displaying Jesus’s authority across every domain: false teaching overturned (synagogue), demons cast out (exorcism), fever healed (Peter’s mother-in-law), the socially excluded restored (leper), and the mission sustained by pre-dawn prayer. The Messianic Secret begins: Jesus silences demons who know his identity (vv.25, 34, 44).

Cross-Reference

  • Lev 14The cleansing ritual for leprosy — Jesus sends the healed man to the priest to fulfil Mosaic law. The miracle is authenticated through covenant procedure.
  • Isa 61:1The Spirit of the Lord is on me…to proclaim freedom for captives — the exorcism fulfils the Isaiah servant’s promise of liberation.
  • Deut 18:15-18The prophet like Moses whose words must be obeyed — the people’s reaction (“what is this authority?”) echoes the promised prophet: Jesus’s authority marks him as Moses’s fulfilment.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:22

Marcus: "He taught as one who had authority, not as the teachers of the law" — the contrast is between prophetic, first-person authority and the scribal method of citing precedents ("Rabbi X says..."). The Qumran Melchizedek text (11QMelch) shows that some Second Temple Jews expected an eschatological teacher with supernatural authority, the "herald" of Isa 52:7. Jesus's teaching style activated these expectations.

1:24

Marcus: "What do you want with us, Jesus of Nazareth?" — the ti hēmin kai soi formula is an LXX idiom of hostile confrontation (Judg 11:12; 2 Sam 16:10; 1 Kings 17:18). "The Holy One of God" (ho hagios tou theou) appears in the Psalms of Solomon and at Qumran as a designation for Elisha and for priestly figures. The demon's identification of Jesus with this title is the first explicit messianic recognition in Mark, and it comes from the adversary.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:21-28

Rhoads: The exorcism in the synagogue establishes the key conflict that drives the first half of Mark: Jesus vs the unclean spirits. But it also establishes a narrative device — the demons' testimony. They consistently identify Jesus correctly (1:24, 34; 3:11; 5:7), but Jesus silences them. The dramatic irony deepens: the most accurate Christological declarations come from hostile witnesses.

1:35-39

Rhoads: The pre-dawn prayer scene is the narrator's first glimpse into Jesus's interior life — and it is immediately interrupted. Simon and company "hunt" for him (katediōxen — pursued, chased). Jesus's response — "Let us go somewhere else" — reveals a character who defines his mission against the crowd's expectations. The pressure to repeat successful acts (healing in Capernaum) is explicitly refused in favor of broader proclamation.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
1:21-28

Chrysostom notes that Jesus taught in the synagogue "as one who had authority, and not as the teachers of the law" — the contrast is not about tone but about source. The scribes cited tradition; Jesus cited nothing but himself. The unclean spirit's recognition — "I know who you are — the Holy One of God" — is, for Cyril of Alexandria, an involuntary confession wrested from the enemy: demons must acknowledge what they most wish to deny. Origen adds that the command "Be quiet! Come out of him!" is significant: the silencing precedes the liberation, so that the crowd is not deceived into thinking the demon's testimony is the basis of faith.

1:35-45

Chrysostom says the early morning withdrawal to pray (v35) is Mark's first portrait of the Son's relationship with the Father — the busyness of ministry never displaces the silence of communion. The healing of the leper (vv.40-45) is, for Ambrose, both physical and symbolic: leprosy in Scripture is the type of sin, and the "touching" Jesus does is the scandal of incarnation — the holy enters what is unclean and makes it clean rather than becoming unclean himself. The command to silence is, for Bede, a lesson in humility: the healed man was to fulfill the law's requirement first, not broadcast the miracle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:22

He taught as one who had authority — not like the scribes. The scribes derived authority from quotation; Jesus spoke in his own name. This is the defining characteristic of his teaching in Mark: it proceeds from his own person, not from tradition.

1:34

The Messianic Secret — Jesus silences the demons who know his identity. He will not allow his identity to be defined by demonic confession. The disclosure must happen on his terms, culminating at the cross.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:35

The pre-dawn prayer is the most important detail in this section. Jesus’s authority in public rests on communion with the Father in private. The pattern of withdrawal to pray before major engagements reveals that his power flows from sustained relationship.

1:38-39

“Let us go somewhere else” — Jesus resists the crowd’s demand to stay and continue healing. His mission is to preach the kingdom throughout Galilee. The healings are signs; the preaching is the substance.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:41

Orgistheis (angered) is preferred by many textual critics as the more difficult reading. If original, the anger is directed at what leprosy has done to the man — the righteous indignation of the new creation’s Lord against disorder introduced by sin and disease.

1:44

“As a testimony to them” — directed at the priests. It is a legal certification and a challenge: the priestly establishment must confront a Levitically valid cleansing they cannot attribute to any natural process.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Jesus
Messiah, Son of God; central figure
Mark presents Jesus through actions. The characteristic euthys (“immediately”) appears 41 times, keeping Jesus always in motion. Identity is revealed progressively: by the Father (1:11), by demons (1:24), by Peter (8:29), by the centurion (15:39).
John the Baptist
Forerunner; new Elijah
John fulfils Mal 3:1 and Isa 40:3, wearing Elijah’s clothing (2 Kings 1:8). His imprisonment triggers the next stage (1:14). His fate prefigures Jesus’s: both are “delivered up” (paradidomi).
Simon (Peter)
Fisherman; first-called disciple
Peter’s house becomes a healing site (1:29-31). Mark’s Gospel preserves Peter’s eyewitness testimony; it begins with Peter’s call and ends with a specific message to Peter (16:7).

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities 18.116-119
Josephus describes John as a good man who exhorted moral living and was executed by Herod Antipas for political reasons.
Confirms John’s historical existence, baptismal ministry, and execution — corroborating Mark 1:4-5 and 6:14-29.
Dead Sea Scrolls — 4Q521
A Qumran fragment speaks of a coming figure who heals the blind, raises the dead, and proclaims good news to the poor.
Confirms that Mark’s kingdom announcement fits recognized Second Temple messianic expectation.

Reception History

Origen, Commentary on Matthew (c.AD 248)
Origen identified Mark as the condensed account of Peter’s preaching in Rome, preserving Peter’s characteristic immediacy and action-focus.
C.H. Dodd, The Apostolic Preaching (1936)
Dodd argued Mark 1:14-15 preserves the earliest apostolic kerygma: time fulfilled, kingdom near, repent, believe — the four-point structure underlying all NT preaching.

Greek-Rooted Reading

Greek-Rooted Reading

1:1 Arche — same word as Genesis 1:1 (LXX). Mark opens a new Genesis.
1:10 Schizo — torn/ripped. Recurs at 15:38 (Temple veil). The Gospel is framed by two tearings.
1:15 Kairos (appointed time) vs chronos (calendar time). The appointed moment has arrived — not merely a date but the hinge of history.
1:41 Orgistheis (angered) — the harder textual reading. Jesus is righteously angry at what disease has done to the man.

Textual Notes

1:1Inclusion of "Son of God"
Sinaiticus (corrected), Alexandrinus include "the Son of God" (huiou theou); Sinaiticus (original), a few early MSS omit it.
External evidence favours inclusion; the phrase is programmatic for Mark's entire Gospel and is almost certainly original.
1:2"In Isaiah the prophet" vs "in the prophets"
NA28/UBS5 reads "in Isaiah the prophet" (en tō Ēsaia tō prophētē); some MSS read "in the prophets" (en tois prophētais), correcting the attribution since Mal 3:1 is also quoted.
"In Isaiah the prophet" is the harder reading and almost certainly original — a scribe would more likely soften the attribution than introduce an apparent error. Mark deliberately attributes the composite citation to its most significant source.

Scholarly Debates

Was Mark written before or after AD 70?
Before AD 70
Papias, Irenaeus; most conservative scholars. Mark 13 predicts the Temple’s destruction; if written after 70, fulfilment would be noted more explicitly. Composition c.AD 50–65.
Pre-70 date
After AD 70
Some critical scholars. The specificity of Mark 13 and its use as a source by Matthew and Luke (both post-70) may indicate Mark itself is c.AD 70–75.
Post-70 date
“After John was put in prison” (1:14) and the absence of past-tense reference to the Temple’s fall supports pre-70 composition. Papias’s testimony and the eyewitness-quality of Petrine detail also favour an early date.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 1 introduces every major theme in compressed form: kingdom proclaimed and demonstrated, authority established over teaching/demons/disease, first disciples called, Messianic Secret begins.
