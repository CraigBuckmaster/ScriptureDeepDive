Temple Controversies: Tenants, Caesar, Resurrection, the Great Commandment

Context

The parable of the wicked tenants is the most transparent of Jesus’s parables — the chief priests immediately understand it is about them (v.12). The vineyard is Israel (Isa 5), the tenants are the leaders, the servants are the prophets, and the son is Jesus. The tax question and the resurrection question follow as further attempts to trap Jesus. Each reply silences the questioners.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 5:1-7My beloved had a vineyard on a fertile hillside — the vineyard parable that Jesus’s parable directly echoes.
  • Ps 118:22The stone the builders rejected has become the cornerstone — applied to Jesus’s rejection and vindication.
  • Exod 3:6I am the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob — the text Jesus uses to argue for resurrection from the Torah itself.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:1-9

Marcus: The wicked tenants parable draws explicitly on Isa 5:1-7 (the vineyard song), which would have been immediately recognized by a Jewish audience. Marcus argues that the escalating violence of the tenants (beating → wounding → killing) and the repeated sending of servants maps onto the prophetic tradition: Israel's history is the history of rejecting the prophets. The son's death "outside the vineyard" (12:8) echoes Heb 13:12 (Jesus suffered "outside the gate") and may reflect awareness that crucifixion took place outside Jerusalem's walls.

12:26-27

Marcus: Jesus's argument for resurrection from Exod 3:6 uses a present-tense verb ("I am the God of Abraham") to establish ongoing relational existence. Marcus notes this exegetical move has parallels in 4 Maccabees 7:19 and the rabbinic tradition (b. Sanhedrin 90b), where the Abrahamic promise establishes that the patriarchs must be alive to receive it. Jesus's argument is not novel but represents the strongest available form of Torah-based resurrection argument — which is why the Sadducees are silenced.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:1-12

Rhoads: The wicked tenants parable is the one parable in Mark where the audience recognizes itself in the story immediately: "They knew he had spoken the parable against them" (12:12). This is the inverse of the Sower parable (4:12: those outside do not understand). Here the insiders — the establishment — understand perfectly and are condemned by their understanding. Rhoads argues this is the climax of the controversy sequence: the parable exposes not confusion but deliberate rejection. The transparent allegory is the point.

12:13-27

Rhoads: The tax and resurrection questions follow a pattern: hostile questioners, a complimentary setup, the trap question, Jesus's request for evidence, the counter-revelation. The narrator characterizes both groups (Pharisees+Herodians, Sadducees) through their framing of questions designed to discredit rather than discover. Jesus's counter-questions consistently expose the bad faith of the inquiry. Rhoads argues that the controversy sequence as a whole characterizes Jesus as rhetorical master of his opponents — each engagement strengthens his authority rather than weakening it.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
12:1-12

The Parable of the Tenants is, for Origen, the most explicit allegorical statement of salvation history in the Synoptics: the vineyard is Israel (from Isaiah 5), the tenants are the religious leaders, the servants are the prophets, the son is the Messiah. Chrysostom notes that the leaders' recognition that the parable was "against them" (v12) and their desire to arrest Jesus on the spot is the parable's action confirming its own prediction — they become the tenants who reject the son at the very moment the son describes their rejection. The capstone quotation from Psalm 118 is, for Bede, the pivot between rejection and vindication.

12:13-27

The tribute question (vv.13-17) is, for Chrysostom, a dilemma that Jesus dissolves by questioning its premise: Caesar's image on the coin places the coin within Caesar's domain; but the human being is made in God's image (Genesis 1:27), and thus belongs to God. The Sadducees' resurrection question (vv.18-27) is answered by Jesus on two fronts: they err about the power of God (resurrection is not a simple extension of present life) and about Scripture (the burning bush passage implies the continued existence of the patriarchs). Bede notes that Jesus uses the most Torah-centric argument imaginable for a Sadducee audience.

12:10-11

The cornerstone — Ps 118:22 applied to the resurrection. The Jewish leaders are the builders who reject; the resurrection is the divine act that makes the rejected stone the cornerstone. The parable predicts both the crucifixion and the vindication.

12:17

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:10-11

The cornerstone — Calvin: the application of Ps 118 is the first explicit resurrection prophecy in the Passion narrative. Jesus predicts both his rejection and his vindication in a single quotation. The leaders understand the threat and intensify their plans.

12:17

Calvin: Give to Caesar…and to God — the distinction is the foundation of two-kingdoms theology. Caesar’s domain is temporal administration; God’s domain is the conscience and the soul. The coin bears Caesar’s image; the human being bears God’s image.

Pharisees and Herodians — the same coalition as 3:6. Political adversaries united against Jesus. The flattering introduction (“you teach the way of God in accordance with the truth”) is a rhetorical setup for the trap.

12:25

They will be like the angels — the resurrection state transcends present marital arrangements. Jesus does not deny bodily resurrection; he denies that the resurrection body is simply the present body extended.

Greek Word Study

Shema SHEH-ma — hear/listen (Hebrew command): Hear, O Israel — shemaʳ Yisraʼēl. The Shema (Deut 6:4-5) is the most fundamental Jewish prayer, recited morning and evening. Jesus quotes it in full to answer the greatest commandment question. The identity claim (the LORD our God, the LORD is one) is prior to the love command: love flows from the identity of the one who is loved.

Context

The greatest commandment exchange is the one genuine theological conversation in the Jerusalem controversies — the scribe agrees with Jesus and is commended as “not far from the kingdom.” The Davidic-sonship question (vv.35-37) is Jesus’s own question, not a trap — the first time he takes the initiative since the authority challenge. The widow’s offering closes the Temple section with a judgment on the religious institution’s exploitation.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 6:4-5The Shema — the foundational Jewish confession that Jesus quotes as the greatest commandment.
  • Lev 19:18Love your neighbor as yourself — the second commandment Jesus adds.
  • Ps 110:1The LORD said to my Lord: sit at my right hand — the text Jesus uses to argue that the Messiah is more than David’s son.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:35-37

Marcus: The Davidic sonship question (Ps 110:1) is the one place in Mark where Jesus himself raises a Christological question rather than responding to one. Marcus argues this reflects the historical Jesus's use of Ps 110 as a key to self-understanding: the Messiah is simultaneously David's son (human lineage, Davidic covenant) and David's Lord (divine prerogative). The combination requires a Christology of both descent and exaltation — precisely what Mark's Gospel has been building toward since 1:1.

12:41-44

Marcus: The widow's offering is placed immediately after the warning about scribes who "devour widows' houses" (12:40). Marcus argues this juxtaposition is deliberate and not simply a commendation of her generosity. The Temple system that Jesus has just condemned is the same system that has consumed this widow's last resources. Her gift (holon ton bion autēs — her entire livelihood) may be the very extraction that 12:40 warned against. The commendation of her faith does not excuse the system.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:28-34

Rhoads: The greatest commandment exchange is the one genuinely collegial conversation in the Jerusalem controversy section. The narrator distinguishes this scribe from the others: he "noticed that Jesus had given them a good answer" — he is characterized by perception and intellectual honesty. Jesus's commendation ("You are not far from the kingdom of God") is the narrative's only positive evaluation of a member of the Jerusalem establishment. Rhoads argues the scene functions as a narrative safety valve: not all members of the system are irredeemably hostile.

12:41-44

Rhoads: The widow's offering is the Gospel's last scene in the Temple. She is characterized by a single action and Jesus's commentary — she never speaks. The narrator gives her no interiority, no history, no name. Yet she becomes the chapter's final and most memorable figure. Rhoads notes this is characteristic of Markan minor-character technique: the nameless and voiceless are given the highest commendation. The Gospel's value scale is consistently inverted: what the world overlooks, the narrator elevates.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
12:28-34

The scribe's question about the greatest commandment is, for Chrysostom, unique in the controversy narratives: he asks in good faith and receives a full answer. The combination of Deuteronomy 6:4-5 (love of God) with Leviticus 19:18 (love of neighbour) is, for Origen, Jesus's own hermeneutical key to the entire Torah. The scribe's endorsement — "to love your neighbour as yourself is more important than all burnt offerings and sacrifices" — leads Jesus to say "You are not far from the kingdom of God." Bede observes that "not far" is not yet "in" — the step from insight to commitment remains.

12:35-44

The question about David's son (vv.35-37) is, for Chrysostom, Jesus's counter-question about his own identity: if the Messiah is merely David's descendant, why does David call him Lord? The answer implicit in the question is that the Messiah is both David's son (by birth) and David's Lord (by nature). The widow's offering (vv.41-44) closes the Temple section with a quiet reversal: the largest visible offerings are surpassed by the smallest. Origen says the widow is the figure of total self-giving, which is the logic of the cross now approaching.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
12:34

You are not far from the kingdom of God — the only commendation Jesus gives to a member of the Jerusalem establishment. The scribe’s recognition that love surpasses sacrifice is “not far” — but not yet there. Knowing the right answer is not the same as entering the kingdom.

12:44

She gave everything she had to live on — the widow’s offering is simultaneously a commendation of her faith and a judgment on the Temple that exploits her. Jesus has just warned about teachers who devour widows’ houses (v.40); here is one such widow, giving her last coins to that same institution.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:34

Not far from the kingdom — Calvin: the scribe has seen through the religious superstructure to the ethical core. But insight is not salvation. The kingdom requires not just understanding love but practicing it — which requires the grace the scribe has not yet received.

12:40-44

Calvin: The widows’ houses devoured / widow’s offering sequence is deliberately juxtaposed. The same institution that exploits the powerless is the recipient of their last coins. The widow’s generosity does not excuse the system that requires it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:35-37

How can he be his son? — Jesus is not denying Davidic descent but extending it. The Messiah is both David’s son (human lineage) and David’s Lord (divine authority). Ps 110:1 requires a Messiah who transcends Davidic categories while remaining within them.

12:44

All she had to live on (holon ton bion) — bios means life/livelihood. She gave not just money but the means of sustaining her life. The contrast with the rich who gave “out of their wealth” (ek tou perisseuontos) is total: surplus vs life-support.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The scribe who asked about the commandments
Named as wise by Jesus; “not far from the kingdom”
The only member of the religious establishment who receives genuine commendation from Jesus in the Passion narrative. His answer demonstrates that some within the system have genuinely understood the Torah’s intention.
The poor widow
Unnamed; model of complete giving
Contrasted directly with the rich donors and the teachers who devour widows’ houses. Her two copper coins given out of poverty are the chapter’s final image of genuine covenant faithfulness.

Ancient Sources

Dead Sea Scrolls — 4Q525
A wisdom text from Qumran that ranks the commandments and emphasizes the dual love command — showing that the Shema and neighbor-love were being linked in pre-Christian Jewish thought.
Confirms that Jesus’s synthesis of Deut 6 and Lev 19 was not novel but represented the best of Second Temple ethical reflection.
Philo, Decalogue 51
Philo identifies love of God and love of neighbor as the two summary commandments of the Decalogue.
Provides Hellenistic Jewish background for the two-commandment synthesis Jesus articulates.

Reception History

Augustine, On Christian Doctrine 1.22
Augustine used the double love commandment as the hermeneutical key for all Scripture interpretation: whatever interpretation does not promote the love of God and neighbor is a misinterpretation, regardless of its technical accuracy.
Thomas Aquinas, Summa Theologiae II-II Q.44
Aquinas argued that the two commandments of love are not additions to the law but the summary that every specific commandment expresses. All particular laws are specifications of the love command.

Textual Notes

12:4"Struck on the head" or "wounded in the head" — kephaliōsantes
The verb kephaliōo̱ (to head/strike on the head) is unique to Mark; some MSS read lithoboleō (stoned) or atimazō (dishonoured). NA28 retains kephaliōsantes as the harder reading.
The hapax legomenon is certainly original — scribes consistently simplified. The meaning (struck on the head, or dealt with in summary fashion) is debated but the form is secure.
12:23"At the resurrection" — positioning variant
Minor variants in the positioning of en tē anastasei (at the resurrection) within the sentence; all MSS include the phrase. NA28 follows the most widely attested word order.
Textually stable; the Sadducees' denial of resurrection is confirmed in v.18. No substantial doubt about the meaning.

Scholarly Debates

Is the widow’s offering praised or lamented?
Praise view
Jesus commends the widow’s complete generosity as the model of covenant faithfulness. She gives more than the rich because she gives everything.
Commendation
Lament view
Jesus is lamenting that the Temple system has consumed this widow’s last resources, fulfilling 12:40 (“they devour widows’ houses”). Her giving is tragic, not exemplary.
Indictment of the system
"This poor widow has put more into the treasury than all the others" (v.43) is commendation language, not lament. The structural placement after 12:40 creates irony, but the primary register of Jesus’s comment is praise of her faith, not critique of her exploitation.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 12 is the Temple controversies chapter: five exchanges that expose the religious establishment’s failure. The chapter ends with the widow as the living embodiment of everything the controversies were about.
