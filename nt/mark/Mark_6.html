Mark 6

Rejection, Mission, Death of John, and Miraculous Provision

Author

John Mark, son of Mary of Jerusalem (Acts 12:12), cousin of Barnabas (Col 4:10), and close associate of both Paul and Peter. Early and unanimous tradition identifies him as “the interpreter of Peter”: Papias of Hierapolis (c. AD 125, preserved in Eusebius, HE 3.39.15) records that Mark “wrote down accurately, though not in order, as much as he remembered of the things said or done by the Lord” — drawing entirely on Peter’s teaching. Irenaeus (Against Heresies 3.1.1), Clement of Alexandria (Hypotyposeis, in Eusebius HE 6.14.6), and the Anti-Marcionite Prologue all confirm the Petrine connection. Mark himself is mentioned in Acts 12:12, 25; 13:13; 15:37–39; Col 4:10; Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11; and 1 Pet 5:13 (“my son Mark”). He was present at some events he records (the young man fleeing naked, 14:51–52, is widely identified as a Markan self-cameo), and his vividness throughout reflects the eyewitness testimony he received directly from Peter in Rome.

When written

c. AD 55–65, almost certainly in Rome, most likely before the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 — which Mark 13 predicts but nowhere records as fulfilled. Clement of Alexandria states the Gospel was written “while Peter was still preaching publicly in Rome.” If so, the most probable window is the late 50s to mid-60s, during Paul’s Roman imprisonment (Acts 28) and Peter’s residence in the capital. Mark is the earliest of the four Gospels (the Markan Priority hypothesis, held by the majority of NT scholars), and both Matthew and Luke appear to have used it as a primary source. The Gospel’s Latin loanwords (legiōn, denarius, praetorium, centurio), its explanation of Jewish customs for non-Jewish readers (7:3–4), and its Latinisms of idiom all point to a Roman Gentile audience.

What prompted it

To preserve Peter’s eyewitness testimony in permanent written form for a church facing persecution under Nero (AD 64), and to answer the most urgent question that question raised: who is Jesus, and why did he die? Mark’s opening declaration — “The beginning of the good news about Jesus the Messiah, the Son of God” (1:1) — is simultaneously title, thesis, and answer. The Gospel is structured around the progressive revelation of that identity: demons recognise Jesus before disciples do (1:24); Peter confesses “You are the Messiah” at the midpoint (8:29); a Roman centurion declares “Surely this man was the Son of God” at the cross (15:39). The relentless pace — euthys (“immediately”) appears 41 times — reflects both Peter’s preaching style and the urgency of a community for whom the Passion narrative was not ancient history but recent, costly, and still unfolding.

Verses 1–13 — Rejected at Nazareth; The Twelve Sent Out
12345678910111213

Greek Word Study

skandalizō skan-da-LI-zo — to take offense/stumble: They took offense at him — eskandalizonto. The verb shares its root with “scandal.” Familiarity breeds contempt: the neighbors of Jesus’s family cannot reconcile his evident authority with his known origins. Their offense is a form of category error — they know the container (carpenter’s son) and so reject the content (Messiah).

Places

Nazareth
Lower Galilee; Jesus’s hometown, c.6km from Sepphoris
Nazareth was a small village in Lower Galilee, probably 200–400 inhabitants. Its insignificance relative to nearby Sepphoris made the neighbors’ familiarity with Jesus’s family even more complete. The place that should have been most receptive proves most resistant.

Context

Nazareth’s rejection of Jesus is the inverse of the Capernaum synagogue’s amazement (1:22). There, authority in teaching led to wonder. Here, authority in teaching leads to offense — because they know who his family is. Jesus’s inability to do miracles there (v.5) is not a limitation on divine power but a statement about faith as the condition of miraculous reception. Immediately after, the Twelve are sent out in pairs — the mission expands even as the hometown rejects.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 11:21Do not prophesy in the name of the LORD or you will die by our hands — the rejection of the prophet in his hometown is an OT pattern: Jeremiah, Elijah, Amos.
  • Deut 25:9The sandal-removal ceremony — shaking dust from feet inverts this: instead of removing a sandal in honor, dust is shaken off in testimony against those who reject the covenant.
  • Luke 4:24No prophet is accepted in his hometown — Luke’s parallel expands the saying and connects it to Elijah and Elisha’s Gentile ministry.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:3

Marcus: "Isn't this the carpenter?" (ho tektōn) — the occupation is not incidental. In Galilean society a tektōn worked on building projects in wood and stone. The newly built Hellenistic city of Sepphoris was under reconstruction throughout Jesus's youth, 6km from Nazareth. Marcus suggests Jesus may have worked there, making him not a rural peasant but someone with experience of Hellenistic urban culture — which would explain his comfort in Gentile settings later in the Gospel.

6:7

Marcus: The sending in pairs (ana duo) reflects the Jewish legal requirement for two witnesses (Deut 17:6; 19:15) and parallels the later rabbinic practice of sending emissaries (shlichim) in pairs. The mission instructions (no bag, no bread, no money, no extra tunic) reflect the practice of charismatic wandering preachers attested in early Syrian Christianity (Didache 11-12), suggesting continuity between Jesus's mission pattern and early church itinerant ministry.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:1-13

Rhoads: The Nazareth rejection scene and the mission charge are structurally paired: Jesus is rejected in his hometown (6:1-6a), then sends the Twelve to be welcomed or rejected in other towns (6:6b-13). The parallel extends to the disciples' instructions: just as Jesus does not stay where he is not received, the Twelve are to shake the dust and leave. Rhoads argues that the disciples' mission is a narrative extension of Jesus's own ministry pattern — they enact what he embodies.

6:6

Rhoads: "He was amazed at their lack of faith" — this is the narrator's only attribution of amazement to Jesus (elsewhere it is the crowds who marvel at him). The reversal is significant: the one who produces wonder is himself astonished by unbelief. Rhoads reads this as characterization through exception: Mark's Jesus is consistently confident, authoritative, and undeterred — which makes the moment of amazement more revealing. Unbelief in the face of evident truth produces a reaction that nothing else does.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1-6

Chrysostom notes the paradox of Nazareth: the people who knew Jesus best believed him least. Their question "Isn't this the carpenter?" is, for Origen, the stumbling block of the incarnation — divine glory hidden in human ordinariness. Jesus's saying that a prophet is not honoured in his hometown is, for Theophylact, not a complaint but an observation about the psychology of familiarity: proximity without reverence produces contempt. The detail that he "could not do many miracles there" is, for Chrysostom, not a limitation of power but a principle of how divine grace operates — it does not override the will that refuses to receive.

6:7-13

The sending of the Twelve in pairs is, for Bede, both a practical and theological decision: two witnesses fulfil the legal requirement, and two provide mutual accountability and encouragement. The instructions about staff, sandals, bag, and belt are, for Origen, a theology of apostolic simplicity: the missionary takes nothing from the world into the mission field, so that whatever God provides is visibly his provision. The shaking of dust from the feet is, for Chrysostom, a formal declaration that the messengers have fulfilled their responsibility — the hearers' rejection becomes their own judgment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:5

He could not do any miracles there — not a limitation on omnipotence but a theological principle. Mark’s “could not” (ouk edynato) is deliberately provocative. Jesus’s miracles are not demonstrations of power over unbelief but the flowering of faith’s receptivity.

6:7

Two by two — the sending in pairs reflects the Jewish legal requirement for two witnesses and provides mutual accountability and support for the mission.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:5-6

Calvin: The will of God and the faith of the recipients are not two rival causes; they are ordered causes. God wills to heal through the channel of faith. When that channel is blocked by unbelief, God’s will to heal is not frustrated but appropriately withheld. Calvin resists the implication that human unbelief limits God absolutely.

6:11

Shake the dust off your feet — the gesture is both symbolic and judicial. It declares that the town has made a definitive choice and will be held responsible for it. The dust of their own land testifies against them.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:3

Mary’s son — not Joseph’s son. The designation “Mary’s son” rather than Joseph’s is unusual in Jewish naming convention. It may reflect Joseph’s death before Jesus’s ministry, or may be a deliberate slight (implying uncertain paternity).

6:13

Anointed many sick people with oil — the only explicit mention of anointing with oil for healing in the Synoptics. James 5:14-15 institutionalizes this practice. The oil is not the medium of healing but a physical sign of the Spirit’s work.

Verses 14–29 — The Death of John the Baptist
14151617181920212223242526272829

Greek Word Study

kairos KY-ros — the right/appointed time: Finally the opportune time came (v.21) — eukairou hēmeras. The same word Mark uses for the kingdom’s decisive time (1:15) is here used for Herod’s birthday banquet and John’s execution. The irony is deliberate: the appointed time that fulfils God’s purposes operates through the same history as the appointed time that Herodias exploits for murder.

Context

The death of John the Baptist is narrated as a flashback — the framing device is Herod’s guilty conscience about Jesus (v.14-16). The story is the longest non-Jesus narrative in Mark and reads like court intrigue: a powerful man, a resentful woman, a reckless oath, a dancing girl. John’s fate is Jesus’s fate in miniature: the righteous prophet silenced by political power. His disciples’ burial of the body (v.29) prefigures Joseph of Arimathea’s burial of Jesus.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kings 19:1-3Jezebel’s threat against Elijah — Herodias maps onto Jezebel exactly: the foreign woman of power who pursues the righteous prophet to his death. The parallels are too precise to be coincidental.
  • Esth 5:3The king’s rash promise (up to half my kingdom) echoes Ahasuerus’s promise to Esther — but here the model is inverted: Esther asks for lives to be saved; Herodias’s daughter asks for a death.
  • Matt 14:1-12Matthew’s parallel adds that John was the one who condemned Herod’s marriage — confirming Mark’s compressed account.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:14-16

Marcus: Herod's guilty-conscience identification of Jesus as John "raised from the dead" is politically and theologically significant. Marcus notes that resurrection of a specific individual before the general eschatological resurrection was not a standard Jewish expectation — making Herod's statement extraordinarily theologically advanced, even if driven by guilt rather than faith. The passage also shows that Jesus's fame had penetrated the highest levels of Galilean political society, making his eventual trial less surprising.

6:20

Marcus: Herod "liked to listen to John" and was "greatly puzzled" (ēporei) — the verb appears in Dan 2:1 LXX for the troubled perplexity of Nebuchadnezzar before a prophet. The parallel is pointed: as Babylon's king was disturbed and fascinated by the divine prophet Daniel, so Antipas's tetrarch is disturbed and fascinated by John. Both rulers end up destroying what they feared. The typology of the powerful man who destroys the prophet he knows to be righteous recurs in Mark 15 with Pilate.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:14-29

Rhoads: The beheading of John is a flashback embedded within the mission of the Twelve (6:7-13, 30). Structurally, this is another intercalation: Twelve sent out / John executed / Twelve return. The juxtaposition is deliberate. While the Twelve are proclaiming the kingdom, the kingdom's forerunner is being killed for proclamation. Rhoads argues that the intercalation prepares the reader for the cost of the disciples' mission — and for Jesus's own death at the hands of a similar coalition of power and social pressure.

6:17-29

Rhoads: Herodias is one of the few antagonist characters in Mark given a motivation (she nursed a grudge, eneikhon). The narrator characterizes her through this cold, sustained enmity — contrasted with Herod's conflicted fascination. The daughter (unnamed in Mark) is characterized entirely through action: she dances, she consults her mother, she returns with urgency, she presents the head. Her lack of stated motivation makes her more chilling — she is the instrument of her mother's will, unquestioned.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
6:14-29

The death of John the Baptist is narrated in the Catena as a study in moral cowardice. Herod's fear of John, his conflicted fascination with his teaching, and his final capitulation to Herodias's vanity through his daughter's dance are, for Ambrose, the anatomy of a man who knows what is right and lacks the will to do it. Chrysostom notes that the oath ("up to half my kingdom") is both grandiose and foolish — Herod binds himself with a public promise rather than a private commitment, leaving himself no escape. John's death is, for Bede, the last act of the preparatory age: the herald is silenced before the king's own passion begins.

6:26-29

The king's grief at his own rash oath is, for Chrysostom, the portrait of a man in complete bondage to his own public image: he is "greatly distressed" but does not want to refuse because of those reclining with him. Bede sees in Herod the type of all religious formality without moral backbone — he respected John and listened to him, but could not act on what he heard. The disciples' taking of John's body and laying it in a tomb is noted immediately before the feeding of the five thousand — the death of the herald and the abundance of the king run in deliberate sequence.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:20

Herod feared John and protected him — the most psychologically complex portrait of a villain in Mark. Herod knows John is righteous, protects him, likes to listen to him, and yet beheads him to save face before dinner guests. Pilate will follow the same tragic pattern in chapter 15.

6:29

John’s disciples took his body and laid it in a tomb — the note prefigures 15:46-47. Both the forerunner and the Lord have disciples who care for their bodies after violent death.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:26

Because of his oaths and his dinner guests — the murder of John the Baptist comes down to social embarrassment. Calvin: the fear of public shame is one of the most powerful forces leading people away from righteousness. Herod knew what was right; he chose what was convenient.

6:29

John’s burial — Calvin: the disciples’ courage in claiming John’s body is itself an act of testimony. They honour the martyr when the powerful seek to hide the evidence of their crime.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:17

His brother Philip’s wife — Josephus confirms this detail (Antiquities 18.109-119) but calls the brother “Philip” (a different Philip from the tetrarch). The marriage was condemned by John as violating Lev 18:16 (marrying a brother’s wife while the brother lives).

6:20

He liked to listen to him — the imperfect (ēkouen) indicates a regular practice. Herod was a hearer but not a doer. James 1:22 describes the same tragic pattern.

Verses 30–56 — Feeding Five Thousand; Walking on Water; Healings at Gennesaret
303132333435363738394041424344454647484950515253545556

Greek Word Study

egō eimi e-GHO ei-MI — I am (divine name): Take courage! It is I — the Greek is egō eimi, the same construction as the Septuagint’s translation of God’s name in Exod 3:14. Jesus’s self-identification to the terrified disciples in the storm is not merely “it’s me” but the divine “I AM.” The walking on the sea is a theophany.

Context

The feeding of five thousand and the sea-walking are the twin miracles of Mark 6:30-56. The feeding echoes Moses’s manna in the wilderness; the walking on water echoes YHWH’s mastery over the sea (Job 9:8). Mark’s editorial comment (v.52) is devastating: the disciples had not understood about the loaves — their hearts were hardened. The same word used for Pharaoh’s hardening is applied to the disciples.

Cross-Reference

  • Num 27:17The people who have no shepherd — the phrase Jesus’s compassion fulfils. Israel without leadership is Moses’s anxiety; Jesus’s compassion is the answer.
  • 2 Kings 4:42-44Elisha feeds a hundred men with twenty loaves — the closest OT parallel. Jesus feeds five thousand with five loaves. The multiplication is on a different scale entirely.
  • Job 9:8He alone stretches out the heavens and treads on the waves of the sea — the divine sea-walking that Jesus performs.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:34

Marcus: "Sheep without a shepherd" (probata mē echonta poimena) — the phrase is a direct echo of Num 27:17, where Moses asks God to appoint a leader for Israel "so the LORD's people will not be like sheep without a shepherd," and of 1 Kings 22:17 and Ezek 34:5-8, where the same image describes Israel's condition under failed leadership. Jesus's compassion is not merely emotional but messianic: he is responding to the very condition that the shepherd-king passages promised the Messiah would remedy.

6:52

Marcus: "Their hearts were hardened" (pēpōrōmenē) — the perfect passive of pōroō, the same judicial hardening language used for Pharaoh (Exod 4:21 LXX), for the Pharisees (Mark 3:5), and for Israel in Isa 6:10. Marcus argues that Mark is making a pointed theological statement: the disciples' incomprehension is not innocent slowness but a form of the same hardening that afflicts Jesus's opponents. The distinction between insiders and outsiders is less stable than 4:11 suggests.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:30-44

Rhoads: The feeding of five thousand is structured around a reversal of expected roles. The disciples begin as problem-identifiers ("Send the people away") and end as servers of abundance ("They all ate and were satisfied"). The twelve basketfuls — each disciple presumably carrying one — visually represents the overflow that results when Jesus transforms their limited resources. Rhoads notes that the eucharistic action sequence (take, give thanks, break, give) will reappear at 14:22-23, retrospectively reading the feeding as a foreshadowing of the Last Supper.

6:45-56

Rhoads: Jesus's walking on the sea and his "about to pass by them" (parelthen) uses LXX theophanic language (Exod 33:19-23; 1 Kings 19:11) — when YHWH passes by, the divine glory is revealed to the prophet. Mark's narrator frames the sea-walking as a theophany the disciples fail to receive: "they had not understood about the loaves; their hearts were hardened" (6:52). The cumulative narrative effect is a growing gap between what Jesus reveals and what the disciples can receive — a gap the reader is positioned to observe and lament.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
6:30-44

The feeding of the five thousand is, for Chrysostom, the eucharistic sign par excellence: Jesus takes, blesses, breaks, and gives — the four verbs of the Last Supper. Origen notes that the twelve baskets of leftovers exceed what they began with, which is a figure of divine abundance: the gift of God is never diminished by distribution. The detail that Jesus had compassion because the crowds were "like sheep without a shepherd" (v34) is, for Ambrose, the key to understanding the miracle — the feeding of bodies proceeds from the recognition of spiritual need.

6:45-56

Jesus walking on the water is, for Chrysostom, the second sea miracle in Mark — but the disciples' response is, if anything, worse than after the storm (4:41): they are "completely amazed, for they had not understood about the loaves." Bede says this parenthetical remark is Mark's editorial comment on the condition of the disciples' hearts throughout this period — the miracles accumulate, but the revelation is not yet complete. The healings at Gennesaret where people touched the hem of his cloak echo the bleeding woman (5:28) — Theophylact notes the contact motif as Mark's repeated picture of faith as reaching toward Christ.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:34

Sheep without a shepherd — Jesus’s compassion (splanchnisthē) is the deepest Markan emotion word. He is moved at the gut level by the crowd’s leaderless condition. The compassion precedes the feeding: he teaches first (v.34), then feeds. Word before bread.

6:52

Their hearts were hardened — the same Greek word (pōroō) used of Pharaoh and of the Pharisees in 3:5. The disciples’ incomprehension after the feeding is not intellectual but spiritual: they have seen without understanding.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:37

You give them something to eat — Calvin: the command to the disciples precedes the miracle. Jesus does not bypass human agency but works through it. The disciples must distribute what they cannot produce. The church’s role is to be the conduit of provision it did not generate.

6:52

Their hearts were hardened — Calvin: a warning that the accumulation of miracles does not automatically produce faith. The disciples have seen more than any previous generation; yet they are harder than expected. Privilege without obedience produces greater blindness.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:45

Immediately Jesus made his disciples get into the boat — the word euthys again. The urgency of the dismissal suggests that the crowd’s enthusiasm (John 6:15: they intended to make him king) required quick decisive action. The withdrawal to the mountain to pray is both response to the crowd’s misunderstanding and preparation for the next challenge.

6:52

Their hearts were hardened (pēpōrōmenē) — the perfect passive suggests a state resulting from an ongoing process. The disciples’ incomprehension is not innocent confusion but the beginning of the same hardening that Mark attributes to Jesus’s opponents.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Herod Antipas
Tetrarch of Galilee and Perea; John’s killer
Herod’s moral portrait is Mark’s most complex — he knows John is righteous, protects and listens to him, yet kills him to save face. He reappears in 8:15 (leaven of Herod) and 13:9.
Herodias
Wife of Herod Antipas; the driving force behind John’s death
The Jezebel-figure of the Gospel: a woman of political power who exploits the opportune moment. Her grudge (eneikhon, 6:19) is cold and sustained.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities 18.109-119
Josephus describes John’s execution by Herod Antipas in detail, attributing the motive to Herod’s fear of John’s popular influence rather than Herodias’s grudge.
Josephus confirms the historical event but provides a political rationale where Mark emphasizes the personal/domestic one. Both may be true simultaneously.
2 Kings 4:42-44 (Elisha feeding)
The closest OT parallel to the feeding of 5,000 — Elisha feeds 100 with 20 loaves and there are leftovers.
Jesus’s feeding is on an incomparably larger scale, establishing the Elisha-Jesus typology while far exceeding it.

Reception History

N.T. Wright, Jesus and the Victory of God
Wright argued that the feeding of the five thousand was both a real historical event and a deliberate symbolic act: Jesus was enacting the new Exodus, feeding Israel in the wilderness as YHWH had done through Moses. The groups of hundreds and fifties (6:40) echo the Sinai organization of Exod 18:21.
Karl Barth, Church Dogmatics II/2
Barth used the walking on water to expound the divine freedom: God is not bound by the laws of the creation he sustains. The theophanic egō eimi (6:50) is the revelation of the God who is free to act within creation as its Lord, not its prisoner.

Mark 6 presents rejection (Nazareth), martyrdom (John), and miraculous provision together. The pattern that will define the Passion — faithful mission, opposition, and divine sustenance — is established in miniature across these three sections.

Textual Notes

6:3"The carpenter" or "the carpenter's son"
NA28/UBS5 reads ho tektōn (the carpenter, applied to Jesus); some MSS read ho tou tektonos huios (the carpenter's son, harmonising with Matt 13:55).
The harder reading ("the carpenter") is likely original; the harmonising variant avoids direct identification of Jesus with a manual trade.
6:22Herodias's daughter — "Herodias herself" or "his daughter Herodias"
NA28 reads hē thygatēr autou Hērōdiados (his daughter Herodias); variant hē thygatēr tēs Hērōdiados (the daughter of Herodias). The NA28 reading makes the dancer Herod's daughter by Herodias, named Herodias like her mother.
Most interpreters and translators (NIV: "daughter of Herodias") follow the variant. Josephus names the dancer Salome — not Herodias. The name confusion likely arose through scribal error.

Scholarly Debates

Did Jesus physically walk on water?
Historical view
The event is recorded in three Gospels (Matt, Mark, John) with distinctive independent details. The disciples’ reaction (terror → worship) is consistent with genuine encounter with the unexpected.
Historical miracle
Symbolic/theological view
Some interpreters read the walking on water as a post-resurrection appearance that has been placed in the ministry narrative. The egō eimi and the disciples’ worship (Matt 14:33) support this reading.
Post-resurrection tradition
"He was about to pass by them" (6:48) uses language unique to OT theophany (Exod 33, 1 Kings 19). The event is best understood as a genuine historical occurrence with rich theological significance.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 6 is the mission chapter: rejection at Nazareth leads to the wider Galilean mission; the feeding of five thousand enacts the kingdom’s provision; John’s death prefigures the cost of faithfulness.
