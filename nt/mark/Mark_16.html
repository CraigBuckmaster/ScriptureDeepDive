Mark 16

The Empty Tomb; The Risen Lord; The Great Commission

See on Timeline — Resurrection
Author

John Mark, son of Mary of Jerusalem (Acts 12:12), cousin of Barnabas (Col 4:10), and close associate of both Paul and Peter. Early and unanimous tradition identifies him as “the interpreter of Peter”: Papias of Hierapolis (c. AD 125, preserved in Eusebius, HE 3.39.15) records that Mark “wrote down accurately, though not in order, as much as he remembered of the things said or done by the Lord” — drawing entirely on Peter’s teaching. Irenaeus (Against Heresies 3.1.1), Clement of Alexandria (Hypotyposeis, in Eusebius HE 6.14.6), and the Anti-Marcionite Prologue all confirm the Petrine connection. Mark himself is mentioned in Acts 12:12, 25; 13:13; 15:37–39; Col 4:10; Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11; and 1 Pet 5:13 (“my son Mark”). He was present at some events he records (the young man fleeing naked, 14:51–52, is widely identified as a Markan self-cameo), and his vividness throughout reflects the eyewitness testimony he received directly from Peter in Rome.

When written

c. AD 55–65, almost certainly in Rome, most likely before the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 — which Mark 13 predicts but nowhere records as fulfilled. Clement of Alexandria states the Gospel was written “while Peter was still preaching publicly in Rome.” If so, the most probable window is the late 50s to mid-60s, during Paul’s Roman imprisonment (Acts 28) and Peter’s residence in the capital. Mark is the earliest of the four Gospels (the Markan Priority hypothesis, held by the majority of NT scholars), and both Matthew and Luke appear to have used it as a primary source. The Gospel’s Latin loanwords (legiōn, denarius, praetorium, centurio), its explanation of Jewish customs for non-Jewish readers (7:3–4), and its Latinisms of idiom all point to a Roman Gentile audience.

What prompted it

To preserve Peter’s eyewitness testimony in permanent written form for a church facing persecution under Nero (AD 64), and to answer the most urgent question that question raised: who is Jesus, and why did he die? Mark’s opening declaration — “The beginning of the good news about Jesus the Messiah, the Son of God” (1:1) — is simultaneously title, thesis, and answer. The Gospel is structured around the progressive revelation of that identity: demons recognise Jesus before disciples do (1:24); Peter confesses “You are the Messiah” at the midpoint (8:29); a Roman centurion declares “Surely this man was the Son of God” at the cross (15:39). The relentless pace — euthys (“immediately”) appears 41 times — reflects both Peter’s preaching style and the urgency of a community for whom the Passion narrative was not ancient history but recent, costly, and still unfolding.

Verses 1–8 — The Empty Tomb

Greek Word Study

ēgerthē ay-GER-thay — he has been raised (divine passive): He has risen — ēgerthē: the divine passive. God raised Jesus from the dead. The passive voice is the NT’s consistent grammar for the resurrection: not that Jesus raised himself but that the Father raised the Son (Acts 2:24; Rom 8:11). The resurrection is the Father’s vindication of the Son.

Places

The tomb
Near Golgotha; Joseph of Arimathea’s newly cut rock tomb
The same tomb where the women watched the burial (15:47) is where they return at dawn. The tomb’s location near Golgotha and the specific large stone make confusion impossible: they are at the right place. The stone is rolled away; the linen lies there; the angel is seated where the body was.

Context

Mark’s Gospel ends at 16:8 in the oldest and most reliable manuscripts: the women flee, trembling and afraid, and say nothing to anyone. This abrupt ending has puzzled readers since antiquity — hence the later addition of vv.9-20. But the ending is profoundly Markan: the Gospel began with an announcement (“the beginning of the good news”) and ends by throwing the story forward. The reader, like the women, is left with the empty tomb and the commission to go to Galilee.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:6

Marcus: "He has risen!" (ēgerthē) — the divine passive declares the resurrection as an act of God. Marcus notes that the passive voice is the consistent NT grammar for the resurrection: not that Jesus raised himself but that the Father raised the Son (Acts 2:24; 3:15; Rom 8:11; Gal 1:1). The angel's announcement combines identification ("Jesus the Nazarene, who was crucified") with proclamation ("He has risen") — the risen one is the crucified one. The resurrection does not replace the Passion; it vindicates it.

16:7

Marcus: "Tell his disciples and Peter" — the specific naming of Peter in the resurrection announcement is, for Marcus, the Gospel's most personal moment. Peter is the one whose denial was most explicit (14:66-72); the one most specifically warned (14:30); the one who "broke down and wept" (14:72). His specific inclusion in the first resurrection message is the Gospel's enacted statement of grace: the most conspicuous failure is the most specifically sought. Marcus reads this as confirming the Gospel's Petrine origin: Peter would not have preserved the denial without the restoration.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:1-8

Rhoads: The empty tomb scene characterizes the women through action and speech: they buy spices, they walk at dawn, they ask about the stone, they enter, they see, they flee. The narrator gives them speech (15:47: two Mary's watch where he was buried; 16:3: who will roll the stone?) but no interiority until the final verse: "trembling and bewildered... they said nothing to anyone, because they were afraid." Rhoads argues that the women's silence mirrors and reverses the disciples' flight at 14:50 — not abandonment but paralysis before the overwhelming event.

16:8

Rhoads: "They said nothing to anyone, because they were afraid" — the most discussed ending in the NT. Rhoads argues that 16:8 is the intended ending: it is consistent with Mark's narrative technique of withheld resolution, consistent with the women's characterization (fear, silence), and consistent with the Gospel's opening gambit (the reader knows what the characters do not). The Gospel's silence is the reader's invitation: the story does not resolve — it opens. The reader is the next character who must decide what to do with the empty tomb.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
16:1-8

The women's visit to the tomb is, for Chrysostom, the faithful remnant: they were last at the cross and first at the resurrection. The practical concern about the stone (v3: "Who will roll the stone away?") is, for Origen, the figure of every human obstacle to the resurrection faith — the stone is already rolled back before they arrive. The young man in white is, for Bede, an angel — Mark's restraint in not naming the figure is itself significant. The command to tell the disciples "and Peter" is, for Chrysostom, the pastoral heart of the resurrection announcement: Peter is named specifically so he will know he is not excluded despite his denial.

16:2-8

The ending of Mark at verse 8 — "they said nothing to anyone, for they were afraid" — is, for Origen and Chrysostom alike in the Catena tradition, not a deficient ending but a deliberately open one. The fear is the human response to the shattering of all categories; the silence is temporary, not permanent (the Gospel exists as proof that the silence was eventually broken). Bede says the abrupt ending is Mark's final characteristic: all his endings are sudden, driving the reader forward. The resurrection is announced, the mission is commissioned, and the reader is left to be the continuation of the story.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:6

He has risen! He is not here — the resurrection is announced by location: not here. The tomb is evidence by its emptiness. The specific “see the place where they laid him” is an invitation to verify.

16:7

Go, tell his disciples and Peter — the specific naming of Peter is the most tender detail in the resurrection announcement. Peter, who denied three times (14:66-72), is specifically included. The one who most needed restoration is specifically named.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:8

They said nothing to anyone, because they were afraid — Calvin: the women’s silence is immediately temporary; the appearance of the risen Jesus to them (as Matt and John record) overcomes the fear. Mark’s ending anticipates what will happen; it does not describe it. The silence is the gap that the reader’s own encounter with the risen Christ must fill.

16:7

He is going ahead of you into Galilee — Calvin: the return to Galilee reverses the journey of the Passion. The disciples were scattered (14:50); the risen Lord reassembles them in Galilee where they were first called. The resurrection is a new beginning, not merely a restoration.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:8

The original ending of Mark — the vast majority of textual scholars regard 16:8 as the original ending of Mark. The abrupt close (gar endings in Greek — “they were afraid, for...”) is linguistically unusual but not impossible. The Gospel’s characteristic “euthys” urgency carries into the ending: the story is still moving.

16:7

And Peter — the two-word addition singles out Peter from the group. The angel’s message includes him specifically. This anticipates Peter’s restoration (alluded to in John 21) and his central role in the early church. Mark’s Gospel, traditionally the voice of Peter’s preaching, ends with the risen Christ seeking Peter out.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:15

Go into all the world and preach the gospel to all creation — the commission is universal in scope. The Galilean mission to Israel (6:7-13) and the Gentile encounters (7:24-37) have been building to this: the gospel is for all creation.

16:19

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:16

Whoever believes and is baptized will be saved — Calvin: faith is the condition; baptism is the sign that confirms and seals it. The verse does not make baptism a second condition of salvation alongside faith but joins the sign to the reality it represents.

16:19-20

He was taken up into heaven and sat at the right hand of God. Then the disciples went out and preached everywhere — Calvin: the Ascension is the precondition of the mission. The Lord who goes to the right hand of God is precisely the one who then works with his disciples throughout the world.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:9-20

The shorter ending of Mark (only known in a few manuscripts) and the longer ending (16:9-20) are both secondary additions. The longer ending synthesizes material from Matt 28, Luke 24, John 20, and Acts. It became canonical in the Western church and is included in the NT canon, but it is not part of the original Markan text.

16:19

He was taken up into heaven — the Ascension is the expected conclusion to the resurrection narrative. The longer ending places Jesus at the right hand of God, fulfilling the Ps 110:1 quotation in 12:36 and the trial declaration in 14:62.

Marcus Commentary

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8; Mark 8–16 (Word Biblical Commentary) — Faithful Paraphrase
16:9-14

Marcus treats the longer ending of Mark (vv.9-20) with careful historical caution: the manuscript evidence strongly suggests it was not original to Mark's Gospel but was added by a second-century editor drawing on the other Gospels. Nonetheless, it entered the canon early and was widely read as Scripture in the patristic period. The pattern of the appearances — Mary first, then two disciples, then the Eleven — mirrors Luke and John. The repeated theme of unbelief (vv.11, 13, 14) is the longer ending's theological contribution: the resurrection was not self-evident even to those who loved Jesus.

16:15-20

The mission commission in vv.15-16 is, for Marcus, a post-Easter expansion of the Galilean mission (6:7-13): the scope is now universal and the authority is now fully disclosed. The confirming signs (vv.17-18) represent the charismatic dimension of early Christian mission as documented in Acts and the Pauline letters. Marcus notes that the ascension in v19 is the most compressed in the canon — no cloud, no disciples watching, just the simple statement of session at the right hand. The final verse "the Lord worked with them" is for Marcus the theological thesis of Acts, summarised in a single sentence.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
16:9-14

The appearances in the longer ending are, for Chrysostom, a progressive restoration of the scattered community: first to Mary Magdalene (the individual), then to two disciples on the road, then to the Eleven. The repeated unbelief of those who heard the testimony — they did not believe Mary, they did not believe the two — is, for Origen, not a rebuke of the early witnesses but a record of what faith required: these disciples had to be confronted personally before they believed. The rebuke in v14 ("He rebuked their stubborn refusal to believe") is, for Bede, the last correction before the commission.

16:15-20

The Great Commission in Mark is the broadest in scope: "all creation." Chrysostom notes that this exceeds even Matthew's "all nations" — the Markan commission is cosmic. The confirming signs are, for Ambrose, not conditions for salvation but evidences accompanying the proclamation in the apostolic era. The ascension is described with characteristic Markan economy: he was taken up, he sat at God's right hand, they went out. Bede says the final verse — "the Lord worked with them" — is Mark's last and most important word: the Gospel does not end with the ascension but with the continuing partnership between the exalted Christ and his sent people.

Ancient Sources

Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus (4th century)
The two most complete and oldest surviving Greek NT manuscripts both end Mark at 16:8 with a scribal note that the ending was disputed in the manuscripts known to them.
The most important external evidence that 16:1-8 is the original ending of Mark.
Eusebius, Quaestiones ad Marinum (c.AD 300)
Eusebius explicitly states that accurate copies of Mark known to him ended at 16:8 and that the longer ending was absent from almost all manuscripts.
The earliest explicit textual commentary on the ending question; confirms the scholarly consensus.

Reception History

N.T. Wright, The Resurrection of the Son of God (2003)
Wright argued that Mark’s original ending at 16:8 is deliberate: the Gospel ends where new creation begins. The women’s fear and silence are the appropriate response to the eruption of God’s new world into the old. The reader is left to respond in the women’s place.
Richard Bauckham, Jesus and the Eyewitnesses (2006)
Bauckham argued that Mark’s Gospel is grounded in Peter’s eyewitness testimony, and that the specific mention of Peter in 16:7 is the Gospel’s final personal touch: the one whose testimony underlies the whole narrative is specifically invited to the resurrection encounter that will restore him.

Literary Structure

Peter in 16:7: The Restoration Anticipated16:7
Mark 16 ends as it began: abruptly, incompletely, with a command to “go.” The Gospel’s characteristic urgency (euthys) carries into the ending: the story is still in motion. The empty tomb is the beginning, not the conclusion.

Greek-Rooted Reading

Greek-Rooted Reading

16:6 Ēgerthē — he has been raised (divine passive). God raised Jesus. The consistent NT grammar for the resurrection: an act performed by the Father on the Son.
16:7 Kai tō Petrō — and Peter. Two Greek words; the most tender detail in the resurrection announcement. The most conspicuous failure is specifically named and included.
16:8 Gar — for/because. The original ending ends with a “for” — one of the strangest grammatical endings in ancient literature. The Gospel refuses to wrap up; it throws the reader forward.

Textual Notes

16:8The original ending — ending at ephobonto gar
Sinaiticus, Vaticanus, and the majority of early MSS end at 16:8 with ephobonto gar ("for they were afraid"). Later MSS add the Longer Ending (16:9-20); a few add the Shorter Ending.
The Longer Ending (16:9-20) is absent from the two oldest complete Greek MSS, was unknown to Clement and Origen, and is noted as absent or disputed by Eusebius and Jerome. Most textual critics regard 16:8 as the original ending of Mark, whether deliberately abrupt or accidentally truncated.
16:9-20The Longer Ending — textual status
Irenaeus (c.AD 185) is the earliest patristic witness to cite from the Longer Ending, confirming it circulated by the late second century. The vocabulary and style differ measurably from 1:1-16:8.
The Longer Ending is canonical (included in the NT canon since the fourth century) but not original to Mark. It is a second-century synthesis drawing on resurrection traditions from Matthew, Luke, John, and Acts. Its canonical status does not depend on Markan authorship.

Scholarly Debates

Where does the original Mark end?
16:8 original ending
The oldest manuscripts (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus), the earliest patristic witnesses (Clement, Origen, Eusebius), and internal stylistic evidence all point to 16:8 as the original ending. The abrupt ending is Markan in character.
Original ending at 16:8
16:9-20 canonical ending
The longer ending is included in the NT canon (Irenaeus quotes it as early as c.AD 185) and has been the church’s received text for most of Christian history.
Canonical longer ending
"He has risen" at 16:6 is the Gospel’s theological climax. The story’s continuation — the disciples’ mission, the Lord working with them — is real but occurs outside the Gospel’s frame. Mark’s ending is not incomplete; it is deliberately open.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 16 is the resurrection chapter: the empty tomb, the angel’s announcement, and the commission. The Gospel that began with “the beginning of the good news” ends not with a conclusion but with a continuing mission.
