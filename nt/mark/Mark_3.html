Mark 3

The Twelve Appointed; Controversy Deepens

Author

John Mark, son of Mary of Jerusalem (Acts 12:12), cousin of Barnabas (Col 4:10), and close associate of both Paul and Peter. Early and unanimous tradition identifies him as “the interpreter of Peter”: Papias of Hierapolis (c. AD 125, preserved in Eusebius, HE 3.39.15) records that Mark “wrote down accurately, though not in order, as much as he remembered of the things said or done by the Lord” — drawing entirely on Peter’s teaching. Irenaeus (Against Heresies 3.1.1), Clement of Alexandria (Hypotyposeis, in Eusebius HE 6.14.6), and the Anti-Marcionite Prologue all confirm the Petrine connection. Mark himself is mentioned in Acts 12:12, 25; 13:13; 15:37–39; Col 4:10; Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11; and 1 Pet 5:13 (“my son Mark”). He was present at some events he records (the young man fleeing naked, 14:51–52, is widely identified as a Markan self-cameo), and his vividness throughout reflects the eyewitness testimony he received directly from Peter in Rome.

When written

c. AD 55–65, almost certainly in Rome, most likely before the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 — which Mark 13 predicts but nowhere records as fulfilled. Clement of Alexandria states the Gospel was written “while Peter was still preaching publicly in Rome.” If so, the most probable window is the late 50s to mid-60s, during Paul’s Roman imprisonment (Acts 28) and Peter’s residence in the capital. Mark is the earliest of the four Gospels (the Markan Priority hypothesis, held by the majority of NT scholars), and both Matthew and Luke appear to have used it as a primary source. The Gospel’s Latin loanwords (legiōn, denarius, praetorium, centurio), its explanation of Jewish customs for non-Jewish readers (7:3–4), and its Latinisms of idiom all point to a Roman Gentile audience.

What prompted it

To preserve Peter’s eyewitness testimony in permanent written form for a church facing persecution under Nero (AD 64), and to answer the most urgent question that question raised: who is Jesus, and why did he die? Mark’s opening declaration — “The beginning of the good news about Jesus the Messiah, the Son of God” (1:1) — is simultaneously title, thesis, and answer. The Gospel is structured around the progressive revelation of that identity: demons recognise Jesus before disciples do (1:24); Peter confesses “You are the Messiah” at the midpoint (8:29); a Roman centurion declares “Surely this man was the Son of God” at the cross (15:39). The relentless pace — euthys (“immediately”) appears 41 times — reflects both Peter’s preaching style and the urgency of a community for whom the Passion narrative was not ancient history but recent, costly, and still unfolding.

Verses 1–12 — Healing on the Sabbath; Crowds from Every Direction
123456789101112

Greek Word Study

orgē or-GAY — anger/wrath: He looked around at them in anger — meta orgēs. This is one of the few places where Mark describes an emotion in Jesus without softening it. Combined with “deeply distressed” (syllypoumenos), the passage reveals both the righteous anger of God at hardness of heart and the grief of a God who loves those who reject him.

Places

Synagogue (location unspecified, likely Capernaum)
Galilean synagogue; the regular Sabbath venue
The synagogue is the recurring stage for Sabbath controversy. Jesus’s choice to heal there on the Sabbath is deliberate and public — not hidden compassion but prophetic challenge to the synagogue authorities.
The Sea of Galilee (withdrawal)
Northwest shore; Jesus’s boat as mobile pulpit
Jesus withdraws from the synagogue to the sea — from institutional religion to open-air ministry. The boat becomes a platform and escape mechanism (3:9). The sea geography is both practical and symbolic: the kingdom cannot be contained by the synagogue.

Context

The Sabbath healing crystallizes the conflict: the Pharisees have already decided to kill Jesus (v.6) before his Galilean ministry is even half over. The coalition with the Herodians — normally political rivals — shows how threatening Jesus has become. The crowd geography of vv.7-8 is remarkable: all four points of the compass — Galilee, Judea, Idumea, Transjordan, and the Phoenician coast — converge on Jesus.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 20:8-11Remember the Sabbath day to keep it holy — the commandment at the heart of the controversy.
  • Lev 18:5Keep my decrees and laws, for the person who obeys them will live by them — the life-principle Jesus applies in his “to save life or to kill” question.
  • Isa 35:3-6Then will the lame leap like a deer — the eschatological healing that Jesus’s miracle in the synagogue fulfils.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:5

Marcus: Jesus's anger (orgē) combined with grief (syllypoumenos) at hardness of heart (pōrōsis) is a theologically rich combination. In the LXX, pōrōsis describes the hardening that comes as divine judgment on persistent rejection (Isa 6:10; Exod 7:3). The fact that Jesus experiences grief alongside anger suggests that the hardening is not merely judicial but is mourned as a tragedy. This is consistent with the prophetic tradition in which divine judgment is always accompanied by divine sorrow.

3:6

Marcus: The Pharisee-Herodian coalition is historically striking. The Pharisees were a religious reform movement; the Herodians were supporters of Roman client-king Herod Antipas — political adversaries under normal conditions. Marcus notes that a similar coalition appears in the Damascus Document (CD 1:14-21) as an example of parties united by hostility to the righteous teacher. The unusual alliance signals the unprecedented threat Jesus represented to both groups.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:1-6

Rhoads: The synagogue healing scene economizes information to heighten conflict. The man's condition is stated in one phrase; the Pharisees' intent is stated before the action; Jesus's anger and question are compressed into two sentences. The narrator reveals Jesus's inner state (anger, grief) but never reveals the Pharisees' interior response to the healing. The withholding of their reaction after the miracle focuses the reader entirely on the decision to kill — character revealed through action, not psychology.

3:7-12

Rhoads: The summary scene (3:7-12) is a narrator's aerial view — compressed summary of what has been happening across the whole region. Such summaries bracket and interpret the surrounding episodes. The geographic inventory (Galilee, Judea, Jerusalem, Idumea, Transjordan, Tyre, Sidon) signals the widening scope of the narrative. The demons' repeated falling and confessing create a counterpoint to the human characters' silence and confusion.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1-6

Chrysostom notes that Jesus looked at the Pharisees "with anger and deep distress at their stubborn hearts" — the only place in the Synoptics where Jesus's anger is explicitly named. Aquinas, following Gregory the Great, says that anger at sin is not a vice but a virtue: the absence of righteous anger in the face of injustice is indifference, not holiness. The healing on the Sabbath, says Bede, is deliberate provocation — Jesus does not wait for a less contentious moment. The Pharisees' immediate plot with the Herodians signals, for Origen, the unholy alliance that only Jesus could produce: political and religious enemies united by a common threat.

3:7-12

Theophylact says the great crowd from every region of Israel pressing on Jesus — to the point of needing a boat (v9) — represents the universal reach of the Gospel before the Gentile mission is formally commissioned. The unclean spirits' repeated identification of Jesus as "the Son of God" is, for Chrysostom, a pattern throughout Mark: the spiritual world recognizes what the religious establishment denies. The silencing command is, for Origen, protective of the faith: belief built on demonic testimony would be corrupt from its foundation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:5

He looked around at them in anger — Jesus’s anger is righteous and specific: it is directed at hard hearts that would rather preserve religious tradition than see a man healed. The grief (syllypoumenos) softens and deepens the anger: he is not indignant but heartbroken.

3:6

The Pharisees and Herodians plot together — political opposites united by threat. The death conspiracy begins in chapter 3, not chapter 11. Mark’s passion shadow falls early.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:5

Calvin: The hardness of heart (pōrōsis) is a judicial condition — God’s response to persistent rejection. The Pharisees have not merely misunderstood; they have hardened themselves against the evident truth. Jesus’s grief is the grief of a God who wills that none perish.

3:6

The coalition with the Herodians is a moral inversion: religious leaders ally with the compromised collaborators they otherwise despised in order to eliminate the prophet who is exposing them both.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:5

Syllypoumenos — deeply grieved/distressed. This is the most emotionally charged verse in Mark’s first three chapters. The divine anger and grief at human hardness are held together without resolution — a window into the character of God.

3:12

He gave strict orders not to tell anyone — the Messianic Secret in its strongest form to this point. The prohibition is given to the demons, not the healed. The demonic knowledge of Jesus’s identity is suppressed because it cannot be the channel of covenant disclosure.

Verses 13–35 — The Twelve Appointed; Beelzebul Controversy; True Family
1314151617181920212223242526272829303132333435

Greek Word Study

apostolos a-POS-to-los — sent one/apostle: He appointed twelve that he might send them out — apostellō, the root of apostolos. The Twelve are defined by two purposes: to be with him (identity) and to be sent by him (mission). The order is deliberate: communion precedes commission. They are not sent because they are capable; they are sent because they are his.

Places

Mountain (location unspecified, likely above the Sea of Galilee)
Elevated ground in Galilee; possibly the Horns of Hattin area
The mountain appointment echoes Sinai — Moses received the law and appointed covenant representatives on the mountain. Jesus appoints the new Israel’s twelve representatives on the same kind of elevated covenant ground.

Context

The mountain appointment of the Twelve creates a new Israel — twelve tribes, twelve apostles. The Beelzebul controversy is the most theologically serious confrontation to this point: the Jerusalem scribes attribute the Spirit’s work to Satan, which Jesus identifies as the unforgivable sin. The true family section redefines kinship: biological family is relativized by covenant family. Jesus’s own family thinks he is mad (v.21).

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 24:1-11Moses’s appointment of twelve representatives at Sinai — the Twelve echo this founding moment of Israel’s covenant community.
  • Isa 49:6I will make you a light for the Gentiles — the apostolic mission will eventually fulfil the servant’s universal scope.
  • Isa 49:24-25Can plunder be taken from warriors? — the strong man passage (3:27) alludes to this: Jesus is plundering Satan’s house.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:14

Marcus: The appointment of twelve to be "with him" and to be sent out creates a dual mandate that mirrors the prophet-disciple relationship in 1 Kings 19:19-21 and 2 Kings 2. The number twelve is constitutive: Israel was organized around twelve tribal representatives at Sinai (Exod 24:4) and at the Jordan crossing (Josh 4:2). The appointment of twelve apostles is not merely administrative but symbolically reconstitutes Israel around Jesus as its new center.

3:28-30

Marcus: The "blasphemy against the Holy Spirit" has Second Temple parallels in the concept of apostasy as an unforgivable offense. The Damascus Document (CD 8:18-21) speaks of those who have "departed from the way" as beyond covenant restoration. Jesus defines the unforgivable sin with specific precision: it is the attribution of the Spirit's work to Beelzebul — a judgment rendered by those who have had full access to the evidence and inverted it deliberately.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:13-19

Rhoads: The mountain appointment scene introduces the Twelve as a named group but immediately subverts any simple hero-characterization. The list ends with Judas Iscariot "who betrayed him" — the final name in the list bears the shadow of the entire narrative. Mark's characterization of the disciples will become increasingly problematic: given names, given authority, given the secret of the kingdom (4:11), and yet consistently failing to understand. The narrator introduces this complexity from the first naming.

3:20-35

Rhoads: The Beelzebul controversy employs the Markan "sandwich" structure (intercalation) for the first time: family arrives (3:21) / Beelzebul controversy (3:22-30) / family arrives (3:31-35). The technique invites readers to interpret each story through the other. The family thinks Jesus is "out of his mind" (3:21); the scribes think he acts by demonic power (3:22). Both misread him. The true family — those who do God's will — is defined in contrast to both misreadings.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
3:13-19

The appointment of the Twelve is, for Ambrose, an act of sovereign choice: Jesus went up the mountain and "called to him those he wanted" — election precedes commission. Chrysostom notes that the number twelve deliberately mirrors the twelve tribes of Israel: the new community constitutes the restored people of God. The two-fold purpose — "that they might be with him and that he might send them out" — is, for Aquinas, the irreducible structure of apostolic ministry: presence before proclamation, being before doing.

3:20-35

The Beelzebul controversy and the true family discourse are, for Origen, structurally linked. The scribes say Jesus is demon-possessed; Jesus responds with the parable of the strong man. His mother and brothers wait outside; Jesus responds by redefining family as those who do God's will. Bede notes the double scandal: rejected by the establishment and apparently abandoned by his family, Jesus refuses both accusations and redefines both categories. The saying about the unforgivable sin is, for Chrysostom, not a statement about divine limitation but about the condition that makes forgiveness impossible — the persistent attribution of God's work to Satan closes the very capacity for repentance.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:14-15

Appointed twelve — the number is Israel-constituting. To be with him and to be sent out are the two poles of discipleship: identity and mission, reception and proclamation.

3:28-29

The unforgivable sin is not a past act but a present state: calling the Spirit’s work Satanic. It is the sin of those who, seeing all the evidence, permanently invert good and evil. It is not a sin committed accidentally but the final hardening of a persistently refused conscience.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:28-30

Calvin: The blasphemy against the Holy Spirit is the sin of those who, with full information and against their conscience, attribute the Spirit’s work to evil. It is not a single lapse but a hardened posture. Calvin is careful not to terrify tender consciences: those who fear they have committed it almost certainly have not, since the sin involves no such fear.

3:35

The new family constituted by the will of God is the church. Calvin notes that this does not nullify natural family bonds but relativizes them: covenant bond is primary.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:19

Judas Iscariot, who betrayed him — the betrayer is named in the same breath as the appointment. His presence in the Twelve is not a mistake but a divine purpose. His name closes the list — the narrative shadow falls from the beginning.

3:29

Eternal sin (aiōniou hamartēmatos) — not merely a sin with eternal consequences but a sin that is permanently unresolved, never forgiven. The context (v.30) defines it specifically: attributing the Holy Spirit’s activity to an unclean spirit.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The Twelve Apostles
Appointed representatives of the new Israel
Their appointment on the mountain echoes Moses at Sinai. Named list: Peter, James, John (Boanerges), Andrew, Philip, Bartholomew, Matthew, Thomas, James son of Alphaeus, Thaddaeus, Simon the Zealot, Judas Iscariot.
Jerusalem scribes
Teachers from the capital; Beelzebul accusers
Their accusation — that Jesus casts out demons by Satan — is the most serious theological charge in the Gospel and occasions the unforgivable sin warning.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Jewish War 2.118
Josephus describes the Zealots as a movement of violent resistance to Rome. Simon the Zealot’s inclusion alongside Levi the tax collector (a Roman collaborator) in the Twelve is one of the most remarkable features of apostolic community.
The Twelve include men whose political convictions were mutually exclusive. The kingdom creates a community that overrides political identity.

Reception History

Karl Barth, Church Dogmatics IV/2
Barth used the Beelzebul controversy to define sin as the confusion of good and evil — specifically, calling the reconciling work of God “evil.” The unforgivable sin is the sin of religion that has hardened into opposition to the Spirit.
Dietrich Bonhoeffer, Life Together
Bonhoeffer used Mark 3:14 — “that they might be with him” — as the basis for the priority of community life over mission. Formation precedes sending.

Greek-Rooted Reading

Greek-Rooted Reading

3:5 Orgē (anger) + syllypoumenos (deeply grieved) — two emotions held together in one look. Divine anger and divine grief are not contradictions.
3:14 Apostellō — to send out. The root of “apostle.” The Twelve exist to be with Jesus, then to be sent from him.
3:29 Aiōnios (eternal) — the sin of permanently inverting good and evil. Not a momentary lapse but a fixed moral posture.

Textual Notes

3:14Appointment of the Twelve — "whom he also named apostles"
Sinaiticus (original), Vaticanus lack "whom he also named apostles" (hous kai apostolous ōnomasen); Western and Byzantine MSS include it.
The shorter reading is probably original; the addition harmonises with Luke 6:13. The Twelve's apostolic designation may be secondary in Mark.
3:29Eternal sin — "has no forgiveness"
NA28 ouk echei aphesin (has no forgiveness); minor variants in word order but the sense is stable across the tradition.
Textually secure; the theological weight has generated more interpretive than textual controversy.

Scholarly Debates

What is the unforgivable sin?
Specific blasphemy view
The sin is unique to those who witnessed Jesus’s exorcisms and attributed them to Satan — a historically bounded sin not available to later generations.
Historically bounded
Persistent state view
The sin is a permanent hardening against the Spirit’s testimony that can occur in any generation, whenever someone persistently and finally rejects the clear evidence of the Spirit’s work.
Persistent hardening
"He said this because they were saying he has an impure spirit" (3:30) — the context is specific. But the principle — calling the Spirit’s work demonic — may apply whenever the same inverted judgment is fully and finally made.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 3 is the chapter of formation and opposition simultaneously: the Twelve are appointed as the conflict with Jerusalem intensifies. Both the covenant community and its enemies are taking definitive shape.
