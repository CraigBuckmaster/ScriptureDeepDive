Mark 4

Parables of the Kingdom; Stilling the Storm

Author

John Mark, son of Mary of Jerusalem (Acts 12:12), cousin of Barnabas (Col 4:10), and close associate of both Paul and Peter. Early and unanimous tradition identifies him as “the interpreter of Peter”: Papias of Hierapolis (c. AD 125, preserved in Eusebius, HE 3.39.15) records that Mark “wrote down accurately, though not in order, as much as he remembered of the things said or done by the Lord” — drawing entirely on Peter’s teaching. Irenaeus (Against Heresies 3.1.1), Clement of Alexandria (Hypotyposeis, in Eusebius HE 6.14.6), and the Anti-Marcionite Prologue all confirm the Petrine connection. Mark himself is mentioned in Acts 12:12, 25; 13:13; 15:37–39; Col 4:10; Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11; and 1 Pet 5:13 (“my son Mark”). He was present at some events he records (the young man fleeing naked, 14:51–52, is widely identified as a Markan self-cameo), and his vividness throughout reflects the eyewitness testimony he received directly from Peter in Rome.

When written

c. AD 55–65, almost certainly in Rome, most likely before the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 — which Mark 13 predicts but nowhere records as fulfilled. Clement of Alexandria states the Gospel was written “while Peter was still preaching publicly in Rome.” If so, the most probable window is the late 50s to mid-60s, during Paul’s Roman imprisonment (Acts 28) and Peter’s residence in the capital. Mark is the earliest of the four Gospels (the Markan Priority hypothesis, held by the majority of NT scholars), and both Matthew and Luke appear to have used it as a primary source. The Gospel’s Latin loanwords (legiōn, denarius, praetorium, centurio), its explanation of Jewish customs for non-Jewish readers (7:3–4), and its Latinisms of idiom all point to a Roman Gentile audience.

What prompted it

To preserve Peter’s eyewitness testimony in permanent written form for a church facing persecution under Nero (AD 64), and to answer the most urgent question that question raised: who is Jesus, and why did he die? Mark’s opening declaration — “The beginning of the good news about Jesus the Messiah, the Son of God” (1:1) — is simultaneously title, thesis, and answer. The Gospel is structured around the progressive revelation of that identity: demons recognise Jesus before disciples do (1:24); Peter confesses “You are the Messiah” at the midpoint (8:29); a Roman centurion declares “Surely this man was the Son of God” at the cross (15:39). The relentless pace — euthys (“immediately”) appears 41 times — reflects both Peter’s preaching style and the urgency of a community for whom the Passion narrative was not ancient history but recent, costly, and still unfolding.

Verses 1–20 — The Parable of the Sower and Its Explanation
1Again Jesus began to teach by the lake. The crowd that gathered around him was so large that he got into a boat and sat in it out on the lake, while all the people were along the shore at the water’s edge.2He taught them many things by parables, and in his teaching said:3“Listen! A farmer went out to sow his seed.4As he was scattering the seed, some fell along the path, and the birds came and ate it up.5Some fell on rocky places, where it did not have much soil. It sprang up quickly, because the soil was shallow.6But when the sun came up, the plants were scorched, and they withered because they had no root.7Other seed fell among thorns, which grew up and choked the plants, so that they did not bear grain.8Still other seed fell on good soil. It came up, grew and produced a crop, some multiplying thirty, some sixty, some a hundred times.”9Then Jesus said, “Whoever has ears to hear, let them hear.”10When he was alone, the Twelve and the others around him asked him about the parables.11He told them, “The secret of the kingdom of God has been given to you. But to those on the outside everything is said in parables12so that, “they may be ever seeing but never perceiving, and ever hearing but never understanding; otherwise they might turn and be forgiven!”13“Don’t you understand this parable? How then will you understand any parable?14The farmer sows the word.15Some people are like seed along the path, where the word is sown. As soon as they hear it, Satan comes and takes away the word that was sown in them.16Others, like seed sown on rocky places, hear the word and at once receive it with joy.17But since they have no root, they last only a short time. When trouble or persecution comes because of the word, they quickly fall away.18Still others, like seed sown among thorns, hear the word;19but the worries of this life, the deceitfulness of wealth and the desires for other things come in and choke the word, making it unfruitful.20Others, like seed sown on good soil, hear the word, accept it, and produce a crop—some thirty, some sixty, some a hundred times what was sown.”

Greek Word Study

mystērion mys-TAY-ri-on — mystery/secret: The secret (mystērion) of the kingdom has been given to you. Mystērion in NT usage is not a puzzle to be solved but a divine purpose previously hidden and now disclosed to insiders. The disciples receive the kingdom’s inner logic; outsiders hear only the surface story.

Context

The parable of the Sower is Jesus’s explanation of why his ministry produces mixed results. The parable is also the key to all parables (v.13). Four soil types — path, rocky, thorny, good — represent four responses to the word. The Isaiah 6 citation in v.12 is not divine determinism but prophetic acknowledgment that some will harden against clear light.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 6:9-10Ever seeing but never perceiving — the judicial hardening Isaiah was warned about. Jesus applies it to those who hear the parables without inner reception.
  • Isa 55:10-11My word will not return to me empty but will accomplish what I desire — the seed metaphor for the word’s efficacy.
  • Ezek 2:7You must speak my words whether they listen or fail to listen — the prophetic commission underlying the parable logic.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:11-12

Marcus: The Isa 6:9-10 citation is crucial to Marcus's reading. He argues (following Jewish exegetical conventions) that the hina ("so that") clause reflects the pesher-style interpretation found at Qumran: scripture is being applied to the present situation as its prophetic fulfilment. The parables do not create blindness — they reveal and confirm the blindness already present in those who have heard and refused.

4:14

Marcus: "The farmer sows the word" — Marcus notes that in Second Temple Judaism the "word" (logos/davar) was often personified as a divine agent (see Wis 18:15; 4 Ezra 9:31). The sower parable may be deliberately evoking this tradition: the word sown is not merely human instruction but the powerful divine speech that, in creation, accomplished what it named.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:1-9

Rhoads: The parable of the Sower is the narrative's first extended speech by Jesus. The narrator has previously summarized Jesus's teaching (1:14-15, 21-22) but not reported it directly. Now the reader hears the teacher's voice at length — and the content of the speech is about different kinds of hearing. The parable is thus reflexive: a story about hearing embedded in a scene of listening, told by a character whose words the reader is at this moment receiving.

4:10-12

Rhoads: "When he was alone" — the private teaching scenes consistently separate insiders (the Twelve + others) from outsiders. The narrator's characterization of the disciples as "those around him" with the Twelve is positively coded here: they are privileged with explanation that the crowd is denied. But Mark's narrative will progressively undermine this privilege — the insiders will prove not to understand either (4:13: "Don't you understand this parable?").

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1-9

Origen's homilies on this parable are among the most extensive in the Catena tradition. He notes that the sower sows indiscriminately — on all soils — as a figure of divine generosity that does not withhold the word from those who may reject it. The four soils are, for Chrysostom, not four categories of people but four stages that can characterize any single person's response to the word across a lifetime. The detail "He who has ears to hear, let him hear" closes the parable with an invitation, not a judgment — the parable itself is the seed.

4:10-20

The disciples' private question reveals, for Chrysostom, the virtue of perseverance in seeking understanding. The mystery of the kingdom is not withheld from the crowd to condemn them but because the crowd has not yet asked — asking is already the beginning of reception. Bede notes that the explanation of the parable given to the disciples is itself an act of trust: Jesus opens his interpretive method to them, making them not just recipients of the word but interpreters of it. The four soils in interpretation become, for Origen, a call to self-examination: on which soil does each hearer land?

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:11-12

The mystery of the kingdom has been given — given, not earned. The parables simultaneously reveal to insiders and conceal from those who have hardened themselves. This is not arbitrary but judicial: the parables confirm the posture of the heart that was already formed before the hearing.

4:19

The deceitfulness of wealth — chrēmatos apatē. Wealth’s deception is that it promises what only the kingdom provides. Jesus identifies it as the most practically dangerous of the three thorns because it is the most culturally normalized.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:11

The mystery of the kingdom is given — free gift, not human discovery. Calvin uses this verse to ground his doctrine of election: the disciples receive what they could not have acquired by insight or effort. Grace is the prior condition of all understanding.

4:16-17

Those who receive the word with joy but have no root — Calvin’s warning against what he calls “temporary faith,” which produces initial enthusiasm without covenant depth. The joy is real but not saving because it is rootless.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:12

Otherwise they might turn and be forgiven — the hina clause is deliberately ambiguous. Is the concealment purpose or result? Most interpreters take it as result: the parables function as a test, and those who harden respond to the test by seeing without perceiving.

4:20

A hundred times what was sown — the Greek is paradoxical: the harvest is the same word as the sowing (speirō), not a different word for harvest. The emphasis is on continuity: the sown word, faithfully received, produces an exponential return of itself.

Verses 21–34 — Lamp, Growing Seed, Mustard Seed
21He said to them, “Do you bring in a lamp to put it under a bowl or a bed? Instead, don’t you put it on its stand?22For whatever is hidden is meant to be disclosed, and whatever is concealed is meant to be brought out into the open.23If anyone has ears to hear, let them hear.”24“Consider carefully what you hear,” he continued. “With the measure you use, it will be measured to you—and even more.25Whoever has will be given more; whoever does not have, even what they have will be taken from them.”26He also said, “This is what the kingdom of God is like. A man scatters seed on the ground.27Night and day, whether he sleeps or gets up, the seed sprouts and grows, though he does not know how.28All by itself the soil produces grain—first the stalk, then the head, then the full kernel in the head.29As soon as the grain is ripe, he puts the sickle to it, because the harvest has come.”30Again he said, “What shall we say the kingdom of God is like, or what parable shall we use to describe it?31It is like a mustard seed, which is the smallest of all seeds on earth.32Yet when planted, it grows and becomes the largest of all garden plants, with such big branches that the birds can perch in its shade.”33With many similar parables Jesus spoke the word to them, as much as they could understand.34He did not say anything to them without using a parable. But when he was alone with his own disciples, he explained everything.

Greek Word Study

automatē au-to-MA-tay — by itself/automatically: The soil produces grain automatically (v.28) — automatē, the root of our English “automatic.” The growing-seed parable is unique to Mark. The farmer’s non-role in the growth is the point: the kingdom grows by its own divine power, not by human management or strategy.

Context

Three further parables extend the kingdom’s logic: the lamp (the hidden will be revealed), the growing seed (divine growth without human control), and the mustard seed (from smallest to largest). All three together address the disciples’ likely anxiety about the kingdom’s small and contested beginning. The teaching is eschatological reassurance.

Cross-Reference

  • Dan 4:12A great tree whose branches sheltered all creatures — the tree of Nebuchadnezzar’s dream is the background for the mustard seed’s large branches. Jesus deflates the image: the kingdom is not Babylonian empire but a garden plant — yet it shelters all birds.
  • Ezek 17:22-24I will plant a tender sprig and it will become a splendid cedar — the messianic tree of Ezekiel, here recontextualized into the mustard seed’s paradox of small beginnings.
  • Joel 3:13Put in the sickle, for the harvest is ripe — the harvest imagery of v.29 draws on Joel’s eschatological judgment scene, applying it to the kingdom’s growth parable.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:26-29

Marcus: The parable of the Growing Seed is unique to Mark. Marcus notes its eschatological-apocalyptic register: the earth "automatically" (automatē) producing grain echoes the apocalyptic expectation of miraculous abundance in the new age (1 Enoch 10:19: the vine will produce wine by itself; 2 Bar 29:5: the earth will produce ten-thousandfold). The kingdom's growth is on the same register as these eschatological abundance texts — divine miracle, not natural process.

4:30-32

Marcus: The mustard seed was proverbially the smallest seed in Jewish usage (b. Niddah 5:2; m. Tohorot 8:8). The tree it becomes is not, botanically, the largest — but the proverb depends on the contrast between the proverbial smallest and the unexpected largest. The birds nesting in its branches echo the LXX of Dan 4:9-21 (the tree sheltering the nations), deliberately scaling down the imperial tree metaphor to a garden plant: the kingdom is not Babylonian empire but prodigious growth from humble origins.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:21-25

Rhoads: The lamp sayings are gnomic aphorisms delivered in the same setting as the parable explanations but addressed to insiders. They shift register from parabolic narrative to direct wisdom speech. Mark's Jesus characteristically code-switches between these modes: extended parable, compressed aphorism, direct command, eschatological prediction. The variety of speech modes contributes to the character's rhetorical authority — no single register fully captures or contains him.

4:26-34

Rhoads: "With many similar parables Jesus spoke the word to them, as much as they could understand. But when he was alone with his disciples, he explained everything" (4:33-34). This editorial comment by the narrator establishes a systemic distinction between public and private teaching. The reader, positioned as a privileged insider by the narrator's reliable perspective, receives both the parables and their explanations — a position of understanding denied to the public in the story.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
4:21-25

Chrysostom says the lamp saying is the direct application of the parable: the word, once received, is not for private possession but for proclamation. The lamp hidden under a bowl is, for Bede, a figure of any truth suppressed for fear of men. The saying "With the measure you use, it will be measured to you" is, for Augustine, a law of spiritual generosity: those who share freely what they have received find their capacity for understanding expanded; those who hoard it find it diminish.

4:26-34

The parable of the growing seed (unique to Mark) is, for Chrysostom, the most encouraging of the kingdom parables: the farmer does not understand how the seed grows, and he is not required to. The kingdom advances by a power intrinsic to the word itself, not by human management. The mustard seed is, for Origen, not primarily about numerical growth but about the contrast between the insignificant beginning and the vast result — a lesson in not despising the day of small things.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:22

Whatever is hidden is meant to be disclosed — the Messianic Secret is temporary. Jesus conceals his identity now but the concealment is purposive: the full disclosure will come at the appointed time. The lamp parable is a promise that the hiddenness is not final.

4:25

Whoever has will be given more — the economy of the kingdom is counter-intuitive. Those who receive truth grow in capacity for more truth; those who reject it lose even what they had. Understanding the word is cumulative in both directions.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:26-29

Calvin: The growing seed parable is a comfort for those distressed by the kingdom’s slow progress. The farmer sleeps; the seed grows. The church does not need to manufacture its own growth by superior strategy. It needs to sow the word and trust the soil’s (divine) power.

4:30-32

The mustard seed is the pattern of all God’s work: he begins with what human wisdom would dismiss as insufficient and produces what human expectation could not anticipate. Calvin applies this to the Reformation: a small movement hidden in Wittenberg becomes a vast shelter.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:28

First the stalk, then the head, then the full grain — the sequential development of the crop describes the kingdom’s staged growth: present in seed-form (Jesus’s ministry), growing through the church’s mission, complete at the eschatological harvest.

4:33

As much as they could understand — Jesus accommodates his teaching to his hearers’ capacity. The parables are not maximally obscure; they are calibrated to what can be received.

Verses 35–41 — Stilling the Storm
35That day when evening came, he said to his disciples, “Let us go over to the other side.”36Leaving the crowd behind, they took him along, just as he was, in the boat. There were also other boats with him.37A furious squall came up, and the waves broke over the boat, so that it was nearly swamped.38Jesus was in the stern, sleeping on a cushion. The disciples woke him and said to him, “Teacher, don’t you care if we drown?”39He got up, rebuked the wind and said to the waves, “Quiet! Be still!” The wind died down and it was completely calm.40He said to his disciples, “Why are you so afraid? Do you still have no faith?”41They were terrified and asked each other, “Who is this? Even the wind and the waves obey him!”

Greek Word Study

siōpa si-OH-pa — be quiet/be silent: Quiet! Be still! — siōpa, pephimōso. The first word is the same used to silence the demon in 1:25. Jesus commands the storm with the same authority he commands unclean spirits. The word “pephimōso” (be muzzled) is a strong restraining command. The sea is treated as a hostile power that submits to the word of the Creator.

Places

Sea of Galilee — crossing to eastern shore
The lake itself; heading toward Gerasene/Gentile territory
The crossing is not incidental: it is the first deliberate movement from Jewish to Gentile territory. The storm that must be overcome to reach the other side may carry symbolic freight — the opposition to the kingdom’s Gentile expansion.

Context

The storm-stilling is simultaneously a nature miracle and a Christological disclosure. The disciples’ terror shifts from the storm to Jesus: they were afraid of the waves; now they are terrified of the one who commanded them. The question “Who is this?” (v.41) is the right response to the wrong kind of fear — it begins the disciples’ long journey toward understanding.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 107:28-30He calmed the storm to a whisper; he stilled the waves — the psalm that the disciples would have known as YHWH’s characteristic act. Jesus does what the psalm says only YHWH does.
  • Job 38:8-11Where were you when I shut up the sea? — God’s creation authority over the sea is precisely what Jesus exercises.
  • Ps 89:9You rule over the surging sea; when its waves mount up, you still them — another YHWH sea-mastery text fulfilled in the stern of a Galilean fishing boat.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:35-41

Marcus: The sea-stilling echoes YHWH's sovereignty over the sea throughout the OT (Pss 65:7; 89:9; 107:28-30; Job 38:8-11). The specific combination of rebuking the wind and saying "Peace! Be still!" (siōpa, pephimōso) to the water recalls Ps 104:7 (LXX: "At your rebuke they flee"). The sea was associated in the ancient Near East with chaos and death (cf. Leviathan, Yam, the Abyss). Jesus's act is not merely a weather miracle but a creation-sovereignty demonstration.

4:38

Marcus: "Teacher, don't you care if we drown?" The disciples' cry has OT precedent: Ps 44:23-24 ("Awake, Lord! Why do you sleep?... why do you hide your face?"). The sleeping-God motif in the Psalms is the context for the disciples' reproach. But where the Psalmist calls on God to wake from sleep and act, Jesus is literally asleep — and acts on waking. The echo frames Jesus's sleeping as the divine rest that precedes the decisive saving act.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:35-41

Rhoads: The storm scene is the narrator's first use of the sea as a narrative space. The boat, the water, the wind, and the fear are recurring elements across chs. 4-8. Rhoads identifies the sea crossings as structural markers: each crossing initiates a new narrative episode, typically involving movement between Jewish and Gentile territory. The storm is the first transition — and it produces the narrative's most important question to date: "Who is this? Even the wind and waves obey him!" (4:41). The disciples' question carries the weight of the entire Gospel.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
4:35-41

The stilling of the storm is, for Chrysostom, both a nature miracle and a discipleship test in sequence: the disciples wake Jesus with rebuke ("Don't you care?") and he responds with rebuke of the sea and of their fear. Ambrose notes that Jesus sleeping in the stern is the picture of God apparently absent during the church's trials — and the disciples' error is in concluding absence means indifference. The question "Who is this?" is, for Theophylact, not ignorance but wonder: they knew who Jesus was as a teacher; they were being confronted with who he was as Lord of creation.

4:36-39

Chrysostom notes that the other boats accompanying them (v36) are mentioned only here in the Synoptics — they too would have benefited from the stilling. Bede observes that Jesus asleep on the cushion is the posture of complete trust: the one who commands the sea rests in the storm he has not yet stilled. The disciples' rebuke — "Don't you care if we drown?" — is, for Ambrose, the voice of faith under pressure reverting to complaint. The calming is immediate and total: "completely calm" (pantelōs) is Mark's superlative of stillness, the anti-type of the chaos the disciples feared.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:38

Don’t you care if we drown? — the disciples’ question is the most raw in the Gospel. Their terror produces a charge against Jesus’s concern. His response does not address their emotional state directly but acts — and then challenges their faith. The rebuke is gentle but searching.

4:41

Who is this? — the correct question. The disciples have been with Jesus through exorcisms, healings, and teaching. The storm-stilling finally breaks through their slow understanding. But the answer is not yet given; it must await Peter’s confession in 8:29.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:38

Calvin: The disciples’ charge — “don’t you care?” — is our own charge in every crisis of faith. Calvin does not rebuke the disciples for asking; he notes that the prayer, however faithless in form, is directed to the right person. The very fact that they wake Jesus rather than doing nothing shows a residual, if inadequate, faith.

4:41

They were terrified and asked each other who this is. Calvin: the right sequence is always fear first, then question. The fear of God precedes the knowledge of God. The disciples’ terror is the beginning of understanding, not its end.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:39

Quiet! Be still! — two imperatives. The first (siōpa) is immediate: stop. The second (pephimōso) is a command to remain quiet, as if anticipating the wind’s temptation to restart. The same double command appears in 1:25 to the demon.

4:41

The wind and the waves obey him — hypakouousin: present tense, general truth. The disciples express an ongoing, habitual reality they have just discovered: the elements are permanently subject to his word.

Ancient Sources

Babylonian Creation Myth (Enuma Elish)
The cosmic battle between the creator Marduk and the chaos-sea Tiamat — a Near Eastern background for sea-mastery as a divine prerogative.
Jesus’s storm-stilling draws on the same cultural resonance without adopting the polytheistic framework: the one God’s sovereign mastery over chaos is expressed through the word of Jesus.

Reception History

D.E. Nineham, Saint Mark (Pelican Commentary)
Nineham noted that Mark 4:35-41 combines the sea-crossing theme (Gentile mission) with the storm-stilling theme (Christological disclosure) in a way that makes the pericope more than a nature miracle: it is a programmatic statement about who Jesus is and where the mission is going.
C.S. Lewis, Miracles
Lewis used the storm-stilling to argue that miracles are not violations of nature but restorations — the Creator reaching back into the creation to demonstrate what nature was originally designed to do before it was disordered.

Mark 4 is the kingdom’s self-explanation: the parables are not illustrations but the kingdom’s logic made audible. The storm-stilling is then the kingdom’s power made visible.

Textual Notes

Minor variation: some MSS read eis tēn thalassan (into the sea — i.e., a boat on the water); others en tē thalassē (on the sea). Context (v.1b: the boat) supports the boat-on-the-water reading.

4:40"Have you no faith?" or "Why are you afraid?"
NA28/UBS5 reads both clauses: "Why are you afraid? Have you still no faith?" Some MSS omit "still" (oupō vs ou).
The "still" (oupō) makes the rebuke more pointed: after everything they have witnessed, faith should be further along. Likely original.

Scholarly Debates

Do the parables conceal or reveal?
Conceal-only view
Matthew’s parallel says the parables are used because the people cannot understand. The function is primarily concealment from the resistant.
Concealment function
Reveal-to-insiders view
The parables reveal to those with open hearts while confirming the hardness of those who have already rejected. The same story functions differently based on the posture of the hearer.
Dual function
"He explained everything to his disciples" (4:34) confirms that the parables are not permanently opaque but require the insider relationship for full understanding. The parables sort hearers rather than simply excluding them.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 4 is the kingdom chapter: three kingdom parables explain the mixed reception of Jesus’s ministry, then the storm-stilling demonstrates the kingdom’s power in a single awe-inducing act.
