Mark 2

Healing and Controversy: Authority Over Sin, Sabbath, and Society

Author

John Mark, son of Mary of Jerusalem (Acts 12:12), cousin of Barnabas (Col 4:10), and close associate of both Paul and Peter. Early and unanimous tradition identifies him as “the interpreter of Peter”: Papias of Hierapolis (c. AD 125, preserved in Eusebius, HE 3.39.15) records that Mark “wrote down accurately, though not in order, as much as he remembered of the things said or done by the Lord” — drawing entirely on Peter’s teaching. Irenaeus (Against Heresies 3.1.1), Clement of Alexandria (Hypotyposeis, in Eusebius HE 6.14.6), and the Anti-Marcionite Prologue all confirm the Petrine connection. Mark himself is mentioned in Acts 12:12, 25; 13:13; 15:37–39; Col 4:10; Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11; and 1 Pet 5:13 (“my son Mark”). He was present at some events he records (the young man fleeing naked, 14:51–52, is widely identified as a Markan self-cameo), and his vividness throughout reflects the eyewitness testimony he received directly from Peter in Rome.

When written

c. AD 55–65, almost certainly in Rome, most likely before the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 — which Mark 13 predicts but nowhere records as fulfilled. Clement of Alexandria states the Gospel was written “while Peter was still preaching publicly in Rome.” If so, the most probable window is the late 50s to mid-60s, during Paul’s Roman imprisonment (Acts 28) and Peter’s residence in the capital. Mark is the earliest of the four Gospels (the Markan Priority hypothesis, held by the majority of NT scholars), and both Matthew and Luke appear to have used it as a primary source. The Gospel’s Latin loanwords (legiōn, denarius, praetorium, centurio), its explanation of Jewish customs for non-Jewish readers (7:3–4), and its Latinisms of idiom all point to a Roman Gentile audience.

What prompted it

To preserve Peter’s eyewitness testimony in permanent written form for a church facing persecution under Nero (AD 64), and to answer the most urgent question that question raised: who is Jesus, and why did he die? Mark’s opening declaration — “The beginning of the good news about Jesus the Messiah, the Son of God” (1:1) — is simultaneously title, thesis, and answer. The Gospel is structured around the progressive revelation of that identity: demons recognise Jesus before disciples do (1:24); Peter confesses “You are the Messiah” at the midpoint (8:29); a Roman centurion declares “Surely this man was the Son of God” at the cross (15:39). The relentless pace — euthys (“immediately”) appears 41 times — reflects both Peter’s preaching style and the urgency of a community for whom the Passion narrative was not ancient history but recent, costly, and still unfolding.

Verses 1–12 — The Paralysed Man Lowered Through the Roof
123456789101112

Greek Word Study

aphiēmi aph-EE-ay-mi — forgive/release/send away: Your sins are forgiven — aphiēntai. The same root used in the Lord’s Prayer and throughout the NT. The paralytic’s physical condition is the visible manifestation of the deeper problem Jesus addresses first. The healing proves the forgiveness is real.

Places

Capernaum — Jesus’s house
Northwest shore of the Sea of Galilee; “his own town” (2:1)
Capernaum is Jesus’s operational base. The crowd that fills the house and spills outside reflects his rapid notoriety across Galilee (1:28). The roof-opening through which the paralytic is lowered would have been a light brushwood-and-plaster construction typical of Galilean domestic architecture.

Context

The healing of the paralytic through the roof is the first direct controversy with the scribes and the first explicit claim of divine authority over sin. Jesus’s question “Which is easier” is a logical trap: the visible miracle proves the invisible pardon. The Son of Man title (v.10) appears for the first time — the Danielic figure who has authority on earth.

Cross-Reference

  • Dan 7:13-14The Son of Man received authority, glory and sovereign power — Jesus’s claim to forgive sins invokes this Danielic title.
  • Isa 43:25I, even I, am he who blots out your transgressions — YHWH’s exclusive claim, which is why the scribes recognize the implication of Jesus’s words.
  • Ps 103:3He forgives all your sins and heals all your diseases — the psalm that links forgiveness and physical restoration, the precise combination Jesus performs.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:4

Marcus: The roof-opening detail is significant historically. Galilean village houses typically had flat roofs of wooden beams, reeds, and packed mud — easily removable for lowering a pallet. This is not a tile roof (as Luke's version has it). Mark's version may reflect the Palestinian setting more accurately. The four friends' action creates a social disturbance that Jesus converts into a theological teaching moment.

2:10

Marcus: "The Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins" — this is the first Son of Man saying in Mark. The title (bar enasha in Aramaic) was an ordinary idiom for "a person" or "I," but it also carried the exalted Danielic resonance of 7:13-14. Marcus argues that Mark's Jesus exploits the ambiguity deliberately: the title asserts both the humanity of the one who forgives and his divine-Danielic authority.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:1-12

Rhoads: The healing of the paralytic introduces a narrative technique that will recur throughout Mark 2-3: the embedded controversy. The crowd waits outside; friends create the opening from above; Jesus addresses the paralytic; the scribes object silently; Jesus reads their thoughts. The story moves through five distinct perspectives and shifts of attention in twelve verses — a narrative density characteristic of Mark's storytelling economy.

2:10-12

Rhoads: "But I want you to know..." — Jesus directly addresses the narrator's audience as much as the scribes. This breaking of the story-world frame (addressing the narrative challenge as though answering objectors outside the scene) is rare in Mark but significant. The healing becomes theatrical proof, performed for the watching crowd and the skeptical scribes simultaneously.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1-7

Chrysostom observes that the four men who lowered the paralytic through the roof display a faith notable for its ingenuity — they did not give up when the crowd blocked the door, but found another way. This, says Aquinas following Ambrose, is the figure of intercessory prayer: the faith of others can carry the helpless to Christ. The scribes' objection "Who can forgive sins but God alone?" is, for Cyril of Alexandria, the most accurate theological statement in the chapter — which is precisely why Jesus accepts the premise and fulfils it.

2:8-12

The question "Which is easier?" is, for Theophylact, a rhetorical trap Jesus sets for the scribes: healing is visible and testable; forgiveness is not. By performing the visible sign, Jesus provides evidence for the invisible authority. Chrysostom notes that the crowd glorified God, not Jesus — and Mark records this without correction, which is itself instructive. The miracle does not compel worship but directs it; the proper response to divine power is theological reorientation, not merely amazement.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:5-7

Calvin: The scribes are technically correct that only God forgives sins. Their error is not in the theology but in the conclusion — they should have asked who this man is, not accused him of blasphemy. Jesus’s response does not deny their theological principle; it confirms it while claiming to embody it.

2:10

The healing is a “perceptible sign” that authenticates the imperceptible grace. God accommodates human weakness by providing visible evidence of invisible realities.

2:10

Verses 13–22 — Levi Called; Eating with Sinners; Fasting Question
13141516171819202122

Greek Word Study

nēsteuō nay-STEW-oh — to fast: Fasting in Second Temple Judaism marked mourning, penitence, and waiting. Jesus’s answer reframes the question: while the bridegroom is present there is nothing to mourn. The disciples’ non-fasting is not irreverence but recognition that the messianic era has arrived.

Places

Capernaum — Jesus’s house
Northwest shore of the Sea of Galilee; “his own town” (2:1)
Capernaum is Jesus’s operational base. The crowd that fills the house and spills outside reflects his rapid notoriety across Galilee (1:28). The roof-opening through which the paralytic is lowered would have been a light brushwood-and-plaster construction typical of Galilean domestic architecture.

Context

Mark 2:13-28 groups three controversy stories that reveal Jesus’s radical claim to re-order the religious life of Israel: eating with sinners (the physician metaphor), non-fasting (the bridegroom/new wine metaphor), and grain-picking on the Sabbath (David precedent + the Son of Man as Sabbath Lord). Each controversy escalates the implicit Christological claim.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Sam 21:1-6David eating the consecrated bread — the precedent Jesus cites for his disciples’ grain-picking. Human need overrides ritual restriction when the anointed king’s need is at stake.
  • Hos 6:6I desire mercy, not sacrifice — the OT principle Jesus embodies. Table fellowship with sinners is mercy over ritual purity.
  • Isa 62:5As a bridegroom rejoices over his bride — the divine-human marriage metaphor Jesus applies to himself. His presence is the wedding, not the time for fasting.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:14

Marcus: The call of Levi at the toll booth is structurally parallel to the fishermen's call (1:16-20): Jesus sees, calls, and the man follows immediately. But the setting is more politically charged. Tax collectors (telōnai) collected indirect taxes at customs posts and were widely despised as economic collaborators with Rome. In rabbinic literature they are regularly grouped with robbers and murderers. The call of Levi across this social-religious barrier signals that the kingdom's boundaries are being redrawn.

2:27-28

Marcus: "The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath" has a partial parallel in the later Mekhilta de-Rabbi Yishmael (on Exod 31:14): "The Sabbath is given to you, not you to the Sabbath." This rabbinic principle, which Jesus' saying anticipates, shows that the statement is not anti-Jewish but represents a valid position within Jewish debate. The radicality is the second clause: "So the Son of Man is Lord of the Sabbath" — a claim with no rabbinic parallel.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:15-22

Rhoads: The meal at Levi's house introduces the "many tax collectors and sinners" as a social type — background characters who function as evidence of Jesus's mission scope. The Pharisees are characterized by their question to the disciples (not directly to Jesus), which places them as observers unable to engage the main character directly. Jesus's responses in 2:17-22 are three self-contained aphorisms rather than a sustained argument — the character communicates through compressed wisdom, not extended discourse.

2:23-28

Rhoads: The grain-field Sabbath controversy is the second of five controversies in Mark 2-3. The narrator characterizes the Pharisees primarily through their questions and accusations, never giving them interiority. The effect is to present them as functionally antagonistic without explaining their motives — a rhetorical choice that shapes the reader's sympathy. Jesus consistently outmaneuvers them through scriptural counter-argument, establishing his rhetorical superiority within their own tradition.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
2:13-17

The calling of Levi at the tax booth is, for Chrysostom, a deliberate provocation: Jesus calls the most socially ostracized professional imaginable, and then reclines at table with his colleagues. Bede notes that the meal at Levi's house is the first extended fellowship scene in Mark — Jesus eating with sinners before any of them have repented, which is itself a parable of grace. The saying "I have not come to call the righteous, but sinners" is, for Augustine, ironic: the self-declared righteous exclude themselves; the declaration of need is already the beginning of coming.

2:18-28

The fasting controversy and the Sabbath dispute are, for Cyril of Alexandria, two faces of one argument: the old forms of religion are vessels, not the wine. The new wine of the kingdom cannot be contained in the wineskins of legal observance designed for the preparatory era. The Sabbath saying "The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath" is, for Chrysostom, not an abolition but a re-grounding: the purpose of rest is human flourishing, not ritual compliance. The Son of Man's lordship over the Sabbath is the authoritative reinterpretation that only the lawgiver can provide.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:14

Levi the tax collector — a collaborator with Roman occupation. His call is the most socially scandalous of the four: he is not merely a fisherman but a traitor to his own people. That Jesus calls him demonstrates the unconditioned nature of grace.

2:27-28

The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath — a principle that has no parallel in rabbinic literature of the period. Then the climax: the Son of Man is Lord of the Sabbath. Jesus does not merely interpret the Sabbath; he claims ownership of it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:21-22

Calvin: The new patch and new wine parables are warnings to the disciples as much as rebukes to the Pharisees. The gospel cannot be domesticated into old religious structures. It requires new forms, new practices, new community. Calvin uses this passage against those who would pour the Reformation’s wine into medieval ecclesiastical wineskins.

2:27

The Sabbath was made for human flourishing. Calvin insists that Jesus is not abolishing the Sabbath but restoring its original purpose — which the Pharisees had obscured with regulatory accumulation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:14

Levi son of Alphaeus — identified in the parallel accounts as Matthew (Matt 9:9). He was a telōnēs (tax collector/toll collector) — a position that required collaboration with Roman authority and was associated with extortion. His call is the most socially transgressive in Mark.

2:26

In the days of Abiathar the high priest — a textual problem: 1 Sam 21 says Ahimelech, Abiathar’s father, was the priest. Various solutions have been proposed: Abiathar was present but not officiating; the title refers to the Abiathar era rather than the moment; or it is a genuine error in the tradition.

Ancient Sources

Tosefta Shabbat
Rabbinic text stating that the Sabbath was given to Israel and not Israel to the Sabbath — a principle similar to Jesus’s v.27 statement.
Jesus’s Sabbath teaching draws on existing rabbinic logic but radicalizes it by adding the Son of Man claim.
Josephus, Antiquities 18.17
Josephus confirms that tax collectors were widely despised in first-century Jewish society as collaborators and extortionists.
Confirms the social scandal of Jesus’s association with tax collectors in Mark 2:15-16.

Reception History

Augustine, Sermon 98
Augustine used the physician metaphor (2:17) as the defining statement of Jesus’s mission: he came for the sick, not the well. The church is not a society of the perfect but a hospital for sinners.
Dietrich Bonhoeffer, Discipleship
Bonhoeffer used the calling of Levi (2:14) to argue that discipleship is not the result of extended deliberation but immediate obedience to a direct call. The decision is made before it is reasoned.

Greek-Rooted Reading

Greek-Rooted Reading

2:5 Aphiēntai — present passive: your sins are being forgiven, right now, by divine action. Not a promise but a pronouncement.
2:10 Huios tou anthrōpou — Son of Man. Jesus’s preferred self-designation, appearing 14 times in Mark. Simultaneously humble (mortal) and exalted (Danielic).
2:19 Nymphios (bridegroom) — Jesus applies the divine marriage metaphor (Isa 62:5, Hos 2:19) to himself. His presence is the wedding feast.

Textual Notes

2:1Returning to Capernaum — "at home"
Some MSS read eis oikon (into a house); others read en oikō (at home/in the house). NA28 prefers en oikō.
The reading affects whether Jesus is in someone's house or his own established residence; both fit the Capernaum-base theme.
2:16Pharisees addressing the disciples
NA28/UBS5 "the scribes of the Pharisees" (hoi grammateis tōn Pharisaiōn); variant: "the scribes and the Pharisees."
"Scribes of the Pharisees" is the more specific reading and likely original; the simpler "scribes and Pharisees" is a harmonisation.

Scholarly Debates

Did Jesus abolish the Sabbath?
Abolition view
Some Reformation and modern interpreters read 2:27-28 as annulling the Sabbath command entirely for Christians.
Abolition
Transformation view
Jesus restores the Sabbath’s original purpose rather than abolishing it. The Son of Man as Sabbath Lord re-establishes the Sabbath under new covenant terms.
Transformation
"The Sabbath was made for man" implies purpose, not abolition. Jesus critiques Pharisaic over-regulation, not the creational principle of Sabbath rest (Gen 2:2-3).
