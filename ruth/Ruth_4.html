Ruth 4

The Gate; Redemption; Obed; The Line to David

Hebrew History Context Cross-Ref Places People Timeline
Author

Unknown. Jewish tradition (Talmud, Bava Batra 14b) attributes the book to Samuel, though the text itself is anonymous. The author writes with intimate knowledge of Bethlehem and Moabite custom, and with a literary artistry that makes Ruth one of the most polished narratives in the OT. The genealogical close (4:18–22) pointing to David suggests a post-Davidic setting for the final form.

When written

Uncertain. The narrative is set in the period of the judges (1:1), and the archaic legal customs (the sandal ceremony, 4:7) suggest the author was writing at some distance from the events. Most scholars date the composition between c.1000–800 BC, with some placing it in the post-exilic period. The book's positive portrayal of a Moabite woman has been read as a response to the strict exclusivism of Ezra-Nehemiah, though this is disputed.

What prompted it

Ruth preserves the ancestry of David — and ultimately of the Messiah — through a Moabite woman whose loyalty to her mother-in-law becomes the vehicle of covenant faithfulness. The book explores how ḥesed (steadfast covenant love) operates through ordinary human faithfulness — gleaning, redemption, marriage — and culminates in the line that will produce Israel's greatest king. It implicitly challenges ethnic exclusivism: the grandmother of David was a Moabite convert whose loyalty exceeded that of many Israelites.

Verses 1–22 — The Gate; Redemption; Obed; The Line to David
1Meanwhile Boaz went up to the town gate and sat down there just as the guardian-redeemer he had mentioned came along. Boaz said, "Come over here, my friend, and sit down." So he went over and sat down. 2Boaz took ten of the elders of the town and said, "Sit here," and they did so. 3Then he said to the guardian-redeemer, "Naomi, who has come back from Moab, is selling the piece of land that belonged to our relative Elimelek. 4I thought I should bring the matter to your attention and suggest that you buy it in the presence of these seated here and in the presence of the elders of my people. If you will redeem it, do so. But if you will not, tell me, so I know. For no one has the right to do it except you, and I am next in line." "I will redeem it," the man said. 5Then Boaz said, "On the day you buy the land from Naomi, you also acquire Ruth the Moabite, the dead man's widow, in order to maintain the name of the dead with his property." 6At this, the guardian-redeemer said, "Then I cannot redeem it because I might endanger my own estate. You redeem it yourself. I cannot do it." 7(Now in earlier times in Israel, for the redemption and transfer of property to become final, one party took off his sandal and gave it to the other. This was the method of legalizing transactions in Israel.) 8So the guardian-redeemer said to Boaz, "Buy it yourself." And he removed his sandal. 9Then Boaz announced to the elders and all the people, "Today you are witnesses that I have bought from Naomi all the property of Elimelek, Kilion and Mahlon. 10I have also acquired Ruth the Moabite, Mahlon's widow, as my wife, in order to maintain the name of the dead with his property, so that his name will not disappear from among his family or from the town records. Today you are my witnesses!" 11Then the elders and all the people at the gate said, "We are witnesses. May the LORD make the woman who is coming into your home like Rachel and Leah, who together built up the house of Israel. May you have standing in Ephrathah and be famous in Bethlehem. 12Through the offspring the LORD gives you by this young woman, may your family be like that of Perez, whom Tamar bore to Judah." 13So Boaz took Ruth and she became his wife. When he made love to her, the LORD enabled her to conceive, and she gave birth to a son. 14The women said to Naomi: "Praise be to the LORD, who this day has not left you without a guardian-redeemer. May he become famous throughout Israel! 15He will renew your life and sustain you in your old age. For your daughter-in-law, who loves you and who is better to you than seven sons, has given him birth." 16Then Naomi took the child, laid him on her lap and cared for him. 17The women living there said, "Naomi has a son!" And they named him Obed. He is the father of Jesse, the father of David. 18This is the genealogy of Perez: Perez the father of Hezron, 19Hezron the father of Ram, Ram the father of Amminadab, 20Amminadab the father of Nahshon, Nahshon the father of Salmon, 21Salmon the father of Boaz, Boaz the father of Obed, 22Obed the father of Jesse, and Jesse the father of David.

Hebrew Word Study

גֹּאֵלgōʾēlkinsman-redeemer

The legal climax of the book. The anonymous nearer redeemer (Boaz calls him pĕlōnî ʾalmōnî — "so-and-so," a deliberate anonymity that contrasts with Boaz's named, faithful action) declines because the Ruth obligation would "endanger his own estate." Self-interest disqualifies him; Boaz's readiness qualifies him. The gōʾēl who will not redeem remains nameless; the one who does is in the genealogy of the Messiah.

עֵדʿēdwitness

V.9–10: Boaz twice calls the elders "my witnesses" (ʿēdîm). The legal transaction requires public testimony. The whole community participates in the redemption — it is not a private deal but a public covenant act, ratified before the gate of the city (the ancient courtroom) and blessed by the whole people.

Theological Context

Ruth 4 is the legal resolution — and then the theological explosion. The gate scene (vv.1–12) moves with precision through ancient property and marriage law. Then in three rapid verses (vv.13–17) the narrative accelerates: Boaz takes Ruth, the LORD enables conception (the only direct divine action in the book), a son is born, and the women name him. Naomi who came back empty now holds a child on her lap. And then the genealogy (vv.18–22) reveals what the child means: Obed is the grandfather of David. The entire book — the famine, the deaths, the loyalty, the gleaning, the threshing floor, the gate — was the backstory of Israel's greatest king and, by extension, of the Messiah.

Cross-References

Gen 38

Tamar mentioned in v.12. Like Ruth, Tamar secured the continuation of the covenant line through unconventional means when the male actors failed to fulfil their obligations. Both women appear in Matthew's genealogy.

Matt 1:5

Ruth in the Messiah's genealogy — Salmon the father of Boaz by Rahab, Boaz the father of Obed by Ruth. The Moabite gleaner becomes an ancestor of Jesus.

Ps 127:3–5

Sons as a heritage from the LORD — the son given to Naomi reverses every loss she enumerated in 1:20–21.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1–4

Boaz's strategy at the gate is legally precise and personally generous. He allows the nearer redeemer first right of refusal — he does not manipulate the outcome. But he presents the full obligation: land plus Ruth. The nearer redeemer's refusal is not villainous; it is legally and economically rational. His problem is that he thinks only about his own estate. Boaz thinks about the dead and about Ruth.

4:10

I have acquired Ruth the Moabite as my wife, in order to maintain the name of the dead with his property. Boaz states his purpose publicly and precisely. He is not primarily acquiring a wife; he is perpetuating a covenant name. The personal and the legal are inseparable. This is ḥesed — loyalty to the living and the dead simultaneously.

4:13

The LORD enabled her to conceive. The only direct divine action in the book, and it is understated to the point of brevity. God has been present throughout as the one behind the "coincidences" — but here the narrator names him directly. The child is God's gift.

4:15

Better to you than seven sons. The highest possible tribute in a culture where sons were the ultimate provision. Ruth the Moabite, who had no legal claim on Naomi and no obligation to stay, has become more valuable than the ideal family. Ḥesed has exceeded every natural and legal category.

4:17–22

The genealogy is the book's detonator. Everything — the famine, the deaths, the Moabite widow's loyalty, the gleaning, the midnight proposal — was the hidden mechanism producing the line that would produce David, and through David, the Messiah. Ruth 1:1 began "in the days when the judges ruled." Ruth 4:22 ends with "Jesse the father of David." The book spans the darkest era to the brightest name. The LORD who seemed absent was the author of every chapter.

Ancient Sources & Scholarship

The sandal ceremony

V.7–8: the sandal transfer as property conveyance. Comparable customs appear in ancient Near Eastern law codes and Deuteronomy 25:9 (where the widow removes the sandal of the brother-in-law who refuses levirate duty). The author notes this is an archaic custom, suggesting he is writing at some distance from the events.

The archaic legal note is a dating clue.

The town gate

V.1: the gate was the ancient city's judicial and commercial centre — where elders sat, legal matters were resolved, and commercial transactions witnessed. Boaz's legal proceedings follow standard ancient procedure.

The gate as courthouse is archaeologically attested throughout the ancient Near East.

Reception History

The genealogy and Matthew 1

Matthew opens his gospel with the genealogy of Jesus, explicitly including Ruth (1:5). The Moabite widow of Bethlehem is named in the first paragraph of the NT. Her ḥesed was not merely an episode in Israelite history — it was part of the mechanism of the Incarnation.

Calvin on Ruth 4

The book as a demonstration that "God governs all things by his secret providence." Every apparent accident — the famine, the Moabite marriages, the deaths, the harvest timing — is reread at the end as the purposeful work of God toward the Davidic line.

Theological Themes

Ruth 4 completes every thread. The legal redemption is achieved; the anonymous redeemer's self-interest is exposed; Boaz's ḥesed is vindicated; Naomi's emptiness is reversed; and the genealogy reveals that this harvest-season story of loyalty was woven into the fabric of redemptive history. The Moabite widow's ḥesed in chapter 1 was the hidden beginning of the line that would produce David — and the Messiah.
