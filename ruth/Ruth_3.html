Ruth 3

Naomi's Plan; Ruth's Proposal; Boaz's Oath

Hebrew History Context Cross-Ref Places People Timeline
Author

Unknown. Jewish tradition (Talmud, Bava Batra 14b) attributes the book to Samuel, though the text itself is anonymous. The author writes with intimate knowledge of Bethlehem and Moabite custom, and with a literary artistry that makes Ruth one of the most polished narratives in the OT. The genealogical close (4:18–22) pointing to David suggests a post-Davidic setting for the final form.

When written

Uncertain. The narrative is set in the period of the judges (1:1), and the archaic legal customs (the sandal ceremony, 4:7) suggest the author was writing at some distance from the events. Most scholars date the composition between c.1000–800 BC, with some placing it in the post-exilic period. The book's positive portrayal of a Moabite woman has been read as a response to the strict exclusivism of Ezra-Nehemiah, though this is disputed.

What prompted it

Ruth preserves the ancestry of David — and ultimately of the Messiah — through a Moabite woman whose loyalty to her mother-in-law becomes the vehicle of covenant faithfulness. The book explores how ḥesed (steadfast covenant love) operates through ordinary human faithfulness — gleaning, redemption, marriage — and culminates in the line that will produce Israel's greatest king. It implicitly challenges ethnic exclusivism: the grandmother of David was a Moabite convert whose loyalty exceeded that of many Israelites.

Verses 1–18 — Naomi's Plan; Ruth's Proposal; Boaz's Oath

Then go down to the threshing floor, but don't let him know you are there until he has finished eating and drinking. 4When he lies down, note the place where he is lying. Then go and uncover his feet and lie down. He will tell you what to do." 5"I will do whatever you say," Ruth answered. 6So she went down to the threshing floor and did everything her mother-in-law told her to do. 7When Boaz had finished eating and drinking and was in good spirits, he went to lie down at the far end of the grain pile. Ruth approached quietly, uncovered his feet and lay down. 8In the middle of the night something startled the man; he turned—and there was a woman lying at his feet! 9"Who are you?" he asked. "I am your servant Ruth," she said. "Spread the corner of your garment over me, since you are a guardian-redeemer of our family." 10"The LORD bless you, my daughter," he replied. "This kindness is greater than that which you showed earlier: You have not run after the younger men, whether rich or poor. 11And now, my daughter, don't be afraid. I will do for you all you ask. All the people of my town know that you are a woman of noble character. 12Although it is true that I am a guardian-redeemer of our family, there is another who is more closely related than I. 13Stay here for the night, and in the morning if he wants to do his duty as your guardian-redeemer, good; let him redeem you. But if he is not willing, as surely as the LORD lives, I will do it. Lie down until morning." 14So she lay at his feet until morning, but got up before anyone could be recognized; and he said, "No one must know that a woman came to the threshing floor." 15He also said, "Bring me the shawl you are wearing and hold it out." When she did so, he poured into it six measures of barley and placed it on her. Then he went back to town. 16When Ruth came to her mother-in-law, Naomi asked, "How did it go, my daughter?" Then she told her everything Boaz had done for her 17and added, "He gave me these six measures of barley, saying, 'Don't go back to your mother-in-law empty-handed.'" 18Then Naomi said, "Wait, my daughter, until you find out what happens. For the man will not rest until the matter is settled today."

Hebrew Word Study

כָּנָףkānāpwing / corner of garment

V.9: Ruth asks Boaz to spread his kānāp over her. The same word Boaz used in 2:12 when praying for God's kānāp to cover her. A marriage proposal in ancient Near Eastern idiom — Ezekiel 16:8 uses the identical gesture for God's covenant-marriage to Israel. Ruth is asking Boaz to be the human embodiment of the divine protection he himself invoked.

אֵשֶׁת חַיִלʾēšet ḥayilwoman of noble character/excellence

V.11: the same phrase as Proverbs 31:10 — the most honoured title a woman could receive in ancient Israel. Boaz says all the town knows Ruth is an ʾēšet ḥayil. Her excellence is not beauty but ḥesed; not status but faithfulness.

Theological Context

Ruth 3 is the most narratively delicate chapter in the book. Naomi's plan requires Ruth to make a bold, boundary-crossing approach to Boaz on the threshing floor at night — a setting with obvious potential for misinterpretation. The chapter's genius is that nothing improper occurs. Ruth's proposal is both legally precise (invoking the gōʾēl obligation) and personally courageous (she is offering herself to an older man of standing when she could have sought younger men). Boaz's response is equally precise: he accepts, praises her ḥesed, acknowledges the legal complication, and resolves it with a vow. The six measures of barley are the earnest — the pledge that he will complete the transaction.

Cross-References

Ezek 16:8

God spreads his kānāp over Israel in covenant marriage — the same gesture Ruth requests of Boaz. The marriage proposal is simultaneously a covenant-making act.

Prov 31:10

ʾēšet ḥayil — "who can find a woman of noble character?" Boaz applies the Proverbs 31 title to Ruth. The book of Ruth and Proverbs 31 are often read together in Jewish tradition as portraits of the same virtue.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:9

Spread the corner of your garment over me — kānāp. Ruth's request is legally, sexually, and theologically loaded. Legally: she is invoking the levirate/gōʾēl obligation. Personally: she is proposing marriage. Theologically: she is asking Boaz to be the human embodiment of the divine protection he prayed for in 2:12. The prayer becomes a proposal; the theology becomes biography.

3:10

This kindness is greater than that which you showed earlier. Boaz recognises Ruth's ḥesed in choosing him rather than a younger man. She has not sought personal advantage; she has sought to perpetuate the family line of Elimelek. Her proposal is an act of covenantal faithfulness to the dead, not just personal desire.

3:11

ʾēšet ḥayil — woman of noble character. All the people of my town know it. Ruth's reputation has preceded any legal outcome. She is already known in Bethlehem not as "the Moabite" but as a woman of excellence. Ḥesed has rewritten her social identity.

3:17

Don't go back to your mother-in-law empty-handed. Boaz's exact words echo Naomi's complaint in 1:21: "I went away full but the LORD has brought me back empty." The reversal of Naomi's emptiness is now underway — and the agent is the same man who will complete the legal redemption in ch.4.

3:18

The man will not rest until the matter is settled today. Naomi's confidence in Boaz is the chapter's final word. Her theology has completed its journey from lament (1:20–21) to trust. She who accused God of afflicting her now rests in the faithfulness of God's human agent.

Ancient Sources & Scholarship

Threshing floor customs

Threshing floors were communal spaces at the edge of towns, used for grain processing after harvest. Men sometimes slept there during harvest to guard the grain. The setting is public enough to be non-scandalous; private enough for a confidential proposal.

The threshing floor setting is legally and narratively precise.

Garment-spreading as proposal

Ezekiel 16:8 uses the kānāp-spreading gesture for God's covenant marriage to Israel. Ancient Near Eastern marriage customs sometimes included the groom spreading his cloak over the bride. Ruth's request is a recognised legal-social act, not merely romantic.

The gesture has clear legal precedent in the ancient world.

Reception History

Jewish reading of Ruth 3

The rabbis consistently read the chapter as an example of ṣniʿut (modesty): Ruth acted boldly but blamelessly. Boaz's praise of her (v.11) is taken as the narrative's verdict: her character was beyond question.

ʾĒšet ḥayil in liturgy

In many Jewish homes Proverbs 31:10–31 is sung to the wife on Friday night (Shabbat). Ruth is the living embodiment of that poem — and Boaz's application of the title to her in 3:11 is its first and most moving occurrence.

Theological Themes

ḤesedThe KānāpʾĒšet ḤayilLegal RedemptionCouragePrayer Becoming Action

Ruth 3 is the chapter where prayer becomes proposal. Boaz's prayer for God's kānāp to cover Ruth becomes Ruth's request for Boaz's kānāp to cover her. The divine protection is mediated through human ḥesed. Ruth's courage — choosing covenant faithfulness over personal advantage — earns her the highest title in ancient Israel: ʾēšet ḥayil.
