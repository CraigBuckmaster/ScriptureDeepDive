Ruth 1

Naomi's Loss; Ruth's Loyalty; Return to Bethlehem

Hebrew History Context Cross-Ref Places People Timeline
Author

Unknown. Jewish tradition (Talmud, Bava Batra 14b) attributes the book to Samuel, though the text itself is anonymous. The author writes with intimate knowledge of Bethlehem and Moabite custom, and with a literary artistry that makes Ruth one of the most polished narratives in the OT. The genealogical close (4:18–22) pointing to David suggests a post-Davidic setting for the final form.

When written

Uncertain. The narrative is set in the period of the judges (1:1), and the archaic legal customs (the sandal ceremony, 4:7) suggest the author was writing at some distance from the events. Most scholars date the composition between c.1000–800 BC, with some placing it in the post-exilic period. The book's positive portrayal of a Moabite woman has been read as a response to the strict exclusivism of Ezra-Nehemiah, though this is disputed.

What prompted it

Ruth preserves the ancestry of David — and ultimately of the Messiah — through a Moabite woman whose loyalty to her mother-in-law becomes the vehicle of covenant faithfulness. The book explores how ḥesed (steadfast covenant love) operates through ordinary human faithfulness — gleaning, redemption, marriage — and culminates in the line that will produce Israel's greatest king. It implicitly challenges ethnic exclusivism: the grandmother of David was a Moabite convert whose loyalty exceeded that of many Israelites.

Verses 1–22 — Naomi's Loss; Ruth's Loyalty; Return to Bethlehem
1In the days when the judges ruled, there was a famine in the land. So a man from Bethlehem in Judah, together with his wife and two sons, went to live for a while in the country of Moab. 2The man's name was Elimelek, his wife's name was Naomi, and the names of his two sons were Mahlon and Kilion. They were Ephrathites from Bethlehem, Judah. And they went to Moab and lived there. 3Now Elimelek, Naomi's husband, died, and she was left with her two sons. 4They married Moabite women, one named Orpah and the other Ruth. After they had lived there about ten years, 5both Mahlon and Kilion also died, and Naomi was left without her two sons and her husband. 6When Naomi heard in Moab that the LORD had come to the aid of his people by providing food for them, she and her daughters-in-law prepared to return home from there. 7With her two daughters-in-law she left the place where she had been living and set out on the road that would take them back to the land of Judah. 8Then Naomi said to her two daughters-in-law, "Go back, each of you, to your mother's home. May the LORD show you kindness, as you have shown kindness to your dead husbands and to me. 9May the LORD grant that each of you will find rest in the home of another husband." Then she kissed them goodbye and they wept aloud 10and said to her, "We will go back with you to your people." 11But Naomi said, "Return home, my daughters. Why would you come with me? Am I going to have any more sons, who could become your husbands? 12Return home, my daughters; I am too old to have another husband. Even if I thought there was still hope for me—even if I had a husband tonight and then gave birth to sons— 13would you wait until they grew up? Would you remain unmarried for them? No, my daughters. It is more bitter for me than for you, because the LORD's hand has turned against me!" 14At this they wept aloud again. Then Orpah kissed her mother-in-law goodbye, but Ruth clung to her. 15"Look," said Naomi, "your sister-in-law is going back to her people and her gods. Go back with her." 16But Ruth replied, "Don't urge me to leave you or to turn back from you. Where you go I will go, and where you stay I will stay. Your people will be my people and your God will be my God. 17Where you die I will die, and there I will be buried. May the LORD deal with me, be it ever so severely, if even death separates you and me." 18When Naomi realized that Ruth was determined to go with her, she stopped urging her. 19So the two women went on until they came to Bethlehem. When they arrived in Bethlehem, the whole town was stirred because of them, and the women exclaimed, "Can this be Naomi?" 20"Don't call me Naomi," she told them. "Call me Mara, because the Almighty has made my life very bitter. 21I went away full, but the LORD has brought me back empty. Why call me Naomi? The LORD has afflicted me; the Almighty has brought misfortune upon me." 22So Naomi returned from Moab accompanied by Ruth the Moabite, her daughter-in-law, arriving in Bethlehem as the barley harvest was beginning.

Hebrew Word Study

חֶסֶדḥesedsteadfast love / loyal kindness

The governing word of the entire book. Naomi prays that the LORD will show ḥesed to her daughters-in-law (v.8) — the same ḥesed she has received from them. Ḥesed is covenantal loyalty that goes beyond what is legally required: it is the overflow of committed relationship. Ruth's loyalty to Naomi in the verses that follow is itself a living embodiment of ḥesed.

מָרָהMārahbitter

V.20: Naomi renames herself Mārah — bitter — the same word used in Exodus 15:23 for the bitter waters at Marah. She is naming her experience with the vocabulary of wilderness suffering. But the narrative has already introduced the element that will transform bitterness: Ruth is with her, and barley harvest is beginning.

שַׁדַּיŠaddaythe Almighty

Used twice in vv.20–21 — the ancient patriarchal name for God, associated with powerful divine action. Naomi's lament uses God's most sovereign name to express the totality of her loss. She believes God is responsible for her affliction — which is also faith, however anguished: she has not abandoned God, only argued with him.

Theological Context

Ruth 1 is a study in triple bereavement and its aftermath. Naomi loses husband and both sons in Moab — foreign soil, far from the covenant community. Her theology of the moment is honest and raw: "the LORD's hand has turned against me." The chapter's dramatic reversal begins not with divine intervention but with a human decision: Ruth's clinging. The great declaration of vv.16–17 is not a confession of faith in the abstract — it is a concrete act of loyalty to a destitute mother-in-law with nothing to offer. Ruth's God-choice and people-choice are inseparable.

Cross-References

Exod 15:23

Marah — the bitter waters. Naomi's self-naming draws on the Exodus vocabulary of wilderness suffering. As Marah was transformed, so will Naomi's bitterness be.

Matt 1:5

Ruth appears in the genealogy of Jesus — the Moabite daughter-in-law becomes an ancestor of the Messiah. Her loyalty in ch.1 is the foundation of everything that follows.

Deut 23:3

Moabites excluded from the assembly of Israel to the tenth generation. Ruth's inclusion despite this prohibition is a deliberate counter-narrative: ḥesed transcends ethnic law.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

In the days when the judges ruled. The book opens by locating itself in Israel's darkest period — repeated apostasy, violence, moral confusion. Yet it tells a story of ordinary people acting with extraordinary faithfulness. The contrast is the point: ḥesed is most visible against the backdrop of an age without it.

1:16–17

Ruth's declaration is one of the most beautiful speeches in Scripture. Its structure is three parallel couplets, each intensifying the commitment: go/stay, people/God, die/buried. The final clause — "may the LORD deal with me severely if even death separates us" — is a self-imprecatory oath, the strongest form of promise available. She is not expressing sentiment; she is binding herself legally and theologically to Naomi.

1:20–21

Naomi's theology of suffering is honest rather than pious. She names God as the agent of her affliction — not Satan, not fate, not bad luck, but the LORD and Shaddai. This is not blasphemy; it is the language of lament psalms. She is in dialogue with God even while accusing him. The narrative will vindicate both her faith and her complaint.

1:22

The chapter ends with two details that bracket the entire story: Ruth the Moabite (her ethnic identity, which makes her loyalty all the more remarkable) and the barley harvest (the physical setting that will enable the provision of ch.2). The hand of God is already moving in the seasonal rhythms of creation.

1:8–9

Naomi's double prayer for ḥesed is the book's thesis statement compressed into a petition: "May the LORD show you kindness, as you have shown kindness." Human ḥesed and divine ḥesed mirror each other. Ruth will embody the answer to this prayer — not by returning to Moab but by staying with Naomi.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

Key People

NaomiBethlehemite widow

Her name means "pleasant"; she renames herself "bitter." The narrative will restore her original name through Ruth's faithfulness.

RuthMoabite daughter-in-law

Her loyalty is the engine of the entire book. She chooses Naomi's God and people before she has any reason to expect reward.

OrpahMoabite daughter-in-law

She weeps and kisses Naomi goodbye — not villainous, simply reasonable. She makes the sensible choice. Ruth makes the extraordinary one.

ElimelekNaomi's husband, now dead

His departure from Bethlehem during famine frames the question of the book: what does faithfulness to covenant look like under pressure?

Ancient Sources & Scholarship

Levirate Custom

The background to Naomi's speech about sons (vv.11–13) is the levirate obligation — a brother-in-law marrying a widow to raise up children in the dead man's name (Deut 25:5–10). Naomi correctly notes she has no more sons to provide.

The legal context gives weight to Naomi's despair and sets up the Boaz plot.

Moab

Located east of the Dead Sea, on the plateau above the Arnon Gorge. Moabites were ancient enemies of Israel, descended from Lot (Gen 19:37). The Mosaic law excluded them from the assembly (Deut 23:3). Ruth's inclusion is a deliberate narrative challenge to ethnic exclusivism.

The geography and ethnic tension are essential to the story's theological argument.

Reception History

Jewish liturgical use

Ruth is read during the Feast of Weeks (Shavuot) — the harvest festival — connecting the barley harvest of the story to the giving of Torah at Sinai. Ruth's conversion to Israel's God parallels Israel's covenant at Sinai.

Augustine on Ruth

Ruth as a figure of the Gentile church grafted into Israel's covenant. Her declaration — "your people shall be my people and your God my God" — became a touchstone for understanding Gentile inclusion.

Literary Structure

Expositionvv.1–5

Famine, migration, deaths — triple loss

Road scenevv.6–18

Naomi urges return; Orpah leaves; Ruth clings

Arrivalvv.19–22

Town stirred; Naomi's lament; harvest beginning

Ruth 1 is structured around three departures: Elimelek's (death), Orpah's (return to Moab), and the departure that doesn't happen — Ruth's staying. The chapter ends exactly where the redemption story needs to begin: Bethlehem, harvest season.

Theological Themes

ḤesedLoyaltySuffering and FaithGentile InclusionProvidenceConversion

Ruth 1 establishes the book's central virtue: ḥesed — loyal love that exceeds obligation. Ruth embodies it toward Naomi before God has given her any reason to expect reward. The bitterness of Naomi's lament is honest theology, not apostasy; and the harvest beginning in the final verse signals that God has not finished.
