Ruth 2

Ruth Gleans; Boaz Notices; The Gōʾēl Revealed

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline
Author

Unknown. Jewish tradition (Talmud, Bava Batra 14b) attributes the book to Samuel, though the text itself is anonymous.

When written

Uncertain. Set in the period of the judges (1:1). Most scholars date the composition between c.1000–800 BC.

What prompted it

Ruth preserves the ancestry of David through a Moabite woman whose loyalty becomes the vehicle of covenant faithfulness, exploring how ḥesed operates through ordinary human acts and culminating in the Davidic line.

Verses 1–7 — Boaz Introduced; Ruth Gleans; The Overseer's Report
1Now Naomi had a relative on her husband's side, a man of standing from the clan of Elimelek, whose name was Boaz.2And Ruth the Moabite said to Naomi, "Let me go to the fields and pick up the leftover grain behind anyone in whose eyes I find favor." Naomi said to her, "Go ahead, my daughter."3So she went out, entered a field and began to glean behind the harvesters. As it turned out, she was working in a field belonging to Boaz, who was from the clan of Elimelek.4Just then Boaz arrived from Bethlehem and greeted the harvesters, "The LORD be with you!" "The LORD bless you!" they answered.5Boaz asked the overseer of his harvesters, "Who does that young woman belong to?"6The overseer replied, "She is the Moabite who came back from Moab with Naomi.7She said, 'Please let me glean and gather among the sheaves behind the harvesters.' She came into the field and has worked steadily from morning till now, except for a short rest in the shelter."

Hebrew Word Study

גִּבּוֹר חַיִל gibbôr ḥayil — man of standing / valour: V.1: the title given to Boaz. The same construction (ḥayil) will be applied to Ruth in 3:11 as ʾēšet ḥayil — woman of noble character. The book pairs the two characters using the same vocabulary of excellence, signalling their fitness for each other before they have met.

מִקְרֶה miqreh — chance / as it happened: V.3: "as it turned out" — literally "her chance chanced upon." The Hebrew deliberately uses a coincidence idiom to describe what the narrative frames as divine providence. The author is signalling to the reader: this is not accident.

Places & Geography

Bethlehem's fields
Terraced fields south of the town on the slopes toward the Shephelah
The barley harvest (April–May) followed by wheat harvest (May–June) gave Ruth a two-month window of gleaning opportunity.
Gleaning rights
Legal provision under Lev 19:9–10 and Deut 24:19–21
Landowners were required to leave field edges and fallen grain for the poor and the foreigner. This was a legal right, not charity — dignified work with guaranteed access.

Context

The opening of ch.2 works on two levels. On the surface: a Moabite widow exercises her legal gleaning rights in the fields outside Bethlehem. Beneath the surface: the gōʾēl (kinsman-redeemer) has just been named in v.1, and Ruth has "chanced" into his field. The narrative's use of coincidence language highlights that the coincidence is providential.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1

A man of standing from the clan of Elimelek. Boaz is introduced before Ruth meets him — the reader knows what Ruth does not yet know. The gōʾēl is in place; the story just needs to bring them together.

2:3

As it turned out. The Hebrew miqreh (chance) is the narrator's wink: Ruth chose a field at random and chose Boaz's. The God who is never named in Ruth's speech is orchestrating every encounter.

Verses 8–17 — Boaz's Generosity: Protection, Meal, and an Ephah
8So Boaz said to Ruth, "My daughter, listen to me. Don't go and glean in another field and don't go away from here. Stay here with the women who work for me.9Watch the field where the men are harvesting, and follow along after the women. I have told the men not to lay a hand on you. And whenever you are thirsty, go and get a drink from the water jars the men have filled."10At this, she bowed down with her face to the ground. She asked him, "Why have I found such favor in your eyes that you notice me—a foreigner?"11Boaz replied, "I've been told all about what you have done for your mother-in-law since the death of your husband—how you left your father and mother and your homeland and came to a people you did not know before.12May the LORD repay you for what you have done. May you be richly rewarded by the LORD, the God of Israel, under whose wings you have come to take refuge."13"May I continue to find favor in your eyes, my lord," she said. "You have put me at ease by speaking kindly to your servant—though I do not have the standing of one of your servants."14At mealtime Boaz said to her, "Come over here. Have some bread and dip it in the wine vinegar." When she sat down with the harvesters, he offered her some roasted grain. She ate all she wanted and had some left over.15As she got up to glean, Boaz gave orders to his men: "Let her gather among the sheaves and don't reprimand her.16Even pull out some stalks from the bundles for her and leave them for her to pick up, and don't rebuke her."17So Ruth gleaned in the field until evening. Then she threshed the barley she had gathered, and it amounted to about an ephah.

Hebrew Word Study

כְּנָפֶיךָ kĕnāpêkā — your wings / under whose wings: V.12: Boaz prays Ruth will be rewarded under the LORD's kānāp — wings. In ch.3, Ruth uses this same word asking Boaz to spread his kānāp over her (3:9), a marriage proposal. Her request literalises his prayer: she is asking him to be the human agent of the divine protection he invoked here.

חֵן ḥēn — favour / grace: V.10: Why have I found ḥēn in your eyes? Undeserved favour. Boaz's answer reframes it: her ḥesed to Naomi is the reason. Human ḥēn responds to human ḥesed, and both mirror the divine pattern.

Context

Boaz's behaviour in vv.8–16 consistently exceeds the legal minimum. The law required leaving field edges; Boaz permits gleaning among the sheaves and instructs his men to pull out extra stalks. The law required access to water; Boaz provides it proactively. The meal inclusion (v.14) is entirely above and beyond — Boaz serves Ruth himself and she eats until satisfied with food left over. He is practising ḥesed before anyone has asked him to.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 12:1You left your father and mother and your homeland (v.11). Boaz interprets Ruth's action through the lens of Abraham's call — she has done what Abraham did: left everything for the God of Israel.
  • Ps 91:4Under his wings — the kānāp refuge image of the Psalms now applied to Ruth's covenantal move. In ch.3 Ruth will ask Boaz to spread his kānāp over her, literalising this prayer as a proposal.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:11–12

Boaz recognises Ruth's act as Abrahamic in scope. She left homeland, family, and security for the God of Israel. His prayer frames her ḥesed toward Naomi as the basis for divine ḥesed toward her.

2:14

The meal scene is more than hospitality. Boaz serves Ruth himself, gives her roasted grain (associated with priestly meals), and she eats until satisfied with some left over. Early Christian commentators noted the Eucharistic overtones.

2:16

Pull out some stalks from the bundles for her. Boaz secretly instructs his men to make her gleaning easier without her knowing. This is generosity that does not seek credit — ḥesed practiced in secret.

Verses 18–23 — Return to Naomi: The Gōʾēl Revealed
18She carried it back to town, and her mother-in-law saw how much she had gathered. Ruth also brought out and gave her what she had left over after she had eaten enough.19Her mother-in-law asked her, "Where did you glean today? Where did you work? Blessed be the man who took notice of you!" Then Ruth told her mother-in-law about the one at whose place she had been working. "The name of the man I worked with today is Boaz," she said.20"The LORD bless him!" Naomi said to her daughter-in-law. "He has not stopped showing his kindness to the living and the dead." She added, "That man is our close relative; he is one of our guardian-redeemers."21Then Ruth the Moabite said, "He even said to me, 'Stay with my workers until they finish harvesting all my grain.'"22Naomi said to Ruth her daughter-in-law, "It will be good for you, my daughter, to go with the women who work for him, because in someone else's field you might be harmed."23So Ruth stayed close to the women of Boaz to glean until the barley and wheat harvests were finished. And she lived with her mother-in-law.

Hebrew Word Study

גֹּאֵל gōʾēl — kinsman-redeemer: V.20: the pivotal legal term. A gōʾēl was a near kinsman with the legal right and obligation to redeem property, redeem persons from slavery, and marry a brother's widow. Naomi's recognition of Boaz as gōʾēl transforms what looked like a lucky day of gleaning into the beginning of a legal redemption plot. This word will drive chs.3–4.

Context

The return scene pivots the entire story. Naomi's theology shifts in v.20 — from "the LORD has brought misfortune upon me" (1:21) to "The LORD bless him!" She uses the divine name for the first time since her lament, now in blessing rather than accusation. The gōʾēl recognition is the moment Naomi sees the hand of God in the coincidence of v.3. He has not stopped showing ḥesed to the living and the dead.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:20

He has not stopped showing ḥesed to the living and the dead. Naomi's exclamation is theologically precise: Boaz's generosity to Ruth is simultaneously ḥesed to the living (Ruth and Naomi) and to the dead (Elimelek, Mahlon, Kilion). Covenant faithfulness extends beyond death.

2:20b

That man is our gōʾēl. The legal machinery has been identified. Naomi knows what this means: there is a path to redemption. The word gōʾēl appearing here for the first time is the structural hinge of the entire book.

2:12 — Under God's Wings

VersionTranslation
NIV"May you be richly rewarded by the LORD, the God of Israel, under whose wings you have come to take refuge."
NoteKānāp — wings. In 3:9 Ruth uses this word asking Boaz to spread his kānāp over her. His prayer in 2:12 becomes her marriage proposal in 3:9.

Ancient Sources

Gleaning law
Lev 19:9–10; Deut 24:19–22. Hard physical work — bending and gathering all day — but a legal right, not begging.
The gleaning provision is the legal infrastructure the story depends on.
An ephah
Approximately 22 litres / 10–12 kg of grain. An enormous amount for one day — Boaz's instructions to "pull out extra stalks" explain it.
The quantity signals extraordinary favour — Naomi's amazed response is appropriate.

Reception History

Luther on gleaning
The gleaning laws as OT welfare provision — dignified work with guaranteed access. Luther cited Ruth 2 in his social ethics writings on care for the poor.
The kānāp image in liturgy
Boaz's prayer became part of Jewish proselyte liturgy: the gēr (foreigner) who comes under God's wings is welcomed into the covenant community. Ruth is the paradigmatic case.

Chapter 2 is structured around two encounters: Ruth-and-field (vv.1–7) and Ruth-and-Boaz (vv.8–17). The first appears accidental; the second is wholly gracious. Together they show providence operating through circumstance and through character.

Theological Themes Tracker

Ruth 2 is the providence chapter. "As it turned out" frames divine orchestration. Boaz embodies ḥesed before anyone asks; Naomi recognises the gōʾēl and begins to hope again. The kānāp image will become the pivot of ch.3.
