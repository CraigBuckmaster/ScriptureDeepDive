Ruth 2

Ruth Gleans; Boaz Notices; The Gōʾēl Revealed

Hebrew History Context Cross-Ref Places People Timeline
Author

Unknown. Jewish tradition (Talmud, Bava Batra 14b) attributes the book to Samuel, though the text itself is anonymous. The author writes with intimate knowledge of Bethlehem and Moabite custom, and with a literary artistry that makes Ruth one of the most polished narratives in the OT. The genealogical close (4:18–22) pointing to David suggests a post-Davidic setting for the final form.

When written

Uncertain. The narrative is set in the period of the judges (1:1), and the archaic legal customs (the sandal ceremony, 4:7) suggest the author was writing at some distance from the events. Most scholars date the composition between c.1000–800 BC, with some placing it in the post-exilic period. The book's positive portrayal of a Moabite woman has been read as a response to the strict exclusivism of Ezra-Nehemiah, though this is disputed.

What prompted it

Ruth preserves the ancestry of David — and ultimately of the Messiah — through a Moabite woman whose loyalty to her mother-in-law becomes the vehicle of covenant faithfulness. The book explores how ḥesed (steadfast covenant love) operates through ordinary human faithfulness — gleaning, redemption, marriage — and culminates in the line that will produce Israel's greatest king. It implicitly challenges ethnic exclusivism: the grandmother of David was a Moabite convert whose loyalty exceeded that of many Israelites.

Verses 1–23 — Ruth Gleans; Boaz Notices; The Gōʾēl Revealed
1Now Naomi had a relative on her husband's side, a man of standing from the clan of Elimelek, whose name was Boaz. 2And Ruth the Moabite said to Naomi, "Let me go to the fields and pick up the leftover grain behind anyone in whose eyes I find favor." Naomi said to her, "Go ahead, my daughter." 3So she went out, entered a field and began to glean behind the harvesters. As it turned out, she was working in a field belonging to Boaz, who was from the clan of Elimelek. 4Just then Boaz arrived from Bethlehem and greeted the harvesters, "The LORD be with you!" "The LORD bless you!" they answered. 5Boaz asked the overseer of his harvesters, "Who does that young woman belong to?" 6The overseer replied, "She is the Moabite who came back from Moab with Naomi. 7She said, 'Please let me glean and gather among the sheaves behind the harvesters.' She came into the field and has worked steadily from morning till now, except for a short rest in the shelter." 8So Boaz said to Ruth, "My daughter, listen to me. Don't go and glean in another field and don't go away from here. Stay here with the women who work for me. 9Watch the field where the men are harvesting, and follow along after the women. I have told the men not to lay a hand on you. And whenever you are thirsty, go and get a drink from the water jars the men have filled." 10At this, she bowed down with her face to the ground. She asked him, "Why have I found such favor in your eyes that you notice me—a foreigner?" 11Boaz replied, "I've been told all about what you have done for your mother-in-law since the death of your husband—how you left your father and mother and your homeland and came to a people you did not know before. 12May the LORD repay you for what you have done. May you be richly rewarded by the LORD, the God of Israel, under whose wings you have come to take refuge." 13"May I continue to find favor in your eyes, my lord," she said. "You have put me at ease by speaking kindly to your servant—though I do not have the standing of one of your servants." 14At mealtime Boaz said to her, "Come over here. Have some bread and dip it in the wine vinegar." When she sat down with the harvesters, he offered her some roasted grain. She ate all she wanted and had some left over. 15As she got up to glean, Boaz gave orders to his men: "Let her gather among the sheaves and don't reprimand her. 16Even pull out some stalks from the bundles for her and leave them for her to pick up, and don't rebuke her." 17So Ruth gleaned in the field until evening. Then she threshed the barley she had gathered, and it amounted to about an ephah. 18She carried it back to town, and her mother-in-law saw how much she had gathered. Ruth also brought out and gave her what she had left over after she had eaten enough. 19Her mother-in-law asked her, "Where did you glean today? Where did you work? Blessed be the man who took notice of you!" Then Ruth told her mother-in-law about the one at whose place she had been working. "The name of the man I worked with today is Boaz," she said. 20"The LORD bless him!" Naomi said to her daughter-in-law. "He has not stopped showing his kindness to the living and the dead." She added, "That man is our close relative; he is one of our guardian-redeemers." 21Then Ruth the Moabite said, "He even said to me, 'Stay with my workers until they finish harvesting all my grain.'" 22Naomi said to Ruth her daughter-in-law, "It will be good for you, my daughter, to go with the women who work for him, because in someone else's field you might be harmed." 23So Ruth stayed close to the women of Boaz to glean until the barley and wheat harvests were finished. And she lived with her mother-in-law.

Hebrew Word Study

גֹּאֵלgōʾēlkinsman-redeemer

V.20: the pivotal legal term. A gōʾēl was a near kinsman with the legal right — and obligation — to redeem property, redeem persons from slavery, avenge blood, and marry a brother's widow (levirate). Naomi's recognition of Boaz as gōʾēl transforms the gleaning scene into the beginning of a legal redemption plot. This word will drive chapters 3 and 4.

כְּנָפֶיךָkĕnāpêkāyour wings

V.12: Boaz prays that Ruth will be rewarded under the LORD's kānāp (wings/hem). In ch.3, Ruth will use the same word — asking Boaz to spread his kānāp over her (3:9), which is a marriage proposal. Her request literalises his prayer: she is asking him to be the human agent of the divine protection he invoked.

חֵןḥēnfavour/grace

V.10: Ruth asks why she has found ḥēn in Boaz's eyes. The word means underserved favour — she has no natural claim on his attention. Boaz's answer (vv.11–12) reframes it: her ḥesed to Naomi is the reason. Human ḥēn responds to human ḥesed, and both mirror the divine pattern.

Key Places

Bethlehem fieldsJudean highlands, c.800m elevation

The barley harvest (late April–May) and wheat harvest (May–June) provided the seasonal window for the entire story. Fields outside ancient Bethlehem have been continuously cultivated for millennia.

Gleaning rightsLegal provision, not charity

Leviticus 19:9–10 and Deuteronomy 24:19–21 required leaving field edges and fallen grain for the poor and the foreigner. Gleaning was hard work — bending, gathering, threshing — but it was a dignified provision, not begging.

Theological Context

Chapter 2 works on two levels simultaneously. On the surface it is a story of agricultural provision — a Moabite widow gleaning behind harvesters. Beneath the surface it is the beginning of a legal-theological process: the gōʾēl system being set in motion. Boaz's notice of Ruth, his inquiry about her, his extraordinary generosity (pulling out extra stalks), and his mealtime inclusion — all exceed the legal minimum. He is already practising ḥesed before anyone has asked him to. The chapter ends with Naomi recognising the hand of God in what appears to be a coincidence (v.3: "as it turned out").

Cross-References

Lev 19:9–10; Deut 24:19

The gleaning law — the legal infrastructure that makes Ruth's survival possible. Boaz's generosity goes beyond the law's requirement.

Ps 91:4

Under his wings — the same image Boaz uses in v.12. God as protector whose kānāp shelters the vulnerable. Ruth has come under those wings by coming to Israel's God.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:3

As it turned out — literally "her chance chanced upon." The Hebrew deliberately uses a coincidence idiom to describe what the narrative presents as divine providence. The author is winking at the reader: this is not accident. The God who is never mentioned directly in Ruth's speech nevertheless orchestrates every encounter.

2:11–12

Boaz's speech to Ruth parallels the Abrahamic call (Gen 12:1): "you left your father and mother and your homeland." Ruth has done what Abraham did — left everything for the God of Israel. Boaz recognises this and frames his kindness as a response to her faith, not her ethnicity.

2:14

The shared meal is more than hospitality. Boaz serves Ruth himself, offers her roasted grain (a priestly food at the sanctuary), and she eats until satisfied with some left over. The Eucharistic overtones were noted by early Christian commentators: the generous host, the foreigner welcomed to the table, the provision that exceeds need.

2:20

He has not stopped showing ḥesed to the living and the dead. Naomi's exclamation is theologically precise: Boaz's generosity to Ruth is simultaneously ḥesed to the living (Ruth and Naomi) and to the dead (Elimelek, Mahlon, Kilion). Covenant faithfulness extends beyond death. And Naomi uses the divine name for the first time since her lament: "The LORD bless him."

2:12

Under whose wings you have come to take refuge. The kānāp image — God's wings as protection — appears in the Psalms (17:8; 36:7; 57:1; 91:4). Ruth is now sheltered under that protection. In ch.3 she will ask Boaz to spread his kānāp over her, literalising the prayer as a marriage proposal.

Ancient Sources & Scholarship

Gleaning laws

Lev 19:9–10; Deut 24:19–22 mandated leaving field edges and fallen grain for the poor and foreigner (gēr). The law gave Ruth legal access; Boaz's generosity far exceeded it.

The gleaning provision is the legal infrastructure the story depends on.

An ephah of barley

V.17: approximately 22 litres — about 10–12 kg of grain. An enormous amount for one day's gleaning. Ruth's haul suggests Boaz's instructions to "pull out stalks" were generously interpreted by his workers.

The quantity signals extraordinary favour.

Reception History

Luther on gleaning

The gleaning laws as the OT form of welfare provision — not a handout but dignified work with guaranteed access. Luther cited Ruth 2 in his social ethics writings.

The kānāp image in liturgy

Boaz's prayer (v.12) and the kānāp image became part of Jewish proselyte liturgy — the ger (foreigner) who comes under God's wings is welcomed into the community. Ruth is the paradigmatic case.

Chapter 2 is structured around two encounters: Ruth-and-field (vv.2–7) and Ruth-and-Boaz (vv.8–16). The first encounter appears accidental; the second is wholly gracious. Together they show providence operating through both impersonal circumstance and personal virtue.
