title: Why Is It Important That Jesus Was Jewish?

For most of church history, the Jewish identity of Jesus was acknowledged in principle and neglected in practice. Centuries of Christian anti-Judaism obscured the fact that everything about Jesus — his language, his Scriptures, his categories, his disciples, his family, his messianic claims — was Jewish. Only in the past half-century has scholarship given this fact the weight it deserves.

**Jesus was a first-century Galilean Jew.** He spoke Aramaic (his native tongue), read Hebrew (for public reading of Scripture), and likely knew enough Greek to conduct business in the Hellenized cities that surrounded Galilee. He was circumcised on the eighth day (Luke 2:21), attended synagogue regularly (Luke 4:16, "as was his custom"), observed the feasts (John 7, John 10), and kept the dietary laws in a Pharisaic household. He was called "rabbi" by his disciples and debated the interpretation of Torah with other Jewish teachers. His Scripture was the Hebrew Bible. His prayers, proverbs, and parables drew on rabbinic idiom.

**His message was thoroughly Jewish.** The kingdom of God was a Jewish hope, shaped by the Psalms and prophets. His call to repentance echoed John the Baptist and the prophets before him. His critique of Temple and religious leadership was a prophetic critique from **within** Israel, not an attack from outside. His parables used Galilean agricultural and fishing imagery. His beatitudes extend Isaiah and the Psalms. His Lord's Prayer is structured like a Jewish *qaddish*. Even his distinctive elements — authority to reinterpret Torah, self-reference as Son of Man — work within Jewish apocalyptic and wisdom categories.

**His disciples were Jewish.** The Twelve were all Jewish men — fishermen, tax collectors, zealots, ordinary Galileans. His closest circle included women of his family, women who had traveled with him from Galilee, and other Jewish disciples. The earliest church after his death consisted entirely of Jews worshiping in the Jerusalem Temple. The inclusion of Gentiles was itself a remarkable Jewish claim — that Israel's Messiah had come to bring the Gentile nations into the blessings promised to Abraham.

**His suffering and death happened inside the Jewish story.** The Passover setting of the Last Supper, the atoning logic of "this is my body given for you," the messianic expectations of crucifixion-and-resurrection read backward through Daniel 7 and Isaiah 53 — all these presuppose Jewish scriptural and liturgical frameworks. The resurrection was announced to Jewish women on the day after the Jewish Sabbath.

**Why this matters for reading the Bible:** Understanding Jesus as Jewish means:
- Reading the Old Testament as his Bible, not merely as our preparatory reading.
- Taking seriously Judaism — ancient and modern — as Jesus's own religious tradition, not a foil.
- Recognizing that Christian claims about Jesus are claims about Israel's Messiah, and hence belong within a continuing conversation with the Jewish people.
- Rejecting the long Christian habit of opposing Jesus to Judaism, legalism, or "Jewish works-righteousness" — distortions that have fed terrible evils in history.

To know Jesus you must know his Jewish world. He did not step out of it to found a new religion. He came to fulfill it.
