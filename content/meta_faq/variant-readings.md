If you compare modern Bible translations carefully you will sometimes notice footnotes like "Some early manuscripts do not include verses 9–20" (Mark's long ending), or "The earliest manuscripts lack John 7:53–8:11" (the woman caught in adultery), or "Some late manuscripts read..." These footnotes point to **textual variants** — places where the surviving Greek or Hebrew manuscripts disagree.

The sheer number of variants sounds alarming if you encounter the figure without context: roughly **400,000 variants** exist across the Greek New Testament manuscripts. But context transforms the figure. Over 99% of variants are trivial — differences of spelling, word order that Greek grammar permits freely, obvious scribal slips. Of the substantive variants, the overwhelming majority have no effect on doctrine. A handful of longer passages are genuinely contested, and in every such case the translator flags the uncertainty.

Three categories are worth understanding:

**Unintentional changes** are the most common. A scribe's eye skips from one line ending in "God" to the next line ending in "God," and the intervening words drop out (a *parablepsis*). A scribe hears "we" and "you" identically in dictated Greek and writes the wrong one (they differ by a single letter, ἡμεῖς / ὑμεῖς). These mistakes are almost always correctable by comparing other manuscripts.

**Harmonizations** happen when a scribe familiar with one Gospel subtly conforms a parallel passage in another — e.g., adding words from Matthew's Lord's Prayer to the shorter Lukan version. Careful comparison usually catches these.

**Deliberate changes** are rare but real. The Alexandrian scribe of the *pericope adulterae* probably inserted a well-loved oral tradition about Jesus into John's Gospel where it fit thematically. The scribe who wrote the long ending of Mark probably found the abrupt 16:8 ending unsatisfying. Rarely, doctrinal concerns drove a change — as in the fourth-century addition of trinitarian language in 1 John 5:7–8 (the "Johannine Comma"), preserved by the KJV but absent from all modern translations.

None of this touches the core of Christian faith. The incarnation, cross, resurrection, Pauline gospel, and ethical teaching are attested in multiple independent textual streams. No major doctrine hangs on a disputed verse.

**How to read a footnote.** When your translation notes a variant, do three things. First, read the verse and its context **without** the disputed words. Second, ask whether the surrounding theology changes. Third, consult the introduction to your translation — it explains the committee's policy for flagging variants and the base text used (usually NA28 or UBS5 for the NT, BHS/BHQ for the OT).

The footnotes are not cracks in the foundation. They are the craftsmanship of translators showing their work.
