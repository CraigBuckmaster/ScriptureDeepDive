The Hebrew Bible did not drop from the sky as a finished collection. It came together over roughly a millennium, through a process more like community recognition than central decree.

The earliest stratum is the Torah (Genesis–Deuteronomy). Deuteronomy 31 describes Moses depositing the book of the Law beside the ark of the covenant; 2 Kings 22 recounts the dramatic rediscovery of a long-lost Torah scroll during Josiah's reforms (c. 622 BCE). By the time of Ezra (mid-5th century BCE), the Torah functioned as authoritative scripture for the restored Jewish community — Nehemiah 8 narrates its public reading at the Water Gate.

The second division, the Prophets (Nevi'im), coalesced over the following centuries. The Former Prophets (Joshua through Kings) were grouped with the Latter Prophets (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and the Twelve) by the late Persian or early Hellenistic period. Ben Sira's grandson, writing a Greek prologue to his grandfather's book around 132 BCE, already refers to "the Law, the Prophets, and the other writings of our ancestors" — a threefold division consistent with the modern Hebrew Bible.

The Writings (Ketuvim) was the last division to solidify. It contains diverse material: Psalms, Proverbs, Job, the Megilloth, Daniel, Chronicles, Ezra-Nehemiah. Though most of these books were widely used well before the Common Era, the exact boundaries of this third division were not universally fixed until later. The so-called "Council of Jamnia" (c. 90 CE) is sometimes cited as a canon-setting event, but most scholars now view it as a rabbinic discussion rather than a formal closure.

Qumran (the Dead Sea Scrolls) shows a Jewish community in the late Second Temple period that treated a wide range of books as scripture — including some, like Jubilees and the Enochic literature, that did not enter the Hebrew Bible. This reminds us that canonical lines were still being drawn within Judaism during the time of Jesus and the apostles.

The Protestant Old Testament follows the 39-book Hebrew canon (counted differently in the Hebrew tradition, which gives a total of 24 books by combining several). The Catholic and Orthodox canons include additional books from the Greek Septuagint tradition — the Deuterocanon or "Apocrypha."

The canon was not imposed from above. It was received by communities that heard in these texts the voice of the living God, tested them over centuries of use, and found them to ring true.
