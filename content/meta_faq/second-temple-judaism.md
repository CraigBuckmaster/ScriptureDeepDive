"Second Temple Judaism" refers to the Judaism of the period from the rebuilding of the Jerusalem temple under Zerubbabel (c. 516 BCE) until its destruction by Titus's Roman legions (70 CE) — roughly six centuries. The New Testament takes shape in the final decades of this period, and understanding it is indispensable for understanding Jesus, Paul, and the early church.

Second Temple Judaism was not a monolithic system. It was a **diverse and contested religious world** in which different groups contested the meaning of Torah, Temple, land, and the covenant. The scholarly label "common Judaism" captures what most Jews shared (Torah, Temple, Sabbath, circumcision, food laws, kinship with Abraham); the diversity captures the fierce disagreements on how to live as God's people under Gentile rule.

**Major movements:**

**Pharisees.** A reform movement stressing detailed application of the Torah to daily life, the authority of the "oral Torah" as interpretative tradition, belief in the resurrection of the dead, and angelology. Not a small elite — a popular lay movement with broad influence. The New Testament presents them often in conflict with Jesus, but Jesus stands closer to them in many respects than to the other major groups. After 70 CE, rabbinic Judaism emerges from Pharisaic tradition.

**Sadducees.** A priestly-aristocratic party based at the Temple, holding political power, rejecting resurrection and angels, accepting only the Torah (not the broader canon) as fully authoritative, more willing to accommodate Roman rule. The Sadducees disappeared with the Temple.

**Essenes.** A monastic-like movement of priestly dissenters who considered the Jerusalem priesthood corrupt and withdrew to desert communities practicing extreme ritual purity. The Dead Sea Scrolls community at Qumran was likely Essene or Essene-related. They expected two messiahs (priestly and royal) and the imminent eschatological battle.

**Zealots and Sicarii.** Revolutionary groups committed to armed resistance against Rome. The Zealot movement coalesced in the 60s CE; the Sicarii ("dagger-men") engaged in targeted assassinations. Their 66 CE revolt led to the destruction of the Temple.

**Herodians.** Supporters of the Herodian dynasty and Roman-aligned political order. Appear occasionally in the Gospels as unlikely allies of Pharisees against Jesus.

**Samaritans.** A distinct Israelite community centered on Mt. Gerizim with their own Pentateuch and temple (destroyed c. 128 BCE). Regarded by Jews as half-breeds and apostates; regarded themselves as the true Israel.

**Diaspora Jews.** Communities of Jews throughout the Greco-Roman world — especially Alexandria, Antioch, Asia Minor, Rome. Read the Septuagint. Attracted "God-fearers," Gentiles drawn to Jewish monotheism and ethics but not fully converting. Provided the initial seedbed for Christian mission.

**Key shared features of Second Temple Judaism:**

- **Monotheism** — uncompromised in the face of Greco-Roman polytheism.
- **Torah** — the foundational story and law. Interpretation varied; authority did not.
- **Temple** — the central institution until 70, focus of sacrifice, pilgrimage, and Jerusalem's economy.
- **Sabbath** — the day that defined Jewish communal identity anywhere in the empire.
- **Messianic expectation** — not uniform, but widespread. Different groups expected different figures: a Davidic king, a priestly Messiah, a prophet like Moses, a heavenly Son of Man, two Messiahs.
- **Resurrection** — an increasingly prominent hope, debated between Pharisees (yes) and Sadducees (no).
- **Apocalypticism** — a cluster of ideas about imminent divine intervention, cosmic judgment, and the reordering of the age.

**For reading the New Testament:** every person and group in the Gospels belongs somewhere on this map. Jesus, John the Baptist, Paul, Peter, the Jerusalem apostles all emerge from this world. Understanding its categories is not scholarly decoration. It is the indispensable background without which much of the New Testament cannot be heard.
