Most of the Bible is **narrative** — story. Genesis, Exodus, Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings, Chronicles, Ezra, Nehemiah, Esther, Ruth, Jonah, the Gospels, and Acts together constitute more than half the canon. Learning to read narrative well is essential for reading the Bible well.

Biblical narrative has distinctive features that differ from both ancient histories and modern novels.

**Economy.** Biblical writers are famously sparse with detail. Where a modern novelist might devote pages to a character's appearance and inner life, the biblical narrator says only what is necessary. When he does mention something — Esau's red hair (Gen 25:25), David's beauty (1 Sam 16:12), Zacchaeus's short stature (Luke 19:3) — it matters. Every detail is load-bearing.

**Showing, not telling.** Biblical narrative rarely moralizes. The narrator tells us David did something and leaves us to judge it. 2 Samuel 11 is a masterpiece of ethical restraint: "And David remained in Jerusalem." "And Bathsheba came to him." "And he lay with her." The narrator is not neutral — the terse prose is an indictment — but he invites us to see rather than lectures us.

**Repetition and variation.** Hebrew narrative uses **type-scenes** — recognizable situations (a man meets a woman at a well; a mother overcomes barrenness; a younger brother prevails over an elder) — deployed with subtle variations. Noticing when a story conforms to and departs from its type-scene reveals the narrator's emphasis. The three "wife-sister" episodes in Genesis (12, 20, 26), for instance, form a pattern that illuminates each individual one.

**Dialogue.** In biblical narrative, the first words a character speaks are characteristic — a signature. God's first words establish his nature ("Let there be light"). Jesus's first words in each Gospel set that Gospel's theme. Paul's first recorded words after conversion ("Who are you, Lord?") capture his entire subsequent life.

**Narrative perspective.** The narrator of biblical story tends to be **omniscient and reliable** — he knows God's thoughts, angels' conversations, private motives. But he rarely evaluates out loud. We must infer judgment from the structure: who gets the last word, whose prayer is answered, whose family line endures.

**Not a moral lesson in disguise.** Biblical narrative does not fit the "aim for the moral at the end" model. Genesis 22 is not chiefly a story about obeying even when hard. Ruth is not chiefly a story about loyalty. These stories carry moral weight, but they are primarily about the character and purposes of God. The moral emerges organically from the theological.

**Genre cues across the canon.**

- **Torah narrative** (Genesis–Deuteronomy): origin stories shaping Israel's identity.
- **Former Prophets** (Joshua–Kings): the covenant history of the nation.
- **Chronicles**: the same history reframed for post-exilic worship.
- **Gospels**: ancient *bios* (biography) with theological shaping, climaxing in the passion.
- **Acts**: a Hellenistic history of the early church.

Read biblical narrative with a pen in hand. Ask: what is **not** said? what is repeated? what is the narrator's silent judgment? The texture is the theology.
