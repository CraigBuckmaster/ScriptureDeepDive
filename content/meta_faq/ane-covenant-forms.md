The word "covenant" — Hebrew *berit*, Greek *diathēkē* — describes a binding agreement formalized by oath, often sealed by a sacrifice. The biblical authors use this word to describe God's relationship with Israel, and understanding ancient covenant conventions illuminates almost every page of the Old Testament.

**The Ancient Near East knew several covenant types:**

**1. Parity covenants.** Between equals — two kings, or two families, or two individuals. Examples: David and Jonathan (1 Sam 18:3), Laban and Jacob (Gen 31).

**2. Royal grants.** A sovereign unilaterally promises a gift — usually land, dynasty, or priesthood — to a loyal servant or his descendants. Often unconditional. Examples: God to Abraham (Gen 15), to David (2 Sam 7), to Phinehas (Num 25:11–13).

**3. Suzerain-vassal treaties.** The dominant form for understanding much of biblical covenant language. A great king (*suzerain*) binds a lesser king or nation (*vassal*) by covenant, promising protection in exchange for loyalty and tribute. These treaties followed a recognizable format preserved in hundreds of surviving texts (especially Hittite, 14th–13th century BCE, and Neo-Assyrian, 8th–7th century BCE).

**The standard Hittite suzerain-vassal treaty form had six parts:**

- **Preamble.** Identification of the suzerain: "Thus says [great king]."
- **Historical prologue.** Reminder of the suzerain's past kindness to the vassal, establishing gratitude as the basis for obligation.
- **Stipulations.** The terms — what the vassal owes (exclusive allegiance, tribute, military support).
- **Deposit and public reading.** The treaty stored in the temple and read periodically.
- **Witnesses.** The gods of both nations invoked as witnesses.
- **Blessings and curses.** Consequences for keeping or breaking the covenant.

**Deuteronomy — especially chapters 1–30 — strikingly parallels this form:**

- Preamble: "These are the words that Moses spoke" (Deut 1:1–5).
- Historical prologue: the whole journey from Horeb to Moab (Deut 1–4).
- Stipulations: the commandments and statutes (Deut 5–26).
- Deposit and reading: the book placed beside the ark, read every seven years (Deut 31:9–13).
- Witnesses: heaven and earth (Deut 30:19; 31:28 — unusual in the ANE, where deities would serve).
- Blessings and curses: Deut 27–28.

Joshua 24 (the covenant renewal at Shechem) shows the same pattern on a smaller scale. The **Decalogue** (Exod 20; Deut 5) itself opens with the preamble and historical prologue: "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of Egypt."

**The theological force of the parallel.**

- **Grace precedes law.** Just as the suzerain first rehearses his kindness, Yahweh rehearses his deliverance before requiring obedience. Covenantal obedience is gratitude, not a merit system.
- **Exclusive loyalty.** Just as vassals could have no other suzerain, Israel can have no other God — the first commandment.
- **Consequences.** Blessings and curses are not arbitrary; they follow from the nature of the covenant itself.
- **Covenant community.** Israel is not a collection of individuals but a people bound by covenant to the same Lord.

**The new covenant in the prophets and the New Testament.** Jeremiah 31 announces a new covenant — not the old covenant renewed, but something qualitatively different: law written on hearts, sins forgotten, all knowing the Lord directly. The New Testament claims that this covenant has been inaugurated in Jesus's blood (Luke 22:20; Heb 8–10). The ANE covenant forms make clear what a momentous claim this is.

To know the Bible is to know it as a covenant document. Covenant categories run through the whole canon.
