"King James Only" (KJVO) is the conviction, held by a minority of Protestants, that the **King James Version (1611)** — often in its 1769 revision — is the only legitimate English Bible, or in stronger forms, is itself divinely inspired and preserved. The movement developed in the twentieth century as new translations (RV, ASV, RSV, NIV) proliferated, and it exists on a spectrum from moderate to absolute.

A useful typology (originally from James White's *The King James Only Controversy*):

- **Preferred translation.** The KJV is valued for its literary quality, familiar cadences, or traditional usage; other translations are accepted as legitimate. (This is not really KJVO.)
- **Preserved text.** The *Textus Receptus* (the Greek text underlying the KJV) is accepted as a reliable representation of the original, but the KJV's English is not itself inspired. (This view has serious scholarly defenders.)
- **Inspired translation.** The 1611 translators were themselves moved by the Spirit, so the KJV is inerrant in English. Modern translations corrupt this inspired text.
- **Advanced revelation.** The KJV is inspired in a way that makes it **superior** to the original-language manuscripts — the English text corrects the Greek.

Moderate positions have scholarly advocates (Edward Hills, Jakob van Bruggen). The stronger positions, associated most closely with Peter Ruckman and David Otis Fuller in the twentieth century, make claims that mainstream scholarship — evangelical and otherwise — finds unsustainable.

**Mainstream evangelical response:**

- The KJV is a treasure of English literature and a faithful translation for its time.
- Its base text (Textus Receptus) is a late-medieval Byzantine Greek text that did not have access to most of the early papyri and great uncials discovered since 1611.
- Early manuscripts generally support a text shorter than the TR in places like 1 John 5:7–8, the longer ending of Mark, and the *pericope adulterae*, though these are flagged in every modern translation.
- Modern translations are not "liberal corruptions" — the ESV, NASB, NIV, and CSB are conservative evangelical translations made by committees of believing scholars using the best available Greek and Hebrew texts.
- The claim that God "preserved" His word specifically in the 1611 KJV imports a doctrine of preservation that no previous generation held — the pre-Reformation church used the Vulgate for a thousand years.

**What the KJV still offers:**

- Unmatched literary power and rhythm. The KJV shaped English prose for 400 years.
- A consistent translation philosophy (formal equivalence) executed by a multidenominational committee of remarkable scholarship.
- Deep resonance in hymnody, literature, and memorized quotation.

The wise path is to read the KJV alongside other translations, honor its history, and recognize that God's word is not confined to a single seventeenth-century English rendering.
