**Hermeneutics** is the discipline of interpretation — in biblical studies, the theory and practice of reading Scripture rightly. The word comes from the Greek *hermēneuō*, "to interpret" (Hermes, the messenger-god who translated divine messages to humans).

Every reader is already interpreting, consciously or not. Hermeneutics asks: what methods, assumptions, and disciplines make interpretation faithful rather than idiosyncratic? It aims to equip readers to do what they are doing anyway — read Scripture — with humility, rigor, and fruitfulness.

**The traditional fourfold sense** (medieval exegesis):

- **Literal** — what the text says in its plain historical sense.
- **Allegorical** — the text's symbolic reference to Christ and the church.
- **Tropological (moral)** — what the text teaches about how to live.
- **Anagogical** — what the text points to about the last things.

This scheme produced rich readings but could exceed its constraints; Reformers pushed back toward the priority of the literal-historical sense.

**The Reformation watershed.** The Reformers argued for the priority of the **literal sense** (what the author intended), accessible through the ordinary rules of language and genre, to which the allegorical and moral senses must be anchored. Calvin: "The true meaning of Scripture is the natural and obvious meaning." Luther: "Unless I am convinced by Scripture and plain reason..."

**Modern hermeneutical contributions.**

- **Historical-critical method** (19th–20th century): reading texts in their historical setting, asking who wrote to whom, when, and why. When used as a supplement to faithful reading, invaluable; when used to dissect against the grain of the text's claims, destructive.
- **Literary approaches**: paying attention to narrative art, structure, characterization, and genre (Robert Alter, Meir Sternberg, Leland Ryken).
- **Theological interpretation of Scripture** (TIS): reading Scripture as the living word of the church, attending to the rule of faith, historic creeds, and canonical context (Stephen Fowl, Kevin Vanhoozer, Brevard Childs).
- **Reader-response and postcolonial readings**: attending to how reader location shapes interpretation, for both good and ill.

**Core principles of sound hermeneutics.**

**1. Start with the plain sense.** The historical-grammatical meaning — what the words meant in their original context — is the anchor. Other senses build on this foundation; they do not replace it.

**2. Read in context.** The immediate paragraph, the chapter, the book, the whole canon. Isolated verses are prooftexts waiting to be abused.

**3. Respect the genre.** Proverbs is not law, apocalyptic is not chronicle, psalms are not systematic theology. Reading each genre by its own rules is essential.

**4. Read the Old Testament in light of the New, and the New in light of the Old.** The Bible interprets itself. Jesus in Luke 24:27 "explained to them what was said in all the Scriptures concerning himself" — the whole canon is Christologically oriented.

**5. Read in community.** Hermeneutics is not a solo act. The church across history — fathers, medieval scholars, Reformers, contemporary scholars, your local community — offers checks and enrichments. Private interpretation unchecked by communal wisdom is a recipe for error.

**6. Read for transformation, not just information.** Bible study that does not shape life has missed the point. The Spirit illumines Scripture to change the reader.

**7. Submit your interpretation to the text.** Good hermeneutics practices **suspicion of yourself** before suspicion of the text. Ask: am I reading this to confirm what I already think, or am I letting the text question me?

**Common pitfalls.**

- **Prooftexting** — isolating verses to support predetermined conclusions.
- **Allegorizing** — finding symbolic meanings unwarranted by the text.
- **Spiritualizing** — draining a concrete claim of its concrete meaning.
- **Eisegesis** — reading meaning *into* the text rather than drawing it *out* (exegesis).
- **Reading the Bible like a newspaper** (flat literal) or **like poetry** (universalized spiritual), when the genre demands something else.
- **Ignoring church tradition** — treating yourself as the first person to read this text.

Hermeneutics at its best is not a set of tricks for extracting meaning but a way of submitting to the text, the church, the author, and the Spirit who inspired both.
