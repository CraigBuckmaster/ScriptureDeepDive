**Plenary verbal inspiration** is a theological doctrine articulated especially in the Reformed and conservative evangelical traditions to describe the nature of Scripture's inspiration. "Plenary" means "full" or "complete"; "verbal" means "extending to the words." Taken together, the phrase claims that the Bible is inspired by God **in all its parts** and **in its very words**, not merely in its general ideas or its authoritative decisions.

**Two key proof-texts.**

- **2 Timothy 3:16** — "All Scripture is God-breathed (*theopneustos*) and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting and training in righteousness." Paul affirms both plenary (all Scripture) and the divine origin (God-breathed).
- **2 Peter 1:20–21** — "Above all, you must understand that no prophecy of Scripture came about by the prophet's own interpretation of things. For prophecy never had its origin in the human will, but prophets, though human, spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit."

**What plenary verbal inspiration affirms.**

- **Divine authorship.** Scripture is God's self-revelation, given by the Holy Spirit.
- **Human authorship.** The biblical writers were not passive stenographers. They used their own vocabulary, style, historical setting, and personality. The Spirit's inspiration worked through them, not around them.
- **The product is God's word.** What the writers produced — the words on the page — is what God intended to produce. The text is therefore authoritative and reliable in what it teaches.
- **All of Scripture.** Not merely the "red letters" of Jesus, not merely moral teaching, not merely theological statements, but the whole canonical text — history, poetry, prophecy, genealogy — bears divine authority.
- **The words, not just the ideas.** The inspired text is not an abstract meaning separable from the words that communicate it. When we interpret Scripture, we interpret a textual object the Spirit gave.

**What plenary verbal inspiration does not teach.**

- **Mechanical dictation.** The Spirit did not override the writers; he worked through their personalities, research (Luke 1:1–4), emotions, and even their particular vocabulary.
- **Ignorance of the original cultural setting.** Inspired writers still wrote for specific audiences in specific times using specific genres.
- **Uniformity of style.** Isaiah's Hebrew is poetic; Mark's Greek is rough; Hebrews's Greek is polished. These stylistic differences reflect different authors, not different levels of inspiration.
- **Imperviousness to textual criticism.** Scripture is inspired in its original form. Textual criticism seeks to recover that form; the resulting small variations (well below the level of doctrine) do not undermine inspiration.
- **Dictation of translations.** No English Bible is itself inspired; translations inherit inspiration by faithfully rendering the inspired original.

**Related doctrines.**

- **Inerrancy** — the claim that Scripture, rightly interpreted in its original manuscripts, does not err in what it teaches. Held strongly by many evangelicals (the 1978 Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy articulates a careful version). Other Christians hold to **infallibility** — that Scripture does not fail to achieve its saving purpose — without asserting inerrancy in every historical and scientific detail.
- **Illumination** — the Spirit's ongoing work to help readers understand what was once and for all inspired.
- **Perspicuity** — the claim that the central message of Scripture (the gospel) is clear enough for ordinary readers to grasp, even when particular passages are difficult.

Christians affirming plenary verbal inspiration read Scripture as God's word — authoritative, truthful, sufficient — even while humbly recognizing the need for careful interpretation, translation, and ongoing study. The doctrine is less a barrier to reading than a warrant for reading Scripture with the seriousness it deserves.
