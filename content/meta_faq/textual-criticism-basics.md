**Textual criticism** is the scholarly discipline that seeks to recover the earliest attainable form of an ancient text by comparing surviving manuscripts, versions, and citations. Because the original autographs of biblical books no longer exist, every modern Bible depends on textual-critical judgments — even if most readers never notice.

The task is necessary because **every manuscript copied by hand has variants**. Scribes drop or repeat words, transpose letters, mis-hear dictation, harmonize passages, or (rarely) deliberately alter a text. Over centuries of transmission, thousands of variants accumulate. Textual criticism sorts through them.

**External evidence** asks: which witnesses support which reading? The age, geographic spread, and independence of the witnesses matter more than sheer numbers. A reading attested by two early manuscripts from different regions outweighs a reading attested by hundreds of later copies descended from a single exemplar.

**Internal evidence** asks: which reading best explains the others? Two rules of thumb guide the analysis:

- **The shorter reading is usually original.** Scribes more often expand than abbreviate.
- **The harder reading is usually original.** Scribes more often smoothed difficulties than created them.

Both rules have exceptions and must be used with judgment. Sometimes a longer reading is original and a scribal eye-skip produced the shorter. Sometimes a grammatical oddity really was a scribal error the original author did not commit.

**The New Testament** has the richest manuscript tradition of any ancient document — over 5,800 Greek manuscripts, some within a century of composition. The overwhelming majority of variants are trivial: spelling differences, changes of word order that do not affect translation, synonym substitutions. A handful of places involve longer variants of theological interest — the long ending of Mark (16:9–20), the *pericope adulterae* (John 7:53–8:11), the Johannine Comma (1 John 5:7–8 KJV), the "trinitarian" doxology of the Lord's Prayer — and in each case modern critical Bibles carefully flag the uncertainty.

**The Old Testament** has fewer early witnesses but enjoys the remarkable fidelity of Jewish scribal tradition. The **Masoretic Text** has preserved the Hebrew with astonishing accuracy over a millennium, as confirmed by the Dead Sea Scrolls. The **Septuagint** and other ancient versions sometimes preserve earlier readings where the MT has suffered corruption. Modern OT translation consults all these witnesses.

**Critical apparatus** is the technical name for the footnotes in a scholarly edition that list textual variants. Learning to read an apparatus is the door into serious biblical study.

Textual criticism is not skepticism about the Bible. It is the opposite — the painstaking discipline that makes confident reading possible. No other ancient document has been subjected to such scrutiny, and none has survived it so well.
