The Judaism Jesus knew was not a single monolithic religion but a contested field of movements, each with its own take on Torah, Temple, and the political crisis of Roman occupation. The New Testament assumes familiarity with these groups. Reading the Gospels intelligibly requires a basic map.

**Pharisees.** A popular lay reform movement (though including some priests and scribes) devoted to the detailed application of the Torah to everyday life. They developed the tradition of the "oral Torah" — inherited interpretive commentary meant to "build a fence around the Torah." Pharisees believed in:

- Resurrection of the dead.
- Angels and demons.
- Divine providence balanced with human responsibility.
- The authority of scripture plus oral tradition.

Not a small elite: they had influence in synagogues, schools, and popular piety. **Jesus stands closer to the Pharisees on many issues than to any other group** (resurrection, angels, scriptural authority, devotion to Torah). His fiercest disputes are with Pharisees partly for the same reason siblings argue — they are close cousins who disagree vehemently about their shared heritage. After 70 CE, Pharisaic Judaism evolved into rabbinic Judaism, the tradition of the Mishnah, Talmud, and most subsequent Judaism.

**Sadducees.** A priestly-aristocratic party based at the Temple, dominant in the high-priestly family, Temple operations, and the Sanhedrin. They held:

- Only the Torah (not the Prophets or Writings) as fully authoritative.
- No resurrection, no angels (per Acts 23:8 and Mark 12:18 pars.).
- Greater accommodation with Roman rule.

Sadducees disappeared when the Temple was destroyed; their religious platform depended on Temple operations.

**Essenes.** A separatist movement, likely including the Dead Sea Scrolls community at Qumran. They regarded the Jerusalem priesthood as corrupt, withdrew to desert communities, practiced rigorous ritual purity, shared property in common, and expected two messiahs (priestly and royal) and an imminent eschatological war. The NT does not name them explicitly, though John the Baptist's wilderness ministry may reflect Essene influence.

**Zealots.** A political-religious movement committed to armed resistance against Rome. The name "Zealot" derives from Phinehas (Num 25) and the Maccabees; the movement crystallized in the mid-first century CE and sparked the Jewish War of 66–70. One of Jesus's disciples, Simon, is called "the Zealot" (Luke 6:15) — whether in the formal party sense or as a descriptor of religious intensity is debated. The **Sicarii** ("dagger-men") were a terrorist offshoot that assassinated collaborators.

**Samaritans.** A distinct Israelite community centered on Mt. Gerizim, claiming descent from the northern tribes (Jews said from Assyrian settlers, a contested history). They accepted only the Torah (in their own Samaritan Pentateuch recension) and had their own temple until it was destroyed in 128 BCE. Relations with Jews were hostile — the woman at the well (John 4), the parable of the Good Samaritan (Luke 10), and the village's refusal of hospitality (Luke 9:51–56) all assume this hostility.

**Herodians.** Supporters of the Herodian dynasty and Roman-aligned political order. Appear as unlikely allies of Pharisees against Jesus (Mark 3:6; 12:13).

**Diaspora Jews and God-fearers.** Outside Palestine, Jewish communities lived throughout the Greco-Roman world — Alexandria, Antioch, Asia Minor, Rome. "God-fearers" were Gentiles attracted to Jewish monotheism and ethics who attended synagogues without fully converting. Many early Christian converts came from this group.

**Followers of John the Baptist.** A renewal movement that continued after John's death (Acts 18:25; 19:3–4). Jesus's own ministry began within John's movement before developing beyond it.

**Jesus and his disciples** belong on this map as a new movement, sharing much with Pharisaic piety while differentiating themselves in significant ways — relaxed Sabbath enforcement, table fellowship with sinners, authoritative reinterpretation of Torah, and above all, the centrality of Jesus himself.
