**Prophecy** is one of the most misread genres of the Bible. In popular imagination prophecy means prediction of future events — often cryptic timetables for the end of the world. In biblical context, prophecy is mostly something different: the prophet as God's covenant messenger, calling Israel (and sometimes the nations) back to faithfulness, announcing judgment and hope, and occasionally revealing what is coming.

**Who were the prophets?** Figures like **Elijah**, **Elisha**, **Amos**, **Hosea**, **Isaiah**, **Jeremiah**, and **Ezekiel** were not primarily crystal-ball seers. They were **covenant enforcers**, raised up by God at moments of national crisis. They spoke God's word into specific political, economic, and religious situations — Assyrian threat, Babylonian exile, post-exilic restoration, social injustice. The primary audience was the prophet's own contemporaries, not people millennia later.

**Two kinds of prophecy.**

- **Forthtelling** — declaring God's word about the present. Most prophetic literature is this: indicting covenant violations (idolatry, oppression, injustice), calling for repentance, warning of consequences. Amos's denunciation of Bashan cows (the luxurious elite of Samaria) trampling the poor is forthtelling.
- **Foretelling** — announcing what will happen. Prophets do speak of the future — God's coming judgment, exile, restoration, and ultimately the messianic age. But even foretelling is usually anchored to the prophet's present: God is acting in history, so what is to come matters for how we live now.

**Key literary features.**

- **Oracles.** Short, often poetic units delivered in specific settings. The oracles are sometimes arranged thematically, sometimes roughly chronologically, but the arrangement rarely follows a strict timeline.
- **Lawsuit (rîb) form.** God as plaintiff, heaven and earth as witnesses, Israel in the dock (Mic 6; Isa 1).
- **Woe oracles.** Indictments that begin "Woe!" (Isa 5; Hab 2; Matt 23).
- **Oracles against the nations.** Judgment on Israel's neighbors (Isa 13–23; Jer 46–51; Amos 1–2).
- **Salvation oracles.** Promises of restoration and hope (Isa 40–55; Jer 30–33).
- **Symbolic acts.** Prophets living out their message — Isaiah walking naked (Isa 20), Jeremiah breaking a jar (Jer 19), Hosea marrying an unfaithful wife (Hos 1), Ezekiel lying on his side for over a year (Ezek 4).
- **Visions.** Dream-like encounters with God that are both real experience and symbolic communication (Isa 6; Jer 1; Ezek 1; Dan 7).

**Common reading mistakes.**

- Treating every prophetic passage as a puzzle pointing to the 21st century. Most prophecy was addressed to the prophet's own generation.
- Reading messianic prophecies in isolation from their original context. "Unto us a child is born" had a first application in Isaiah's day; its fuller meaning was seen later.
- Ignoring **conditional prophecy** — the implicit condition that repentance can avert judgment (Jer 18:7–10; Jonah).
- Expecting chronological arrangement where themes are at play.

**Reading well.** Ask: what was happening when this was spoken? To whom was it addressed? What covenant obligation was being broken or restored? What aspect of God's character is on display? How does the New Testament pick up these themes? Then — only then — ask what it means for today. The prophets' word for their time is the starting point for our word today.
