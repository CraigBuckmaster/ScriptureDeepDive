"Apocrypha" is the name Protestants give to a set of Jewish religious writings produced roughly between 300 BCE and 100 CE that were included in the Greek Septuagint and early Christian Bibles but excluded from the Hebrew canon. Catholics and Orthodox call these books **deuterocanonical** ("second canon") and regard them as Scripture. Protestants regard them as valuable historical and devotional literature but not inspired.

The core collection — the **Apocrypha proper** — contains:

- **Tobit** — a folk tale set in the Assyrian exile, featuring the angel Raphael and a romantic quest.
- **Judith** — a heroic novella about a pious widow who assassinates the Assyrian general Holofernes.
- **Wisdom of Solomon** — a Hellenistic wisdom book extending the tradition of Proverbs and Ecclesiastes.
- **Sirach (Ecclesiasticus)** — a lengthy collection of proverbs and moral instruction by Ben Sira, c. 180 BCE.
- **Baruch** and the **Letter of Jeremiah** — prophetic works attributed to Jeremiah's scribe.
- **1 and 2 Maccabees** — historical accounts of the Maccabean revolt against Antiochus IV Epiphanes (167–160 BCE).
- **Additions to Esther** — Greek expansions giving Esther a more explicitly religious tone.
- **Additions to Daniel** — the Prayer of Azariah, the Song of the Three Young Men, Susanna, and Bel and the Dragon.

These books were part of the Greek Bible the early church inherited from Hellenistic Judaism. The New Testament alludes to them in places (Heb 11:35 seems to reference 2 Maccabees 7; James contains echoes of Sirach) but does not cite them with the formula "as it is written" that introduces canonical Scripture.

**The term "Apocrypha" is confusing** because it is used more broadly in different contexts:

- For Catholics, "Apocrypha" means **Pseudepigrapha** — later Jewish and Christian writings falsely attributed to biblical figures (1 Enoch, Jubilees, Testament of the Twelve Patriarchs, Apocalypse of Abraham). These are excluded from all canons.
- For Protestants, "Apocrypha" means the deuterocanonical books listed above.
- In New Testament studies, "NT Apocrypha" refers to a large body of later gospels, acts, epistles, and apocalypses (Gospel of Thomas, Acts of Paul, Apocalypse of Peter) that were never seriously considered canonical.

Whatever the terminology, the Apocrypha is **worth reading**. 1 and 2 Maccabees are the primary sources for the intertestamental period, without which the world of the New Testament is unintelligible. Sirach is one of the finest wisdom texts in any tradition. Wisdom of Solomon shaped early Christian thinking about the soul and the Logos. Tobit and Judith shaped Christian art for a millennium.

Protestants who have never read the Apocrypha miss important background. Catholics who have read only the Apocrypha miss the Hebrew roots. The wise reader reads both.
