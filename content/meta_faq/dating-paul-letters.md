Dating Paul's letters is an exercise in triangulating internal clues (who Paul is with, where he is writing from, what collection he is organizing, what crisis he is addressing) with the fixed points of his movements reconstructed from Acts. No letter gives an explicit date. All datings are approximate, and scholars disagree on a few by 5–10 years.

A mainstream evangelical chronology:

- **Galatians** — c. 48–49 CE on the South-Galatia theory (before the Jerusalem Council, Acts 15), or c. 54–55 on the North-Galatia theory. Either way, it is among the earliest and addresses a crisis about Gentile circumcision.
- **1 Thessalonians** — c. 50–51 CE from Corinth, soon after Paul's departure from Thessalonica (Acts 17). Earliest letter on the North-Galatia view; second-earliest on the South-Galatia view.
- **2 Thessalonians** — c. 51 CE, if authentic (some scholars dispute Pauline authorship). Follows up on confusion about the day of the Lord.
- **1 Corinthians** — c. 54–55 CE from Ephesus. Addresses multiple crises in the Corinthian church.
- **2 Corinthians** — c. 55–56 CE from Macedonia, responding to the aftermath of 1 Corinthians and a painful intervening visit.
- **Romans** — c. 57–58 CE from Corinth, written to introduce himself to a church he had not founded, laying out his gospel in preparation for his planned visit en route to Spain.
- **Philemon** — c. 60–62 CE from Roman imprisonment.
- **Colossians** — c. 60–62 CE from Roman imprisonment, closely tied to Philemon.
- **Ephesians** — c. 60–62 CE from Roman imprisonment, a circular letter related to Colossians.
- **Philippians** — c. 60–62 CE from Roman imprisonment (though some scholars argue for an Ephesian imprisonment producing it earlier).
- **1 Timothy** — c. 62–64 CE after release from Rome (traditional view); or post-Pauline (critical view).
- **Titus** — c. 62–64 CE on the traditional view.
- **2 Timothy** — c. 64–67 CE during Paul's final imprisonment and awaiting execution (traditional view).

**Disputed authorship.** Modern critical scholarship often accepts the "undisputed seven" (Romans, 1–2 Corinthians, Galatians, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, Philemon) as unambiguously Pauline, but considers 2 Thessalonians, Ephesians, Colossians, and the Pastoral Epistles to be "disputed" — authored later by followers. Conservative evangelical scholarship generally accepts all 13 as Paul's own, allowing for the significant role of his scribes (e.g., Tertius in Rom 16:22) and associates.

**How to date a Pauline letter as you read.** Ask: Where is Paul? Who is with him? What church is he writing to, and when did he last visit? What crisis or occasion prompted the letter? Cross-reference these with Acts and with other Pauline letters. The letters are deeply contextual. Knowing the likely setting transforms the reading from abstract theology to pastoral correspondence with specific urgency — which it was.
