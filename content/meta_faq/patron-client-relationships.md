In the Greco-Roman world and much of the Ancient Near East, relationships between unequal social powers were structured by **patronage**. A wealthy or powerful person (the *patron*) offered protection, favor, and resources to a less powerful person (the *client*) who reciprocated with loyalty, public praise, and service. Understanding patron-client conventions changes how we read many New Testament passages, especially those involving gifts, grace, faith, and gratitude.

**The basic pattern:**

- **Patrons** gave: money, protection, job recommendations, introductions, legal support.
- **Clients** owed: public praise of the patron (in the forum, at parties, in letters), presence at the patron's morning greetings (*salutationes*), electoral support, testimony against the patron's enemies.
- **Brokers** connected patrons and clients — intermediaries who extended the patron's reach and the client's access.

Roman society was saturated with this pattern. Seneca wrote a whole treatise, *De Beneficiis* ("On Benefits"), analyzing the obligations of giving and receiving. Cicero's letters to and from his patrons and clients read like an endless calculation of who owes what to whom. The emperor himself was the ultimate patron, the *princeps*, the first benefactor of Rome.

**New Testament categories that reflect patronage:**

- ***Charis*** (Greek "grace" / "favor" / "benefit"). In patronage, *charis* meant the favor the patron gave — and the thanks (Latin *gratia*, whence both "grace" and "gratitude") the client owed. Paul's gospel of grace is saying: God has become the supreme patron. His *charis* is unearned, but it creates in the recipient a life of praise and loyalty (*charis* answered with *charis*).
- ***Pistis*** (Greek "faith" / "faithfulness"). In patronage, *pistis* was the loyalty a client owed his patron — persistent, public, active. Paul's "justification by faith" is saying: the right relationship with God is established by loyal adherence to him, not by earning merit.
- ***Euangelion*** (Greek "good news"). Greek cities made public announcements — *euangelia* — of imperial benefactions. The church's "good news" is that God has granted a benefaction surpassing any emperor's.
- **Glory, honor, peace, salvation.** All language Rome used for imperial benefaction, now reapplied to what God has done through Christ.

**Key examples.**

- **The Centurion and the sick servant (Luke 7:1–10).** The centurion's relationship to the Jewish elders reveals patronage — "he has built us our synagogue" (v. 5). His plea to Jesus invokes his own command-and-obey world. His faith that Jesus can heal from a distance stuns Jesus because it recognizes Jesus's kingly patronage over illness.
- **Paul in Philemon.** Paul's plea for Onesimus is a masterpiece of patronage negotiation. Paul is simultaneously Philemon's spiritual patron (he led Philemon to faith), his debtor (he speaks of paying what Onesimus owed), and the elder exercising authority. The letter is a delicate dance of honor.
- **Jesus healing the Samaritan leper (Luke 17).** Only one of ten returns to give thanks — a commentary on the ingratitude of beneficiaries in an honor culture, where praise owed to the patron is the minimum expectation.

**Pastoral implications.**

- **Grace is not charity in the modern sense** — detached, anonymous giving. It is personal patronage that creates relationship. God's grace implicates the recipient in a covenant of allegiance.
- **Faith is not mere assent.** It is loyal adherence, ongoing trust, open allegiance. The NT *pistis* is closer to "fidelity" than "belief."
- **Gratitude is ethics.** In this world, the ethical life of the Christian is response: *you belong to God now; live as one who has received an undeserved benefit from the supreme patron*.

The gospel revolutionized patronage by claiming God as the supreme, free-giving patron who incorporates sinners into his family. The ethics of discipleship are the ethics of grateful loyalty.
