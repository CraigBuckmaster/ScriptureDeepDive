The four canonical Gospels — Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John — are a distinctive genre. They are not news bulletins, straight biographies in the modern sense, mere sermons, or mythological narratives. They are a form of ancient biography (*bios*) shaped by the conviction that their subject's identity and actions have world-changing significance.

**Ancient biography.** Scholars including Richard Burridge (*What Are the Gospels?*) and Craig Keener have shown that the Gospels closely match the features of ancient Greco-Roman *bios* writings of authors like Plutarch, Suetonius, and Tacitus. Ancient biographies were selective rather than comprehensive, arranged topically as often as chronologically, and aimed to reveal the character and teaching of their subject for imitation or admiration.

The Gospels fit this genre with specific features:

- **Length similar to other ancient biographies** (short books — short scrolls).
- **Focus on a single figure** with other characters appearing in relation to him.
- **Anecdotal arrangement** — brief scenes, sayings, and miracle reports.
- **Concentrated attention to the subject's death** (typical of ancient biography; extreme in the Gospels).
- **Aim beyond information** — to shape the reader's allegiance.

**Distinctive features of the Gospels.**

- **Rooted in Jewish Scripture.** Each Gospel frames Jesus as the fulfillment of the Old Testament — Matthew through formula quotations, Mark through Isaiah's prologue, Luke through the song of Mary and the travel-to-Jerusalem structure, John through the new-creation "in the beginning" opening.
- **Proportionally enormous passion narratives.** All four Gospels devote roughly a third of their space to the last week of Jesus's life. This is unusual for ancient biography and reflects the theological conviction that the cross and resurrection are the climax of the story.
- **Unselfconscious inclusion of the miraculous.** The Gospels report healings, exorcisms, nature miracles, and the resurrection as part of ordinary narrative. This sets them apart from many ancient biographies while fitting the conventions of some (Philostratus's *Life of Apollonius*).
- **Anonymous composition.** Unlike most ancient biographies, the Gospels do not name their authors in the body of the text; the titles ("according to Matthew") came from early tradition.

**The four Gospels have distinct voices.**

- **Matthew** — Jesus as the Davidic Messiah, teacher of the new Torah, heir of Israel's promise.
- **Mark** — Jesus as the Suffering Servant, revealed slowly as the Son of God whose messianic secret unfolds through the cross.
- **Luke** — Jesus as Savior of all peoples, champion of the marginalized, Spirit-anointed servant.
- **John** — Jesus as the pre-existent Word, incarnate God, whose "I am" statements echo the divine name.

Reading the four together preserves the full portrait. Reading one at a time lets that Gospel's particular voice speak. Both are valuable.

**What the Gospel genre is not.**

The Gospels are not modern journalism demanding word-for-word quotation fidelity (ancient speeches were paraphrased, not recorded). They are not chronological biographies (events are often arranged topically). They are not simple histories indifferent to theological meaning. They are apostolic testimony shaped into a new literary form to proclaim Jesus Christ — not dispassionately but invitationally.

Read the Gospels as what they are: historically rooted, theologically driven biographical narratives that demand a response.
