title: How Do Ancient Near Eastern Creation Myths Compare to Genesis?

Genesis 1–2 did not appear in a literary vacuum. The Ancient Near East had a rich tradition of creation stories, and understanding them illuminates the distinctive claims of Israel's creation account. The comparisons reveal both striking parallels and radical differences.

**Enuma Elish** (Babylonian, c. 18th–12th century BCE in various forms). This Akkadian epic tells how the god Marduk defeats the primordial goddess Tiamat in cosmic combat, splits her corpse to form the heavens and the earth, and establishes the order of the Babylonian pantheon. Humans are created from the blood of the slain rebel god Kingu to be slaves relieving the gods of labor.

**Atrahasis** (Babylonian, c. 17th century BCE). A flood story embedded in a broader account of humanity's creation from clay mixed with the blood of a slain god. Humans are created to do the work the lesser gods refused, then nearly destroyed when their noise disturbs the gods' rest.

**Memphite Theology** (Egyptian, preserved on the Shabaka Stone c. 700 BCE but possibly representing earlier tradition). The god Ptah creates by thought in his heart and speech with his tongue — a remarkably close parallel to Genesis 1's "God said, and it was so."

**Gilgamesh Epic** (Babylonian). While primarily not a creation story, it includes the flood narrative that parallels Genesis 6–9.

**Egyptian creation accounts** vary by cult center. At Heliopolis, Atum creates from primeval waters; at Hermopolis, the Ogdoad of primeval deities precede; at Thebes, Amun is the hidden creator.

**What Genesis shares with ANE creation accounts:**

- A pre-creation state of watery chaos (Gen 1:2, *tohu wabohu* over the *tehom* — linguistically related to Tiamat).
- Creation as the ordering of chaos rather than purely *ex nihilo*.
- A pattern of forming and filling.
- Divine speech as a creative agent.
- Humans with a privileged role.
- A connection between creation and human social/religious life.

**What Genesis claims that ANE accounts do not:**

- **Radical monotheism.** There is one God, not a pantheon. The sun, moon, and stars are created objects, not deities — the Hebrew avoids even naming them by their standard Canaanite names (*sun* and *moon* become "the greater light" and "the lesser light").
- **Creation by word alone, not combat.** Genesis has no Chaoskampf — no battle between God and the waters. The deep is not a personal enemy but a neutral pre-creation state that God orders effortlessly.
- **Humans in the image of God.** Not fashioned as slaves from a rebel god's blood, but created as image-bearers commissioned to rule.
- **Dignity of the created order.** Matter is good — repeated seven times in Genesis 1. This is a startling assertion in a world where material was often denigrated or viewed as the detritus of divine conflict.
- **Sabbath as climax.** Creation peaks not in the creation of humans but in God's rest — an orientation of the whole cosmos toward worship and relationship.

**What this comparison is for.**

- Not to suggest Genesis was "borrowing." The relationship is more like conversation: Genesis speaks the language of its context while subverting that context's theology.
- Not to diminish Genesis. The ANE parallels make Genesis's distinctiveness visible.
- Not to flatten Genesis into myth. Genesis intends to speak of real origins, even if its genre is confessional-theological rather than modern-scientific.

Read Genesis knowing what else the Ancient Near East was saying. The polemic and the poetry both come sharper into focus.
