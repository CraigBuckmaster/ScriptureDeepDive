Matthew, Mark, and Luke are called the **Synoptic Gospels** (from Greek *synopsis*, "seeing together") because they largely tell the same story in the same order, often in identical or nearly identical language, while John tells a markedly different version with its own distinct themes. The **Synoptic Problem** is the scholarly label for the question: what is the literary relationship between these three Gospels?

A few statistics frame the problem. Mark has 661 verses, of which roughly 600 also appear in Matthew, and around 350 appear in Luke. Matthew and Luke share about 230 verses that are not in Mark — almost all of them sayings of Jesus. Much of the Markan material is reproduced in Matthew and Luke with minimal verbal change; some passages preserve identical Greek word-for-word.

This level of agreement cannot be explained by independent eyewitness memory. People remembering the same event produce similar but not identical accounts. **The Synoptic Gospels are literarily related.** The question is: how?

**Four main proposals have competed:**

**1. Markan priority (the most widely held solution).** Mark was written first. Matthew and Luke independently used Mark as a source, along with a hypothetical sayings source called **Q** (from German *Quelle*, "source") for the 230 shared sayings not in Mark. This is the **Two-Source Hypothesis**. A refinement, the **Four-Source Hypothesis**, adds Matthew-specific material (**M**) and Luke-specific material (**L**). The Two-Source hypothesis is the mainstream critical position, taught in most introductions.

**2. Matthean priority (the Griesbach or Two-Gospel Hypothesis).** Matthew was written first, Luke used Matthew, and Mark abbreviated both. This was the dominant view until the nineteenth century and still has defenders (William Farmer, the Institute for Biblical Research). Proponents point to the tradition that Matthew wrote first and to passages where Mark seems to conflate Matthew and Luke.

**3. The Farrer Hypothesis (Markan priority without Q).** Mark was first; Matthew used Mark; Luke used both Mark and Matthew. This dispenses with the hypothetical Q document — a parsimony that appeals to some (Michael Goulder, Mark Goodacre).

**4. The Augustinian Hypothesis.** Matthew was first, Mark abbreviated Matthew, and Luke used both. This was the view of Augustine and dominated the medieval church.

**Why the debate matters.** The Synoptic Problem is not a skeptic's puzzle but a question about how the Gospel texts came to us. Every serious Gospels commentator must take a position on it to explain why passages are worded this way rather than that. Knowing the scholarly landscape enables readers to weigh commentary claims about editorial intent.

**What the debate does not touch.** The historical reliability, divine inspiration, and theological truth of the Gospels do not depend on solving the Synoptic Problem. All the proposed solutions assume the Gospels are based on apostolic memory and careful composition. Whatever literary process produced them, they are the church's authoritative testimony to Jesus.
