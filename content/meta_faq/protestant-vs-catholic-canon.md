title: Why Do Protestant and Catholic Bibles Have Different Numbers of Books?

Protestant Bibles contain 39 Old Testament books; Catholic Bibles contain 46. Orthodox Bibles contain still more. The New Testament is identical across all three traditions at 27 books. The difference lies in which books of the Hellenistic Jewish world were received as Scripture by Christians.

Two ancient streams carried the Old Testament into the Christian era. The **Hebrew Bible**, preserved by Jewish scribes in the Masoretic tradition, contained the books known today as the Protestant OT — no more, no less. The **Septuagint (LXX)**, the Greek translation begun in Alexandria around the third century BCE, included those same books **plus** several others — Tobit, Judith, Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach, Baruch, 1 and 2 Maccabees, and expansions of Esther and Daniel. These additional books had been written or preserved in Greek by Hellenistic Jews; most were not included in the rabbinic canon that crystallized after the fall of the Second Temple.

Early Christian communities used the Septuagint. The New Testament writers quote it far more often than the Hebrew. As Christianity spread through the Greek-speaking world, the Greek Bible — including the extra books — became the default. Jerome (c. 400 CE), translating the Latin Vulgate, argued for the shorter Hebrew canon and labelled the extras "Apocrypha" (meaning "hidden" or "obscure"). Augustine pushed back: these books had been read in the churches for centuries; the African councils at Hippo and Carthage listed them among the Scriptures. Rome followed Augustine, and the broader canon became the medieval standard.

The **Reformers** returned to Jerome's position. Luther printed the disputed books in a separate section between the testaments, calling them "useful and good to read" but not on par with Scripture. The **Westminster Confession (1646)** stated that the Apocrypha had no divine authority. The **Council of Trent (1546)**, responding to the Reformation, formally affirmed the longer canon for Catholics, declaring the deuterocanonical books ("second canon") to be fully inspired Scripture.

**Eastern Orthodoxy** includes still more books — 3 and 4 Maccabees, Psalm 151, the Prayer of Manasseh, 1 Esdras — with some variation by region.

The NT is untouched by this debate. All three traditions agree on the same 27 books. Disputes about the shape of the biblical library concern the books the apostles read, not the books the apostles wrote.

The practical upshot: when Protestants, Catholics, and Orthodox read "the Bible" together, they are reading substantially the same book, with a handful of additional writings preserved in some traditions that the other traditions consider valuable historical documents but not Scripture. Serious conversation across these lines is easier than the numbers suggest.
