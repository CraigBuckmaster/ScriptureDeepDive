Scholars of the historical Jesus often divide the modern quest into three distinct periods, each with its own methods and characteristic assumptions. Knowing the quests helps readers understand why a book written in 1900 and a book written in 2000 about "the historical Jesus" can look so different.

**The First Quest (c. 1778 – 1906).** The Enlightenment's historicist turn reached biblical studies through **Hermann Samuel Reimarus**, whose posthumous *Fragments* (1778) depicted Jesus as a failed Jewish revolutionary whose disciples stole the body and invented the resurrection. The quest proceeded through the nineteenth century with figures like **D. F. Strauss** (*The Life of Jesus Critically Examined*, 1835), who treated the miracles as myths, and **Ernest Renan** (*Vie de Jésus*, 1863), who gave a Romantic liberal-Protestant Jesus. The quest ended with **Albert Schweitzer's** devastating survey *The Quest of the Historical Jesus* (1906), which showed that each reconstructed Jesus bore an uncanny resemblance to the scholar who reconstructed him. Schweitzer himself offered a Jesus who was a mistaken apocalyptic prophet — deeply strange and foreign to modern sensibilities, but historically serious.

**The No Quest Period (c. 1906 – 1953).** Rudolf Bultmann and his students largely abandoned the quest as futile and theologically misguided. Bultmann argued that the historical Jesus was both inaccessible through the faith-colored Gospel traditions and irrelevant to Christian proclamation, which addresses the risen Christ encountered in the kerygma. What mattered was not the *Jesus of history* but the *Christ of faith*. This half-century was dominated by **form criticism** (analyzing the oral-tradition units behind the Gospels) and existential theology.

**The New Quest (c. 1953 – 1970s).** Bultmann's students pushed back. **Ernst Käsemann's** 1953 lecture "The Problem of the Historical Jesus" argued that continuity between Jesus and the early church's Christ was theologically necessary: without it, Christianity becomes mere mythology. The New Quest developed **criteria of authenticity** — especially **dissimilarity** (a saying is probably authentic if unlike both contemporary Judaism and later Christianity) and **coherence** (a saying is probably authentic if it fits the pattern established by dissimilarity-passing sayings). Leaders included **Günther Bornkamm**, **James Robinson**, and **Norman Perrin**. The results were modest — a minimalist Jesus, but a recognizable one.

**The Third Quest (late 1970s – present).** The Third Quest rejected the assumption that a "real Jesus" must be different from his Jewish context. Drawing on the Dead Sea Scrolls, archaeology, and newly intensive study of Second Temple Judaism, Third Questers sought a Jesus who was **deeply and recognizably Jewish**. Leading voices:
- **E. P. Sanders** — Jesus as restoration prophet of Israel (*Jesus and Judaism*, 1985).
- **N. T. Wright** — Jesus as the climax of Israel's story (*Jesus and the Victory of God*, 1996).
- **John P. Meier** — meticulous historical judgment across five volumes of *A Marginal Jew*.
- **Dale Allison** — Jesus as apocalyptic prophet with continuities to later Christian faith.
- **Craig Keener** — Gospel historicity in light of ancient biographies.

The Third Quest remains active. It is the most promising era of historical Jesus research, because it reads Jesus within — not against — his Jewish world.
