Historical Jesus scholars have developed various **criteria of authenticity** — rules of thumb for judging whether a particular saying or event in the Gospels goes back to the historical Jesus. No criterion is decisive by itself; careful scholars triangulate several.

**Criterion of multiple attestation.** A tradition appearing in independent sources is likelier to be historical than one attested only in a single stream. A saying found in Mark, Q, John, and (say) Paul has stronger historical claim than one found only in Matthew. On this criterion, the feeding of the multitude (in all four Gospels), the institution of the Lord's Supper (Mark, Paul, independently), and Jesus's baptism by John (Mark, Q, John) are especially well-attested.

**Criterion of dissimilarity.** A saying is likely historical if it differs from both contemporary Judaism (so Jesus did not merely parrot Jewish tradition) and the early church (so the church did not invent it). The paradigmatic example is Jesus's forbidding oaths (Matt 5:34) — dissimilar to Jewish tradition, awkward for a church that took oaths. This criterion is useful but limited: it overshoots by excluding things Jesus shared with his Jewish context (which he obviously did) and things the church continued (which is much of the Gospel content).

**Criterion of embarrassment.** A tradition likely to have embarrassed the early church is unlikely to have been invented. Jesus's baptism by John (placing John in a seemingly superior role), his crucifixion (a cursed death under Roman law), his cry of dereliction from the cross ("My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?") — all embarrassing for later Christians to explain. The fact that these traditions survive suggests they go back to Jesus.

**Criterion of coherence.** A saying or event coheres with the pattern already established by passing other criteria. This is a consolidation criterion, not an independent one.

**Criterion of Aramaic retroversion.** Sayings that retro-translate naturally into Aramaic (Jesus's native tongue) — especially those preserving poetic parallelism or untranslated Aramaic words (*abba*, *talitha cumi*) — may more plausibly go back to Jesus than sayings that work only in Greek.

**Criterion of Palestinian-environmental fit.** Sayings that assume specifically Palestinian conditions (agriculture, social stratification, Galilean lake ecology, Jerusalem geography) are more likely to go back to Jesus than those at home only in later Hellenistic or Diaspora contexts.

**Criterion of rejection and execution.** Jesus was executed by Roman authorities as a political threat. Any reconstruction of Jesus must explain **why** he was killed. Sayings and actions consonant with the kind of figure Rome would crucify gain in historical plausibility.

**Critiques.** No criterion is watertight. Dissimilarity excludes too much (Jesus was Jewish); multiple attestation cannot verify sayings uniquely preserved in one source (which the canonical Gospels frequently are); the burden of proof in all these criteria has been much debated. Third Quest scholars increasingly argue for a more **holistic** approach: does this saying fit the whole pattern of a plausibly Jewish, Second Temple, crucified prophet of God's kingdom?

The criteria are tools, not magic wands. Like all historical tools, they depend on the wisdom of the scholar using them.
