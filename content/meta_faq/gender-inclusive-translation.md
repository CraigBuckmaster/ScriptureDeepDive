One of the sharper-edged debates in modern Bible translation is when and how to render Greek and Hebrew terms for "man," "son," "brother," and "mankind" in English. The question is not primarily theological but **linguistic and sociolinguistic**: which English word best captures what the original communicated?

The relevant pattern: classical Greek, biblical Hebrew, and older English all used masculine words with generic force. Greek *anthrōpos* and *adelphoi*, Hebrew *ish* and *ʾadam*, and older English "man" and "brothers" could refer to **all humans** regardless of sex when the context demanded. The default of older English matched the default of the biblical languages, and "man" in "mankind" meant "humanity."

English has shifted. In contemporary usage, "man" increasingly means "male person" unless specifically marked otherwise. "Brothers" often sounds male-exclusive even where the author clearly addressed mixed communities (Paul to the *adelphoi* in the Corinthian church certainly included women). "Whoever" is a more natural generic than "he" in most contemporary English.

Modern translations handle this shift differently.

- **NIV 2011**, **NRSVue**, **CEB**, **NLT** translate *adelphoi* as "brothers and sisters" when the context makes mixed address obvious, use "humanity" where *anthrōpos* is generic, and prefer "they" or "whoever" where older translations used generic "he." They do **not** change language for God, Christ, or the persons of the Trinity.
- **ESV**, **NASB**, **CSB**, **KJV/NKJV** retain more of the older conventions, preserving "brothers," "man," and generic "he" in most cases — though the ESV does translate *adelphoi* as "brothers [and sisters]" in many footnotes.

**What these translations do NOT do:**

- They do not change the biblical text's references to specific male individuals (Jesus is still "he," Paul is still "he," Abraham is still "he").
- They do not change the gendered language of the persons of the Trinity (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit retain their biblical forms; Christ is "he").
- They do not alter texts about male headship, the role of women in the church, or specific gendered language that the text intentionally uses.

The debate is therefore a translation-philosophy question, not a doctrinal one: should a translator render a generic ancient term with a generic modern term that reads accurately to contemporary ears, or with a traditional rendering that preserves linguistic continuity with older English?

**Practical advice:**

Read the preface to your translation. Every serious modern translation explains its gender-language policy, usually in the first five pages. Translators who make changes generally do so conservatively and explain the principles. Understanding those principles quiets most of the anxiety in popular conversation about this topic.

The goal of every faithful translation is the same: to help the reader hear what the original said to the original audience, in words that land the way the original words landed.
