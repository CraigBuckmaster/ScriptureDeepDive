Roughly a third of the Old Testament is poetry — Psalms, Proverbs, Job, Song of Solomon, Lamentations, large stretches of the prophets, and scattered poems across the Torah and historical books. Hebrew poetry differs significantly from English verse, and learning its patterns transforms reading.

**Parallelism** is the signature feature of Hebrew poetry. The poet states an idea in one line and then restates, develops, or completes it in the next. Robert Lowth's classic analysis (1753) identified three main forms:

- **Synonymous parallelism.** The second line restates the first in different words.
    *"The heavens declare the glory of God; / the skies proclaim the work of his hands."* (Ps 19:1)
- **Antithetic parallelism.** The second line states the opposite of the first.
    *"The memory of the righteous is a blessing, / but the name of the wicked will rot."* (Prov 10:7)
- **Synthetic parallelism.** The second line develops, completes, or intensifies the first.
    *"Blessed is the man who does not walk in the counsel of the wicked / or stand in the way of sinners / or sit in the seat of mockers."* (Ps 1:1)

Modern analysis (especially James Kugel, Robert Alter) has refined this: the second line nearly always **intensifies** or **sharpens** rather than merely restating. The principle Kugel called "A, and what's more, B" — the second line is not mere echo but advance.

**Imagery.** Hebrew poetry leans heavily on **concrete metaphor** drawn from shepherding, farming, warfare, family, and courtroom. Abstract theology is rendered in sensory terms. God is a rock, a fortress, a shepherd, a hen gathering chicks, a warrior, a judge, a midwife, a devouring fire. Learn to linger on the images — a Hebrew poet chose them carefully.

**Compactness.** Hebrew poetry uses far fewer words than English prose. A typical line is three to four words. Every word is weighted. Translations inevitably pad this out, but the compression is a feature: what the poet chose to include is essential.

**Formal structures.** Some Hebrew poems display complex structures that are easy to miss in translation:

- **Acrostics** — poems where each line or section begins with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet (Psalm 119, Lamentations 1–4).
- **Chiasms** — ABC...CBA symmetries where the center is the focus.
- **Inclusio** — a poem begins and ends with the same word or phrase, framing the whole (Psalm 8: "O LORD, our Lord, how majestic is your name in all the earth").
- **Strophes / stanzas** — thematic units signaled by refrains ("How long, O LORD?" in Psalm 13).

**Genres of Hebrew poetry.** Within the category of poetry, biblical poems fall into distinct sub-genres:

- **Hymns of praise** (Ps 8, 103, 150).
- **Laments** — individual or communal (Ps 22, 44, Lamentations).
- **Thanksgivings** (Ps 30, 116).
- **Wisdom poems** (Ps 1, 37, Proverbs).
- **Royal psalms** (Ps 2, 45, 110).
- **Liturgical psalms** (Ps 24, 118).
- **Prophetic oracles** (much of Isaiah, Jeremiah, the Twelve).

Read Hebrew poetry slowly. Read aloud. Notice the parallelism. Savor the images. Expect the second line to advance, not merely repeat. Poetry is the language of the heart's response to God; it cannot be skimmed.
