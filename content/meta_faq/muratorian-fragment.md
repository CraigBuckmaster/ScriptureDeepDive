The Muratorian Fragment is a damaged Latin manuscript, discovered in the Biblioteca Ambrosiana by Ludovico Antonio Muratori and published in 1740, which preserves the earliest known list of books received as Scripture in one segment of the early church.

The document is fragmentary: it begins mid-sentence, omitting the start (probably a discussion of Matthew and Mark), and ends abruptly. What survives includes Luke (called "the third book of the Gospel"), John, Acts, thirteen Pauline letters, Jude, two letters of John, the Wisdom of Solomon, and the Apocalypse of John. It mentions an Apocalypse of Peter as accepted by some but not all, and it explicitly rejects several works as unsuitable for public reading, including writings associated with Marcion, the Gnostic Valentinus, the Montanists, and a forged letter to the Laodiceans.

Most scholars date the fragment to the late second century (c. 170–200 CE), making it roughly contemporary with Irenaeus of Lyons. A minority view pushes the date to the fourth century, treating the fragment as a later summary of earlier tradition. If the earlier date is correct, the fragment is remarkable evidence that the broad shape of the New Testament was already clear within roughly a century of its composition — the four Gospels, the Pauline collection, Acts, most Catholic Epistles, and Revelation.

Noteworthy features:

- **Wisdom of Solomon** appears in the list of Christian writings, attributed (curiously) to Philo. This probably reflects the Greek Bible's habit of including Wisdom alongside Solomon's canonical books.
- **The Shepherd of Hermas** is mentioned as valuable but not suitable for public reading alongside the prophets and apostles, because it was written "very recently, in our own times" by Hermas, brother of Pius, bishop of Rome. This detail has been key to dating the fragment.
- **Hebrews, James, 1 and 2 Peter** are **not mentioned** — either because the text omits them or because they were not yet received in the community that produced the list. This does not mean these books were rejected, only that their canonical status was still settling in the second century.
- **The explicit rejection of Marcion and the Gnostics** shows the list is a pastoral document, not a historical survey. It is telling congregations what to read and what to avoid.

The Muratorian Fragment is important not because it is the final word on the canon — it isn't — but because it gives us a window into the canon-recognizing process roughly 120 years after the completion of the New Testament. Already, most of the eventual 27 books are in place, and the criteria of apostolicity, orthodoxy, and universal use are being applied. The fragment reminds us that canon recognition was gradual, communal, and pastoral rather than sudden, conciliar, and administrative.
