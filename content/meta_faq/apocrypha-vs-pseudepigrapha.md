The terms **Apocrypha** and **Pseudepigrapha** often get confused because they describe overlapping bodies of ancient Jewish and Christian literature outside the canon. The distinction is worth learning because it affects how you read the biblical text and its cultural surround.

**Apocrypha** in Protestant usage refers to a specific set of Jewish religious writings from roughly 300 BCE to 100 CE that were included in the Greek Septuagint and the Latin Vulgate but excluded from the Hebrew canon. Catholic and Orthodox Christians call these books **deuterocanonical** ("second canon") and regard them as Scripture. Protestant Bibles of the Reformation era often printed them as a separate section — valuable but not inspired. Today most Protestant publishers omit them, though ecumenical editions (NRSVue, CEB) include them.

The books traditionally called Apocrypha include: Tobit, Judith, Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach, Baruch, 1 and 2 Maccabees, additions to Esther and Daniel, the Prayer of Manasseh, 1–2 Esdras, and 3–4 Maccabees (in some traditions).

**Pseudepigrapha** ("falsely inscribed") are Jewish and Christian writings from the same general period and somewhat later that are **attributed to earlier biblical figures** — Enoch, Abraham, Moses, Isaiah, Ezra — but were almost certainly written centuries after those figures lived. These books were never accepted as Scripture by any major Christian or Jewish tradition (with the notable exception of **1 Enoch** and **Jubilees** in Ethiopian Orthodoxy). They are nonetheless invaluable for understanding the religious milieu of the New Testament.

Important pseudepigraphical works include:

- **1 Enoch** — a large apocalyptic work attributed to Enoch (Gen 5). Its section on the "Watchers" shaped ideas about fallen angels and demons. **Jude 14–15 quotes 1 Enoch directly**, describing its prophecy about the coming of the Lord with his holy ones.
- **Jubilees** — a retelling of Genesis and early Exodus with a 364-day solar calendar, found at Qumran.
- **4 Ezra (2 Esdras 3–14)** — a post-70-CE apocalyptic work wrestling with the destruction of Jerusalem.
- **Testaments of the Twelve Patriarchs** — moral-spiritual farewell speeches attributed to Jacob's sons.
- **Psalms of Solomon** — 18 psalms from the 1st century BCE including a messianic hymn (Ps Sol 17) that influenced NT Christology.
- **Sibylline Oracles** — Jewish and Christian prophecies cast in the voice of a pagan sibyl.
- **Apocalypse of Abraham**, **Testament of Moses**, **Lives of the Prophets**, **Joseph and Aseneth** — and many others.

**How to tell them apart in practice:** If the book is in your Catholic or Orthodox Bible but not your Protestant one, it's deuterocanon / Apocrypha. If it claims to be written by a biblical figure but was clearly composed centuries later, it's pseudepigrapha. The early church and rabbinic Judaism mostly excluded both categories from Scripture, though the categories were not uniformly policed.

Reading beyond the canon in both directions enriches one's sense of the world in which the biblical books were written and received.
