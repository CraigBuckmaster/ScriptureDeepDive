title: What Are the Main Greek Manuscript Witnesses to the New Testament?

The New Testament is better-attested than any other document from antiquity. Over **5,800 Greek manuscripts** survive in whole or in part, ranging from tiny papyrus fragments to complete Bibles on vellum. Textual critics group these witnesses by age, material, and script.

**Papyri (𝔓).** The oldest New Testament manuscripts are papyrus fragments. Roughly 140 papyri have been catalogued, dating from the early second century through the eighth. The most important include:

- **𝔓52 (Rylands Papyrus):** a postcard-sized fragment of John 18, dated c. 125 CE — the earliest known piece of the NT, within a generation of the Gospel's composition.
- **𝔓46:** a nearly complete codex of Paul's letters, c. 200 CE, including Hebrews.
- **𝔓66** and **𝔓75:** nearly complete copies of John, c. 200 CE.
- **𝔓45:** substantial portions of the Gospels and Acts, mid-third century.

**Majuscule (uncial) codices.** Written in all-capital Greek letters on parchment or vellum. Around 320 survive. The four great "old uncials" are:

- **Codex Sinaiticus (ℵ, fourth century):** the oldest complete New Testament, discovered at St. Catherine's Monastery on Sinai by Constantin von Tischendorf in the 1850s.
- **Codex Vaticanus (B, fourth century):** almost as old and arguably even better preserved; long kept in the Vatican Library.
- **Codex Alexandrinus (A, fifth century):** once held by the Patriarch of Alexandria, now in the British Library.
- **Codex Bezae (D, fifth century):** a bilingual Greek-Latin manuscript with a distinctive text-type, especially in Acts.

**Minuscule manuscripts.** Written in a cursive script that came into use around the ninth century. Around 2,900 survive; most reflect the later Byzantine text tradition.

**Lectionaries.** About 2,400 manuscripts arranged for reading in church services across the year.

**Versions.** Beyond Greek, the NT was translated early into **Latin** (Old Latin, then Jerome's Vulgate), **Syriac** (Peshitta), **Coptic** (in several dialects), **Armenian**, **Georgian**, **Gothic**, and **Ethiopic**. These versions preserve early textual traditions and are invaluable for textual criticism.

**Patristic citations.** The church fathers quoted the NT so extensively that much of the text could be reconstructed from their writings alone.

Scholars group these witnesses into **text-types** reflecting distinct transmission streams:

- **Alexandrian** (𝔓46, 𝔓75, ℵ, B) — widely considered the earliest and least altered.
- **Western** (D, Old Latin) — marked by expansions and harmonizations.
- **Byzantine / Majority** — the later, dominant stream that underlies the *Textus Receptus* and the KJV.
- **Caesarean** — a proposed regional type, less clearly defined.

Modern critical editions (Nestle-Aland 28, UBS 5) weigh these witnesses to reconstruct the earliest recoverable text. The result is a New Testament whose substance is virtually undisputed, with textual footnotes primarily about minor verbal variation.
