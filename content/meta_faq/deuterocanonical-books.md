The **deuterocanonical books** are the seven (or more, depending on count) Old Testament books included in Catholic and Orthodox Bibles but not in Protestant Bibles. The term means "second canon," a designation coined in the sixteenth century to distinguish these books from the protocanonical ("first canon") books shared by all traditions. Catholics consider them fully Scripture; Protestants call them Apocrypha and regard them as useful but not inspired.

The seven deuterocanonical books of the Catholic canon are:

- **Tobit** — a moral tale about a righteous Jew in Assyrian exile whose blind father sends him on a journey under angelic protection. Themes of providence, prayer, and filial piety.
- **Judith** — a narrative of a beautiful Jewish widow who infiltrates the Assyrian camp and beheads the enemy general Holofernes. A celebrated figure in Christian art.
- **Wisdom of Solomon** — a Hellenistic Jewish philosophical-religious text, written in Greek probably in Alexandria (1st century BCE). Deeply influenced early Christian theology.
- **Sirach (Ecclesiasticus)** — a long wisdom book by Yeshua ben Sira (c. 180 BCE), translated from Hebrew to Greek by his grandson. One of the richest collections of ancient Jewish wisdom literature.
- **Baruch** — a short prophetic-liturgical work attributed to Jeremiah's scribe, including a reflective prayer and exhortation to return to the Law.
- **1 Maccabees** — the primary historical account of the Jewish revolt under Judas Maccabeus and the Hasmoneans against the Seleucid persecution (167–134 BCE). Essential for understanding the intertestamental period.
- **2 Maccabees** — a theologically rich but more rhetorical history of the same era, emphasizing resurrection, prayer for the dead, and intercession by saints.

In addition, Catholic Bibles include **additions to Esther** (Greek expansions making the book more explicitly religious) and **additions to Daniel** (the Prayer of Azariah, the Song of the Three Young Men, Susanna, and Bel and the Dragon). Orthodox Bibles include still more (1 Esdras, 3 Maccabees, 4 Maccabees, Psalm 151, the Prayer of Manasseh), though there is variation across Orthodox traditions.

**Why the disagreement?** These books were part of the Greek Septuagint but not of the later rabbinic Hebrew canon. Christian tradition largely preserved them; Jerome, translating the Latin Vulgate, introduced a category called "ecclesiastical" books. The **Reformers** followed Jerome, printing these books in a separate section marked "useful, not canonical." The **Council of Trent (1546)** responded by definitively affirming them as canonical for Catholics.

**Doctrinal weight.** Several distinctively Catholic doctrines find support in the deuterocanon: prayer for the dead (2 Macc 12), intercession of saints (2 Macc 15), purgatory (read into 2 Macc 12), and almsgiving as atonement (Tob 12). Protestants, accepting these books only as historical documents, find the doctrinal basis elsewhere or do not hold them.

Reading the deuterocanon is valuable regardless of one's view on canonicity. These books illuminate the world between the testaments, the religious imagination of Second Temple Judaism, and the literary context of the New Testament.
