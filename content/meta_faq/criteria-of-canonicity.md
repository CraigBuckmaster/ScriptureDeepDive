title: What Criteria Did the Early Church Use to Recognize Scripture?
tags: [canon, history, hermeneutics]

The early church did not **invent** the New Testament canon; it **recognized** the books that had already proved themselves in the life of the churches. Three criteria, applied with pastoral wisdom over generations, shaped that recognition.

**Apostolicity.** A canonical book had to trace back to an apostle or an apostolic companion. Matthew and John were apostles; Mark was Peter's associate (per Papias, c. 125 CE); Luke travelled with Paul (see the "we" passages in Acts). Paul's letters were obviously apostolic; the letters of James and Jude came from brothers of the Lord who had become pillars of the Jerusalem church. Hebrews was included despite uncertainty about its human author because its content was judged apostolic in character.

**Orthodoxy.** A book had to be consistent with the **rule of faith** — the core apostolic teaching passed down through the churches about the God of Israel, the incarnation, death, and resurrection of Jesus, and the hope of his return. Books that contradicted this rule (Gnostic Gospels of Thomas, Judas, and Philip; docetic works that denied Jesus's humanity; later Valentinian texts) were rejected as representing a different religion, not a different emphasis.

**Catholicity (universal use).** A canonical book had to be widely received by churches across the Roman world, not merely favored in one region or by one party. This is why some early works that enjoyed local popularity — the *Shepherd of Hermas* in Rome, the *Gospel of the Hebrews* among some Jewish Christians — were not ultimately included. The book had to speak to the whole church.

The criteria interlocked. An apostolic pedigree without orthodox content was suspect (some heretical groups claimed spurious apostolic authorship). Orthodox content from an unknown source was not enough without demonstrable apostolic connection. Universal use without content was impossible — what was read everywhere was read because of what it said.

Some books satisfied the criteria obviously and were never seriously questioned (four Gospels, thirteen Paulines, 1 Peter, 1 John). Others were debated for centuries before their value was fully recognized (Hebrews, James, 2 Peter, 2–3 John, Jude, Revelation). The debates strengthened the canon rather than undermining it: the same books kept being proposed, the same books kept being tested, and the same books kept being received.

For the Old Testament the criteria differed — prophetic source and reception within the Jewish community mattered most — but the underlying pattern is similar: the Spirit of God produced the books, and the Spirit of God guided the people of God to recognize them.

Canon is not a club with a gatekeeper. It is a family the church came to know.
