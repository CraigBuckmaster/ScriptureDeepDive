Noah's flood (Genesis 6–9) is not the only ancient flood story. The Ancient Near East preserved multiple flood traditions, some of which are strikingly close to Genesis in detail. The parallels have been known to scholarship since George Smith's 1872 decipherment of the Gilgamesh flood tablet.

**The most important ANE flood accounts:**

**Gilgamesh Epic Tablet XI** (Babylonian, early 2nd millennium BCE, drawing on still earlier sources). Utnapishtim tells Gilgamesh how the gods decided to destroy humanity because their noise disturbed the gods. One god (Ea) warned Utnapishtim to build a vast boat. He loaded his family, craftsmen, and animals; the flood came; the boat rested on Mt. Nimush; he released three birds to test for dry ground (dove, swallow, raven — compare Gen 8:7–12); he sacrificed, and the gods gathered "like flies" to the sweet smell.

**Atrahasis Epic** (Babylonian, 17th century BCE). The fuller context of the flood: the gods create humans to do the labor they refused; humans multiply and their noise disturbs the gods; drought and plague fail to curb them; the gods decide on a flood; Enki warns Atrahasis ("exceedingly wise"); he builds a boat and survives. Afterward the gods institute natural checks on human fertility.

**Sumerian Flood Story (Eridu Genesis)** (third millennium BCE or earlier). A fragmentary tablet telling how Ziusudra, king of Shuruppak, survived a flood decreed by the gods.

**Close parallels to Genesis:**

- A divine decision to destroy humanity through a flood.
- A righteous individual warned in advance.
- Instructions to build a large wooden boat.
- Inclusion of family members and representative animals.
- A specific mountain as the boat's landing place.
- The release of birds to test for dry land.
- Post-flood sacrifice that the deity finds pleasing.
- A divine commitment not to destroy humanity this way again.

**Profound differences:**

- **Why the flood.** In Babylonian accounts, humans are too noisy and disturb the gods' rest — a capricious divine irritation. In Genesis, God sends the flood in moral judgment against universal corruption and violence.
- **Monotheism.** Genesis has one God acting in consistent moral judgment and mercy; the ANE accounts feature gods in disagreement (one warns while others destroy) and conflict.
- **Moral purpose.** Noah is righteous (Gen 6:9); his salvation is consistent with God's character. Utnapishtim is saved by divine favoritism, not by righteousness.
- **The aftermath.** In Genesis, God establishes a covenant, renews the creation mandate, and places the rainbow as a sign of restraint. In Babylonian accounts, the gods grieve their impulsiveness and recoil from their own destructiveness ("We were sorry we sent the flood").
- **Sacrifice.** Babylonian gods come "like flies" to the food, famished because humans had stopped feeding them. Yahweh smells the aroma and responds with covenant promise.

**What the comparison teaches.**

- **Memory of a real event.** Widespread ancient memory of a catastrophic flood suggests a historical disaster so traumatic that civilizations around the Near East preserved it in story.
- **Theological reshaping.** Genesis takes the shared memory and tells it as an act of the one righteous Creator, establishing moral order rather than capricious divine frustration.
- **Polemic.** By telling the story this way, Israel proclaims her distinctive theology against the surrounding worldviews.

Genesis is neither a Hebrew copy of Gilgamesh nor an isolated invention. It is Israel's retelling of a shared memory in the light of her covenant faith — a theological claim about what a flood is and who God is.
