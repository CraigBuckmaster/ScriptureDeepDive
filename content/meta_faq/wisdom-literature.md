**Wisdom literature** is a distinctive genre within the Old Testament, represented canonically by **Proverbs**, **Job**, and **Ecclesiastes**, with **Song of Solomon** and certain psalms (1, 37, 49, 73, 112, 119) sharing wisdom features. The deuterocanonical **Wisdom of Solomon** and **Sirach** belong to this tradition as well.

Wisdom literature's distinctive move is to consider the **structure of creation and human experience** as places where God's character can be discerned. Rather than appealing to covenant history (as the Torah does) or prophetic oracle (as Isaiah or Jeremiah does), wisdom writers observe how the world works: how ants store food (Prov 6:6), how the sluggard's garden decays (Prov 24:30), how suffering falls on the innocent (Job), how mortality levels the rich and poor (Ecclesiastes). They ask: what does reality teach about God and about how to live?

**Shared features.**

- **Fear of the LORD** as the beginning of wisdom (Prov 1:7; Job 28:28; Eccl 12:13).
- **Observational language** rather than revelatory formula. No "thus says the LORD."
- **Two-way structure** — paths of wisdom and folly, blessing and curse, life and death.
- **International context.** Ancient Near Eastern wisdom traditions (Egyptian, Babylonian, Edomite) parallel the biblical material. Proverbs 22–24 adapts Egyptian wisdom (the *Instruction of Amenemope*); Job is set in Uz, outside Israel. Wisdom is a conversation across cultures.

**The three canonical books each play a distinct role.**

**Proverbs** collects the "default rule" of wisdom: righteousness brings blessing, folly brings ruin, the fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge. Proverbs' sayings are **probabilistic generalizations** — how the world tends to work — not contractual guarantees. Proverbs 22:6 ("train up a child in the way he should go") is not a promise; it is wise counsel that more often than not is borne out.

**Job** confronts the limits of Proverbs' worldview. Job is the most righteous man on earth, and yet catastrophic suffering befalls him. His friends apply Proverbs-style logic and torment him with conclusions that must be wrong. Job resists, demanding an audience with God. When God finally speaks, he does not explain; he enlarges the picture with a tour of creation's vastness and mystery. Job is a **theodicy book** that refuses easy theodicy.

**Ecclesiastes** (Qoheleth) takes the observational stance to its limit. Under the sun — apart from revelation beyond the grave — everything is *hebel*, vapor, breath, fleeting. The preacher wrestles with mortality, injustice, and the futility of endless striving. His final counsel is not pessimism but chastened gratitude: *"Go, eat your bread with joy and drink your wine with a merry heart, for God has already approved what you do"* (9:7).

**How to read wisdom.** Proverbs gives you the general rules. Job breaks the rules to show that God is larger than our systems. Ecclesiastes tests the rules against mortality. Reading all three together is far more illuminating than reading any in isolation. Wisdom is not a simple system; it is an invitation to disciplined, reverent attention to the world God made.
