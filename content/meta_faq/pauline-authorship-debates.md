Of the thirteen letters traditionally ascribed to Paul in the New Testament, modern critical scholarship divides them into three tiers of confidence in Pauline authorship.

**The Undisputed Seven.** Virtually all scholars accept these as Paul's own composition: **Romans, 1 Corinthians, 2 Corinthians, Galatians, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, Philemon**. These letters share a consistent vocabulary, literary style, theological emphases, and historical situation. They form the core of the Pauline corpus on which almost all modern Pauline theology is built.

**The Disputed.** Three letters are debated but often accepted: **2 Thessalonians, Colossians, Ephesians**. Arguments against Pauline authorship include differences in vocabulary and style from the undisputed seven, the appearance of more developed ecclesiology and Christology, and the resemblance of Ephesians to Colossians. Arguments for Pauline authorship include personal greetings matching known Pauline associates, consistent theology when allowed to develop over time, and the early, unanimous attribution to Paul in church tradition.

**The Pastorals.** Three letters — **1 Timothy, 2 Timothy, Titus** — form a distinct group widely disputed in modern critical scholarship. Arguments against Pauline authorship: substantially different vocabulary (over one-third of the words in 1 Timothy do not appear in the undisputed Paulines), more developed church-order concerns (episcopal bishops, deacons, age-qualified widows), and difficulty fitting the implied itinerary into Acts. Arguments for Pauline authorship: early, unanimous tradition; the plausibility of post-Acts movement if Paul was released after his first Roman imprisonment; and the genuine personal notes (2 Tim 4:13, "bring the cloak I left at Troas, and the books").

**What "disputed" does and does not mean.** In critical parlance, "pseudonymous" refers to a writer composing in the name of a revered predecessor — a practice with ancient precedent both inside and outside Christianity. Pseudonymous writers might be disciples preserving their teacher's thought. Defenders of Pauline authorship counter that the specific personal details in these letters (people, places, requests) are not consistent with a later disciple's composition and would amount to deception.

**Evangelical responses vary.** Many conservative evangelical scholars defend Pauline authorship of all thirteen, arguing that the stylistic and theological differences are within the range of a single author writing to different audiences with different scribes across different decades. They note that secretarial (amanuensis) latitude can account for significant stylistic variation (Rom 16:22 explicitly names Tertius as Paul's scribe for Romans). Other evangelicals accept the disputed and pastoral letters as genuinely authoritative Scripture regardless of the precise mechanism of their composition, viewing them as faithfully preserving Pauline teaching.

**Practical implications.** The authorship question affects how scholars reconstruct Pauline theology chronologically — whether concepts in Ephesians or the Pastorals are "late Paul" or post-Pauline. It does not affect the canonical authority of these letters for the church, which received them all as Scripture. Readers can benefit from knowing the debate exists without needing to resolve it to read the letters fruitfully.

Reading Ephesians as Paul's mature reflection on the cosmic church, or as a student's faithful extension of Pauline thought, both preserve its message. The canonical form is what the church received.
