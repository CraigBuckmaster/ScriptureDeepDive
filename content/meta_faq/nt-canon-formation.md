title: How Was the New Testament Canon Formed?

The New Testament canon emerged through a centuries-long process of recognition, not a single conciliar decree. Early churches read apostolic letters and Gospels aloud in worship; the books that endured the longest and travelled the furthest became those the whole church received as Scripture.

Within the New Testament itself we glimpse the earliest stage. Paul asks the Colossians to exchange their letter with Laodicea (Col 4:16); Peter refers to "all Paul's letters" and places them alongside "the other scriptures" (2 Pet 3:15–16). By the early second century, writers like Clement of Rome, Ignatius, and Polycarp quote or allude to most of the New Testament — treating these texts as authoritative well before any formal list existed.

The first attested canon list is the **Muratorian Fragment** (likely late second century, though some date it to the fourth). It includes the four Gospels, Acts, thirteen Pauline letters, Jude, two Johannine letters, and Revelation — most of the NT we know. **Irenaeus** (c. 180 CE) famously defended exactly four Gospels as the church's received collection, no more and no fewer. **Origen** (early third century) categorized books as recognized (*homologoumena*), disputed (*antilegomena*), or spurious.

Three criteria guided recognition:

- **Apostolic origin:** authored by an apostle or an apostolic associate (Mark with Peter, Luke with Paul).
- **Orthodoxy:** consistent with the apostolic "rule of faith" preserved in the churches.
- **Catholicity:** widely used across diverse Christian communities, not a local sectarian favorite.

Some books now inside the canon were disputed: Hebrews (authorship unknown), James (thought weak on grace by some), 2 Peter (latest attestation), 2 and 3 John (short, little-copied), Jude (quotes Enoch), and Revelation (rejected by some Eastern churches for centuries). Some books outside — 1 Clement, the Shepherd of Hermas, the Didache — enjoyed quasi-canonical status in particular regions before eventually being excluded.

**Athanasius's 39th Festal Letter (367 CE)** is the first surviving list identical to the 27-book New Testament Christians read today. Regional councils at **Hippo (393 CE)** and **Carthage (397 CE)** affirmed the same list for North Africa. The Eastern church reached similar conclusions through usage.

Protestants, Catholics, and Orthodox all share the identical 27-book NT canon. The differences among traditions — real and important — lie in the Old Testament, not the New.

The shape of the New Testament reflects how it was used: Gospels first, proclaiming Jesus; Acts narrating the church's birth; epistles shaping congregations; Revelation climaxing the story. It is less a library chosen than a library received.
