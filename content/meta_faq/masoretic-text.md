The **Masoretic Text (MT)** is the authoritative Hebrew text of the Old Testament preserved by Jewish scribes called Masoretes ("transmitters" or "traditioners") who worked between roughly 500 and 1000 CE. Nearly every modern Old Testament translation is based primarily on the Masoretic Text, with variants weighed against other ancient witnesses.

The text the Masoretes received was already the product of centuries of careful transmission by Jewish scribes, but the Masoretes added layer upon layer of quality control:

- **Consonantal text.** They preserved and standardized the consonantal Hebrew text inherited from earlier centuries — the text with which Qumran scrolls largely agree.
- **Vocalization.** They invented a system of **niqqud** (dots and strokes under and around the consonants) to indicate vowels, which classical Hebrew does not otherwise write. Three competing vocalization systems (Babylonian, Palestinian, Tiberian) developed; the **Tiberian** system, associated with the family of Aaron ben Moses ben Asher, became standard.
- **Accentuation.** They added **te'amim** — cantillation marks indicating how the text was to be chanted and how phrases were to be parsed syntactically. These marks are invaluable for understanding ambiguous passages.
- **Marginal notes.** They recorded statistical data (middle verse of each book, most frequent word, number of letters) and cross-references for quality control. These notes, called the **Masorah**, are a wonder of ancient book-making.

The two most important surviving Masoretic manuscripts are the **Aleppo Codex** (c. 930 CE, partly damaged in a 1947 fire) and the **Leningrad Codex** (1008 CE, complete). The Leningrad Codex is the basis of the standard scholarly edition, *Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia*, used in most modern Bibles.

The Dead Sea Scrolls (discovered 1947–56) dramatically confirmed the fidelity of Masoretic transmission. Scrolls from the first century BCE match the medieval Masoretic Text with astonishing precision in many books — demonstrating that the scribal tradition was careful for at least a thousand years before the Masoretes themselves. Where the scrolls differ, they sometimes agree with the Septuagint instead, reminding us that textual pluriformity existed in the Second Temple period.

Modern translations follow the Masoretic Text in the vast majority of cases, departing from it only when strong evidence (LXX agreement, internal logic, or manuscript variants) suggests the MT itself has suffered corruption. Translation footnotes that say "Heb. uncertain" or "Some ancient versions read..." are usually signaling such departures.

The Masoretic Text is not the original Hebrew Bible — no such thing survives. It is the best-preserved and most thoroughly documented form of the Hebrew Bible, produced by scribes who saw their work as the most sacred of labors.
