A **critical apparatus** is the set of footnotes at the bottom of a scholarly edition of a biblical text that records textual variants found in the manuscript evidence. It is the translator's (and serious reader's) window into the decisions that shape every modern Bible.

The two most important critical editions are:

- **Nestle-Aland 28 (NA28)** — the standard critical edition of the Greek New Testament, used by nearly every translation team since the late 20th century.
- **Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia (BHS)** and its successor **Biblia Hebraica Quinta (BHQ)** — the scholarly editions of the Hebrew Old Testament based on the Leningrad Codex.

Reading a critical apparatus takes practice because of the dense abbreviations involved. Consider a simplified NT apparatus entry:

```
1:1  ¶ υιου θεου ] txt ℵ¹ B D W f¹³ 33 it vg sy co // omit ℵ* Θ 28
```

Translation: At Mark 1:1, the words "Son of God" are in the main text (that's what "txt" means) supported by Codex Sinaiticus (first corrector), Codex Vaticanus, Codex Bezae, Codex Washingtonianus, a family of minuscules (f¹³), minuscule 33, the Old Latin, the Vulgate, Syriac, and Coptic versions. They are **omitted** by the original hand of Sinaiticus (ℵ*), Codex Koridethi (Θ), and minuscule 28.

Some common sigla and abbreviations:

- **𝔓** = papyri. **ℵ** = Sinaiticus. **A, B, C, D** etc. = major uncial codices.
- **f¹, f¹³** = manuscript families descended from common archetypes.
- **𝔐** or **Byz** = the Majority / Byzantine text (the bulk of later Greek manuscripts).
- **it** = Old Latin versions. **vg** = Vulgate. **sy** = Syriac. **co** = Coptic.
- **lat, sa, bo** = Latin, Sahidic (Upper Egyptian), Bohairic (Lower Egyptian).
- **𝑣𝑖𝑑** ("videtur") = "as it appears" — the reading is probable but not certain because the manuscript is damaged.
- ***** (asterisk after a siglum) = original hand. **c** = later corrector.

NA28 signals the editors' confidence in a reading with a bracketed letter in the margin: {A} very certain, {B} nearly certain, {C} judged probable, {D} barely preferred over the alternative.

For Hebrew OT, the BHS apparatus uses slightly different conventions, drawing on the Masoretic manuscripts, the Dead Sea Scrolls (designated by cave and number, e.g., 1QIsaa), the Septuagint (𝔊), the Samaritan Pentateuch (𝔐̇), the Targums (𝔗), and the Vulgate (𝔙).

You do not have to master the apparatus to read the Bible well. But knowing it exists — and knowing that every modern translation committee worked through it line by line — is part of reading Scripture as a grown-up.
