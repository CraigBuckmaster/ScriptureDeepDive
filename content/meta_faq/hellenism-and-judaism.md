**Hellenism** — the diffusion of Greek language, culture, and thought — was the single most transformative force in the Jewish world between Alexander the Great (d. 323 BCE) and the New Testament era. Understanding Jewish engagement with Hellenism illuminates almost everything distinctive about Second Temple Judaism and the world into which Christianity was born.

**What Hellenism brought.**

- **Greek language** (*koine*) as the lingua franca from the Mediterranean to the Indus. Even Judea, which continued to speak Aramaic and Hebrew, conducted much public and commercial life in Greek.
- **The polis** — the Greek city-state as a model of civic life with gymnasium, agora, theater, and council.
- **Education** in grammar, rhetoric, poetry, philosophy.
- **Philosophy** — Platonism, Aristotelianism, Stoicism, Epicureanism, Cynicism.
- **Religion** — a bewildering array of gods, mystery cults, emperor cults, and syncretistic movements.
- **A cosmopolitan imagination** — the idea of a single human world connected by culture and commerce.

**Two Jewish responses, each taken many ways.**

**Engagement and adaptation.** Many Jews embraced Greek as a language and culture while preserving Jewish faith. The **Septuagint** translated the Torah into Greek for Jews who no longer read Hebrew. **Philo of Alexandria** (c. 20 BCE – 50 CE) wrote vast philosophical-theological works in Greek synthesizing Plato and Moses. Jewish literature in this period — Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach, 4 Maccabees, Joseph and Aseneth — thinks with Greek categories while remaining deeply Jewish. The diaspora communities of Alexandria, Antioch, Asia Minor, and Rome sustained Judaism-plus-Greek culture for centuries.

**Resistance.** Other Jews saw Hellenism as a fatal threat. The Maccabean revolt against Antiochus IV's forced hellenization (167–160 BCE) established a narrative of faithful resistance. The Qumran community retreated from a Jerusalem elite they regarded as hellenized and compromised. Pharisaic and later rabbinic movements sharpened boundary markers — Sabbath, food laws, circumcision — that kept Jewish identity distinct.

**How the two responses coexisted.**

- Hebrew remained the language of Scripture and prayer; Greek became the language of public life and diaspora Bible.
- Jerusalem had its own Greek theater; yet the Temple strictly excluded Gentile men beyond the outer court.
- Jewish philosophers argued Jewish Scripture was the source of Greek wisdom; rabbis warned against raising children on Greek.
- Jews like **Josephus** and **Paul** moved fluently between Jewish and Hellenistic categories.

**For reading the New Testament.**

The NT was written in Greek. Its authors used Greek philosophical terms (logos, wisdom, conscience, soul) and Greek rhetorical structures (diatribe, chreia, household code). Paul's letters are full of Greco-Roman rhetorical conventions. John's Prologue ("In the beginning was the Word") speaks to Hellenists who knew the logos concept from Stoic and Philonic philosophy.

Yet the NT is relentlessly Jewish. Its God is the God of Abraham. Its categories are the covenant categories of Israel. Its Messiah is Israel's Messiah, not a Greek savior figure. The NT demonstrates the quintessential Second Temple Jewish achievement: speaking to the Hellenistic world while keeping faith with the God of Israel.

**How this shapes Jesus's context.**

- Jesus lived in Galilee, a region pock-marked with Greek cities (the Decapolis, Sepphoris, Tiberias). He almost certainly knew some Greek.
- His teachings engage both rabbinic tradition and popular Hellenistic themes without being dominated by either.
- The Gospels were written in Greek for readers across the empire.
- The early church was a Jewish reform movement that quickly became the most successful Greek-speaking Jewish diaspora mission in history.

Hellenism is not a foreign element the New Testament opposes. It is the water the first Christian fish were swimming in.
