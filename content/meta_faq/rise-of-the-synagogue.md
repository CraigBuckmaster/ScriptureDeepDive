The **synagogue** — from Greek *synagōgē*, "gathering" — became the central institution of Jewish religious life by the first century CE. Its development between the Babylonian exile and the New Testament period transformed Judaism and created the model on which early Christian worship was built.

**Before the synagogue there was only the Temple.** For centuries of Israel's history, worship was tethered to the Temple in Jerusalem — sacrifice there, festival pilgrimage there, priesthood functioning there. When the Babylonians destroyed the First Temple in 586 BCE, exiled Jews faced a crisis: how to be Jewish without a temple?

**Origins in the exile.** The precise origins of the synagogue are not recorded, but the exile (586–539 BCE) is the probable crucible. Separated from Jerusalem, Jewish communities gathered to read the Torah, recite the *Shema*, pray, and instruct the young. These gatherings did not replace the Temple (hoped-for and eventually rebuilt) but complemented it. Over the Persian and Hellenistic centuries, the synagogue spread throughout the diaspora and eventually back into the land of Israel.

**By the first century CE,** synagogues existed in virtually every Jewish community of any size. Archaeological remains at Gamla, Masada, Herodium, Capernaum, and elsewhere document the first-century physical form. Philo and Josephus mention synagogues routinely. The New Testament names dozens of them: Nazareth, Capernaum, Jerusalem, Antioch of Pisidia, Iconium, Thessalonica, Berea, Corinth, Ephesus.

**What synagogues did.**

- **Scripture reading.** A weekly cycle of Torah reading, accompanied by readings from the Prophets (*haftarot*). Luke 4:16–30 shows Jesus reading from Isaiah in the Nazareth synagogue.
- **Translation and exposition.** Where Hebrew was no longer understood, the reading was followed by an Aramaic paraphrase (*targum*) and a sermon or exhortation (Luke 4:20–21; Acts 13:15).
- **Prayer.** The *Shema* (Deut 6:4–9, 11:13–21; Num 15:37–41), the *Amidah* (Eighteen Benedictions), and other structured prayers.
- **Community functions.** Courts, schools, guest accommodation, charity distribution, community gatherings.

**Leadership roles.**

- **Archisynagōgos** (ruler of the synagogue) — the chief administrator.
- **Hazzan** — attendant who handled the scrolls.
- **Council of elders** — deliberative and disciplinary.
- The **Pharisees** were especially influential in synagogues — teaching, debating, shaping the developing oral tradition.

**Connection to early Christianity.**

The synagogue was the primary site of Christian mission in the diaspora. Paul's standard pattern was to enter a city and first preach in the synagogue (Acts 13, 14, 17, 18, 19). Many Gentiles — so-called **God-fearers** — were attracted to synagogue monotheism and ethics without fully converting; they became a bridge population through which Christianity rapidly spread.

**Architectural and liturgical legacy.**

The structure of early Christian worship closely resembles synagogue worship: public prayer, Scripture reading, exposition, psalm-singing. Even the layout of early church buildings reflects synagogue orientation. When the temple was destroyed in 70 CE and Jews increasingly worshipped only in synagogues, the parallel to the early church became even more pronounced.

**After 70 CE** the synagogue became the essentially sole public institution of Judaism. **Rabbinic Judaism** — the tradition that codified the Mishnah (c. 200 CE) and later the Talmud — was formed in this post-Temple synagogue world. The tense process by which early Christianity separated from synagogue Judaism in the late first and second centuries shapes both traditions to this day.

Understanding the synagogue illuminates Jesus's ministry, Paul's mission strategy, and the shape of early Christian worship. The church was born in the synagogue before it outgrew it.
