The **Two-Source Hypothesis** is the most widely held academic solution to the Synoptic Problem — the puzzle of how Matthew, Mark, and Luke ended up so similar in so many passages yet so different in others. Developed in the nineteenth century by H. J. Holtzmann and refined by B. H. Streeter, it proposes that Matthew and Luke each drew on two primary written sources: the **Gospel of Mark** and a **sayings source called Q**.

**Markan priority.** On this view, Mark was written first, around the late 50s to the mid-60s CE. Matthew and Luke, writing later (c. 70–90 CE), each had a copy of Mark in front of them and reproduced most of it, sometimes word-for-word. This explains why over 90% of Mark appears in Matthew and why the narrative order is nearly identical where the three Gospels overlap.

Several arguments support Markan priority:

- **Mark is shorter.** It is easier to explain Matthew and Luke expanding Mark than to explain Mark cutting two-thirds of Matthew.
- **Mark's Greek is rougher.** Matthew and Luke consistently improve Mark's grammar, add reverential qualifiers (e.g., softening "could not do" to "did not do"), and smooth awkward phrasing.
- **Mark's oddities persist.** Passages in Mark that seem theologically awkward (Mark 6:5 "he could not do any miracle there"; Mark 3:21 Jesus's family thinks he is out of his mind) are modified or omitted in Matthew and Luke — in the direction you would expect later Gospels to move.
- **Order of material.** When Matthew and Luke agree in order, they usually agree with Mark; when they diverge, they diverge in opposite directions. The natural explanation is that both are using Mark as a framework.

**Q.** Roughly 230 verses appear in both Matthew and Luke but are not in Mark — almost all of them sayings of Jesus (the Sermon on the Mount / Plain, the Lord's Prayer, the Beatitudes, parables like the Lost Sheep). These are labeled **Q material**. Because no Q manuscript survives, its existence is hypothetical — but the alternative (that Luke used Matthew directly) has to explain why Luke so often **disrupts** Matthew's arrangement of the sayings, scattering them through his Gospel. On the Q hypothesis, Matthew collected Q material into blocks (the Sermon on the Mount is a good example); Luke distributed it across his journey narrative.

**M and L.** The Four-Source refinement adds material unique to Matthew (**M** — the Magi, Joseph's dreams, the wheat and tares) and material unique to Luke (**L** — the Good Samaritan, the Prodigal Son, Zacchaeus) as two additional sources, whether written or oral.

**Common objections.** Some scholars argue Q is unnecessary if Luke knew Matthew (the Farrer Hypothesis). Others defend Matthean priority (Griesbach, Augustine). These remain minority positions in academic biblical studies.

The Two-Source Hypothesis is a **scholarly model**, not a doctrine. It explains patterns in the text without diminishing the Gospels' authority. It is a tool for understanding how the Gospels may have been composed, nothing more and nothing less.
