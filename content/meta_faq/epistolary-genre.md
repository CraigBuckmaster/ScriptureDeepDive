Twenty-one of the New Testament's twenty-seven books are **letters** — correspondence between early Christian leaders and churches or individuals. Reading them well requires understanding ancient letter-writing conventions and the specific pastoral situations each letter addresses.

**Ancient letters followed a standard structure.**

- **Salutation** — sender, recipient, greeting.
- **Thanksgiving or blessing** — often naming themes the letter will develop.
- **Body** — the main content.
- **Paraenesis** — practical exhortation.
- **Closing** — final greetings, benediction.

The New Testament letters follow this pattern with Christian modifications. "Paul, a servant of Christ Jesus" opens Romans in the sender slot. "To all God's beloved in Rome" identifies the recipients. "Grace to you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ" greets them — transforming the conventional "grace" (*charis*) greeting into a theological affirmation. The thanksgiving often serves as a letter's table of contents: read Romans 1:8–15 or 1 Corinthians 1:4–9 closely and many of the letter's themes announce themselves.

**Ancient letters were occasional.** They were written to address specific situations, not as systematic theology textbooks. Paul wrote to Corinth because the Corinthians had written him questions (1 Cor 7:1). He wrote Galatians because Judaizers had threatened the churches' gospel. He wrote Romans in part to introduce himself to a church he had not founded, in preparation for visit and onward mission. Knowing the **occasion** (the "why" of a letter) transforms reading.

**Mirror-reading** is the technique of reconstructing the situation from the letter's responses. If Paul insists "Christ did not send me to baptize but to preach the gospel" (1 Cor 1:17), he is probably responding to Corinthian factionalism that was attaching too much significance to who baptized whom. If James spends three chapters on the rich and the poor, there was probably a socioeconomic crisis in the churches he addresses. Mirror-reading must be careful — it can over-read — but some version of it is indispensable.

**Recognize the types.**

- **Pastoral letters** (Paul to churches) address specific community problems with theological resources.
- **Personal letters** (Philemon, 2 and 3 John) are more intimate and shorter.
- **Sermonic letters** (Hebrews, 1 John) read more like extended homilies than typical correspondence.
- **Circular letters** (Ephesians, 1 Peter) were probably intended to be read in multiple churches.
- **Apostolic pastoral** (1–2 Timothy, Titus) address young leaders on church order.

**Reading strategies.**

- **Read the whole letter in one sitting.** Letters are meant to be heard end-to-end. Reading Romans in 20-minute chunks misses the argument's arc.
- **Outline the flow of thought.** Mark where Paul transitions, illustrates, answers objections.
- **Note the "therefore" and "so then."** These signal the move from theology to application.
- **Pay attention to the plural.** "You" in the letters is usually the plural "y'all" — addressed to the community, not the individual.
- **Watch for diatribe style** in Paul — imagined objector speaking: "What shall we say then? Shall we go on sinning so that grace may increase? By no means!"

**Theology via letters.** The New Testament epistles were written before any systematic theology. They do theology on the move, addressing pastoral crises. This makes them **more** trustworthy for theology, not less — the doctrines emerge from the practical soil of early church life. But it also means we should read them sympathetically, as letters to real people in real trouble, not as textbook chapters.
