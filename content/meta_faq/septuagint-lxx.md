The **Septuagint (LXX)** is the ancient Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible, produced in Alexandria by Greek-speaking Jews starting around the third century BCE. The name "Septuagint" comes from the Latin word for "seventy," reflecting the legend in the pseudepigraphical *Letter of Aristeas* that seventy-two (rounded to seventy) Jewish translators produced the Torah in seventy-two days, their translations miraculously identical. Despite the legend, historical evidence suggests the translation was a gradual, book-by-book project stretching over centuries.

The Septuagint is one of the great literary and religious documents of antiquity. For the Jews of the diaspora, it was "their Bible" — the Hebrew Bible in the lingua franca of the Hellenistic world. Philo of Alexandria read the Septuagint; so did the earliest Christian communities. The **New Testament writers overwhelmingly quote the Septuagint** rather than the Hebrew. When Jesus in Luke 4 reads from "the scroll of Isaiah" and declares "today this scripture is fulfilled," Luke's Greek rendering matches the LXX. When Hebrews 10:5 quotes Psalm 40, "a body you have prepared for me," it follows the Septuagint rather than the Hebrew ("ears you have dug for me").

The LXX differs from the Masoretic Text in important ways:

- **Canon shape.** The LXX includes books absent from the Hebrew canon — Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, 1–2 Maccabees, Baruch, and expansions of Esther and Daniel (the "Apocrypha" or "deuterocanon").
- **Book order.** The LXX groups books by genre (law, history, wisdom, prophets) rather than by the Hebrew threefold division (Torah, Prophets, Writings).
- **Text-critical value.** Some LXX readings preserve older forms of the Hebrew text than the MT. The book of Jeremiah, for instance, is one-eighth shorter in the LXX and in a different order — and Qumran Jeremiah fragments match the LXX, suggesting the shorter version is older.
- **Translation quality.** The LXX is not a uniform document. Torah books are fairly literal; the Writings (especially Job and Proverbs) are looser and more interpretive; some books (Daniel) exist in multiple Greek editions.

Because of its role in the early church, the LXX took on enduring importance. Church fathers quoted it as the inspired text. Orthodox Christianity still treats it as the canonical form of the Old Testament. Modern Bible translations consult it constantly, especially where the MT seems corrupted.

Whenever a New Testament quotation of the Old Testament looks **different** from what your English OT says, the LXX is probably the reason. The NT authors were reading the Bible their communities actually used — the Greek Bible of the Hellenistic Jewish world.
