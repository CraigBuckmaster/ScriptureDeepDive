**Historical Jesus scholarship** is the academic effort to reconstruct what can be known about Jesus of Nazareth using the tools of historical inquiry. It is distinct from — though related to — theological reflection on Jesus Christ, the object of Christian faith. The scholar asks: if you set aside for a moment the question of faith, what can a historian establish about this first-century Jewish teacher from the available evidence?

The field has a complicated history. It can be divided into **three "quests"** with distinct methodologies and assumptions:

**The First Quest (late 18th – early 20th century)** was propelled by Enlightenment rationalism. Writers like Reimarus, Strauss, and Renan treated the Gospel miracles as myths or metaphors and sought "the historical Jesus behind the Christ of faith." Albert Schweitzer's *Quest of the Historical Jesus* (1906) devastatingly critiqued this movement: nineteenth-century scholars, Schweitzer showed, had each produced a Jesus in their own image — a liberal Protestant Jesus, a romantic Jesus, a moralist Jesus. Schweitzer's own reconstruction — Jesus as a failed apocalyptic prophet — was at least honest about Jesus's radical strangeness to modern sensibilities, even if it too reflected Schweitzer's assumptions.

**The New Quest (mid-20th century)** emerged in the 1950s, associated with Ernst Käsemann and the Bultmann school's more moderate students. The guiding principle was the **criterion of dissimilarity**: a saying is most likely authentic if it differs both from contemporary Judaism (so Jesus didn't just parrot Jewish tradition) and from the early church's later teaching (so the church didn't just invent it). This produced a minimalist Jesus but confirmed that some historical Jesus research was possible even in the post-Bultmann era.

**The Third Quest (late 20th century to present)** rejected the assumption that Jesus must be different from his Jewish context. On the contrary: a historically plausible Jesus must be **deeply Jewish**, shaped by Second Temple apocalyptic, messianic, and covenantal categories. Leaders of this movement include E. P. Sanders (Jesus as restoration-of-Israel prophet), N. T. Wright (Jesus as climax of Israel's story), John Meier (multi-volume careful historian), and Dale Allison (apocalyptic prophet). The Third Quest takes seriously Jewish sources, Dead Sea Scrolls, archaeology of Galilee, and sociological data.

**The Jesus Seminar** (1985-present), led by Robert Funk, represents a more skeptical strand, famously voting on the authenticity of Jesus's sayings with colored beads. Mainstream Third Quest scholars consider its methodology idiosyncratic.

**Where scholarship converges.** Across the spectrum, historical scholars generally agree Jesus: was a first-century Palestinian Jew; was baptized by John the Baptist; gathered disciples; taught in parables; performed acts interpreted as healings and exorcisms; proclaimed the kingdom of God; conflicted with authorities; was crucified under Pontius Pilate; and was remembered by his followers as having been raised from the dead.

**What historical inquiry cannot settle.** The resurrection, the deity of Christ, and the atoning significance of the cross are theological claims beyond the reach of historical method alone. Historical research can clear away distortions and provide context; it cannot by itself found the faith of the church. For that, historical work is necessary but not sufficient.
