The English-speaking world enjoys dozens of faithful Bible translations, each produced by committees of careful scholars. Choosing among them is less about finding "the correct" translation and more about matching the right tool to your purpose.

**Three questions to ask:**

1. **Who made it, and what was their philosophy?** Every major translation has a preface explaining who translated it and what principles guided them. Read it. A translation by a mainline committee (NRSVue) and one by an evangelical committee (ESV) will differ in emphasis, even where they agree on text.

2. **What base text does it use?** Modern translations typically use the Nestle-Aland or UBS Greek New Testament and the Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia for the Hebrew Old Testament. The KJV and NKJV use a Byzantine/Textus Receptus tradition for the NT. The difference matters in a handful of verses, not the core message.

3. **What is your use case?** Study, devotional reading, memorization, public teaching, or first-read by a new believer each call for different features.

**Translations by use case:**

**For careful study (formal / word-for-word):**
- **NASB** (1995 or 2020): very literal, scholarly, preserves Hebrew idiom.
- **ESV**: formal with readable prose, widely adopted in conservative evangelical circles.
- **NRSVue**: ecumenical, mainline, with the Apocrypha available in editions that include it.
- **CSB**: formal-with-dynamic-flex, more readable than the ESV in prose passages.

**For general reading and balanced use:**
- **NIV (2011)**: the most-read English translation in the world, aims to balance accuracy with readability.
- **NET Bible**: a unique translation with tens of thousands of translator notes explaining judgment calls — excellent for serious readers who want to see the craft.

**For readability (dynamic):**
- **NLT**: produced by a committee, not a paraphrase; very readable.
- **GNT / TEV**: Simple English, excellent for new readers or ESL.
- **CEV**: clarity-focused; very accessible.

**For literary beauty:**
- **KJV**: the fountainhead of English biblical prose; still unmatched in rhythm.
- **Robert Alter's Hebrew Bible**: a single-scholar literary translation of the OT with deep philological notes.

**For children:**
- **ERV / ICB / NIrV**: reading levels targeted at grade 3–5.

**For the Catholic tradition:**
- **NABRE**: the official US Catholic translation.
- **Jerusalem Bible / NJB**: literary, scholarly, includes the deuterocanon.

**Avoid as your only Bible:** single-author paraphrases (The Message, The Living Bible, the Passion Translation). These can be useful supplements but should be read alongside a committee translation.

**Best practice:** read more than one. A formal translation (ESV/NASB/NRSV) alongside a dynamic one (NIV/NLT) will expose you to the texture of the original in ways no single translation can.

There is no perfect English Bible. There are many good ones. Read one, deeply, every day, and the differences between them will become part of your education rather than a source of anxiety.
