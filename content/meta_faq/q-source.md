**Q** is a hypothetical source — a document or oral tradition — thought by most critical scholars to have supplied the **~230 verses of sayings** that Matthew and Luke share but Mark does not. The name comes from the German word *Quelle*, "source."

No copy of Q has ever been found. Its existence is a scholarly reconstruction, not a physical document. But the pattern of agreements between Matthew and Luke in non-Markan sayings material is so close — sometimes exact Greek-for-Greek — that many critics judge a common written source the most economical explanation.

**Representative Q material:**

- The core of the **Sermon on the Mount / Plain** (Beatitudes, love of enemies, golden rule).
- The **Lord's Prayer** (differently expanded in Matthew and Luke).
- John the Baptist's preaching ("brood of vipers").
- The temptation of Jesus (Matthew's and Luke's accounts are in the same order but differ from Mark's brief summary).
- Parables: the Lost Sheep, the Talents / Pounds, the Wedding Feast / Great Banquet.
- Sayings: "Foxes have holes"; "No servant can serve two masters"; woes against the scribes and Pharisees.

**Why Q?**

The argument runs: if Luke used Matthew, why does he so often **break up** Matthew's teaching arrangements? Matthew gathers five great discourses (chapters 5–7, 10, 13, 18, 24–25). Luke scatters the same material across his travel narrative (chapters 9–19), often placing the same sayings in dramatically different contexts. Scholars find it easier to explain both patterns if both authors drew independently from a common source, each arranging the material according to their own editorial purposes.

**Dissent.**

Not all scholars accept Q. The **Farrer Hypothesis** (associated with Austin Farrer, Michael Goulder, and most recently Mark Goodacre) argues that Luke simply used both Mark and Matthew, eliminating the need for a hypothetical document. Proponents argue that a missing document with no citation by any ancient writer is a less economical explanation than direct use. The debate remains live in scholarly journals.

**What Q would be if it existed.**

Q seems to have been primarily a **sayings source** — relatively little narrative. It opens with John the Baptist and Jesus's temptation; it does not clearly contain a passion narrative; it closes with apocalyptic sayings. This resembles in genre some ancient Jewish wisdom collections (e.g., the Instruction of Amenemope) and has affinities with the later Gospel of Thomas, though the content is different.

**What Q is not.**

Q is not a "lost Gospel" preserving a simpler, non-supernatural Jesus. Reconstructions of Q retain miracles (John's question to Jesus about his works), strong eschatology (apocalyptic woes), and high christological statements (Matt 11:27 par. Luke 10:22). Any Q Jesus is recognizably the Jesus of the Synoptic Gospels.

The Q hypothesis is a tool for explaining literary patterns. It neither elevates nor threatens the canonical Gospels. If Q existed, it was absorbed into Matthew and Luke; if it did not, those Gospels preserve the same traditions without an intermediate document.
