The **Dead Sea Scrolls** are a collection of roughly 900 Jewish manuscripts and manuscript fragments, discovered between 1947 and 1956 in eleven caves near Khirbet Qumran on the northwest shore of the Dead Sea. They date from roughly the third century BCE to the first century CE and constitute one of the most important archaeological finds in the history of biblical studies.

The scrolls fall into three broad categories:

- **Biblical manuscripts (~220 scrolls).** Every book of the Hebrew Bible is represented except Esther. The **Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaa)**, a nearly complete Isaiah dated to the second century BCE, predates the oldest previously known Hebrew Isaiah manuscript by roughly a thousand years. The variance between 1QIsaa and the medieval Masoretic Text is strikingly small — a powerful testimony to the fidelity of Jewish scribal transmission.
- **Sectarian texts (~350 scrolls).** Documents unique to the community that collected the scrolls: the **Community Rule (1QS)**, the **War Scroll (1QM)**, the **Thanksgiving Hymns (1QHa)**, biblical commentaries called **pesharim** that interpret the prophets as speaking about contemporary events, and the famous **Copper Scroll**, which lists buried treasure in an unknown location.
- **Other Jewish literature (~330 scrolls).** Books known from other Jewish traditions — **Tobit**, **Sirach**, **1 Enoch**, **Jubilees** — and works previously unknown entirely.

The community behind the scrolls is widely (though not universally) identified with the **Essenes**, a Jewish sect described by Josephus, Philo, and Pliny the Elder. They seem to have been priestly dissenters who rejected the Jerusalem temple establishment as corrupt, withdrew to the wilderness, practiced ritual purity with rigorous intensity, and expected the imminent arrival of two messiahs — a priestly Messiah of Aaron and a royal Messiah of Israel.

The scrolls have revolutionized biblical studies in several ways:

- They confirm the remarkable textual stability of the Hebrew Bible over more than a millennium.
- They show that **textual pluriformity** existed in the Second Temple period — some scrolls match the Masoretic Text, others match the Septuagint, still others represent variant forms found nowhere else.
- They illuminate the religious world of Second Temple Judaism, showing a vibrant diversity of movements (Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, Zealots, apocalyptic groups) into which Jesus and early Christianity were born.
- They document Jewish messianic, eschatological, and apocalyptic expectations that form the background of the New Testament.
- They provide linguistic evidence for Hebrew and Aramaic usage in the centuries immediately before the New Testament — including Aramaic Job and Aramaic Tobit, which shed light on Jesus's native tongue.

The scrolls do not rewrite Christianity, but they vastly enrich our understanding of the world in which Christianity was born. Any serious student of the Bible benefits from some familiarity with them.
