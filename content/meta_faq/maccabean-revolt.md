The **Maccabean Revolt** (167–160 BCE) was the Jewish uprising against the Seleucid king **Antiochus IV Epiphanes**, who had attempted to abolish Judaism and hellenize Judea by force. Its memory shaped Second Temple Jewish identity and provides crucial background to the Gospels and Revelation, even though the revolt happened centuries before Jesus.

**The setting.** After Alexander the Great's death (323 BCE), his empire fractured among his generals. Judea fell first under the Ptolemies of Egypt, then (198 BCE) under the Seleucids of Syria. By the early 2nd century BCE, a Hellenizing faction among the Jerusalem priestly elite was pushing for the adoption of Greek customs — gymnasiums, Greek names, participation in pan-Hellenic culture. This produced deep tension with traditional Jews who saw these innovations as betrayal of Torah and ancestral faith.

**Antiochus IV** (r. 175–164 BCE). The Seleucid king, struggling with Roman pressure and Ptolemaic wars, decided to consolidate his realm by imposing Greek religion and culture. In 167 BCE he issued a decree outlawing Sabbath observance, circumcision, and Torah reading; ordering the sacrifice of pigs on Jewish altars; and, most infamously, erecting a statue of Zeus on the altar of the Jerusalem Temple — the "abomination of desolation" (Dan 11:31; cf. Mark 13:14).

**Mattathias and his sons.** The revolt broke out at Modein when a priest named Mattathias refused to offer a pagan sacrifice, killed both a Jewish collaborator and a royal officer, and fled to the hills with his five sons. On his death, leadership passed to his son **Judas Maccabeus** ("the Hammer"), whose guerrilla tactics inflicted a series of astonishing defeats on larger Seleucid armies.

**Temple rededication (164 BCE).** Judas captured Jerusalem and cleansed the Temple. The rededication ceremony is commemorated by the Jewish feast of **Hanukkah** ("Dedication"). According to the Talmudic tradition, a one-day supply of ritually pure oil miraculously lasted eight days until new oil could be prepared. Jesus in John 10:22–23 is walking in the Temple "at the time of the Feast of Dedication."

**Continued resistance.** Antiochus IV died in 164 BCE; his successors continued the conflict. Judas was killed in battle in 160; his brothers **Jonathan** and **Simon** succeeded him. By 142 BCE the Seleucids granted Judea effective independence. The **Hasmonean dynasty**, descendants of Mattathias, then ruled an independent Jewish kingdom for about a century until Pompey's Roman incursion in 63 BCE.

**Consequences for Second Temple Judaism.**

- **Theology of martyrdom.** The mothers and sons of 2 Maccabees 7, tortured rather than compromise Torah, establish a paradigm of faithful suffering that shapes later Jewish and Christian martyrology.
- **Resurrection hope.** The martyrs' confidence that God would raise them to new life (2 Macc 7:9, 14, 23) marks a significant development in Second Temple resurrection theology.
- **Apocalyptic literature.** The Book of **Daniel** took its final form in this context; its visions of beasts representing empires and the triumph of God's kingdom respond to Antiochus's persecution.
- **Hanukkah** as annual Jewish remembrance.
- **Deep Jewish suspicion of Hellenism** that remained active through the Roman period.
- **Fault lines between Jewish parties** — Sadducees (Hasmonean-aligned), Pharisees (more popular, sometimes opposed the Hasmoneans), Essenes (rejected the Hasmoneans as illegitimate priests) — that shaped the world of Jesus.

To read the Gospels with ears for Jewish memory, know Antiochus and Judas. Their shadows fall on every page.
