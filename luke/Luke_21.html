Luke 21

The Widow's Gift, Signs of the End, and the Son of Man

Author

Luke, a Gentile physician (Col 4:14) and the most accomplished literary stylist in the New Testament. His Greek is the most polished in the NT corpus — modelled on the style of educated Hellenistic historians such as Thucydides and Polybius. Early and unanimous church tradition (Irenaeus c. AD 180, Clement of Alexandria, Origen, Eusebius, Jerome) identifies him as the author of both this Gospel and the Acts of the Apostles. He was a close companion of Paul (Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11, “only Luke is with me”), and the “we” passages in Acts (16:10–17; 20:5–15; 21:1–18; 27:1–28:16) confirm direct eyewitness participation in events he narrates. Luke is the only Gentile author in the canon. He explicitly describes his method in the prologue (1:1–4): careful investigation of all available tradition, consultation of eyewitnesses and ministers of the word, and orderly composition for a patron named Theophilus — a preface that conforms to the best conventions of Hellenistic historiography.

When written

c. AD 62–70, most likely during Paul’s two-year imprisonment in Caesarea (Acts 24:27, c. AD 57–59) or shortly thereafter, with publication before Acts (which presupposes it, Acts 1:1). A pre-AD 70 date is supported by the absence of any reference to the destruction of Jerusalem as a past event; the Gospel’s detailed prediction of the siege (21:20–24) reads as prophecy, not retrospective commentary. A minority of scholars favour AD 70–85, arguing that the precision of 21:20 reflects post-event knowledge. The two-volume work (Luke–Acts) is approximately 27,000 words — the largest single contribution to the NT by word count.

What prompted it

Luke writes for Theophilus (“friend of God”), probably a Gentile patron of high social standing (“most excellent,” Acts 1:1, a term used for Roman officials), who has already received some instruction in the faith but needs a reliable, ordered account to confirm its certainty (1:4). More broadly, Luke’s two-volume work addresses the expanding Gentile mission: How does the story of a Jewish Messiah become good news for all nations? His Gospel foregrounds the inclusion of the poor, women, Samaritans, and Gentiles; his Acts shows the gospel moving from Jerusalem to Rome. Luke also addresses a pastoral crisis: the delay of the Parousia. His travel narrative (9:51–19:28) reframes discipleship as a sustained journey of formation, not merely expectation of an imminent end. The Gospel’s persistent themes — prayer, joy, the Holy Spirit, reversal of fortune, universal salvation — all serve this dual apologetic and pastoral purpose.

Greek Word Study

lepta duo LEP-ta DY-o — two very small copper coins: The lepton (v.2) was the smallest coin in circulation — worth 1/128 of a denarius (roughly 6 minutes of a labourer's wages). Two leptons were the minimum legally acceptable Temple offering. The widow gave the minimum possible — which was everything she had.

hypomonē hy-po-mo-NE — patient endurance / standing firm: The command (v.19) is not simply "be patient" but to stand firm under sustained pressure — the endurance of one who holds a position against assault. The same word appears in the Revelation letters (Rev 2-3) as the characteristic virtue of persecuted churches.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
21:3-4

Jesus evaluates giving by what remains, not what is given. The rich gave from their surplus; the widow gave her whole living (bios). The percentage is 100%. The contrast is not between large and small amounts but between giving that costs nothing and giving that costs everything. The widow's giving is costly discipleship in miniature.

21:8

"Do not follow them" — false Christs and false timetables are the first danger. The instruction is not about distant eschatological events but about the susceptibility of every generation to prophetic pretenders who claim to have decoded the schedule. Jesus's first command in the discourse is: do not be deceived.

21:14-15

"Make up your mind not to worry beforehand how you will defend yourselves" — prostithesthe is a deliberate decision: pre-decide not to pre-plan. The assurance is extraordinary: "I will give you words and wisdom that none of your adversaries will be able to resist." This is not advice to be unprepared; it is confidence in the promise that the Spirit will supply what is needed.

21:18-19

"Not a hair of your head will perish" (v.18) appears to contradict "they will put some of you to death" (v.16). The contradiction is deliberately paradoxical: physical death does not constitute "perishing" in the ultimate sense. The life gained by standing firm (v.19) is the life that cannot be taken by those who kill the body (12:4).

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:3-4

Calvin uses the widow's two coins to critique both external and internal forms of religious assessment. External: we measure by size of gift; God measures by ratio of sacrifice. Internal: we measure by emotional generosity; God measures by actual cost. The widow's gift is the model because it is both total (100%) and genuine (not from surplus).

21:8

Calvin insists that the warning against false prophets claiming to know the schedule is addressed to every generation, not merely the first. The instruction "do not follow them" is an implicit instruction not to try to calculate what Jesus says is not calculable. Every generation produces its own false timetables; Jesus's response to all of them is the same: do not follow.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:2

Two lepta — about 1/64 of a denarius, which was one day's wage for a labourer. The lepton was so small that it could not be further subdivided. The widow could have kept one coin and given one; instead she gave both. The voluntariness of total giving, not merely its extent, is part of Jesus's commendation.

21:12

"Before all this" — Luke's Olivet Discourse is notable for its temporal markers: the persecution of disciples (vv.12-19) comes before the cosmic signs (vv.9-11), which themselves precede the destruction of Jerusalem (vv.20-24). The sequencing is more explicit in Luke than in Matthew or Mark, contributing to Luke's reputation for chronological precision.

21:19

Ktēsasthe — aorist middle imperative: "you will gain/acquire" life. The aorist implies a decisive acquisition, not a gradual accumulation. Patient endurance results in a definitive winning of life.

Greek Word Study

kairoi ethnōn kai-ROI ETH-non — times of the Gentiles: The phrase (v.24) is unique to Luke's Olivet Discourse and has generated extensive debate. It refers to a period during which Gentile nations exercise dominion over Jerusalem — a period that began in 587 BC, resumed in AD 70, and (in some interpretations) will last until a specific future moment.

apolytrōsis a-po-ly-TRO-sis — redemption / liberation: The word (v.28) appears only here in Luke's narrative sections (it occurs in the infancy hymns). When the cosmic signs appear, disciples are not to be terrified but to "lift up your heads, because your redemption is drawing near." The end of the age is not primarily a moment of judgment for the faithful but a moment of liberation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
21:20

Luke's version of the abomination-of-desolation saying (Matt 24:15 / Mark 13:14) is strikingly different: "When you see Jerusalem being surrounded by armies, you will know that its desolation is near." Luke replaces the Danielic "abomination of desolation" with a concrete military description — armies surrounding Jerusalem. This is either a clarification for Gentile readers, a specifically different saying, or (on the preterist reading) an accurate prediction of the Roman siege.

21:24

"Jerusalem will be trampled on by the Gentiles until the times of the Gentiles are fulfilled" — the phrase "times of the Gentiles" suggests a specific, divinely limited period. Paul's mystery of Israel's partial hardening "until the full number of the Gentiles has come in" (Rom 11:25) appears to refer to the same period.

21:28

"Stand up and lift up your heads, because your redemption is drawing near" — the cosmic signs that produce terror in "the nations" (v.25) are the signal for disciples to lift their heads in expectation. The same events are simultaneously a judgment on the resistant world and a liberation for the faithful. The same Son of Man who comes in judgment is the redeemer of those who have been watching and praying.

21:33

"Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will never pass away" — this is one of the most absolute claims Jesus makes. The universe is more temporary than his words. The implication is not merely that he is a reliable prophet but that his words have the character of divine utterance — sharing the permanence of the one who spoke the creation into existence.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:20-24

Calvin treats vv.20-24 as a specific prediction of the Jerusalem destruction of AD 70, fulfilled with remarkable accuracy by the Roman siege under Vespasian and Titus. The "desolation" described in v.22 is the literal physical desolation of the city and Temple. Calvin uses this as evidence of Jesus's prophetic authority: the accuracy of the near prediction validates the far ones.

21:32

Calvin takes "this generation" as referring to the Jewish people as an enduring ethnic and religious community — not the individual generation alive in AD 30. The "all these things" that will happen before this generation passes include the entire eschatological sequence, which requires Israel's persistence as a people throughout the age.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:20

Luke's substitution of "armies surrounding Jerusalem" for "the abomination of desolation" (Matt 24:15 / Mark 13:14) has been used to argue that Luke was written after AD 70 (vaticinium ex eventu — prophecy after the fact). Most evangelical scholars maintain a pre-70 date and see Luke's version as a clarification of the Danielic phrase for non-Jewish readers, not as post-event writing.

21:24

"Times of the Gentiles" — this phrase occurs only here in the NT. Its connection to Rom 11:25 ("the full number of the Gentiles") and to the broader Pauline theology of Israel's place in God's plan has made it a central text in both covenant theology and dispensationalism, each reading it differently within their respective frameworks.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
21:24

Kairoi ethnōn — the "times" (kairoi, the appointed, significant times rather than ordinary chronos time) of the Gentiles. The phrase implies a divinely scheduled period with a beginning and an end. Robertson notes the connection to Paul's "fullness of the Gentiles" in Rom 11:25.

21:32

Genea autē — "this generation." The demonstrative autē (this one, here) normally points to the immediately present group. The natural reading is the generation alive in Jesus's day. If the reference is entirely to AD 70, the saying was fulfilled on schedule; if it includes the Parousia, further interpretation is required.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The Poor Widow
Model of total trust
The widow's two coins (21:1-4) is Luke's last positive character study before the passion narrative. She contrasts with the establishment (20:47), the wealthy donors (21:1), and the disciples who admire the Temple's grandeur (21:5-6). Her total giving out of total poverty is the chapter's theological ground note.
The Disciples
Future witnesses and martyrs
The Olivet Discourse is addressed to the disciples who will face arrest, trials, persecution, and death. The promise of Spirit-given words (21:15) and indestructible life (21:18-19) are addressed directly to them and to every generation of disciples facing the same pressures.

Reception History

The Widow's Offering and Christian Stewardship
The widow's two coins has shaped Christian theology of stewardship more than almost any other passage. The principle that God measures giving by sacrifice rather than amount — by what remains rather than what is given — has been standard in Christian teaching on giving from the earliest centuries. Ambrose, Chrysostom, and countless later preachers have used it to argue against using the logic of comfortable giving.
"This Generation" in Eschatological Debate
The phrase "this generation will certainly not pass away until all these things have happened" (21:32) has been at the centre of the ongoing scholarly and theological debate about the relationship between the destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70) and the Parousia. Preterists argue that everything was fulfilled in AD 70; futurists argue that "generation" means the Jewish people or the end-time generation; and partial preterists argue for a double fulfilment. No consensus has been reached; the debate continues.

Literary Structure

Contrast — vv.1-4 / 5-6The widow who gives everything contrasts with the Temple that will lose everything. The stones that impress the disciples will be thrown down; the two coins that nobody noticed will be remembered. Luke places these two episodes in direct sequence to create a deliberate contrast: genuine devotion versus impressive religion.
Frame — vv.37-38The closing note — Jesus teaching daily in the Temple, sleeping on the Mount of Olives, crowds arriving early — provides a serene pastoral frame for the chapter's apocalyptic content. The Olivet Discourse is delivered and then life continues; the urgency of the teaching is set within an ordinary teaching rhythm.

Greek Word Study

hypomenē hy-po-mo-NE — endurance / patient waiting / perseverance: "By standing firm (hypomenē) you will gain life" (v.19). Hypo (under) + menō (to remain): remaining under pressure without giving way. The chapter's eschatological teaching (wars, earthquakes, persecution, cosmic signs) is framed by this call to endurance. The disciple who endures to the end possesses their psychē (soul/life) not by escaping tribulation but by remaining faithful through it.

kairoi ethnōn KAI-roi eth-NON — the times of the Gentiles: "Jerusalem will be trampled on by the Gentiles until the times of the Gentiles are fulfilled" (v.24). Kairoi (appointed times, plural) of the nations — a unique phrase in the Gospels. The period of Gentile dominion over Jerusalem has a divinely appointed limit (achri hou — until). Paul's "full number of the Gentiles" (Rom 11:25) uses similar language. The times are moving toward a fulfilment.

nēphō NE-pho — be sober / be alert / watch: "Be careful, or your hearts will be weighed down" (v.34) — blepete. The closing exhortation of the eschatological discourse is sustained watchfulness (agrypeite, v.36 — to stay awake all night) and prayer. The disciple who knows the end is coming is not anxious but alert — watchful prayer (proseuchomenoi) is the appropriate posture for those living in the "in-between" of the already and not-yet.

Textual Notes

21:19"Win life" vs "gain your souls"
NA28 reads ktēsasthe tas psychas hymōn ("you will gain your lives/souls"). KJV: "In your patience possess ye your souls"; NIV: "stand firm, and you will win life for yourselves." The Greek psychas can mean "souls," "lives," or "selves."
The word psychē (life/soul) connects to the paradox of 17:33: whoever tries to save their psychē will lose it; whoever loses it will preserve it. Endurance "gains" life in the ultimate sense while risking it in the temporal sense.
21:36"Escape all that is about to happen"
Some MSS read "escape all these things that are about to happen" (with "these things"); others omit "these things." NA28 includes the phrase. The sense is the same in either case.
No theological significance; both readings express the same hope of escape through watchfulness and prayer.

Scholarly Debates

"This generation" in 21:32: Near or Far Fulfilment?
Entirely fulfilled in AD 70
Preterist interpreters: N.T. Wright, R.C. Sproul
"This generation" refers to the generation alive in AD 30; "all these things" refers to the Jerusalem destruction of AD 70. Luke 21:20-24 describes the siege; the Parousia language (21:25-27) is cosmic imagery for a historical event.
Partially fulfilled in AD 70, fully at Parousia
Many evangelical scholars: Bock, Marshall
The near events (AD 70) and far events (Parousia) are both in view; "this generation" may extend beyond a single human lifetime. A double fulfilment, with AD 70 as a type of the final end.
"Generation" = the Jewish people
Calvin, some Reformed interpreters
"This generation" (genea) means the Jewish people as an enduring race — they will persist until the final eschatological fulfilment.
No scholarly consensus. The preterist reading requires significant reinterpretation of the cosmic language in vv.25-27; the futurist reading requires extending "this generation" beyond its most natural meaning. The partial-preterist (double fulfilment) reading is held by many evangelical NT scholars as the most satisfactory.

Theological Themes Tracker

Luke 21 presents the end of the age from two angles simultaneously. The widow's total giving (21:1-4) and the command to stand firm under persecution (21:19) and to watch and pray (21:36) establish that the Christian response to eschatological pressure is not calculation or terror but faithful perseverance. The Olivet Discourse itself is organised around this pastoral concern: before the terrifying signs come, disciples will face persecution; when the signs come, they are to lift their heads; throughout, they are to watch and pray. The chapter is less an eschatological timetable than a manual for faithful endurance.
