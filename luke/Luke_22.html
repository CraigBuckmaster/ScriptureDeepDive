Luke 22

The Last Supper, Gethsemane, Arrest, and Trial

Author

Luke, a Gentile physician (Col 4:14) and the most accomplished literary stylist in the New Testament. His Greek is the most polished in the NT corpus — modelled on the style of educated Hellenistic historians such as Thucydides and Polybius. Early and unanimous church tradition (Irenaeus c. AD 180, Clement of Alexandria, Origen, Eusebius, Jerome) identifies him as the author of both this Gospel and the Acts of the Apostles. He was a close companion of Paul (Phlm 24; 2 Tim 4:11, “only Luke is with me”), and the “we” passages in Acts (16:10–17; 20:5–15; 21:1–18; 27:1–28:16) confirm direct eyewitness participation in events he narrates. Luke is the only Gentile author in the canon. He explicitly describes his method in the prologue (1:1–4): careful investigation of all available tradition, consultation of eyewitnesses and ministers of the word, and orderly composition for a patron named Theophilus — a preface that conforms to the best conventions of Hellenistic historiography.

When written

c. AD 62–70, most likely during Paul’s two-year imprisonment in Caesarea (Acts 24:27, c. AD 57–59) or shortly thereafter, with publication before Acts (which presupposes it, Acts 1:1). A pre-AD 70 date is supported by the absence of any reference to the destruction of Jerusalem as a past event; the Gospel’s detailed prediction of the siege (21:20–24) reads as prophecy, not retrospective commentary. A minority of scholars favour AD 70–85, arguing that the precision of 21:20 reflects post-event knowledge. The two-volume work (Luke–Acts) is approximately 27,000 words — the largest single contribution to the NT by word count.

What prompted it

Luke writes for Theophilus (“friend of God”), probably a Gentile patron of high social standing (“most excellent,” Acts 1:1, a term used for Roman officials), who has already received some instruction in the faith but needs a reliable, ordered account to confirm its certainty (1:4). More broadly, Luke’s two-volume work addresses the expanding Gentile mission: How does the story of a Jewish Messiah become good news for all nations? His Gospel foregrounds the inclusion of the poor, women, Samaritans, and Gentiles; his Acts shows the gospel moving from Jerusalem to Rome. Luke also addresses a pastoral crisis: the delay of the Parousia. His travel narrative (9:51–19:28) reframes discipleship as a sustained journey of formation, not merely expectation of an imminent end. The Gospel’s persistent themes — prayer, joy, the Holy Spirit, reversal of fortune, universal salvation — all serve this dual apologetic and pastoral purpose.

Greek Word Study

diathēkē dia-THE-kē — covenant: The word (v.20) that interprets the Last Supper. The cup is the "new covenant in my blood" — the language of Jeremiah 31:31-34 fulfilled. A covenant is a binding agreement sealed in blood; Jesus is establishing the new covenant by his own death. Every subsequent Eucharist re-presents this covenant inauguration.

to hyper hymōn ekchynnomenon to hy-per hy-MON ek-chyn-NO-me-non — which is poured out for you: The present passive participle (v.20) is significant: "being poured out" — the shedding of blood is described as ongoing (liturgical present), not merely future. In the Supper, the disciples participate proleptically in what is about to happen at Golgotha.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:15

"I have eagerly desired to eat this Passover with you before I suffer" — the language of intense longing. Jesus desires the Last Supper not as a duty but as the moment toward which his entire ministry has been moving. This is the meal that will interpret his death; he has longed to eat it.

22:19-20

"This is my body given for you... This cup is the new covenant in my blood" — the words of institution. The bread is his body; the cup is the new covenant sealed in blood. Luke's version is close to Paul's in 1 Cor 11:24-25 (and different from Mark's), suggesting a common liturgical tradition. "Do this in remembrance of me" — not merely intellectual recall but a participatory re-presentation.

22:27

"I am among you as one who serves" — the climactic statement of Luke 22's service theme. Jesus does not say "I will serve" or "I came to serve" but "I am" — present tense, ongoing reality. The one at whom the disciples are reclining has been serving them all along. He is the table's host and its servant simultaneously.

22:31-32

"Simon, Simon, Satan has asked to sift all of you as wheat. But I have prayed for you, Simon, that your faith may not fail." The double address (Simon, Simon) signals solemnity. Satan's request echoes Job 1:6-12 — the adversary seeking permission to test. Jesus does not pray that Peter will not fail (he knows he will); he prays that Peter's faith will not ultimately fail. The distinction is between a temporary lapse and a permanent apostasy.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:19-20

Calvin insists that "This is my body" and "this cup is the new covenant in my blood" are not identities of substance (Rome) nor mere memorial symbols (Zwingli) but a sacramental union: the bread and cup are genuine means by which Christ offers himself to those who receive them in faith. The sign and the thing signified are distinct but inseparable in the sacrament.

22:31-32

Calvin uses the sifting-as-wheat image to argue that Satan's activity in the world is bounded by divine permission and overruled by divine intercession. Satan can test but not ultimately destroy. Christ's prayer for Peter is the guarantee of all believers' perseverance — not that they will not fall, but that their faith will not ultimately fail.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:19b-20

The longer text of the words of institution (including "given for you; do this in remembrance of me" and the full cup saying) is absent from some Western manuscripts (notably D and some Old Latin versions). NA28 includes the longer text on the strength of the overwhelming manuscript evidence. The shorter text was probably produced by scribal oversight or a desire to avoid a perceived repetition.

22:36

"If you don't have a sword, sell your cloak and buy one" — the command has generated extensive debate. Options: (1) literal instruction to arm for self-defence; (2) symbolic language for spiritual readiness; (3) fulfilment of Isa 53:12 (numbered with transgressors) by ensuring the disciples appeared armed. The "two swords are enough" response (v.38) suggests Jesus is not expecting literal military resistance.

22:3

Eisēlthen de Satanas — "Satan entered into Judas." Luke is direct about the supernatural dimension of the betrayal in a way the other Gospels also note (John 13:2, 27). This is not an excuse for Judas — he "consented" (synetheto, v.6, a deliberate agreement) — but an acknowledgement that the passion involves cosmic as well as human agents.

22:20

Kainē diathēkē — "new covenant." The adjective kainē means not merely "another" but qualitatively new, fresh, superior to what came before. The Sinai covenant is not merely renewed; it is superseded and fulfilled in a covenant of a different order.

Places

Garden of Gethsemane
31.7793 N, 35.2397 E
An olive grove on the lower western slope of the Mount of Olives. Gat shemanim means "oil press" in Aramaic. Jesus went there "as usual" (22:39) -- his habitual evening retreat during Passion Week. Here he prayed in anguish sweating like drops of blood (22:44), was strengthened by an angel, and was betrayed by Judas with a kiss and arrested by the Temple guard.
House of the High Priest (Caiaphas)
31.7699 N, 35.2286 E
Located in the upper city, the residence of Caiaphas served as venue for the preliminary Sanhedrin hearing (22:54-71). In the courtyard below, Peter warmed himself by a fire (22:55) and denied Jesus three times. Then the Lord turned and looked straight at Peter (22:61) -- across that same courtyard -- triggering Peter's bitter weeping.

Greek Word Study

thelēma the-LE-ma — will: The word at the centre of Gethsemane (v.42): "not my will, but yours be done." Jesus's prayer is not merely about outcome — will the cup be taken away? — but about alignment: his human will surrendered to the Father's. The thelēma of the Son perfectly mirrors the thelēma of the Father, and the submission of one to the other is the moment at which the atonement is sealed in the garden before it is enacted at the cross.

ho huios tou anthrōpou ho hy-IOS tou an-THRO-pou — the Son of Man: At the trial (v.69) Jesus uses the Son of Man title with its Daniel 7 resonance: "from now on, the Son of Man will be seated at the right hand of the mighty God." The precise moment of his greatest apparent humiliation is when he claims the most exalted position. The trial produces the clearest Christological statement in Luke.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:42

"Father, if you are willing, take this cup from me; yet not my will, but yours be done." The prayer is the fullest expression of Christ's humanity in the Gospel: genuine reluctance, genuine anguish, genuine submission. It is not performance. The eternal Son of God, in his humanity, shrinks from what the Father asks — and freely chooses to embrace it. This is the moment the atonement is won in the will before it is enacted in the body.

22:44

"His sweat was like drops of blood" — the phenomenon of hematidrosis, where extreme stress causes capillaries beneath the skin to rupture, producing blood-tinged sweat. Luke the physician (Col 4:14) is the only evangelist to record this detail. It confirms the genuine physiological reality of Jesus's agony.

22:61

"The Lord turned and looked straight at Peter" — one of the most poignant moments in all the Gospels. Jesus, newly arrested and in the high priest's courtyard, turns at the sound of the rooster's crow. His eyes meet Peter's. No word is spoken. Peter goes out and weeps bitterly. The look is not condemnation but the fulfilment of a prediction — and perhaps also the look that would hold Peter through his restoration.

22:69

"From now on, the Son of Man will be seated at the right hand of the mighty God" — Jesus's answer at the trial is a claim to Ps 110:1 and Dan 7:13 simultaneously. He is telling his judges that they are looking at the one who will be their judge. The moment of his lowest humiliation is the occasion for his highest claim. The council has heard enough; they need no other evidence.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:42

Calvin insists that the prayer "if you are willing, take this cup" is not a prayer that failed — as if God was unwilling and Jesus resigned himself to rejection. Rather, the conditional "if you are willing" is itself the content of the submission: Jesus is not pressing an alternative agenda but handing the outcome entirely to the Father. The prayer is heard; the submission is the hearing.

22:67-70

The council's two questions (Are you the Messiah? Are you the Son of God?) and Jesus's answers constitute a Christological declaration under oath at the most dangerous possible moment. Calvin notes that Jesus does not deny either claim; he refuses the framing of the first (they will not believe) and accepts the second in their own words ("You say that I am"). The confession under trial is the most costly confession in the Gospel.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:43-44

The angel strengthening Jesus and the sweat like blood are absent from some important manuscripts (p69vid, p75, Codex Alexandrinus, and others). They are present in Sinaiticus, Vaticanus, and many others. NA28 includes them with brackets. Most textual critics consider them original, perhaps omitted early because they seemed to compromise Christ's divinity.

22:70

"You say that I am" — the Greek sy legeis hoti egō eimi is ambiguous: it could mean "you say so" (a deflection) or "yes, that is what I am" (an affirmation in their words). The council clearly takes it as an affirmation — "we have heard it from his own lips." Most scholars read it as a qualified affirmation: "yes, though not quite in the terms you use."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Jesus
Servant, Sufferer, Son of Man
Luke 22 presents three faces of Jesus in sequence: the loving host who gives himself at the table, the obedient son who surrenders his will in the garden, and the calm confessor who claims the most exalted position in the moment of his greatest humiliation.
Judas
The betrayer
His betrayal is described with unusual restraint in Luke — no extended explanation, no psychological analysis. "Satan entered Judas" (22:3) and "he consented" (22:6) are together the full account.
Peter
The denier
Luke's Peter is warned with full foreknowledge ("I have prayed for you, Simon"), denies three times, and is undone by a single look from Jesus. His bitter weeping (22:62) is the beginning of the journey toward restoration (24:12, 34).
The Council
Sanhedrin
The assembly of chief priests and teachers of the law that constitutes the Jewish ruling body. Their trial of Jesus is conducted at daybreak (technically legal) and produces the confession that becomes the basis of the capital charge.

Reception History

Gethsemane in Christian Spiritual Direction
"Not my will, but yours be done" has been the foundation of the Christian tradition of conformity to divine will. Ignatius of Loyola placed it at the centre of the Spiritual Exercises; Thomas à Kempis built the Imitation of Christ around it; Luther's Heidelberg Disputation used it to argue for the theology of the cross over the theology of glory. The prayer is simultaneously the most human moment in the Gospels and its most redemptively significant.
The Lord's Supper in Christian Worship
Luke 22:19-20 (with 1 Cor 11:23-26) is the liturgical foundation of the Eucharist/Lord's Supper in all Christian traditions. The words of institution have been pronounced in Christian worship since the first century and are attested in the Didache (c. AD 100), Justin Martyr's First Apology (c. AD 150), and in every subsequent liturgical tradition. The debates over the presence of Christ in the elements have shaped the history of Christianity from the Reformation onward.

Literary Structure

The Servant King — vv.14-27The Last Supper discourse holds together two realities that the world considers opposites: king (he confers a kingdom, v.29) and servant (I am among you as one who serves, v.27). The meal is the place where this paradox is most fully displayed — he serves them bread and wine that are himself, seated at the table they will soon abandon.
Prophecy and Fulfilment — vv.21-22 / 47-48; vv.31-34 / 54-62The betrayal (foretold v.21-22, enacted v.47-48) and Peter's denial (foretold v.31-34, enacted v.54-62) both follow the same pattern: Jesus speaks with precise foreknowledge; the event unfolds exactly as described. The double fulfilment within the same chapter emphasises that nothing in the passion is accidental — it follows a predetermined pattern.

Greek Word Study

diathēkē dia-THE-ke — covenant: "This cup is the new covenant in my blood" (v.20). Diathēkē — covenant or testament. The Last Supper interprets Jesus's death through the lens of Jeremiah 31:31 ("a new covenant") and Exodus 24:8 ("the blood of the covenant"). The cup is not merely a memorial of death but the inauguration of the covenant relationship that the blood establishes. The "new" covenant does not abolish the Mosaic but fulfils and supersedes it.

peirasmos pei-RAS-mos — testing / trial / temptation: "Lead us not into testing" — the same word (peirasmos) appears in Gethsemane: "Pray that you will not fall into temptation/testing" (v.40, 46). The hour of the passion is the peirasmos that will scatter the disciples (v.31: Satan has asked to sift them like wheat). The disciples' failure to pray in Gethsemane leaves them unprepared for the test. The word bridges the Lord's Prayer (11:4) and the passion narrative.

hypostrepho hy-pos-TRE-pho — turn around / return: "And the Lord turned and looked straight at Peter" (v.61). The moment of the rooster's crow is also the moment Jesus turns (epistrapheis) and looks at Peter. The glance accomplishes what the cock's crow announces: Peter "went outside and wept bitterly." In Luke alone, Jesus is present to witness the denial and to respond to it with a look — the look that breaks Peter's heart and begins his restoration.

Textual Notes

22:19b-20Longer vs. shorter text of the institution narrative
The longer text (including "given for you; do this in remembrance of me" and the full second cup saying) is absent from Codex Bezae and some Old Latin versions. NA28 includes it on the strength of the overwhelming manuscript evidence and its presence in 1 Cor 11:24-25.
The longer text is almost certainly original. The shorter text may have been produced by liturgical revision (removing the double cup) or accidental haplography.
22:43-44The angel and the bloody sweat
Present in Sinaiticus, D, and many others; absent from p75, the corrector of Sinaiticus, and some Alexandrian MSS. NA28 includes with brackets.
Genuine textual uncertainty; the internal evidence (Luke's medical vocabulary, emphasis on prayer and humanity of Jesus) favours inclusion. The external evidence is evenly balanced.

Scholarly Debates

Is Luke 22:43-44 (the angel and the bloody sweat) original?
Original
Most textual critics, NA28 (with brackets)
The passage is present in many important early witnesses and absent mainly in Alexandrian manuscripts. Its omission may reflect early theological embarrassment about Christ's agony and its implications for his divinity. The stylistic features are consistent with Luke.
Later addition
Westcott-Hort, some scholars
The best Alexandrian witnesses (p75, the corrector of Sinaiticus) omit it. Early Church Fathers sometimes seem unaware of it. The theological difficulty it presents may explain its original absence rather than subsequent omission.
NA28's bracketed inclusion reflects genuine uncertainty. The passage is theologically consistent with Luke's emphasis on Jesus's humanity and prayer life, and its omission is more explicable on theological grounds than its inclusion.

Theological Themes Tracker

Luke 22 is the theological hinge of the Gospel — the night on which everything converges. At the table, Jesus gives himself in bread and wine and establishes the new covenant in his blood. In the garden, he surrenders his human will to the Father's. At the arrest, he heals the last person he will heal before the resurrection. In the courtyard, Peter denies him. Before the council, Jesus claims his exaltation at the precise moment of his humiliation. The chapter holds together the deepest contradiction of the Gospel: the Son of Man served, suffered, and was condemned — and by this became the eternal king.
