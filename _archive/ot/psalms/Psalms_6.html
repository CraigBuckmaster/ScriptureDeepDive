Psalms 6

A Cry in Anguish

Verses 1–5 — “LORD, Do Not Rebuke Me in Your Anger”
12345

Hebrew Word Study

חָנֵּנִי ḥānnēnî — be gracious to me / have mercy: "LORD, have mercy (ḥānnēnî) on me, for I am faint" (v.2). The first penitential psalm. David is physically broken: bones troubled, soul in anguish. The plea is not for justice but for mercy — grace when you deserve judgment.

Context

The first of seven penitential psalms (6, 32, 38, 51, 102, 130, 143). David is physically and spiritually broken. His bones are troubled (v.2), his soul is in anguish (v.3). The question: "how long, LORD, how long?" (v.3) — the Psalter’s recurring cry of impatience in suffering.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 38:1–4"LORD, do not rebuke me in your anger" — the same opening, the same penitential posture.
  • Heb 12:5–6"The Lord disciplines the one he loves" — the NT theology of divine correction.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1–2

"LORD, do not rebuke me in your anger. Have mercy on me, for I am faint; heal me, for my bones are in agony." The plea combines confession (implicit: I deserve rebuke) with appeal (explicit: have mercy).

6:3–5

"My soul is in deep anguish. How long, LORD, how long? Turn, LORD, and deliver me." The "how long?" is not rebellion but anguished faith. He asks God to turn — implying God has turned away.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:2

Alter: "my bones are shaken" — the physical vocabulary is not metaphorical. The psalmist’s body participates in his spiritual anguish.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1

Calvin: David asks for moderated discipline, not removal. Calvin: the request acknowledges God’s right to rebuke and asks only for restraint in its intensity.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:1

The NET: the parallel between "anger" and "hot displeasure" uses two different words for divine wrath, intensifying the plea.

6:5

The NET: "among the dead no one proclaims your name" — in Sheol, there is no worship. This is David’s argument for staying alive: the dead cannot praise.

Kidner Notes

kidner
6:1–3

Kidner: the first penitential psalm. David does not deny deserving rebuke; he asks that it be moderated. The plea is: discipline me, but not in fury.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
6:1–5

VanGemeren: the seven penitential psalms form a category the early church used in Lent. Psalm 6 is the first: raw, physical, urgent.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
6:3

Goldingay: "how long?" is asked over 20 times in the Psalter. It is the default question of suffering faith: not "why?" but "how much longer?"

Verses 6–10 — “The LORD Has Heard My Weeping”
678910

Hebrew Word Study

שָׁמַע shāmaʿ — to hear / to listen: "The LORD has heard (shāmaʿ) my cry for mercy" (v.9). The pivot from despair to confidence. David was drowning in tears (v.6) — flooding his bed, drenching his couch. Then the sudden turn: the LORD has heard. Past tense. The prayer was answered before the psalm ended.

Context

David’s tears are physical: he floods his bed, drenches his couch with weeping, his eyes grow weak with sorrow (vv.6–7). Then the dramatic turn: "Away from me, all you who do evil, for the LORD has heard my weeping" (v.8). From tears to triumph in one verse. The enemies will be ashamed and dismayed (v.10). The lament’s resolution comes not through changed circumstances but through the conviction that God heard.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 34:17–18"The LORD is close to the brokenhearted and saves those who are crushed in spirit" — the theology underlying Psalm 6’s resolution.
  • Rev 21:4"He will wipe every tear from their eyes" — the final answer to David’s weeping.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:6–7

"I am worn out from my groaning. All night long I flood my bed with weeping." Physical exhaustion from grief. The body processes sorrow.

6:8–9

"Away from me, all you who do evil, for the LORD has heard my weeping." The turn. Confidence breaks through despair. Jesus quotes this in Matthew 7:23.

6:10

"All my enemies will be overwhelmed with shame and anguish." The reversal: what David experienced (shame, anguish) will be redirected to his enemies.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:8

Alter: "away from me" is a dismissal of enemies spoken with sudden authority. The weeping man becomes the commanding man in a single breath.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:8–9

Calvin: the "LORD has heard" is the pivot on which the entire psalm turns. Calvin: prayer changes the one who prays before it changes the circumstances. David is the same person, in the same danger, but now confident.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:6

The NET: "flood my bed" uses the verb sahâ (to swim/overflow). The image is of tears literally pooling on the bed. Ancient sleep was on the floor — tears would collect.

6:8

The NET: Jesus echoes this verse in Matt 7:23 ("away from me, you evildoers"). The dismissal of evildoers from God’s presence is both Davidic and christological.

Kidner Notes

kidner
6:8–9

Kidner: the sudden turn from weeping to command is one of the Psalter’s characteristic moves. Faith does not wait for evidence; it declares what it has heard from God.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
6:8–10

VanGemeren: the abrupt shift from lament to confidence is the "certainty of being heard" — a feature of biblical laments where the psalmist receives inner assurance mid-prayer.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
6:6

Goldingay: "flood my bed with weeping" is hyperbole that communicates emotional truth. The exaggeration IS the accuracy — grief feels like drowning.
