Psalms 51

Have Mercy on Me, O God

Verses 1–12 — “Wash Me, and I Will Be Whiter Than Snow”
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

חָנֵּנִי ḥānnēnî — have mercy on me / be gracious to me: "Have mercy on me (ḥānnēnî), O God, according to your unfailing love; according to your great compassion blot out my transgressions" (v.1). THE penitential psalm. Written after Nathan confronted David about Bathsheba and Uriah (2 Sam 12). Three words for sin: transgression (peshaʿ, rebellion), iniquity (ʿāwōn, crookedness), sin (ḥaṭṭāʾâ, missing the mark). Three requests for cleansing: blot out (v.1), wash (כבס, v.2), cleanse (v.2). David’s confession is total: "Against you, you only, have I sinned" (v.4). "Surely I was sinful at birth, sinful from the time my mother conceived me" (v.5). "Create in me a pure heart, O God, and renew a steadfast spirit within me" (v.10). The verb "create" (bārāʾ) is used only for divine creation (Gen 1:1). David cannot clean himself; he needs a new creation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
51:1–2

"Have mercy on me, O God, according to your unfailing love." Three appeals: mercy (ḥānnēnî), unfailing love (ḥesed), great compassion (raḥamîm). David’s case rests entirely on God’s character, not his own.

51:3–4

"I know my transgressions; my sin is always before me. Against you, you only, have I sinned." The confession’s paradox: David sinned against Bathsheba, Uriah, and the nation — yet ultimately all sin is against God. The "you only" is theological, not relational.

51:5

"Surely I was sinful at birth, sinful from the time my mother conceived me." Not blaming his mother but acknowledging the depth: sin is not just behaviour but condition. Original sin in the Psalter.

51:7

"Cleanse me with hyssop, and I will be clean; wash me, and I will be whiter than snow." Hyssop was used to sprinkle blood at Passover (Exod 12:22) and in leprosy purification (Lev 14:6). David needs sacrificial, priestly cleansing.

51:10

"Create in me a pure heart, O God, and renew a steadfast spirit within me." Bārāʾ (create) — only God creates. David cannot repair himself; he needs to be remade from the inside.

51:11–12

"Do not cast me from your presence or take your Holy Spirit from me. Restore to me the joy of your salvation." David’s deepest fear: not punishment but loss of God’s presence. Not death but spiritual abandonment. He asks for restored joy, not restored circumstances.

Verses 13–19 — “The Sacrifices of God Are a Broken Spirit”
13141516171819

Hebrew Word Study

נִשְׁבָּרָה nishbārâ — broken / crushed: "My sacrifice, O God, is a broken spirit (nishbārâ); a broken and contrite heart you, God, will not despise" (v.17). The psalm’s theological climax. The sacrifice God wants is not an animal but a shattered self. Brokenness is the offering. God does not despise the crushed heart — he receives it. This transforms the entire sacrificial system: the real sacrifice was always internal.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
51:13

"Then I will teach transgressors your ways, so that sinners will turn back to you." Forgiven sinners become effective teachers. David’s testimony will convert others. The one who fell farthest speaks with the most authority about grace.

51:16–17

"You do not delight in sacrifice, or I would bring it. My sacrifice is a broken spirit; a broken and contrite heart you will not despise." God bypasses the sacrificial system to identify what he truly wants: a shattered, honest heart. Ritual without repentance is empty.

51:18–19

"May it please you to prosper Zion. Then there will be righteous sacrifices." David’s personal sin affected the nation. His prayer expands: restore Zion too. The private confession becomes a public intercession.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

David
King of Israel; after Nathan’s confrontation
Written after David’s adultery with Bathsheba and murder of Uriah (2 Sam 11–12). Nathan said "You are the man!" David’s response was this psalm: not defence but total confession.
