Psalms 33

Sing to the LORD a New Song

Verses 1–12 — “By the Word of the LORD the Heavens Were Made”
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

דְבַר dəbar — word: "By the word (dəbar) of the LORD the heavens were made, their starry host by the breath of his mouth" (v.6). Creation by speech. God spoke and it was. The word that made the heavens is the same word that addresses Israel in Torah. Creation and revelation share the same source.

Context

A hymn of praise. Sing a new song (v.3), for God’s word is right and true (v.4). By God’s word the heavens were made (v.6). Let all the earth fear the LORD (v.8). He spoke, and it came to be (v.9). God foils the plans of nations (v.10) but his plans stand forever (v.11). "Blessed is the nation whose God is the LORD" (v.12).

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 1:3"And God said, ‘Let there be light’" — creation by divine speech.
  • John 1:1–3"In the beginning was the Word... through him all things were made" — the Word theology developed.
  • Heb 11:3"The universe was formed at God’s command" — faith and creation by word.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
33:6

"By the word of the LORD the heavens were made." Six words that contain all of cosmology. The medium of creation is speech.

33:9

"He spoke, and it came to be; he commanded, and it stood firm." The power of divine speech: words become reality.

33:11–12

"The plans of the LORD stand firm forever. Blessed is the nation whose God is the LORD." Human plans fail; God’s endure. National blessing depends on divine allegiance.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
33:6

Alter: "by the breath of his mouth" parallels "by the word." Word and breath are the same act: speech is shaped breath. Creation is God exhaling purposefully.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
33:12

Calvin: "blessed is the nation whose God is the LORD" — Calvin: national blessing is theological, not military or economic. The nation that acknowledges God has the only asset that endures.

Verses 13–22 — “The Eyes of the LORD Are on Those Who Fear Him”
13141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

עֵינֵי ʿēnē — the eyes of: "The eyes (ʿēnē) of the LORD are on those who fear him, on those whose hope is in his unfailing love" (v.18). The divine gaze: not surveillance but care. God watches TO deliver, TO preserve life, TO keep alive in famine. The eyes that surveyed creation (v.13–14) now focus on the faithful.

Context

God looks down from heaven and sees all humanity (vv.13–15). No king is saved by the size of his army; no warrior escapes by great strength (v.16). A horse is vain hope for deliverance (v.17). But: God’s eyes are on the God-fearing (v.18). He delivers from death and sustains in famine (v.19). The psalm closes: "We wait in hope for the LORD; he is our help and our shield. In him our hearts rejoice. May your unfailing love be upon us, LORD, even as we put our hope in you" (vv.20–22).

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 20:7"Some trust in chariots, some in horses" — the same trust-in-God-not-military theology.
  • Prov 21:31"The horse is made ready for battle, but victory rests with the LORD" — the same principle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
33:16–17

"No king is saved by the size of his army. A horse is a vain hope for deliverance." Military power is insufficient. Salvation comes from God alone.

33:18–19

"The eyes of the LORD are on those who fear him. He delivers them from death and keeps them alive in famine." The divine gaze is protective: not watching to condemn but watching to save.

33:20–22

"We wait in hope for the LORD. May your unfailing love rest upon us, even as we put our hope in you." Hope and love are reciprocal: we hope; God loves. The relationship is mutual trust.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
33:17

Alter: "a horse is a vain hope" — the Hebrew sheker (false/deceptive). The horse deceives: it looks like salvation but isn’t.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
33:18–19

Calvin: God’s eyes on the faithful are the counterpart to God’s eyes on all humanity (v.13). He sees everyone; he watches over the God-fearing specifically. Universal knowledge, particular care.

33:22

Calvin: "even as we put our hope in you" — the "even as" is proportional: the measure of our hope is the measure of love we experience.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
33:18

The NET: "whose hope is in his unfailing love" (ḥesed) — the hope is not abstract optimism but confidence grounded in God’s demonstrated covenant loyalty.

33:17

The NET: sheker (vain/false) means the horse is a lying promise. It looks strong but cannot deliver on its implied guarantee.

Kidner Notes

kidner
33:16–17

Kidner: the horse proverb echoes Psalm 20:7. The Psalter repeats itself because Israel kept forgetting: armies do not save; God saves.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
33:18

VanGemeren: "the eyes of the LORD" is intimate surveillance — not Big Brother but Good Shepherd. God’s watching is protection, not oppression.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
33:20–22

Goldingay: the psalm ends with communal waiting. Not individual piety but corporate hope: "we wait... our hearts rejoice... upon us."
