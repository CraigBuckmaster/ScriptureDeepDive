Psalms 31

Into Your Hands I Commit My Spirit

Verses 1–18 — “Be My Rock of Refuge”
123456789101112131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

רוּחִי rūḥî — my spirit: "Into your hands I commit my spirit (rūḥî)" (v.5). Jesus’ last words from the cross (Luke 23:46). David entrusts his entire being — breath, life-force, inner self — to God’s keeping. The ultimate act of trust: giving your spirit to the one who gave it.

חֶסֶד ḥesed — steadfast love / loyal love: "How abundant are the good things that you have stored up for those who fear you" (v.19). The psalm alternates between desperate lament and extravagant confidence. David is besieged, forgotten like the dead, surrounded by terror (vv.9–13). Yet: "I trust in you, LORD; I say, ‘You are my God.’ My times are in your hands" (vv.14–15).

Context

David takes refuge in God (v.1), asks for deliverance (v.2), commits his spirit (v.5), and then describes his distress: eyes weak with sorrow, life consumed by anguish, strength failing, bones wasting (vv.9–10). He is forgotten like the dead, broken like pottery (v.12). Terror on every side (v.13). Yet the pivot: "But I trust in you, LORD. My times are in your hands" (vv.14–15).

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 23:46"Father, into your hands I commit my spirit" — Jesus’ final words quoting Psalm 31:5.
  • Jer 20:10"I hear many whispering, ‘Terror on every side!’" — Jeremiah echoes David’s language.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:5

"Into your hands I commit my spirit." Jesus made this his dying prayer. The most sacred sentence in the Psalter — the last words of the Son of God.

31:12

"I am forgotten as though I were dead; I have become like broken pottery." The imagery: a dead person erased from memory, a smashed pot beyond repair.

31:14–15

"But I trust in you, LORD. My times are in your hands." The great "but" of faith. Times — all of them, every season, including death — belong to God.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:5

Alter: the brevity is its power. Five Hebrew words. No explanation, no negotiation. Pure transfer of trust.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
31:5

Calvin: this is the prayer every believer should die with. Calvin: if the Son committed his spirit to the Father, so should every child of God.

31:15

Calvin: "my times" includes the time of death. Calvin: whoever can say "my times are in your hands" has conquered the fear of death.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
31:5

The NET: rūaḥ (spirit) is the breath/life-force God gave at creation (Gen 2:7). Committing it back is returning what was always God’s.

31:13

The NET: "terror on every side" (māgōr missābîb) becomes Jeremiah’s signature phrase (Jer 6:25; 20:3, 10; 46:5). David coined it; Jeremiah lived it.

Kidner Notes

kidner
31:5

Kidner: Jesus’ use of this verse transformed it from a psalm of refuge into the prayer of the dying. Every Christian who has died reciting it follows Christ.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
31:5

VanGemeren: "into your hands" places the entire person — spirit, body, future — under divine custody. The hands that formed creation hold the spirit that returns to them.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
31:15

Goldingay: "my times are in your hands" is the psalm’s theological centre. Not my plans, not my schedule, not my preferred timeline — but my actual times, as God assigns them.

Verses 19–24 — “How Great Is Your Goodness”
192021222324

Hebrew Word Study

טוּב ṭūb — goodness / good things: "How abundant is your goodness (ṭūb) that you have stored up for those who fear you" (v.19). Stored up — like treasure reserved, accumulated, waiting. God’s goodness is not improvised but prepared in advance. The suffering of vv.9–18 is answered by the hidden treasure of v.19.

Context

The psalm’s second half erupts in praise. God’s goodness is stored up (v.19), his people are sheltered in his presence from intrigues and accusation (v.20). "Praise be to the LORD, for he showed me the wonders of his love when I was in a city under siege" (v.21). The closing exhortation: "Be strong and take heart, all you who hope in the LORD" (v.24).

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 8:28"In all things God works for the good of those who love him" — the stored-up goodness theology.
  • Eph 1:3–4"Every spiritual blessing in Christ" — blessings stored up before the foundation of the world.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:19

"How abundant is your goodness, stored up for those who fear you." Goodness exists before you experience it. It is already prepared.

31:21

"He showed me the wonders of his love when I was in a city under siege." The most desperate circumstances are when God’s love becomes most wonderful.

31:24

"Be strong and take heart, all you who hope in the LORD." The psalm ends with a call to courage for all who wait.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:19

Alter: "stored up" (ṣāfantā) is the verb for hiding treasure. God’s goodness is cached, secured, reserved for the right moment.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
31:19

Calvin: the stored goodness means that suffering does not indicate God’s poverty. He has resources the sufferer cannot yet see. Faith trusts the treasury before seeing the withdrawal.

31:24

Calvin: "be strong and take heart" is addressed to the congregation. Calvin: one person’s testimony of deliverance strengthens the entire community.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
31:19

The NET: ṣāfan means to hide, to treasure up, to store away. The same root appears in Proverbs 2:7: "he holds success in store for the upright."

31:24

The NET: the imperative "be strong" (ḥizqū) is the same verb used for Joshua: "be strong and courageous" (Josh 1:6). The call to courage echoes the conquest.

Kidner Notes

kidner
31:19

Kidner: "stored up" is the language of hidden treasure. The suffering person does not see the goodness yet — but it is already there, laid aside, waiting.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
31:19–24

VanGemeren: the stored-goodness theology transforms suffering: what looks like absence is actually accumulation. God is preparing what he will reveal.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
31:24

Goldingay: the exhortation shifts from David’s personal testimony to communal encouragement. His experience becomes everyone’s resource.
