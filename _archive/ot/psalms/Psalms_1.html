Psalms 1

The Two Ways

Verses 1–3 — “Blessed Is the One” — The Righteous as a Tree
123

Hebrew Word Study

אַשְׁרֵי ʾashre — blessed / happy / fortunate: "Blessed (ʾashre) is the one who does not walk in step with the wicked" (v.1). The Psalter’s first word is ʾashre — not a prayer or a command but a declaration of blessing. The word describes a state of flourishing, not just emotional happiness. The blessed person avoids three progressions: walking → standing → sitting with the wicked.

תּוֹרָה torâ — instruction / law / Torah: "But whose delight is in the law (torâ) of the LORD" (v.2). Torâ means instruction, guidance, teaching — broader than legal code. The blessed person meditates on it day and night. The tree image follows: planted by water, yielding fruit, leaf unfading. Torah is the water; the person is the tree.

Context

Psalm 1 is the gateway to the entire Psalter. It establishes the fundamental choice: the way of the righteous or the way of the wicked. The righteous person is compared to a tree planted by streams of water (v.3) — rooted, fruitful, enduring. The psalm is not a prayer but a wisdom poem, linking the Psalter to the wisdom tradition (Proverbs, Job, Ecclesiastes).

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 17:7–8"Blessed is the one who trusts in the LORD... like a tree planted by the water" — the same tree image.
  • Josh 1:8"Meditate on it day and night" — the same Torah-meditation command.
  • Matt 7:24–27The wise and foolish builders — Jesus’ version of the two ways.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

"Blessed is the one who does not walk in step with the wicked." Three verbs of increasing commitment: walk, stand, sit. The descent into sin is gradual, not sudden.

1:2

"But whose delight is in the law of the LORD, and who meditates on his law day and night." Meditation is not emptying the mind but filling it — chewing on Torah continuously.

1:3

"That person is like a tree planted by streams of water, which yields its fruit in season." The tree is planted, not wild. God positioned it. The fruit comes in season — not on demand.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:1

Alter: the three-stage descent (walking → standing → sitting) is matched by three categories of sinners (wicked → sinners → scoffers). The parallelism tightens as the commitment deepens.

1:3

Alter: "planted" (shātúl) implies transplantation — moved from one location to another by deliberate act. God repositions the righteous beside life-giving water.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1–2

Calvin: the blessed person’s delight is in Torah, not in self-effort. The tree’s fruitfulness is passive — it receives water and bears fruit. So the righteous receive grace and produce righteousness. The order matters: delight first, fruit follows.

1:3

Calvin: "yields its fruit in season" — Calvin: patience is required. Not all seasons are harvest. Some are growth, some are rest, some are waiting. Fruitfulness has a rhythm.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:1

The NET: ʾashre is a plural noun used as an exclamation: "happinesses of..." The blessed state is multifaceted, not singular.

1:3

The NET: "streams of water" (palge-mayim) are irrigation channels, not natural streams. The tree’s water supply is engineered, reliable, and abundant.

Kidner Notes

kidner
1:1

Kidner: the three verbs (walk, stand, sit) trace a progression from casual association to settled identification with evil. Sin begins with a walk and ends with a seat.

1:3

Kidner: the tree by water channels is irrigated — deliberately planted where water is guaranteed. The righteous person’s vitality is not accidental but cultivated.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
1:1–3

VanGemeren: Psalm 1 is the Psalter’s hermeneutical key. It teaches the reader how to read every psalm that follows: through the lens of Torah-obedience and the two-ways theology.

1:2

VanGemeren: "meditates day and night" uses the verb hāgâ — to mutter, recite, ponder. Torah meditation is vocal and continuous, not silent and occasional.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
1:1

Goldingay: the psalm opens with the negative (what NOT to do) before the positive (Torah delight). Avoidance precedes embrace. You must leave the wrong path before finding the right one.

1:3

Goldingay: "whatever they do prospers" is a general principle, not an absolute promise. The wisdom tradition knows exceptions (Job, Ecclesiastes). Psalm 1 states the norm; other psalms explore the exceptions.

Verses 4–6 — “Not So the Wicked” — Chaff Driven by Wind
456

Hebrew Word Study

מֹץ mōṣ — chaff: "Not so the wicked! They are like chaff (mōṣ) that the wind blows away" (v.4). The contrast is total: tree vs chaff. Rooted vs rootless. Fruitful vs empty. Enduring vs blown away. Chaff is the waste product of threshing — weightless husks separated from the grain. The wicked have no substance.

Context

The psalm’s second half inverts every image from the first. The righteous are rooted; the wicked are airborne. The righteous bear fruit; the wicked are waste. The righteous endure judgment; the wicked cannot stand (v.5). The LORD "watches over" (knows, attends to) the way of the righteous, but the way of the wicked leads to destruction (v.6). The psalm’s final word is “perish” — the Psalter opens with blessing and closes its first poem with warning.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 3:12"His winnowing fork is in his hand, and he will clear his threshing floor" — the same chaff-and-grain separation.
  • Ps 37:1–2"Do not fret because of the wicked... they will soon wither like the grass" — the same impermanence.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:4–5

"Not so the wicked! They are like chaff. Therefore the wicked will not stand in the judgment." Chaff has no weight, no root, no future. It cannot survive the winnowing.

1:6

"The LORD watches over the way of the righteous, but the way of the wicked leads to destruction." The psalm ends with two destinies: one watched by God, one dissolving into nothing.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:4

Alter: the chaff image is agricultural: after threshing, the grain falls heavy while the husks are caught by any breeze. The wicked are literally insubstantial.

1:6

Alter: the symmetry of v.6 is precise: "the LORD knows the way of the righteous / but the way of the wicked will perish." Knows vs perishes. Divine attention vs annihilation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:5

Calvin: "the wicked will not stand in the judgment" — they cannot endure scrutiny. The last judgment is the ultimate threshing floor. Calvin: only those rooted in Christ’s righteousness can stand.

1:6

Calvin: two ways, two destinations, one God who distinguishes. Calvin: the psalm is not about human achievement but divine discrimination — God knows his own.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:4

The NET: mōṣ (chaff) is the light husk separated from grain during winnowing. In the ANE, threshing floors were placed on hilltops to catch the wind that blew the chaff away.

1:6

The NET: yōdēaʿ (knows/watches over) carries the full weight of Hebrew "knowing" — experiential, relational, covenantal. God does not merely observe; he enters into the righteous person’s journey.

Kidner Notes

kidner
1:4

Kidner: "not so" is emphatically brief. After three verses of the righteous, the wicked receive two words of dismissal before the chaff image. The asymmetry is the point: substance vs emptiness.

1:6

Kidner: "the LORD watches over" (yōdēaʿ) means knows intimately — attends to, cares for. The righteous path is personally known to God. The wicked path has no such companion.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
1:4–6

VanGemeren: the two ways establish the Psalter’s moral framework. Every psalm that follows assumes this binary: there are those who trust God and those who do not. The categories are not always neat — the lament psalms show the righteous in crisis — but the fundamental orientation holds.

1:6

VanGemeren: "watches over" (yōdēaʿ) is covenantal language. God knows his people as a husband knows his wife (Gen 4:1). The relationship is intimate, not merely cognitive.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
1:4

Goldingay: chaff is the perfect anti-image to the tree. Everything the tree has (roots, fruit, leaves, permanence), the chaff lacks. The psalm’s poetry is architectural: each half mirrors and inverts the other.

1:6

Goldingay: "perish" (ʾabad) is the psalm’s last word. The Psalter’s first poem ends with destruction. This is not vindictiveness but honesty: the way without God has no destination.
