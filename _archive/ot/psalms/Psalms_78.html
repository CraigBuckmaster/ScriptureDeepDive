Psalms 78

Give Ear, O My People, to My Teaching

Verses 1–39 — “The Things Our Ancestors Have Told Us”
123456789101112131415161718192021222324252627282930313233343536373839

Hebrew Word Study

מָשָׁל māshāl — parable / proverb / wisdom poem: "I will open my mouth with a parable (māshāl); I will utter hidden things, things from of old" (v.2). Jesus quotes this in Matthew 13:35. The longest historical psalm (72 verses). Asaph retells Israel’s history as a cautionary tale: the exodus, the wilderness rebellions, the manna and quail, the plagues on Egypt. The purpose: "so the next generation would know" (v.6). History is pedagogy. The first half traces God’s faithfulness against Israel’s stubbornness: they tested God in the wilderness (v.18), forgot his deeds (v.11), turned back like a faulty bow (v.57).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
78:1–4

"We will tell the next generation the praiseworthy deeds of the LORD. He decreed statutes for Jacob... which he commanded our ancestors to teach their children." Intergenerational faith transmission: the previous generation’s story becomes the next generation’s foundation.

78:7–8

"Then they would put their trust in God and not forget his deeds. They would not be like their ancestors — a stubborn and rebellious generation." The purpose of history: produce trust, prevent repetition.

78:19–25

"They spoke against God: ‘Can God really spread a table in the wilderness?’ He rained down manna; he gave them the grain of heaven." The test: can God provide? The answer: manna. Human doubt, divine abundance.

Verses 40–72 — “He Chose David His Servant”

Hebrew Word Study

דָוִד Dāwîd — David: "He chose David (Dāwîd) his servant and took him from the sheep pens... to be the shepherd of his people Jacob" (vv.70–71). The psalm recounts Egypt’s plagues (vv.44–51), the wilderness wandering (vv.52–55), Israel’s continued rebellion even in the promised land (vv.56–58), and God’s judgment: Shiloh rejected, the ark captured (vv.60–64). Then the turn: God awoke as from sleep (v.65), chose Judah and Mount Zion (vv.68–69), and selected David the shepherd to be king (vv.70–72). History moves toward David.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
78:40–42

"How often they rebelled against him in the wilderness. Again and again they put God to the test. They did not remember his power." Repetitive rebellion: the pattern never breaks on its own. Human nature repeats; grace must interrupt.

78:60–61

"He abandoned the tabernacle of Shiloh, the tent he had set up among humans. He sent the ark of his might into captivity." God withdraws from Shiloh — the most shocking event in Israel’s pre-monarchic history.

78:70–72

"He chose David his servant and took him from the sheep pens; from tending the sheep he brought him to be the shepherd of his people. And David shepherded them with integrity of heart; with skilful hands he led them." The psalm’s climax: from sheep pens to throne. David the shepherd shepherds God’s people. Integrity and skill: character and competence.
