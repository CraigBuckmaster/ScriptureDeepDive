Psalms 103

Praise the LORD, My Soul

Verses 1–12 — “As Far as the East Is from the West”
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

רַחַם raḥam — to have compassion / to show mercy: "As a father has compassion (raḥam) on his children, so the LORD has compassion on those who fear him" (v.13). The greatest praise psalm. David commands his soul: praise! (v.1). Then the catalogue of benefits: forgives ALL sins, heals ALL diseases, redeems life from the pit, crowns with love and compassion, satisfies with good things, renews youth like the eagle’s (vv.2–5). God is slow to anger, abounding in love (v.8). He does not treat us as our sins deserve (v.10). "As far as the east is from the west, so far has he removed our transgressions from us" (v.12). The most beloved forgiveness verse in the Bible. East and west never meet — the distance is infinite.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
103:1–2

"Praise the LORD, my soul; all my inmost being, praise his holy name. Praise the LORD, my soul, and forget not all his benefits." Self-command to praise. "Forget not" — the antidote to ingratitude.

103:3–5

"Who forgives ALL your sins and heals ALL your diseases, who redeems your life from the pit, who crowns you with love and compassion, who satisfies your desires with good things so that your youth is renewed like the eagle’s." Five benefits: forgiveness, healing, redemption, crowning, satisfying. ALL is emphasized.

103:8–10

"The LORD is compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, abounding in love. He does not treat us as our sins deserve." The Exodus 34:6 formula again. Grace exceeds justice.

103:11–12

"As high as the heavens are above the earth, so great is his love. As far as the east is from the west, so far has he removed our transgressions." Two unmeasurable distances: heaven-to-earth (vertical love) and east-to-west (horizontal removal). Infinite love, infinite forgiveness.

Verses 13–22 — “From Everlasting to Everlasting the LORD’s Love Is with Those Who Fear Him”
13141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

חָצִיר ḥāṣîr — grass / vegetation: "The life of mortals is like grass (ḥāṣîr); they flourish like a flower of the field; the wind blows over it and it is gone, and its place remembers it no more" (vv.15–16). Human transience: grass, flower, wind — gone. BUT: "from everlasting to everlasting the LORD’s love is with those who fear him" (v.17). Human life is brief; God’s love is eternal. The psalm closes by expanding the praise: angels, heavenly hosts, all his works, everywhere in his dominion — "Praise the LORD, my soul" (vv.20–22). The final word mirrors the first: the psalm is a circle of praise.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
103:13–14

"As a father has compassion on his children, so the LORD has compassion on those who fear him; for he knows how we are formed, he remembers that we are dust." The tenderness: God knows our composition. He remembers what we forget: we are dust. His expectations are calibrated to our capacity.

103:15–17

"The life of mortals is like grass — the wind blows over it and it is gone. But from everlasting to everlasting the LORD’s love is with those who fear him." The contrast: grass vs forever. Human life vs divine love. Transience vs eternity. We pass; love endures.

103:19–22

"The LORD has established his throne in heaven. Praise the LORD, you his angels. Praise the LORD, all his heavenly hosts. Praise the LORD, all his works everywhere in his dominion. Praise the LORD, my soul." The expanding circle: self → angels → heavenly hosts → all works everywhere. Then back to self: "praise the LORD, my soul." The universe praises; and so do I.
