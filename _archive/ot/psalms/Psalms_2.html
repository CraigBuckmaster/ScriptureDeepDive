Psalms 2

The LORD’s Anointed

Verses 1–6 — “Why Do the Nations Conspire?”
123456

Hebrew Word Study

מָשִׁיחַ māshîaḥ — anointed one / Messiah: "Against the LORD and against his anointed (māshîaḥ)" (v.2). The first appearance of "Messiah" in the Psalter. The nations rage against God AND his anointed king. The psalm is both historical (David’s kingship) and prophetic (Christ’s kingdom). The NT quotes it more than any other psalm.

Context

The nations conspire, the rulers plot — against God and his anointed (vv.1–2). Their aim: to break free from divine rule (v.3). God’s response: laughter from heaven (v.4). Then rebuke and terror (v.5). Then the declaration: "I have installed my king on Zion, my holy mountain" (v.6). The political rebellion is futile because God’s king is already enthroned.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 4:25–28The early church quotes Psalm 2:1–2 and identifies the nations as Herod and Pilate conspiring against Christ.
  • Rev 19:15–16"He will rule them with an iron sceptre" — Revelation quotes Psalm 2 for the returning Christ.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1–3

"Why do the nations conspire? The kings of the earth rise up against the LORD and against his anointed." Political rebellion against God is as old as nations. The conspiracy is both specific (against David) and universal (against Christ).

2:4–6

"The One enthroned in heaven laughs. He rebukes them in his anger. I have installed my king on Zion." God’s response to rebellion is not anxiety but laughter. The king is already installed; the conspiracy is too late.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:1

Alter: "why do the nations conspire?" — the question expects no rational answer. The conspiracy is futile by definition because it opposes the Creator.

2:4

Alter: God’s laughter is the psalm’s most striking image. The tone shifts from political urgency to divine comedy. The threat is real to humans; it is absurd to God.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1–2

Calvin: the conspiracy against Christ is the ultimate form of the nations’ rage. Calvin: every rejection of the gospel is a replay of Psalm 2. The nations still conspire; God still laughs.

2:6

Calvin: "I have installed my king" is the divine past tense: the enthronement is accomplished before the rebellion even begins. Christ’s kingdom is not a response to opposition; it precedes it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:2

The NET: māshîaḥ (anointed) refers historically to the Davidic king anointed with oil. The term becomes the title "Messiah" (Hebrew) / "Christ" (Greek).

2:4

The NET: "the one enthroned in heaven" uses the participle yōshēb — the one who sits permanently. God’s throne is not temporary; the nations’ rebellions are.

Kidner Notes

kidner
2:1–2

Kidner: the psalm assumes a cosmic conflict between human autonomy and divine sovereignty. The nations want freedom from God’s rule; God has already settled the matter.

2:4

Kidner: God’s laughter is not amusement but contempt for pretension. The Creator laughs at creatures who imagine they can overthrow him.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
2:1–6

VanGemeren: Psalm 2 pairs with Psalm 1 as the Psalter’s double introduction: Psalm 1 addresses the individual (righteous vs wicked), Psalm 2 addresses the nations (rebellion vs submission). Together they frame everything that follows.

2:6

VanGemeren: "my king on Zion" is the Davidic king, but the NT consistently reads this as Christ. The psalm operates on both levels simultaneously.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
2:1–3

Goldingay: the nations’ conspiracy is described with four verbs: conspire, plot, rise up, band together. The escalation suggests increasing organised resistance — which God treats as laughable.

2:4–6

Goldingay: the shift from earth to heaven (v.4) reframes everything. What looks threatening from below looks comic from above. Perspective determines response.

Verses 7–12 — “You Are My Son”; “Kiss the Son”
789101112

Hebrew Word Study

בְנִי bənî — my son: "You are my son (bənî); today I have become your father" (v.7). The divine decree of sonship. Historically: the Davidic king adopted as God’s son at coronation. Prophetically: Christ’s eternal sonship declared. The NT applies this verse to Jesus at his baptism (Matt 3:17), transfiguration (Matt 17:5), and resurrection (Acts 13:33; Heb 1:5).

נַשְׁקוּ nashqū — kiss: "Kiss (nashqū) the son, lest he be angry" (v.12). The kiss of submission — a vassal’s gesture of loyalty to the king. To "kiss the son" is to submit to God’s anointed. Refusal provokes wrath; submission brings blessing. The psalm ends where Psalm 1 began: "blessed" (ʾashre).

Context

God declares the king his son (v.7), promises the nations as inheritance (v.8), and authorises iron-sceptre rule (v.9). Then the warning to the kings: be wise, serve the LORD with fear, kiss the son (vv.10–12). The psalm closes with the same word that opened Psalm 1: "blessed" (ʾashre) — blessed are all who take refuge in him. The two-psalm introduction is complete: from individual blessing (Ps 1) to cosmic kingdom (Ps 2), both ending in ʾashre.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 3:17"This is my Son, whom I love" — the Father’s declaration echoing Psalm 2:7.
  • Heb 1:5"You are my Son; today I have become your Father" — applied to Christ’s superiority over angels.
  • Acts 13:33Paul applies Psalm 2:7 to Jesus’ resurrection: "You are my son; today I have become your Father."
  • Rev 2:26–27"To the one who is victorious... I will give authority over the nations — ‘ruling them with an iron sceptre’" — quoting Ps 2:9.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:7

"You are my son; today I have become your father." The decree of sonship. Applied to every Davidic king at coronation, but uniquely fulfilled in Christ.

2:8–9

"Ask me, and I will make the nations your inheritance. You will break them with a rod of iron." The son’s authority is global. The iron sceptre is judgment on rebellion.

2:10–12

"Kiss the son, or he will be angry. Blessed are all who take refuge in him." The choice: submit or perish. The psalm’s final word is "blessed" — connecting back to Psalm 1:1.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:7

Alter: "today I have begotten you" uses adoption language. The king becomes God’s son at the moment of enthronement. The metaphor is familial, not biological.

2:12

Alter: "kiss the son" — the verb nashaq means to kiss in homage. The psalm’s final imperative is an act of the body, not just the mind: physically submit to the anointed.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:7

Calvin: the sonship decree applies to David typologically and to Christ ultimately. Calvin: every promise to the Davidic king is a down payment on the promise to Christ. Psalm 2 is messianic to its core.

2:12

Calvin: "blessed are all who take refuge in him" — the only safety is in the Son. Calvin: this is the gospel in the OT: trust in God’s anointed is the path to blessing.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:7

The NET: "today I have become your father" uses the verb yālad (beget/give birth). In the royal context, this is adoption language — the king is declared God’s son at coronation.

2:12

The NET: "kiss the son" — the word "son" here is bar (Aramaic) rather than the Hebrew bēn. This is unusual and debated; some read "kiss purely" instead. Most translations retain "kiss the son."

Kidner Notes

kidner
2:7

Kidner: "today" is the coronation day for the Davidic king, but for Christ it encompasses eternity. The decree is not a moment but a reality. Hebrews applies it to the resurrection.

2:12

Kidner: "kiss the son" is the vassal’s allegiance-kiss. In ANE diplomacy, kissing the sovereign’s feet signified submission. The psalm demands the same toward God’s king.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
2:7

VanGemeren: the sonship decree is the psalm’s christological centre. Every Davidic king was a shadow; Christ is the substance. The "today" that began at each coronation finds its eternal fulfilment in the incarnation.

2:12

VanGemeren: "blessed are all who take refuge in him" closes the two-psalm introduction. Psalm 1: blessed is the individual who delights in Torah. Psalm 2: blessed are all who take refuge in the Son. Torah and Messiah are the twin foundations of the Psalter.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
2:7–9

Goldingay: the decree is direct divine speech. God addresses the king with "you are my son" and offers the nations. The extravagance of the offer (all nations) hints that the fulfilment exceeds any historical king.

2:12

Goldingay: "take refuge" (hāsâ) is one of the Psalter’s key verbs. The psalms repeatedly return to God as refuge, shelter, fortress, rock. This is the relational word that balances the psalm’s political language.

People of the Chapter

David
King of Israel; traditional author
Psalm 2 was likely composed for a Davidic coronation. The king is anointed, enthroned, and declared God’s son. The NT applies every element to Christ.
