Psalms 23

The LORD Is My Shepherd

Verses 1–4 — “I Shall Not Want” — The Shepherd
1234

Hebrew Word Study

רֹעִי rōʿî — my shepherd: "The LORD is my shepherd (rōʿî), I lack nothing" (v.1). The most beloved verse in the Bible. Six words in Hebrew. The shepherd provides (v.1), restores (v.3), and leads (v.3). Even in the valley of the shadow of death, the shepherd’s rod and staff comfort (v.4). The psalm moves from third person ("he leads me") to second person ("you are with me") at the moment of greatest danger — intimacy deepens in darkness.

צַלְמָוֶת ṣalmāwet — shadow of death / deep darkness: "Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death (ṣalmāwet), I will fear no evil, for you are with me" (v.4). Ṣalmāwet may be a compound word (shadow + death) or a single word meaning "deep darkness." Either way: the darkest possible valley, walked through, not avoided. "Through" is the key preposition: the valley is traversed, not inhabited.

Context

The Psalter’s most famous psalm. David the former shepherd describes God as his shepherd. Green pastures, still waters, restored soul, right paths (vv.2–3). The crisis comes in v.4: the valley of death’s shadow. But the shift from "he" to "you" at this point is the psalm’s most intimate literary move: in the darkest valley, God becomes "you" — addressed directly, not described.

Cross-Reference

  • John 10:11"I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep" — Jesus claims to be the shepherd of Psalm 23.
  • Ezek 34:11–16"I myself will search for my sheep and look after them" — God as shepherd in the prophets.
  • Rev 7:17"The Lamb at the centre of the throne will be their shepherd" — the eschatological shepherd.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
23:1

"The LORD is my shepherd, I lack nothing." Complete provision. The shepherd metaphor: God provides, protects, guides.

23:2–3

"He makes me lie down in green pastures, he leads me beside quiet waters, he restores my soul." Rest, refreshment, restoration — the shepherd’s three gifts.

23:4

"Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for you are with me." The shift from "he" to "you" at the point of maximum danger is the psalm’s literary masterpiece.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:1

Alter: six Hebrew words. The brevity is the power. No qualification, no condition, no exception. The LORD is my shepherd. I lack nothing. Full stop.

23:4

Alter: ṣalmāwet is either "shadow of death" (compound) or "deep darkness" (superlative). Both readings work: the valley is either death’s shadow or the deepest possible gloom.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:1

Calvin: "I shall not want" is not a prediction of ease but a declaration of sufficiency. Calvin: the sheep may still be hungry, thirsty, or threatened — but never ultimately lacking, because the Shepherd is there.

23:4

Calvin: "for you are with me" — the reason for fearlessness is not the absence of evil but the presence of God. Calvin: the valley is real; the Shepherd is realer.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:1

The NET: rōʿî (my shepherd) uses the intimate possessive. Not "a shepherd" but "MY shepherd." The relationship is personal, not generic.

23:4

The NET: "rod and staff" are the shepherd’s two tools: the rod (club) for protection against predators; the staff (crook) for guiding and rescuing sheep. Comfort comes from both defence and direction.

Kidner Notes

kidner
23:1

Kidner: "I shall not want" is the psalm’s thesis: if God is the shepherd, deficiency is impossible. Not "I shall not want much" but "I shall not want."

23:4

Kidner: "through" is the critical word. Not "into" (trapped), not "around" (avoided), but "through" (traversed). The valley has an exit.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
23:1–4

VanGemeren: Psalm 23 follows Psalm 22 deliberately. The forsaken sufferer (Ps 22) becomes the sheep in God’s care (Ps 23). Cross → comfort. The sequence is theological.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
23:4

Goldingay: the pronoun shift from "he" to "you" is the psalm’s emotional fulcrum. In green pastures, David talks ABOUT God. In the death-valley, he talks TO God. Proximity increases with danger.

Verses 5–6 — “You Prepare a Table Before Me” — The Host
56

Hebrew Word Study

טוֹב ṭōb — good / goodness: "Surely goodness (ṭōb) and love will follow me all the days of my life" (v.6). The psalm’s closing confidence: not just for today but for "all the days" and "forever." The word "follow" (rādaf) literally means "pursue" — the same verb used for enemies chasing. But here, goodness and ḥesed are the pursuers. David is chased by grace.

Context

The metaphor shifts from shepherd to host. God prepares a banquet in the presence of David’s enemies (v.5) — the enemies watch while David feasts. His head is anointed with oil, his cup overflows. Then the psalm’s grand finale: goodness and ḥesed will PURSUE him every day, and he will dwell in God’s house forever (v.6). The psalm that began with "I shall not want" ends with "forever." From sufficiency to eternity.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 15:22–24"Bring the fattened calf! Let’s have a feast" — the prodigal’s father prepares a table in the presence of the older brother.
  • Rev 19:9"Blessed are those who are invited to the wedding supper of the Lamb" — the eschatological table.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
23:5

"You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies." The enemies are not removed; they watch while David eats. God’s provision is conspicuous.

23:6

"Surely goodness and love will follow me all the days of my life, and I will dwell in the house of the LORD forever." The pursuit of grace. Chased by goodness and love. The destination: God’s house. The duration: forever.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:5

Alter: the anointing with oil is a sign of honour and welcome. The overflowing cup: God’s generosity exceeds the vessel’s capacity.

23:6

Alter: "forever" (ʿorek yāmîm) literally "for length of days" — an indefinitely extended future. The psalm’s last word opens onto eternity.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:5

Calvin: the table in enemies’ presence is God’s deliberate display: he feeds his child while the enemies can only watch. Calvin: grace is public.

23:6

Calvin: the pursuit of goodness and love is the counterpart to the enemies’ pursuit. Calvin: if you are going to be chased, better by grace than by malice.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:5

The NET: "anoint my head with oil" was a gesture of hospitality: an honoured guest received oil for refreshment. The psalmist is God’s honoured guest.

23:6

The NET: "dwell in the house of the LORD" can also be translated "return to the house of the LORD." Either way: the psalm’s destination is God’s permanent presence.

Kidner Notes

kidner
23:5

Kidner: the table in enemies’ presence is not bravado but confidence. God provides precisely where threat is greatest.

23:6

Kidner: "follow" (rādaf) means chase relentlessly. The same verb describes Pharaoh’s army pursuing Israel. Here, goodness and love are the relentless pursuers.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
23:5–6

VanGemeren: the host metaphor completes the pastoral image. The Shepherd of vv.1–4 becomes the Host of vv.5–6. Both roles: God provides and protects.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
23:6

Goldingay: "dwell in the house of the LORD forever" is the psalm’s destination. The journey through green pastures, dark valleys, and enemy territory ends at home.
