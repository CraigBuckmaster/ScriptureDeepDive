Job 34

Elihu’s Second Speech: God Cannot Do Wrong

Verses 1–15 — “It Is Unthinkable That God Would Do Wrong”
123456789101112131415

Hebrew Word Study

חָלִילָה ḥālîlâ — far be it / God forbid: "Far be it (ḥālîlâ) from God to do evil, from the Almighty to do wrong" (v.10). Elihu defends God’s justice categorically. He quotes Job (v.5: "I am innocent but God denies me justice") and responds: it is unthinkable that God perverts justice. This is the friends’ position restated, but with stronger philosophical grounding.

Context

Elihu addresses the assembled listeners (vv.2–4) and quotes Job directly (v.5–6). His response is essentially: God IS just because God MUST be just. If God were unjust, creation itself would collapse (vv.14–15). The argument from necessity: if God withdrew his spirit, all life would perish. God sustains everything; therefore God cannot be malicious.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 18:25"Will not the Judge of all the earth do right?" — Abraham’s foundational question, which Elihu answers affirmatively.
  • Rom 9:14"Is God unjust? Not at all!" — Paul’s answer to the same question.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
34:5–6

Elihu quotes Job: "I am in the right, but God denies me justice." He engages Job’s actual words, which is progress beyond the friends.

34:10–12

"Far be it from God to do evil! He repays everyone for what they have done. It is unthinkable that God would do wrong." Elihu affirms divine justice as a necessary truth.

34:14–15

"If it were his intention and he withdrew his spirit and breath, all humanity would perish together." The argument from sustaining power: God who sustains all life cannot be life’s enemy.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
34:10

Calvin: "far be it from God to do evil" is theologically correct. But Elihu, like the friends, struggles to reconcile this truth with Job’s experience. The assertion is right; the application to Job’s case is incomplete.

34:14–15

Calvin: the sustaining-power argument is Elihu’s best. God’s ongoing maintenance of creation is continuous mercy. Even in suffering, the fact that we exist at all is evidence of divine restraint.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
34:10

The NET: ḥālîlâ is an emphatic denial: "profanity!" or "God forbid!" It expresses horror at the very idea.

34:14–15

The NET: the withdrawal of God’s spirit (rûaḥ) is the reverse of Genesis 2:7. God breathed life in; if he breathed it back, life ends.

Clines Notes

clines
34:10–12

Clines: Elihu’s argument is philosophical rather than experiential. God MUST be just because the alternative is incoherent. This is stronger than the friends’ appeal to observation but still does not address Job’s experience.

34:14–15

Clines: the sustaining-power argument is genuinely interesting. If God were malicious, he could simply withdraw his breath and everything dies. The fact that life continues proves God is not hostile. But it does not prove God is fair in every case.

Habel Notes

habel
34:1–15

Habel: Elihu argues from divine necessity. God cannot do wrong because God’s nature is justice. The argument is metaphysical, not empirical — a different approach from the friends.

Verses 16–37 — “Job Speaks Without Knowledge”
16171819202122232425262728293031323334353637

Hebrew Word Study

מִשְׁפָּט mishpāṭ — justice / judgment: "Can someone who hates justice (mishpāṭ) govern?" (v.17). Elihu’s rhetorical question: if God governs, he must love justice, because governance requires justice. The one who holds the universe together cannot be the one who tears justice apart.

Context

Elihu presses his case: God is impartial (v.19), sees all (v.21–22), removes leaders without investigation because he already knows (v.24–25), overthrows the wicked in the sight of all (v.26). His conclusion: Job adds rebellion to his sin by multiplying words against God (v.37). Elihu is harsher here than in his first speech, moving from pedagogy toward accusation.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 33:13–15"From heaven the LORD looks down and sees all mankind" — the omniscience Elihu invokes.
  • Dan 2:21"He changes times and seasons; he deposes kings and raises up others" — the sovereign governance Elihu describes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
34:17–19

"Can someone who hates justice govern? Will you condemn the just and mighty One? Is he not the One who says to kings, ‘You are worthless’?" Elihu argues from political analogy: the sovereign must love justice.

34:21–23

"His eyes are on the ways of mortals; he sees their every step. There is no deep shadow, no utter darkness, where evildoers can hide." God’s omniscience means no injustice goes unnoticed.

34:31–33

"Suppose someone says to God, ‘I am guilty but will offend no more.’ Should God then reward you on your terms?" Elihu challenges Job’s demand for a specific kind of justice.

34:36–37

"Job speaks without knowledge; his words lack insight." Elihu’s verdict echoes what God will say from the whirlwind (38:2). This is either prophetic anticipation or the source God borrows from.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
34:17

Calvin: "can someone who hates justice govern?" is a strong argument. Governance requires justice. But it is an argument about God’s character, not about Job’s specific case. Elihu proves God must be just without explaining how justice operates in this instance.

34:37

Calvin: the accusation of "rebellion" goes too far. Job’s protest was bold but not rebellious. God himself will vindicate Job as one who "spoke what is right" (42:7).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
34:17

The NET: the logic is: governance requires justice; God governs; therefore God is just. The syllogism is valid but does not address exceptions.

34:37

The NET: "adds rebellion to his sin" — the word for rebellion (peshaʿ) implies willful defiance against a sovereign. Elihu treats Job’s complaint as political insurrection.

Clines Notes

clines
34:37

Clines: "he adds rebellion to his sin" is Elihu at his harshest. He accuses Job of compounding the problem by protesting. But Clines notes: God never endorses this accusation. In the epilogue, God rebukes the friends, not Job.

34:36–37

Clines: "Job speaks without knowledge" anticipates God’s opening words in 38:2. Whether Elihu is prophetically anticipating God or merely saying the same thing for different reasons is the central question of the Elihu speeches.

Habel Notes

habel
34:16–37

Habel: Elihu’s second speech moves from philosophical defence of divine justice to accusation of Job. The shift mirrors the friends’ trajectory: start with theology, end with condemnation.
