Job 11

Zophar’s First Speech: God’s Wisdom Is Deeper

Verses 1–12 — “Can You Fathom the Mysteries of God?”
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

תַעֲלֻמָה taʿalumâ — hidden thing / mystery / deep secret: "Can you fathom the mysteries (taʿalumâ) of God?" (v.7). Zophar’s best argument: God’s wisdom is inaccessible. Higher than heavens, deeper than Sheol, longer than earth, wider than sea. The implication: Job cannot understand his own suffering because God’s reasons are hidden.

Context

Zophar is the bluntest of the three friends. No gentle opening, no night vision, no appeal to tradition. He leads with accusation: “Will your idle talk reduce others to silence?” (v.3). His theology matches the others (retribution) but his manner is harshest. He tells Job that God is actually letting him off easy: “Know this: God has even forgotten some of your sin” (v.6).

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 55:8–9"My thoughts are not your thoughts" — the same divine transcendence, but in Isaiah it’s comfort; in Zophar it’s accusation.
  • Rom 11:33–34"How unsearchable his judgments" — Paul’s doxology uses similar language without Zophar’s cruelty.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:2–4

"Are all these words to go unanswered?" Zophar has no patience. He considers Job’s speeches empty talk.

11:5–6

"Oh, how I wish that God would speak... he would tell you the secrets of wisdom. Know this: God has even forgotten some of your sin." The cruelest line since Bildad’s “your children sinned.” Zophar says Job deserves worse.

11:7–9

"Can you fathom the mysteries of God? They are higher than the heavens, deeper than Sheol, longer than the earth, wider than the sea." Four dimensions of divine transcendence. Zophar’s best theology and worst pastoral care in the same breath.

11:12

"A witless person can no more become wise than a wild donkey can be born human." Zophar insults Job’s intelligence. The debate has descended from theology to personal attack.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:6

Calvin: Zophar’s claim that God has “forgotten some of your sin” assumes facts not in evidence. He has no access to Job’s sin ledger. This is theology by imagination, not revelation.

11:7–9

Calvin: the transcendence passages are true. But Zophar uses transcendence to silence rather than comfort. God’s inscrutability should produce humility in all parties, not just in Job.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:6

The NET: "God has even overlooked some of your guilt" — Zophar assumes hidden sin and claims God is showing restraint.

11:7

The NET notes taʿalumâ is related to the verb meaning “to hide” — the mysteries of God are things deliberately concealed.

Clines Notes

clines
11:6

Clines: "God has even forgotten some of your sin" is extraordinary. Zophar claims Job deserves MORE suffering than he’s getting. The retribution principle, taken to its logical extreme, becomes absurd.

11:7–9

Clines: the four-dimensional description of divine wisdom is impressive rhetoric. Zophar is not stupid. His theology of transcendence is correct; his application is monstrous.

Habel Notes

habel
11:1–12

Habel: Zophar is the third and final expert witness. His testimony: divine wisdom is inscrutable, therefore Job’s protest is presumptuous. The prosecution rests its case.

Verses 13–20 — “If You Devote Your Heart to God”
1314151617181920

Hebrew Word Study

תִקְוָה tiqwâ — hope / expectation: "You will be secure, because there is hope (tiqwâ)" (v.18). Zophar’s conditional promise: devote your heart, stretch out your hands, put away sin — THEN life will be brighter, THEN you will be secure, THEN your hope will not be disappointed. The if-then theology at its most seductive.

Context

Zophar’s closing parallels Eliphaz’s (5:17–27): submit, repent, be restored. All three friends reach the same destination by different routes. The promise is genuine in their framework: if suffering = sin, then repentance = restoration. The problem is the first premise.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 3:5–6"Trust in the LORD with all your heart" — the wisdom theology Zophar draws on.
  • 2 Chr 7:14"If my people... humble themselves and pray" — the conditional restoration promise.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:13–15

"If you devote your heart to him... if you put away the sin that is in your hand." The prescription: repent. The assumption: there is sin to repent of.

11:16–19

"You will forget your trouble... life will be brighter than noonday." The restored life Zophar describes sounds like Job’s former life (ch 29). The promise is real — but the path is wrong.

11:20

"But the eyes of the wicked will fail... their hope will become a dying gasp." The threat behind the promise: refuse to repent, and destruction awaits. Zophar leaves no middle ground.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:13–15

Calvin: the prescription is not wrong in itself — devoting one’s heart to God is always right. The error is making it a transactional cure for suffering that has a different cause.

11:20

Calvin: the threat reveals Zophar’s true colours. His theology offers two options: repent or die. There is no category for innocent suffering in his system.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:18

The NET: tiqwâ (hope) is a key word in Job. The friends promise hope through repentance; Job will eventually find hope through encounter.

11:20

The NET: “their hope will become a dying gasp” — literally “an expiring of soul.” The wicked’s last breath IS their last hope.

Clines Notes

clines
11:13–20

Clines: Zophar’s speech ends like Eliphaz’s and Bildad’s: a conditional promise followed by a veiled threat. The three friends are variations on a single theme. Job has heard the same sermon three times.

11:20

Clines: "their hope will become a dying gasp" is literally "the breathing out of the soul." Zophar ends with death. His theology leaves no room for innocent suffering.

Habel Notes

habel
11:13–20

Habel: the prosecution’s closing argument. All three witnesses have testified. The retribution principle has been stated, supported by experience (Eliphaz), tradition (Bildad), and transcendence (Zophar). Job’s rebuttal begins in chapter 12.
