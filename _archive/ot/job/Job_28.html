Job 28

The Wisdom Hymn: Where Shall Wisdom Be Found?

Verses 1–11 — Human Mining: We Dig for Gold and Sapphires
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

מָקוֹם māqōm — place / source / mine: "There is a mine (māqōm) for silver and a place where gold is refined" (v.1). The chapter opens with human technological achievement: mining, smelting, tunnelling. We can find silver, gold, iron, copper, sapphires — we can dig through rock and overturn mountains. Human ingenuity is extraordinary.

Context

The voice shifts from Job to the narrator (or to a poetic interlude). The wisdom hymn stands between the dialogue (ch 3–27) and Job’s final defence (ch 29–31). It asks the book’s deepest question: where is wisdom found? The mining imagery establishes the premise: humans can find anything hidden in the earth. But wisdom is more hidden than any mineral.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 2:4–5"Search for it as for hidden treasure... then you will understand the fear of the LORD" — the same search metaphor.
  • Prov 8:10–11"Choose my instruction instead of silver" — wisdom valued above precious metals.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
28:1–4

"There is a mine for silver, a place where gold is refined. Iron is taken from the earth, copper smelted from ore." Human technology is celebrated: we can extract what is hidden.

28:5–8

"The earth, from which food comes, is transformed below as by fire. Sapphires come from its rocks. No bird of prey knows that hidden path." Mining goes where even eagles cannot see. Human achievement reaches into the earth’s depths.

28:9–11

"Human hands assault the flinty rock and lay bare the roots of the mountains. They tunnel through the rock; their eyes see all its treasures." But the greatest treasure — wisdom — remains beyond reach.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
28:1–4

Calvin: the catalogue of human industry honours God’s gift of intelligence to humanity. We are not criticised for mining; we are shown its limits.

28:9–11

Calvin: "they lay bare the roots of the mountains" — human power is real but relative. What we uncover physically, we cannot uncover spiritually.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
28:1

The NET: māqōm means both "place" and "source." There is a source for silver — but is there a source for wisdom?

28:3–4

The NET discusses the mining techniques described: fire-setting (heating rock to crack it), shaft-sinking, water management. These are accurately attested in ancient Near Eastern mining.

Clines Notes

clines
28:1–11

Clines: the mining section is the most detailed technological description in the OT. The poet knew mining operations: shafts, tunnels, fire-setting to crack rock, water diversion. The point: human skill is immense. It is not enough for wisdom.

28:7–8

Clines: "no bird of prey knows that hidden path" — eagles have the sharpest eyes in the animal kingdom. Even they cannot see what miners see. But miners cannot see what only God sees.

Habel Notes

habel
28:1–11

Habel: the wisdom hymn is the narrator’s commentary on the entire dialogue. The friends sought wisdom through tradition; Job sought it through experience. Both failed. The chapter explains why.

Verses 12–28 — “The Fear of the Lord — That Is Wisdom”
1213141516171819202122232425262728

Hebrew Word Study

חָכְמָה ḥok̅mâ — wisdom: "Where can wisdom (ḥok̅mâ) be found? Where does understanding dwell?" (v.12). The question is repeated in v.20. Wisdom cannot be found in the land of the living (v.13), in the deep sea (v.14), or bought with gold, silver, onyx, or sapphire (vv.15–19). Even Destruction and Death say, "only a rumour of it has reached our ears" (v.22).

יִרְאַת yirʾāṭ — fear / reverence / awe: "The fear (yirʾāṭ) of the Lord — that is wisdom, and to shun evil is understanding" (v.28). The chapter’s conclusion. After exhaustive searching, the answer: wisdom belongs to God, and the human path to wisdom is reverence. This is not anti-intellectual; it is the recognition that the deepest questions require relationship, not just research.

Context

The refrain "where can wisdom be found?" (vv.12, 20) structures the second half. Wisdom is not in the land of the living (v.13), not in the deep (v.14), cannot be purchased (vv.15–19), is hidden from every living thing (v.21), and even Death has only heard a rumour (v.22). Only God understands it (v.23). The conclusion (v.28) is the theological key to the entire book: the fear of the LORD is wisdom. Job’s friends had knowledge. Job himself had experience. Neither is wisdom. Wisdom is reverence before the God whose ways are beyond tracing.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 1:7"The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge" — the foundational wisdom principle, now confirmed.
  • Prov 9:10"The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" — the Proverbs parallel to Job 28:28.
  • 1 Cor 1:30"Christ Jesus, who has become for us wisdom from God" — the NT identification of wisdom with a person.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
28:12–14

"But where can wisdom be found? Where does understanding dwell? No mortal comprehends its worth; it cannot be found in the land of the living." Wisdom is not a commodity. It cannot be extracted like ore or traded like gold.

28:15–19

Gold, crystal, coral, jasper, rubies, topaz — none can buy wisdom. The most valuable things on earth cannot purchase the one thing that matters most.

28:20–22

"Where then does wisdom come from? It is hidden from the eyes of every living thing... Destruction and Death say, ‘Only a rumour of it has reached our ears.’" Even death’s realm has only heard whispers of wisdom.

28:23–28

"God understands the way to it... when he established the force of the wind and measured out the waters, when he made a decree for the rain... he looked at wisdom and appraised it. And he said to the human race: ‘The fear of the Lord — that is wisdom.’" The book’s theological foundation: wisdom is not information but relationship with God.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
28:28

Calvin: "the fear of the Lord — that is wisdom" is the book’s most important sentence. It will be confirmed in chapter 42 when Job, having encountered God, repents — not of sin but of speaking beyond his knowledge. Fear of the LORD is not terror but reverence before mystery.

28:15–19

Calvin: the inability of wealth to purchase wisdom condemns the entire transactional theology the friends offered: do good → get good. Wisdom transcends transaction.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
28:12

The NET: the question "where" (meʾāyin) is emphatic — from what source? Wisdom’s origin is the question.

28:28

The NET: yirʾāṭ is not terror but reverential awe — the posture of a creature before its Creator. This is the same word used of Job in 1:1.

Clines Notes

clines
28:28

Clines: this verse is both the answer and the problem. If wisdom is the fear of the LORD, and Job already fears the LORD (1:1), then Job already has wisdom. So why is he suffering? The chapter deepens the mystery rather than resolving it.

28:23–27

Clines: God looked at wisdom "when he established" the world. Wisdom was present at creation — the same theology as Proverbs 8:22–31 where Wisdom was God’s companion at the beginning.

Habel Notes

habel
28:12–28

Habel: the narrator declares what neither the friends nor Job could discover through argument: wisdom belongs to God alone. The human path to wisdom is not debate but reverence. This reframes the entire dialogue as a pursuit that, however brilliant, could never reach its goal.
