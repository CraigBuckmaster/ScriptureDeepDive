Job 12

Job’s Reply: I Am Not Inferior to You

Verses 1–12 — “Ask the Animals, and They Will Teach You”
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

מְשָׁל māshāl — proverb / byword / object lesson: "I have become a laughingstock (māshāl) to my friends" (v.4). Job is no longer a sage but a cautionary tale. The word māshāl can mean proverb, parable, or byword — Job has become all three.

Context

Job opens with biting sarcasm: “Doubtless you are the only people who matter, and wisdom will die with you!” (v.2). He is done being polite. Then an appeal to nature: even animals know that God’s hand has done this (v.9). Job’s argument is not that God is absent but that God is responsible. The friends blame Job; Job blames God.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 1:3"The ox knows its master" — animals perceive what humans miss.
  • Rom 1:20"God’s invisible qualities are clearly seen from creation" — Paul’s version of learning from nature.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
12:2–3

"Doubtless you are the only people who matter!" Sarcasm. Job is intellectually equal to the friends and tired of being patronised.

12:4

"I have become a laughingstock." The social dimension of suffering: not just pain but shame. The righteous man is mocked by his peers.

12:7–9

"Ask the animals, and they will teach you." Nature testifies that God did this. Job does not deny divine agency — he insists on it. The friends say Job sinned; Job says God acted.

12:10

"In his hand is the life of every creature and the breath of all mankind." Job’s theology is more God-centred than the friends’. They focus on human sin; Job focuses on divine sovereignty.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:9

Calvin: "the hand of the LORD has done this" — Job’s theology is more orthodox than the friends’. He attributes everything to God, not to secondary causes or hidden sin.

12:12

"Is not wisdom found among the aged?" Job questions the assumption that age = wisdom. The friends’ appeal to tradition is not automatically valid.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:4

The NET: "a laughingstock to his friends" — māshāl carries the sense of becoming a public example, a walking proverb of ruin.

12:9

The NET notes this is the only place in Job where the covenant name YHWH ("the LORD") appears in the dialogue. Everywhere else it’s El, Eloah, or Shaddai.

Clines Notes

clines
12:2

Clines: the sarcasm marks a shift. Job is no longer defensive. He attacks. The friends’ claim to wisdom is challenged directly.

12:7–9

Clines: “ask the animals” inverts the friends’ appeal to tradition. You don’t need ancient sages; even beasts know God is sovereign over suffering.

Habel Notes

habel
12:1–12

Habel: Job begins his rebuttal of all three witnesses. He challenges their credentials (v.2), their logic (vv.7–9), and their conclusion (v.12). The defendant cross-examines the experts.

Verses 13–25 — God’s Sovereign Power over Nations
13141516171819202122232425

Hebrew Word Study

תּוּשִׁיָּה tūshiyyâ — sound wisdom / counsel / effective power: "With God are wisdom and power; counsel (tūshiyyâ) and understanding are his" (v.13). Tūshiyyâ means wisdom that works — effective, practical, result-producing counsel. God’s wisdom is not theoretical but active, world-shaping.

Context

Job delivers his own doxology — but unlike the friends’ doxologies, his emphasises destruction. God tears down and it cannot be rebuilt (v.14). He dries up waters and floods the land (v.15). He makes nations great and destroys them (v.23). He strips leaders of reason and makes them stagger like drunkards (v.25). Job agrees God is sovereign. He disagrees that sovereignty equals justice.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 40:23–24"He brings princes to naught and reduces rulers to nothing" — the same sovereign power over nations.
  • Dan 2:21"He sets up kings and deposes them" — Daniel’s affirmation of what Job describes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
12:13–14

"To God belong wisdom and power... what he tears down cannot be rebuilt." Job’s doxology of divine sovereignty. Everything the friends attribute to God, Job agrees with — but draws darker conclusions.

12:15

"If he holds back the waters, there is drought; if he lets them loose, they devastate the land." God controls extremes. Both drought and flood come from him.

12:17–21

God strips counsellors, judges, kings, priests, advisers of their wisdom and authority. No human power is secure against divine sovereignty.

12:22–25

"He reveals the deep things of darkness... he makes nations great, and destroys them." The speech ends with nations staggering in darkness. Job’s point: God’s sovereignty produces chaos as often as order.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:14–15

Calvin: Job correctly identifies God as the ultimate cause of both building and demolishing, drought and flood. His theology is more complete than the friends’ — they see only the constructive side.

12:24–25

Calvin: nations staggering in darkness is history confirming Job’s point. Great empires fall without explanation. The retribution principle does not govern geopolitics.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:13

The NET: tūshiyyâ appears frequently in wisdom literature (Prov 2:7; 3:21; 8:14) and describes wisdom that achieves results.

12:22

The NET: “reveals deep things of darkness” — God exposes what is hidden. Job will later demand that God expose the reason for HIS suffering.

Clines Notes

clines
12:13–25

Clines: Job’s doxology is a counter-doxology. Where Eliphaz praised God’s constructive power (5:9–16), Job praises God’s destructive power. Same God, opposite emphasis.

12:24–25

Clines: leaders wandering in trackless wastes, groping in darkness, staggering like drunkards. This is not chaos theory — it is God doing this deliberately. That is what terrifies Job.

Habel Notes

habel
12:13–25

Habel: Job presents counter-evidence. The defence’s argument: God’s sovereignty does not always produce justice. It also produces chaos, confusion, and destruction. The retribution principle cannot account for this.
