Job 35

Elihu’s Third Speech: God Is Beyond Human Reach

Verses 1–8 — “Your Wickedness Affects Only Other Humans”
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

שְׁחָקִים shəḥāqîm — clouds / skies: "Look up at the heavens (shəḥāqîm) and see; gaze at the clouds so high above you" (v.5). Elihu’s argument: God is so transcendent that human sin cannot harm him and human righteousness cannot benefit him. The skies are above human reach; so is God. Your moral life affects your neighbours, not your Creator.

Context

Elihu argues that God is unaffected by human behaviour. "If you sin, how does that affect him? If your sins are many, what does that do to him?" (v.6). This is a philosophical argument about divine self-sufficiency. It is partly true (God is not diminished by sin) but misses the relational dimension: the God of the Bible cares about human behaviour precisely because he is in relationship with humanity.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 50:12–13"If I were hungry I would not tell you, for the world is mine" — God’s self-sufficiency stated by God himself.
  • Acts 17:25"He is not served by human hands, as if he needed anything" — Paul’s Athens speech makes the same point.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
35:5–7

"Look up at the heavens. If you sin, how does that affect God? If you are righteous, what do you give to God?" The transcendence argument: God is beyond being helped or harmed by humans.

35:8

"Your wickedness only affects others like yourself, and your righteousness only other human beings." The conclusion: morality is horizontal, not vertical. It affects neighbours, not God.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
35:6–7

Calvin: God is not diminished by sin or enhanced by righteousness in his essential being. But Calvin adds: God nonetheless responds to human action because of his covenant commitment, not because of his need.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
35:5

The NET: shəḥāqîm (clouds/skies) represents the barrier between human and divine realms. Elihu uses spatial distance as a metaphor for ontological difference.

Clines Notes

clines
35:5–8

Clines: the self-sufficiency argument is philosophically valid but relationally impoverished. The Bible’s God is not a deistic watchmaker; he is a covenant partner who grieves over sin (Gen 6:6) and rejoices in righteousness.

Habel Notes

habel
35:1–8

Habel: Elihu removes the relational dimension from theology. If God is unaffected by human action, Job’s complaint is meaningless — but so is Job’s righteousness. The argument undermines the entire premise of the book.

Verses 9–16 — “Where Is God My Maker?”
910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

עֹשֶׂה ʿōseh — maker / the one who made: "No one says, ‘Where is God my Maker (ʿōseh), who gives songs in the night?’" (v.10). Elihu’s best line: people cry out under oppression (v.9) but don’t ask the right question. They want relief but not the Giver of relief. They want deliverance but not relationship with the Deliverer.

Context

Elihu observes that oppressed people cry out but God doesn’t answer (v.12–13). Why? Because their cries are empty — motivated by pain, not by genuine seeking. They want rescue from suffering but not encounter with God. Elihu’s insight is sharp: the cry matters less than the orientation of the crier. But applied to Job, it fails: Job has been seeking God since chapter 23.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 42:8"At night his song is with me" — the God who gives songs in the night.
  • Acts 16:25Paul and Silas singing hymns in prison at midnight — the lived version of Elihu’s theology.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
35:9–11

"People cry out under oppression... but no one says, ‘Where is God my Maker, who gives songs in the night, who teaches us more than the animals?’" The distinction: crying FROM pain vs crying TO God. One is reactive; the other is relational.

35:12–13

"He does not answer because of the arrogance of the wicked. God does not listen to their empty plea." Elihu’s explanation for divine silence: not absence but discernment.

35:14–16

"How much less when you say you do not see him... you say your case is before him, but you must wait. Job opens his mouth with empty talk; without knowledge he multiplies words." Elihu tells Job to wait. The advice is partly right — Job WILL encounter God — but the accusation of "empty talk" is wrong.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
35:10

Calvin: "songs in the night" is the experience of faith: worship when circumstances give no reason to worship. Paul and Silas at midnight (Acts 16:25) is the NT embodiment.

35:14

Calvin: "you must wait" is actually the right counsel. Job will encounter God. The timing is not Job’s to determine. But Elihu delivers good advice with bad tone.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
35:10

The NET: ʿōseh (Maker) is a participial form emphasising ongoing relationship. God is not a distant creator but an active maker who continues to engage.

35:16

The NET: "multiplies words without knowledge" — Elihu uses the same phrase God will use in 38:2, but without God’s authority to say it.

Clines Notes

clines
35:10

Clines: "who gives songs in the night" is a beautiful image and Elihu’s most memorable line. God gives music in darkness. The theological insight: worship in suffering is the authentic response. But telling a sufferer to sing is different from singing alongside them.

35:16

Clines: "without knowledge he multiplies words" is ironic. Elihu, who has spoken more than any friend, accuses Job of multiplying words. The charge has a certain self-referential quality.

Habel Notes

habel
35:9–16

Habel: Elihu diagnoses Job’s prayers as incorrectly oriented. The right question is not "why do I suffer?" but "where is God my Maker?" This reframing has merit but is delivered without empathy.
