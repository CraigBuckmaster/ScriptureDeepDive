Job 25

Bildad’s Third Speech: The Friends Exhausted

Verses 1–3 — “Dominion and Awe Belong to God”
123

Hebrew Word Study

מָשָל moshāl — dominion / rule: "Dominion (moshāl) and awe belong to God; he establishes order in the heights of heaven" (v.2). Bildad begins with true theology but cannot connect it to Job’s situation. God rules — agreed. What follows from that for a blameless sufferer? Bildad has no answer.

Context

The shortest speech in the entire dialogue — only 6 verses. Bildad has run out of arguments. Zophar never speaks a third time. The friends’ theology has been exhausted by Job’s evidence. The cycle collapses not because Job won the argument but because the friends’ framework has nothing left to say.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 6:1–3"Holy, holy, holy is the LORD Almighty" — the same awe before divine majesty that Bildad invokes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
25:2–3

"Dominion and awe belong to God; he establishes order in the heights." True, but generic. Bildad can no longer make the specific argument that Job’s suffering proves guilt.

25:3

"Can his forces be numbered? On whom does his light not rise?" God’s armies are countless, his light universal. But Bildad offers no application.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:2

Calvin: Bildad states what everyone agrees on. The dispute was never about God’s sovereignty but about its implications for innocent suffering.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:2

The NET: moshāl is authority to govern. Bildad affirms God’s cosmic rule but cannot explain how that rule permits innocent suffering.

Clines Notes

clines
25:1–3

Clines: Bildad’s opening is a hymnic fragment — praise without argument. He has retreated from accusation to doxology because the accusation failed.

Habel Notes

habel
25:1–3

Habel: the prosecution’s third witness delivers the shortest testimony yet. The case has collapsed.

Verses 4–6 — “How Can a Mortal Be Righteous?”
456

Hebrew Word Study

רִמָּה rimmâ — worm / maggot: "How much less a mortal, who is but a maggot (rimmâ) — a human being, who is only a worm!" (v.6). Bildad ends where Eliphaz’s night vision began (4:17): no human can be righteous before God. The argument has not advanced in 20 chapters. The friends are repeating themselves.

Context

Bildad’s conclusion — humans are worms before God — recycles Eliphaz’s first speech almost verbatim. The moon is not bright enough, the stars are impure; how much less a maggot? The argument from divine transcendence was stated better by Zophar (11:7–9). Bildad adds nothing. The friends have said everything they can say, and it was not enough.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 8:4–6"What is mankind that you are mindful of them?" — the Psalm that dignifies humanity; Bildad’s speech degrades it.
  • Job 4:17–19Eliphaz’s night vision — the same argument Bildad now repeats, 20 chapters later.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
25:4–6

"How can one born of woman be righteous? If even the moon is not bright and the stars are not pure in his eyes, how much less a mortal — a maggot; a human being — a worm!" The argument is recycled from Eliphaz (4:17–19; 15:14–16). After 20 chapters, the friends’ theology has not developed.

25:5–6“If even the moon is not bright and the stars are not pure in his eyes, how much less a mortal!” The argument from greater to lesser: if celestial bodies fall short of God’s purity, humanity has no chance. Bildad’s anthropology is the opposite of Genesis 1:26–27.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:5–6

Calvin: the moon-and-stars argument proves too much. If no creature is pure before God, then the friends themselves are worms. Their condemnation of Job condemns themselves.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:6

The NET: rimmâ (maggot) and tōlēʿâ (worm) are the lowest forms of life. Bildad’s anthropology is the opposite of Genesis 1:26–27 where humans are made in God’s image.

Clines Notes

clines
25:4–6

Clines: this is Bildad’s last word in the book. He ends by calling humanity a worm. The friends began with sympathy (2:13) and end with contempt. Their theological framework has degraded their pastoral capacity.

Habel Notes

habel
25:4–6

Habel: the final prosecution testimony reduces all humanity to worms. The argument has moved from “Job sinned” to “all humans are worthless.” The case has collapsed into nihilism.
