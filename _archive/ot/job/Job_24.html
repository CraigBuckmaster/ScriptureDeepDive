Job 24

Job: Why Does God Not Set Times for Judgment?

Verses 1–12 — Injustice Catalogued
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

עִתִּים ʿittîm — times / appointed times: "Why does the Almighty not set times (ʿittîm) for judgment?" (v.1). The question: if God is just, why doesn’t he schedule regular court sessions? The wicked should be judged on a fixed calendar, not at some indefinite future point. Justice delayed is justice denied.

Context

Job catalogues social injustice: the wicked move boundary stones (v.2), drive away the orphan’s donkey (v.3), thrust the needy from the road (v.4). The poor harvest fields not their own (v.6), go naked without covering (v.7), are drenched by rain with no shelter (v.8). The city’s dying groan (v.12) — and God charges no one with wrongdoing. This is Job’s social justice speech: the retribution principle fails not just for Job but for the entire world.

Cross-Reference

  • Hab 1:2–4"How long, LORD, must I call for help, but you do not listen?" — the prophet’s identical complaint about unanswered injustice.
  • Amos 2:6–7"They sell the innocent for silver, and the needy for a pair of sandals" — Amos’ catalogue of the same social crimes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
24:1

"Why does the Almighty not set times for judgment?" The fundamental complaint: God allows injustice to persist unchecked.

24:2–4

"There are those who move boundary stones; they pasture flocks they have stolen. They drive away the fatherless’ donkey." Concrete social crimes. Not abstractions but observable exploitation.

24:5–12

The victims: the poor glean like wild donkeys, harvest others’ fields, go naked and hungry, carry sheaves while starving, press olive oil but go thirsty. "The groans of the dying rise from the city, and the souls of the wounded cry out. But God charges no one with wrongdoing." The most devastating line in the chapter.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:1

Calvin: the demand for scheduled judgment reflects Job’s legal mindset. Justice should be regular, predictable, and public — not mysterious, delayed, and hidden.

24:12

Calvin: "God charges no one" is the observation that generates the problem of theodicy. The world groans, and God appears silent. This is the deepest complaint of the OT prophets as well.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
24:1

The NET: ʿittîm are fixed, appointed times — like festival days or court calendar dates. Job wants judgment regularised.

24:12

The NET: "charges no one with wrongdoing" is literally "does not attribute unseemliness" — God does not see (or act on) the injustice.

Clines Notes

clines
24:1

Clines: the ʿittîm request is for scheduled judgment — regular assize courts where the wicked are tried. Job wants institutional justice, not ad hoc intervention.

24:12

Clines: “God charges no one with wrongdoing” is the chapter’s thesis. The wicked are not prosecuted. Job moves from personal complaint to systemic critique.

Habel Notes

habel
24:1–12

Habel: Job presents evidence of systemic injustice. His argument has expanded from personal injury to social critique: the retribution principle fails everywhere, not just in Uz.

Verses 13–25 — Darkness Lovers; “If This Is Not So, Who Can Prove Me False?”
13141516171819202122232425

Hebrew Word Study

אוֹר ōr — light: "There are those who rebel against the light (ōr)" (v.13). The rebels against light: murderers, adulterers, thieves. They prefer darkness because it hides their deeds. Job describes a world where the wicked thrive precisely because they operate in secret — and God does not intervene.

Context

Job describes the nocturnal wicked: the murderer rises at dusk (v.14), the adulterer waits for twilight (v.15), the thief breaks in at night (v.16). Morning is their darkness (v.17). They are friends with the terrors of midnight. Job’s challenge at the end is definitive: “If this is not so, who can prove me false?” (v.25). He demands a counter-argument. The friends have none.

Cross-Reference

  • John 3:19–20"People loved darkness instead of light because their deeds were evil" — Jesus’ same diagnosis of those who rebel against light.
  • Eph 5:11–12"Have nothing to do with the fruitless deeds of darkness" — Paul’s echo of Job’s light/darkness moral framework.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
24:13–17

The rebels against light: murderer, adulterer, thief. They say “no eye will see me” (v.15). Deep darkness is their morning (v.17). Evil is nocturnal; it flees the light.

24:18–20

Job describes swift judgment on the wicked — or does he? Some scholars read vv.18–24 as the friends’ position that Job is quoting to refute. The text is debated.

24:24–25

"They are exalted for a little while, and then they are gone... if this is not so, who can prove me false, and reduce my words to nothing?" Job’s closing challenge. His case stands unless refuted. No one refutes it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:13–17

Calvin: the rebels against light are real. Evil operates in darkness not because God is absent but because sinners flee the light. The problem is not God’s ignorance but his patience.

24:25

Calvin: "who can prove me false?" — Job’s confidence is not arrogance but the strength of someone who has evidence. He has observed what the friends refuse to acknowledge.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
24:13

The NET: "rebels against the light" (ōr) may carry overtones of rebelling against God himself, since God is frequently associated with light in Scripture.

24:25

The NET: "reduce my words to nothing" is literally "make my word a lie." Job challenges anyone to disprove his observations. No one does.

Clines Notes

clines
24:13–17

Clines: the darkness-dwellers passage is Job’s contribution to moral philosophy. Evil prefers concealment. The retribution principle assumes God illuminates all sin; Job’s evidence is that much sin thrives in the dark, unpunished.

24:25

Clines: the closing challenge is a legal motion to dismiss. Job’s case stands unless the friends can refute it. They cannot, and Zophar never speaks again. Silence is the friends’ only remaining response.

Habel Notes

habel
24:13–25

Habel: Job’s closing argument for the second cycle. The prosecution’s theory (retribution) has been tested against evidence (the wicked prosper, the innocent suffer) and found wanting. The defence rests.
