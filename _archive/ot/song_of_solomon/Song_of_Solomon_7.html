Song of Solomon 7

The Dance and the Vineyard

Verses 1–9 — The Second Wasf: Feet to Head
123456789

Hebrew Word Study

שׁוּלַּמִּית shullamît — the Shulammite: "Come back, come back, O Shulammite (shullamît)!" (v.1/6:13). The beloved’s only name in the poem. Related to "Solomon" (shəlōmō) and to "peace" (shālōm). She is the feminine counterpart of the king: the peace-woman matching the peace-king.

Context

The second wasf describes the beloved from feet upward (the reverse of ch 4’s head-down): sandalled feet, curved thighs, navel like a goblet, waist like a mound of wheat, breasts like fawns, neck like ivory tower, eyes like pools, nose like the tower of Lebanon, head like Carmel, hair that traps a king. The upward direction mirrors her rising: she is ascending in the lover’s vision. "How beautiful your sandalled feet, O prince’s daughter!" (v.1).

Cross-Reference

  • Eph 5:28–29"Husbands ought to love their wives as their own bodies" — the bodily love the wasf celebrates.
  • Gen 2:23"This is now bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh" — the first human celebration of the body that the wasf extends.

"How beautiful your sandalled feet, O prince’s daughter! Your graceful legs are like jewels... your navel is a rounded goblet... your waist is a mound of wheat." The upward wasf: from feet to waist.

"Your head crowns you like Mount Carmel. Your hair is like royal tapestry; the king is held captive by its tresses." The wasf culminates at the head. The king is captured by her beauty — power reversed.

Verses 10–13 — “I Belong to My Beloved”; To the Vineyards
10111213

Hebrew Word Study

תְּשׁוּקָה təshūqâ — desire / longing: "I belong to my beloved, and his desire (təshūqâ) is for me" (v.10). The final form of the mutual-belonging formula. The progression is complete: "my beloved is mine and I am his" (2:16) → "I am my beloved’s and my beloved is mine" (6:3) → "I belong to my beloved, and his desire is for me" (7:10). The last version is pure surrender and received desire. The word təshūqâ appears only three times in the OT: Gen 3:16 (the woman’s desire for her husband, post-fall), Gen 4:7 (sin’s desire for Cain), and here. The Song redeems the Genesis curse: desire restored to delight.

Context

The formula reaches its final form: "I belong to my beloved, and his desire is for me." Then the beloved invites: "Come, my beloved, let us go to the countryside... let us go early to the vineyards" (vv.11–12). The vineyard — where she was forced to labour in chapter 1 (v.6) — is now a place of chosen love. "There I will give you my love" (v.12). The mandrakes give off fragrance (v.13) — fertility plants associated with Rachel’s desire for children (Gen 30:14–16). Love, fertility, and the land are united.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 3:16"Your desire will be for your husband" — the curse. Song 7:10 redeems it: desire restored from curse to gift.
  • Gen 4:7"Sin is crouching at your door; its desire is for you" — the only other use of təshūqâ before the Song.
  • Gen 30:14–16Rachel, Leah, and the mandrakes — the same fertility association.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:10

"I belong to my beloved, and his desire is for me." The formula’s final evolution: pure belonging. She no longer claims; she is claimed. His desire directed at her is her identity.

7:11–13

"Come, my beloved, let us go to the countryside... there I will give you my love." The vineyard of forced labour (1:6) becomes the vineyard of chosen intimacy. The love is now given, not compelled.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:10

Alter: the Genesis 3:16 echo is deliberate. The Song places təshūqâ in a redeemed context: desire without domination, longing without curse. What the fall distorted, love restores.

7:11–13

Alter: the mandrakes’ fragrance and the vineyard’s budding connect love to the natural world’s fertility. Human desire and agricultural abundance are not metaphor but shared reality.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:10

Calvin: "his desire is for me" redeems the fall’s distortion of desire. Calvin: in Christ, the curse on sexuality is lifted. Marital desire becomes what God originally intended: mutual delight without dominion.

7:11–13

Calvin: the vineyard invitation is the restored Eden. What was lost in Genesis 3 is glimpsed in the Song: love without shame, desire without curse, intimacy without fear.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:10

The NET: təshūqâ (desire/longing) occurs only in Gen 3:16, 4:7, and here. The intertextual connection with the curse is widely recognised.

7:13

The NET: "mandrakes" (dūdāʾam) were associated with fertility and love-magic in the ANE. The root is the same as dōd (beloved). Love-plants for the lover.

Longman Notes

longman
7:10

Longman: the təshūqâ connection to Genesis 3:16 is the Song’s most theologically charged moment. The curse’s distorted desire is redeemed into the Song’s mutual delight. This is Eden restored in love.

7:11–13

Longman: the vineyard invitation closes a circle: from vineyard labour (1:6) to vineyard love (7:12). What was obligation becomes joy. This is the gospel pattern: what the law demanded, grace enables.

Garrett Notes

garrett
7:10

Garrett: the formula’s progression (2:16 → 6:3 → 7:10) is the poem’s relational arc in miniature. From mutual claiming to mutual belonging to received desire. The relationship matures through the poem.

7:11–13

Garrett: the countryside/vineyard setting returns the poem to pastoral origins. The urban court gives way to rural simplicity. Love’s truest expression is not in the palace but in the garden.
