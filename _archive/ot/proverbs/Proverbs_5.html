Proverbs 5

The Adulteress: Sweet Entrance, Bitter End; The Faithful Wife

Verses 1–14 — The Adulteress: Sweet Entrance, Bitter End
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

נֹפֶת תִּטֹּפְנָה nōpet tiṭṭōpĕnāh — drip honey: Nōpet is honeycomb honey — the richest, sweetest form. The adulteress's speech is described in terms of immediate, intense pleasure. The contrast with "bitter as gall" (v.4) is the book's central warning: what is sweetest at first can be most bitter in the end. The deceptive nature of sin is not that it offers nothing real but that it is a short-term gain masking a long-term catastrophe.

שְׁאוֹל šĕʾôl — the grave / Sheol: The realm of the dead. The adulteress's steps lead "straight to Sheol" (v.5) — her house is literally a path to death. This is not metaphor for regret; it is a stark theological statement that the road of adultery ends in Sheol.

Context

Proverbs 5 is the first extended warning against the adulteress (she appeared briefly in 2:16–19). The chapter has three movements: the warning (vv.1–14), the positive alternative (vv.15–20), and the theological grounding (vv.21–23). The most striking feature is the extended deathbed speech in vv.11–14 — the man who did not listen imagines himself at the end of life, groaning over what his choices have cost. Proverbs asks the young man to live from the end: what will you wish you had chosen?

Cross-Reference

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
    5:3–4

    The pleasure-to-pain reversal is Proverbs' anatomy of temptation. What the adulteress offers is real pleasure — the lips drip honey. The warning is not that the honey is fake. It is that the honey leads to gall, that the oil leads to a double-edged sword. Sin is accurately described as pleasurable; the deception is in the timeline, not the pleasure. The young man must learn to think beyond the immediate.

    5:11–14

    The regret speech is among the most psychologically realistic passages in wisdom literature. The man at the end of his life has three losses: dignity (given to the cruel, v.9), wealth (consumed by strangers, v.10), and health (body spent, v.11). And beneath all of these: the despair of realising that he was warned, and did not listen.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    5:3–6

    The adulteress passage in ch.5 deploys the same double-register as ch.2's Strange Woman: literal warning about adultery and symbolic portrait of the anti-wisdom path. Alter notes the female figure's 'feet go down to death' (v.5) echoes ch.2's 'house sinks down to death' (2:18) — the same destination, the same descent imagery, repeated with greater intensity.

    5:15–23

    The positive vision of the faithful wife (vv.15–23) is unusually erotic for Proverbs — 'rejoice in the wife of your youth... let her breasts satisfy you at all times.' Alter notes the erotic language is deliberate: the legitimate pleasures of marital intimacy are the answer to the adulteress's false promise of pleasure. The counter is not abstinence but faithful, joyful marriage.

    Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

    Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
    5:3–6

    The adulteress's honey-lips and smooth speech (vv.3–4) reverse Lady Wisdom's offer: Wisdom's speech saves life; the adulteress's speech destroys it. Waltke notes the bitter-end structure — sweet beginning (honey and oil, v.3), bitter outcome (wormwood and sharp sword, v.4) — is the book's fundamental characterisation of folly: it presents pleasure before revealing death.

    5:9–14

    The consequences listed in vv.9–14 are social, economic, and psychological: honour given to others, years to the cruel, wealth to strangers, groaning in the assembly. Waltke notes the 'assembly' (qahal) is the covenant community — the adulterer's final ruin is public community shame. Wisdom's ethics are never merely private; their consequences are always communal.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    5:3–4

    Calvin: 'The beginning of illicit pleasures is sweet to the taste, but the end is more bitter than wormwood. This is the common lot of all who give themselves to sin — the pleasure is brief, the pain enduring.' The temporal inversion (sweetness now, bitterness later) is the structure of all temptation in Calvin's moral theology.

    5:18–19

    Calvin on rejoicing in the wife of your youth: 'God has appointed marriage as the remedy for licentiousness, and commands us to be satisfied with the wife he has given us. The man who seeks pleasures abroad when he has a wife at home is not merely sinning against his wife but against God's ordering of human life.'

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    5:4

    The NET note on 'wormwood' (la'anah) explains it is a bitter, poisonous plant used throughout the OT as an image of judgment and consequence (Jer 9:15; 23:15; Amos 5:7; Rev 8:11). The movement from honey (v.3) to wormwood (v.4) is the trajectory of every choice that begins with sensory appeal and ends with consequence.

    5:14

    The NET note on 'I was soon in serious trouble in the midst of the assembled congregation' (vagal bera'ah betokh qahal) explains that qahal (assembly) is the formal covenant community gathered for worship and legal proceedings. Public shame before the assembled community was one of the most severe social consequences in ancient Israel — honour was communal and its loss was communal.

    Verses 15–23 — The Positive Vision: Rejoice in the Wife of Your Youth
    151617181920212223

    Hebrew Word Study

    Context

    The positive alternative (vv.15–19) is one of the most beautiful passages in Proverbs — a frank, joyful celebration of sexual delight within marriage. The water imagery (cistern, well, springs, fountain) is an ancient metaphor for sexuality. The instruction is not mere duty but delight: "may you ever be intoxicated with her love" (v.19). Proverbs does not call the young man to asceticism but to fidelity. The pleasure the adulteress falsely promises is genuinely available within covenant marriage.

    Cross-Reference

    • Song of SongsThe entire Song of Songs is the positive vision of which Prov 5:15–19 is a brief sketch — the celebration of marital love as a reflection of God's love for Israel and Christ's love for the church.
    • 1 Cor 7:2–5Paul's instruction that spouses have authority over one another's bodies and should not deprive each other echoes the Proverbs framework: sexual fulfilment within marriage is the protection against immorality.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
    5:15–19

    Proverbs is not a book of sexual repression. The counter to the adulteress is not celibacy but marital joy. The same intense language used to describe the adulteress's seduction (honey, smoothness) is now applied to the wife: "may you ever be intoxicated with her love" (šāgāh, the same word used of being drunk). The point is that legitimate, covenant-bounded sexual delight is the gift God intended — the adulteress is offering a counterfeit of something real.

    5:21–23

    The theological anchor: "your ways are in full view of the LORD, and he examines all your paths." The ultimate restraint is not social shame or physical consequences (though those are real) but the omniscience of God. Every hidden thing is seen. The man who sins believing no one sees is deceived about fundamental reality — God sees everything.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    5:15–20

    The erotic celebration of marital love in vv.15–20 is Proverbs' most positive treatment of human sexuality. Alter notes the instruction to 'be intoxicated always' with the wife's love (v.19) deliberately uses the language of intoxication — the same loss of control that makes adultery dangerous becomes, within marriage, a gift. The danger is not desire itself but desire misdirected.

    5:21

    'A man's ways are in full view of the LORD' — the chapter closes with a surveillance frame that contextualises all the personal sexual ethics within a theological horizon. Alter notes this prevents the chapter's ethics from being read as mere prudential calculation (avoid adultery because of its social consequences) — the theological ground is that human life is lived before God.

    Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

    Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
    5:15–19

    The cistern/well/springs metaphor for the faithful wife is one of Proverbs' most distinctive passages. Waltke notes the water imagery encodes the entire covenantal sexual ethic: drink from your own cistern, not another's well; your springs should be for you alone, not strangers. The body's pleasures are legitimate within the covenant boundaries they are given in.

    5:21–23

    The closing theological frame (vv.21–23) grounds the entire chapter's ethics in divine surveillance: 'for a man's ways are in full view of the LORD, and he examines all his paths.' Waltke notes this is not legalistic deterrence but covenantal accountability — the one who knows 'all your paths' is the one who ordered the paths for flourishing.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    5:15

    Calvin on 'drink water from your own cistern': 'The cistern represents the wife, and the instruction to drink only from one's own cistern is the covenant command of sexual faithfulness. The image is deliberately domestic and concrete — faithfulness is not a grand heroic virtue but a daily, intimate practice.'

    5:21

    Calvin on divine oversight: 'God's knowledge of human paths is not merely surveillance but care — he does not watch to condemn but to guide. Yet the same knowledge that guides the faithful becomes the ground of judgment for the foolish. God's omniscience is equally ground for comfort and warning.'

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    5:15

    The NET note on 'drink water from your own cistern' discusses the sexual metaphor: cistern/well = wife; water = sexual intimacy. The note identifies the same metaphor in Song of Songs 4:15 and discusses how the domestic water imagery (cisterns, wells, springs) creates a sustained metaphor for the appropriate containment and enjoyment of sexual desire within the covenant household.

    5:19

    The NET note on 'a loving doe, a graceful deer — may her breasts satisfy you always, may you ever be intoxicated with her love' discusses the animal simile (doe, deer as symbols of beauty and grace in ancient Near Eastern love poetry, cf. Song of Songs 2:7; 3:5) and the 'intoxication' language for legitimate marital love. The erotic register is intentional and positive — not despite its sensory specificity but because of it.

    Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    The adulteress
    The book's central antagonist
    Developed most fully here: speech like honey/oil, feet leading to death, paths that wander. Her defining characteristic is deception — she does not know where her own paths lead (v.6).
    The wife
    The positive alternative
    Not named; described with water imagery (cistern, well, fountain) and as a "loving doe." She is the real, available, covenant version of what the adulteress falsely offers.

    Ancient Sources

    Water as Sexuality Metaphor
    Cisterns, wells, springs, and fountains as sexual metaphors appear in Song 4:12 ("a spring enclosed, a sealed fountain") and are common in ANE love poetry. Proverbs uses the same imagery to ground the positive vision of marital intimacy.
    The water imagery connects Proverbs 5 to the broader ancient love-poetry tradition.
    Deathbed Regret Speech
    The anticipatory regret speech of vv.11–14 has parallels in Egyptian wisdom literature (Instruction of Any includes warnings about the end of the one who ignores instruction). But Proverbs' version is more psychologically specific — naming the loss of honour, wealth, and health.
    The anticipatory regret device is a distinctive feature of Proverbs' pedagogical strategy.

    Reception History

    Song of Songs
    The positive vision of ch.5 finds its full literary expression in the Song — the most extended celebration of marital love in Scripture.
    Augustine on disordered love
    Augustine's analysis of sin as disordered love (amor inordinatus) is the Augustinian development of Proverbs' insight: the adulteress offers a real good (pleasure) in a disordered way. The cure is not suppression of desire but redirection to the right object — the wife of your youth, and ultimately God.

    Literary Structure

    The honey-to-wormwood structure of the adulteress's appeal is Proverbs' clearest statement of folly's temporal strategy: offer pleasure first, conceal consequence. All temptation works by presenting the short-term gain and suppressing the long-term cost. Wisdom is the capacity to hold both simultaneously.

    Textual Notes

    5:16'Should your springs overflow in the streets'
    MT: yafutsu ma'yanoteykha chutsah (should your springs overflow in the streets). Interpretive question: is v.16 a statement ('your springs should not overflow in the streets') or a question ('why should your springs overflow in the streets?'). LXX reads as prohibition. Most English versions read as rhetorical question or statement.
    The interpretive ambiguity does not affect the main point: sexual intimacy is a private covenant gift, not a public shared resource. Whether read as prohibition ('do not let your springs overflow') or rhetorical question ('why would you let your springs overflow?'), the instruction is the same: marital intimacy is exclusively for the covenant partner.

    Scholarly Debates

    Does Proverbs 5 Endorse Sexual Desire or Merely Regulate It?
    Regulation of desire, not celebration
    Some interpreters
    The chapter's primary concern is protection from adultery. The positive commands (rejoice in your wife, be intoxicated with her love) are remedies for temptation rather than celebrations of sexuality in themselves. The chapter is prudential: use marital love to avoid the greater danger.
    Genuine celebration of marital sexuality
    Waltke, Fox, most recent commentators
    The erotic language of vv.15–19 is not merely functional (use marriage to avoid adultery) but genuinely celebratory. The 'intoxication' metaphor, the doe/deer simile, the 'at all times' qualifier all signal that marital sexual joy is itself a good to be pursued, not merely a prophylactic. The chapter integrates covenantal faithfulness with erotic celebration.
    The celebratory reading is more consistent with the chapter's imagery. Proverbs is not prudish — it endorses the full enjoyment of marital love as a genuine good. The comparison with Song of Songs (which shares vocabulary and imagery) supports reading vv.15–19 as wisdom's erotic theology, not merely its damage-limitation clause.

    Theological Themes Tracker

    Proverbs 5 is the book's most direct engagement with human sexuality — its dangers (the adulteress) and its proper gift (the faithful wife). The chapter refuses a purely negative ethic (avoid adultery) in favour of a positive vision: covenant faithfulness and erotic joy belong together. The fear of the LORD that opens the book produces in ch.5 a sexual ethic that is simultaneously moral, covenantal, and celebratory.
