Ezra 7

Ezra the Scribe

Verses 1–10 — Ezra’s Genealogy and Commission
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

סוֹפֵר sōpēr — scribe / teacher / expert: Ezra is called sōpēr māhîr — a "skilled scribe" or "expert teacher" of the Torah of Moses. The title is both literary (he copies/studies) and authoritative (he teaches/interprets). Ezra is the prototype of rabbinic Judaism: the scribe who replaces the prophet as the primary mediator of God’s word.

יַד yaḏ — hand: "The hand of the LORD his God was on him" (yaḏ-YHWH ʾelōāw) appears six times in Ezra 7–8 (7:6, 7:9, 7:28, 8:18, 8:22, 8:31). It is Ezra’s signature theological phrase — everything he accomplishes is attributed to divine enablement, not personal competence.

Historical Context

Ezra’s mission is dated to 458 BC, the seventh year of Artaxerxes I. This is approximately 58 years after the temple completion (516 BC). A new generation has arisen. Ezra’s role is not construction but formation: he comes to teach Torah and reform the community’s practice.

Context

Ezra’s genealogy (vv.1–5) traces his priestly lineage from Aaron through Zadok to Seraiah (the last chief priest before the exile, executed by Nebuchadnezzar in 2 Kgs 25:18–21). The genealogy is his credential: he is Aaronic, Zadokite, and the heir of the pre-exilic priestly establishment. He has both the bloodline and the expertise.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 25:18–21Seraiah, Ezra’s ancestor, executed by Nebuchadnezzar. The priestly line survived despite this.
  • Neh 8:1–12Ezra reads the Torah publicly — the culmination of the mission introduced here.
  • Mal 2:7"The lips of a priest ought to preserve knowledge" — Ezra embodies this ideal.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1–5

Ezra’s genealogy skips several generations (comparing with 1 Chr 6:3–15) to connect the key links: Aaron → Phinehas → Zadok → Seraiah → Ezra. The genealogy is theological, not exhaustive.

7:6

"The king had granted him everything he asked, for the hand of the LORD his God was on him." Royal favour is attributed to divine causation. Ezra does not boast of his persuasive skills; he confesses God’s hand.

7:9–10

The journey: 1st day of month 1 departure, 1st day of month 5 arrival — exactly four months for approximately 900 miles. Ezra "devoted himself to the study and observance of the Law of the LORD, and to teaching." Study, practice, teaching — the three-fold commitment of a true scribe.

7:10

The order is crucial: study first, then practice, then teaching. Ezra did not teach what he had not first learned and lived. This sequence is the foundation of authentic spiritual leadership.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:6

Calvin: "Ezra ascribes nothing to himself — he attributes the king’s favour to the hand of God. This is the habit of true piety: to trace every blessing to its source."

7:10

Calvin on Ezra’s commitment: "He studied to know, practised to live, and taught to transmit. This is the whole duty of a minister of God’s word."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:6

The NET notes sōpēr māhîr translates literally as "swift/skilled scribe." The term implies both speed of writing and depth of understanding — expertise, not merely penmanship.

7:10

The NET observes three infinitives in sequence: lidrōš (to seek/study), laʿaśōt (to do/practise), úləlammēḏ (to teach). The progression is intentional and pedagogically significant.

Williamson Notes

williamson
7:1–5

Williamson notes the gap between 6:22 and 7:1 spans fifty-eight years (516–458 BC) — the entire period is passed over in silence. The narrator is interested in two returns, not continuous history.

7:6

The title sōpēr māhîr may reflect a Persian bureaucratic appointment: Ezra as the imperial commissioner for Jewish law in Trans-Euphrates. Williamson sees both religious and administrative authority in the title.

Kidner Notes

kidner
7:6

Kidner: "A ready scribe — or better, a skilled one. Ezra’s expertise was in Torah, not administration. He was being sent not to govern but to teach. The most important thing a community needs after its buildings is its book."

7:10

Kidner on the three-fold commitment: "He had set his heart — the verb implies deliberate resolution, not mere inclination. Study, practice, teaching: the sequence cannot be rearranged without damage."

Verses 11–28 — Artaxerxes’ Letter; Ezra’s Doxology
111213141516171819202122232425262728

Hebrew Word Study

דָּת dāt — law / decree / religion: Artaxerxes authorises Ezra to enforce "the law (dāt) of your God and the law of the king." The Aramaic dāt (from Old Persian dāta) covers both religious and civil law. Persian imperial policy: local religious law has the force of imperial law within the community.

Historical Context

Artaxerxes’ letter (vv.12–26, in Aramaic) is one of the most generous imperial grants in the Bible. It authorises: unlimited silver and gold from the royal treasury; tax exemption for all temple personnel; judicial authority to enforce Torah with penalties up to death. The generosity likely reflects Persian policy of using local religious leaders to maintain order in provinces.

Context

The letter shifts back to Aramaic (vv.12–26) for the imperial document, then returns to Hebrew for Ezra’s personal response (vv.27–28). The language shift mirrors Ezra 6: Aramaic for official business, Hebrew for covenant reflection. Ezra’s doxology (vv.27–28) is his only first-person speech so far — and it is pure praise.

Cross-Reference

  • Neh 2:1–8Artaxerxes grants Nehemiah a similar commission thirteen years later (445 BC) — the same king, the same policy of supporting Jewish restoration.
  • Dan 6:25–27Darius’s decree honouring Daniel’s God — a parallel pattern of Persian kings endorsing YHWH.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:12–13

Artaxerxes calls Ezra "priest and teacher of the Law of the God of heaven." The Persian king recognises Ezra’s religious authority and commissions it for imperial purposes.

7:15–16

The silver and gold from the king and his advisers is "freely offered to the God of Israel, whose dwelling is in Jerusalem." A Persian king makes a freewill offering to YHWH. The irony is exquisite.

7:23

"Whatever the God of heaven has prescribed, let it be done with diligence" — Artaxerxes commands obedience to Torah. Imperial decree enforces divine law.

7:25–26

Ezra receives judicial authority: anyone who disobeys Torah faces "death, banishment, confiscation, or imprisonment." This is a theocratic commission backed by imperial force.

7:27–28

Ezra’s doxology: "Praise be to the LORD." His first personal words are not about himself but about God. The "hand of the LORD my God was on me" — the signature phrase again.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:23

Calvin: "When even a heathen king commands diligent obedience to God’s law, how much more should believers honour it. The shame of the church is that pagans sometimes show more respect for God’s word than God’s own people."

7:27–28

Calvin on Ezra’s praise: "He did not attribute anything to his own wisdom or reputation. The hand of God — this was the only cause he would acknowledge."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:14

The NET notes "seven counsellors" matches the known Persian administrative structure: Herodotus (3.31) and Esth 1:14 both reference seven principal advisers to the Persian king.

7:26

Four penalties listed in ascending severity: death, banishment, confiscation of property, imprisonment. The NET observes this matches Achaemenid judicial practice attested in other sources.

Williamson Notes

williamson
7:12–26

Williamson argues the letter is authentic Aramaic, not a Jewish composition. The formulae, vocabulary, and legal provisions are consistent with known Persian administrative practice. The generosity reflects imperial policy, not Jewish fantasy.

7:25–26

The judicial commission makes Ezra a kind of Persian-appointed judge-commissioner: he has authority to enforce Torah and imperial law simultaneously. Williamson calls this "a remarkable merging of sacred and secular authority."

Kidner Notes

kidner
7:21–23

Kidner: "Artaxerxes’ letter reads like a convert’s enthusiasm, but is more likely a politician’s prudence: keep the local deity happy, and the province stays peaceful. God can use pragmatism as readily as piety."

7:27–28

Ezra’s doxology: Kidner notes this is the first time the narrator says "I" — the shift from third to first person marks the most intimate moment in the book so far. And that moment is praise, not autobiography.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Ezra
Priest-scribe; leader of the second return
Descended from Aaron through Zadok and Seraiah. Called a "skilled scribe in the Law of Moses." His three-fold commitment — study, practice, teaching — makes him the prototype of rabbinic Judaism. "The hand of the LORD his God was on him."
Artaxerxes I
King of Persia; commissions Ezra
Artaxerxes I Longimanus (465–424 BC) grants Ezra extraordinary authority: unlimited treasury access, tax exemptions for temple staff, judicial power to enforce Torah. He later commissions Nehemiah (445 BC).
