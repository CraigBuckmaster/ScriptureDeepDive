Daniel 11

The Kings of the North and South

Verses 1–20 — “From Persia to the Seleucids”
1234567891011121314151617181920

Hebrew Word Study

מֶלֶךְ melek̅ — king: The most detailed predictive prophecy in the Bible. The angel traces kings from Persia (v.2: three more kings then Xerxes), through Alexander (v.3: divided to the four winds), to the Ptolemies (south) and Seleucids (north) warring over Israel for two centuries. Every verse corresponds to documented history.

Context

This chapter contains more historically verifiable predictions than any other passage in the Bible. The Ptolemaic-Seleucid wars (vv.5–20) can be mapped verse by verse onto the historical record from 323 to 175 BC. The "beautiful land" (v.16) is Israel, caught between the two rival Hellenistic kingdoms. The marriage alliance of v.6 is the union of Berenice (Ptolemy II’s daughter) and Antiochus II (c.250 BC). The invasion of v.7–8 is Ptolemy III’s retaliatory campaign.

  • 1 Macc 1–6The Maccabean narrative that documents the events Daniel 11 prophesies.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:2–20

Calvin traces each verse to its historical fulfilment, marvelling at the precision. He uses the chapter as evidence for the divine inspiration of Scripture: no human author in the 6th century could have predicted 3rd–2nd century politics with this accuracy.

11:16

Calvin notes the "beautiful land" (Israel) is the stage on which empires perform. Israel’s geography — the land bridge between Africa and Asia — makes it the eternal battleground of empires.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:2

The NET identifies the four Persian kings as Cambyses, pseudo-Smerdis, Darius I, and Xerxes I. The "fourth" whose wealth provokes conflict with Greece is Xerxes, whose invasion of Greece (480 BC) set the stage for Alexander’s retaliatory conquest.

11:5–20

The NET provides the most detailed verse-by-verse historical identifications, noting that scholars achieve nearly complete agreement on the Ptolemaic-Seleucid correspondences.

Longman Notes

longman
11:2–20

Longman: the chapter’s historical precision is either the strongest evidence for supernatural prophecy or the strongest evidence for late dating. There is no neutral position. The reader’s prior commitments determine which conclusion is drawn.

11:5–15

Longman notes the moral pattern beneath the political detail: every king’s rise is temporary, every alliance is broken, every victory is reversed. The political lesson: no human arrangement endures.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
11:2–20

Goldingay reads the chapter as "interpreted history" — political events given theological significance. The details are not trivia but evidence: God governs the specific movements of empires, not just their general trajectory.

11:16

Goldingay: the "beautiful land" stands at the intersection of every geopolitical conflict. Israel’s centrality is not accidental but providential: the land of promise is also the land of testing.

Collins Notes

collins
11:1–20

Collins notes this is the passage that most sharply divides conservative and critical scholars on Daniel’s date. Critical scholars argue no 6th-century author could produce this precision; the prophecy must have been written after the events (vaticinium ex eventu). Conservative scholars counter that divine inspiration explains the accuracy.

11:16

Collins identifies "the beautiful land" (eret-hatstsevi) as Israel, the same term as 8:9. The land’s beauty is theological, not merely geographical: it is the place of God’s dwelling.

Verses 21–45 — “The Abomination That Causes Desolation”

Hebrew Word Study

שִׁיקוּץ shiqquts — abomination: "They will set up the abomination (shiqquts) that causes desolation" (v.31). Antiochus IV Epiphanes: the "contemptible person" (v.21) who seizes power through intrigue. He invades Egypt, is humiliated by Rome (v.30, "ships of Kittim"), takes out his rage on Jerusalem. He abolishes the daily sacrifice and sets up the abomination — an altar to Zeus in the temple. "Those who know their God will firmly resist" (v.32). Verses 36–45 may shift to a future antichrist: "the king who exalts himself above every god" (v.36).

Context

Antiochus IV Epiphanes (175–164 BC) is the "contemptible person" of v.21. His desecration of the Jerusalem temple in December 167 BC is the historical event behind "the abomination of desolation" (v.31). Jesus references this in the Olivet Discourse (Matt 24:15), applying it to a future event (possibly the Roman destruction of AD 70 or an end-times desecration). The transition at v.36 is debated: many scholars see the description exceeding Antiochus and pointing to a future eschatological figure.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:31

"They will set up the abomination that causes desolation." Jesus quotes this in Matt 24:15 as a sign of the end.

11:32–33

"The people who know their God will firmly resist. Those who are wise will instruct many." Knowing God produces resistance; wisdom produces teaching under persecution.

11:35

"Some of the wise will stumble, so that they may be refined." Suffering as purification. The refining has a terminus: "the time of the end."

11:36

"The king will exalt himself above every god." At this point, many see the text shifting to a future figure. Self-exaltation "above every god" exceeds Antiochus and echoes 2 Thess 2:4.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:31

Calvin identifies the abomination as Antiochus’s altar to Zeus in the temple (167 BC). He then notes Jesus’ reapplication to a future event, suggesting the pattern of temple-desecration recurs across history.

11:32–35

Calvin takes special encouragement from "those who know their God will firmly resist." He applies this directly to Protestants facing persecution: knowledge of God produces courage under tyranny.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:31

The NET provides the historical detail: Antiochus sent his general Apollonius to Jerusalem, built a fortress (Akra), and erected an altar to Zeus Olympios on the temple’s great altar on December 25, 167 BC.

11:36–45

The NET notes the scholarly debate on whether these verses describe Antiochus (continuing the sequence) or a future figure (breaking the historical sequence). The language of v.36 ("exalts himself above every god") seems to exceed what any historical figure achieved.

Longman Notes

longman
11:31

Longman: the "abomination of desolation" creates a prophetic chain: Daniel prophesies it → Antiochus fulfils it → Jesus reapplies it → the NT expects a final fulfilment. Each iteration amplifies the pattern.

11:32–35

Longman: "those who know their God" is the chapter’s pastoral centre. In the midst of the most detailed political prophecy in the Bible, the essential message is personal: know God, and you will stand firm.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
11:31

Goldingay: the "abomination" (shiqquts) is a contemptuous pun on the Phoenician deity Baal Shamem (“Lord of Heaven”), repointed as shiqquts shomem (“abomination of desolation”). The wordplay is deliberate theological mockery.

11:36–45

Goldingay reads the transition at v.36 as the text’s eschatological horizon expanding: the historical Antiochus becomes a type of the ultimate opponent of God. The specific becomes paradigmatic.

Collins Notes

collins
11:21–45

Collins provides detailed verse-by-verse historical identifications through v.39. He notes that vv.40–45 do not correspond to any known event in Antiochus’s career — suggesting either unfulfilled prophecy or a shift in referent.

11:31–35

Collins reads the "wise" (maskilim) as the forerunners of the apocalyptic community, possibly proto-Essenes. Their response to persecution is not military (unlike the Maccabees) but instructional and martyrological.

Ancient Sources

The Prayer of Nabonidus (4Q242, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A fragmentary Qumran text describes a Babylonian king struck with illness for seven years who is healed by a Jewish exorcist — strikingly parallel to Nebuchadnezzar’s madness in Daniel 4.
The Prayer of Nabonidus may preserve an older version of the tradition behind Daniel 4, or reflect a common Near Eastern motif of royal humbling. Either way, it confirms the antiquity of the Daniel traditions.
1 Maccabees 1–2 (2nd c. BC)
Describes the historical desecration of the Jerusalem temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC — the event Daniel 8:9–14 and 11:31 prophesy as the “abomination of desolation.”
The fulfilment is documented in non-biblical Jewish sources, confirming Daniel’s prophetic precision whether the prophecy is dated to the 6th or 2nd century BC.
Josephus, Antiquities 10.11 (1st c. AD)
Josephus records that when Alexander the Great approached Jerusalem (332 BC), the high priest showed him Daniel’s prophecy about a Greek king conquering Persia, and Alexander was so impressed he offered sacrifices at the temple.
Whether historically accurate or legendary, Josephus’s account shows that Daniel’s prophecies were understood as referring to Greek conquest well before the Christian era.
