Daniel 6

Daniel in the Lion’s Den

Verses 1–14 — “He Still Prays Three Times a Day”
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

בָּרֵךְ bārēk̅ — to kneel / to bless: "Three times a day he got down on his knees (bārēk̅) and prayed, giving thanks to his God, just as he had done before" (v.10). Darius appoints Daniel as administrator. Rivals find no corruption. They target his religion. The irrevocable decree: pray to no god except the king for thirty days. Daniel opens his window toward Jerusalem and prays exactly as before. "Just as he had done before" is the key: continuity IS resistance.

Context

The irrevocable decree reflects the Medo-Persian legal principle that royal edicts, once signed, could not be revoked (cf. Esther 1:19; 8:8). Darius is trapped by his own law. Daniel’s prayer posture — kneeling, windows open toward Jerusalem — follows Solomon’s prayer at the temple dedication (1 Kgs 8:48). Three-times-daily prayer (morning, afternoon, evening) became standard Jewish practice and may originate from this passage. The stone sealed with the king’s signet (v.17) creates tomb-imagery that the NT develops.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 8:48Solomon: if they pray toward this city and this temple — the precedent for Daniel’s Jerusalem-facing prayer.
  • Ps 55:17Evening, morning and noon I cry out to God — the three-times-daily prayer Daniel practises.
  • Matt 27:60–66Joseph’s tomb sealed with a stone and a guard — the den/tomb parallel with the stone and seal.
  • Acts 5:29We must obey God rather than human beings — the apostles follow Daniel’s precedent of civil disobedience.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:10

Calvin reads Daniel’s unchanged prayer routine as a rejection of both defiance and cowardice: he does not add drama (to provoke) or reduce frequency (to hide). He simply continues.

6:14

Calvin notes Darius’s genuine distress: the king who signed the decree now works all day to rescue the man it targets. The law’s rigidity becomes a parable of law without grace.

NET Bible Notes

Longman Notes

longman
6:3–5

Longman: the phrase "no corruption, trustworthy, neither corrupt nor negligent" is the most comprehensive character reference in the OT. Daniel’s career survives the most thorough investigation his enemies can conduct.

6:10

Longman emphasises "just as he had done before" as the interpretive key: faithfulness is not a crisis response but a daily habit. The den tests a practice that has been consistent for decades.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
6:5

Goldingay: "unless it has to do with the law of his God" is the enemies’ inadvertent confession. They know Daniel’s faith is his only vulnerability. Integrity makes persecution predictable.

6:10

Goldingay reads the open window as Daniel’s refusal to treat faith as a private matter. In an empire that demands religious conformity, visible difference is the act of resistance.

Collins Notes

collins
6:1–14

Collins identifies the narrative pattern as a "courtier-in-danger" tale, common across ancient Near Eastern literature. The distinctiveness is theological: Daniel’s danger comes specifically from his religious practice, not from political intrigue.

6:10

Collins notes that the "open windows" are an act of public, not private, worship. Daniel does not pray in secret; he prays visibly. The courage is in the publicity.

Verses 15–28 — “My God Sent His Angel and Shut the Mouths of the Lions”
1516171819202122232425262728

Hebrew Word Study

מַלְאַךְ malʾak̅ — angel / messenger: "My God sent his angel (malʾak̅) and shut the mouths of the lions" (v.22). Daniel thrown in; stone sealed with the king’s signet. Darius fasts all night. At dawn: "Daniel, has your God been able to rescue you?" Daniel answers: alive, unhurt. Darius orders the accusers thrown in — devoured before reaching the floor. Darius decrees: "In every part of my kingdom people must fear the God of Daniel" (v.26). Daniel prospers through Darius’s reign and into Cyrus’s (v.28).

Context

The sealed stone and the king’s signet create deliberate tomb-imagery. The dawn visit, the question expecting no answer, the impossible survival — the parallels to the empty tomb are structural, not incidental. The punishment of the accusers and their families reflects ancient Near Eastern collective punishment practice (cf. Esther 9). Darius’s decree is the third pagan-ruler confession in Daniel (after Nebuchadnezzar twice), progressively expanding the recognition of God’s sovereignty.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 91:13You will tread on the lion and the cobra — divine protection from dangerous animals.
  • Heb 11:33Who through faith shut the mouths of lions — the Hebrews 11 citation of this event.
  • 2 Tim 4:17The Lord rescued me from the lion’s mouth — Paul uses Daniel’s imagery for his own deliverance.
  • Matt 28:1–6At dawn, the women went to the tomb. The stone was rolled away. "He is not here" — the resurrection parallels the den-opening.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:20–22

Calvin: the king’s question "has your God been able?" expects "no." Daniel’s "yes" reverses everything. Calvin sees this as the pattern of all faith: the question the world asks in despair, the answer God provides in power.

6:26–27

Calvin reads Darius’s decree as the most comprehensive pagan confession in Daniel: "living God," "endures forever," "kingdom will not be destroyed," "rescues and saves," "performs signs and wonders." Five theological affirmations from a polytheist.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:17

The NET notes the double seal (king’s signet + nobles’ signet) ensures no tampering from either side. The security measures underscore the impossibility of human rescue.

6:22

The NET observes that "found innocent" (zakah) implies a legal acquittal: the lions’ den functioned as a divine court, and God’s verdict is "not guilty."

Longman Notes

longman
6:22

Longman: "my God sent his angel" connects to the fourth figure in the furnace (3:25, 28). The pattern: God’s presence in the place of death, whether fire or lions. The angel does not remove the danger but neutralises it from within.

6:28

Longman notes that Daniel "prospered" through both the Medo-Persian and Persian periods. The verb is emphatic: not just survived but thrived. Faithfulness produces long-term flourishing.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
6:17

Goldingay: the stone and seal create a narrative coffin. Daniel is entombed alive. The morning opening is a resurrection narrative in miniature.

6:26–28

Goldingay reads the three pagan confessions (Nebuchadnezzar 2×, Darius 1×) as the cumulative argument of the court-tales: the nations themselves testify that Israel’s God is sovereign. The witnesses are the empire’s own rulers.

Collins Notes

collins
6:16–24

Collins compares the sealed-stone motif to the sealed tomb tradition: stone placed, authority’s seal applied, impossible escape expected. The resurrection parallels are structural and may have shaped early Christian narration of the empty tomb.

6:26–28

Collins notes that Darius’s decree uses the same formula as Nebuchadnezzar’s (ch.4): "his kingdom will not be destroyed." The Medo-Persian king echoes the Babylonian king. The confession transfers across empires.

Ancient Sources

The Prayer of Nabonidus (4Q242, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A fragmentary Qumran text describes a Babylonian king struck with illness for seven years who is healed by a Jewish exorcist — strikingly parallel to Nebuchadnezzar’s madness in Daniel 4.
The Prayer of Nabonidus may preserve an older version of the tradition behind Daniel 4, or reflect a common Near Eastern motif of royal humbling. Either way, it confirms the antiquity of the Daniel traditions.
1 Maccabees 1–2 (2nd c. BC)
Describes the historical desecration of the Jerusalem temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC — the event Daniel 8:9–14 and 11:31 prophesy as the “abomination of desolation.”
The fulfilment is documented in non-biblical Jewish sources, confirming Daniel’s prophetic precision whether the prophecy is dated to the 6th or 2nd century BC.
Josephus, Antiquities 10.11 (1st c. AD)
Josephus records that when Alexander the Great approached Jerusalem (332 BC), the high priest showed him Daniel’s prophecy about a Greek king conquering Persia, and Alexander was so impressed he offered sacrifices at the temple.
Whether historically accurate or legendary, Josephus’s account shows that Daniel’s prophecies were understood as referring to Greek conquest well before the Christian era.
