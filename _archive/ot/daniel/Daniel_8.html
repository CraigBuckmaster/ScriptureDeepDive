Daniel 8

The Ram and the Goat

Verses 1–14 — “The Shaggy Goat Is the King of Greece”
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

חָזוֹן ḥāzōn — vision: "In the vision (ḥāzōn) I saw a ram standing beside the canal" (v.3). A two-horned ram (Medo-Persia) charges west, north, south. A goat with a prominent horn (Greece/Alexander) crosses the earth without touching ground and shatters the ram. At the height of power, the horn breaks; four replace it. From one comes a small horn that grows toward the Beautiful Land, takes away the daily sacrifice, desecrates the sanctuary for 2,300 evenings and mornings.

Context

The vision shifts back to Hebrew (from Aramaic in chs.2–7), signalling a more Israel-focused perspective. The canal at Susa (Ulai, v.2) places Daniel in the administrative capital of the future Persian empire — he receives the vision at the geographical seat of the empire that will replace Babylon. The 2,300 evenings and mornings (v.14) has been calculated as either 1,150 days (evening + morning = 1 day) or 2,300 literal days, both approximately matching the period of Antiochus IV’s temple desecration (167–164 BC).

Cross-Reference

  • Dan 2:39The third kingdom of bronze — the same Greek empire described here as a goat.
  • Dan 11:3–4A mighty king whose kingdom will be divided to the four winds — the same Alexander/Diadochi event.
  • 1 Macc 1:10–64The historical account of Antiochus IV’s desecration that Daniel 8:9–14 prophesies.
  • Matt 24:15Jesus: "When you see the abomination of desolation spoken of through Daniel" — referencing this chapter and ch.11.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:3–4

The ram with two horns: Medo-Persia. The longer horn emerged later — Persia dominated. The ram charges in three directions: the exact pattern of Persian conquest.

8:5–7

The goat from the west: Greece. "Without touching the ground" — the speed of Alexander’s conquest (334–323 BC). The single horn: Alexander himself.

8:8

At the height of power, the horn breaks. Four replace it. Alexander’s death at 32 and the four Diadochi.

8:9–12

The small horn: Antiochus IV Epiphanes (175–164 BC). He desecrated the temple, prohibited Jewish worship, erected an altar to Zeus.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:3–8

Calvin reads the ram-and-goat vision as confirming ch.2’s statue with greater specificity. God repeats the message with increasing detail so that fulfilment can be verified with precision.

8:9–14

Calvin identifies the little horn as Antiochus IV and sees the 2,300 evenings and mornings as the period of temple defilement. The specificity of the timeline is a divine gift for the persecuted: suffering is measured and will end.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:5

The NET notes that "without touching the ground" is the most vivid description of military speed in the OT. Alexander conquered from Greece to India in eleven years (334–323 BC).

8:14

The NET provides both calculations: 2,300 literal evenings and mornings = 2,300 days (~6.3 years), or 1,150 days (~3.15 years) if each evening-morning pair counts as one day. The shorter figure approximates the desecration period.

Longman Notes

longman
8:5–7

Longman: the goat’s speed ("without touching the ground") is not just military rapidity but supernatural enablement. Alexander’s conquests exceeded normal strategic possibility. God empowered the instrument of his plan.

8:9–14

Longman reads the small horn as both historically Antiochus and typologically pointing to a future antichrist figure. The type-antitype relationship means the historical fulfilment does not exhaust the prophetic meaning.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
8:1–14

Goldingay notes the shift from Aramaic to Hebrew signals a narrowing of focus: chs.2–7 (Aramaic) address the nations; chs.8–12 (Hebrew) address Israel specifically. The linguistic shift is a theological signal.

8:9–14

Goldingay reads the "beautiful land" (hats-tsevi) as both geographical (Israel) and theological (the place of God’s presence). The desecration is not just territorial but sacramental.

Collins Notes

collins
8:1–14

Collins notes this is the most historically specific vision in Daniel: ram = Medo-Persia (v.20), goat = Greece (v.21), large horn = Alexander (v.21), four horns = four successor kingdoms (v.22). No other OT prophecy names the empires this explicitly.

8:9–14

Collins identifies the "small horn" as Antiochus IV Epiphanes with virtual certainty. The description matches the historical record: he attacked the "beautiful land" (Israel), removed the daily sacrifice, and desecrated the sanctuary (1 Macc 1:41–64).

Verses 15–27 — “The Vision Concerns the Time of the End”
15161718192021222324252627

Hebrew Word Study

קֵץ qēṣ — end: "The vision concerns the time of the end (qēṣ)" (v.17). Gabriel appears — the first named angel in the Bible (v.16). Gabriel explains: ram = Medo-Persia, goat = Greece, four horns = four kingdoms. A fierce king will destroy mighty and holy people (v.24), stand against the Prince of princes, but be destroyed "not by human power" (v.25). Daniel is exhausted and ill for days; "appalled by the vision; it was beyond understanding" (v.27).

Context

Gabriel’s first biblical appearance. The same angel later announces John the Baptist’s birth (Luke 1:19) and Jesus’s conception (Luke 1:26). The phrase "not by human power" (v.25) echoes the "not by human hands" stone of 2:34 — God’s interventions consistently bypass human instrumentality. Daniel’s physical devastation (v.27) is the consistent response of prophets to apocalyptic revelation: not triumph but trauma.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 1:19I am Gabriel. I stand in the presence of God — the same angel, centuries later, announces the new era.
  • Luke 1:26Gabriel was sent from God to Nazareth — the angel of Daniel’s visions becomes the angel of the incarnation.
  • Dan 2:34A stone cut out, not by human hands — the same "not by human" pattern as 8:25.
  • Rev 19:20The beast was captured and thrown into the lake of fire — the ultimate fulfilment of the horn’s destruction.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:15–16

"Gabriel, tell this man the meaning." Gabriel’s first appearance in Scripture.

8:20–22

"The two-horned ram = Medo-Persia. The shaggy goat = Greece. The large horn = the first king." The most explicit kingdom-identification in Daniel.

8:25

"He will take his stand against the Prince of princes. Yet he will be destroyed, but not by human power." Antiochus’s end: not military defeat but madness and disease (1 Macc 6:8–16).

8:27

"I was worn out. I was appalled by the vision; it was beyond understanding." Seeing the future costs the seer physically.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:15–17

Calvin notes that Gabriel’s appearance produces terror, not comfort. Angels are not soft; they are overwhelming. The fear of the LORD begins with genuine fear.

8:25–27

Calvin reads Daniel’s exhaustion as evidence of the vision’s authenticity: a fabricator would report triumph, not trauma. The prophet’s suffering validates the revelation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:16

The NET notes that Gabriel (“man of God” or “God is my warrior”) is one of only two angels named in the Protestant canon (the other: Michael, 10:13).

8:25

The NET observes that "destroyed, but not by human power" matches the historical record: Antiochus IV died of disease during a campaign in Persia (164 BC), not in battle. The death was perceived as divine judgment.

Longman Notes

longman
8:25

Longman: "not by human power" is the consistent Danielic principle: empires are not overthrown by other empires (human power) but by divine intervention. The pattern: Babylon falls to God’s plan (ch.5), Antiochus falls to God’s disease (ch.8), all kingdoms fall to God’s stone (ch.2).

8:27

Longman: Daniel’s illness is not weakness but appropriate response. The human frame is not designed to bear apocalyptic knowledge without cost.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
8:17

Goldingay: "the vision concerns the time of the end" introduces eschatological language. "The end" (qēṣ) does not mean the end of the world but the end of the current crisis. Daniel’s "end" is Antiochus’s downfall; the NT’s "end" extends the concept.

8:27

Goldingay notes that Daniel goes back to work ("I went about the king’s business") after his collapse. The visionary returns to ordinary life. Apocalyptic knowledge does not exempt from daily duty.

Collins Notes

collins
8:15–27

Collins notes the angelus interpres (interpreting angel) motif: the apocalyptic visionary sees but cannot understand; an angel provides the interpretation. This becomes standard in apocalyptic literature (Zechariah, 4 Ezra, Revelation).

8:25

Collins argues the "Prince of princes" (sar sarim) is God himself, making the horn’s offence theologically ultimate: not just attacking Israel but opposing God directly.

Ancient Sources

The Prayer of Nabonidus (4Q242, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A fragmentary Qumran text describes a Babylonian king struck with illness for seven years who is healed by a Jewish exorcist — strikingly parallel to Nebuchadnezzar’s madness in Daniel 4.
The Prayer of Nabonidus may preserve an older version of the tradition behind Daniel 4, or reflect a common Near Eastern motif of royal humbling. Either way, it confirms the antiquity of the Daniel traditions.
1 Maccabees 1–2 (2nd c. BC)
Describes the historical desecration of the Jerusalem temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC — the event Daniel 8:9–14 and 11:31 prophesy as the “abomination of desolation.”
The fulfilment is documented in non-biblical Jewish sources, confirming Daniel’s prophetic precision whether the prophecy is dated to the 6th or 2nd century BC.
Josephus, Antiquities 10.11 (1st c. AD)
Josephus records that when Alexander the Great approached Jerusalem (332 BC), the high priest showed him Daniel’s prophecy about a Greek king conquering Persia, and Alexander was so impressed he offered sacrifices at the temple.
Whether historically accurate or legendary, Josephus’s account shows that Daniel’s prophecies were understood as referring to Greek conquest well before the Christian era.
