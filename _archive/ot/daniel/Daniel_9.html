Daniel 9

The Seventy Weeks

Verses 1–19 — “We Have Sinned and Done Wrong”
12345678910111213141516171819

Hebrew Word Study

תְחִנָּה təḥinnâ — plea for mercy: "I turned to the Lord God and pleaded with him in prayer and petition (təḥinnâ), in fasting, and in sackcloth and ashes" (v.3). Daniel reads Jeremiah’s prophecy of 70 years (v.2; cf. Jer 25:11–12). He responds with confession: "We have sinned and done wrong" (v.5). The prayer includes himself ("we"). It confesses sin, acknowledges God’s justice, and appeals to mercy: "We do not make requests because of our righteousness, but because of your great mercy" (v.18).

Context

The first year of Darius the Mede (v.1), approximately 539 BC. Daniel reads Jeremiah 25:11–12 and 29:10, which prophesied 70 years of Babylonian exile. The exile began in 605 BC (Daniel’s own deportation), meaning 66 years have passed. Daniel’s prayer is not speculative but responsive to Scripture: he reads the promise and prays it into fulfilment. The prayer (vv.4–19) is one of the great confessions of the OT, paralleling Nehemiah 9 and Ezra 9.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 25:11–12This whole country will be a desolate wasteland for seventy years — the text Daniel is reading.
  • Jer 29:10When seventy years are completed, I will bring you back — the promise that triggers Daniel’s prayer.
  • Neh 9:5–37Nehemiah’s great confession prayer — the same genre as Daniel 9:4–19.
  • Rom 3:25God presented Christ as a sacrifice of atonement — the NT fulfilment of the atonement Daniel’s prayer anticipates.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:2–3

Daniel reads Jeremiah. Scripture interprets Scripture. His response to reading God’s word is prayer, not speculation.

9:4–6

"Lord, the great and awesome God who keeps his covenant. We have sinned." Covenant confession: God faithful; we unfaithful.

9:17–19

"For your sake, Lord, look with favour on your desolate sanctuary. Not because of our righteousness, but your great mercy." The appeal is to God’s character, not Israel’s merit.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:2–3

Calvin notes that Daniel’s study of Jeremiah demonstrates that prophets read and build upon earlier prophets. Scripture is cumulative; each generation’s revelation builds on the previous.

9:18–19

Calvin emphasises the prayer’s basis: not Israel’s righteousness but God’s great mercy. Calvin sees this as the foundational prayer-theology of the Reformation: sola gratia expressed in OT confession.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:2

The NET notes that Daniel "understood from the Scriptures" (bin min-hassefarim) — the first recorded instance of a prophet studying written Scripture as normative revelation.

9:18

The NET observes the prayer’s climactic shift: from "we have sinned" (confession) to "because of your great mercy" (appeal). The basis shifts from human failure to divine character.

Longman Notes

longman
9:3–19

Longman: Daniel’s prayer is a model of intercession. He does not pray FOR Israel from a distance but AS Israel with personal identification: "we have sinned." The intercessor includes himself in the confession.

9:18

Longman: "because of your great mercy" is the hinge of the prayer. Everything before this verse is about human failure; everything after is about divine character. The transition is the theology of grace.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
9:2

Goldingay: Daniel reading Jeremiah is the OT’s clearest example of canonical hermeneutics — one prophet interpreting another. The 70 years is not a deadline Daniel passively awaits but a promise he actively prays into fulfilment.

9:4–19

Goldingay notes the prayer contains no petition for the 70-weeks revelation. Daniel prays for mercy and restoration; God answers with an apocalyptic timetable he didn’t ask for. The answer exceeds the prayer.

Collins Notes

collins
9:1–19

Collins identifies the prayer as the most liturgically developed passage in Daniel — it follows the standard deuteronomistic confession pattern (historical review, sin acknowledgment, appeal to covenant). Collins suggests it may have been composed independently and incorporated into the Daniel narrative.

9:2

Collins notes the hermeneutical significance: Daniel interprets Jeremiah’s 70 years, and then Gabriel reinterprets Daniel’s interpretation as 70 "sevens" (490 years). Scripture generates interpretation that generates further interpretation.

Verses 20–27 — “Seventy Sevens Are Decreed”
2021222324252627

Hebrew Word Study

שָׁבֻעִים shābuʿîm — weeks / sevens: "Seventy sevens (shābuʿîm) are decreed for your people" (v.24). Gabriel arrives while Daniel still prays. 490 years decreed for six purposes: finish transgression, end sin, atone for wickedness, bring everlasting righteousness, seal prophecy, anoint the Most Holy (v.24). Timeline: 7 + 62 sevens until the Anointed One (v.25). The Anointed One "cut off and will have nothing" (v.26). The most debated prophecy in the Bible.

Context

The 70-weeks prophecy is the most interpreted passage in Daniel. Four main frameworks: (1) The traditional-messianic reading: 69 sevens from Artaxerxes’ decree (445 BC, Neh 2:1–8) to Christ’s crucifixion (~AD 33), with the 70th week future (the tribulation). (2) The critical reading: all 70 sevens fulfilled by the Maccabean period, ending with Antiochus’s death (164 BC). (3) The symbolic reading: 70 × 7 = the complete span of judgment, not a precise chronology. (4) The christological reading: 69 sevens to Christ’s ministry, the 70th week is the cross and destruction of Jerusalem (AD 70). No consensus exists across traditions.

Cross-Reference

  • Lev 25:8Count off seven sabbath years — seven times seven years — the jubilee framework behind the 70 sevens.
  • Matt 26:28This is my blood of the covenant, poured out for the forgiveness of sins — the atonement Daniel 9:24 anticipates.
  • Gal 4:4When the set time had fully come, God sent his Son — the "fullness of time" as the 69 sevens completing.
  • Heb 9:26He has appeared once for all at the culmination of the ages to do away with sin — the "finish transgression" of Dan 9:24.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:24

"Seventy sevens are decreed to finish transgression, end sin, atone for wickedness, bring everlasting righteousness." Six purposes. The first three negative (end sin); last three positive (establish righteousness). The cross accomplishes all six.

9:25

"From the decree to restore Jerusalem until the Anointed One: seven sevens and sixty-two sevens." 69 sevens (483 years). From Artaxerxes’ decree (445 BC) to Christ’s triumphal entry.

9:26

"The Anointed One will be cut off and will have nothing." Cut off: killed. Have nothing: no kingdom. The cross.

9:27

"In the middle of the seven he will put an end to sacrifice." The final seven: the most debated half-week in prophetic literature.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:24

Calvin reads the six purposes as fulfilled in Christ’s first advent: transgression finished, sin ended, wickedness atoned, righteousness established, prophecy sealed, the Most Holy (Christ) anointed. All are accomplished at the cross.

9:26–27

Calvin identifies the Anointed One cut off as Christ and the ruler who destroys the city as Titus (AD 70). The 70th week encompasses Christ’s ministry and the destruction of Jerusalem.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:24

The NET notes the six infinitives describe the total programme of the 490 years. The first three are restorative (ending sin’s effects); the last three are constructive (establishing God’s order). Together they describe complete salvation.

9:25–27

The NET provides both the traditional-messianic and critical-Maccabean calculations, noting that neither produces an exact chronological fit without some adjustment. The passage may function more as theological pattern than precise calendar.

Longman Notes

longman
9:24

Longman: the six purposes describe what only the cross achieves. No other event in history can claim to have "finished transgression" and "brought in everlasting righteousness." The prophecy finds its only adequate fulfilment in Christ.

9:26

Longman: "cut off and will have nothing" is the most compressed crucifixion-prophecy in the OT. The Messiah dies (cut off), receives no earthly kingdom (has nothing), and yet precisely through this apparent failure, the six purposes of v.24 are accomplished.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
9:24–27

Goldingay reads the 70 sevens as a reinterpretation of Jeremiah’s 70 years: not 70 literal years but 70 sabbatical cycles (490 years). The concept is jubilee-scaled: the exile is not 70 years of punishment but 490 years of preparation for the ultimate jubilee.

9:26

Goldingay: the phrase "cut off and will have nothing" is startling in its bleakness. The Anointed One is not triumphant but eliminated. The text forces the reader to ask: how can the Messiah die and God’s purposes still succeed? The NT answers: the death IS the success.

Collins Notes

collins
9:24–27

Collins argues the original reference is to the Maccabean period: the 62 sevens end with the murder of the high priest Onias III (171 BC), and the "one who causes desolation" is Antiochus IV. Collins acknowledges the christological reading as a legitimate second-level interpretation but not the original historical meaning.

9:26

Collins notes that "cut off" (yikkaret) is covenant-curse language (cf. Gen 17:14; Lev 7:20). The Anointed One suffers the covenant penalty, which is precisely the theology of substitutionary atonement.

Ancient Sources

The Prayer of Nabonidus (4Q242, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A fragmentary Qumran text describes a Babylonian king struck with illness for seven years who is healed by a Jewish exorcist — strikingly parallel to Nebuchadnezzar’s madness in Daniel 4.
The Prayer of Nabonidus may preserve an older version of the tradition behind Daniel 4, or reflect a common Near Eastern motif of royal humbling. Either way, it confirms the antiquity of the Daniel traditions.
1 Maccabees 1–2 (2nd c. BC)
Describes the historical desecration of the Jerusalem temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC — the event Daniel 8:9–14 and 11:31 prophesy as the “abomination of desolation.”
The fulfilment is documented in non-biblical Jewish sources, confirming Daniel’s prophetic precision whether the prophecy is dated to the 6th or 2nd century BC.
Josephus, Antiquities 10.11 (1st c. AD)
Josephus records that when Alexander the Great approached Jerusalem (332 BC), the high priest showed him Daniel’s prophecy about a Greek king conquering Persia, and Alexander was so impressed he offered sacrifices at the temple.
Whether historically accurate or legendary, Josephus’s account shows that Daniel’s prophecies were understood as referring to Greek conquest well before the Christian era.
