Daniel 3

The Fiery Furnace

Verses 1–18 — “We Will Not Serve Your Gods”
123456789101112131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

צְלֵם ṣəlēm — image / statue: "King Nebuchadnezzar made an image (ṣəlēm) of gold, sixty cubits high and six cubits wide" (v.1). The golden statue — 90 feet tall. All must bow when the music plays or be thrown into a blazing furnace. Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego refuse. Their answer: "The God we serve is able to deliver us. But even if he does not, we will not serve your gods" (vv.17–18). The "but even if he does not" is the theological centre: faith is not contingent on rescue.

Context

The 90-foot golden statue appears to be Nebuchadnezzar’s response to ch.2: since the dream said only the HEAD was gold, he builds an ENTIRE statue of gold — rejecting the prophecy of succession. The dimensions (60 × 6 cubits) and the six instruments listed in v.5 have led some commentators to connect the passage with the "number of the beast" imagery in Rev 13. The Dura plain where the statue stood has been identified with a site near modern Fallujah, Iraq.

Cross-Reference

  • Ex 20:3–5The first and second commandments: no other gods, no graven images — the Torah basis for the three friends’ refusal.
  • Matt 10:28Do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul — the principle the three friends embody.
  • Heb 11:34Who through faith "quenched the fury of the flames" — the Hebrews 11 honour roll includes this event.
  • 1 Pet 4:12–13Do not be surprised at the fiery ordeal: share in Christ’s sufferings and rejoice.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1

The image: 90 feet of gold. After ch.2 declared the gold head was Nebuchadnezzar, he builds an ENTIRE statue of gold — a direct rejection of the dream’s message.

3:4–6

The music plays; everyone bows. The compliance is total except for three. Music-bow-worship: Babylon’s liturgy of sensory overload producing uniform submission.

3:16–18

"We do not need to defend ourselves. God is able to deliver us. BUT EVEN IF HE DOES NOT." Three-part declaration: no defence needed, God CAN rescue, even if he DOESN’T we still won’t bow. Faith without guarantee.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1–6

Calvin reads the golden statue as Nebuchadnezzar’s attempt to unify his empire through compulsory worship — the first recorded instance of state-imposed religious conformity. The pattern recurs under Rome, and Calvin’s readers in Reformation Europe knew it well.

3:16–18

Calvin calls the "but even if he does not" the highest expression of faith in the OT: faith that expects deliverance but does not REQUIRE it. The three friends honour God’s freedom to act or not act.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:1

The NET notes the statue’s proportions (10:1 height-to-width ratio) suggest an obelisk or pillar rather than a humanoid figure. The gold may have been overlay rather than solid.

3:17–18

The NET observes the careful logic: (1) our God is ABLE; (2) he WILL deliver; (3) but EVEN IF he does not. The conditional does not express doubt about ability but acknowledges divine sovereignty over outcomes.

Longman Notes

longman
3:1

Longman: the statue’s construction is Nebuchadnezzar’s theological counter-move to ch.2. The dream said his kingdom would be replaced; the statue says it will last forever. Idolatry is always an attempt to secure what God has declared temporary.

3:17–18

Longman calls this "the most theologically mature statement of faith in the Old Testament": it combines confidence in God’s power with acceptance of God’s freedom. Rescue is expected but not demanded.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
3:1–6

Goldingay reads the forced worship as a parable of every regime that demands total allegiance: the music is propaganda, the furnace is the state’s enforcement mechanism, the crowd’s compliance is social pressure.

3:17–18

Goldingay: the "even if he does not" clause is what separates biblical faith from prosperity theology. The three friends do not bargain with God; they simply refuse the alternative.

Collins Notes

collins
3:1–6

Collins compares the forced-worship scene to the loyalty oaths required by Hellenistic rulers, particularly Antiochus IV’s requirement that Jews sacrifice to Greek gods (1 Macc 1:41–50). Whether the chapter describes Babylonian or Hellenistic persecution, the theological point is identical.

3:16–18

Collins notes this is the book’s clearest statement on martyrdom theology: the faithful may die, and that does not invalidate their faith. This became the foundational text for Jewish and Christian martyrdom.

Verses 19–30 — “I See Four Men Walking in the Fire”
192021222324252627282930

Hebrew Word Study

בַר אֱלָהִין bar ʾĕlāhîn — son of the gods / divine being: "I see four men walking in the fire, unbound and unharmed, and the fourth looks like a son of the gods (bar ʾĕlāhîn)" (v.25). The furnace is heated seven times hotter (v.19). The soldiers who throw them in die from the heat (v.22). Four figures walk freely inside. The three emerge without even the smell of smoke (v.27).

Context

Nebuchadnezzar’s identification of the fourth figure as "a son of the gods" uses Aramaic bar ʾĕlāhîn, which could mean a divine being, an angel, or (in Christian reading) the pre-incarnate Christ. The king later clarifies: "God sent his angel" (v.28). The complete immunity from fire (no burn, no singe, no scorch, no smell) goes beyond natural survival and emphasises total divine protection. This event becomes paradigmatic for Jewish and Christian martyrdom theology.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 43:2When you walk through the fire, the flames will not set you ablaze — the promise Daniel 3 dramatises literally.
  • Ps 91:11–12He will command his angels concerning you — angelic protection in mortal danger.
  • Acts 12:6–11Peter freed from prison by an angel — the NT pattern of divine rescue from state persecution.
  • Rev 1:13–14One like a son of man among the lampstands — the glorified Christ present amid fire, echoing the fourth figure.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:19–22

Seven times hotter. The soldiers die carrying them in. Babylon’s own servants killed by Babylon’s own instrument of punishment.

3:25

"The fourth looks like a son of the gods." The christological reading: the pre-incarnate Christ in the furnace. Either way: divine presence in the fire.

3:27

No burn, no singe, no scorch, no smell. The catalogue of what fire didn’t do. The deliverance is total.

3:28–30

Nebuchadnezzar’s second confession. Stronger than ch.2: now he decrees protection for their God.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:25

Calvin notes that Nebuchadnezzar’s pagan theology provides the correct category ("son of the gods") even though he misidentifies the person. God uses imperfect understanding to deliver truth.

3:27–28

Calvin emphasises the evidential nature of the miracle: the entire court witnesses the absence of fire-damage. God provides public, verifiable proof for the sake of the watching empire.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:25

The NET notes that bar ʾĕlāhîn is literally "son of gods/divine beings." In Nebuchadnezzar’s polytheistic framework, this means a supernatural being. In v.28 he reinterprets: God "sent his angel."

3:27

The NET catalogues the fourfold negation: not harmed, not singed, not scorched, no smell. Each element rules out a different degree of fire exposure.

Longman Notes

longman
3:25–27

Longman: the fourth figure is deliberately left ambiguous. The text does not identify him as Christ, as an angel, or as a theophany. The ambiguity is the point: God’s presence takes forms that exceed human categories.

3:28–30

Longman notes the progression of Nebuchadnezzar’s confessions: ch.2 (God of gods), ch.3 (decree protecting God’s servants), ch.4 (personal worship). Each crisis moves him further toward genuine faith.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
3:25

Goldingay: the fire that kills the guards but not the prisoners inverts the expected logic of power. The instrument of state violence becomes the stage for divine protection. Babylon’s strongest weapon is God’s weakest challenge.

3:28–30

Goldingay reads the decree as politically significant: the empire’s official policy now protects the God it tried to suppress. The state that demanded worship of its idol now forbids blasphemy against the idol’s rival.

Collins Notes

collins
3:25

Collins notes that the "son of the gods" motif connects to the divine council tradition (Job 1:6; Ps 82:1). The fourth figure is a member of the heavenly court, dispatched to protect the faithful. The christological reading is a later development, not the original intention.

3:28–30

Collins reads Nebuchadnezzar’s decree as a recognition of YHWH’s power within a polytheistic framework — he acknowledges this God’s effectiveness without abandoning his own gods. Full conversion comes only in ch.4.

Ancient Sources

The Prayer of Nabonidus (4Q242, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A fragmentary Qumran text describes a Babylonian king struck with illness for seven years who is healed by a Jewish exorcist — strikingly parallel to Nebuchadnezzar’s madness in Daniel 4.
The Prayer of Nabonidus may preserve an older version of the tradition behind Daniel 4, or reflect a common Near Eastern motif of royal humbling. Either way, it confirms the antiquity of the Daniel traditions.
1 Maccabees 1–2 (2nd c. BC)
Describes the historical desecration of the Jerusalem temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC — the event Daniel 8:9–14 and 11:31 prophesy as the “abomination of desolation.”
The fulfilment is documented in non-biblical Jewish sources, confirming Daniel’s prophetic precision whether the prophecy is dated to the 6th or 2nd century BC.
Josephus, Antiquities 10.11 (1st c. AD)
Josephus records that when Alexander the Great approached Jerusalem (332 BC), the high priest showed him Daniel’s prophecy about a Greek king conquering Persia, and Alexander was so impressed he offered sacrifices at the temple.
Whether historically accurate or legendary, Josephus’s account shows that Daniel’s prophecies were understood as referring to Greek conquest well before the Christian era.
