The Tenth Plague Announced; Moses Departs in Anger

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
11:4-6

MacArthur: Every firstborn son in Egypt will die, from the firstborn of Pharaoh to the firstborn of the female slave. The comprehensive social range of the judgment -- from throne to mill -- leaves no Egyptian institution exempt from the final plague.

11:7

MacArthur: Not a dog will bark at any person or animal belonging to the Israelites. Even the proverbially omnipresent barking dogs will be silent in Goshen. The silence is the sound of covenant protection.

Hebrew Word Study

בְּכוֹר bĕkōr — firstborn: V.5: "Every firstborn son in Egypt will die" — bĕkōr, the eldest male child who held privileged status, inheritance rights, and double portion. The targeting of firstborns represents the complete inversion of Egypt's future: the nation defined by making Israel's firstborn sons slaves now faces the death of its own. Paul reads the Exodus typologically: Christ is the "firstborn (prōtotokos) over all creation" (Col 1:15) and the "firstborn from the dead" (Col 1:18; Rev 1:5).

צָעֲקָה tsāʿaqāh — a great cry: V.6: "There will be loud wailing throughout Egypt — worse than there has ever been or ever will be." The great cry of the Egyptians mirrors Israel's cry of oppression in 2:23 ("Their cry for help because of their slavery went up to God"). The God who heard Israel's cry now causes Egypt's cry. This reversal — oppressor and oppressed exchanging positions — is the signature move of the Exodus narrative.

Context

Exodus 11 is the hinge chapter between the nine plagues and the final Passover plague. The chapter announces the tenth plague in advance, giving the reader (and Israel) the framework before Passover night unfolds in ch.12. The announcement moves from the LORD's private word to Moses (vv.1-3) to Moses's public proclamation to Pharaoh (vv.4-8). The silver and gold request (v.2) prefigures the Exodus as a kind of "back wages" for centuries of slavery (cf. Gen 15:14).

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 15:14They will come out with great possessions -- the silver and gold request (v.2) fulfils God's word to Abraham. The Exodus is not just liberation; it is compensation.
  • Exod 4:22-23Israel is my firstborn son... let my son go, so he may worship me. If you refuse, I will kill your firstborn son -- the tenth plague is the direct execution of the warning given before the plagues began. The firstborn logic has run from ch.4 to ch.11.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:2–3

The command to ask Egyptians for silver and gold items (cf. 3:21–22) is the fulfilment of the promise made at the burning bush. Sarna notes that the word translated 'borrow' (sha'al) actually means 'ask' or 'request' — there is no expectation of return implied. The Egyptians willingly give because Moses had 'great esteem in Egypt.' The wealth transfer is the spoiling of Egypt — fulfilment of Gen 15:14 ('they shall come out with great possessions').

11:4

Moses' announcement formula — 'Thus says the LORD: About midnight I will go out through Egypt' — returns to the prophetic messenger form of 5:1. After chapters of plague demonstrations, the final announcement has the weight of everything that has preceded it. Sarna notes the LORD himself going through Egypt (not an angel) is the most direct divine involvement yet: the God who has worked through signs and wonders now acts personally.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:4–8

Moses' announcement is delivered, the narrator tells us, while Moses was 'hot with anger' (v.8). This emotional specification — the only time Moses' anger is noted in the plague narrative — marks the announcement as personally weighted. Moses has spent multiple chapters in negotiation with a man who has repeatedly deceived him. The anger is not uncontrolled rage but the righteous fury of the prophet delivering final judgment. He leaves in anger; he will not return.

11:7

The dog metaphor — not even a dog growling against Israel — is one of the Bible's most vivid expressions of protected silence. Alter notes the contrast it establishes: on one side, the greatest cry in human history; on the other, not even the ambient sound of a dog's response to disturbance. The discrimination is expressed not just in degree but in kind: death vs. absolute peace.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:5

Calvin on the death of the firstborn: 'The firstborn represented the whole nation; thus in the person of the firstborn the whole people is stricken.' The judgment is corporate through the representative — the firstborn carries the community's future. God's judgment on Pharaoh's firstborn is simultaneously a judgment on Egypt's entire claim to dominance over Israel. The one who refused to let God's firstborn (Israel, 4:22) go loses his own firstborn.

11:7

Calvin on the distinction between Israel and Egypt: 'God thus distinguished his Church from the unbelievers, that even the dogs should enjoy peace in the dwellings of the Israelites, while Egypt was overwhelmed with mourning.' The silence in Goshen is Calvin's image of the peace that covenant membership brings — not exemption from all hardship but divine protection at the moments that matter most.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:4

The NET note on 'about midnight' (kachatsot halaylah) explains kachatsot as 'approximately midnight' or 'at the stroke of midnight.' The precision of the timing — announced in advance — is itself a sign: the God who announces 'midnight' and fulfils it at midnight demonstrates comprehensive control over time as well as nature. The note also discusses whether vv.4–8 were spoken to Pharaoh (before Moses' departure in 10:29) or narrated to the reader after the departure — the grammar is ambiguous.

11:5-7

The NET note on 'from the firstborn of Pharaoh on the throne to the firstborn of the female slave at her handmill to every firstborn of the cattle' traces the comprehensive social range — top to bottom of Egyptian society — that the final plague will strike. No household is exempt. The note traces the contrast with Israel: not even a dog will bark at Israel.

Verses 9–10 — The Purpose of the Hardening; Summary of the Plague Narrative
910

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
11:9-10

MacArthur: Pharaoh will refuse to listen to you -- so that my wonders may be multiplied in Egypt. The refusal is anticipated; the multiplication of signs is the purpose. The plagues are not primarily persuasion but proclamation.

11:9-10

MacArthur: The brief statement that Moses and Aaron performed all these signs before Pharaoh is the plague narrative's summary sentence. The multiplication of wonders (v.9) was always the announced purpose. The confrontation with Pharaoh was never primarily about persuasion but about divine display — YHWH demonstrating his nature before Egypt and the nations.

Hebrew Word Study

פֶּסַח pesaḥ — Passover / to pass over: V.1 (anticipating ch.12): The term pesaḥ — the Passover — comes from the verb pāsaḥ, meaning to "pass over" or "spare." When the destroyer came through Egypt, the blood on the doorposts caused God to "pass over" those houses. The Passover is Israel's founding memorial: every generation must re-enact it as if they personally came out of Egypt (Mishnah Pesachim 10:5). The NT makes it the interpretive frame for the crucifixion — Jesus is "our Passover lamb" (1 Cor 5:7).

אֱמֶת ʾemet — truth / faithfulness: V.4: Moses's departure "in fierce anger" (bĕḥorî-ʾaf) from Pharaoh demonstrates prophetic anger as a moral response to injustice rather than personal offense. The word ḥārā (to burn with anger) is used of God's own anger throughout the OT. Moses's anger here is righteous alignment with the divine response to Pharaoh's continued hardness.

Context

The two-verse closing of ch.11 provides the theological summary of the entire plague narrative: Pharaoh's refusal is within God's purposive will; the multiplication of wonders is intentional. The hardening of Pharaoh's heart is among the most theologically discussed passages in the OT -- simultaneously human responsibility (Pharaoh hardens himself, e.g. 8:15,32) and divine sovereignty (God hardens Pharaoh, e.g. 9:12; 10:1). Both are true; neither cancels the other.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 9:17For Scripture says to Pharaoh: "I raised you up for this very purpose, that I might display my power in you and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth" -- Paul cites the hardening as the paradigm of divine sovereignty and human responsibility in tension.
  • Exod 9:16I have raised you up for this very purpose, that I might show you my power and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth -- the divine purpose stated before the final plagues.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:9–10

The summary in vv.9–10 is a transitional statement linking the plague narrative (chs.7–11) to the Passover institution (ch.12). Sarna notes it serves as a colophon — a closing summary that frames what has preceded and prepares for what follows. The phrase 'Moses and Aaron did all these wonders' provides the human framing; the divine purpose ('that my wonders may be multiplied') provides the theological framing.

11:9-10

The summary statement that God hardened Pharaoh's heart to multiply signs serves a theological purpose: the plagues are not primarily instruments of persuasion but of demonstration. Sarna notes the purpose stated is that YHWH's wonders might be multiplied — the plagues are the display of divine power before the nations, not just pressure on one pharaoh.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:9–10

The summary is brief and structural — it closes the plague narrative proper and opens the Passover section. Alter notes the chiastic frame: the section began with God telling Moses that Pharaoh would not listen (7:3–4) and ends with the same statement (11:9). The narrative loop is closed; everything that needed to be demonstrated has been demonstrated. The stage is cleared for the final act.

11:9-10

The summary closes the plague narrative before the Passover institution begins. Alter notes the structural economy: ten plagues, narrated with escalating detail and divine-hardening notices, are closed with this formulaic summary before the pivot to the redemptive ordinance that will make the exodus permanent and repeatable.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:9

Calvin on the purpose of multiplied wonders: 'God had a different object in view from what appears to human wisdom; for, though Pharaoh's obstinacy seemed to impede his work, God had it in his power to extract glory from his wickedness.' The apparent failure of each plague (Pharaoh still refuses) was always within the divine plan. The multiplication of refusals was the multiplication of opportunities for divine demonstration.

11:9-10

Calvin on the purpose of multiplied wonders: God desired not only to deliver Israel but to display his power before all the nations. The plagues are covenant signs for those who have eyes to see. Even Pharaoh's resistance serves this purpose — the greater the resistance, the more complete the display of divine power.

The NET note on 'Moses and Aaron did all these wonders' explains this is a retrospective summary that encompasses chs.7–11. The note discusses the division of roles: Moses received the divine instructions and announcements; Aaron executed the staff-related signs; Moses announced the later plagues directly. The collaborative structure mirrors the prophet-spokesman arrangement established in ch.4.

The NET note on 'Moses and Aaron did all these wonders' identifies this as a summary that functions as chapter closure. The note discusses the textual and narrative reasons for placing the summary here rather than after ch.12 — the plague narrative proper ends here; the Passover institution is a distinct literary unit.

Ancient Sources

Silver and gold from Egypt
V.2: the plundering of Egypt (Exod 12:35-36) fulfils Gen 15:14 ("they will come out with great possessions"). Israel is compensated for centuries of uncompensated labour. The liberation includes restitution.
The silver and gold become the materials for the tabernacle (Exod 25:3).
Firstborn theology
The firstborn represented the continuation of the family line and the focus of its inheritance. Killing the firstborn was the destruction of Egypt's future. The same logic explains why Israel's firstborn were redeemed (Exod 13:2) -- they belong to the LORD who spared them on Passover night.
The firstborn motif runs from Exod 4:22 through ch.11 to ch.13.

Reception History

Romans 9 and the hardening
Paul's discussion of divine sovereignty and human responsibility in Rom 9 is grounded entirely in the Exodus hardening narrative. He cites Exod 9:16 (v.17) and 4:21 as the paradigmatic texts.
Passover and Easter
The tenth plague and the Passover (ch.12) became the foundational typology for Christian Easter -- the death of the firstborn passed over when covered by the blood, pointing to the death of Christ and the redemption of those under his blood.

Scholarly Debates

The Death of the Firstborn: Theological Justice and the Problem of Innocence
Corporate/representative judgment
Calvin, MacArthur, Reformed tradition
The firstborn represents the entire Egyptian social and religious order. The judgment is corporate, directed at Egypt's claim to divine status and its systemic oppression of Israel's children. Egyptian society killed Israelite firstborn sons (1:22); now Egypt loses its own firstborn. The principle of equivalence operates at the national level.
Theodicy: innocent children as victims
Critical and pastoral challenge
The death of Egyptian children — who had no personal role in Pharaoh's decisions — raises the most acute theodicy question in the Exodus narrative. The children of slaves and prisoners (11:5) were not responsible for the oppression. This objection is taken seriously in Jewish and Christian tradition.
Divine sovereignty over life
Sarna, Fretheim, evangelical response
All human life belongs to God; he gives and takes according to his purposes. The deaths serve the explicit purpose of the global proclamation of the divine name (9:16) and the liberation of Israel. The narrative does not answer the theodicy question abstractly but embeds it in the larger covenant story where God's justice and mercy are both operative. Egypt had killed Israelite infants for generations; the tenth plague is judgment proportionate to decades of Pharaoh's own decree.
The theodicy question is real and should not be dismissed. Jewish and Christian tradition has wrestled with it seriously. The most substantive responses note: (1) the corporate dimension of ancient society (Pharaoh's choices implicated Egypt); (2) the precise proportionality (Israel's infant sons were killed; Egypt's firstborn sons die); (3) the prior warnings that made the outcome avoidable; (4) the theological purpose of universal revelation.
