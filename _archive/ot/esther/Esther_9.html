Esther 9

The Jews Defend Themselves; Purim Established

Verses 1–19 — The 13th of Adar: "The Reverse Occurred"
12345678910111213141516171819

Hebrew Word Study

נַהֲפוֹךְ nahapōk̅ — it was reversed / turned around: "The reverse occurred" (wənahapōk̅ hûʾ) — the verb nahapōk̅ is the book’s theological keyword. What was planned against the Jews happened to their enemies instead. The reversal is not partial but total. This single word is the book’s thesis.

לא שָׁלְחוּ lōʾ shāləḥû — they did not lay hands on / they did not plunder: Three times the text notes the Jews "did not lay their hands on the plunder" (vv.10, 15, 16). Though the counter-decree authorised plundering, the Jews refused. This deliberate restraint echoes Saul’s failure with Agag — what Saul should have done (refuse the plunder, 1 Sam 15:19), the Jews now do.

Historical Context

On the 13th of Adar, the Jews defended themselves throughout the empire. In Susa alone, 500 men were killed including Haman’s ten sons. Esther requests an additional day in Susa (the 14th), and Haman’s sons are publicly displayed. The provincial Jews celebrate on the 14th; Susa Jews on the 15th — hence the two-day Purim observance.

Context

The narrator emphasises restraint over violence. The Jews kill their attackers but refuse the plunder. In a book that replays the Saul-Agag conflict, this detail is crucial: where Saul took Agag’s plunder and lost the kingdom, Mordecai’s community refuses it and preserves the people. The correction of Saul’s failure is complete.

MacArthur Study Notes

9:1

"The reverse occurred." Three words that summarise the entire book. The day intended for Jewish destruction becomes the day of their enemies’ defeat.

9:5–6

The Jews strike down their enemies in Susa. The text records numbers but not celebration of killing. The violence is described matter-of-factly, not triumphally.

9:10

"But they did not lay their hands on the plunder." First refusal. The restraint corrects Saul’s error with Agag — a deliberate echo.

9:13–14

Esther requests an additional day in Susa and the public display of Haman’s ten sons. The additional day is Esther’s own initiative — she acts with authority now, not just survival.

Verses 20–32 — Purim Established and Confirmed

Hebrew Word Study

פּוּרִים pûrîm — Purim / lots: The festival is named for the lot (pûr) Haman cast to choose the date of destruction (3:7). The name commemorates not the victory but the mechanism of the threat — the random chance that God governed. Purim celebrates the God who controls what pagans call fate.

זִכָּרוֹן zikkārōn — memorial / remembrance: "These days should be remembered (nizkārîm) and observed in every generation." Memory is the purpose of the festival. Purim exists so that no generation forgets: the reversal happened, and the hidden God was its author.

Context

The establishment of Purim is described twice: Mordecai’s letter (vv.20–28) and Esther’s confirmation (vv.29–32). The double authorisation — from the male leader and the female leader — reflects the book’s dual leadership. Both are needed; both carry authority. Esther’s letter has "full authority" (v.29).

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 12:14Passover as memorial — the pattern Purim follows: a festival of deliverance that every generation must observe.
  • Deut 16:11–12Festival observance as communal memory — joy rooted in historical rescue.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:20–22

Mordecai establishes Purim: annual observance on the 14th and 15th of Adar. Joy, feasting, giving to the poor. The festival combines celebration with charity.

9:23–26

The narrative summary: Haman plotted, cast the lot (pur), planned destruction. Esther interceded. Haman was hanged. The summary compresses ten chapters into four verses.

9:27–28

The obligation is binding on Jews and converts "without fail, in every generation." The festival is permanent. Purim is still celebrated today — the longest-running festival in the book.

9:29–32

Esther writes with "full authority" to confirm Purim. The orphan girl who concealed her identity now issues royal decrees under her own name. The arc is complete.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:27–28

Calvin: the annual observance guards against forgetfulness. Every generation must hear again what God did, lest they take deliverance for granted.

9:29

Calvin: Esther’s confirming letter shows that the queen has grown from reluctant instrument to authoritative leader. God completes the work he begins.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:26

The NET notes the Akkadian loanword pûr gives the festival its foreign-sounding name — a reminder that the story unfolds in exile, not in the homeland.

9:32

The NET observes "the command of Esther confirmed these regulations" gives the queen independent legislative authority — remarkable for the ancient world.

Levenson Notes

levenson
9:20–28

Levenson traces Purim’s development: from historical event to annual commemoration to canonical text. The book of Esther is itself a Purim text — read aloud every year at the festival.

9:28

Levenson: "never cease from among the Jews" — the festival’s permanence is the book’s final theological claim. What God reversed, God’s people remember forever.

Jobes Notes

jobes
9:26

Jobes: the festival is named after the lot, not the victory. Purim celebrates not Jewish power but divine sovereignty over what looks like chance. The name encodes the theology.

9:29

Jobes on Esther’s authority: she confirms Purim with "full authority" — her own name, her own seal. The concealed girl of chapter 2 is now the authoritative queen of chapter 9.
