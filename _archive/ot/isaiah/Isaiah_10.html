Isaiah 10

Assyria: The Rod of God’s Anger

Verses 1–19 — “I Send Him Against a Godless Nation”
12345678910111213141516171819

Hebrew Word Study

מַטֶּה maṭṭeh — rod / staff: "Woe to the Assyrian, the rod (maṭṭeh) of my anger, in whose hand is the club of my wrath! I send him against a godless nation" (vv.5–6). But Assyria does not intend this; in his heart he plans to destroy many nations (v.7). "Does the axe raise itself above the person who swings it?" (v.15). Assyria is God’s instrument but will be judged for exceeding its mandate. The remnant of Israel’s trees will be so few a child could count them (v.19).

Context

This passage is the OT’s fullest statement on instrumental sovereignty: God USES Assyria as his rod of judgment, but Assyria’s arrogance in exceeding the mandate brings judgment on the instrument itself. The "rod of anger" metaphor (v.5) makes Assyria a tool in God’s hand, not an independent actor. The axe/saw/rod/club analogies (v.15) drive the point home: tools do not congratulate themselves. Sennacherib’s boasts (vv.8–14) are historically documented in Assyrian royal inscriptions — the arrogance Isaiah describes is verbatim.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 37:22–29God’s response to Sennacherib’s arrogance: "Because you rage against me, I will put my hook in your nose" — the fulfilment of 10:5–19’s warning.
  • Hab 1:5–11God raises the Babylonians as his instrument, just as he raised Assyria — the same pattern in a later generation.
  • Jer 27:6I have given all these lands to Nebuchadnezzar my servant — the same instrumental-sovereignty theology applied to Babylon.
  • Rom 9:17–21The potter’s sovereignty over the clay — Paul’s theological development of Isa 10’s instrumental logic.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1–4

The seventh woe (continuing from 5:8–23): "Woe to those who make unjust laws." Legislation as injustice.

10:5–7

"Woe to the Assyrian, the rod of my anger!" God uses Assyria but Assyria exceeds the commission. The instrument is guilty.

10:12–15

"When the Lord finishes all his work against Mount Zion, he will say, I will punish the king of Assyria for the wilful pride of his heart." The tool will be judged for thinking it is the carpenter.

10:15

"Does the axe raise itself above the one who swings it?" The supreme statement of instrumental sovereignty. Tools don’t boast.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:5–7

Calvin reads this as the key to all political theology: God uses nations as instruments without removing their moral responsibility. Assyria is both God’s rod AND a guilty empire. Both are simultaneously true.

10:15

Calvin: the axe analogy settles the question of sovereignty and freedom. The axe does not choose to cut — but if it could think, and it congratulated itself, it would be absurd. So is Assyria’s pride.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:5

The NET notes the "woe" on Assyria is structurally the seventh woe — completing the series begun in 5:8. The six woes on Judah are followed by a seventh on the instrument of judgment.

10:15

The NET provides four tool analogies: axe, saw, rod, club. Each is a variation on the same theme: the tool has no autonomous power. The repetition is deliberately exhaustive.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
10:5–19

Oswalt reads this as the theological centre of First Isaiah’s political theology: God is sovereign over empires, but empires remain morally accountable. The two truths coexist without resolution.

10:15

Oswalt: the axe-question is the most rhetorically effective one-liner in the prophets. It reduces imperial arrogance to absurdity in a single sentence.

Childs Notes

childs
10:5–19

Childs reads the Assyrian-rod passage as the canonical explanation for why exile happened (God sent the rod) and why the rod was broken (it exceeded its commission). The passage prevents both despair (God is in control) and fatalism (the instrument is judged).

10:15

Childs notes the tool-analogies function as a hermeneutical principle for reading all of Isaiah: every empire in the book (Assyria, Babylon, Persia) is simultaneously God’s instrument and God’s opponent.

Verses 20–34 — “A Remnant Will Return”
202122232425262728293031323334

Hebrew Word Study

שְׁאָר shəʾār — remnant: "A remnant (shəʾār) will return, a remnant of Jacob will return to the Mighty God" (v.21). In that day, Israel will no longer rely on the one who struck them (Assyria) but will truly rely on the LORD (v.20). The destruction decreed is overflowing with righteousness (v.22). Though your people be like the sand of the sea, only a remnant will return (v.22). Do not be afraid of the Assyrians (v.24). God will raise his staff over the waters as he did at the sea (v.26). The yoke will be broken (v.27). The chapter ends with Assyria’s march toward Jerusalem — place by place — stopped by God’s axe (vv.28–34).

Context

The Shear-Jashub prophecy (7:3) is fulfilled here: "a remnant will return." Paul quotes v.22 in Romans 9:27–28 for his theology of the remnant within Israel. The place-names in vv.28–32 trace an actual approach-route to Jerusalem from the north, giving the poem a reportorial immediacy. The final image (v.33–34) is God as lumberjack felling the tall trees — the Assyrian advance halted by divine intervention, fulfilled when the angel kills 185,000 in Sennacherib’s camp (37:36).

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 9:27–28Isaiah cries out concerning Israel: "Though the number be as the sand of the sea, only the remnant will be saved" — Paul quotes Isa 10:22.
  • Rom 11:5So too, at the present time there is a remnant chosen by grace — Paul’s development of Isaiah’s remnant theology.
  • Isa 37:36The angel of the LORD put to death 185,000 in the Assyrian camp — the fulfilment of the felling in 10:33–34.
  • Ex 14:21–27God divided the sea — the exodus event 10:26 references: God will repeat the Red Sea for Assyria.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:20–22

A remnant will return to the Mighty God. But only a remnant. Paul quotes this in Rom 9:27–28. The promise is real but sobering.

10:24–26

"Do not be afraid of the Assyrians." God will raise his staff as at the sea. The exodus pattern repeats: what God did to Egypt, he will do to Assyria.

10:28–32

The Assyrian march: Aiath, Migron, Micmash, Geba, Ramah, Gibeah, Gallim, Laishah, Anathoth, Madmenah, Gebim, Nob. Place by place, closing in. Then stopped.

10:33–34

"The LORD will lop off the boughs with great power." The tall trees fall. The Assyrian advance is felled by God’s axe.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:21–22

Calvin reads the remnant promise as simultaneously hopeful and devastating: survival is guaranteed, but survival is ONLY a remnant. God’s faithfulness preserves; God’s justice reduces.

10:33–34

Calvin sees the felling-of-trees as the setup for 11:1: the great trees of Assyria fall, and from the lowly stump of Jesse a shoot emerges. The mighty are brought low; the humble are raised.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:21

The NET notes "Mighty God" (ʾēl gibbōr) is the same title given to the child in 9:6. The remnant returns to the very figure who was promised. The theology is circular: the Messiah is the remnant’s destination.

10:28–32

The NET maps the place-names to an approach from the north, about 10 miles from Jerusalem. The geographic precision creates a sense of the noose tightening.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
10:20–34

Oswalt reads the remnant-return as the positive counterpart to 10:5–19’s judgment: the rod will be broken AND the remnant will return. Both happen because of the same divine sovereignty.

10:33–34

Oswalt: the tree-felling transitions directly to 11:1’s shoot-from-the-stump. The great forest of Assyria falls; the tiny shoot of Jesse rises. The chapter-break obscures the literary connection.

Childs Notes

childs
10:20–34

Childs reads the remnant theology as the canonical thread that connects every section of Isaiah: 1:9 (survivors); 4:2–3 (the Branch); 6:13 (the stump); 7:3 (Shear-Jashub); 10:20–22 (the remnant returns). The remnant IS Isaiah’s hope.

10:33–34

Childs notes the transition from the felled forest (10:34) to the sprouting stump (11:1) is the most important chapter-break in Isaiah: destruction gives way to the messianic king.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
