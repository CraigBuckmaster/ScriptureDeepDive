Isaiah 36

Sennacherib’s Siege of Jerusalem

Verses 1–10 — “The Rabshakeh’s Challenge: On Whom Are You Depending?”
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

בָּטַח bāṭaḥ — to trust / to depend on: "On what are you basing this confidence (bāṭaḥ) of yours?" (v.4). Historical narrative begins: 701 BC. Sennacherib of Assyria invades Judah, captures all the fortified cities, and sends his field commander (Rabshakeh) to Jerusalem (v.2). The Rabshakeh speaks in Hebrew to the defenders on the wall (v.11). His argument: (1) Egypt is a broken reed (v.6). (2) You’ve removed your own altars (v.7) — misunderstanding Hezekiah’s reforms. (3) Even the LORD told me to attack (v.10). Psychological warfare through theological manipulation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
36:1–3

"In the fourteenth year of King Hezekiah, Sennacherib attacked all the fortified cities of Judah and captured them." Parallel: 2 Kings 18:13–37. Isaiah shifts from poetry to prose. The test of everything he’s preached.

36:4–6

"On whom are you depending? Egypt is a splintered reed of a staff that pierces the hand of anyone who leans on it." The Rabshakeh uses Isaiah’s own argument (chs.30–31): Egypt is useless. The enemy quotes the prophet.

36:10

"The LORD himself told me to march against this country and destroy it." The Rabshakeh claims divine authorisation. The theological audacity: a pagan officer claiming YHWH’s mandate.

Verses 11–22 — “Has Any God Delivered His Land from the King of Assyria?”
111213141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

הִצִּיל hiṣṣîl — to deliver / to rescue: "Has the god of any nation ever delivered (hiṣṣîl) his land from the hand of the king of Assyria?" (v.18). Hezekiah’s officials beg: speak in Aramaic, not Hebrew — don’t let the people on the wall hear (v.11). The Rabshakeh deliberately raises his voice (v.13). His pitch to the common people: make peace with me, enjoy your own vines and fig trees (v.16). Then the theological challenge: no god has saved anyone from Assyria (vv.18–20). "How then can the LORD deliver Jerusalem?" (v.20). The people are silent — the king had commanded them not to answer (v.21). Hezekiah’s officials tear their clothes (v.22).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
36:11–13

"Please speak in Aramaic, not Hebrew!" The officials want diplomatic language; the Rabshakeh wants propaganda. He shouts louder. The language choice is a weapon.

36:18–20

"Has any god of any nation delivered his land from the king of Assyria? How then can the LORD deliver Jerusalem?" The Rabshakeh’s fatal error: equating YHWH with the gods of Hamath and Arpad. This is the challenge Isaiah has prepared Hezekiah to answer.

36:21–22

"The people remained silent. They tore their clothes." Silence and torn clothes: the posture of crisis. The silence is obedient (the king commanded it) and faithful (they don’t negotiate with the enemy).
