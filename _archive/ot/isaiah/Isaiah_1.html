Isaiah 1

The Great Arraignment

Verses 1–15 — “Though Your Sins Are Like Scarlet”
123456789101112131415

Hebrew Word Study

רִיב rîb — lawsuit / controversy: "Hear me, you heavens! Listen, earth! The LORD has spoken: I reared children and brought them up, but they have rebelled against me" (v.2). Heaven and earth summoned as witnesses to a covenant lawsuit (rîb). The ox knows its owner; the donkey its master’s manger — but Israel does not know (v.3). Wounds, welts, open sores — not bandaged or soothed (v.6). Why more beatings? (v.5). Sacrifices are meaningless when hands are full of blood (vv.11–15). "Though your sins are like scarlet, they shall be as white as snow" (v.18).

Context

Isaiah opens not with his call (that comes in ch.6) but with God’s legal case against Judah. The covenant-lawsuit (rîb) genre is well attested in the prophets (Mic 6:1–8; Hos 4:1–3): heaven and earth are summoned as witnesses because they were present at the original covenant (Deut 30:19; 32:1). The date is the reigns of Uzziah through Hezekiah (c.740–700 BC). Judah is prosperous but morally bankrupt. The sacrificial critique (vv.11–15) does not reject ritual per se but ritual divorced from justice.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 32:1Listen, you heavens; hear, you earth — Moses summoned the same witnesses at the covenant’s beginning.
  • Mic 6:1–8The LORD has a case against his people — the same rîb genre.
  • 1 John 1:7The blood of Jesus purifies us from all sin — the NT fulfilment of the scarlet-to-snow promise.
  • Amos 5:21–24I hate your religious festivals; let justice roll — the same worship-without-justice critique.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:2–3

Heaven and earth as witnesses. The ox knows; the donkey knows; Israel does not. Animals outperform God’s people in basic recognition.

1:5–6

The nation described as a beaten body: wounds, welts, open sores. The metaphor is medical: Judah is critically ill and refusing treatment.

1:11–15

"The multitude of your sacrifices — what are they to me?" God rejects the QUANTITY of worship when the QUALITY of life is corrupt. Hands full of blood cannot offer acceptable sacrifice.

1:18

"Though your sins are like scarlet, they shall be as white as snow." The most famous verse in ch.1. The offer of total cleansing stands amid the total indictment.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:2–3

Calvin reads the ox-and-donkey comparison as devastating: dumb animals recognise their provider; Israel does not recognise its God. The indictment begins with ingratitude, the root of all other sins.

1:11–15

Calvin insists this passage does not reject sacrifice (which God commanded) but sacrifice offered by bloodstained hands. The problem is not liturgy but hypocrisy. Calvin applies this to any church that substitutes ceremony for righteousness.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:2

The NET notes the verb "reared" (giddal) implies parental investment: God raised Israel from infancy to adulthood. The rebellion is not stranger-against-stranger but child-against-parent.

1:18

The NET observes the conditional grammar: "IF you are willing and obedient" (v.19). The scarlet-to-snow transformation requires response; it is offered, not imposed.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
1:2–4

Oswalt emphasises the covenant-lawsuit framework: the heavens and earth were witnesses at Sinai (Deut 4:26); now they are summoned to testify. The entire cosmos is God’s courtroom.

1:10–15

Oswalt reads the sacrifice-rejection as the prophetic insistence that cult without character is abomination. The sacrificial system was designed to EXPRESS a relationship, not REPLACE one.

Childs Notes

childs
1:1–15

Childs reads ch.1 as the canonical introduction to the entire 66-chapter book: it establishes the themes (judgment, sin, offer of grace) that will be developed across First, Second, and Third Isaiah. The chapter is a miniature of the whole.

1:18

Childs notes the scarlet-to-snow promise functions proleptically: it anticipates the servant’s atoning work (ch.53) and the new creation (ch.65). The offer made here is fulfilled THERE.

Verses 16–31 — “The Faithful City Has Become a Prostitute”
16171819202122232425262728293031

Hebrew Word Study

צְדָקָה ṣədāqâ — righteousness / justice: "See how the faithful city has become a prostitute! She once was full of justice; righteousness (ṣədāqâ) used to dwell in her — but now murderers!" (v.21). The prescription: wash yourselves, make yourselves clean; stop doing wrong, learn to do right; seek justice, defend the oppressed (vv.16–17). Zion will be delivered with justice, her penitent ones with righteousness (v.27). But rebels and sinners will be destroyed (v.28). They will be ashamed of the sacred oaks and gardens they chose (v.29).

Context

The "faithful city become a prostitute" metaphor (v.21) launches the infidelity-imagery that Hosea, Jeremiah, and Ezekiel develop extensively. The term ṣədāqâ (righteousness/justice) appears five times in this chapter — it is Isaiah’s master-concept, meaning both ethical conduct and the right-ordering of society. The "sacred oaks and gardens" (v.29) refer to Canaanite fertility worship practised alongside YHWH worship. The chapter ends with fire imagery: the strong man becomes tinder, his work a spark (v.31).

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 2:20–21I planted you as a choice vine — but you have become a corrupt, wild vine — the same faithful-turned-unfaithful imagery.
  • Ezek 16:15–19You used your beauty and became a prostitute — Ezekiel’s extended version of Isa 1:21.
  • Jas 1:27Pure religion is to look after orphans and widows — the NT version of Isa 1:17’s prescription.
  • Rev 21:27Nothing impure will enter the new Jerusalem — the ultimate fulfilment of the purified-city vision.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:16–17

"Wash yourselves. Stop doing wrong. Learn to do right. Seek justice. Defend the oppressed." Five imperatives: the prescription after the diagnosis.

1:21

"The faithful city has become a prostitute!" The sharpest single-verse summary of Judah’s apostasy.

1:25–26

"I will turn my hand against you; I will thoroughly purge away your dross." Judgment as purification, not destruction. God refines; he does not discard.

1:27

"Zion will be delivered with justice." The promise embedded in the judgment: deliverance comes, but it comes THROUGH justice, not apart from it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:16–17

Calvin calls these the clearest ethical imperatives in Isaiah: not mystical directives but plain moral commands. The religion God wants is justice enacted, not ritual performed.

1:25–27

Calvin reads the refining-fire metaphor as the theology of redemptive suffering: God’s judgment burns away sin, not the sinner. The dross is removed; the silver remains. This is hope disguised as threat.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:21

The NET notes "prostitute" (zonah) is legal-covenant language: Israel’s exclusive relationship with YHWH (the "marriage") has been violated by other loyalties.

1:27

The NET observes the wordplay: Zion redeemed with mishpat (justice) and ṣədāqâ (righteousness) — the same pair that was supposed to characterise her in the first place (v.21). God restores what was lost.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
1:16–17

Oswalt notes the imperatives move from negative ("stop") to positive ("learn," "seek," "defend"). Repentance is not merely ceasing evil but actively pursuing good. The ethical life is not passive.

1:25–27

Oswalt reads the refining as the programme for the entire book: Isaiah traces how God purifies Judah through judgment (chs.1–39), exile (ch.40ff), and ultimately through the Servant’s suffering (ch.53).

Childs Notes

childs
1:21–31

Childs sees the prostitute-city / purified-city contrast as the book’s narrative arc in miniature: unfaithful Jerusalem (ch.1) → judged Jerusalem (chs.2–39) → comforted Jerusalem (chs.40–55) → glorified Jerusalem (chs.60–66). The chapter is a map of the entire book.

1:27

Childs notes that "redeemed with justice" holds together what later theology sometimes separates: redemption IS justice, not an alternative to it. God’s saving act is simultaneously his righteous act.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
