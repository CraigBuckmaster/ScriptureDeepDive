Isaiah 52

Beautiful Feet and the Suffering Servant Begins

Verses 1–12 — “How Beautiful on the Mountains Are the Feet”
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

מְבַשֶּר məbasher — one who brings good news / herald: "How beautiful on the mountains are the feet of those who bring good news (məbasher), who proclaim peace, who bring good tidings, who proclaim salvation, who say to Zion, ‘Your God reigns!’" (v.7). Paul quotes this in Romans 10:15 for the preaching of the gospel. "Awake, awake, Zion, put on your strength! Put on your garments of splendour, Jerusalem!" (v.1). "Burst into songs of joy together, you ruins of Jerusalem, for the LORD has comforted his people, he has redeemed Jerusalem" (v.9). "The LORD will lay bare his holy arm in the sight of all the nations, and all the ends of the earth will see the salvation of our God" (v.10). "Depart, depart, go out from there! Touch no unclean thing!" (v.11). The new exodus: leave Babylon, but not in haste like the first exodus — "for the LORD will go before you, the God of Israel will be your rear guard" (v.12).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
52:1–2

"Awake, awake, Zion! Free yourself from the chains on your neck." The city told to dress up and break free. Captivity is ending.

52:7

"How beautiful on the mountains are the feet of those who bring good news, who proclaim peace, who say, Your God reigns!" Romans 10:15: the gospel-preaching verse. The feet are beautiful because of the message they carry. The message has four parts: good news, peace, good tidings, salvation.

52:10

"The LORD will lay bare his holy arm in the sight of all the nations." The arm of 51:9 is bared. The nations will see. Salvation is public, not private.

52:12

"You will not leave in haste or go in flight; for the LORD will go before you, and the God of Israel will be your rear guard." The new exodus surpasses the first: no haste, no flight. God is both vanguard and rearguard.

Verses 13–15 — “My Servant Will Be Raised and Lifted Up: The Fourth Servant Song Begins”
131415

Hebrew Word Study

מַשְׁחִית mashchit — marred / disfigured: "See, my servant will act wisely; he will be raised and lifted up and highly exalted. Just as there were many who were appalled at him — his appearance was so disfigured (mashchit) beyond that of any human being and his form marred beyond human likeness — so he will sprinkle many nations, and kings will shut their mouths because of him" (vv.13–15). THE FOURTH SERVANT SONG BEGINS (52:13–53:12). Three verses that frame the entire song. The servant’s trajectory: exalted (v.13) → disfigured (v.14) → exalting nations (v.15). The disfigurement is beyond human — the suffering exceeds normal experience. Kings are speechless. The cross before the cross.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
52:13

"My servant will act wisely; he will be raised and lifted up and highly exalted." Three verbs of elevation: raised, lifted, exalted. The same verbs used of God in 6:1 ("high and exalted"). The servant shares God’s position.

52:14

"His appearance was so disfigured beyond that of any human being." The suffering that makes the servant unrecognisable. The Passion narrative: scourging, thorns, nails produced literal disfigurement.

52:15

"He will sprinkle many nations, and kings will shut their mouths." Sprinkling: the priestly purification act (Lev 14:7; Num 19:18). The servant is both sacrifice and priest. Kings are silenced — not by power but by the spectacle of suffering that redeems.
