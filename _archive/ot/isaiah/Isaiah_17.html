Isaiah 17

The Oracle Against Damascus

Verses 1–8 — “Damascus Will Cease to Be a City”
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

מַפָּלָה mappālâ — ruin / heap of ruins: "Damascus will no longer be a city but will become a heap of ruins (mappālâ)" (v.1). The glory of Jacob will fade; the fat of his body will grow thin (v.4). A few olives will remain on the topmost branches — four or five on the outer limbs (v.6). "In that day people will look to their Maker and turn their eyes to the Holy One of Israel" (v.7). They will not look to the altars, Asherah poles, or incense altars (v.8).

Context

Damascus and Ephraim (northern Israel) are paired because they formed the anti-Assyrian coalition of the Syro-Ephraimite war (735–732 BC, cf. ch.7). Damascus fell to Tiglath-Pileser III in 732 BC, and northern Israel was devastated at the same time. The remnant metaphor (a few olives left, v.6) connects to the remnant theology of chs.6, 10, and 11. Verse 7 is surprising: judgment produces a TURN to God. The purpose of devastation is redirection, not annihilation.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 16:9The king of Assyria captured Damascus and deported its people — the historical fulfilment of Isa 17:1.
  • Isa 7:1–9Rezin of Syria and Pekah of Israel attack Jerusalem — the political context behind this oracle.
  • Amos 1:3–5For three sins of Damascus, even for four — Amos’s earlier Damascus oracle.
  • Jer 49:23–27Damascus has become feeble; anguish and sorrows have seized her — Jeremiah’s parallel oracle.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:1–3

Calvin reads the pairing of Damascus and Ephraim as a warning: when you ally with the ungodly, you share their fate. Ephraim’s alliance with Damascus brought Assyria down on both.

17:7–8

Calvin highlights the surprising grace: judgment CAUSES people to look to God. The devastation that seems purely negative becomes the occasion for genuine worship.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:1

The NET notes Damascus is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world (still a major city today). The "cease to be a city" language may refer to temporary devastation rather than permanent destruction.

17:6

The NET observes the olive-gleaning metaphor: after the harvest, a few olives remain on the highest branches, unreachable. The remnant survives because it was too small to attract the harvester’s attention.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
17:1–8

Oswalt reads the Damascus oracle as part of the Syro-Ephraimite context: Damascus and Ephraim threatened Judah in ch.7; now both are judged. The coalition that attacked God’s people is dismantled by God’s instrument (Assyria).

17:7–8

Oswalt: the turn to the Maker (v.7) is the oracle’s purpose. Judgment is not vindictive but pedagogical. The goal is redirection, not destruction.

Childs Notes

childs
17:1–8

Childs reads the Damascus-Ephraim pairing as the canonical fulfilment of ch.7’s warning: the coalition will fail. The narrative arc (threat in ch.7 → judgment in ch.17) completes within First Isaiah.

17:7–8

Childs notes the "look to their Maker" reversal anticipates Second Isaiah’s call to "look to the rock from which you were cut" (51:1). Judgment produces the looking that grace rewards.

Verses 9–14 — “The Roaring of the Nations Is Like the Roaring of Great Waters”
91011121314

Hebrew Word Study

שָׁאוֹן shāʾōn — roar / tumult: "Oh, the raging (shāʾōn) of many nations — they rage like the raging sea! But he rebukes them and they flee far away" (v.12). The cities will be deserted like the abandoned places of the Hivites and Amorites (v.9). Israel forgot the God of their salvation and the Rock of their fortress (v.10). They plant pleasant plants and exotic vines but the harvest will be nothing (v.11). The nations roar, but God rebukes and they scatter like chaff (vv.12–13). "In the evening, sudden terror! Before morning, they are gone!" (v.14).

Context

The sea-roaring imagery (vv.12–13) personifies the nations as chaotic waters — the same primordial chaos-sea of Genesis 1:2 and Isaiah 27:1. God’s rebuke stills the waters, as in Psalm 106:9 and Mark 4:39 (Jesus calming the storm). Verse 14 may refer to the overnight destruction of Sennacherib’s army (37:36), where 185,000 died between evening and morning. The "pleasant plantings" and "imported vines" (vv.10–11) likely reference pagan fertility-garden worship.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 46:6Nations are in uproar, kingdoms fall; he lifts his voice, the earth melts — the same roaring-rebuked pattern.
  • Mark 4:39Jesus rebuked the wind and said, "Quiet! Be still!" — the NT enactment of God rebuking the sea.
  • Isa 37:36The angel of the LORD put to death 185,000 — possible fulfilment of the overnight terror of 17:14.
  • Ps 2:1–4Why do the nations conspire? He who sits in heaven laughs — the same nations-raging-God-responding pattern.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:12–14

Calvin reads the sea-rebuke as a creation metaphor: the God who commanded the primordial waters in Gen 1 commands the nations with the same authority. Imperial armies are no more threatening than ocean waves to the One who made the ocean.

17:14

Calvin: the overnight reversal is God’s signature. What terrifies at evening is gone by morning. The speed of divine action matches the speed of divine judgment.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:12

The NET notes the sea-roaring metaphor draws on the ancient Near Eastern chaos-sea tradition (Tiamat in Babylonian myth; Yam in Canaanite myth). God’s rebuke of the nations is cosmogonic: he tames political chaos as he once tamed primordial chaos.

17:14

The NET observes the evening-morning pattern echoes the creation narrative (Gen 1): evening followed by morning constitutes a "day." A single divine day is enough to reverse the threat.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
17:9–14

Oswalt reads the nations-as-sea imagery as the chapter’s theological climax: no matter how many nations rage, God’s rebuke is sufficient. The pattern is: threat → rebuke → scatter.

17:14

Oswalt: the overnight reversal echoes the exodus (morning comes and the enemy is gone) and anticipates Sennacherib’s defeat. The same God acts the same way across centuries.

Childs Notes

childs
17:9–14

Childs reads the passage as a canonical statement of divine sovereignty over political chaos: the nations rage, but God laughs (Ps 2). The canonical reader encounters this pattern throughout Scripture.

17:14

Childs notes the evening-morning structure invites the reader to trust through the night: the terror is real, but the morning will come.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
