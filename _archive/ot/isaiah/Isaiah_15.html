Isaiah 15

The Oracle Against Moab (Part 1)

Verses 1–5 — “Moab Is Destroyed in a Night”
12345

Hebrew Word Study

שַׁדַּד shaddad — to devastate / to destroy: "In a night Ar is destroyed (shaddad) — Moab is ruined! In a night Kir is destroyed — Moab is ruined!" (v.1). Moab mourns on the high places (v.2). Wailing in the streets; every head shaved, every beard cut off (v.2). Hearts cry out for Moab (v.5). Fugitives flee as far as Zoar (v.5).

Context

The Moab oracle (chs.15–16) is distinctive because the prophet WEEPS for the enemy. Isaiah grieves for Moab’s suffering even as he announces it (15:5; 16:9, 11). This empathy within judgment is rare in prophetic literature and anticipates the "weeping prophet" motif of Jeremiah. Moab was Israel’s neighbour east of the Dead Sea, descended from Lot (Gen 19:37). The cities named (Ar, Kir, Dibon, Nebo, Medeba) trace a route of devastation from north to south.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 19:37Moab, son of Lot — the kinship between Israel and Moab adds pathos to the oracle.
  • Jer 48Jeremiah’s extended Moab oracle — drawing heavily on Isa 15–16.
  • Ruth 1:22Ruth the Moabite returned with Naomi — the most famous Moabite in biblical history. Even judgment on Moab includes grace.
  • Num 25:1–3Israel began to commit sexual immorality with Moabite women — the troubled history between the peoples.

Mourning rites: shaved heads, cut beards, sackcloth. The entire nation weeps. Armed men cry aloud.

The prophet’s heart: "My heart cries out over Moab." Isaiah grieves for the enemy he prophesies against.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:1–5

Calvin notes the prophet’s empathy is deliberate: God does not delight in the death of the wicked (Ezek 33:11). Isaiah models the divine heart — grief over judgment, not satisfaction.

15:5

Calvin: "my heart cries out" shows that true prophecy is not cold announcement but anguished participation. The prophet suffers the judgment he proclaims.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:1

The NET identifies Ar as Moab’s capital and Kir (Kir-Hareseth) as its fortress city. The destruction of both in one night represents total collapse.

15:2–4

The NET catalogues the mourning customs: ascending high places for weeping, shaving heads, cutting beards. These are standard ancient Near Eastern grief rituals.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
15:1–5

Oswalt reads the Moab oracle as evidence that God’s sovereignty extends to ALL nations, not just Israel’s enemies. Moab is judged, but Moab is also mourned.

15:5

Oswalt: Isaiah’s grief for Moab anticipates the universal compassion of Second Isaiah. Even in First Isaiah’s judgment oracles, mercy is present.

Childs Notes

childs
15:1–5

Childs reads the Moab oracle as canonical witness to God’s sovereignty over all nations: judgment is not vindictive but sovereign. The prophet’s tears confirm this.

15:5

Childs notes the weeping-prophet motif connects Isaiah to Jeremiah, who also weeps over Moab (Jer 48:36). Prophetic grief is a genre convention, but it is also genuine.

Verses 6–9 — “The Waters of Nimrim Are Dried Up”
6789

Hebrew Word Study

דָּם dām — blood: "The waters of Dimon are full of blood (dām), but I will bring still more upon Dimon" (v.9). The grass withers; the vegetation fails; nothing green remains (v.6). Refugees carry their possessions across the Ravine of the Willows (v.7). The wailing echoes across Moab’s borders (v.8). The wordplay: Dimon/dam (blood) — the river runs red.

Context

The environmental devastation (dried waters, withered grass, no green) may reflect the scorched-earth tactics of the invader or divine curse on the land. The refugees fleeing south toward Edom with their possessions (v.7) prefigure every refugee column in history. The "more upon Dimon" (v.9) — lions among the fugitives and remnant — means the judgment is not yet complete. Chapter 16 continues the oracle.

Cross-Reference

  • Joel 1:10–12The fields are ruined, the ground is dried up, the grain is destroyed — the same agricultural devastation.
  • Rev 16:4–7The rivers and springs of water became blood — Revelation’s development of the blood-in-waters imagery.
  • Isa 34:2–7The LORD has a sacrifice in Bozrah — the parallel Edomite devastation with blood imagery.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:6–9

Calvin reads the dried waters and dead vegetation as covenant-curse fulfilment (Deut 28:23–24): the land reflects the spiritual state of its people.

15:9

Calvin: the "more upon Dimon" is a reminder that partial judgment can always intensify. Repentance before the full measure arrives is still possible.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:7

The NET identifies the "Ravine of the Willows" as the Wadi Zered (or Wadi Hesa), the southern border between Moab and Edom. The refugees flee across Moab’s boundary.

15:9

The NET notes the Dimon/dam wordplay: the place-name sounds like the word for blood. The city’s name becomes its fate.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
15:6–9

Oswalt reads the landscape-death as creation mourning alongside the people: when judgment falls, even the earth suffers. The ecological dimension of sin is real.

15:9

Oswalt: "I will bring still more" serves as the transition to ch.16. The Moab oracle is not finished; the two chapters form a single literary unit.

Childs Notes

childs
15:6–9

Childs notes the chapter ends without resolution — the oracle continues into ch.16. The canonical reader must keep reading. The judgment is a process, not a single moment.

15:9

Childs reads the escalation ("still more") as the prophetic warning pattern: judgment arrives in stages, each more severe, until either repentance or completion.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
