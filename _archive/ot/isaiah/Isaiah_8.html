Isaiah 8

Maher-Shalal-Hash-Baz

Verses 1–10 — “God Is with Us”
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

מַהֵר שָׁלָל חָשׁ בַּז maher shalal chash baz — quick to the plunder, swift to the spoil: "Name him Maher-Shalal-Hash-Baz" (v.3). Isaiah’s second son: the longest personal name in the Bible, meaning "quick to plunder, swift to spoil." Before the child can say "mother" or "father," Damascus and Samaria will be plundered (v.4). Because Judah rejected the gentle waters of Shiloah, the Euphrates flood (Assyria) will sweep through, reaching even Judah "up to the neck" (vv.7–8). But: "God is with us" (ʿimmānū ʾēl, v.10).

Context

The "waters of Shiloah" (v.6) is the gentle spring that supplied Jerusalem — a metaphor for God’s quiet provision. Judah rejected the gentle for the dramatic: Assyria’s Euphrates-like military flood. The Immanuel name returns (v.8, 10) as both address to the land and defiant confession: despite the flood, God IS with us. Isaiah’s family becomes a "sign and symbol" for Israel (v.18). The two children’s names (Shear-Jashub, Maher-Shalal-Hash-Baz) together prophesy: swift judgment, but a remnant returns.

Cross-Reference

  • John 9:7Jesus sends the blind man to wash in the Pool of Siloam — the same Shiloah waters, the gentle provision Judah once rejected.
  • Isa 7:14Immanuel — the name given in ch.7 returns as a confession in 8:8, 10.
  • Matt 4:15–16Matthew quotes Isa 9:1–2 (which follows from this passage) for Jesus’ Galilean ministry.
  • Rom 9:33A stone of stumbling — Paul quotes Isa 8:14.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:6–8

Calvin reads the water-contrast as a universal principle: when people reject God’s gentle provision, they receive overwhelming alternatives. The quiet stream rejected becomes a roaring river imposed.

8:10

Calvin notes the Immanuel confession (v.10) comes AFTER the flood announcement: faith declares God’s presence not in the absence of crisis but in its midst.

Oswalt Notes

8:1–10

Oswalt reads the two-child naming (Shear-Jashub in ch.7, Maher-Shalal-Hash-Baz in ch.8) as Isaiah’s prophetic-family ministry: his household IS his message. Names, children, encounters are all prophetic signs.

8:6–8

Oswalt: the Shiloah/Euphrates contrast is the book’s clearest expression of the faith-vs-politics choice: trust the quiet provision of God or the overwhelming force of empire. There is no middle ground.

Childs Notes

childs
8:1–10

Childs reads the passage as the escalation of the Immanuel theology: the name spoken as sign (7:14), as address (8:8), and as confession (8:10). Each usage deepens the meaning.

8:14

Childs notes the "sanctuary or stumbling stone" duality: God’s presence is either refuge or ruin, depending on the receiver’s response. The same God, opposite outcomes.

Verses 11–22 — “To the Law and to the Testimony”
111213141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

תּוֹרָה tōrâ — instruction / Torah: "To the law (tōrâ) and to the testimony! If they do not speak according to this word, they have no light of dawn" (v.20). God warns Isaiah not to follow the people’s fear (v.11). The LORD Almighty is the one you are to dread (v.13). He will be a sanctuary — or a stone of stumbling (v.14). Isaiah will bind up the testimony among his disciples (v.16). "I will wait for the LORD, who is hiding his face from Jacob" (v.17). Isaiah and his children are signs (v.18). When they say "consult the dead," respond: to the LAW and the TESTIMONY (vv.19–20).

Context

The "bind up the testimony, seal the law among my disciples" (v.16) is often read as the moment Isaiah creates a circle of followers who preserve his teaching — possibly the origin of the "disciples of Isaiah" who may have compiled Second and Third Isaiah. The rejection of necromancy (v.19) addresses the desperate search for guidance when God seems silent. Isaiah’s answer: the written word (Torah and testimony) is the standard, not spiritual experimentation. The passage ends in darkness and distress (vv.21–22) — which sets up the great light of 9:1–2.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Pet 2:7–8The stone the builders rejected has become the cornerstone; a stone of stumbling — Peter quotes Isa 8:14.
  • Rom 9:33I lay in Zion a stone that causes people to stumble — Paul combines Isa 8:14 with 28:16.
  • Deut 18:10–12Let no one be found among you who practises divination or consults the dead — the Torah basis for Isa 8:19’s prohibition.
  • Luke 2:34Simeon: This child is destined to cause the falling and rising of many in Israel — echoing Isa 8:14’s stumbling stone.

Fear God, not circumstances. He will be either sanctuary OR stumbling stone. Same God, opposite outcomes depending on response.

"Bind up the testimony among my disciples." Isaiah creates a community of faithfulness. "I and the children the LORD has given me are signs and symbols in Israel."

Consult the dead? NO. "To the law and to the testimony!" The written word as the standard against which all claims are tested.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:14

Calvin reads the sanctuary-or-stumbling duality as the fundamental human choice: the same Christ is salvation for those who trust and ruin for those who reject. The object does not change; the response determines the outcome.

8:20

Calvin makes this verse programmatic: all spiritual claims must be tested against written Scripture. No experience, no vision, no voice overrides the word. This becomes a Reformation principle: sola Scriptura is anticipated by Isaiah.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:16

The NET notes "disciples" (limmudim) is the same word used in 50:4 (the Servant’s "instructed tongue"). Isaiah’s disciples and the Servant’s disciples share vocabulary.

8:20

The NET observes the phrase "no light of dawn" (shachar) puns on the darkness of v.22. Without Torah, there is no morning. The word IS the light.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
8:11–22

Oswalt reads the passage as Isaiah’s personal commission within the larger mission: he cannot change the nation, but he can preserve the testimony among a faithful few. The remnant theology becomes practical.

8:20

Oswalt: "to the law and to the testimony" is Isaiah’s hermeneutical principle. When confusion reigns, return to Scripture. The principle is as relevant today as in the 8th century BC.

Childs Notes

childs
8:16–18

Childs reads the "binding of the testimony" as the canonical moment: Isaiah’s oracles are entrusted to a community of transmission. This is the origin of the book of Isaiah as a written document — preserved by disciples across generations.

8:21–22

Childs notes the darkness-ending deliberately precedes 9:1–2’s great light. The canonical arrangement is not accidental: maximum darkness before maximum light.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
