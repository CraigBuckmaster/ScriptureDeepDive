Isaiah 66

All Nations, All Flesh: The Final Vision

Verses 1–14 — “Heaven Is My Throne and the Earth Is My Footstool”
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

כִּסֵא kissēʾ — throne: "Heaven is my throne (kissēʾ) and the earth is my footstool. Where is the house you will build me?" (v.1). Stephen quotes this in Acts 7:49–50 at his trial. God cannot be housed; temple-building is presumptu ous without the humility of v.2: "These are the ones I look on with favour: those who are humble and contrite in spirit, and who tremble at my word." The 57:15 paradox restated: the throne-God favours the trembling. Zion gives birth before labour pains — a nation born in a day (v.8). "As a mother comforts her child, so will I comfort you; and you will be comforted over Jerusalem" (v.13). God as MOTHER: the maternal comfort that answers ch.40’s opening "comfort, comfort." "You will see this and your heart will rejoice" (v.14).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
66:1–2

"Heaven is my throne, earth my footstool. Where is the house you will build me?" Acts 7:49–50: Stephen’s defence. The temple-critique: God inhabits heaven, not buildings. He looks on the humble and trembling.

66:7–8

"Before she goes into labour, she gives birth. Who has ever seen anything like this? Can a country be born in a day?" Birth without labour: the new creation is sudden, unprecedented, miraculous.

66:13

"As a mother comforts her child, so will I comfort you." God as mother. The book that opened with "comfort, comfort" (40:1) closes with maternal comfort. The inclusio is complete.

Verses 15–24 — “All Flesh Will Come and Bow Down Before Me”
15161718192021222324

Hebrew Word Study

כָּל בָּשָׂר kol bāsār — all flesh: "From one New Moon to another and from one Sabbath to another, all flesh (kol bāsār) will come and bow down before me, says the LORD" (v.23). The final vision. God comes with fire and chariots to judge (v.15). Those who consecrate themselves for pagan practices will meet their end (v.17). "I am about to come and gather the people of all nations and languages, and they will come and see my glory" (v.18). Survivors are sent to distant nations — Tarshish, Libya, Lydia, Tubal, Greece, distant islands — "and they will proclaim my glory among the nations" (v.19). "They will bring all your people from all the nations as an offering to the LORD" (v.20). "I will select some of them also to be priests and Levites" (v.21) — GENTILES as priests. The new heavens and new earth endure (v.22). All flesh worships forever (v.23). The book closes with v.24: the dead bodies of the rebels, where "the worms that eat them do not die, and the fire that burns them is not quenched." Jesus quotes this three times in Mark 9:48 for Gehenna. Isaiah ends with both the universal worship of v.23 AND the eternal judgment of v.24. Both are the final word.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
66:18–19

"I am about to gather the people of all nations. They will come and see my glory. They will proclaim my glory among the nations." The global mission: every nation, every language. The nations who never heard will see.

66:21

"I will select some of them to be priests and Levites." Gentiles as Levitical priests. The most radical inclusion statement in the OT. The priesthood itself opens to all nations.

66:22–23

"The new heavens and the new earth that I make will endure before me. All flesh will come and bow down before me." The endurance promise: what God creates will last. The worship: universal, perpetual, all-encompassing.

66:24

"The worms do not die, and the fire is not quenched." Jesus quotes this in Mark 9:48. Isaiah’s last verse is not comfort but warning. The book refuses to end on only one note. Worship AND judgment. Grace AND fire. Both are Isaiah’s final word.
