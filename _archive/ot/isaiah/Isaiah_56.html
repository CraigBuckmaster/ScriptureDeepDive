Isaiah 56

Third Isaiah Begins: A House of Prayer for All Nations

Verses 1–8 — “My House Will Be Called a House of Prayer for All Nations”
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

תְפִלָּה təfillâ — prayer: "My house will be called a house of prayer (təfillâ) for all nations" (v.7). Jesus quotes this when cleansing the temple (Mark 11:17). Third Isaiah (chs.56–66) begins with radical inclusion. The foreigner who binds himself to the LORD should not say "The LORD will surely exclude me" (v.3). The eunuch should not say "I am only a dry tree" (v.3). God gives them "an everlasting name that will endure forever" (v.5). "Their burnt offerings and sacrifices will be accepted on my altar; for my house will be called a house of prayer for all nations" (v.7). Foreigners and eunuchs — those excluded by Deuteronomy 23:1–3 — are explicitly included. The Ethiopian eunuch of Acts 8 is the living fulfilment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
56:3–5

"Let no foreigner say, The LORD will exclude me. Let no eunuch say, I am a dry tree. I will give them an everlasting name." The categories Deuteronomy excluded are now included. The law trajectory moves toward ever-wider inclusion.

56:7

"My house will be called a house of prayer for all nations." Jesus quotes this when overturning tables (Mark 11:17). The temple purpose: universal prayer. Jesus rage is not against commerce but against exclusion.

56:8

"The Sovereign LORD, who gathers the exiles of Israel, declares: I will gather still others to them besides those already gathered." The gathering is not finished. "Still others" — the door remains open.

Verses 9–12 — “Israel Blind Watchmen”
9101112

Hebrew Word Study

צֹפֶה ṣōfeh — watchman / lookout: "Israel watchmen (ṣōfeh) are blind, they all lack knowledge; they are all mute dogs, they cannot bark; they lie around and dream, they love to sleep" (v.10). After the inclusive vision (vv.1–8), the rebuke: the leaders fail. Blind watchmen, mute guard-dogs, shepherds who look to their own gain (vv.10–11). "Come, let us get wine! Let us drink our fill of beer! Tomorrow will be like today, or even far better" (v.12). The leaders party while the people perish. The contrast with the universal welcome of vv.1–8 is deliberate.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
56:10–11

"Blind watchmen, mute dogs, shepherds who lack understanding." Three images of failed leadership: eyes that cannot see, mouths that cannot warn, guides who cannot guide. The shepherd critique: they serve themselves.

56:12

"Come, let us drink! Tomorrow will be like today, or better!" The leaders intoxicated optimism echoes 22:13 ("let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die") but with a different spin: not nihilism but delusion.
