Isaiah 13

The Oracle Against Babylon

Verses 1–16 — “The Day of the LORD Is Coming”
12345678910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

מַשָּא massāʾ — oracle / burden: "A prophecy (massāʾ) against Babylon that Isaiah son of Amoz saw" (v.1). The Oracles Against Nations begin. God musters an army (v.4). The Day of the LORD comes: cruel, with wrath and fierce anger (v.9). The stars, sun, and moon go dark (v.10). "I will punish the world for its evil" (v.11). People more rare than gold (v.12). Babylon’s infants dashed, houses looted, wives violated (vv.15–16).

Context

The Oracles Against Nations (chs.13–23) is a standard prophetic genre (cf. Jer 46–51; Ezek 25–32; Amos 1–2). Isaiah places Babylon FIRST even though Assyria was the current threat in the 8th century — a remarkable prophetic anticipation of Babylon’s rise. The cosmic imagery (stars dark, sun black, moon not shining, v.10) becomes the template for NT eschatology: Jesus quotes it in Mark 13:24–25, and Rev 6:12–13 develops it. The Day of the LORD language here is the most developed in First Isaiah.

Cross-Reference

  • Rev 14:8Fallen! Fallen is Babylon the Great! — Revelation’s Babylon-oracle draws from Isa 13.
  • Mark 13:24–25The sun will be darkened, the moon will not give its light, the stars will fall — Jesus quotes Isa 13:10.
  • Rev 6:12–13The sun turned black, the moon turned blood-red, the stars fell — developing Isa 13:10’s cosmic signs.
  • Jer 50–51Jeremiah’s extended Babylon oracle — the same theology with later historical detail.

Calvin’s Commentary

Calvin notes Isaiah names Babylon as the primary target even though Babylon was not yet the dominant power. The prophetic horizon extends beyond the immediate political landscape.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
13:1–16

Oswalt reads the Babylon oracle as programmatic: Babylon represents the paradigm of human pride and self-exaltation that the entire oracle-against-nations section addresses. Every nation judged in chs.13–23 is a variation on the Babylon-theme.

13:10

Oswalt: the cosmic language signals that the Day of the LORD transcends any single historical event. Historical Babylon falls in 539 BC; eschatological Babylon falls in Revelation 18.

Childs Notes

childs
13:1–16

Childs reads the oracle as the canonical beginning of the nations-section: Babylon first because Babylon is Isaiah’s macro-adversary (chs.13–14, 21, 39, 43–47). The book’s arc is FROM Babylon (exile) THROUGH the servant TO the new creation.

13:10

Childs notes the cosmic imagery moves the text beyond any single historical referent. The text is simultaneously about 539 BC (historical fall) and about the eschaton (final judgment). Both readings are canonical.

Verses 17–22 — “Babylon Will Never Be Inhabited Again”
171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

תִפְאֶרֶת tifʾĕreṭ — beauty / glory: "Babylon, the jewel (tifʾĕreṭ) of kingdoms, the pride and glory of the Babylonians, will be overthrown by God like Sodom and Gomorrah" (v.19). God stirs up the Medes against them (v.17). Babylon will never be inhabited again (v.20). Desert creatures, owls, wild goats, hyenas, and jackals will fill her (vv.21–22). "Her time is at hand, and her days will not be prolonged" (v.22).

Context

The Medes (v.17) were the initial conquerors of Babylon — Cyrus the Persian allied with the Medes before conquering Babylon in 539 BC. The "never inhabited again" prophecy was progressively fulfilled: Babylon declined under the Persians, was partially rebuilt by Alexander (who died there in 323 BC), then gradually abandoned. By the first century AD it was largely deserted. Today the ruins near Hillah, Iraq, are uninhabited. The Sodom-Gomorrah comparison (v.19) frames Babylon’s fall as divine judgment, not merely political overthrow.

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase

Calvin reads the Sodom comparison as a measure of finality: Sodom was destroyed once and never rebuilt. Babylon will follow the same trajectory. Human glory is not permanent.

13:20–22

Calvin notes the catalogue of wild animals filling Babylon’s palaces is anti-creation: where human civilisation was, wilderness returns. Empire reverts to chaos.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press

The NET identifies the Medes as the initial conquerors: Cyrus was king of both Persia and Media. The Medes are named because they were the military force; the Persians provided the political framework.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
13:17–22

Oswalt reads the Medes oracle as evidence for the passage’s early date: the Medes were a significant power in Isaiah’s time (8th century), and the specific naming fits a prophet who sees the future in general strokes rather than a later author writing with hindsight.

13:19

Oswalt: "overthrown by God" — the divine passive is key. Babylon’s fall is not merely political but theological. God overthrows; the Medes are the instrument.

Childs Notes

childs
13:17–22

Childs reads the permanent desolation as the canonical counterpoint to the new Jerusalem of chs.60–66: Babylon falls forever; Zion rises forever. The two cities represent the two outcomes of human history.

13:19–22

Childs notes the wild-animal catalogue reverses creation’s order: where God placed humans to rule, animals now roam undomesticated. Empire’s fall is de-creation.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
