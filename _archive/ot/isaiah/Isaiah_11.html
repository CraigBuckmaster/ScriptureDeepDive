Isaiah 11

The Shoot from the Stump of Jesse

Verses 1–9 — “The Wolf Will Live with the Lamb”
123456789

Hebrew Word Study

חֹטֶר ḥōṭer — shoot / branch: "A shoot (ḥōṭer) will come up from the stump of Jesse; from his roots a Branch will bear fruit" (v.1). The Spirit of the LORD rests on him: wisdom, understanding, counsel, might, knowledge, fear of the LORD — six attributes, or seven if the fear of the LORD is the crown (v.2). He judges with righteousness; with the breath of his lips he slays the wicked (v.4). The wolf lives with the lamb, the leopard lies down with the goat, a little child leads them (v.6). Nothing harms or destroys on all God’s holy mountain; the earth is full of the knowledge of the LORD as the waters cover the sea (v.9).

Context

The stump of Jesse (not David) is significant: the dynasty is cut back to its pre-royal root. The Messiah emerges not from Davidic glory but from Davidic ruin. The seven-fold Spirit (v.2) is traditionally counted as: wisdom, understanding, counsel, might, knowledge, fear of the LORD (with fear appearing twice in v.3, creating seven). Revelation 5:6 describes the Lamb with "seven spirits" — possibly drawing on this Isaiah text. The peace-kingdom vision (vv.6–9) is the most beloved utopian text in the OT: predator and prey reconciled, creation at peace, a child safe among lions.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 3:16–17The Spirit descending on Jesus at baptism — the fulfilment of the Spirit resting on the shoot (11:2).
  • Rom 15:12The Root of Jesse will spring up; in him the Gentiles will hope — Paul quotes Isa 11:10.
  • Rev 5:5The Lion of the tribe of Judah, the Root of David — Revelation’s titles draw from Isa 11:1, 10.
  • Rev 22:1–5The river of life; no more curse — the new creation that fulfils Isa 11:6–9’s peace kingdom.
  • Rom 8:19–21Creation itself will be liberated from its bondage to decay — the cosmic restoration Isa 11 envisions.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:1

Calvin reads the stump as essential: the Messiah comes not from Davidic glory but from Davidic humiliation. The dynasty must be reduced to nothing before the shoot appears. This is the cross-principle: exaltation through humiliation.

11:6–9

Calvin interprets the animal-peace as both literal (the creation will be restored) and figurative (violent humans will be tamed by the gospel). Both readings coexist: the gospel transforms hearts now; the new creation transforms nature later.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:1

The NET notes "stump" (gezaʿ) implies the tree has been CUT DOWN. The Davidic dynasty is not just weakened but felled. The shoot’s emergence from a stump is resurrection in agricultural metaphor.

11:6–9

The NET observes the four predator-prey pairs (wolf/lamb, leopard/goat, calf/lion, cow/bear) systematically dismantle the natural order of violence. The peace is comprehensive, not selective.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
11:1–9

Oswalt reads the passage as the climax of the Immanuel section (chs.7–12): the child of 7:14 and 9:6 now receives the Spirit, governs with justice, and establishes cosmic peace. The messianic portrait is complete.

11:6–9

Oswalt: the peace-kingdom reverses Eden’s curse (Gen 3:14–19). The enmity between serpent and woman’s seed is resolved: the infant plays safely by the cobra’s den. The Messiah undoes what Adam’s sin caused.

Childs Notes

childs
11:1–9

Childs reads the passage as the canonical answer to the question raised by 6:13 ("the holy seed is the stump"): what grows from the stump? The shoot of Jesse. The stump-theology finds its resolution here.

11:9

Childs notes "the earth full of the knowledge of the LORD as waters cover the sea" is the eschatological vision toward which the entire book moves. Hab 2:14 quotes it; the NT inherits it.

Verses 10–16 — “The Second Exodus”
10111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

נֵס nēs — banner / signal: "In that day the Root of Jesse will stand as a banner (nēs) for the peoples; the nations will rally to him" (v.10). The Lord will reach out his hand a SECOND time to reclaim the remnant — from Assyria, Egypt, Cush, Elam, Babylonia, Hamath, and the islands of the Mediterranean (v.11). He will gather the exiles of Israel and the scattered people of Judah from the four quarters of the earth (v.12). Ephraim and Judah reconciled (v.13). The sea dried up as at the exodus (v.15). A highway from Assyria, as there was for Israel when they came up from Egypt (v.16).

Context

The "second time" (v.11) explicitly invokes the exodus as the pattern: what God did once (delivering from Egypt), he will do again (delivering from exile). The geographical scope (Assyria, Egypt, Cush, Elam, Babylonia, Hamath, islands) covers the entire known world — the regathering is universal. The reunion of Ephraim (northern) and Judah (southern) reverses the division of 1 Kings 12. The highway motif (v.16) becomes central in Isaiah (35:8; 40:3; 62:10). Rom 15:12 quotes v.10 for the inclusion of the Gentiles.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 15:12The Root of Jesse will spring up; in him the Gentiles will hope — Paul quotes v.10 as the scriptural basis for Gentile inclusion.
  • Ex 14:21–22God divided the sea — the first exodus that vv.15–16 explicitly parallel.
  • Isa 35:8–10A highway will be there, the Way of Holiness — the highway theme expanded.
  • Isa 40:3Prepare the way for the LORD — the highway of the second exodus.
  • Rev 7:9A great multitude from every nation — the fulfilment of the universal gathering of 11:10–12.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:10

Calvin reads the "banner for the peoples" as the gospel proclaimed to all nations: Christ is the rallying point not just for Israel but for every people. The scope is universal from the beginning.

11:15–16

Calvin: the second exodus is the pattern for all subsequent deliverances. Every time God rescues his people, he reenacts the exodus. The pattern is permanent.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:10

The NET notes "Root of Jesse" shifts from "shoot FROM the stump" (v.1) to "Root OF Jesse" — the Messiah is both Jesse’s descendant and Jesse’s origin. Rev 22:16: "I am the Root and Offspring of David."

11:11

The NET lists the geographic spread: east (Elam, Babylonia), south (Egypt, Cush), north (Assyria, Hamath), west (islands). The compass is complete.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
11:10–16

Oswalt reads the second-exodus theme as the bridge between First and Second Isaiah: ch.11 announces it; chs.40–55 develop it in detail. The concept of a new exodus is arguably Isaiah’s most influential contribution to NT theology (Mark 1:2–3; Luke 9:31).

11:15–16

Oswalt: the highway motif transforms geography into theology. A physical road becomes a metaphor for God’s provision of a way home. The highway IS the grace.

Childs Notes

childs
11:10–16

Childs reads the passage as the conclusion of the Immanuel section: the messianic king (11:1–9) draws the nations (v.10) and regathers Israel (vv.11–16). The section that began with Ahaz’s fear (ch.7) ends with universal hope (ch.11).

11:16

Childs notes the "highway from Assyria" anticipates 40:3 ("prepare the way of the LORD"). The highway-theology is Isaiah’s literary and theological spine.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
