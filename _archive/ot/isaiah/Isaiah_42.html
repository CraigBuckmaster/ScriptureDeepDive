Isaiah 42

The First Servant Song: A Bruised Reed He Will Not Break

Verses 1–9 — “Here Is My Servant, Whom I Uphold”
123456789

Hebrew Word Study

עֶבֶד ʿebed — servant: "Here is my servant (ʿebed), whom I uphold, my chosen one in whom I delight; I will put my Spirit on him, and he will bring justice to the nations" (v.1). THE FIRST SERVANT SONG (42:1–4). Matthew 12:18–21 quotes this entire passage for Jesus. The servant’s method: "He will not shout or cry out, or raise his voice in the streets. A bruised reed he will not break, and a smouldering wick he will not snuff out" (vv.2–3). Gentleness as messianic strategy. He brings justice not by force but by persistence: "he will not falter or be discouraged till he establishes justice on earth" (v.4). God declares: "I will make you a covenant for the people and a light for the Gentiles, to open eyes that are blind, to free captives from prison" (vv.6–7). Simeon quotes v.6 at Jesus’ presentation: "a light for revelation to the Gentiles" (Luke 2:32).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
42:1

"Here is my servant, whom I uphold, my chosen one in whom I delight." God’s delight in the servant. At Jesus’ baptism: "This is my Son, in whom I am well pleased" (Matt 3:17) — echoing Isaiah 42:1.

42:2–3

"A bruised reed he will not break, and a smouldering wick he will not snuff out." The tenderest messianic image. The almost-broken are not discarded. The almost-extinguished are not snuffed. Matthew 12:20 applies this to Jesus’ healing ministry.

42:6–7

"I will make you a covenant for the people and a light for the Gentiles, to open eyes that are blind." The servant IS the covenant — not just bringing it but embodying it. Simeon (Luke 2:32) and Paul (Acts 13:47) quote the light-to-Gentiles promise.

Verses 10–25 — “Sing to the LORD a New Song”

Hebrew Word Study

שִׁיר חָדָשׁ shîr ḥādāsh — new song: "Sing to the LORD a new song (shîr ḥādāsh), his praise from the ends of the earth" (v.10). The servant’s mission triggers global worship: sea, islands, desert, Kedar, Sela shout from the mountaintops (vv.10–12). God like a warrior, like a woman in labour (v.13–14). "I will lead the blind by ways they have not known; I will turn the darkness into light before them" (v.16). But Israel is blind and deaf — the servant is needed because the people are not serving (vv.18–20). God is robbed and plundered, trapped and hidden (v.22). "Who among you will listen to this?" (v.23). God handed them over to plunderers because they would not walk in his ways (v.24).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
42:10–12

"Sing to the LORD a new song, his praise from the ends of the earth." The servant’s quiet ministry (vv.1–4) produces global singing (vv.10–12). The contrast: whisper-gentle method, earth-filling result.

42:16

"I will lead the blind by ways they have not known. I will turn the darkness into light." God as guide for the blind. The blind-leading motif: 35:5 promised it; 42:16 delivers it; Jesus enacts it.

42:18–20

"Hear, you deaf; look, you blind! Who is blind but my servant? Who is deaf like the messenger I send?" The paradox: the servant is both the light-bringer (v.6) AND the blind nation (v.19). Israel needs to be rescued before it can rescue others.
