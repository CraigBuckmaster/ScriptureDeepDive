Isaiah 2

Swords into Ploughshares

Verses 1–5 — “They Will Beat Their Swords into Ploughshares”
12345

Hebrew Word Study

תוֹרָה tōrâ — instruction / law / Torah: "The law (tōrâ) will go out from Zion, the word of the LORD from Jerusalem" (v.3). The mountain of the LORD’s temple established as highest of mountains (v.2). All nations stream to it (v.2). God will judge between nations; they will beat swords into ploughshares, spears into pruning hooks. Nation will not take up sword against nation, nor train for war anymore (v.4). "Come, let us walk in the light of the LORD" (v.5).

Context

This oracle appears almost identically in Micah 4:1–5 — whether Isaiah borrowed from Micah, Micah from Isaiah, or both from a shared tradition is debated. The vision of universal peace centred on Zion reverses the tower of Babel: at Babel, nations scattered; here, they stream together. The United Nations building in New York has Isa 2:4 inscribed on its wall (the "Isaiah Wall"). Joel 3:10 deliberately reverses the image: "Beat your ploughshares into swords" — before the final battle, weapons are NEEDED; after it, they are not.

Cross-Reference

  • Mic 4:1–5The parallel oracle: nearly word-for-word identical. Two prophets, one vision.
  • Joel 3:10Beat your ploughshares into swords — the deliberate reversal for the pre-judgment period.
  • Rev 21:24–26The nations will walk by the light of the new Jerusalem — the fulfilment of Isa 2:2–4.
  • Eph 2:14–16Christ is our peace, who made the two groups one and destroyed the dividing wall — the NT’s peace-making theology.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:2–3

"In the last days the mountain of the LORD will be established as the highest. All nations will stream to it." The eschatological vision: Zion elevated, nations drawn by gravity of glory.

2:4

"They will beat their swords into ploughshares." The most famous peace text in the Bible. Military technology repurposed for agriculture. War-training abolished.

2:5

"Come, let us walk in the light of the LORD." The response: if THIS is the future, then walk toward it now.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:2–4

Calvin reads the fulfilment as beginning with the gospel’s spread: the nations streaming to Zion is fulfilled as the church draws people from every nation. The ultimate consummation awaits Christ’s return.

2:4

Calvin notes the double transformation: swords to ploughshares AND spears to pruning hooks. Both offensive and defensive weapons become agricultural tools. The economy shifts from war to cultivation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:2

The NET notes "in the last days" (bəʾacharit hayyamim) is the standard eschatological formula. The vision is not for the immediate future but for the culmination of history.

2:4

The NET observes the passive "they will beat" — the subject is the nations themselves. God judges; the nations voluntarily disarm. Peace is a response to divine justice, not a political achievement.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
2:2–4

Oswalt reads this as the positive counterpart to ch.1’s negative indictment: ch.1 is what Judah IS; ch.2 is what Zion WILL BE. Judgment is not the last word; this vision is.

2:4

Oswalt: the swords-to-ploughshares image is not pacifism per se but eschatological peace — war ends because God’s justice makes it unnecessary. Peace is the RESULT of divine rule, not human negotiation.

Childs Notes

childs
2:2–5

Childs reads this oracle as the canonical thesis for the Book of Isaiah: despite 66 chapters of judgment, exile, suffering, and restoration, the trajectory is toward THIS — universal peace under God’s rule.

2:4

Childs notes the UN inscription is an ironic secular appropriation: the text is inseparable from its theological grounding ("the LORD will judge between nations"). Remove the judge, and the peace has no foundation.

Verses 6–22 — “The Eyes of the Arrogant Will Be Humbled”
678910111213141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

גַּבְהוּת gabhūṭ — pride / haughtiness: "The eyes of the arrogant will be humbled and human pride (gabhūṭ) brought low; the LORD alone will be exalted in that day" (v.11). The Day of the LORD against everything proud and lofty: cedars of Lebanon, oaks of Bashan, high mountains, fortified walls, trading ships, every lofty tower (vv.12–16). Idols will totally disappear (v.18). People will flee to caves (v.19). "Stop trusting in mere humans, who have but a breath in their nostrils" (v.22).

Context

The Day of the LORD tradition (first fully developed in Amos 5:18–20) is here applied to human pride in all its forms: natural (cedars, mountains), military (walls, towers), economic (trading ships), and religious (idols). The catalogue is comprehensive: nothing that human beings have elevated will remain standing. The cave-fleeing imagery (vv.19–21) is developed in Revelation 6:15–17 ("they called to the mountains and rocks, fall on us"). Verse 22 is one of the Bible’s most radical anthropological statements: humans are just breath.

Cross-Reference

  • Amos 5:18–20The Day of the LORD will be darkness, not light — the original Day-of-the-LORD warning that Isaiah develops.
  • Rev 6:15–17They hid in caves and called to the mountains to fall on them — directly developing Isa 2:19–21.
  • Phil 2:9–11Every knee will bow; every tongue confess — the positive counterpart to Isa 2’s humbling of the proud.
  • Ps 46:10Be still and know that I am God; I will be exalted among the nations — the peaceful version of Isa 2:11’s exaltation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:11–12

"The LORD alone will be exalted in that day." The Day of the LORD strips away every competing exaltation: nature, military, commerce, religion.

2:17–18

Human pride brought low; idols totally disappear. The two problems (pride and idolatry) are revealed as one problem: humans worship what they’ve elevated.

2:19–21

Cave-fleeing. The terror of the LORD’s splendour. The same mountains people trusted for protection now become hiding places FROM God.

2:22

"Stop trusting in mere humans." The chapter’s practical conclusion: misplaced trust is the root of both pride and idolatry.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:12–16

Calvin reads the catalogue (cedars, mountains, towers, ships) as a systematic demolition of every category of human achievement. God is not threatened by human greatness; he simply removes it.

2:22

Calvin calls this the most important verse in the chapter for daily application: the temptation to trust in human leaders, institutions, and achievements is constant. Isaiah’s answer: they are breath.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:12

The NET notes "the LORD Almighty has a day in store" uses the prophetic perfect — the day is so certain it is described as already scheduled.

2:22

The NET observes "breath in their nostrils" echoes Gen 2:7 (God breathed into Adam’s nostrils). The very breath that makes humans alive is borrowed. Remove it, and they are dust.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
2:6–22

Oswalt reads the Day of the LORD passage as the book’s first extended warning: the peace of 2:2–4 requires the humbling of 2:6–22. There is no shortcut from arrogance to ploughshares. Pride must be demolished first.

2:22

Oswalt: this verse is Isaiah’s anthropology in a sentence. Humans are contingent, dependent, temporary. Every political and military structure built on this foundation will collapse.

Childs Notes

childs
2:6–22

Childs notes the canonical placement: the universal peace vision (vv.2–4) is immediately followed by the universal humbling (vv.6–22). The book refuses to offer hope without first exposing what must be removed. The structure is: vision → demolition → reconstruction.

2:22

Childs reads the closing verse as a hermeneutical instruction: the reader of Isaiah should approach the entire book with this anthropological realism — humans are breath; God alone is solid.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
