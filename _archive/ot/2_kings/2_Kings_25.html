2 Kings 25

The Destruction of Jerusalem

Verses 1–12 — Siege, Famine, Breach; Zedekiah Captured and Blinded
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

shamayim shamayim — heavens/sky: The siege begins on the tenth day of the tenth month (v.1) and the wall is breached on the ninth day of the fourth month (v.3–4) — approximately eighteen months. These dates become perpetual fast days in Jewish tradition (Zech 8:19). The calendar of catastrophe is fixed in communal memory.

Context

In Zedekiah’s ninth year, Nebuchadnezzar besieges Jerusalem. The siege lasts eighteen months. Famine becomes severe. The wall is breached. Zedekiah flees toward the Jordan but is captured in the plains of Jericho — where Joshua entered the land, the last king exits it. He is brought to Nebuchadnezzar at Riblah. His sons are killed before his eyes, then his eyes are put out — the last thing Zedekiah sees is the death of his dynasty. He is bound in bronze shackles and taken to Babylon. The king who should have shepherded God’s people is blinded, chained, and led away.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 39:1–10Jeremiah’s parallel account adds that Nebuchadnezzar’s officers sat in the Middle Gate after the breach and that Jeremiah was released from prison.
  • Jer 52:4–11Jeremiah 52 reproduces this chapter almost verbatim — the catastrophe was important enough to record in two separate canonical locations.
  • Ezek 12:12–13Ezekiel prophesied that the king would “cover his face so that he cannot see the land” and would be taken to Babylon — he would “not see it.” Fulfilled in Zedekiah’s blinding.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:7

Zedekiah’s blinding is the ultimate symbol of Judah’s spiritual condition: a nation that refused to see God’s truth is finally made physically unable to see anything. The punishment mirrors the sin.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:1

The date “ninth year, tenth month, tenth day” is preserved with calendrical precision because it marks the beginning of Jerusalem’s end. Both Jeremiah (52:4) and Ezekiel (24:1–2) record the same date independently.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:1

The Babylonian Chronicles confirm the siege, and the Lachish Letters (ostraca found at Tell ed-Duweir) provide eyewitness testimony from Judah’s last days: “We are watching for the signals of Lachish... for we cannot see Azekah.” The fortress cities are falling one by one.

25:6

Riblah (modern Ribleh in Syria’s Beqaa Valley) was Nebuchadnezzar’s military headquarters — approximately 300 km north of Jerusalem. It was a standard Mesopotamian practice to bring captured kings to the imperial court for judgment.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:5

The narrator records Zedekiah’s flight and capture without commentary. The facts speak: the Davidic king abandons his city, is caught in open country, and is subjected to a foreign emperor’s judgment. Every dimension of the Davidic promise — city, throne, freedom — is stripped away.

Verses 13–21 — Temple Destroyed; Vessels Taken; Executions at Riblah
131415161718192021

Context

Nebuzaradan, commander of the guard, burns the temple, the royal palace, and every important building in Jerusalem. The bronze pillars Jachin and Boaz, the bronze Sea, the movable stands — everything Solomon built is broken up and carried to Babylon. The gold and silver vessels are taken. The chief priest Seraiah and the second priest Zephaniah are executed at Riblah. The narrator catalogues the destruction with archival precision, listing every object removed. The inventory is a reverse echo of 1 Kings 7, where Solomon’s temple furnishings were catalogued with pride. What was built with glory is dismantled with thoroughness.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 7:15–51Solomon’s temple furnishings — the bronze pillars, the Sea, the stands. Every item described there is destroyed here. The destruction list is the construction list in reverse.
  • Lam 1:1–2“How deserted lies the city, once so full of people! How like a widow is she, who once was great among the nations!” The book of Lamentations is the emotional response to this chapter.
  • Ps 74:3–8“Turn your steps toward these everlasting ruins, all this destruction the enemy has brought on the sanctuary.” A psalm lamenting the temple’s destruction.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:9

The destruction of the temple demonstrates that God values his own holiness more than his own house. He would rather burn his dwelling place than inhabit a defiled one. This is the ultimate refutation of the false theology that God will always protect Jerusalem (Jer 7:4).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:13

The bronze pillars (each 18 cubits/27 feet tall) and the bronze Sea (10 cubits/15 feet in diameter) were among the most impressive metalwork objects in the ancient Near East. Their destruction and transport to Babylon represents both material loss and symbolic defeat.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:8

The seventh day of the fifth month (Av) in Nebuchadnezzar’s nineteenth year = approximately August 586 BC (or 587, depending on calendar calculation). The ninth of Av (Tisha B’Av) became the most significant fast day in Judaism, commemorating both temple destructions (586 BC and 70 AD).

25:13

The weight of bronze from the temple pillars, Sea, and stands was incalculable. Babylonian records confirm the transport of looted temple furnishings from conquered cities to Babylon, where they were displayed in Marduk’s temple (cf. Dan 5:2–4).

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:13

The reverse inventory — listing what was removed rather than what was made — is the narrator’s literary technique for expressing irreversible loss. The reader who remembers 1 Kings 7 recognises each item and knows it is gone. The narrative carries its grief in its structure.

Verses 22–30 — Gedaliah Appointed; Assassinated; Remnant Flees; Jehoiachin Released
222324252627282930

Context

Nebuchadnezzar appoints Gedaliah as governor over the remnant. Ishmael, a royal descendant, assassinates him. The survivors flee to Egypt, fearing Babylonian reprisal — completing the circle: Israel came out of Egypt with Moses; now the remnant returns to Egypt in terror. But the book does not end there. Its final four verses shift to 561 BC: Evil-Merodach of Babylon releases Jehoiachin from prison, gives him a seat above the other captive kings, and provides a regular allowance for the rest of his life. The Davidic king is not dead. The dynasty is in exile, not extinct. The last word is not destruction but a whisper of survival — a lamp still burning in the darkness.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 40–41Jeremiah provides the extended account of Gedaliah’s governorship and Ishmael’s assassination, including the flight to Egypt against Jeremiah’s explicit warning.
  • 2 Sam 7:14–16“When he does wrong, I will punish him... But my love will never be taken away from him... Your throne will be established forever.” Jehoiachin’s release is the “never taken away” clause in action.
  • Matt 1:12“After the exile to Babylon: Jeconiah was the father of Shealtiel.” The genealogy passes through the exiled king. The messianic line survives Babylon.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:27

Jehoiachin’s release is God’s final word in Kings — and it is a word of mercy. After judgment, mercy. After exile, provision. After death, a flicker of life. The God who punishes is also the God who preserves. The book closes not with thunder but with a quiet table setting in Babylon.

25:30

The last word is not “destruction” or “exile” but “as long as he lived.” God sustains the Davidic king for the rest of his life. The promise endures because the Promiser endures. This is the foundation on which all subsequent hope — return from exile, rebuilding, messianic expectation — will be built.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:27

ʾEvil-Mĕrōdak (Amel-Marduk, “man of Marduk”) reigned 562–560 BC. His release of Jehoiachin in the 37th year of captivity (= 561 BC) is the latest datable event in Kings and establishes the terminus post quem for the book’s final composition.

25:28

The phrase “spoke kindly” (dibbēr ʿîtlō tōbōt) echoes Joseph’s brothers’ treatment in Genesis (Gen 50:21) and Ruth’s treatment by Boaz (Ruth 2:13). The language of grace recurs at moments of unexpected kindness.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:22

Gedaliah’s administrative seat at Mizpah (Tell en-Nasbeh, 12 km north of Jerusalem) is confirmed by a seal impression found at the site reading “Belonging to Gedaliah who is over the house” — a title for the chief minister.

25:27

The Jehoiachin’s Rations Tablets from Babylon record provisions allocated to “Yaukin king of Yahud” and five princes. These cuneiform documents, dating to 592 BC, confirm the biblical account of Jehoiachin’s captive existence and eventual favoured status.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:27

The book’s final four verses are the most theologically significant in the Deuteronomistic History. After 400 pages of failure and judgment, the narrator ends with a Davidic king eating at a foreign king’s table. It is not triumph; it is survival. It is not restoration; it is hope. The promise lives because the king lives.

25:30

“Day by day the king gave Jehoiachin a regular allowance as long as he lived.” The final sentence is not a summary but a promise: the provision continues indefinitely. As long as Jehoiachin lives, David’s line lives. And as long as David’s line lives, God’s promise lives.
