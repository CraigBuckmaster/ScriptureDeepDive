Nehemiah 7

The Register of Returnees

Verses 1–4 — Guards Appointed; City Still Under-Populated
1234

Hebrew Word Study

יָרֵא yārēʾ — to fear / to revere: Hanani is chosen because "he was a man of integrity and feared God more than most people." The criterion for leadership is not competence but character — specifically, the fear of God.

Context

The wall is built but the city is empty. "The city was large and spacious, but there were few people in it" (v.4). Defence without population is unsustainable. The wall protects a shell. Chapter 11 will address repopulation; here Nehemiah sets the security structure first.

Cross-Reference

  • Neh 11:1–2The repopulation solution: lots cast, volunteers blessed.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1–2

Gatekeepers, singers, and Levites are appointed — security plus worship. The wall serves both functions: defence and liturgical boundary.

7:2

"Hanani and Hananiah the commander of the citadel." Dual leadership: civic and military. Nehemiah delegates to trusted men, not to a bureaucracy.

7:3

"The gates of Jerusalem are not to be opened until the sun is hot." Enhanced security protocols. The wall is built; now it must be guarded.

7:4

"Few people in it, and the houses had not yet been rebuilt." The wall without inhabitants is an empty fortress. Nehemiah sees the next problem before finishing the current one.

Verses 5–73 — The Genealogical Register

Hebrew Word Study

הִתְיַחֵשׂ hityaḥēś — to reckon genealogy: "God put it into my heart to assemble the nobles and register the people by genealogies" (v.5). The registration is divinely prompted. Nehemiah does not take a census for administrative convenience but because God directs it.

Historical Context

This register is virtually identical to Ezra 2 with minor variations in names and numbers. Both derive from an official record of the first return under Zerubbabel (538 BC). Nehemiah finds this document approximately ninety years later and uses it as the basis for community identity in his generation.

Context

The register serves a specific purpose in Nehemiah’s narrative: establishing who belongs to the community before the covenant renewal ceremony of chapters 8–10. Identity precedes covenant. You must know who the covenant people are before you can renew the covenant.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezra 2:1–70The parallel register — virtually identical, confirming a common source document.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:5

"God put it into my heart." Nehemiah attributes the genealogical impulse to divine prompting. Even administrative acts can be Spirit-led.

7:6–69

The list reprises Ezra 2 with minor variations. The repetition is not redundancy but emphasis: the community’s identity is grounded in continuity with the first return.

7:64–65

Those who could not prove ancestry are excluded from the priesthood — the same standard as Ezra 2:62–63. Genealogical integrity remains non-negotiable.

7:70–73

Contributions for the work: gold, silver, priestly garments. "The people settled in their own towns." The chapter ends with settlement, preparing for the assembly of chapter 8.

Williamson Notes

williamson
7:5–73

Williamson provides a detailed textual comparison between Nehemiah 7 and Ezra 2, cataloguing the numerical variations. He concludes both derive from a common Vorlage (source document) with minor scribal transmission differences.

7:70–73

Williamson notes the contribution figures differ from Ezra 2:68–69. He attributes the differences to separate counting traditions or different stages of collection.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Hanani
Nehemiah’s brother; appointed gate commander
The man who brought the original devastating report (1:2) is now entrusted with guarding the rebuilt wall. The messenger becomes the guardian.
