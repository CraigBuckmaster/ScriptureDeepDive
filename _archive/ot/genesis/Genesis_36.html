Genesis 36

The Descendants of Esau; the Kings of Edom

Hebrew Word Study

אֱדוֹם ʾĕdôm — Edom / Red: Related to ʾādôm (red) and the red stew of 25:30. Edom became the name of Esau's descendants and their territory east and south of the Dead Sea.

תּוֹלְדֹת tôlĕdōt — generations / account: The toledot formula organizes all of Genesis. Ch.36's toledot of Esau is the rejected line's account — necessary to show God's sovereignty operates even outside the chosen line.

Historical Context

The Esau genealogy of Genesis 36 is one of the most comprehensive genealogical lists in the OT, covering three generations of Esau's descendants and the chieftains (allufim) of Edom. It fulfils the promise made to Abraham that he would be "the father of many nations" (17:4-5) and to Rebekah that "two nations are in your womb" (25:23). The list also demonstrates the fulfillment of the Abrahamic blessing on Esau specifically: "I will make you into a great nation" (cf. 17:20 for Ishmael; the principle applies to both rejected sons). Edom will be a persistent presence — and enemy — in Israel's later history.

Context

The toledot of Esau stands between the end of the Jacob narrative (ch.35) and the beginning of the Joseph narrative (ch.37). It functions as a bracket — Esau's line is established and then set aside, exactly as Ishmael's line was established (ch.25) before returning to Isaac. The pattern: rejected line receives a complete genealogy then exits the main story, while the chosen line carries the covenant forward. Esau's departure from Canaan echoes Lot's separation from Abraham (ch.13) — both involve too many possessions to coexist, both result in the chosen man remaining in the land of promise.

Places

Edom / Seir
Transjordan; modern southern Jordan
Esau moves to Seir because the land cannot support both Jacob's and Esau's flocks — the same reason Abraham and Lot separated. Edom occupies the territory east and south of the Dead Sea, which becomes the permanent Edomite homeland. The Table of Nations logic applies: Esau's descendants fill a specific geography.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
36:1

The toledoth of Esau (36:1,9) is the formal heading that signals a complete genealogical unit. Like the toledoth of Ishmael (25:12-18), it gives Esau's line its full due before the narrative moves on to Jacob's descendants. The principle of the rejected line receiving its complete genealogical treatment before the covenant line continues is consistent: God has not forgotten or minimised those outside the primary covenant.

36:6-8

Esau's voluntary separation from Jacob — moving to Seir because the land could not support both — echoes Abraham and Lot's separation (13:5-12). In both cases the separation is amicable and territory-based; in both cases the non-covenant figure moves away from the land of promise. Esau settles in Edom; the land of Canaan remains for Jacob's descendants. Providence works through human logistics.

36:15-19

The list of Esau's chiefs (allufim — "thousands," but used for clan leaders) is the first instance of the political structure that will characterise Edom throughout its history. The word alluf comes from elef (thousand) — each chief commands a military unit of a thousand. Edom is from its foundation a structured political entity. The nation that Israel will encounter at the Exodus already has this political form.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
36:1

Sarna notes that the double toledoth heading (v.1: "Esau who is Edom"; v.9: "Esau the father of the Edomites") is unusual — most genealogies have a single heading. He reads the double heading as reflecting two distinct source units that the editor has joined: vv.1-8 (Esau's wives and sons) and vv.9-19 (the chieftains). Both use the same toledoth formula to signal their completeness.

36:31

The note "before any Israelite king reigned" (v.31) is often cited as evidence for a post-Mosaic author, since it implies knowledge of Israel's kingship. Sarna reviews the interpretive options: (1) the note is editorial, added by a later hand; (2) Moses writes it anticipating the kingship promised in 17:6 and 35:11; (3) it is a scribal gloss. Sarna reads it as consistent with Mosaic authorship writing in light of the covenant promise of kings.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
36:6-8

The separation of Esau from Jacob is described with the same vocabulary as Abraham and Lot's separation (13:6: "the land could not support them while they stayed together"). Alter: "The parallel is not accidental. Both separations are amicable, both are necessity-driven, both result in the covenant heir retaining the land. The narrative uses the earlier separation as the template for understanding the later one. Esau's departure is orderly, dignified, and providential."

36:31

The parenthetical note about Edomite kings "before any Israelite king reigned" is one of the most discussed editorial markers in Genesis. Alter notes it reads like a later addition from someone aware of the Davidic monarchy. But within the narrative it also serves a rhetorical function: Edom had kings before Israel did. The people outside the covenant developed political structures first. Israel's kingship — when it comes in 1 Samuel — arrives late and with ambivalence.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
36:1-2

Calvin on the Esau genealogy: "Moses here turns aside, for a little while, from the principal object of his history, that he may briefly dispatch the race of Esau... God was not unmindful of his promise, that Esau also should become a great nation." The genealogy is evidence of covenant faithfulness even to the rejected line. Esau's exclusion from the primary covenant does not mean divine abandonment.

36:31

On the Edomite kings before Israel: "Moses, speaking by the Spirit of prophecy, expressly mentions the time when God would establish a kingdom for his people." Calvin reads the note as prophetic: Moses writes of Israelite kingship as a future fact, anticipating David. The Edomite list serves as a foil — the nations of the earth develop their political structures while Israel waits for its covenant king.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
36:15

The NET note on allufim ("chiefs") discusses the term's derivation from elef (thousand) and its use in the OT as a political/military title for clan leaders. The note observes that these chiefs are the predecessors of the kings listed in vv.31-39, suggesting a political evolution from clan confederacy to monarchy in Edom that mirrors Israel's own later development.

36:31

The NET note on "before any Israelite king reigned" surveys the debate about authorship implications. The note presents both the Mosaic-anticipatory reading (looking forward to the promised kings of 17:6; 35:11) and the later-addition view, concluding that either is grammatically possible. The note observes this is one of the key texts in discussions about Pentateuchal authorship.

Verses 20–43 — The Horites of Seir; Kings and Chiefs of Edom

Historical Context

The Horite genealogy (vv.20-30) and the list of Edomite kings (vv.31-39) are the most detailed pre-Israelite political records in Genesis. The eight Edomite kings who reigned before Israel had a king represent a complete royal succession — the monarchy was not hereditary (each king is from a different place and family) but elective, a confederacy of chieftains choosing among themselves. The final list of allufim (vv.40-43) likely represents the political structure of Edom at the time of the Exodus.

The toledot of Esau stands between the end of the Jacob narrative (ch.35) and the beginning of the Joseph narrative (ch.37). Places

Seir / Edom
Southern Transjordan; modern Petra region
The Horites are the pre-Edomite inhabitants of Seir — they will eventually be displaced by Esau's descendants. The kings of Edom who reigned before Israel had a king (v.31) map the political geography of the Transjordanian plateau.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
36:20-28

The Horite genealogy precedes the Edomite king-list because the Horites were the original inhabitants of Seir, the region Esau settled (14:6; Deut 2:12,22). The Edomites absorbed or displaced the Horites. The genealogy documents both peoples because both are part of the history of the region Jacob's brother will occupy. The careful documentation of Horite ancestry — including Anah who discovered hot springs (v.24) — reflects a thoroughness unusual for non-covenant genealogies.

36:31-39

Eight kings reigned in Edom before Israel had a king. The list is notable for what it lacks: no dynastic succession (each king from a different city), no lengths of reign, no moral evaluation. The Edomite monarchy is presented as a fact, not as a model. Israel's later ambivalence about kingship (1 Sam 8) will be shaped partly by these surrounding monarchies — Edom already had kings; Israel wants to be "like the other nations."

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
36:20-30

The Horite list is extraordinary in its specificity — names, parentages, and even one topographical discovery (Anah finding hot springs). Sarna: "The inclusion of this level of detail for a non-Israelite, non-covenant group reflects the understanding in Genesis that all the earth's peoples are descendants of Noah and therefore worthy of historical record. The covenant with Abraham does not eliminate the significance of the non-covenant peoples."

36:40-43

The final list of allufim (vv.40-43) is distinguished from the earlier chieftain list (vv.15-19) by including geographical designations alongside personal names. Sarna: "This suggests the allufim of vv.40-43 are territorial rulers rather than clan chiefs — a later political development in which personal authority has been replaced by geographical jurisdiction." The list reflects the political maturation of Edom over time.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
36:31-39

The Edomite king-list is the only pre-Israelite king-list in the Bible. Alter notes its structural oddity: no dynastic succession, no lengths of reign, each king associated with a different city. The list looks like a confederate system where leadership circulates among the constituent tribes rather than passing from father to son. This political structure — elective rather than dynastic — makes Edom's monarchy fundamentally different from Israel's eventual Davidic dynasty.

36:43

The genealogy ends with "these were the chiefs of Edom, according to their settlements in the land they occupied. This is the family line of Esau, the father of the Edomites." Alter notes the ending formula is the mirror of the Jacob narrative's beginning: the rejected brother's story is fully told and formally closed before the covenant story resumes. The completeness signals both dignity (Esau's line matters) and conclusion (now we move to Israel).

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
36:24

Calvin on Anah finding hot springs: "This information seems trivial... but Moses, designing to write a complete history, omits nothing which may seem to be of importance." Calvin defends the inclusion of minor details in a non-covenant genealogy as reflecting the comprehensiveness of revealed history. God's providential ordering of history extends to the discovery of geothermal features in Edomite territory.

36:31-39

Calvin reads the Edomite king-list as providential irony: "The Edomites had kings before the Israelites... yet God was not unfaithful to his promise; for in his own time he raised up kings for his chosen people." The temporal precedence of Edomite monarchy is a test of faith for those who hold the covenant promise of kings. God's delays do not indicate covenant failure.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
36:24

The NET note on Anah finding "hot springs" in the desert discusses the Hebrew word yemim, one of the most debated hapax legomena in the OT. The note surveys the options: hot springs (most modern translations), mules (from a proposed Aramaic cognate — traditional Jewish reading), water sources. The note favours "hot springs" as most contextually appropriate for a desert discovery.

36:39

The NET note on the final king Hadar (or Hadad in some MSS) notes the textual uncertainty about his name and observes that unlike the preceding seven kings, no successor is listed — possibly because the list was compiled during his reign. The note connects this to 1 Kings 11:14 where an Edomite named Hadad appears as an adversary of Solomon, suggesting the Edomite royal tradition was continuous for centuries.

Ancient Sources

The Chronicler's inclusion demonstrates that Edomite genealogy was considered historically reliable and theologically significant.
Egyptian Execration Texts
Egyptian texts from c. 2000–1800 BC mention Edomite-area chiefs by name, supporting the antiquity of chiefly governance in the region.
The toledot of Esau may preserve historically authentic records of pre-Israelite Edomite leadership.

Reception History

Calvin reads ch.36 as a demonstration of God's common grace — the rejected line flourishes materially while the chosen line carries the spiritual weight of promise. God is generous even to those outside the covenant.
Origen
The Edomite genealogy typifies the "earthly church" — organized, powerful, but outside the true covenant. The spiritual Israel is the chosen remnant, not the historically larger group.

Genesis 36 is the full account of the rejected brother's descendants — given with the same toledoth formula and the same genealogical thoroughness as any other major lineage in Genesis. The Edomite kings who precede Israel's monarchy are a provocation: the non-covenant people has political structures the covenant people lack. But the genealogy closes and the narrative moves on. Textual Notes

36:31לִפְנֵי מְלָךְ מֶלֶךְ לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל — "before any king reigned over Israel"
MT lipnei melokh melekh livnei Yisrael — "before any king reigned over the Israelites." The clause presupposes a time when Israel does have a king. LXX πρὸ τοῦ βασιλεῦσαι βασιλέα ἐν Ισραηλ — identical.
This parenthetical clause is one of the key texts in source-critical arguments for post-Mosaic authorship of Genesis. Conservative responses: (1) Moses wrote it anticipating the promised kings (17:6; 35:11); (2) it is a later scribal gloss; (3) it is post-Mosaic but still pre-exilic. The clause does not require a date after Saul if Moses wrote prophetically.

Scholarly Debates

The Edomite King List: Pre-dynastic or Contemporaneous Record?
Ancient source incorporated
Sarna, Kitchen
The precision of the Edomite king list — eight successive kings with home cities — suggests incorporation of a genuine administrative record. The list has the character of a royal annals excerpt.
Literary-theological summary
von Rad, Westermann
The list serves a theological purpose (demonstrating covenant fulfilment for Esau) and need not be a direct historical source. The precision may be literary rather than archival.
The archival reading is more persuasive. The list's level of detail (personal names, home cities, wife's names) exceeds what a purely theological summary would require. Kitchen identifies parallels with Middle Bronze Age royal succession lists from the region. The list is likely a genuine ancient administrative source incorporated by the author of Genesis.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 36 demonstrates that God's providential care extends beyond the covenant line. Esau receives a complete genealogy, a territory, a political structure, and kings — all expressions of the Abrahamic blessing on the rejected brother. The chapter is a theological pause before the Joseph cycle: the non-covenant world is fully ordered and accounted for; the covenant world is about to experience its most severe test.
