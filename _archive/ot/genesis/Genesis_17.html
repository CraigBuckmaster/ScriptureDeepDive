MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
17:1-2

MacArthur: God appears as El Shaddai -- God Almighty -- for the first time. The name emphasises divine sufficiency and power, appropriate for the moment when God is about to fulfil what is humanly impossible. 'Walk before me faithfully and be blameless' is the covenant obligation that complements the covenant promise.

17:5

MacArthur: The name change from Abram (exalted father) to Abraham (father of many) seals the covenant promise in the patriarch's identity. Every time Abraham is addressed by his new name, the covenant promise is invoked. The name is the daily reminder that God has bound himself to make the impossible real.

Hebrew Word Study

ʾEl Šadday EL sha-DAI — God Almighty / God of the Mountain: God's self-revelation as ʾEl Šadday (v.1) — "I am God Almighty" — introduces the patriarchal divine name that will characterise the Abrahamic covenant period (Exod 6:3: "I appeared to Abraham, to Isaac and to Jacob as God Almighty, but by my name the LORD I did not make myself fully known"). The etymology is debated (Akkadian šadû, mountain; or Hebrew šad, breast, as "all-sufficient one"), but the consistent context is covenant-making with transformative divine power.

ʾabrāhām av-ra-HAM — father of many nations: The renaming of Abram ("exalted father") as Abraham ("father of many/a multitude") (v.5) is a covenant act that changes the man's identity in light of the promise. In the ANE, names expressed essence — Abraham's new name is a daily proclamation of the covenant promise, spoken every time anyone addresses him. For thirteen years (12:4; 16:16; 17:1) Abram waited; the new name now embeds the waiting-for into his very identity: he is the father of many before the nations exist.

hithallek lĕpānay hit-ha-LEKH le-fa-NAI — walk before me: "Walk before me faithfully and be blameless (tāmîm)" (v.1) — the covenant call that precedes the covenant-expansion of ch.17. To walk before God (lĕpānay, "to my face") is to live under divine scrutiny with integrity — in contrast to walking behind (following at a distance) or away from (rebellion). The call to tāmîm (blamelessness, wholeness) is the same word used of Noah (6:9) and will describe the Passover lamb (Exod 12:5). Covenant life requires a moral wholeness that corresponds to God's own character.

Context

The name change from Abram to Abraham is one of the most significant acts in Genesis. In the ancient world, names declared identity and destiny. Giving someone a new name meant fundamentally redefining who they were. Abraham will hear his new name spoken thousands of times — each utterance is a repetition of the impossible promise. God essentially builds the covenant promise into Abraham's daily identity.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 4:16–18Paul quotes "father of many nations" (Gen 17:5) as the basis for Abraham being the spiritual father of both Jews and Gentiles who share his faith.
  • Exod 6:3God tells Moses he appeared to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob as El Shaddai — but his name YHWH was not yet fully known. Genesis 17 is one of those El Shaddai appearances.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:1

God appears as El Shaddai — a divine name appearing here for the first time. Sarna: "The meaning of Shaddai is debated: 'Almighty' (traditional), 'Mountain One' (from Akkadian shadu), or 'Sufficient/Nourishing One' (from Hebrew shad, breast). All carry the sense of overwhelming power and sufficiency. The command — 'walk before me faithfully and be blameless' — uses the same language as Noah's characterisation (6:9), suggesting the same standard of covenant integrity."

17:5

The renaming of Abram to Abraham is more than a name change — it is a reconstitution of identity. Sarna: "The name Abram means 'exalted father'; Abraham means 'father of many.' The new name is a promise before it is a reality — Abraham is called father of many nations when he has no heir. Wearing the name is itself an act of faith."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:1-2

God appears when Abraham is 99 years old — thirteen years after the Hagar episode (16:16). Alter: "The thirteen-year silence is eloquent. After the Hagar arrangement, there is no recorded divine communication until this moment. God waits. Then he appears, not to rebuke the surrogate plan but to reiterate the promise in its most expansive form yet. The silence and the reappearance together communicate the patience and persistence of divine purpose."

17:5

The change from Abram to Abraham is enacted through a single additional syllable — ha. Alter: "The insertion of the divine breath-letter he into the name mirrors the divine breath that animated Adam (2:7). Abraham's new name contains something of the divine name itself. He is reconstituted by this renaming — his identity now carries the promise within it."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:1

El Shaddai emphasises the sufficiency of God for every need of the covenant. Calvin: "God, in calling himself Almighty (El Shaddai), shows that he has all things at his disposal, and that he can do whatever he wills — that nothing is too difficult for him, not even giving a son to a 99-year-old man and a barren woman." The name prepares for the impossible promise that follows.

17:4-5

The name change is a pledge: "Before he has the thing, he has the name. God gives him the name of father of many nations as if the thing itself were already accomplished." Calvin on the renaming as the grammar of faith: what God promises, he names as if already present.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:1

The NET note on El Shaddai surveys the major etymological proposals and concludes that "God Almighty" (the traditional rendering) remains the best translation despite the uncertain etymology. The note observes that the name El Shaddai occurs primarily in the patriarchal narratives and in Job — contexts of covenant promise and extreme testing.

17:5

The NET note on the name change discusses the popular etymology given in the text (father of many nations) versus the actual linguistic derivation. The note observes that the text provides a folk etymology — a theologically significant sound connection — rather than a strict linguistic derivation. Both Abram and Abraham may derive from the same root; the difference lies in the divine significance invested in the new form.

Verses 9–14 — The Sign of the Covenant: Circumcision
91011121314

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
17:10-11

MacArthur: Circumcision as the covenant sign is to be performed on every male in Abraham's household -- born or bought, son or servant. The universality within the household establishes circumcision as the mark of covenant membership rather than ethnic identity alone. It is received, not achieved.

17:14

MacArthur: The uncircumcised male who has not been circumcised 'will be cut off from his people; he has broken my covenant.' Circumcision is not optional for covenant membership. The sign is the public, physical declaration of covenant identity. Refusal to receive it is refusal of the covenant itself.

Hebrew Word Study

mûl MOOL — to circumcise: The covenant sign of circumcision (mûl, v.11) is imposed "in the flesh" — a permanent, embodied marker of covenant membership that cannot be displayed as a social badge but is carried in the body of every male within the covenant community. The rite was practiced by other ANE peoples but takes on unique theological meaning here: it marks the organ of procreation, enacting covenant hope in the very site of covenant continuation. Paul's reinterpretation in Romans 4 — Abraham was justified before circumcision, which is then a "seal" of existing faith — builds on this narrative structure.

ʾôt bĕrît OT be-REET — sign of the covenant: Circumcision as ʾôt bĕrît (v.11 — "sign of the covenant") joins the rainbow (9:13) as a visible covenant-sign. Both are signs that function to prompt remembrance — the rainbow prompts divine remembering; circumcision prompts covenantal self-identification for every male. The ʾôt does not create the covenant but witnesses and marks it: the distinction between the sign and the thing signified will be crucial for Paul's argument that the covenant antedates and transcends its physical sign.

kārat ka-RAT — to cut off / to cut a covenant: "Any uncircumcised male... will be cut off (yikkārēt) from his people" (v.14) — the covenant penalty uses the same root as "to cut a covenant" (kārat bĕrît). The wordplay is intentional: the one who refuses the covenant sign, refusing to be "cut" (circumcised), will himself be "cut" (excommunicated) from the covenant community. The sign that marks covenant inclusion and the penalty for its rejection share the same verb — an elegant covenant-legal pun.

Historical Context

Circumcision was practiced by various ANE peoples (Egyptians, some Semitic groups) for hygienic and/or initiatory reasons. But the Genesis text radically redefines it: not a rite of passage or hygiene but a covenant sign. The distinction is ideological — Israel's circumcision carries a specific theological meaning (covenant identity) absent from neighboring practice. Herodotus (5th c. BC) notes that Phoenicians and Syrians learned circumcision from Egypt, suggesting the practice spread in the ANE context.

  • Gal 6:15Paul argues neither circumcision nor uncircumcision matters — what counts is new creation. The external sign has been replaced by the internal reality.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:9-14

The covenant of circumcision is the first bodily sign of the covenant in Scripture. Sarna: "Circumcision was practised in the ancient Near East by several peoples (Egyptians, some Semitic groups) but not by others (Philistines, Babylonians). What distinguishes Israelite circumcision is not the practice itself but its specific covenantal meaning — it is performed on the eighth day, as a sign of belonging to the covenant community, performed on every male including servants and foreigners."

17:14

The penalty for the uncircumcised male — "cut off from his people" — is the covenant's negative sanction. Sarna: "The cutting of circumcision and the cutting off from the covenant community are etymologically linked — karet (cutting/excision) is both the surgical act and the divine punishment. The uncircumcised man has not received the covenant's mark; he will be excluded from the covenant's benefits."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:10-12

The specificity of the circumcision command — eighth day, every male, including servants and foreigners — is the most detailed legislation in the Abraham narrative. Alter: "The level of specificity signals the seriousness of the sign. This is not merely a rite but a law, with specific requirements and specific sanctions. The covenant has moved from the grand scope of nations and land to the intimate marking of individual bodies."

17:13-14

The penalty for non-circumcision — being cut off (nikhret) from the people — mirrors the rite itself. Alter notes the legal pun: the one who refuses the covenant sign's cutting will be cut off from the covenant community.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:11

Circumcision as covenant sign is an outward seal of an inward reality. Calvin: "Circumcision was given to Abraham as a seal of the righteousness of faith which he already had (Romans 4:11). The sign did not confer righteousness; it confirmed what had already been granted by faith. This is the proper use of sacraments: they are seals of promises already given, not instruments of a grace not yet received."

17:11-12

Calvin: The command extends to every male forever — including foreign servants. The covenant sign reaches the entire household, not merely the biological family. The household is the unit of covenant membership, not merely the individual.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:12

The NET note on the eighth day of circumcision discusses the significance of this timing: it falls after the first Sabbath of the child's life, has medical advantages (blood coagulation factors peak around day 8), and has been the subject of both symbolic and practical interpretations. The note does not resolve the question but notes that the eighth day became the universal Jewish norm.

17:11-14

The NET note explains that circumcision is not the mechanism of the covenant but its sign — the mark of those who stand within the covenant relationship. It distinguishes members from non-members in the household community.

Verses 15–27 — Sarai Becomes Sarah; Isaac Promised; All Males Circumcised
15161718192021222324252627

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
17:17-19

MacArthur: Abraham fell facedown and laughed -- the same incredulity Sarah will express in ch.18. The laughter is not mocking but the honest expression of a man who has been waiting 24 years. God's response is not rebuke but confirmation: 'your wife Sarah will bear you a son; you will call him Isaac' (laughter). The very laughter becomes the child's name.

17:20-21

MacArthur: God blesses Ishmael generously -- twelve rulers and a great nation (v.20). But the covenant will be established with Isaac (v.21). The distinction is between divine blessing (given broadly) and covenant election (given specifically). God's general providence and his specific covenant purpose are both real but not identical.

Hebrew Word Study

Śārāh sa-RAH — Sarah — princess / noble woman: Sarai's renaming as Sarah (v.15) — from śaray (possibly "my princess") to śārāh (princess/noble woman with a broader referent) — mirrors Abraham's renaming. Both new names are slight modifications of the original with significant theological content: Sarah will be "the mother of nations; kings of peoples will come from her" (v.16). The renaming consecrates the woman's role in the covenant alongside the man's — she is not merely the vessel of Abraham's promise but a covenant participant with her own new name and calling.

ṣāḥaq tsa-KHAK — to laugh: Abraham "fell face down; he laughed (wayyiṣḥaq)" (v.17) — the same root that will name his son Yiṣḥāq (Isaac). The laughter is not contemptuous unbelief (God does not rebuke it) but the overwhelmed response to an impossibility being announced with divine seriousness. A hundred-year-old man and a ninety-year-old woman are to become parents. The laughter is faith being stretched beyond what seems possible — the appropriate response to promises that exceed natural capacity.

bĕyôm haʾehûd be-EH-tsem ha-YOM ha-ZEH — on that very day: Abraham circumcised all the males of his household "on that very day, as God had told him" (v.23, 26). The phrase bĕʿeṣem hayyôm hazzeh ("on this very bone of the day" — intensive form) emphasises the immediacy of obedience: Abraham did not delay, consult, or negotiate. The same phrase will be used of the Exodus departure (Exod 12:41, 51). Covenant obedience, when genuine, is immediate — not because delay indicates doubt but because prompt action demonstrates that the command has been truly heard and received.

Historical Context

None

Context

Abraham's laughter in v.17 is psychologically honest — at 99 and 90 years old, a natural child is biologically impossible. Yet God insists on Isaac, not Ishmael, as the covenant heir (v.19). This distinction is not a rejection of Ishmael (v.20 gives him a sevenfold blessing) but a clarification: the covenant promise will travel a specific line. The biological impossibility is the point — Isaac will be a miracle child, making clear that the covenant's fulfillment depends entirely on God, not human biology.

Cross-Reference

  • Heb 11:11–12Sarah and Abraham's faith enabled the conception of Isaac "from one man, and him as good as dead" — pointing beyond biology to divine power.
  • Gal 4:28Paul calls Christians "children of promise" like Isaac — born not of natural processes but of the Spirit's work.
  • Rom 9:7–9Paul distinguishes physical descent from Abraham (like Ishmael) from covenant descent (like Isaac) — "children of the promise are counted as offspring."

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:15-19

Sarah's name change from Sarai parallels Abraham's — both names mean "princess," but the change marks her inclusion in the covenant promise in her own right, not merely as Abraham's wife. Sarna: "The promise of a son 'from Sarah' (v.16) is the most explicit statement yet that Ishmael is not the covenant heir. For the first time, the promise is specifically tied to Sarah and to a son through her."

17:17-18

Abraham falls on his face and laughs — the Hebrew tsachaq, which will give Isaac his name. Sarna notes the laughter as genuinely ambiguous: it could be the laughter of joy at an impossible promise, or disbelief, or both. "Abraham's laughter is not rebuked — unlike Sarah's in chapter 18. His subsequent prayer for Ishmael (v.18) shows his genuine concern for his existing son even as he receives the promise of Isaac."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:17

Abraham falls on his face and laughs. Alter: "The posture and the laughter are simultaneous — reverent prostration and irrepressible laughter at the impossibility of the promise. The comedy of the aged couple producing a child is not suppressed by the narrator; it is built into the story. The promised son will be named for this laughter."

17:19-21

God distinguishes clearly: Isaac will be the covenant heir; Ishmael will be blessed but is not the covenant line. Alter: "The distinction is stated three times in rapid succession. Isaac — the impossible son — will carry the covenant; Ishmael — the possible son — will be blessed but not the bearer of the promise. The covenant passes through the impossible, not through the humanly arranged."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:17-18

Calvin defends Abraham's laughter as faith, not unbelief: "Abraham did not laugh from distrust, as if he had doubted God's word, but from wonder and exultation at the greatness of the promise. Sarah laughed from unbelief (18:12-15); Abraham's laughter was different in kind, though similar in expression." The distinction between faithful wonder and unbelieving doubt, both expressed as laughter, shows the interpretive care required.

17:20

God's promise for Ishmael — twelve rulers, a great nation — shows that divine blessing extends beyond the covenant line. Calvin: "God did not wholly reject Ishmael. He is willing that the blessings of common grace should flow even to those who are not the elect." The covenant particularity of Isaac does not negate the common grace extended to Ishmael.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:17

The NET note on Abraham's laughter discusses the Hebrew qal stem of tsachaq — to laugh — versus the Hithpael (intensive) form used in chapter 18 for Sarah's laughter. The note notes that both forms can indicate either joy or incredulity. The note observes that God does not rebuke Abraham's laughter but does rebuke Sarah's (18:13-15), suggesting a qualitative difference in their responses that the surface text leaves ambiguous.

17:19-21

The NET note on Isaac's name traces the tsachaq (laugh) root through Sarah's incredulous laughter (18:12), the promised name (17:19), Sarah's joyful laughter at the birth (21:6), and Ishmael's 'mocking' (21:9). The name carries the whole emotional arc from doubt to delight.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Abraham (formerly Abram)
Covenant patriarch; newly named
Receives his permanent name and the covenant sign. His laughter at the promise (v.17) is an honest moment of incredulity that God meets with reaffirmation. He obeys immediately (vv.23–27), circumcising all males in his household the same day.
Sarah (formerly Sarai)
Covenant matriarch; newly named
The second name change of the chapter. For the first time, God explicitly names Sarah — not just Abram's wife — as the covenant mother. "Kings of peoples shall come from her" (v.16) — she is a covenant figure in her own right.
Ishmael
Son of Abraham; circumcised; blessed
Though not the covenant heir, Ishmael is circumcised (v.25) and given a sevenfold blessing (v.20) — "I will make him into a great nation." He is not rejected; he is positioned. The nations descended from Ishmael are a fulfilment of this promise.

Ancient Sources

Herodotus, Histories II.104
Herodotus states that Phoenicians and Syrians learned circumcision from the Egyptians — "The Egyptians and Ethiopians have practised circumcision since time immemorial." This confirms the practice existed in the ANE environment from which Abraham came, while Genesis redefines its meaning as covenant sign.
4QMMT (Dead Sea Scrolls)
The Qumran community interpreted circumcision strictly within a covenant-obedience framework, using its presence or absence as a marker of covenant faithfulness. The Genesis 17 institution was foundational for their community self-understanding.

Reception History

Paul, Romans 4:9–12
Paul's argument that Abraham was justified before circumcision (Gen 15:6 precedes Gen 17) makes circumcision a "seal of the righteousness that he had by faith while he was still uncircumcised." The sign confirms but does not create righteousness.
The Jerusalem Council, Acts 15
The earliest church debate concerned whether Gentile converts must be circumcised (i.e., become Jews) to be saved. The council's decision (no) rests on the Pauline reading of Genesis — faith, not circumcision, is the covenant entrance.

Literary Structure

Abram to Abraham: Identity Sealed in the Name17:5The addition of a single letter (h) to Abram's name seals the covenant: father of many nations is embedded in the new name. Identity, calling, and promise are consolidated into the name Abraham bears forever.
Circumcision: Covenant Sign in the Body17:10-11The covenant sign is placed in the organ of generation -- not in the hands (what we do) or the mouth (what we say) but in the body's future-producing capacity. Every son born into the covenant bears it.

Scholarly Debates

Circumcision: Sign, Seal, or Sacrament?
Covenant sign
Wenham, Sarna, Waltke
Circumcision is the covenant sign — the external mark in the flesh that identifies covenant membership. It does not confer salvation but marks those within the covenant community.
Covenant seal (analogous to baptism)
Calvin, Westminster Confession, Rom 4:11
Paul calls circumcision "a seal of the righteousness that Abraham had by faith" (Rom 4:11). Calvin and the Reformed tradition read it as analogous to NT baptism — sign and seal of the covenant of grace.
Physical-only requirement
Dispensational readings
Circumcision belongs to the Mosaic/Abrahamic covenant and has no direct continuity with the new covenant. Baptism is not the equivalent; circumcision was a national marker for ethnic Israel.
The debate is primarily between paedobaptist (Reformed) and credobaptist (Baptist/Dispensational) readings. Paul's treatment in Romans 4 and Colossians 2:11-12 is the key NT text. Most evangelical scholars agree circumcision was a covenant sign; the debate is about its relationship to NT sacramental practice.
