Lamentations 5

Remember, LORD, What Has Happened to Us

Verses 1–18 — “Our Inheritance Has Been Turned Over to Strangers”
123456789101112131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

זְכֹר zəkōr — remember: "Remember (zəkōr), LORD, what has happened to us; look, and see our disgrace" (v.1). The final poem: 22 verses (matching the alphabet count) but NOT an acrostic. The form breaks. When grief is complete, even poetic order fails. Our inheritance turned over to strangers (v.2). Orphans, widows (v.3). We pay for our own water (v.4). Slaves rule over us (v.5). Our ancestors sinned and are no more; we bear their punishment (v.7). Women have been violated (v.11). Princes hung by their hands (v.12). Joy is gone; dancing turned to mourning (v.15). "The crown has fallen from our head. Woe to us, for we have sinned!" (v.16). Mount Zion lies desolate; foxes prowl over it (v.18).

Context

The broken acrostic is the most debated structural feature in Lamentations. Chapters 1–4 follow the Hebrew alphabet; chapter 5 has 22 verses (the right count) but the letters are NOT in order. Scholars debate whether this is deliberate (form fracturing under grief) or simply a different compositional choice. The pastoral reading is compelling: when suffering exceeds language’s capacity to order it, even the alphabet fails. The poem is communal throughout: "our" appears in nearly every verse. This is not one person’s grief but a nation’s.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 79:1–4The nations have invaded your inheritance; your holy temple is defiled — the same temple-loss grief.
  • Ex 20:5Punishing the children for the sin of the parents to the third and fourth generation — the intergenerational theology behind Lam 5:7.
  • Ezek 18:2–4The fathers ate sour grapes; the children’s teeth are set on edge? No. Each person bears their own sin — Ezekiel’s corrective to the logic of Lam 5:7.
  • Mark 13:14When you see the abomination of desolation standing where it should not be — desolation language that echoes Lam 5:18.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1

"Remember, LORD." The opening imperative: REMEMBER. Not "restore" yet, not "forgive" yet, just remember. The first request is for God’s attention.

5:2–5

Inheritance stolen, orphaned, paying for water, slaves ruling. The complete inversion of the covenant promises: land, family, provision, freedom — all reversed.

5:7

"Our ancestors sinned and are no more, and we bear their punishment." The intergenerational grief. The poem does not resolve the fairness question; it simply states it.

5:15–18

Joy gone. Dancing to mourning. Crown fallen. Zion desolate. Foxes on the holy mountain. The catalogue of loss is total.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1

Calvin reads "remember" as the prayer’s foundational theology: if God remembers, he will act. The fear is not that God is unable to help but that he might forget — or choose not to look.

5:7

Calvin acknowledges the tension: the ancestors sinned, but this generation bears the consequences. Calvin resolves it partially by noting that each generation ALSO sinned (v.16), but the full resolution awaits Ezekiel 18.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:1

The NET notes the imperative zəkōr (remember) is the same verb used for God’s covenant faithfulness (Gen 8:1; 9:15; Ex 2:24). The prayer is that God would remember his covenant, not just their suffering.

5:18

The NET observes that foxes prowling on Zion is the ultimate desolation image: the holy mountain has become wilderness. Foxes are wild, unclean, and live in ruins.

Berlin Notes

berlin
5:1

Berlin reads "remember" as performative speech: saying "remember" to God is itself an act of faith, because it assumes God CAN remember, SHOULD remember, and WILL remember. The verb is a prayer disguised as an imperative.

5:16–18

Berlin notes the "crown fallen / Zion desolate / foxes prowl" sequence moves from political loss (crown) to sacred loss (Zion) to complete abandonment (foxes). The hierarchy of devastation is deliberate.

Oconnor Notes

oconnor
5:1–5

O’Connor reads the communal voice as a survival achievement: the ‘we’ who speak are still a community. The fact that they can lament together means they have not been completely annihilated.

5:11–13

O’Connor draws attention to the sexual violence (v.11) and forced labour (vv.12–13) as the signatures of military occupation. The poem does not aestheticise these; it reports them as facts that God must see.

Verses 19–22 — “Restore Us to Yourself, O LORD”
19202122

Hebrew Word Study

חַדֵּשׁ ḥaddēsh — renew / restore: "Restore us (ḥaddēsh) to yourself, LORD, that we may return; renew our days as of old" (v.21). The book’s final four verses. Verse 19: "You, LORD, reign forever; your throne endures from generation to generation." The eternal sovereignty of God affirmed even amid ruins. Verse 20: "Why do you always forget us? Why do you forsake us so long?" The accusation that follows the affirmation. Verse 21: the petition for restoration. Verse 22: "Unless you have utterly rejected us and are angry with us beyond measure." The book ends on a question — or a conditional — that is NOT answered. Jewish liturgical tradition repeats v.21 after v.22, refusing to end on the dark note.

Context

The ending is the most debated feature of Lamentations. Verse 22 is either a conditional ("unless you have utterly rejected us") or a concessive ("even though you have utterly rejected us"). Either way, the book ends without resolution. There is no divine response. The silence after v.22 is the book’s final theological statement: sometimes God does not answer. The Jewish liturgical practice of repeating v.21 after reading v.22 is an interpretive act: the community refuses to let the last word be rejection. The Christian tradition hears the answer in the gospel: God has NOT utterly rejected.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 44:23–26Awake, LORD! Why do you sleep? Why do you hide your face? Rise up and help us — the same unanswered cry.
  • Ps 77:7–9Will the Lord reject forever? Has God forgotten to be merciful? — the psalm form of Lam 5:20–22’s question.
  • Rom 11:1–2Did God reject his people? By no means! — Paul’s answer to Lam 5:22’s question.
  • Isa 49:14–16"The LORD has forsaken me." "Can a mother forget? I will not forget you" — the prophetic answer to Lam 5’s fear.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:19

"You, LORD, reign forever." The eternal throne affirmed. Even when the earthly throne has fallen, the heavenly one endures.

5:20

"Why do you always forget us?" The accusation after the affirmation. Both are held simultaneously: God reigns AND God seems to forget.

5:21

"Restore us to yourself, LORD, that we may return." The petition: restoration begins with God’s initiative. We cannot return until he restores.

5:22

"Unless you have utterly rejected us." The book ends unanswered. No divine speech. No resolution. The silence is the final statement. Jewish tradition repeats v.21. Christian tradition hears the answer in Christ.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:19–20

Calvin holds the tension: v.19 (God reigns forever) and v.20 (why do you forget?) are not contradictory but complementary. The sovereign God who COULD help and SEEMS NOT TO is the hardest theological puzzle in the OT.

5:21–22

Calvin reads the ending as deliberate incompleteness: the book refuses to provide premature closure. Real grief does not resolve on schedule. The unanswered ending respects the listener’s ongoing pain.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:21

The NET notes that "restore us to yourself" (hashivenu) uses the same root as "repent/return" (shuv). The prayer is circular: God must turn us so that we can turn to God. Initiative belongs to divine grace.

5:22

The NET provides both grammatical options: (1) ki im = "unless" (conditional: unless you have rejected us, restore us); (2) ki im = "for indeed" (concessive: for you have indeed rejected us). The ambiguity may be intentional: the poet does not know which is true.

Berlin Notes

berlin
5:19–22

Berlin reads the four-verse ending as a theological structure: sovereignty (v.19), accusation (v.20), petition (v.21), doubt (v.22). The sequence is faith in motion: from affirmation through protest to prayer to the edge of despair. The book ends at the edge, not beyond it.

5:22

Berlin notes that the unanswered ending is the book’s most theologically sophisticated move: silence is the only honest response when the answer has not yet come. To fabricate an answer would be to commit the same sin as the false prophets of 2:14.

Oconnor Notes

oconnor
5:19–22

O’Connor reads the ending as a refusal of false comfort: the book will not pretend that grief resolves easily. By ending in ambiguity, it gives permission to every reader who is still waiting for an answer.

5:22

O’Connor calls v.22 the bravest verse in the Bible: it risks the possibility that God HAS utterly rejected. The poet does not know. The reader does not know. The not-knowing is the space where faith lives or dies.

Ancient Sources

Sumerian City Laments (c.2000 BC)
The Lamentation over the Destruction of Ur and similar Sumerian texts follow a comparable pattern: the deity abandons the city, enemies destroy it, the population mourns, and a tentative plea for restoration concludes.
Lamentations participates in an ancient Near Eastern genre of city-lament that predates it by over a millennium. The genre validates public grief as a literary and theological act.
The Babylonian Chronicles
Cuneiform records document Nebuchadnezzar’s siege and capture of Jerusalem in 597 and 586 BC, confirming the historical catastrophe behind Lamentations.
The archaeological evidence corroborates the destruction described in the poems, including evidence of massive burning in the City of David excavations.
