Lamentations 4

Gold Has Lost Its Lustre

Verses 1–11 — “The Precious Children of Zion, Worth Their Weight in Gold”
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

כֶּתֶם ketem — gold / pure gold: "How the gold (ketem) has lost its lustre, the fine gold become dull! The sacred gems are scattered at every street corner" (v.1). The precious children of Zion, once worth their weight in gold, are now treated as clay pots (v.2). Jackals nurse their young, but my people have become heartless (v.3). Tongues stick to the roof of mouths (v.4). Those who once ate delicacies are destitute (v.5). The punishment of my people is greater than that of Sodom, overthrown in a moment without a hand to help (v.6).

Context

Chapter 4 returns to a single acrostic (22 verses, 22 letters). The imagery is of reversal: gold becomes dull, gems scatter, children treated as clay, delicacy-eaters starve in streets. The Sodom comparison (v.6) is startling: Jerusalem’s suffering is WORSE than Sodom’s because Sodom was destroyed instantly while Jerusalem endures prolonged agony. The siege horrors (vv.4–5, 9–10) are not exaggerated — 2 Kings 6:28–29 records cannibalism during an earlier siege, and Josephus records the same during AD 70.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 19:24–25The LORD rained burning sulfur on Sodom — the destruction Lam 4:6 says was LESS severe than Jerusalem’s.
  • Deut 28:53–57You will eat the fruit of your womb during the siege — the covenant curse Lam 4:10 reports as fulfilled.
  • 2 Kgs 6:28–29A woman boiled her son during the Aramean siege — the historical precedent for the cannibalism described here.
  • Matt 24:19–21How dreadful it will be for pregnant women in those days — Jesus’ warning echoes the siege horrors of Lam 4.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1–2

"Gold has lost its lustre. The precious children treated as clay pots." The reversal: from gold to clay, from treasured to discarded.

4:3–5

"Even jackals offer their breasts to nurse their young, but my people have become heartless." The animal comparison shames: beasts show more tenderness than siege-starved humans.

4:6

"Greater than the punishment of Sodom, which was overthrown in a moment." Sodom died quickly. Jerusalem dies slowly. Slow death is worse.

4:9–10

"Those killed by the sword are better off than those who die of famine. Compassionate women have cooked their own children." The most horrifying verse in the OT.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:6

Calvin reads the Sodom comparison as measuring not sin but suffering: Sodom’s sin may have been greater, but Sodom’s punishment was instantaneous. Jerusalem’s drawn-out agony exceeds even divine fire from heaven.

4:9–10

Calvin does not flinch from the cannibalism but reads it as the fulfilment of the Deuteronomic curse (Deut 28:53). The covenant warned of exactly this. The horror validates the warning.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:1

The NET notes the gold/gems imagery may refer literally to the temple’s gold fittings scattered in the rubble, or metaphorically to the children of Zion. The ambiguity is likely deliberate: both the temple and the people have been devalued.

4:6

The NET observes that "without a hand to help" (literally "no hands turned in her") intensifies the comparison: Sodom had no opportunity to resist; Jerusalem had years of warning and chose not to respond.

Berlin Notes

berlin
4:1–2

Berlin reads the gold-to-clay transformation as a reversal of creation: God formed Adam from clay and made him precious; now Zion’s precious ones revert to clay. The siege reverses human dignity.

4:6

Berlin notes the Sodom comparison is the poet’s most provocative analogy: Sodom is the OT’s paradigm of wickedness. To say Jerusalem suffered MORE than Sodom is to push theological language to its limit.

Oconnor Notes

oconnor
4:3–5

O’Connor reads the nursing-jackals comparison as the collapse of maternal instinct under siege: when starvation overrides the biological drive to nurture, the human community has fallen below the animal kingdom.

4:9–10

O’Connor notes the poet does not condemn the mothers who cannibalised their children but calls them ‘compassionate.’ The adjective is devastating: they WERE compassionate, and the siege broke even that.

Verses 12–22 — “The Punishment of My People Will End”
1213141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

תַּם ṭam — complete / finished: "Your punishment will end (ṭam), Daughter Zion; he will not prolong your exile" (v.22). The kings of the earth did not believe Jerusalem could fall (v.12). It fell because of the sins of her prophets and priests, who shed innocent blood (v.13). They wander blind in the streets (v.14). "The LORD himself has scattered them; he no longer watches over them" (v.16). The hoped-for help never came (v.17). The anointed of the LORD was captured in their traps (v.20) — King Zedekiah, caught fleeing. But: "Your punishment will end. He will not prolong your exile" (v.22).

Context

Verse 20 (“the LORD’s anointed, caught in their traps”) refers to King Zedekiah’s capture as he fled Jerusalem (2 Kgs 25:4–7). The “anointed” (mashiach) language is loaded: the Davidic king, God’s chosen, is now a prisoner. The final verse (v.22) is the chapter’s only note of hope: the punishment is FINITE. It will end. The exile will not last forever. Edom’s turn is coming (v.21–22). This faint forward-looking note prevents the chapter from being pure despair.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 25:4–7Zedekiah fled but was caught near Jericho — the event behind Lam 4:19–20.
  • Isa 40:2Speak tenderly to Jerusalem; her hard service has been completed, her sin paid for — the fulfilment of Lam 4:22’s promise.
  • Obad 15–18The day of the LORD is near for all nations, including Edom — the Edom-judgment Lam 4:21–22 announces.
  • Jer 25:11–12The exile will last seventy years, then Babylon will be punished — the timetable behind "your punishment will end."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:12–13

"The kings of the earth did not believe it." Jerusalem’s fall shocked the world. It was supposed to be impregnable. The prophets and priests caused it with their bloodshed.

4:17

"We wore out our eyes watching for help that never came." The ally that never arrived. The waited-for rescue that was a mirage.

4:20

"The LORD’s anointed, caught in their traps." Zedekiah captured. The Davidic king in chains.

4:22

"Your punishment will end. He will not prolong your exile." The first explicit promise of ending. Finite. It WILL end.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:20

Calvin reads the anointed king’s capture as the nadir: if God’s own chosen ruler can be trapped, no human institution is secure. But Calvin also sees the verse pointing forward: the true Anointed will not be held by any trap.

4:22

Calvin calls this the chapter’s only sunrise: "your punishment will end." Hope is not that suffering was meaningless but that it has a terminus. The promise of ending is the promise of divine faithfulness.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:20

The NET notes that "the LORD’s anointed" (mashiach YHWH) is the most exalted royal title in the OT. Its use for the captured, blinded Zedekiah is tragic irony: the anointed one is now a prisoner.

4:22

The NET observes the verb tam (completed, finished) has a double edge: the punishment is both "completed" (paid in full) and "ended" (no more). Justice is satisfied AND mercy follows.

Berlin Notes

berlin
4:20

Berlin reads the anointed’s capture as the death of political hope: the king who was "the breath of our nostrils" (the source of life itself) is now powerless. The metaphor is respiratory: without the king, the nation suffocates.

4:22

Berlin notes the Edom-address shifts the poem’s perspective: Zion’s punishment will end, but Edom’s is about to begin. The reversal is poetic justice.

Oconnor Notes

oconnor
4:17–20

O’Connor reads the sequence — watching for help, hunted in the streets, the king captured — as the collapse of every escape strategy: allies failed, flight failed, leadership failed. Total institutional failure.

4:22

O’Connor notes the promise is addressed to ‘Daughter Zion,’ not to any individual. The hope is communal: the PEOPLE will survive even if individuals have not. The collective body outlasts the catastrophe.

Ancient Sources

Sumerian City Laments (c.2000 BC)
The Lamentation over the Destruction of Ur and similar Sumerian texts follow a comparable pattern: the deity abandons the city, enemies destroy it, the population mourns, and a tentative plea for restoration concludes.
Lamentations participates in an ancient Near Eastern genre of city-lament that predates it by over a millennium. The genre validates public grief as a literary and theological act.
The Babylonian Chronicles
Cuneiform records document Nebuchadnezzar’s siege and capture of Jerusalem in 597 and 586 BC, confirming the historical catastrophe behind Lamentations.
The archaeological evidence corroborates the destruction described in the poems, including evidence of massive burning in the City of David excavations.
