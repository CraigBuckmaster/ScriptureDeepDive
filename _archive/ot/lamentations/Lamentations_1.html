Lamentations 1

How Deserted Lies the City!

Verses 1–11 — “She Weeps Bitterly in the Night”
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

אֵיכָה ʾēkāh — how! / alas!: "How (ʾēkāh) deserted lies the city, once so full of people!" (v.1). The opening cry of funeral lament. Jerusalem personified as a widow, weeping at night, tears on her cheeks, no one to comfort her (vv.1–2). Among all her lovers, none comforts her (v.2). Judah has gone into exile; the roads to Zion mourn (v.4). Her enemies have become her masters, because the LORD has brought grief on her for her many sins (v.5). All her splendour has departed (v.6). In the days of her affliction, Jerusalem remembers all the treasures she had in days of old (v.7).

Context

The acrostic structure (each verse begins with the next letter of the Hebrew alphabet, 22 letters = 22 verses) imposes poetic order on emotional chaos. The city-as-woman metaphor draws on ancient Near Eastern city-lament tradition (cf. Sumerian Lamentation over the Destruction of Ur, c.2000 BC). The repeated "no one to comfort her" (vv.2, 9, 16, 17, 21) is the poem’s refrain — five occurrences of menahem, the very word that means "comforter." Jerusalem’s condition is the ABSENCE of comfort. The opening word ʾēkāh gives the book its Hebrew title.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 54:4–6God as husband restoring the forsaken wife — the reversal of Lam 1’s widow imagery.
  • Rev 18:7–8Babylon says "I am not a widow" but falls — contrasting Jerusalem’s honest grief with Babylon’s denial.
  • Ps 137:1By the rivers of Babylon we sat and wept — the communal version of Lam 1’s individual grief.
  • Matt 23:37–38Jesus weeps over Jerusalem: "How often I longed to gather your children" — echoing the ʾēkāh tradition.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1–2

"How deserted lies the city!" The opening word ʾēkāh (How!) is a funeral cry. Jerusalem personified as a widow weeping through the night. No one comforts her.

1:5

"Her enemies have become her masters, because the LORD has brought grief for her many sins." Sin as the cause. The poet does not hide from this. But confession does not reduce the grief.

1:8–9

"Jerusalem has sinned greatly. All who honoured her despise her, for they have all seen her naked." The shame is public and total.

1:11

"Look, LORD, and consider, for I am despised." The first direct address to God. She finally speaks to him, not about him.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1–2

Calvin reads the widow metaphor as the loss of the covenant-marriage: Jerusalem had been the bride of God (Isa 54:5; Hos 2:19–20), and now she weeps alone. The "lovers" who fail to comfort are the foreign alliances Judah trusted instead of God.

1:5

Calvin notes the poet’s theological courage: God CAUSED this. The LORD brought the grief. Calvin sees this as the honest theology that makes Lamentations trustworthy — it does not blame random fate but names God as sovereign even in destruction.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:1

The NET notes that ʾēkāh is the exclamation that opens funeral dirges (cf. 2 Sam 1:19; Isa 1:21). The word signals that the entire poem is a death-song for the city.

1:9

The NET observes the structural shift at v.9c: the third-person narrator gives way to first-person speech from the city herself. Jerusalem becomes her own mourner: "Look, LORD, on my affliction."

Berlin Notes

berlin
1:1–4

Berlin emphasises the acrostic as a coping mechanism: the alphabet constrains the grief into a structured progression. When emotion threatens to overwhelm, the poet can always ask: what is the next letter? The form is therapeutic.

1:8–9

Berlin reads the nakedness imagery as the exposure of what should be hidden — not sexual shame per se but the vulnerability of a destroyed city whose walls (her "clothing") are gone.

Oconnor Notes

oconnor
1:1–2

O’Connor reads the weeping widow as a figure of traumatic grief: the tears come at night, unbidden, when defences are down. The specificity — tears on her cheeks, no comforter on either side — marks this as trauma literature, not abstract theology.

1:5–7

O’Connor notes the juxtaposition of sin and suffering without easy resolution. The poet acknowledges sin (v.5) but does not let that acknowledgment reduce the suffering to a simple equation. Grief persists alongside confession.

Verses 12–22 — “Is Any Suffering Like My Suffering?”
1213141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

מַכְאֹב makʾov — pain / sorrow: "Is it nothing to you, all who pass by? Look around and see. Is any suffering (makʾov) like my suffering that was inflicted on me, that the LORD brought on me in the day of his fierce anger?" (v.12). Jerusalem speaks directly now, addressing the passersby. The suffering claims to be unique, incomparable. From heaven God sent fire into her bones (v.13). Her sins are bound into a yoke (v.14). God has rejected all the warriors (v.15). "For these things I weep; my eyes overflow with tears" (v.16). She acknowledges God’s righteousness (v.18) but ends crying out: "See, LORD, how distressed I am!" (v.20).

Context

Verse 12 is the theological centre of chapter 1 and has been appropriated in Christian tradition for Good Friday liturgy (the Reproaches). The claim that this suffering is unique anticipates Lam 2:13 and ultimately points toward a suffering that IS unique — the cross. The shift from third person (vv.1–11) to first person (vv.12–22) means the city stops being described and starts speaking. She becomes her own advocate before God and the world. Her final words in the chapter are a prayer for God to act against her enemies (vv.21–22).

Cross-Reference

  • Mark 15:29–30Those who passed by hurled insults at Jesus on the cross — the Good Friday tradition reads Lam 1:12 christologically.
  • Isa 53:3–4A man of suffering, familiar with pain — the Suffering Servant as the one whose suffering truly IS unique.
  • Ps 69:20Scorn has broken my heart; I looked for sympathy but found none, for comforters but found none — the same "no comforter" motif.
  • Heb 12:29Our God is a consuming fire — the image Lam 1:13 uses: "from on high he sent fire into my bones."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:12

"Is any suffering like my suffering?" The claim of incomparability. Every genuine grief feels unique. The liturgical tradition applies this to Christ on Good Friday.

1:13–15

"From on high he sent fire into my bones. He spread a net for my feet. He has given me into the hands of those I cannot withstand." Three images of divine agency: fire, net, surrender. God is not absent but active.

1:18

"The LORD is righteous, yet I rebelled against his commands." The confession of God’s justice in the middle of the lament. She does not deny her sin. But she does not stop weeping either.

1:20–22

"See, LORD, how distressed I am! May you deal with my enemies as you have dealt with me." The lament turns petitionary. If God’s judgment is just, then apply it to the enemy too.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:12

Calvin notes the universal applicability: every suffering community reads itself into "is any suffering like mine?" The question is rhetorical but not self-pitying — it is an appeal for recognition.

1:18

Calvin praises the juxtaposition: "The LORD is righteous" followed immediately by "Listen, all you peoples; look on my suffering." She holds together God’s justice and her own pain without collapsing either into the other.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:12

The NET notes the textual difficulty: some read "is it nothing to you?" as a question to passersby; others render it "may it not happen to you." The ambiguity intensifies the verse — both readings work.

1:18

The NET observes this is the poem’s clearest theological statement: YHWH is tsaddiq (righteous). The confession is not forced but voluntary — she freely acknowledges the justice she has received.

Berlin Notes

berlin
1:12

Berlin reads the appeal to passersby as a legal strategy: the city calls witnesses. In the ancient world, suffering unwitnessed was suffering invalidated. She needs someone to SEE.

1:18–22

Berlin notes the poem ends with petition, not resignation. The city does not collapse into silence but demands that God act. The lament is forward-looking, not merely backward-mourning.

Oconnor Notes

oconnor
1:12–16

O’Connor reads the fire-in-bones imagery as a description of trauma that is experienced physically, not just emotionally. The body registers what the mind cannot fully process. Lamentations is embodied grief.

1:20–22

O’Connor notes the shift from confession to accusation: she acknowledged her sin (v.18) but now asks God to look at her enemies too. The moral universe is not simple: she sinned AND she was wronged.

Ancient Sources

Sumerian City Laments (c.2000 BC)
The Lamentation over the Destruction of Ur and similar Sumerian texts follow a comparable pattern: the deity abandons the city, enemies destroy it, the population mourns, and a tentative plea for restoration concludes.
Lamentations participates in an ancient Near Eastern genre of city-lament that predates it by over a millennium. The genre validates public grief as a literary and theological act.
The Babylonian Chronicles
Cuneiform records document Nebuchadnezzar’s siege and capture of Jerusalem in 597 and 586 BC, confirming the historical catastrophe behind Lamentations.
The archaeological evidence corroborates the destruction described in the poems, including evidence of massive burning in the City of David excavations.
