Karen H. Jobes

Esther Scholar & Applied Theologian

Biography

Karen Jobes is Gerald F. Hawthorne Professor Emerita of New Testament Greek and Exegesis at Wheaton College. Though primarily a New Testament scholar, her NIVAC commentary on Esther (1999) is widely regarded as one of the finest evangelical treatments of the book. She brings both rigorous historical method and pastoral sensitivity to a text that raises difficult theological questions.

Key Works

Her NIVAC Esther commentary excels at three levels: original meaning (Persian-period context, literary structure, the absence of God's name), bridging contexts (how the book functioned in Jewish and Christian communities), and contemporary significance (providence, identity, courage under threat). She also authored major works on 1 Peter and the Septuagint.

Approach

Jobes reads Esther as a theology of providence without theophany. God is never named but never absent. She traces the "coincidences" that drive the plot — the king's insomnia, the timing of the banquets, Mordecai's unrewarded service — as evidence of invisible divine orchestration. Her approach is literary-theological: the story's artistry is itself the argument for God's presence.
