Acts 4

The Apostles Before the Sanhedrin

Verses 1—22 — Peter and John Before the Sanhedrin: We Cannot Stop Speaking
12345678910111213141516171819202122

Greek Word Study

παρρησία parrēsia — boldness: literally "all-speech" — freedom to say everything without restraint or fear. This word becomes the signature quality of apostolic witness throughout Acts (4:13, 29, 31; 9:27–28; 13:46; 28:31). It is the Spirit's mark on the church's proclamation.

ἀγράμματοι καὶ ἰδιῶται agrammatoi kai idiōtai — unschooled, ordinary men: literally "unlettered and lay-persons" — without formal rabbinic training. The Sanhedrin's category for men who had no Torah certification. Their authority, exercised without credentials, is explicable only by the phrase that follows: "they had been with Jesus."

The supreme Jewish council of 71 members — high priests, elders, and scribes — meeting in the Hall of Hewn Stone on the Temple Mount. Peter and John's examination marks the church's formal confrontation with Israel's highest authority.
Jerusalem — Prison

The apostles are held overnight — the first imprisonment of the church. The overnight detention produces the growth report of v4 (five thousand men) before the examination even begins, demonstrating that imprisonment cannot stop the word.

Context

The examination before the Sanhedrin is the church's first official hearing before Israel's supreme religious court. The Sadducees' disturbance (v2) is ideological as well as political: they denied bodily resurrection, so the apostolic preaching struck at their core theology. The cornerstone quotation (v11 citing Psalm 118:22) is addressed directly to the 'builders' — the Sanhedrin, Israel's religious leadership. The irony is perfect: the experts in building God's people have rejected the very stone God chose.

Cross-Reference

  • Psalm 118:22"The stone the builders rejected has become the cornerstone" — cited in v11. Jesus himself used this text (Matt 21:42; Mark 12:10; Luke 20:17); Peter now applies it to the Sanhedrin. Also cited in 1 Pet 2:7.
  • Isaiah 28:16The cornerstone image combined with Psalm 118 — the stone that the builders reject becomes the most critical structural element. Rejection produces vindication in both Isaiah and the Psalm.
  • Acts 4:12"There is no other name under heaven given to mankind by which we must be saved" — the unique-salvation claim: the doctrinal climax of the healing narrative and the most exclusive Christological statement in Acts.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:8–12

MacArthur traces the bold progression: filled with the Spirit → addressed the rulers → cited Scripture → declared salvation's exclusivity. The Spirit's filling produces specific, articulate, scripturally-grounded proclamation — not vague spiritual feelings.

4:12

"There is no other name under heaven given to mankind by which we must be saved" — the word "must" (dei — necessity): salvation's exclusivity is not arbitrary narrowness but logical necessity given the nature of sin and the nature of the atonement. Only one person has borne the penalty; only one person's name can apply the pardon.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:8

The Spirit's repeated fillings in Acts are demonstrations of the Spirit's inexhaustible supply. The same Spirit who came at Pentecost is given freshly for each new test. This encouragement applies to every generation: the same Spirit who equipped apostles before councils equips believers in analogous situations today.

4:12

Salvation through Christ alone is not triumphalism but the logic of grace. If salvation required any human contribution, there would be many ways to achieve it. Because it is entirely the work of Christ, there is necessarily one way: through him alone.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:4

The NET notes "number of men" (arithmos tōn andrōn) — Luke specifies males, suggesting the 5,000 counts adult men only, meaning the total believing community may have been considerably larger. Luke's precision reflects Roman census-style counting by adult males.

4:11

The NET provides extensive notes on "cornerstone" (kephalē gōnias) — which can mean either the cornerstone at the base (setting the angle of the building) or the capstone at the apex. The theological imagery works either way: Jesus is the foundational reference point or the culminating glory of the building.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
4:8

"Filled with the Holy Spirit" precedes Peter's speech — Robertson notes this is a fresh filling (plēstheis — aorist participle) distinct from Pentecost. The Spirit is not given once but freshly for each new act of witness. This fits the pattern throughout Acts: Spirit-filling accompanies bold proclamation.

4:11

Robertson on the rejected stone: Jesus had used this very text about himself (Matt 21:42), so Peter consciously repeats the Lord's self-interpretation. The confrontation is deliberate: Peter applies the Sanhedrin's own proof-text against them.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:5–6

Keener provides historical background on the Annas dynasty. Annas served as high priest AD 6–15 and retained enormous influence even after Roman deposition, placing five sons and one son-in-law in the office. His presence at the examination reflects actual power despite no current official role (confirmed in Josephus, Antiquities 20.9.1).

4:13

Agrammatoi ("unschooled") means without formal rabbinic education — not illiterate. The Sanhedrin's amazement is at theological authority exercised without proper credentials. They recognised what they had no category for: Spirit-given authority that required no human certification.

Verses 23—37 — The Believers Pray; Barnabas and the Sharing of Property

Greek Word Study

Δέσποτα Despota — Sovereign Lord: the address "Master of slaves" — used only rarely in Jewish prayer but appearing here, Luke 2:29, and Rev 6:10. It emphasises absolute divine authority, implicitly contrasting with the human "lords" (rulers, kings) of Psalm 2. The community addresses God as the one whose sovereignty towers over the Sanhedrin's threats.

παρρησίᾳ parrēsia — boldness: the community does not pray for safety or for their opponents to be silenced — they pray for boldness. This is the apostolic church's prayer: everything oriented toward witness. The Spirit's response (v31: the place shook; they were filled) answers precisely what was asked.

Places

The Gathering Place
31.7705° N, 35.2294° E
After release, Peter and John return to "their own people" (v23) — the broader community. The specific venue is not named (possibly the upper room of 1:13 or a larger house). The physical shaking of the place (v31) is a theophanic response to corporate prayer.
Cyprus (Barnabas)
35.1264° N, 33.4299° E
Barnabas is identified as a Levite from Cyprus — one of the Diaspora islands with a substantial Jewish community. His Cypriot origin will matter later: unnamed men from Cyprus are the first to preach to Gentiles in Antioch (Acts 11:20).

Context

The community's prayer in vv.24–30 is a masterclass in applied Scripture. They do not pray for the opposition to be removed but for themselves to be emboldened. Psalm 2 is used to reframe their situation: they are not victims of historical accident but participants in the cosmic drama the psalm describes. This reframing from victim to participant is itself an act of theological courage. Barnabas is introduced (vv.36–37) immediately after the description of communal sharing — not randomly but as a preview. His first appearance as a generous encourager characterises everything he will do in Acts.

Cross-Reference

  • Psalm 2:1–2Cited in vv.25–26 — "Why do the nations rage and the peoples plot in vain?" Applied to Herod, Pilate, Gentiles, and Israel conspiring against Jesus. The early church read Psalm 2 as the charter of their situation: opposition to Christ is the rage of the nations; God's response is enthroning his King.
  • Isaiah 37:16Hezekiah's prayer before Sennacherib's threat — "Sovereign Lord, you made the heavens and the earth" — echoed in v24. Another moment when God's people faced overwhelming human opposition and appealed to the Creator's sovereignty.
  • Acts 5:1–11Barnabas's generosity (v36–37) is immediately followed by Ananias and Sapphira's counter-example. Luke's literary pairing presents the community's standards: generosity is praised; hypocrisy is exposed.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:29–30

MacArthur notes the prayer's remarkable priorities: not safety, not punishment of opponents, but boldness and signs accompanying proclamation. Church prayer primarily about the church's comfort rather than the gospel's advance misses the apostolic pattern.

4:33

"God's grace was so powerfully at work in them all" — Luke frames the economic sharing in terms of grace (charis): it is not human generosity that enables the community but divine grace working through human generosity. The sharing is the overflow of the resurrection's reality.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:23–24

The church's first resort is prayer, not counter-organisation or public protest. Calvin uses this as a permanent model: when the church faces opposition, the proper first response is to bring the situation before God, not to immediately strategise human solutions.

4:32

"One in heart and mind" — Calvin connects this to Jesus's prayer in John 17:21 for unity. The unity is created by the Word and Spirit, not by institutional structures — which means it can be recreated wherever the Word and Spirit work.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:25

The NET notes a textual difficulty in v25: the Greek phrase connecting "through the Holy Spirit" to David is awkwardly worded in the manuscripts. Several variants exist. The point is clear regardless: the Psalm is attributed to the Spirit speaking through David.

4:34–35

The practice is iterative: "would sell" (imperfect — epipraskon) indicates repeated, ongoing action, not a single collective act. Each completed transaction was brought to the apostles' feet for distribution. The present-tense redistribution system was ongoing, not a one-time event.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
4:24

Robertson notes the unusual address "Sovereign Lord" (Despota) used only rarely in Jewish prayer — emphasising absolute divine authority in contrast to the human lords of Psalm 2. The community's prayer is structured as a direct challenge to the Sanhedrin's claim to final authority.

4:31

"The place where they were meeting was shaken" — a second Pentecost-like experience: wind at Pentecost (2:2), shaking here. Robertson cautions against treating these as normative experiences; they are sovereign acts of divine confirmation at critical founding moments.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:27–28

The conspiracy of Herod, Pilate, Gentiles, and Israel is described as doing "what your power and will had decided beforehand should happen" — the highest statement of divine sovereignty over the crucifixion in Acts outside John 19:11. The enemies' free actions were simultaneously God's predetermined plan.

4:32–35

The community of goods fits the Deuteronomic vision of 15:4 ("there need be no poor people among you"). Keener notes the difference from Qumran: Jerusalem sharing is voluntary and iterative (the imperfect tense in v34 — "they would sell") rather than a one-time surrender of property as an entrance requirement.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Paul↗ People
Apostle to the Gentiles; author of thirteen NT letters
Paul (Saul of Tarsus) is introduced as a persecutor at Stephen’s stoning (Acts 7:58) and is dramatically converted on the Damascus road (Acts 9). He dominates Acts 13–28 across three missionary journeys, planting churches from Cyprus to Greece to Rome. His final journey to Rome as a prisoner frames Acts’ theological climax: the gospel reaching the empire’s capital, "boldly and without hindrance" (Acts 28:31).
Peter↗ People
Lead apostle; spokesperson for the Jerusalem church
Peter (Simon bar Jonah) dominates Acts 1–12 as the church’s primary spokesman and miracle-worker. He preaches at Pentecost (Acts 2), heals at the Beautiful Gate (Acts 3), defends before the Sanhedrin (Acts 4–5), raises Tabitha (Acts 9:36–42), and receives the vision that opens the gospel to Gentiles (Acts 10). His release from Herod’s prison by an angel (Acts 12) closes his narrative in Acts; Paul’s story takes over from ch.13.
Barnabas↗ People
Paul’s first missionary partner; 'Son of Encouragement'
Barnabas (Joseph of Cyprus) is introduced as a generous land-seller in Acts 4:36–37 and is the one who vouches for the newly converted Paul to the suspicious Jerusalem church (Acts 9:27). He leads the Antioch church (Acts 11:22–24) and takes Paul as his partner for the first missionary journey (Acts 13–14). Their sharp disagreement over Mark (Acts 15:36–40) ends their partnership but doubles the missionary coverage.
Stephen↗ People
First martyr; deacon whose speech reframed salvation history
Stephen is appointed as one of the seven deacons in Acts 6 to serve the Hellenistic widows. His speech before the Sanhedrin (Acts 7) is the longest speech in Acts and a sweeping reinterpretation of Israel’s history as a pattern of rejecting God’s messengers. He is stoned while seeing the Son of Man standing at God’s right hand. His death scatters the Jerusalem church and launches the Gentile mission (Acts 8:1–4).
Philip↗ People
Evangelist; deacon who brought the gospel to Samaria and Ethiopia
Philip the evangelist (distinct from Philip the apostle) is one of the seven deacons of Acts 6. Scattered by Stephen’s persecution, he preaches in Samaria with signs and wonders (Acts 8:4–13), then is directed by an angel to the Ethiopian eunuch on the Gaza road (Acts 8:26–40), baptising him and inaugurating the gospel’s African reach. He later hosts Paul at Caesarea (Acts 21:8).
James↗ People
Brother of Jesus; leader of the Jerusalem church
James the brother of Jesus (not the apostle James son of Zebedee) emerges in Acts as the dominant figure of the Jerusalem church. He is not mentioned in Acts 1–9 but appears decisively in Acts 12:17 (Peter reports to him after his prison release), acts as president at the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15:13–21), and receives Paul’s final Jerusalem report (Acts 21:18). Paul calls him a "pillar" of the church (Gal 2:9). He authored the letter of James.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 4Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 4 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
