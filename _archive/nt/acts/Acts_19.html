Greek Word Study

δραχμάς drachmas — fifty thousand drachmas: a drachma was roughly a day's wage. Fifty thousand drachmas represents approximately 137 years of daily wages — an enormous sum reflecting the economic weight of Ephesus's magic industry. The public burning is both an act of personal repentance and a community-level declaration of the Gospel's triumph over the occult.

ηὔξανεν καὶ ἐπληθύνετο ēuxanen kai eplēthyneto — spread widely and grew in power: two imperfect verbs in v.20 — ēuxanen (was growing, like a plant) and eplēthyneto (was multiplying). The growth is organic and ongoing. Every attempt to stop the word ends with the word advancing further — Luke's theological refrain throughout Acts.

Places

Ephesus
37.9500° N, 27.3667° E
The largest city in Roman Asia Minor — a major port city and capital of the province of Asia. Home to the Temple of Artemis (one of the Seven Wonders of the ancient world), a centre of commerce, magic, and religious tourism. Paul's three-year ministry here is his longest recorded stay, and from it 'all the Jews and Greeks who lived in the province of Asia heard the word of the Lord.'
Hall of Tyrannus
37.9500° N, 27.3667° E
The lecture hall of Tyrannus — probably a philosopher or rhetoric teacher. Paul uses it for daily discussions for two years, likely during the midday hours when Tyrannus himself was not using it. The move from synagogue to hired lecture hall is Paul's standard pattern when synagogue doors close.

Context

Paul's three-year Ephesian ministry is the most strategically significant in Acts after Antioch and Corinth. Ephesus was the hub of trade routes reaching all of Asia Minor — the 'all of Asia heard' (v.10) is realistic, not hyperbole: the Gospel spread through Ephesian converts to Colossae, Laodicea, Hierapolis, and beyond (cf. Col 1:7 — Epaphras, a Pauline convert, founded the Colossian church). The seven sons of Sceva (vv.14–16) and the book-burning (vv.19–20) demonstrate the Gospel's confrontation with and victory over the dominant magical culture.

Cross-Reference

  • Colossians 1:7"You learned it from Epaphras, our dear fellow servant" — the Colossian church was founded by a Pauline convert from Ephesus, confirming the "all of Asia heard" claim.
  • Revelation 2:1–7The letter to Ephesus in Revelation: 'you have forsaken the love you had at first.' Paul's three-year ministry planted deeply; the subsequent generation faced the challenge of maintaining first love.
  • 2 Corinthians 1:8–10"We were under great pressure, far beyond our ability to endure, so that we despaired of life itself." Paul's reference to an Ephesian crisis — possibly connected to this period.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
19:2

"Did you receive the Holy Spirit when you believed?" — MacArthur uses this question as the model for pastoral diagnosis: Spirit-reception is the normal marker of genuine Christian conversion. When the Ephesian disciples have not even heard of the Spirit, Paul diagnoses incomplete faith and provides complete instruction.

19:18–19

MacArthur on the book-burning: genuine repentance from occult practice is not gradual reduction but public, irreversible destruction. The converts publicly destroyed their materials — an irreversible declaration that the practices are incompatible with Christ.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
19:6

Calvin on the Spirit coming with tongues and prophecy: this is not a prescription for all Spirit-reception but a specific apostolic confirmation in a gap-bridging situation. Calvin cautions against using this passage to require a second Spirit-experience after conversion — it addresses the unique case of pre-Pentecost disciples needing the full new covenant experience.

19:20

"In this way the word of the Lord spread widely and grew in power" — Calvin: the word grows by its own power (dynamis), not by human strategy. The church's primary strategy must be faithful proclamation; the word itself does the work.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
19:9

The NET notes that "the fifth to the tenth hour" — 11 AM to 4 PM — was the midday period when normal activities ceased. Paul's use of this time slot was strategic: the hall was available and workers had a natural break.

19:15

"Jesus I know, and Paul I know about" — the NET notes the demon uses two different verbs: ginōskō (personally know) for Jesus and epistamai (know about) for Paul. The distinction may indicate the spirit recognises Jesus's direct authority and Paul's delegated authority, but recognises neither in the sons of Sceva.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
19:9

"He took the disciples with him and had daily discussions in the lecture hall of Tyrannus" — Robertson notes kata heméran (daily) — every day for two years. The intensity is remarkable: daily theological discussion for two years, systematically training a core community who then spread the word throughout the province.

19:19

Robertson on fifty thousand drachmas: the public burning was both individual repentance and community declaration. The converts did not gradually transition away from magic — they publicly and permanently destroyed their economic investment in it.

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
19:11–12

Keener notes that contact relics and healing were common in the ancient world. Luke presents this without theological endorsement of the method — God's sovereign grace operates through means he chooses, not through mechanisms humans can control or replicate.

19:19

Keener provides background on magical papyri from Ephesus and Alexandria — the Ephesian Letters (Ephesia grammata) were among the most famous magical texts in the ancient world. The burning of 50,000 drachmas' worth represents the elimination of a major regional industry.

Verses 21—41 — The Riot of the Silversmiths: Great Is Artemis of the Ephesians

Greek Word Study

ναοποιός naopoios — silversmith / shrine-maker: Demetrius made silver shrines (naous — miniature temple replicas) of Artemis — souvenirs for pilgrims to one of the Seven Wonders of the ancient world. The Gospel's success threatened an entire industry built on religious tourism. The riot is economic before it is theological — a pattern Luke emphasises throughout Acts.

γραμματεύς grammateus — city clerk: the grammateus (v.35) was the most important civic official in an Ephesian city — keeper of official records and liaison between the city and Rome. His intervention — legally precise, politically shrewd, theologically neutral — dismisses the assembly and protects Paul without endorsing him. Providential civic protection.

Places

Theatre of Ephesus
37.9500° N, 27.3567° E
The Ephesian theatre — one of the largest in the ancient world, seating c.25,000 — has been archaeologically confirmed. The mob rushes here instinctively (theatres were the standard assembly point for civic emergencies). Paul's desire to enter (v.30) would have been suicidal.
Temple of Artemis
37.9500° N, 27.3639° E
One of the Seven Wonders of the ancient world — a centre of religious tourism, banking, and commerce. Demetrius's 'silver shrines' were among the commercial offerings available to pilgrims. The Gospel's direct attack on idolatry strikes at the economic foundation of the entire city.

Context

The Demetrius riot is Acts' most vivid demonstration of the Gospel's socioeconomic impact. The charge is economic before religious: 'our trade will lose its good name' precedes 'the temple will be discredited.' This is the clearest example in Acts of opposition motivated by financial self-interest (also Philippi, 16:19). The city clerk's intervention is providential: his legally precise dismissal prevents both Paul's death and a Roman crackdown on Ephesus for rioting.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 16:19"When her owners realized that their hope of making money was gone, they seized Paul and Silas" — the Philippi pattern: economic motivation behind opposition.
  • 1 Corinthians 15:32"If I fought wild beasts in Ephesus with no more than human hopes..." — Paul's possible reference to mortal danger at Ephesus.
  • 2 Corinthians 1:8–10"We were under great pressure, far beyond our ability to endure, so that we despaired of life itself." Possibly the riot or its aftermath.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
19:26

Demetrius's accusation — 'Paul has convinced and led astray large numbers of people... practically the whole province of Asia' — is an inadvertent compliment. The opposition confirms the mission's success. MacArthur notes that opposition is often the most reliable measure of the Gospel's actual impact.

19:35–40

MacArthur on the town clerk: God uses pagan civic officials to protect his church. The magistrate's concern is order and legal accountability, not theology — but God's purposes are served by the legal protection his intervention provides.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
19:28

Calvin on the mob shouting "Great is Artemis" for two hours: this is the nature of idolatry exposed — it cannot withstand rational argument (the spirit prevented them hearing Alexander, v.33–34) but sustains itself through collective emotional repetition. Calvin uses this as an illustration of superstition: it survives on volume and unanimity, not on evidence.

19:40

Calvin on the town clerk's warning: the fear of Roman legal consequences is the restraining force. Calvin notes that God uses the fear of earthly authority to restrain violence against his servants — not because earthly authorities are righteous, but because their self-interest happens to align with the church's protection.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
19:31

The NET notes that the "officials of the province" (Asiarchs — v.31) were the leading citizens of Roman Asia, responsible for the imperial cult. That some of them were Paul's friends shows the Gospel had penetrated even the highest social levels of the province.

19:40

The NET notes the town clerk's legal precision: the word "charged with rioting" (engkalesthēnai staseōs) is a specific Roman legal charge. His concern is not for Paul but for Ephesus's relationship with Rome — but his self-interest providentially serves Paul's protection.

Robertson — Word Pictures

A.T. Robertson, Word Pictures in the New Testament — Public Domain
19:32

"Most of the people did not even know why they were there" — Robertson on mob psychology: the crowd has been inflamed by economic argument but most participants have no personal stake. Corporate irrationality is a recurring human phenomenon the Gospel will always provoke.

19:35–40

Robertson on the town clerk's speech: a masterpiece of civic management — acknowledging the goddess's status, defending Paul's legal innocence, warning of Roman consequences, providing a legal process for legitimate grievances. Robertson calls it 'the best speech in Acts by a non-Christian.'

Keener — Acts Commentary

Craig S. Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (4 vols., 2012–2015) — Scholarly Paraphrase
19:24

Keener provides archaeological background: silver shrines of Artemis were common votives — archaeologists have found numerous examples. The industry was significant enough that Demetrius could gather a large assembly of craftsmen. The Gospel's impact after three years of daily teaching was economically measurable.

19:35

Keener notes that the 'image which fell from heaven' (diopetous) refers to a meteorite or similar object enshrined in the Temple — a well-attested feature of ancient religion. The city clerk's appeal to Ephesus's status as 'temple guardian' (neōkoros) addresses the crowd's most fundamental civic identity.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Paul↗ People
Apostle to the Gentiles; author of thirteen NT letters
Paul (Saul of Tarsus) is introduced as a persecutor at Stephen’s stoning (Acts 7:58) and is dramatically converted on the Damascus road (Acts 9). He dominates Acts 13–28 across three missionary journeys, planting churches from Cyprus to Greece to Rome. His final journey to Rome as a prisoner frames Acts’ theological climax: the gospel reaching the empire’s capital, "boldly and without hindrance" (Acts 28:31).
Philip↗ People
Evangelist; deacon who brought the gospel to Samaria and Ethiopia
Philip the evangelist (distinct from Philip the apostle) is one of the seven deacons of Acts 6. Scattered by Stephen’s persecution, he preaches in Samaria with signs and wonders (Acts 8:4–13), then is directed by an angel to the Ethiopian eunuch on the Gaza road (Acts 8:26–40), baptising him and inaugurating the gospel’s African reach. He later hosts Paul at Caesarea (Acts 21:8).

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews (c.93 AD)
Josephus provides extensive first-century background for the political, religious, and social conditions described in Acts — the Herodian family, Pharisees and Sadducees, Roman provincial governance.
Acts’ historical details consistently align with Josephus’s independent account of the same period, supporting the narrative’s historical credibility.
Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS, 1QM)
The Qumran community texts illuminate the diversity of Second Temple Judaism from which Christianity emerged — their eschatological expectations, community rules, and temple criticism parallel aspects of the early church.
The Scrolls demonstrate that the early church’s practices (community meals, shared goods, eschatological expectation) were not unprecedented within Jewish renewal movements.
Greco-Roman Travel Literature and Epistolary Conventions
Acts’ travel narrative (ch.13–28) conforms to the conventions of ancient Greek travel literature, and Paul’s speeches follow rhetorical models documented in Greco-Roman oratory.
The Hellenistic literary conventions confirm Acts’ composition for a sophisticated Greco-Roman audience and support its historical reliability as ancient historiography.

Textual Notes

Acts 19Western text (Codex Bezae D) vs Alexandrian text
Alexandrian (P45, P74, א, B) is the primary scholarly text. Western (Codex Bezae D) is 10–15% longer, with additions that appear to expand and harmonise.
Acts has the most significant NT textual variation. The Western text of Acts is not simply corrupt but may preserve early oral expansions. Most scholars follow the shorter Alexandrian text as more original.
Acts 19 (general)P45 and early papyrus witnesses
The Chester Beatty Papyrus (P45, c.250 AD) is the earliest substantial Acts manuscript, generally supporting the Alexandrian text with some unique readings.
The papyrus evidence has largely confirmed the Alexandrian tradition as the best text of Acts, though the Western text’s substantial additions remain a subject of scholarly investigation.

Scholarly Debates

Authorship and Historicity of Scripture Walkthrough
Traditional Lukan authorship (Hengel, Keener, classical scholarship)
Attributes Acts to Luke the physician (Col 4:14), a companion of Paul, on the basis of the "we" passages, historical accuracy, and early church tradition.
Luke’s medical vocabulary (noted by Hobart) and the detailed geographical and political accuracy of Acts support the traditional attribution.
Anonymous/secondary authorship (Haenchen, Conzelmann)
Questions Lukan authorship on the basis of discrepancies between Acts’ Paul and the Pauline letters, suggesting a later author using Lukan sources.
This view notes that Acts does not always match Paul’s letters precisely, and argues the "we" passages may be a literary device rather than eyewitness markers.
The authorship question remains open; most scholars accept that Acts was composed within a generation of the events described and reflects genuine historical tradition.
