Mark 8

Feeding Four Thousand; Peter's Confession; The Cost of Discipleship

Verses 1–21 — Feeding Four Thousand; The Yeast of the Pharisees
123456789101112131415161718192021

Greek Word Study

zymē ZY-may — yeast/leaven: Watch out for the yeast of the Pharisees and Herod — leaven in Jewish usage was typically a metaphor for hidden, pervasive influence (usually negative). The disciples interpret it literally. Jesus is warning about the Pharisees’ demand for signs (v.11) and Herod’s political calculation — both forms of willful blindness to what is happening.

Context

The feeding of four thousand is parallel to the five thousand but in Gentile territory (Decapolis). The two feedings together form a diptych: one Jewish (twelve basketfuls = twelve tribes), one Gentile (seven basketfuls = seven nations of Canaan or the number of completion). Jesus’s challenge to the disciples in vv.17-21 is the most severe rebuke in Mark: they have witnessed both feedings and still do not understand.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 5:21They have eyes but do not see, ears but do not hear — the prophetic indictment that Jesus applies to his own disciples.
  • Isa 6:9-10Ever seeing but never perceiving — the Isaiah hardening text that Jesus had applied to outsiders in 4:12 is now applied to insiders in 8:18.
  • Num 11:13Where can I get meat for all these people? — Moses’s complaint in the wilderness echoed by the disciples’ question in 8:4.

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:12

Marcus: The "deep sigh" (anastenaxas) in response to the demand for a sign is the strongest emotional reaction in Mark before Gethsemane. Marcus connects it to the LXX of Ezek 21:11-12, where God commands the prophet to sigh deeply because of coming judgment. Jesus's sigh is the prophet's grief at a generation that has witnessed extraordinary things and remained impervious. The rhetorical question "Why does this generation ask for a sign?" echoes Ps 95:8-11 ("Do not harden your hearts as at Meribah") — judgment language for a recalcitrant generation.

8:19-20

Marcus: The two feeding miracles produce different numbers of leftovers: twelve baskets (kophinos — a distinctively Jewish basket type used in Roman sources as a Jewish identifier, Juvenal Sat. 3.14) and seven baskets (spyridos — a large Gentile basket). Marcus argues the numbers are intentional: twelve = the twelve tribes of Israel; seven = the seven nations of Canaan or the number of completeness. The two feedings together enact the inclusion of both covenant communities in the messianic provision.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:14-21

Rhoads: The leaven-warning scene is the narrative's most explicit diagnosis of the disciples' condition. Jesus's seven questions (8:17-21) are a rhetorical crescendo: "Why are you talking about bread? Do you still not see? Do you still not understand? Are your hearts hardened? Do you have eyes but fail to see? Do you have ears but fail to hear? Don't you remember?" Each question isolates a different dimension of their failure. Rhoads identifies this as the low point of the disciples' characterization before Peter's confession — the nadir preceding the turning point.

8:17

Rhoads: "Are your hearts hardened?" — here the narrative's key characterization term (pōroō) is applied directly to the disciples, not to the Pharisees or outsiders. The insider/outsider distinction established in 4:11 has been progressively eroded. Rhoads's analysis: the disciples were characterized positively at their calling (immediate obedience), given privileged access to the parables (4:11), and sent out with authority (6:7-13). Now they are diagnosed with the same hardness as the antagonists. The narrative is preparing for the confessional breakthrough of 8:29 — but also for the collapse of 14:50.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
8:1-21

The second feeding miracle (four thousand, vv.1-10) is treated separately in the Catena from the first. Chrysostom notes the disciples' apparent forgetfulness — "where in this remote place can anyone get enough bread to feed them?" — after having witnessed the feeding of five thousand. Origen says this is not stupidity but a figure of the spiritual forgetfulness that can overtake even those who have witnessed great things. The request for a sign from heaven (vv.11-12) is, for Bede, the permanent condition of unbelief: no sign will satisfy the person who has decided in advance to reject. The leaven of the Pharisees and Herod (v15) is, for Chrysostom, not a single teaching but a general disposition — the corrosive influence of unbelief spreading through a community.

8:14-21

The disciples' literal misunderstanding about bread is, for Chrysostom, Mark's most explicit statement about the disciples' spiritual density in this section of the Gospel. Jesus's series of rhetorical questions — "Do you still not see or understand? Are your hearts hardened?" — repeats the language used of Pharaoh and of the wilderness generation. Origen notes the disturbing echo: the same hardness Jesus diagnoses in the Pharisees appears now in his own disciples. The question "Do you still not understand?" ends the passage without an answer, which Theophylact says is deliberate — the reader is left to supply the answer for themselves.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:17

Are your hearts hardened? — the most devastating question Jesus asks his disciples in Mark. The Greek pōroō is used for Pharaoh, the Pharisees (3:5), and now the Twelve. The disciples are not simply slow learners; they are in danger of the same judicial hardening as Jesus’s opponents.

8:21

Do you still not understand? — the question is left unanswered. Mark does not give the disciples a satisfying moment of comprehension. Their failure to understand is suspended in tension until the confession of 8:29.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:11-12

Calvin: The Pharisees’ demand for a sign from heaven exposes the nature of their unbelief. They have not lacked signs — they have lacked the willingness to receive them. A sign for the sign-demander is not possible because the demand is evidence of the spiritual condition that no sign can cure.

8:17-21

Calvin: The disciples’ hardness is a warning to all who receive extraordinary privilege without the inner illumination of the Spirit. To have witnessed the feedings and not understood is more culpable than never having witnessed them.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:19-20

Twelve…seven — the two numbers invite symbolic interpretation. Twelve recalls the tribes of Israel; seven recalls either the seven nations of Canaan (Deut 7:1) or the number of fullness. The two feedings together suggest that Jesus’s provision encompasses both covenant communities.

8:21

Do you still not understand? — the open question creates narrative suspense. The answer begins in v.27-29 (Peter’s confession) but is not fully given until 15:39. Mark structures the Gospel around this progressive, incomplete comprehension.

Verses 22–26 — The Blind Man of Bethsaida: Two-Stage Healing
2223242526

Greek Word Study

Christos khris-TOS — Messiah/Anointed One: You are the Messiah — su ei ho Christos. Peter’s confession is the pivot of the entire Gospel. In Mark’s structure, everything before 8:29 has been building toward this recognition; everything after 8:29 flows from it. The Messiah, Peter now knows; the suffering Messiah, Peter immediately refuses.

Context

The two-stage healing of the blind man (vv.22-26) is Mark’s structural hinge: the man sees partially (people like trees), then fully. It is a parable of the disciples’ gradual sight. Peter’s confession immediately follows — partial sight: he sees the Messiah but not the suffering Messiah. Jesus then defines discipleship as cross-bearing — the confession must lead to self-denial, not triumph.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 53:3He was despised and rejected — the Suffering Servant text that underlies the first passion prediction.
  • Dan 7:13-14The Son of Man coming in glory with the holy angels (v.38) — the Danielic parousia text that frames the cross-bearing call.
  • Gen 22:2Take your son…offer him as a burnt offering — the Akedah pattern behind “denying yourself and taking up your cross.”

Marcus — Anchor Bible

Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8 / 8–16, Anchor Bible (2000/2009) — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:22-26

Marcus: The two-stage healing of the blind man is unique to Mark and has no parallel in the other Gospels. Marcus argues it functions as a narrative parable: the man sees "people like trees walking" — partial but still distorted vision. The second touch produces clarity. This mirrors the disciples' condition throughout chs. 1-8: they have seen clearly enough to follow, but their vision remains distorted. Peter's confession (8:29) is the "first touch"; full clarity will require the cross and resurrection.

8:31

Marcus: "The Son of Man must suffer" — dei, the divine necessity. Marcus reads this against the background of the Danielic Son of Man (Dan 7:13-14), who in Daniel receives dominion rather than suffering. Mark's Jesus fuses the Danielic Son of Man with the Deutero-Isaianic Suffering Servant — a combination without clear precedent in Second Temple literature. This fusion of two apparently incompatible figures (glorified ruler and suffering servant) into a single identity is one of Mark's most distinctive theological moves.

Rhoads — Mark as Story

David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story (3rd ed., 2012) — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:22-30

Rhoads: The two-stage blind man healing (8:22-26) and Peter's confession (8:27-30) form a structural diptych: partial sight corrected / partial confession corrected. The blind man's "trees walking" is the visual equivalent of Peter's "Messiah" without the cross. Just as the blind man needed the second touch, the disciples need the passion teaching. Rhoads identifies 8:29 as the "hinge" of Mark's narrative — the point where the first half's question (Who is Jesus?) is answered, opening the second half's question (What does it mean?).

8:31-38

Rhoads: Peter's rebuke of Jesus (8:32) is the narrative's most dramatic reversal of the power dynamic. The disciple who just confessed correctly ("You are the Messiah") immediately acts to suppress the Messiah's self-definition. Jesus's counter-rebuke ("Get behind me, Satan") is the harshest characterization of any disciple in Mark. Rhoads reads it as a narrative turning point in the disciples' characterization: from this point on, their misunderstanding is no longer ignorant but resistant — they have heard and refused.

Catena Aurea

Thomas Aquinas, Catena Aurea — Faithful Paraphrase
8:22-26

The two-stage healing of the blind man at Bethsaida (unique to Mark) is, for Origen, the most carefully structured healing miracle in the Gospel — because it mirrors the two-stage opening of the disciples' eyes in the very next section (Peter's confession and the first passion prediction). Chrysostom notes that after the first touch the man sees people "like trees walking" — partial vision, distorted perception — which is exactly the state of the disciples' understanding until the second touch of full revelation.

8:27-38

Peter's confession "You are the Messiah" is, for Chrysostom, both the high-water mark and the immediate limit of the disciples' understanding: Peter confesses the title but cannot receive the content (the suffering Messiah). The rebuke "Get behind me, Satan" is, for Origen, not an identification of Peter with Satan but a command: the adversary's role is to hinder; Peter is hindering. The call to take up the cross (v34) is, for Ambrose, the first time in Mark that discipleship is explicitly defined as participation in Christ's death — the pattern of following is the pattern of dying.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:29

Who do you say I am? — the most important question in the Gospel. Peter’s answer (You are the Messiah) is theologically correct. Jesus’s response (strict silence + first passion prediction) is the correction: the Messiah must suffer.

8:33

Get behind me, Satan! — the most severe rebuke in Jesus’s ministry. Peter has just confessed correctly and is immediately called Satan because he refuses the cross. The confession without the cross is not the gospel — it is a temptation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:31

He must suffer — dei: the divine necessity. Calvin: the cross is not a tragedy that overtakes Jesus but the divine appointment that he fulfils. The “must” is the language of covenant obligation, not external compulsion. Jesus freely accepts what God has appointed.

8:34

Take up their cross — Calvin: the cross was already the Roman symbol of criminal execution. For the disciples to hear “take up your cross” was not a devotional metaphor but a visceral image. The call to follow Jesus involves the readiness to be treated as Jesus will be treated.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:29

You are the Messiah — Mark’s version is shorter than Matthew’s (which adds “Son of the living God”) and more abrupt. Mark’s characteristic brevity: the confession is stated, confirmed by the secrecy command, and immediately complicated by the passion prediction.

8:31

After three days rise again — Mark uses “after three days” where Matthew and Luke use “on the third day.” The difference reflects two different ways of counting the same event (inclusive vs exclusive counting). The resurrection is predicted; the disciples do not yet understand it (9:10).

Peter (Simon)
First among the Twelve; confessor and denier

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Jewish War 2.118; Antiquities 13.171-172
Josephus describes the various first-century Jewish messianic expectations: king, prophet, warrior.
Provides the context for the popular identifications of Jesus (John, Elijah, a prophet) that Mark records in 8:28.

Reception History

Karl Barth, Church Dogmatics IV/2
Barth used 8:31-33 to argue that the cross is not a problem to be overcome but the content of the gospel. Peter’s refusal of the suffering Messiah is the church’s persistent temptation: to want the crown without the cross, the triumph without the passion.
Dietrich Bonhoeffer, Discipleship
Bonhoeffer’s entire theology of discipleship is built on 8:34: “Take up your cross and follow me.” The cross is not a symbol of suffering but of death to the self. Discipleship begins precisely where the attempt to create one’s own life is abandoned.

Literary Structure

The Blind Man as Structural Hinge: Two-Stage Sight8:22-26
The Gospel’s Pivot: Before and After 8:298:27-38
Mark 8 is the Gospel’s structural hinge. The two-stage healing, Peter’s confession, and the cross-bearing call together constitute the theological centre from which the second half of the Gospel flows.

Greek-Rooted Reading

Greek-Rooted Reading

8:17 Pōroō — hardened. The same judicial hardening word used for Pharaoh, the Pharisees, and now Jesus’s own disciples. The warning is real.
8:29 Christos — the Anointed One. Peter’s two-word confession is the Gospel’s pivot.
8:34 Aratō ton stauron autou — let him take up his cross. The word “cross” (stauros) first appears in the Gospel in a command, not a description. The cross is the disciple’s calling before it is the Lord’s death.

Textual Notes

8:10Dalmanutha — an unknown location
Dalmanutha appears only here in the NT and is unknown from any other source. Some MSS replace it with Magadan (harmonising with Matt 15:39) or Magdala.
The obscurity of Dalmanutha argues for its originality — no scribe would substitute a known name for a known one. Its identification remains uncertain.
8:15"The yeast of Herod" or "the yeast of the Herodians"
NA28/UBS5 reads Hērōdou (of Herod); some MSS read Hērōdianōn (of the Herodians, harmonising with the coalition in 3:6).
"Of Herod" is probably original; the Herodians variant is a contextual clarification by a scribe who knew the Pharisee-Herodian alliance from earlier in Mark.

Scholarly Debates

Is Peter’s confession the Gospel’s structural centre?
Centre-pivot view
Mark 8:29 divides the Gospel into two equal halves (chs.1-8: who is Jesus? / chs.9-16: what does it mean?). The two-stage blind man healing and the two-stage Peter confession confirm the structural symmetry.
Structural pivot
Continuous-narrative view
Mark’s Gospel should not be forced into a two-part structure. The material flows more organically without a sharp structural break at 8:29.
Continuous flow
"From that time on" does not appear in Mark as it does in Matthew (16:21). But the first explicit passion prediction (8:31), the first mention of the cross (8:34), and the geographic turn toward Jerusalem (10:1) all cluster around 8:29, supporting the pivot reading.

Theological Themes Tracker

Mark 8 is the Christology and discipleship chapter: who Jesus is (Messiah, 8:29) and what following him costs (the cross, 8:34) are established in sequence at the Gospel’s structural centre.
