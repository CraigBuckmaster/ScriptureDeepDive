Evangelical · NICOT · Hebrew Scholarship

Bruce K. Waltke

1930– · Evangelical OT Scholar · Regent College · Reformed Theological Seminary · NICOT Proverbs

“Proverbs is not a book of timeless truisms; it is a disciplined school of wisdom that trains the human character to perceive reality truly, to value what God values, and to act accordingly. The fear of the Lord is not one theme among many — it is the epistemological foundation on which all wisdom rests.”

— Bruce K. Waltke, The Book of Proverbs: Chapters 1–15 (NICOT, 2004)

Biography

Bruce K. Waltke is one of the most distinguished evangelical Old Testament scholars of the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. Born in 1930, he received his doctorate from Harvard University and has taught at Dallas Theological Seminary, Regent College (Vancouver), and Reformed Theological Seminary, among others. His career spans over six decades of productive scholarship at the intersection of Hebrew linguistics, OT theology, and evangelical faith.

His two-volume commentary on Proverbs in the NICOT series (2004–2005) is the most comprehensive evangelical-scholarly treatment of the book in English. It brings to Proverbs the full weight of Waltke’s linguistic expertise — he is co-author of the standard reference grammar of Biblical Hebrew (Waltke-O’Connor) — combined with deep engagement with ancient Near Eastern wisdom literature and rigorous biblical-theological interpretation.

Waltke has also made major contributions to OT theology more broadly, including a systematic OT theology co-authored with Charles Yu, and foundational work on the Psalms, Micah, and the Pentateuch.

Interpretive Approach

Waltke approaches Proverbs as a unified literary and theological work rather than a miscellaneous collection of folk wisdom. He is particularly attentive to the book’s macrostructure — the programmatic role of Proverbs 1–9 (the extended wisdom poems) in establishing the theological framework within which the individual proverbs of chs. 10–31 are to be read.

His philological work on Hebrew vocabulary in Proverbs is unmatched in evangelical scholarship. He treats each term with the same rigour he brings to his Hebrew grammar — attending to semantic range, cognate languages, usage patterns, and contextual nuance. This makes his commentary an indispensable lexical resource as well as a theological one.

Waltke reads Proverbs within a canonical-theological framework: the wisdom of Proverbs is not a secular pragmatism but a Spirit-given perception of God’s created order. The fear of the Lord is the epistemological foundation; all true wisdom flows from right relationship with the covenant God.

Theological Tradition

Waltke writes from within the Reformed evangelical tradition — committed to the inerrancy and authority of Scripture, to historical-grammatical exegesis, and to the canonical unity of the Old and New Testaments. His theology of wisdom in Proverbs connects the book to the broader biblical-theological narrative: the wisdom personified in Prov 8 is a pointer toward Christ as the wisdom of God (1 Cor 1:30; Col 2:3).

He engages critical scholarship rigorously without being governed by its methodological scepticism. His commentaries are always philologically responsible and historically informed while remaining confessionally evangelical.

Key Works

  • The Book of Proverbs: Chapters 1–15 (NICOT, 2004)Volume one of the definitive evangelical commentary on Proverbs. Covers the programmatic wisdom poems of chs. 1–9 and the first collection of Solomonic proverbs. The primary source for Waltke notes in this tool.
  • The Book of Proverbs: Chapters 15–31 (NICOT, 2005)Volume two. Completes the Solomonic collection and the later appendices including the Sayings of Agur and the poem of the ḥēset ḥayil (the woman of valor, Prov 31:10–31).
  • An Introduction to Biblical Hebrew Syntax (1990)Co-authored with M. P. O’Connor. The standard advanced reference grammar of Biblical Hebrew for English-reading students — “Waltke-O’Connor” is the indispensable technical grammar of the field.
  • An Old Testament Theology (2007)Co-authored with Charles Yu. A comprehensive biblical-theological treatment of the OT canon, situating individual books within the overarching narrative of creation, covenant, and redemption.
