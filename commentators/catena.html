Patristic Compilation · 13th Century

Catena Aurea

Compiled c.1263–1268 by Thomas Aquinas · Dominican Friar · University of Paris & Naples

“The purpose of this work is to open up to students of Holy Scripture an easy and ready access to the opinions of the holy Fathers, so that by their aid we may without difficulty penetrate to the inmost meaning of Holy Writ.”

— Thomas Aquinas, Preface to the Catena Aurea

What Is the Catena Aurea?

The Catena Aurea (Latin: “Golden Chain”) is a verse-by-verse commentary on all four Gospels compiled by Thomas Aquinas between approximately 1263 and 1268, at the request of Pope Urban IV. It is not itself an original commentary but a carefully edited anthology: a chain of patristic quotations drawn from over eighty Church Fathers and early medieval theologians, woven together into a continuous exposition of each passage.

The Fathers whose voices appear in the Catena include Chrysostom, Augustine, Ambrose, Jerome, Gregory the Great, Cyril of Alexandria, Origen, Hilary of Poitiers, Bede, and many others. Aquinas selected, arranged, and sometimes lightly edited these quotations to create a unified reading of each verse — one in which the reader encounters not one interpreter but the accumulated insight of the first six centuries of Christian biblical interpretation.

The work was translated into English by John Henry Newman in the 1840s (before his conversion to Rome), and this translation is now in the public domain. It remains the most comprehensive collection of patristic commentary on the Gospels available in English.

Thomas Aquinas as Compiler

Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274) was the greatest systematic theologian of the medieval period and the intellectual centre of the Dominican order. His Summa Theologiae represents the apex of scholastic theology. But in the Catena Aurea he is not constructing a system — he is curating a tradition. His genius as compiler is in the selection and arrangement: knowing which Father to quote on each verse, how to create dialogue between conflicting interpretations, and when a brief patristic note illuminates more than a lengthy argument.

Aquinas understood the Church Fathers not as authorities to be cited but as readers who had wrestled seriously with the same texts for the same theological stakes. His editorial presence in the Catena is largely invisible — but the intelligence behind the selection is consistently high.

What the Catena Adds

The Catena provides something no modern commentary offers: the voice of the early church in its full diversity. Reading Chrysostom on the Sermon on the Mount, then Augustine, then Ambrose — all on the same verse — reveals the range of early Christian interpretation and shows where the tradition agreed and where it diverged. It is the deep past of the text speaking, before the divisions of East and West, Protestant and Catholic, had fully hardened.

For Matthew specifically, the Catena is the richest resource available: Aquinas drew most extensively on Chrysostom’s ninety homilies on Matthew (the most important patristic commentary on Matthew in existence) and Augustine’s De Sermone Domini in Monte. Reading Catena notes alongside MacArthur, Robertson, and Calvin gives a reader access to what four different centuries of serious Christian scholarship saw in the same passage.
