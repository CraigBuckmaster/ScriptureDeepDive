Biography

Timothy R. Ashley is a Canadian evangelical OT scholar whose contribution to biblical studies is centred on his major commentary on Numbers in the New International Commentary on the Old Testament (NICOT) series, published by Eerdmans in 1993.

The NICOT series represents evangelical scholarship at its most rigorous — combining commitment to the authority of Scripture with full engagement with the Hebrew text, ancient Near Eastern parallels, and the history of interpretation. Ashley’s Numbers is among the series’ most useful volumes for students and preachers: accessible in style but uncompromising in philological depth.

Ashley brings particular strength in Hebrew philology and literary analysis, consistently attending to the structure and narrative artistry of a book that less careful readers dismiss as a mere census list.

Interpretive Approach

Ashley reads Numbers as a unified narrative with careful literary structure, not a miscellaneous collection of laws and lists. He identifies the book’s two census counts (chs.1 and 26) as structural pillars that frame the wilderness generation’s story: counted, failed, and replaced by a new generation counted again on the threshold of the land.

His commentary is particularly strong on the narrative sections — the spy crisis, Korah’s rebellion, Balaam, the Phinehas incident — where he demonstrates that the priestly texts are not liturgical appendices to the Yahwist narrative but integrated theological statements about community, leadership, and holiness.

On the law sections, Ashley provides careful word-study work and attention to the legal logic of the Priestly source without the polemical dismissiveness that sometimes characterises critical scholarship toward the levitical tradition.

Theological Tradition

Ashley writes from within the conservative evangelical tradition, holding to Mosaic authorship of the Pentateuch while engaging seriously with critical scholarship. His commentary is apologetically responsible without being defensive, and theologically rich without being preachy. It is the standard evangelical-scholarly resource on Numbers.
