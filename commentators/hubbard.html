Robert L. Hubbard Jr.

1943– · Evangelical Biblical Scholar · North Park Theological Seminary · NICOT Commentary on Ruth

“Ruth is a story about ordinary people doing extraordinary things for one another — and in so doing, living out the covenant loyalty that Israel’s God himself embodies. Hesed is not an abstract theological concept in Ruth; it is the breath of every scene.”

— Robert L. Hubbard Jr., The Book of Ruth (NICOT, 1988)

Biography

Robert L. Hubbard Jr. is an American evangelical biblical scholar who taught Old Testament and Hebrew at North Park Theological Seminary in Chicago for many years. His 1988 commentary on Ruth in the New International Commentary on the Old Testament (NICOT) series is widely regarded as the definitive evangelical-scholarly treatment of the book — thorough in its philological analysis, careful in its engagement with ancient Near Eastern legal customs, and consistently attentive to the book’s theological dimensions.

Hubbard brings to Ruth the same combination of linguistic precision and theological sensitivity that characterises the best of the NICOT series. He engages the major critical and interpretive debates — the date and provenance of the book, the nature of the go’el institution, the relationship between the sandal ceremony and levirate law — while never losing sight of the narrative as a story about real human beings exercising covenant faithfulness.

Beyond Ruth, Hubbard has contributed to Old Testament studies through his work on the redemption motif across the Hebrew canon and his involvement in the New International Commentary series as a whole.

Interpretive Approach

Hubbard reads Ruth as a carefully crafted literary unit whose artistry serves its theology. He is attentive to the book’s narrative structure, its patterns of repetition and contrast (fullness and emptiness, loyalty and self-interest, insider and outsider), and its rich deployment of the hesed motif as both theme and characterising virtue.

His philological work on key terms — go’el (kinsman-redeemer), hesed (covenant loyalty), and the sandal-removal ceremony — is foundational for any serious study of Ruth. He situates these terms within their ancient Near Eastern legal and social contexts while drawing out their theological significance within the OT canon.

Hubbard is particularly strong on the typological dimensions of the text: Boaz as a type of the divine redeemer, the hesed of Ruth as a mirror of the hesed of YHWH, and the genealogy’s placement of the story within the Davidic—and ultimately messianic—line.

Theological Tradition

Hubbard writes from within the evangelical Protestant tradition, with commitments to the authority and coherence of the biblical text, historical-grammatical exegesis, and theological interpretation that situates Ruth within the larger biblical-theological narrative. He engages critical scholarship seriously without being governed by its methodological presuppositions.

His treatment of Ruth’s Gentile status and her full inclusion in the covenant community is particularly sensitive — he reads the book not as an ethnic polemic but as a narrative demonstration that hesed is a covenant quality available to anyone willing to live by it.
