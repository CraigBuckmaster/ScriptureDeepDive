Evangelical · NICOT Commentary · 20th Century

Peter C. Craigie

1938–1985 · Evangelical OT Scholar · University of Calgary · NICOT Deuteronomy

“Deuteronomy is not simply a restatement of law. It is a document of covenant renewal in which the treaty form of the ancient Near East has been taken up and transformed to express the unique relationship between the God of Israel and his people.”

— — Peter C. Craigie, The Book of Deuteronomy, NICOT (1976)

Biography

Peter Craigie was a British-born OT scholar who taught at the University of Calgary until his death in a car accident in 1985 at age 47. Despite his tragically short career, he produced a body of work whose influence in evangelical OT scholarship has been lasting.

His 1976 NICOT commentary on Deuteronomy remains one of the standard evangelical resources on the book — consistently cited by scholars across traditions for its clarity, learning, and theological depth. He also wrote major works on Psalms (Word Biblical Commentary), ancient Near Eastern warfare, and Ugaritic literature.

Craigie brought to Deuteronomy a combination of deep knowledge of ancient Near Eastern treaty literature, Ugaritic parallels, and the history of interpretation. His engagement with Meredith Kline’s treaty-structure thesis was rigorous and productive, and his treatment of the ethical challenges in Deuteronomy remains one of the most thoughtful in evangelical scholarship.

Interpretive Approach

Craigie reads Deuteronomy through the lens of ancient Near Eastern treaty structure — particularly the Hittite suzerainty treaties of the 14th–13th centuries BC. He argues that Deuteronomy’s six-part structure (preamble, historical prologue, stipulations, document clause, witnesses, blessings/curses) mirrors these treaties and therefore supports an early, Mosaic date for the book.

He consistently attends to the theological dimensions of the legal material, showing how each statute reflects the character of the God who gave it and the community identity of the people who received it. His treatment of the ḥērem (ban/devotion-to-destruction) in chs.7 and 13 is particularly nuanced, engaging the ethical challenge with intellectual honesty.

On the rhetorical and homiletical character of Moses’s speeches, Craigie is perceptive about the ways in which covenant renewal language functions differently from straightforward legislation — it is persuasive, pastoral, and personally addressed.

Theological Tradition

Craigie writes from within the evangelical tradition, holding to Mosaic authorship and the historical reliability of Deuteronomy while engaging seriously with critical scholarship. His commentaries are notable for their intellectual honesty about difficult texts and their refusal to shelter behind easy answers.
