Rhoads & Michie

David Rhoads (b. 1941) & Donald Michie · Narrative Critics · Authors of Mark as Story

“The literary approach is not one more method to be added to the list of methods used to study the Bible. It is a fundamentally different way of looking at the text.”

— David Rhoads & Donald Michie, Mark as Story, 1st edition (1982)

Biography

David Rhoads is Professor Emeritus of New Testament at the Lutheran School of Theology at Chicago. Donald Michie is a literary scholar whose collaboration with Rhoads produced one of the most consequential works in twentieth-century Gospel studies. Their 1982 book Mark as Story: An Introduction to the Narrative of a Gospel founded the discipline of Markan narrative criticism and permanently changed how scholars and readers approach the Second Gospel.

The book grew out of Rhoads’s conviction that Mark deserved to be read as a unified literary work — not merely as a collection of pericopes to be dissected by form criticism or a quarry for reconstructing the historical Jesus, but as a carefully crafted narrative with its own narrator, characters, plot, and rhetoric. Michie’s background in literary studies provided the theoretical framework; Rhoads provided the biblical scholarship. The result was a genuinely interdisciplinary work that brought the tools of narrative theory to bear on a Gospel text for the first time in a sustained way.

A second edition appeared in 1999, joined by Joanna Dewey; the third edition (2012) revised the analysis in light of three decades of subsequent scholarship and includes expanded discussion of the narrator’s rhetoric and the story’s ideological perspective. The three editions together trace the maturation of narrative criticism as a discipline.

Interpretive Approach

Rhoads and Michie’s method is narrative criticism: the close reading of the text as a narrative, attending to the techniques by which it produces meaning. Their framework analyses the text along three axes. First, the narrator — what kind of narrator does Mark employ? What does the narrator know (everything), what does the narrator reveal (selectively), and how does the narrator position the reader in relation to the characters and events? Second, characters — how are Jesus, the disciples, the authorities, and minor characters constructed through action, speech, and the narrator’s commentary? Third, plot — how does Mark structure his narrative, what tensions drive it forward, and how does it resolve (or refuse to resolve)?

Several of their analytical concepts have become foundational in Markan studies. The reliable narrator: Mark’s narrator is omniscient and trustworthy, and the reader who trusts the narrator’s opening declaration (1:1) is positioned to understand what characters within the story cannot. Intercalation (the Markan sandwich): the technique of inserting one story inside another so that each interprets the other. Dramatic irony: the reader knows what characters do not. The disciples’ characterization arc: the disciples begin with positive characterization (immediate obedience, privileged insider access) and progressively harden into incomprehension and failure.

Their approach reads Mark on its own terms rather than treating it as a window onto historical events behind the text. This is methodologically distinct from historical criticism (Marcus) and from theological exposition (MacArthur, Calvin). Together, the three perspectives give a stereoscopic depth to any Markan passage: the historical context, the narrative construction, and the theological import.

Significance

Before Mark as Story, the standard critical assumption was that Mark was the least literary of the Gospels — a rough, unpolished compilation of early tradition by an author with minimal literary skill. Rhoads and Michie’s analysis reversed this judgment. Mark, they demonstrated, is a carefully constructed narrative, and what had been read as clumsiness was often deliberate technique: abrupt transitions as urgency, the withholding of interiority as characterization by action, repetition as structural scaffolding.

Their work also restored attention to the reader’s experience of the text. Historical criticism asked where the text came from; narrative criticism asked what it does — how it shapes the reader’s knowledge, sympathy, and understanding as the story unfolds. This shift of focus from origin to effect produced a new generation of Markan scholarship, of which the third edition of Mark as Story stands as both monument and continuing resource.

Key Works

  • Mark as Story: An Introduction to the Narrative of a Gospel, 3rd ed. (Rhoads, Dewey & Michie, 2012)The primary source for Rhoads notes in this tool. The foundational text of Markan narrative criticism, now in its third edition. Analyses narrator, characters, and plot in systematic detail. Essential reading for anyone who wants to understand how Mark works as a story.
  • Reading Mark, Engaging the Gospel (David Rhoads, 2004)A collection of Rhoads’s essays developing and extending the narrative-critical approach in specific Markan passages and themes, including his work on the Syrophoenician woman and the nature of Markan characterization.
  • The Challenge of Diversity (David Rhoads, 1996)Extends narrative-critical methods to the question of community and identity in early Christianity, showing how the narrative approach illuminates not just the text but its social world.
