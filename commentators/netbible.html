What Are the NET Bible Notes?

The NET Bible (New English Translation) was produced by a team of more than twenty-five biblical scholars associated with Dallas Theological Seminary and is unique among modern translations in the scale and transparency of its annotation apparatus. The Full Notes Edition contains over 60,000 translator notes that document the textual, translational, and exegetical decisions behind every verse of the translation.

These notes fall into three categories: textual notes document manuscript evidence and explain why one reading was chosen over another; translational notes explain why a Hebrew or Greek phrase was rendered a particular way and what alternative renderings are possible; and exegetical notes provide background information, cross-references, and interpretive context needed to understand a passage.

The result is something no other widely-used Bible translation provides: a publicly visible record of the scholarly reasoning behind every translational decision. The NET Bible notes make the translation process transparent in a way that reveals how much interpretive work is embedded in every English rendering of the original text.

Why NET Notes in This Tool?

Most study Bible commentaries (including MacArthur’s) focus on what a passage means theologically. The NET notes focus on a prior question: what does the original text actually say, and how certain are we about that? They document manuscript variants, discuss the semantic range of Hebrew and Greek words, and flag where translators made judgment calls that other scholars might make differently.

This makes NET notes an ideal complement to theological commentary. When MacArthur says “this verse teaches X”, the NET note often reveals whether “this verse” is itself a matter of textual uncertainty, or whether the translation he is commenting on obscures an ambiguity present in the original. Having this critical layer visible makes the tool more honest about the interpretive process.

Scope and Limitation

The NET notes are strongest on textual criticism and translational transparency. They are not devotional, not pastoral, and not primarily theological — they are the annotations of a translation team explaining their work. They do not have the literary sensitivity of Alter, the Jewish theological depth of Sarna, or the doctrinal richness of Calvin. But they provide a critical baseline that all other commentary in this tool rests on: a clear account of what the Hebrew and Greek actually say, and where we are and are not certain about it.
