NT Historical-Critical Scholarship · Contemporary

Joel Marcus

b. 1951 · NT Scholar · Anchor Bible Commentator · Duke Divinity School

“Mark is not a simple, untheological Gospel. It is shot through with a rich and complex understanding of the significance of Jesus — an understanding that Mark himself has helped to create.”

— Joel Marcus, Mark 1–8, Anchor Bible (2000)

Biography

Joel Marcus is Professor of New Testament and Christian Origins at Duke Divinity School, where he has taught for over two decades. He studied at Columbia University and Union Theological Seminary, and his academic formation brought him into deep engagement with both Christian and Jewish interpretive traditions — a dual inheritance that shapes every page of his Markan commentary.

His two-volume Anchor Bible commentary on Mark — Mark 1–8 (2000) and Mark 8–16 (2009) — is widely regarded as the most comprehensive critical commentary on the Second Gospel in English. It synthesises over a century of Markan scholarship while making genuinely original contributions, particularly in the areas of Jewish background, Qumran parallels, Roman imperial context, and the Gospel’s use of the Old Testament.

Marcus brings to the text a rare combination: technical precision in Greek and Hebrew, encyclopaedic knowledge of Second Temple Judaism and its texts (Josephus, Philo, the Dead Sea Scrolls, Pseudepigrapha), and a keen sensitivity to the theological weight of Mark’s narrative. His commentary is the standard first port of call for serious Markan scholarship.

Interpretive Approach

Marcus works in the tradition of redaction criticism and historical-critical scholarship, but he is not reductively so. His primary question at each passage is: what does this text mean in its historical and literary context? That means attending simultaneously to the Greek text, the Old Testament allusions (in both Hebrew and LXX forms), the Jewish background, the Graeco-Roman social world, and the literary structure Mark has given his material.

His treatment of Jewish background is particularly distinctive. Where many NT commentators treat Second Temple Judaism as context rather than dialogue partner, Marcus reads Mark in genuine conversation with it — showing how the Qumran community applied Isaiah 40:3 to their own mission, how Markan exorcism language maps onto Jewish demonology, how the passion predictions echo specific Servant Song texts. The result is a portrait of a Gospel far more embedded in its Jewish matrix than earlier readings acknowledged.

Marcus is also sensitive to the Roman imperial context. His reading of “Legion” (5:9), the feeding of five thousand as a counter-imperial banquet, and the trial before Pilate as a collision of two kingdoms gives Mark a political edge that purely spiritual readings miss. Jesus’s proclamation of the kingdom is heard, on Marcus’s reading, as a direct challenge to Caesar’s claim.

His approach to Mark’s theology is that of a respectful but critical historian: he believes Mark is making genuine theological claims that deserve to be taken seriously, even when he cannot personally endorse them from a confessional standpoint. This makes his commentary unusually useful across a wide range of readers.

Theological Tradition

Marcus writes from within the mainstream of historical-critical NT scholarship. He is Jewish by background and reads the New Testament with a deep appreciation for its Jewish roots — but also with the critical distance of a scholar who does not approach the text as authoritative Scripture in the evangelical sense. This is precisely what makes him so valuable alongside commentators like MacArthur or Calvin: he asks questions that confessional commentators rarely do, and he hears Jewish resonances that Christian commentators often miss.

His work represents the best of what historical-critical scholarship offers: rigorous, honest, learned, and genuinely illuminating — without the reductionism that sometimes afflicts the tradition. He takes Mark’s theology seriously as theology, even while analysing its historical genesis.

Key Works

  • Mark 1–8, Anchor Bible Vol. 27 (2000)The first volume of his magnum opus. Comprehensive introduction to Mark’s authorship, date, sources, theology, and the text of the first eight chapters. Indispensable for serious Markan study.
  • Mark 8–16, Anchor Bible Vol. 27A (2009)Completes the commentary with the second half of Mark, including the most detailed treatment of the Passion narrative in any English-language Markan commentary.
  • The Way of the Lord: Christological Exegesis of the Old Testament in the Gospel of Mark (1992)Marcus’s doctoral research. Demonstrates how Mark systematically deploys OT texts to define Jesus’s identity, particularly through the new-exodus and Servant Song traditions.
  • Mark — A Short Commentary (2020)A more accessible one-volume commentary distilling his major work for students and general readers, without the full scholarly apparatus.
