Jewish · Critical Scholarship · 20th–21st Century

Jacob Milgrom

1923–2010 · Jewish Biblical Scholar · UC Berkeley · Anchor Bible Leviticus & Numbers

“The priestly literature is not a dry legal code. It is a sophisticated theological system in which every ritual enacts a theology of holiness, purity, and the sacred/profane boundary that separates the divine from the human.”

— — Jacob Milgrom, Leviticus 1–16 (Anchor Bible, 1991)

Biography

Jacob Milgrom was born in New York in 1923 and became one of the most prolific and influential scholars of the Hebrew Bible in the twentieth century. He taught for decades at the University of California, Berkeley, where he shaped generations of students in OT studies.

His magnum opus is the three-volume Anchor Bible Commentary on Leviticus (1991, 1999, 2001) — at over 2,700 pages the most comprehensive critical commentary on any biblical book in English. He also wrote the Anchor Bible Numbers (1990), extending the same rigorous method to the wilderness narratives.

Milgrom brought to the priestly texts a combination of deep knowledge of rabbinic and Second Temple literature, Ancient Near Eastern comparative religion, and rigorous philological method. Where earlier scholars had dismissed the levitical legislation as arid ritualism, Milgrom demonstrated that it encodes a coherent theology of holiness, pollution, and moral responsibility.

Interpretive Approach

Milgrom’s central contribution is his interpretation of the priestly purity system as a moral theology. In his reading, the major sources of ritual impurity in Leviticus — death, disease, genital discharge — all represent forces hostile to life. The tabernacle/temple is a sanctuary of life, and impurity pollutes it not through physical contamination but through moral-symbolic associations with the powers of death.

He argues that the sin offering (ḥaṭṭāʾt) functions not primarily to atone for the offerer but to purge the sanctuary itself from the impurity caused by Israel’s sins. This reconceptualisation of the sacrificial system has been enormously influential and remains debated.

On Numbers, Milgrom consistently illuminates the Priestly source’s literary artistry and theological intentions, showing how the wilderness narratives function as a theology of community, leadership, and covenant failure.

Theological Tradition

Milgrom writes from within the Jewish scholarly tradition, combining the philological rigour of the academic guild with deep familiarity with the Talmudic and medieval Jewish interpretive heritage. He does not read the priestly texts as Christian allegory but as documents of a living religious system whose logic and beauty deserve to be understood on their own terms.

Key Works

  • Leviticus 1–16 (Anchor Bible, 1991)The first and most influential volume — over 1,100 pages covering the sacrificial system and purity laws with unparalleled depth.
  • Leviticus 17–22 (Anchor Bible, 1999)The Holiness Code, arguing that its ethics anticipate and extend the priestly purity system into the moral domain.
  • Leviticus 23–27 (Anchor Bible, 2001)The festival calendar, jubilee, and concluding legislation.
  • Numbers (Anchor Bible, 1990)A comprehensive commentary on the wilderness narratives combining literary, historical, and theological analysis.
