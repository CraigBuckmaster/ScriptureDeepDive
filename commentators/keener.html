Evangelical · Socio-Historical NT Scholarship · Contemporary

Craig S. Keener

1960– · NT Scholar · Asbury Theological Seminary · Acts Commentary

“Luke wrote for a Greco-Roman audience that understood the conventions of ancient biography and historiography. To read Acts well, we must read it as Luke’s first readers would have — embedded in the literary, social, and religious world of the first-century Mediterranean.”

— — Craig Keener, Acts: An Exegetical Commentary (2012)

Biography

Craig Keener is Professor of Biblical Studies at Asbury Theological Seminary and one of the most prolific NT scholars of his generation. He has written commentaries on Matthew, John, Romans, Galatians, 1–2 Corinthians, Revelation, and his monumental four-volume Acts (Baker Academic, 2012–2015).

Keener is best known for his socio-historical method — bringing the full weight of Greco-Roman literary, social, and cultural background to illuminate NT texts. His commentaries are famously encyclopedic: the Acts set runs to nearly 5,000 pages and cites over 30,000 ancient sources.

He also wrote a landmark academic study, Miracles: The Credibility of the New Testament Accounts (Baker Academic, 2011), defending the historicity of miracle claims through cross-cultural and historical evidence.

Interpretive Approach

Keener’s primary interpretive tool is ancient context. He believes that the NT writers were sophisticated authors communicating to culturally embedded audiences, and that recovering that original context illuminates meaning that modern readers otherwise miss.

On Acts, he consistently demonstrates that Luke’s historical claims are both plausible and historically coherent against the backdrop of first-century Roman administration, Jewish diaspora practice, and Greco-Roman social conventions. He reads Acts as genuine historiography in the ancient sense — selective but not fabricated, rhetorical but not fictional.

His handling of the Pentecost narrative, Paul’s missionary journeys, and the trial narratives is particularly illuminating, drawing on papyri, inscriptions, and literary sources to establish the verisimilitude of Luke’s account.

Theological Tradition

Keener is a committed evangelical with a heart for cross-cultural mission. His scholarship is in service of the church, not merely the academy. He holds to the historical reliability of Acts and the NT broadly while engaging seriously with critical scholarship and the full range of ancient evidence.
