Reformed Theology · 16th Century

John Calvin

1509–1564 · Reformer & Theologian · Geneva · Pastor, Theologian, Biblical Commentator

“Nearly all the wisdom we possess, that is to say, true and sound wisdom, consists of two parts: the knowledge of God and of ourselves.”

— John Calvin, Institutes of the Christian Religion, I.1.1

Biography

John Calvin was born in Noyon, France in 1509 and trained as a lawyer before the Protestant Reformation redirected his intellectual energies toward theology and Scripture. His conversion to Protestantism, which he described as a “sudden conversion,” occurred sometime around 1533. By 1536 he had published the first edition of his Institutes of the Christian Religion — a systematic exposition of Protestant theology that, in its final 1559 form, stands as the greatest work of the Reformation.

After a period in Basel and Strasbourg, Calvin settled in Geneva in 1541, where he spent the rest of his life as the city’s preacher and theological voice. He preached through the entire Bible, often multiple times per week, and from those sermons grew his monumental series of biblical commentaries — covering the entire New Testament except Revelation, and most of the Old Testament.

Calvin died in 1564, having shaped Reformed theology in a way that still defines large portions of Presbyterianism, Congregationalism, and Reformed Baptist traditions worldwide. His commentaries were written in Latin and French and have been continuously in print since the 16th century.

Interpretive Approach

Calvin’s commentaries are distinguished by three principles that set him apart from both his medieval predecessors and many of his Protestant successors. First, brevitas et facilitas — brevity and clarity. Calvin deliberately rejected the encyclopedic method of medieval biblical commentary, which accumulated layers of citation and qualification. He wanted the reader to see the text, not a library about the text.

Second, the “accommodation” principle: God condescends to human capacity in the way Scripture speaks. When Moses describes the firmament or the size of the sun, he is not giving a cosmological treatise but a description suited to ordinary human perception. This hermeneutical move, which Calvin used consistently, anticipated later discussions of genre, phenomenological language, and the relationship between Scripture and natural science.

Third, the unity of the two Testaments. Calvin read the Old Testament not as a religion of works replacing by a religion of grace, but as the same covenant of grace expressed through different forms. Abraham was saved by faith in Christ, just as Paul was — the difference was in the clarity of the revelation, not the substance of the salvation. This gave his Old Testament commentary a christological depth that made him far more attentive to messianic themes than many modern historical-critical commentators.

Theological Tradition

Calvin is the founder of what became known as the Reformed or Calvinist tradition — a strand of Protestantism defined by its insistence on divine sovereignty in salvation (election, predestination), the supremacy of Scripture, the priesthood of all believers, and the radical sinfulness of human nature apart from grace. The Westminster Confession of Faith (1646), the Heidelberg Catechism (1563), and the Canons of Dort (1619) all represent the tradition Calvin founded, though none are simply identical with his own thought. His commentaries remain living documents in the Reformed community and are increasingly read by evangelicals of all traditions.

Key Works

  • Institutes of the Christian Religion (1536–1559)The systematic theological masterwork of the Reformation. Four books covering the knowledge of God, the knowledge of humanity, the way of salvation in Christ, and the external means of grace.
  • Commentaries on the Pentateuch (1554–1563)His commentary on Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy. The primary source for Calvin notes in this tool. Public domain.
  • Commentaries on the New Testament (1540–1556)Covering all NT books except Revelation. Still read as models of exegetical commentary — precise, pastoral, and theologically substantial.
  • Sermons on Genesis (1559–1561)123 sermons preached through Genesis in Geneva. More expansive and pastoral than the commentary, giving insight into how he translated exegesis into proclamation.
