Evangelical · Contemporary

John MacArthur

b. 1939 · Conservative Evangelical · Pastor-Scholar · Grace Community Church, Sun Valley CA

“The Bible is not a book about religion. It is a book about God and his redemption of fallen humanity — and every page, rightly read, points to Christ.”

— John MacArthur, The MacArthur Study Bible (Introduction)

Biography

John MacArthur was born in Los Angeles in 1939, the fourth generation of a pastoral family. He studied at Talbot Theological Seminary, where he was shaped by the grammatical-historical method of biblical interpretation — the conviction that the plain meaning of the text, read in its original linguistic and historical context, is the authoritative foundation for all theology and preaching.

Since 1969 he has served as pastor-teacher of Grace Community Church in Sun Valley, California, where his expository preaching through entire books of the Bible has shaped a generation of evangelical ministry. His radio broadcast “Grace to You” reaches millions worldwide. He founded The Master’s University and The Master’s Seminary, institutions committed to his convictions about biblical inerrancy and careful exegesis.

MacArthur belongs to the Reformed Baptist tradition, holding to the doctrines of grace (election, effectual calling, perseverance of the saints) while rejecting infant baptism and continuationism. His exegetical output is remarkable in its consistency and volume: over fifty years of consecutive expository preaching through the New Testament and significant portions of the Old Testament.

Interpretive Approach

MacArthur’s method is grammatical-historical exposition: identify the author’s intended meaning through careful attention to grammar, syntax, original language, and historical context, then apply that meaning to the contemporary reader. He is deeply suspicious of allegorical or spiritualising interpretations that bypass the text’s plain sense.

What makes MacArthur distinctive is his insistence on the theological weight of every passage. He consistently asks: what does this text teach about God, about human nature, about sin, about salvation? His commentary is not merely descriptive but confessional — he reads Scripture as a unified revelation of the God who saves. This gives his notes a doctrinal density that is sometimes absent from more academic commentaries.

His weaknesses are the predictable ones: he is less attentive to the literary artistry of the text than literary scholars, thinner on ancient Near Eastern background than Jewish scholars, and his application framework is resolutely evangelical-pastoral rather than historical-critical. But within his own method he is consistently rigorous and pastorally warm.

Theological Tradition

MacArthur stands in the line of Protestant scholasticism as mediated through 19th-century Princeton theology (Hodge, Warfield) and 20th-century fundamentalism. He holds to plenary verbal inspiration and inerrancy of Scripture, the five solas of the Reformation, and a dispensational premillennial eschatology. He is perhaps the most influential expository preacher in the English-speaking evangelical world of the late 20th and early 21st centuries.

Key Works

  • The MacArthur Study Bible (1997)Over 25,000 study notes covering the entire Bible. The primary source for notes in this tool. Notes are faithful paraphrases, not verbatim quotations.
  • The MacArthur New Testament Commentary (33 vols.)Verse-by-verse exposition of every NT book, developed from decades of expository preaching at Grace Community Church.
  • The Gospel According to Jesus (1988)His landmark treatment of Lordship salvation — the controversial argument that saving faith necessarily involves submission to Christ as Lord.
  • Charismatic Chaos (1992)His cessationist critique of the charismatic movement, arguing that miraculous gifts ceased with the apostolic era.
