Jeffrey H. Tigay

1941– · Jewish Biblical Scholar · University of Pennsylvania · JPS Torah Commentary Deuteronomy

“Deuteronomy presents itself as Moses’s final addresses to Israel. Whether or not one accepts the critical view that it was composed centuries later, the text must first be understood in its own terms — as covenant speech addressed to a people on the threshold of their inheritance.”

— — Jeffrey H. Tigay, Deuteronomy, JPS Torah Commentary (1996)

Biography

Jeffrey Tigay is the A. M. Ellis Professor Emeritus of Hebrew and Semitic Languages and Literature at the University of Pennsylvania, one of America’s most distinguished universities for biblical and ancient Near Eastern studies.

His 1996 JPS Torah Commentary on Deuteronomy is the definitive modern Jewish scholarly commentary on the book in English. It stands in the tradition established by Nahum Sarna’s Genesis and Exodus volumes: combining rigorous Hebrew philology, deep engagement with the rabbinic interpretive tradition, sensitivity to ancient Near Eastern context, and accessibility to the educated general reader.

Tigay has also written important academic studies on the literary development of the Gilgamesh epic and on the empirical basis of the documentary hypothesis, bringing a comparativist’s perspective to questions of biblical composition.

Interpretive Approach

Tigay’s approach combines the two great streams of Jewish biblical scholarship: the philological-historical tradition of the modern academy and the hermeneutical tradition of the Jewish community. He is alert to the text’s linguistic and historical dimensions while consistently asking what the text has meant to Jewish readers across the centuries.

His handling of the Deuteronomic law sections is particularly strong: he illuminates the legal logic of each statute, traces its relationship to the parallel legislation in Exodus and Leviticus, and draws on ancient Near Eastern legal texts for comparative illumination. His notes on the Shema, the Decalogue, and the firstfruits liturgy are among the finest treatments of these passages in any commentary.

On the authorship and composition of Deuteronomy, Tigay is frank about the critical consensus while remaining primarily interested in what the text means as it stands. He treats the question of Mosaic authorship with appropriate scholarly caution, noting where the evidence cuts in different directions.

Theological Tradition

Tigay writes from within the Jewish scholarly tradition, combining the rigour of the modern academy with the interpretive heritage of his community. His commentary is neither confessional in the evangelical sense nor dismissively critical — it occupies the productive middle ground of Jewish scholarly engagement with the Torah as both historical document and living religious text.
