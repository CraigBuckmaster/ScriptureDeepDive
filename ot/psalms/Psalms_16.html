Psalms 16

The Path of Life

Verses 1–6 — “Apart from You I Have No Good Thing”
123456

Hebrew Word Study

מִכְתָּם mik̅tām — miktam / inscription / golden poem: A miktam of David. The term appears in six psalm titles (16, 56–60). Its meaning is uncertain — possibly "inscription" or "golden poem." Psalm 16 is the most important because of its resurrection content.

Context

David takes refuge in God (v.1), declares that apart from God he has no good (v.2), delights in the saints (v.3), and rejects other gods (v.4). "LORD, you alone are my portion and my cup; you make my lot secure" (v.5). The ḥēleq (portion) language echoes Ecclesiastes: God is the portion that death cannot take.

Cross-Reference

  • Phil 3:8"I consider everything a loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ" — Paul’s version of "apart from you I have no good thing."
  • Lam 3:24"The LORD is my portion; therefore I will wait for him" — the identical ḥēleq theology.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:2

"Apart from you I have no good thing." Total dependence on God as the source of all good.

16:5–6

"LORD, you are my portion and my cup." The inheritance language: other tribes received land; Levi received God (Num 18:20). David claims the Levitical privilege: God himself is enough.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:2

Alter: "I have no good apart from you" is either "my good is not beyond you" or "you are my only good." Either reading: total sufficiency in God.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:5

Calvin: the portion theology is the heart of contentment. Calvin: whoever has God has everything; whoever lacks God has nothing, regardless of possessions.

16:5

The NET: ḥēleq (portion) + kōs (cup) + gōrāl (lot) — three inheritance terms stacked. The emphasis is unmistakable: God is David’s entire inheritance.

Kidner Notes

kidner
16:5

Kidner: "my portion" is Levitical language. The Levites received no land inheritance; God was their inheritance. David claims this for himself — God above all possessions.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
16:1–6

VanGemeren: the psalm’s first half establishes exclusive devotion. Only God is David’s source. This prepares for the resurrection declaration in the second half.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
16:5

Goldingay: "you make my lot secure" — the lot-casting language of land distribution (Josh 14–19). David’s lot is not territory but the LORD himself.

Verses 7–11 — “You Will Not Abandon Me to the Realm of the Dead”
7891011

Hebrew Word Study

שְׁאוֹל shəʾōl — Sheol / the grave / the realm of the dead: "You will not abandon me to the realm of the dead (shəʾōl), nor will you let your faithful one see decay" (v.10). The resurrection verse. Peter quotes this at Pentecost (Acts 2:25–28): David’s body DID see decay, so the psalm prophesies Christ’s resurrection. Paul repeats the argument (Acts 13:35–37). This is one of the OT’s clearest resurrection texts.

נְתִיב nətîb — path: "You make known to me the path (nətîb) of life" (v.11). The "path of life" leads through death to resurrection. Not avoidance of death but passage through it. "You will fill me with joy in your presence, with eternal pleasures at your right hand." Joy, presence, eternity — the psalm’s final three words describe heaven.

Context

David’s body rests secure (v.9), his soul will not be abandoned to Sheol (v.10), his flesh will not decay (v.10). God will show him the path of life — fullness of joy, eternal pleasures at God’s right hand (v.11). Peter’s Pentecost sermon (Acts 2) and Paul’s Antioch sermon (Acts 13) both identify this as Christ’s resurrection prophecy: David’s body decayed, but the one David prophesied about did not.

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 2:25–28Peter quotes Psalm 16:8–11 at Pentecost: "You will not let your Holy One see decay" — applied to Christ’s resurrection.
  • Acts 13:35–37Paul quotes Psalm 16:10 in Antioch: David died and decayed; Jesus did not. The psalm is prophetic.
  • 1 Cor 15:54–55"Death has been swallowed up in victory" — the resurrection the psalm anticipates.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:9–10

"My body also will rest secure, because you will not abandon me to the realm of the dead." The resurrection hope: not mere survival but bodily preservation through death.

16:11

"In your presence there is fullness of joy; at your right hand are pleasures forevermore." The psalm’s climax: eternal joy in God’s presence. Not temporary comfort but everlasting pleasure.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:10

Alter: "let your faithful one see the pit" — shaḥat (pit/decay) is the grave’s consuming reality. The psalm promises exemption from corruption’s final word.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:10

Calvin: David speaks beyond himself. Calvin: the Spirit inspired words that David’s own experience could not fulfil. The psalm’s true subject is the Son who conquered death.

16:11

Calvin: "fullness of joy" — not partial, not mixed, not temporary. Full, unmixed, eternal. This is the beatific vision promised in Psalm 11:7, now linked to resurrection.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:10

The NET: the key interpretive question: does "faithful one" (hasîdək̅ā) refer to David or to a future figure? The NT’s consistent answer: ultimately Christ.

16:11

The NET: "pleasures" (nəʿîmōṭ) is the plural of delight/pleasantness. At God’s right hand, there are not just pleasures but pleasures upon pleasures — an inexhaustible supply.

Kidner Notes

kidner
16:10

Kidner: "your faithful one" (hasîd) is David, but supremely Christ. David’s body decayed; Christ’s did not. The psalm transcends its original speaker.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
16:10–11

VanGemeren: this is the Psalter’s most significant resurrection text. The NT apostles treated it as foundational proof of Christ’s bodily resurrection.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
16:11

Goldingay: "eternal pleasures at your right hand" — the "right hand" is the place of honour and power. Eternal pleasure is not idle enjoyment but dignified participation in God’s presence.
