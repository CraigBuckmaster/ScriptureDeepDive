Psalms 11

When Foundations Are Destroyed

Verses 1–3 — “In the LORD I Take Refuge”
123

Hebrew Word Study

חָסִיתִי ḥāsîṭî — I have taken refuge: "In the LORD I take refuge (ḥāsîṭî). How then can you say to me: “Flee like a bird to your mountain”?" (v.1). David rejects the counsel to flee. His refuge is not geography but God. When others advise escape, faith stands.

Context

Advisors tell David to flee — the wicked bend their bows, the foundations are being destroyed (v.3). David’s response: my refuge is the LORD, not the mountains. The question of v.3 is the psalm’s crisis: "When the foundations are being destroyed, what can the righteous do?" The answer is in the second half.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 46:1–2"God is our refuge... though the earth give way" — the same stand-firm theology.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:1

"In the LORD I take refuge." Faith’s first word is not analysis but declaration.

11:3

"When the foundations are being destroyed, what can the righteous do?" The defining question of social collapse. Answer: look to God’s throne (v.4).

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:1

Alter: "how then can you say?" is indignant rejection. The advisors mean well; David dismisses them for offering human solutions to a divine problem.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:3

Calvin: when foundations crumble, the righteous must remember that God’s throne is unshaken. Calvin: the question is answered not by action but by theology.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:1

The NET: ḥāsîṭî is a completed action — "I HAVE taken refuge." The decision is already made before the psalm begins.

Kidner Notes

Kidner: "foundations" are the moral and social order. When institutions collapse, only God’s throne remains. The psalm redirects from horizontal chaos to vertical stability.

Vangemeren Notes

VanGemeren: the psalm responds to well-meaning but faithless counsel. "Flee" sounds practical; but God is the only real refuge.

Verses 4–7 — “The LORD Is in His Holy Temple”
4567

Hebrew Word Study

כִסְאוֹ kisʾō — his throne: "The LORD is in his holy temple; the LORD is on his heavenly throne (kisʾō)" (v.4). The answer to v.3: God’s throne endures when human foundations collapse. He observes, tests, and judges. The righteous see his face (v.7).

Context

God’s throne is the answer to crumbling foundations. He observes humanity (v.4), examines the righteous (v.5), hates violence (v.5), rains fire on the wicked (v.6). The psalm closes: "the upright will see his face" (v.7) — the ultimate reward: the beatific vision.

Cross-Reference

  • Hab 2:20"The LORD is in his holy temple; let all the earth be silent" — the same temple-throne confidence.
  • Rev 4:2"There before me was a throne in heaven" — the eternal throne.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:4

"The LORD is on his heavenly throne." When earthly thrones fall, heaven’s throne holds. This is the psalm’s answer.

11:7

"The upright will see his face." The ultimate promise: not just protection but presence. To see God’s face is the highest reward.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:6

Alter: "rain fire and brimstone" echoes Sodom’s destruction (Gen 19:24). God’s judgment on the violent follows the Sodom pattern.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:7

Calvin: seeing God’s face is the reward that outweighs every trial. Calvin: "in your presence there is fullness of joy" (Ps 16:11). The face of God is the destination of faith.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:4

The NET: the dual location — "holy temple" (earthly) and "heavenly throne" (cosmic) — merges the two. God is present in the temple AND reigning from heaven simultaneously.

11:7

The NET: "see his face" uses the verb ḥāzâ (behold, gaze). The vision is sustained, not momentary.

Kidner Notes

kidner
11:7

Kidner: "see his face" is the beatific vision — the goal of all faith. Exile from God’s presence is the worst punishment; seeing his face is the highest reward.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
11:4–7

VanGemeren: the shift from earth (vv.1–3) to heaven (vv.4–7) reframes the crisis. From below, foundations crumble. From above, the throne stands.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
11:7

Goldingay: "see his face" echoes the temple pilgrimage tradition. To "see God’s face" was to enter the sanctuary. The psalm promises the permanent version.
