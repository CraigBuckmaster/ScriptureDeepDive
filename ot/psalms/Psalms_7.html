Psalms 7

A Plea for Justice

Verses 1–9 — “Save and Deliver Me”
123456789

Hebrew Word Study

שֹׁפֵט shōfēṭ — judge: "Let the LORD judge (shōfēṭ) the peoples. Vindicate me, LORD, according to my righteousness" (v.8). David appeals to God as judge. His defence: integrity. His request: fair trial. The psalm combines lament with legal vocabulary — the courtroom in prayer.

Context

David asks for rescue from pursuers (vv.1–2), protests his innocence with a self-imprecation (vv.3–5: if I have done wrong, let the enemy destroy me), and calls God to arise in anger against the rage of enemies (vv.6–7). The appeal: judge me according to my righteousness and integrity (vv.8–9). Like Job, David demands a fair hearing.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 26:1"Vindicate me, LORD, for I have led a blameless life" — the same innocence claim.
  • Rom 12:19"Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord" — the principle David enacts by appealing to God rather than retaliating.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1–2

"LORD my God, I take refuge in you; save and deliver me from all who pursue me." Refuge is the first word; rescue is the request.

7:3–5

"If I have done this... let my enemy pursue me and overtake me." The self-imprecation: David invites destruction if he is guilty. This is the oath of innocence — the same legal instrument Job used (Job 31).

7:8–9

"Vindicate me, LORD, according to my righteousness." David appeals to divine justice, not divine mercy. He believes his case will stand.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:3–5

Alter: the self-imprecation is conditionally structured: "if I have done this... then let the enemy..." The grammar mirrors legal oaths.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:8

Calvin: David appeals to righteousness, not sinlessness. Calvin: in this specific case, he is innocent. He does not claim universal perfection.

Verses 10–17 — “God Is a Righteous Judge”
1011121314151617

Hebrew Word Study

שַׁחַת shaḥat — pit: "Whoever digs a hole (shaḥat) and scoops it out falls into the pit they have made" (v.15). The boomerang principle: evil returns to its sender. The one who digs a trap falls into it. The mischief intended for others lands on the schemer’s own head. Justice is built into the moral structure of reality.

Context

God is described as a righteous judge who displays wrath every day (v.11). If the wicked do not repent, God sharpens his sword, bends his bow, prepares deadly weapons (vv.12–13). Then the boomerang: the wicked conceive trouble, become pregnant with mischief, give birth to disillusionment (v.14). They dig a pit and fall into it (v.15). Their violence returns on their own heads (v.16). David closes with thanksgiving: "I will give thanks to the LORD because of his righteousness" (v.17).

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 26:27"Whoever digs a pit will fall into it" — the same boomerang proverb.
  • Esth 7:10Haman hanged on the gallows he built for Mordecai — the narrative enactment of the principle.
  • Gal 6:7"A man reaps what he sows" — Paul’s statement of the same moral law.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:11–13

"God is a righteous judge, a God who displays his wrath every day. If he does not relent, he will sharpen his sword." Divine patience has a limit. The imagery is military: God arms himself against the unrepentant.

7:14–16

"Whoever is pregnant with evil conceives trouble and gives birth to disillusionment. They dig a pit and fall into it." Evil’s self-destructive nature. The boomerang principle.

7:17

"I will give thanks to the LORD because of his righteousness." The psalm ends in worship, not vengeance. David’s trust in divine justice produces gratitude.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:14

Alter: the pregnancy-to-birth metaphor tracks evil through its full gestation. The mother of evil is also its victim.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:15–16

Calvin: the boomerang principle is God’s active justice, not mere karma. Calvin: God directs the recoil. The pit does not merely exist; God ensures the digger falls in.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:15

The NET: shaḥat (pit) is the same word used in Proverbs 26:27. The wisdom tradition and the psalm tradition share this justice metaphor.

7:17

The NET: the psalm closes with praise — the expected resolution of a lament. The structure: plea → trust → praise.

Kidner Notes

kidner
7:14–16

Kidner: the pregnancy metaphor is vivid: evil is conceived, carried, and delivered — but the birth produces disillusionment, not triumph. The schemer is the first victim.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
7:11–17

VanGemeren: the boomerang principle is not magical but theological: God ensures that evil recoils. The moral universe is not neutral but actively just.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
7:15

Goldingay: the pit image is one of the Psalter’s most common justice images. The trap-setter is caught in their own trap. God’s justice uses the enemy’s own weapons.
