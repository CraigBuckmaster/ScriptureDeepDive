Psalms 20

May the LORD Answer You

Verses 1–5 — “May He Send You Help from the Sanctuary”
12345

Hebrew Word Study

יַעֲנְךָ yaʿănek̅ā — may he answer you: "May the LORD answer you (yaʿănek̅ā) when you are in distress" (v.1). A pre-battle blessing. The congregation prays for the king before he goes to war: may God answer, protect, send help, remember sacrifices, grant desires. The psalm is communal intercession for royal success.

Context

A liturgy of pre-battle intercession. The congregation blesses the king: may the LORD answer you (v.1), protect you (v.1), send help from the sanctuary (v.2), remember your sacrifices (v.3), give you your heart’s desire (v.4). "May we shout for joy over your victory and lift up our banners in the name of our God" (v.5).

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Sam 13:9–12Saul’s pre-battle sacrifice — the ritual context the psalm assumes.
  • Eph 6:18–20"Pray in the Spirit on all occasions" — the NT’s spiritual warfare parallel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
20:1–2

"May the LORD answer you in the day of distress. May the God of Jacob protect you. May he send you help from the sanctuary." Three "may" petitions for the king.

20:4–5

"May he give you the desire of your heart. May we shout for joy over your victory." The congregation’s prayer is for shared triumph: your victory is our celebration.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:1–3

Alter: the five "may he" petitions create a liturgical rhythm — each building on the last. Answer → protect → send help → remember → grant. The arc is from hearing to fulfilment.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:1–5

Calvin: the congregation prays for the king because his success is their survival. Calvin: praying for leaders is not optional but strategic — their battles are ours.

The NET: "help from the sanctuary" means divine aid from Zion, where the ark of the covenant resided. The temple is the source of military aid — God fights from his throne.

Verses 6–9 — “Some Trust in Chariots; We Trust in the Name of the LORD”
6789

Hebrew Word Study

שֵׁם shēm — name: "Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name (shēm) of the LORD our God" (v.7). The psalm’s most famous verse and its theological centre. Military technology vs divine name. Chariots fall; the name stands. The "name" is God’s character, reputation, and power — the sum of who he is.

Context

Confidence declared: "Now this I know: the LORD gives victory to his anointed" (v.6). The pivotal verse: some trust in chariots and horses, but we trust in the name of the LORD (v.7). The result: "they are brought to their knees and fall, but we rise up and stand firm" (v.8). The psalm closes with a final petition: "LORD, give victory to the king! Answer us when we call!" (v.9).

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 31:1"Woe to those who go down to Egypt for help, who rely on horses" — the prophetic development of the same trust theology.
  • 2 Chr 20:15"The battle is not yours, but God’s" — the same principle in narrative form.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
20:6

"Now this I know: the LORD gives victory to his anointed." The shift from petition to declaration. Faith speaks in the present tense: I KNOW.

20:7

"Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the LORD our God." The defining contrast. Technology vs theology. Hardware vs the name.

20:8

"They are brought to their knees and fall, but we rise up and stand firm." The result of trust: the militarily superior collapse; the theologically dependent stand.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:7–8

Alter: the contrast is cinematic: chariots and horses vs a name. The visual (armies) against the invisible (a word). The invisible wins.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:7

Calvin: the choice between chariots and God’s name is the choice every generation faces. Calvin: whatever functions as your ultimate security IS your god. The psalm asks: is it technology or the LORD?

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:7

The NET: "trust" (zāk̅ar) literally means "invoke/remember." Some invoke chariots; we invoke the LORD’s name. The battle is won by what you call upon.

20:8

The NET: "brought to their knees and fall" vs "rise up and stand firm" — two opposite trajectories determined by one choice: where trust was placed.

Kidner Notes

kidner
20:7

Kidner: chariots were the supreme military technology — the tanks of the ancient world. Trusting the name over chariots is trusting the invisible over the overwhelming visible.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
20:6–9

VanGemeren: the psalm’s second half shifts from prayer to proclamation. The congregation has moved from asking to declaring. Prayer produced confidence.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
20:7

Goldingay: "the name" is everything God has revealed about himself. To trust the name is to trust the character behind it. The name is not a magic word but a personal reality.
