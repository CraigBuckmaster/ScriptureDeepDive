Psalms 22

My God, My God, Why Have You Forsaken Me?

Verses 1–18 — “Forsaken” — The Suffering Servant
123456789101112131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

עֲזַבְתָנִי ʿăzabtānî — you have forsaken me: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me (ʿăzabtānî)?" (v.1). Jesus quotes this from the cross (Matt 27:46). The most christological psalm in the Psalter. Every detail of the crucifixion is anticipated: scorned and despised (v.6), mocked by those who shake their heads (v.7), "he trusts in the LORD — let the LORD rescue him" (v.8), bulls of Bashan surround (v.12), poured out like water, bones out of joint (v.14), heart like wax (v.14), strength dried up (v.15), tongue sticks to the roof of the mouth (v.15), pierced hands and feet (v.16), they divide garments and cast lots for clothing (v.18).

כָּרוּ kārū — pierced / dug: "They pierced (kārū) my hands and my feet" (v.16). The most debated word in the psalm. The Masoretic text reads "like a lion" (kaʾari); the LXX and Dead Sea Scrolls support "they pierced" (kārū). The Christian tradition reads "pierced" as a crucifixion prophecy.

Context

David’s lament begins with the cry Jesus made his own. The sufferer is scorned (v.6), mocked (vv.7–8), surrounded by enemies described as bulls, lions, and dogs (vv.12–16). The physical suffering is detailed with medical precision: dehydration (v.15), skeletal visibility (v.17), pierced extremities (v.16). Garments divided, lots cast (v.18). Every element was fulfilled at Calvary.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:1

"My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" Jesus’ cry from the cross. The ultimate forsakenness: God-from-God, separated by sin-bearing.

22:6–8

"I am a worm and not a man, scorned by everyone. All who see me mock me: ‘He trusts in the LORD — let the LORD rescue him.’" The mockery at Calvary scripted 1,000 years beforehand.

22:14–18

"I am poured out like water; all my bones are out of joint; my heart has turned to wax. My strength is dried up. They pierce my hands and my feet. They divide my clothes and cast lots for my garment." The crucifixion in detail — written centuries before crucifixion was invented.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:14–18

Alter: the body-language of suffering is forensically precise: dislocated joints, melted heart, parched throat, pierced limbs. The poem forces the reader to witness what the sufferer endures.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:1

Calvin: Christ’s use of this psalm from the cross proves its prophetic nature. Calvin: David’s suffering was real but Christ’s was the ultimate fulfilment. The type points to the antitype.

22:16–18

Calvin: the lot-casting for garments fulfils the psalm with mathematical precision. Calvin: Scripture does not merely predict; it scripts.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:1

The NET: ʿăzabtānî (you have forsaken me) is a strong word for abandonment. The same verb describes a husband leaving a wife (Isa 54:6) or a city left desolate (Isa 62:4).

22:16

The NET discusses the textual debate: MT "like a lion" (kaʾari) vs "they pierced" (kārū). The Dead Sea Scrolls (Nahal Hever) support "pierced." Most Christian translations follow this reading.

Kidner Notes

kidner
22:1

Kidner: Jesus did not merely quote this psalm; he entered it. The forsakenness was real, not theatrical. The psalm became biography.

22:16–18

Kidner: crucifixion was not practised in David’s era. The description of pierced extremities, visible bones, and divided garments transcends any experience David could have had. The Spirit spoke beyond the speaker.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
22:1–18

VanGemeren: Psalm 22 is the Psalter’s most sustained christological text. The NT applies it to Christ more comprehensively than any other psalm. The suffering described is both David’s and, supremely, Christ’s.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
22:1

Goldingay: the opening cry is not despair but prayer. David addresses God twice: "my God, my God." The relationship persists through the forsakenness. You can only be forsaken by someone you belong to.

Verses 19–31 — “All the Ends of the Earth Will Turn to the LORD”
19202122232425262728293031

Hebrew Word Study

סִפְרוּ sipprū — they will proclaim / they will tell: "They will proclaim (sipprū) his righteousness, declaring to a people yet unborn: He has done it!" (v.31). The psalm’s last words: "he has done it" — in Hebrew a single word that parallels Jesus’ "it is finished" (John 19:30). The work is complete. The suffering produces universal proclamation.

Context

The psalm pivots from lament to praise. David asks for rescue (vv.19–21), then declares: "I will declare your name to my people; in the assembly I will praise you" (v.22 — quoted in Heb 2:12). The vision expands: all the families of nations will worship (vv.27–28), future generations will be told about the Lord (v.30), and the psalm closes with "he has done it!" (v.31) — the OT’s "it is finished."

Cross-Reference

  • John 19:30"It is finished" (tetelestai) — parallels "he has done it!" in Psalm 22:31.
  • Heb 2:12"I will declare your name to my brothers" — quoting Psalm 22:22 for Christ’s identification with humanity.
  • Rev 7:9–10"A great multitude from every nation" — the fulfilment of "all the ends of the earth will turn to the LORD."
  • Phil 2:10–11"Every knee should bow" — the cosmic worship Psalm 22:27–29 anticipates.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:22

"I will declare your name to my people." Hebrews 2:12 applies this to Christ: after the suffering comes proclamation. The one who was forsaken becomes the preacher.

22:27–28

"All the ends of the earth will remember and turn to the LORD, and all the families of the nations will bow down before him, for dominion belongs to the LORD." The vision: universal worship. From one man’s suffering to all nations’ salvation.

22:31

"He has done it!" The psalm’s final declaration. The work is complete. The parallels to "it is finished" (John 19:30) are unmistakable.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:22–31

Alter: the pivot from agony to praise is abrupt — no buffer, no gradual recovery. One moment forsaken, the next proclaiming. The literary structure mimics the theology: death to life without mediation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:27–31

Calvin: the universal worship vision proves the psalm’s scope exceeds David. Calvin: no event in David’s life produced worldwide worship. Only the cross did that.

22:31

Calvin: "he has done it" is the gospel in one word. Calvin: the entire plan of salvation — from eternal decree to Calvary — is summarised in this single declaration of completion.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:22

The NET: the shift from lament to praise is marked by "I will declare" — the vow of praise, common in lament psalms, where the sufferer promises to testify after rescue.

22:31

The NET: ʿasâ (he has done it) is often compared to Jesus’ tetelestai (it is finished) in John 19:30. Both declare completed work.

Kidner Notes

kidner
22:22–31

Kidner: the shift from suffering to praise is the psalm’s most remarkable feature. There is no transition marker — suffering suddenly becomes proclamation. This mirrors the cross: death becomes resurrection without explanation, only event.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
22:27–31

VanGemeren: the universal vision (all nations worship) connects to Psalm 2’s promise (nations as the Son’s inheritance). The messianic psalms form a network: suffering (Ps 22) fulfils enthronement (Ps 2).

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
22:31

Goldingay: "he has done it" is one word in Hebrew: ʿasâ. Completed action. The work is finished. The declaration is as terse as it is monumental.
