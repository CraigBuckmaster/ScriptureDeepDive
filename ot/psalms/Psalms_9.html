Psalms 9

Praise for Justice

Verses 1–10 — “I Will Praise You with All My Heart”
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

מִשְׂגָּב misgab — refuge / high tower / stronghold: "The LORD is a refuge (misgab) for the oppressed, a stronghold in times of trouble" (v.9). The psalm’s theological centre: God is a high place for the low. The misgab is an elevated fortress — unreachable by enemies. God lifts the oppressed above the flood.

Context

David praises God with all his heart (v.1), tells of God’s wonders (v.1), and celebrates the defeat of enemies (vv.3–5). God sits enthroned as righteous judge (v.7), judging the world with equity (v.8). For the oppressed, God is a refuge (v.9). Those who know God’s name trust him; he does not forsake those who seek him (v.10).

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 46:1"God is our refuge and strength, an ever-present help in trouble" — the same refuge theology.
  • Nahum 1:7"The LORD is good, a refuge in times of trouble" — the prophetic parallel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:1–2

"I will give thanks to you, LORD, with all my heart; I will tell of all your wonderful deeds." Wholehearted praise. Not partial, not reserved. The "all" is emphatic.

9:7–8

"The LORD reigns forever; he has established his throne for judgment. He rules the world in righteousness." God’s throne is judicial. He reigns BY judging fairly.

9:9–10

"The LORD is a refuge for the oppressed. Those who know your name trust in you." Knowing God’s name = knowing God’s character. Trust follows knowledge.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:9

Alter: misgab (high fortress) is the opposite of the pit the wicked dig. God’s people are elevated; the wicked are sunk.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:10

Calvin: "you, LORD, have never forsaken those who seek you" — Calvin: this is not a promise of ease but of presence. God’s faithfulness is to people, not to circumstances.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:9

The NET: misgab means an inaccessible height, a place enemies cannot reach. The oppressed person is elevated above the threatening flood.

9:10

The NET: "know your name" is relational knowledge, not informational. In the ANE, knowing a name meant knowing the person’s character and having access to them.

Kidner Notes

kidner
9:9–10

Kidner: the twin assurances: God is a refuge for the oppressed (v.9) and does not forsake those who seek him (v.10). Safety and faithfulness combined.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
9:7–10

VanGemeren: God’s throne for judgment is the hope of the oppressed. Where human courts fail, divine judgment endures. The psalm is eschatological as much as historical.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
9:10

Goldingay: "those who know your name trust you" — the sequence is: know → trust. Trust is not blind; it is informed by character. You trust those you know.

Verses 11–20 — “Do Not Forget the Helpless”
11121314151617181920

Hebrew Word Study

עֲנָוִים ʿănāwîm — afflicted / humble / poor: "He does not ignore the cry of the afflicted (ʿănāwîm)" (v.12). God’s attentiveness to the poor is not occasional but constitutional. He DOES NOT IGNORE. The double negative is emphatic: there is no circumstance in which God overlooks the afflicted person’s cry.

Context

David calls the people to sing praises (v.11), proclaim God’s deeds (v.11), because God avenges blood and remembers the afflicted (v.12). He asks for mercy from enemies who are at the gates of death (vv.13–14). The nations have fallen into their own pit; their foot is caught in their own net (v.15). The boomerang principle again. The psalm closes with a plea: "Arise, LORD, do not let mortals triumph. Let the nations know they are only mortals" (vv.19–20). The final word: human beings are ʾĕnōsh — frail mortals.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 25:4"You have been a refuge for the poor, a shelter from the storm" — the prophetic development of God as refuge for the poor.
  • Luke 1:52–53"He has brought down rulers from their thrones but has lifted up the humble" — Mary’s Magnificat echoes the psalm’s justice theology.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:12

"He who avenges blood remembers; he does not ignore the cry of the afflicted." God’s memory is active justice. He does not forget victims.

9:15–16

"The nations have fallen into the pit they have dug; their feet are caught in the net they have hidden." The boomerang principle from Psalm 7 returns.

9:19–20

"Arise, LORD, do not let mortals triumph. Let the nations know they are only mortals." The psalm ends with the humbling of nations. The Hebrew for "mortals" is ʾĕnōsh — the same word from Psalm 8:4. Humans are frail; only God is eternal.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:19–20

Alter: "let the nations know they are only ʾĕnōsh" — the word carries built-in frailty. Nations that imagine themselves divine are merely mortal. The word is the rebuke.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:12

Calvin: God’s memory of the afflicted is the counterweight to Psalm 10’s question "why do you stand far off?" Calvin: God remembers even when he appears absent. His memory is always working toward justice.

9:20

Calvin: "let them know they are only mortals" — Calvin: the greatest lie any nation believes is that it is immortal. Every empire that forgot its mortality has fallen.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:15

The NET: "the pit they have dug" (shaḥat) uses the same word as Psalm 7:15. The justice image is standardised across the Psalter.

9:20

The NET: ʾĕnōsh here emphasises mortality and weakness — the same word used in Psalm 8:4 for humanity’s smallness before God. Context determines tone: in Psalm 8, it produces wonder; here, it deflates arrogance.

Kidner Notes

kidner
9:15–16

Kidner: the nations falling into their own trap is divine justice using the enemy’s own tools. God does not need new weapons; he redirects existing ones.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
9:19–20

VanGemeren: the closing plea puts nations in their place: they are ʾĕnōsh — mortal, fragile, limited. The psalm deflates imperial pretension.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
9:12

Goldingay: "does not ignore" is powerful precisely because ignoring is what powerful people do to the powerless. God refuses to look away.
