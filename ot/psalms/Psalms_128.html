Psalms 128

Blessed Are All Who Fear the LORD

Verses 1–4 — “You Will Eat the Fruit of Your Labour”
1234

Hebrew Word Study

יְרֵא yērēʾ — to fear / to revere: "Blessed are all who fear (yērēʾ) the LORD, who walk in obedience to him. You will eat the fruit of your labour; blessings and prosperity will be yours" (vv.1–2). The domestic-blessing psalm. Fear of the LORD produces: fruitful labour (v.2), a wife like a fruitful vine (v.3), children like olive shoots (v.3). The home is a garden: vine around the table, olive shoots surrounding it. Fecundity as blessing.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
128:1–2

"Blessed are all who fear the LORD. You will eat the fruit of your labour." The blessing sequence: fear → obedience → fruitful labour → prosperity. Each step follows from the previous.

128:3

"Your wife will be like a fruitful vine within your house; your children will be like olive shoots around your table." The home as garden: vine (productive beauty), olive shoots (new growth). The domestic economy of blessing.

Verses 5–6 — “May You See the Prosperity of Jerusalem”
56

Hebrew Word Study

שָׁלוֹם shālōm — peace: "May you live to see your children’s children — peace (shālōm) be on Israel" (v.6). The blessing extends from home to nation. May you see Jerusalem’s prosperity all your days (v.5). May you see grandchildren (v.6). The pilgrimage blessing: personal domestic joy + national prosperity + intergenerational continuity. Shalom closes the psalm, as it closes so many Songs of Ascent.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
128:5–6

"May you see the prosperity of Jerusalem all the days of your life. May you live to see your children’s children." Three generations of blessing: you, your children, your grandchildren. And over it all: peace on Israel.

Moses↗ People
Prophet, lawgiver, and covenant mediator
Moses stands at the centre of the Pentateuch’s narrative — the one man who spoke with God face to face (Exod 33:11; Num 12:8) and mediated the Sinai covenant to Israel. In Leviticus he receives the priestly legislation; in Numbers he leads, intercedes, and ultimately fails at Meribah (Num 20). The writer of Hebrews calls him "faithful in all God’s house" (Heb 3:5) while distinguishing him from Christ, the Son over the house.
