Psalms 21

The King Rejoices in God’s Strength

Verses 1–7 — “You Have Granted Him His Heart’s Desire”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

עֹז ʿōz — strength / might: "The king rejoices in your strength (ʿōz), LORD" (v.1). A thanksgiving for answered prayer. Psalm 20 asked God to answer the king; Psalm 21 celebrates that he did. The pair: petition → fulfilment.

Context

The king’s thanksgiving for God’s blessings: answered prayers (v.2), rich blessings (v.3), crown of pure gold (v.3), life requested and granted (v.4), splendour and majesty (v.5), eternal blessings (v.6), gladness through God’s presence (v.7).

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 20The companion petition psalm. Ps 20 asks; Ps 21 thanks.
  • 2 Sam 12:30David takes the crown of the Ammonite king — the possible historical setting.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
21:1–2

"The king rejoices in your strength. You have granted him his heart’s desire." The answered prayer: Psalm 20’s petition fulfilled.

21:4–6

"He asked you for life, and you gave it to him — length of days, for ever and ever." The life-request goes beyond natural lifespan — "for ever and ever" points to resurrection hope.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:3

Alter: "a crown of pure gold" is both literal (royal crown) and metaphorical (divine favour as adornment).

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:4

Calvin: "length of days for ever and ever" transcends David. Calvin: the Davidic promises find their eternal fulfilment only in Christ’s unending reign.

NET Bible Notes

21:4

The NET: "for ever and ever" (ʿōlām wā-ʿed) is the strongest possible expression of enduring life. It points beyond any mortal king.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
21:1–7

VanGemeren: Psalm 21 is the thanksgiving that follows Psalm 20’s intercession. The liturgical pair teaches: pray before battle, give thanks after.

Goldingay Notes

Goldingay: "you have made him glad with the joy of your presence" — the king’s greatest blessing is not military victory but divine presence. Joy comes from God’s face, not from the battlefield.

Verses 8–13 — “Your Hand Will Lay Hold on All Your Enemies”
8910111213

Hebrew Word Study

אֵשׁ ʾēsh — fire: "You will make them like a blazing furnace (ʾēsh) when you appear" (v.9). The second half turns from thanksgiving to judgment on enemies. God’s presence is a furnace to the rebellious. The king’s enemies are consumed; the king’s God exalted.

Context

God’s right hand finds all enemies (v.8). They will be destroyed as in a fiery furnace (v.9), their posterity blotted out (v.10). Their plots fail because God sends them fleeing (vv.11–12). The psalm closes: "Be exalted in your strength, LORD; we will sing and praise your might" (v.13). From the king’s joy to the congregation’s worship.

Cross-Reference

  • Mal 4:1"The day is coming; it will burn like a furnace" — the same furnace-judgment imagery.
  • Rev 19:11–16The returning Christ who strikes down nations — the eschatological fulfilment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
21:9

"You will make them like a blazing furnace." God’s presence is fire to the rebellious. The enemies’ destruction is total.

21:13

"Be exalted in your strength, LORD." The psalm circles back to v.1 (the king rejoices in God’s strength). The frame is worship.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:9

Alter: the furnace image is theophanic — God’s appearance itself is the fire that consumes. Presence and judgment are the same event.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:13

Calvin: "be exalted in your strength" — the prayer for God’s strength to be visible. Calvin: God’s power is always real; the prayer is for it to be manifest.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:9

The NET: "like a blazing furnace" is literally "like a furnace of fire at the time of your face" — God’s face IS the fire. The theophany is the judgment.

Kidner Notes

kidner
21:13

Kidner: the psalm ends in corporate worship: "we will sing." The king’s personal thanksgiving becomes the nation’s hymn.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
21:8–13

VanGemeren: the judgment section is not vindictiveness but confidence in divine justice. The enemies plotted evil (v.11); God ensures their plots fail.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
21:13

Goldingay: "we will sing" shifts from the king’s voice to the community’s. The psalm’s ending is choral, not solo.
