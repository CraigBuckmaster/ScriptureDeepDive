Psalms 5

A Morning Prayer for Guidance

Verses 1–7 — “In the Morning I Lay My Requests Before You”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

בֹּקֶר bōqer — morning: "In the morning (bōqer), LORD, you hear my voice; in the morning I lay my requests before you and wait expectantly" (v.3). Morning prayer is intentional: David arranges his requests before God like a priest arranging the morning sacrifice on the altar. The word ʿārak (arrange/lay out) is the sacrificial term for arranging wood and offerings.

Context

David asks God to hear his words, consider his lament, listen to his cry (vv.1–2). He prays in the morning and waits (v.3). God takes no pleasure in wickedness (v.4); the arrogant cannot stand before God’s eyes (v.5). David will enter God’s house in reverence and awe (v.7).

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 55:17"Evening, morning and noon I cry out" — David’s threefold prayer habit.
  • Mark 1:35"Very early in the morning, Jesus got up and went to a solitary place to pray" — the pattern David established.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:3

"In the morning I lay my requests before you and wait expectantly." Morning prayer is not casual but structured. David arranges his prayers and then watches for God’s response.

5:4–6

"For you are not a God who is pleased with wickedness... the arrogant cannot stand in your presence." God’s holiness excludes evil. This is not merely preference but nature.

5:7

"But I, by your great love, can come into your house; in reverence I bow down toward your holy temple." Access to God’s presence is by grace ("your great love"), not by merit.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:3

Alter: "I array my words" — the prayer is composed, ordered, presented as an offering. Not spontaneous outpouring but deliberate arrangement.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:7

Calvin: "by your great love" is the access key. Calvin: no one enters God’s presence by right. Love (ḥesed) is the only door. This is OT grace.

Verses 8–12 — “Lead Me in Your Righteousness”
89101112

Hebrew Word Study

נְחֵנִי nəḥēnî — lead me / guide me: "Lead me (nəḥēnî), LORD, in your righteousness" (v.8). The request for guidance through enemy territory. David does not ask for information but for direction: make your way straight before me. The path is God’s righteousness, not David’s navigation.

Context

David asks for guidance (v.8), describes the enemies’ deceitful speech (vv.9–10: no truth in their mouths, throats like open graves), and calls for their judgement (v.10). Then the psalm brightens: "let all who take refuge in you be glad; let them ever sing for joy" (v.11). God spreads protection over them; those who love God’s name rejoice (v.11). The psalm closes with divine blessing: "you surround them with your favour as with a shield" (v.12).

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 3:13"Their throats are open graves" — Paul quotes Psalm 5:9 in his indictment of human sinfulness.
  • Ps 1:6"The LORD watches over the way of the righteous" — the same protective guidance.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:8

"Lead me in your righteousness because of my enemies — make your way straight before me." The request: not my way but YOUR way. Make it clear because the enemies make everything confusing.

5:11–12

"Let all who take refuge in you be glad... you surround them with your favour as with a shield." The psalm ends with joy and protection. The morning prayer concludes with confidence.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:9

Alter: "open grave" and "slippery tongue" are body-part images: throat, tongue. The enemies’ bodies are instruments of death disguised as speech.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:8

Calvin: "lead me in YOUR righteousness" — not mine. Calvin: the path is God’s; we walk in it. Our righteousness is derivative, not original.

5:12

Calvin: God’s favour as a shield — the shield is not a wall that blocks but a presence that surrounds. The protected person moves freely within divine favour.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:9

The NET: the "open grave" image is visceral: the enemies’ speech swallows and buries victims alive. Paul’s quotation in Rom 3:13 universalises the indictment.

5:12

The NET: ṣinnâ (shield) here is the large body-shield, different from the small māgēn of 3:3. God’s favour is the full-body shield.

Kidner Notes

kidner
5:9

Kidner: "their throat is an open grave" — speech that kills. The throat produces words that bury people. Paul applies this to all humanity (Rom 3:13).

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
5:11–12

VanGemeren: the psalm moves from petition to praise. The trajectory is common: ask → trust → rejoice. The morning prayer ends not in anxiety but in song.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
5:12

Goldingay: "surround them with favour as with a shield" echoes Psalm 3:3 (shield around me). God’s protection is comprehensive — surrounding, not merely frontal.
