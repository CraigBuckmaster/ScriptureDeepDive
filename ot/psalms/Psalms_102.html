Psalms 102

A Prayer of the Afflicted

Verses 1–11 — “My Days Vanish Like Smoke”
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

עָנִי ʿānî — afflicted / poor: "A prayer of an afflicted (ʿānî) person who has grown weak and pours out a lament before the LORD" (superscription). The fifth penitential psalm. Days vanish like smoke, bones burn (v.3). The psalmist is like an owl in the desert, a bird alone on a roof (vv.6–7). Enemies use his name as a curse (v.8). He eats ashes and mingles his drink with tears (v.9). "Because of your great wrath... you have thrown me aside" (v.10).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
102:1–2

"Hear my prayer, LORD. Do not hide your face from me when I am in distress. Turn your ear to me; when I call, answer me quickly." Four urgent requests in two verses.

102:3–7

"My days vanish like smoke; my bones burn like glowing embers. My heart is withered like grass. I am like a desert owl, like an owl among the ruins." The imagery: smoke, embers, withered grass, desert owls. Every image is of desolation.

102:11

"My days are like the evening shadow; I wither away like grass." The second grass image. Fading, lengthening, disappearing.

Verses 12–28 — “He Will Respond to the Prayer of the Destitute”
1213141516171819202122232425262728

Hebrew Word Study

לְדֹר וָדוֹר lədōr wā-dōr — from generation to generation / age after age: "But you, LORD, sit enthroned forever; your renown endures through all generations (lədōr wā-dōr)" (v.12). The pivot: from human transience to divine permanence. God will arise and have compassion on Zion (v.13). "He will respond to the prayer of the destitute" (v.17). The future nations will praise God (v.18). Hebrews 1:10–12 quotes vv.25–27 for Christ’s eternality: "In the beginning you laid the foundations of the earth... they will perish, but you remain."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
102:12–13

"But you, LORD, sit enthroned forever. You will arise and have compassion on Zion, for it is time to show favour to her." The "but you" pivot: human frailty vs divine permanence. The appointed time has come.

102:17–18

"He will respond to the prayer of the destitute; he will not despise their plea. Let this be written for a future generation, that a people not yet created may praise the LORD." The psalmist writes for the unborn. His suffering becomes the next generation’s testimony.

102:25–27

"In the beginning you laid the foundations of the earth; the heavens are the work of your hands. They will perish, but you remain. Like a garment they will be worn out, but you remain the same." Hebrews 1:10–12 applies this to Christ: the universe ages; God does not. Christ’s eternality proved from this psalm.
