Psalms 8

How Majestic Is Your Name

Verses 1–4 — “Out of the Mouth of Babes”
1234

Hebrew Word Study

אַדִּיר ʾaddîr — majestic / glorious / mighty: "LORD, our Lord, how majestic (ʾaddîr) is your name in all the earth!" (v.1). The psalm’s frame: it opens and closes with the same declaration. God’s name fills the earth. The majesty is not abstract but manifest — visible in creation, in children’s praise, in the heavens.

אֱנוֹשׁ ʾĕnōsh — mortal / frail human: "What is mankind (ʾĕnōsh) that you are mindful of them?" (v.4). The anthropological question. Against the backdrop of moon and stars, human beings are vanishingly small. Yet God "is mindful" of them — attends, remembers, cares. The wonder is not humanity’s greatness but God’s attention.

Context

The psalm moves from God’s cosmic majesty (v.1) to the paradox of divine attention to insignificant humanity (v.4). The heavens, moon, and stars are God’s finger-work (v.3). Against that scale, humans are specks. Yet God notices them. Children and infants praise God and silence the enemy (v.2). Jesus quotes this verse when children shout "Hosanna" in the temple (Matt 21:16).

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 21:15–16Jesus quotes Psalm 8:2 when the chief priests object to children’s praise in the temple.
  • Heb 2:6–8The author of Hebrews applies Psalm 8:4–6 to Christ: "you made him a little lower than the angels."
  • 1 Cor 15:27Paul quotes Psalm 8:6 for Christ’s ultimate authority: "God has put everything under his feet."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:1

"LORD, our Lord, how majestic is your name in all the earth!" The psalm’s frame. God’s majesty is not local but universal — "all the earth."

8:2

"Through the praise of children and infants you have established a stronghold against your enemies." The weakest voices silence the strongest enemies. God’s power works through weakness.

8:3–4

"When I consider your heavens, the work of your fingers, the moon and the stars... what is mankind that you are mindful of them?" Scale: the universe is God’s craft project. Against it, humans are incomprehensibly small. Yet God cares.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:3

Alter: "the work of your fingers" — not hands, fingers. The universe is God’s fine motor skill. What is immense to us is delicate to God.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:4

Calvin: the wonder is not that God MADE humanity but that God REMEMBERS humanity. Creation establishes existence; mindfulness establishes relationship. Calvin: to be known by the Creator is the highest dignity.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:1

The NET: ʾaddîr means splendid, magnificent, powerful. The root carries the sense of wide, expansive — God’s name fills the earth as water fills the sea.

8:4

The NET: "mindful" (zāk̅ar) means to remember actively, not merely recall. God’s remembering involves action: attending, caring, intervening.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
8:1–4

VanGemeren: Psalm 8 is a creation hymn — the Psalter’s meditation on Genesis 1–2. It celebrates both the grandeur of creation and the dignity of humanity within it.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
8:4

Goldingay: "what is ʾĕnōsh?" uses the word for frail, mortal humanity. Not ʾādām (humanity-as-dignity) but ʾĕnōsh (humanity-as-weakness). God attends to the weak.

Verses 5–9 — “Crowned with Glory and Honour”
56789

Hebrew Word Study

כָבוֹד kābōd — glory / weight / honour: "You crowned them with glory (kābōd) and honour" (v.5). Humanity’s position: a little lower than the angels (or: than God — the Hebrew ʾĕlōhîm can mean either), crowned with glory and honour, given dominion over creation. This is Genesis 1:26–28 in poetic form. Hebrews 2:6–8 applies it to Christ, who became "a little lower than the angels" in incarnation.

Context

The psalm’s second half answers its own question: what is mankind? Answer: crowned with glory (v.5), given dominion (v.6), rulers over animals (vv.7–8). This is the creation mandate (Gen 1:26–28) celebrated in worship. The psalm returns to its opening frame: "LORD, our Lord, how majestic is your name in all the earth!" (v.9). The humans who seemed insignificant (v.4) are revealed as God’s vice-regents (vv.5–8). Both truths coexist: we are small AND crowned.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 1:26–28"Let us make mankind in our image... let them rule" — the creation mandate Psalm 8 celebrates.
  • Heb 2:6–9"You made them a little lower than the angels; you crowned them with glory and honour... we see Jesus, who was made lower than the angels, now crowned with glory and honour" — the christological reading.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:5–6

"You have made them a little lower than the angels and crowned them with glory and honour. You made them rulers over the works of your hands." The creation mandate: human dignity is derived from divine delegation.

8:7–8

"All flocks and herds, and the animals of the wild, the birds in the sky, and the fish in the sea." Dominion extends over land, air, and sea — the three domains of creation.

8:9

"LORD, our Lord, how majestic is your name in all the earth!" The frame closes. The psalm begins and ends with God’s glory — human dignity exists within it, not above it.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:5

Alter: the ambiguity of ʾĕlōhîm (God or angels) is productive. If "God": humanity is almost divine. If "angels": humanity ranks just below the heavenly court. Either reading celebrates human dignity.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:5–6

Calvin: human dominion is delegated, not autonomous. We rule ON BEHALF OF God, not independently. Calvin: dominion without worship becomes tyranny. The psalm’s frame (God’s majesty) prevents dominion from becoming arrogance.

8:9

Calvin: the return to "how majestic" ensures the psalm’s anthropology stays theocentric. We are glorious because God crowned us. Remove God, and the crown falls.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:5

The NET: ʾĕlōhîm is translated "heavenly beings" (angels) by the NET, following the LXX which Hebrews quotes. The Hebrew could mean "God" (slightly lower than the divine) or "angels" (slightly lower than the heavenly court).

8:6

The NET: "put everything under their feet" uses the language of military conquest (Josh 10:24). Dominion is described in victor terms: the created order is "under foot."

Kidner Notes

kidner
8:5

Kidner: "a little lower than ʾĕlōhîm" — whether "God" or "angels," the point is extraordinary proximity. Humans are almost-divine in their delegated authority.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
8:5–9

VanGemeren: the christological reading (Heb 2) sees Jesus as the true human who fulfils what Adam failed to do: exercise dominion without rebellion. Psalm 8 is humanity’s job description; Christ is the only one who fills it completely.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
8:5

Goldingay: "crowned" is royal language. Every human is crowned by God — not with political power but with dignifying purpose. The imago Dei is a crown, not a title.
