Psalms 3

A Morning Prayer When Enemies Surround

Verses 1–4 — “LORD, How Many Are My Foes!”
1234

Hebrew Word Study

מָגֵן māgēn — shield: "But you, LORD, are a shield (māgēn) around me" (v.3). The first lament in the Psalter. David flees Absalom (superscription). Enemies multiply. But the pivot is "but you" — the turn from crisis to faith. God is shield, glory, and the one who lifts David’s head. The lament structure: complaint (vv.1–2), trust (vv.3–4), petition/confidence (vv.5–8).

Context

The superscription places this psalm during Absalom’s rebellion (2 Sam 15–18). Many say David has no deliverance (v.2). But David cries out to God, who answers from his holy mountain (v.4). The lament formula appears for the first time: situation → appeal → trust.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Sam 15:13–14David flees Jerusalem when Absalom seizes power — the historical context.
  • Ps 18:2"The LORD is my rock, my fortress and my deliverer" — the same shield/refuge language.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1–2

"LORD, how many are my foes! Many are saying, ‘God will not deliver him.’" The crisis is both physical (enemies) and theological (they deny God’s rescue).

3:3–4

"But you, LORD, are a shield around me. I call out to the LORD, and he answers me from his holy mountain." The "but you" pivot: from enemies to God. From many to One.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:3

Alter: "a shield around me" (baʿadî) means "about me" or "on my behalf." The shield is not in front but surrounding — total protection.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:2

Calvin: "God will not deliver him" — the cruelest enemy tactic is theological: convincing the sufferer that God has abandoned them. Calvin: this is Satan’s favourite lie.

3:4

Calvin: "he answers me from his holy mountain" — the distance between David fleeing and God on Zion does not prevent communication. Geography cannot separate God from his servant.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:1

The NET: the superscription "when he fled from his son Absalom" connects the psalm to 2 Samuel 15–18. These historical notes appear on 13 psalms.

3:3

The NET: māgēn (shield) is the small defensive shield carried in battle. God is portable protection, not a distant fortress.

Kidner Notes

kidner
3:1–2

Kidner: "many" repeated three times (vv.1–2). The enemy is overwhelming in number. But one LORD outweighs many foes.

3:3

Kidner: the shield image is defensive but the "lifter of my head" is aggressive — God raises the defeated head in triumph.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
3:1–4

VanGemeren: this is the first individual lament — the genre that dominates the Psalter. The pattern: address God, describe trouble, express trust, ask for help. Nearly a third of all psalms follow this form.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
3:3

Goldingay: "my glory and the lifter of my head" — God restores what enemies took. When others humiliate, God dignifies. The head that hung in shame is raised by the hand of God.

Verses 5–8 — “I Lie Down and Sleep” — Peace in Danger
5678

Hebrew Word Study

יְשׁוּעָה yəshūʿâ — salvation / deliverance: "From the LORD comes deliverance (yəshūʿâ). May your blessing be on your people" (v.8). The psalm ends with a corporate prayer. David’s personal deliverance expands to a prayer for all Israel. The individual lament becomes communal intercession.

Context

David slept and woke — because the LORD sustained him (v.5). He is not afraid of ten thousands drawn up against him (v.6). He calls on God to arise and strike his enemies (v.7). The psalm closes: salvation belongs to the LORD; may blessing rest on the people (v.8). From personal terror to communal confidence in seven verses.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 4:8"In peace I will lie down and sleep" — the companion psalm, also about sleeping in safety.
  • Prov 3:24"When you lie down, you will not be afraid; your sleep will be sweet" — the wisdom parallel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:5

"I lie down and sleep; I wake again, because the LORD sustains me." In the middle of a civil war, David sleeps. Faith produces rest even when circumstances produce terror.

3:7

"Arise, LORD! Deliver me, my God! Strike all my enemies on the jaw." The petition is bold: physical violence against enemies. David does not spiritualise the threat; he asks for physical deliverance.

3:8

"From the LORD comes deliverance. May your blessing be on your people." The psalm expands from personal to national. David’s rescue is Israel’s rescue.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:5

Alter: the verbs are simple and sequential: lay down, slept, woke. The simplicity is the point: ordinary actions performed in extraordinary circumstances become testimony.

3:7

Alter: "strike all my enemies on the jaw; break the teeth of the wicked" is visceral. The psalmist does not euphemise violence. He asks God to do what armies do.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:5

Calvin: David’s sleep in crisis is the model for all believers. Calvin: if God sustains, fear is irrational. The body that trusts God rests; the body that trusts circumstances stays awake.

3:8

Calvin: "from the LORD comes deliverance" — not from armies, not from strategy, not from David’s own skill. The source is named: the LORD. Calvin: this is the psalm’s thesis statement.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:5

The NET: the three verbs (lie down, sleep, wake) form a complete cycle. God’s sustaining power covers every state: active, resting, and transitioning.

3:8

The NET: yəshūʿâ (salvation/deliverance) is the root of the name Joshua/Jesus. The concept of divine rescue is embedded in the Messiah’s name.

Kidner Notes

kidner
3:5

Kidner: sleep is the body’s trust. You cannot sleep while afraid. David’s sleep is the physical expression of his theological confidence.

3:8

Kidner: the leap from personal lament to communal blessing is characteristic of the psalms: the individual’s experience becomes the community’s resource.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
3:5–8

VanGemeren: the shift from "I" to "your people" models how Israel used these psalms: one person’s prayer becomes everyone’s liturgy.

3:7

VanGemeren: "arise, LORD" echoes Numbers 10:35 — the war cry when the ark set out. David invokes God as warrior.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
3:5

Goldingay: "I lie down and sleep" is the psalm’s most remarkable claim. Surrounded by thousands, David sleeps. This is either faith or fiction — and the psalm insists it is faith.

3:8

Goldingay: the final line is a blessing, not a request. The psalm ends giving rather than asking — the mark of a soul that has moved from panic to peace.
