Psalms 10

Why Do You Stand Far Off?

Verses 1–11 — “The Wicked Person Hunts Down the Weak”
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

לָמָה lāmâ — why?: "Why (lāmâ), LORD, do you stand far off? Why do you hide yourself in times of trouble?" (v.1). The Psalter’s most direct accusation of divine absence. Not "why do I suffer?" but "why do YOU hide?" The question assumes God has the power to intervene and chooses not to. This is Job’s question in psalm form.

Context

The wicked boast (v.3), do not seek God (v.4), sneer at enemies (v.5), say "nothing will shake me" (v.6), lurk in ambush (vv.8–9), crush the helpless (v.10), and say "God will never notice" (v.11). The psalm is a catalogue of arrogance: the wicked live as if God does not exist. The question "why do you hide?" is not answered within the psalm — it is held before God as raw protest.

Cross-Reference

  • Job 24:1"Why does the Almighty not set times for judgment?" — Job’s identical complaint.
  • Hab 1:2–3"How long, LORD, must I call for help?" — the prophetic version of the same protest.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1

"Why, LORD, do you stand far off?" The directness is striking. David does not cushion the question.

10:3–4

"The wicked boasts of the cravings of his heart... in his pride he does not seek God; in all his thoughts there is no room for God." The wicked person’s worldview: self-sufficient, Godless.

10:11

"He says to himself, ‘God will never notice; he covers his face and never sees.’" The ultimate arrogance: denying God’s awareness. The wicked live in a fantasy of divine blindness.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:1

Alter: "why do you stand far off?" uses spatial language for theological distance. God’s absence is experienced as physical remoteness.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1

Calvin: the question assumes God’s power and challenges God’s timing. Calvin: David does not doubt God’s ability; he protests God’s apparent inaction. This is the prayer of frustrated faith, not failed faith.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:1

The NET: the double "why" (lāmâ... lāmâ) intensifies the protest. David asks the same question twice because the silence is unbearable.

10:4

The NET: "in all his thoughts there is no room for God" — literally "God is not in all his devices/plans." The wicked’s planning is entirely secular.

Kidner Notes

kidner
10:1

Kidner: the psalm’s honesty is its value. David does not pretend God’s absence is untroubling. He names it and protests it.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
10:1–11

VanGemeren: Psalm 10 is often paired with Psalm 9 (they form a single acrostic in some traditions). Together they present the tension: God IS a refuge (Ps 9:9) AND God seems absent (Ps 10:1). Both are true simultaneously.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
10:4

Goldingay: "in all his thoughts there is no room for God" is not atheism but practical irrelevance. The wicked do not deny God; they ignore God. The effect is the same.

Verses 12–18 — “Arise, LORD! Do Not Forget the Helpless”
12131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

עֲנָוִים ʿănāwîm — afflicted / humble: "You, LORD, hear the desire of the afflicted (ʿănāwîm); you encourage them, and you listen to their cry" (v.17). The answer to v.1. God does NOT stand far off. He hears, encourages, and listens. The psalm moves from accusation to affirmation — the characteristic lament trajectory.

Context

David calls God to arise (v.12), break the arm of the wicked (v.15), and defend the fatherless and oppressed (v.18). The psalm turns: "you, LORD, hear the desire of the afflicted" (v.17). God is king forever (v.16). The nations perish from his land (v.16). The fatherless and oppressed will be vindicated so that "mere earthly mortals will never again strike terror" (v.18). The psalm’s last word answers its first: you seemed absent, but you were listening all along.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 9:12"He does not ignore the cry of the afflicted" — the companion psalm’s identical theology.
  • Luke 18:7–8"Will not God bring about justice for his chosen ones, who cry out to him day and night?" — Jesus’ parable addresses Psalm 10’s question.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:12

"Arise, LORD! Lift up your hand, God. Do not forget the helpless." The imperative: act now. The rationale: people depend on you.

10:16–17

"The LORD is King for ever and ever. You, LORD, hear the desire of the afflicted; you encourage them, and you listen to their cry." The theological resolution: God IS king, God DOES hear.

10:18

"Defending the fatherless and the oppressed, so that mere earthly mortals will never again strike terror." The psalm ends with justice for the vulnerable and the deflation of human arrogance.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:18

Alter: "mere earthly mortals" (ʾĕnōsh min-hāʾāreṣ) — the emphasis is on origin: humans FROM THE EARTH. They are dust-beings pretending to be gods. The psalm returns them to their origin.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:17

Calvin: "you hear the desire" — Calvin: God hears the desire before the prayer. His response begins before the request is articulated. The afflicted person’s longing is already in God’s ear.

10:18

Calvin: "mere earthly mortals will never again strike terror" is eschatological hope. Calvin: in this age, mortals still terrify. But the psalm’s vision extends to the age where terror ceases entirely.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:12

The NET: "arise" (qūmâ) is the war cry — the same verb used in Num 10:35 when the ark set out. David invokes God as warrior.

10:18

The NET: "mere earthly mortals" uses ʾĕnōsh (frail man) + "from the earth." The emphasis is double: mortal AND earthbound. They are neither immortal nor heavenly.

Kidner Notes

kidner
10:16–18

Kidner: the psalm ends where all good laments end: with confidence in God’s character despite unresolved circumstances. The answer is not explanation but trust.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
10:12–18

VanGemeren: the shift from protest (v.1) to praise (v.16) is the lament’s purpose: to process pain until faith surfaces. The psalm does not suppress the question; it holds it until trust answers.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
10:17

Goldingay: "you hear the desire of the afflicted" — not just their words but their desire. God hears what is beneath speech: the longing the afflicted cannot articulate.
