Psalms 14

The Fool Says There Is No God

Verses 1–4 — “All Have Turned Away”
1234

Hebrew Word Study

נָבָל nābāl — fool / morally bankrupt: "The fool (nābāl) says in his heart, “There is no God”" (v.1). Nābāl is not intellectual stupidity but moral bankruptcy — the same word for Nabal (1 Sam 25). The fool denies God not from philosophy but from lifestyle: living as if God does not see or judge.

Context

God looks down from heaven to see if anyone understands or seeks him (v.2). The verdict: all have turned away, all are corrupt, no one does good — not even one (v.3). Paul quotes this in Romans 3:10–12 for his universal sinfulness argument. The "evildoers" devour God’s people like bread (v.4).

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 3:10–12Paul quotes Psalm 14:1–3 for universal depravity: "there is no one righteous, not even one."
  • Gen 6:5"Every inclination of the thoughts of the human heart was only evil" — the flood narrative’s identical assessment.

Alter: "in his heart" means this is an inner conviction, not a public declaration. The fool’s atheism is private and practical.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:1

Calvin: the fool denies not God’s existence but God’s relevance. Calvin: practical atheism — living as if God does not intervene — is more common than theoretical atheism.

Verses 5–7 — “Oh, That Salvation Would Come from Zion!”
567

Hebrew Word Study

יְשׁוּעָה yəshūʿâ — salvation / deliverance: "Oh, that salvation (yəshūʿâ) for Israel would come out of Zion!" (v.7). The psalm ends with longing for corporate deliverance. When God restores his people, Jacob will rejoice. The hope is national, messianic, and eschatological.

Context

Despite universal corruption, God is present with the righteous generation (v.5). The wicked are overwhelmed with dread because "God is present in the company of the righteous" (v.5). The psalm closes with a longing: "Oh, that salvation would come from Zion!" (v.7). The cry for a saviour from Zion anticipates Christ.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 11:26"The deliverer will come from Zion" — Paul applies this verse to Christ’s eschatological salvation.
  • Ps 53Psalm 53 is a near-duplicate of Psalm 14 with minor variations — both traditions preserved.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:5

"God is present in the company of the righteous." Despite the universal corruption diagnosis, God maintains a faithful remnant.

14:7

"Oh, that salvation would come from Zion!" The longing for messianic deliverance. When God restores, all Israel rejoices.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:7

Alter: the final exclamation shifts from observation to desire. The psalm moves from diagnostic (vv.1–4) to hopeful (v.7).

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:7

Calvin: salvation FROM Zion is Christ. Calvin: the psalm’s survey of human corruption makes the need for a divine saviour inescapable.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:7

The NET: "when the LORD restores his people" uses the phrase shūb shəbūṭ — "turn the turning," i.e., reverse the captivity. The same phrase describes return from exile.

Kidner Notes

kidner
14:7

Kidner: the longing for Zion’s salvation transcends David’s situation. This is messianic hope: a deliverer FROM Zion for all God’s people.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
14:5–7

VanGemeren: the remnant theology (v.5) alongside universal corruption (v.3) is the OT pattern: all are fallen, but God preserves a faithful community.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
14:7

Goldingay: "Oh, that!" (mî-yittēn) is a wish, not a demand. The psalm ends in longing, not possession. Salvation is hoped for, not yet realised.
