Psalms 95

Come, Let Us Sing for Joy to the LORD

Verses 1–7a — “He Is the Great God, the Great King”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

נָרִיעָה nārîʿâ — let us shout / let us cry out: "Come, let us sing for joy (nārîʿâ) to the LORD; let us shout aloud to the Rock of our salvation" (v.1). The venite psalm — the call to worship that opens monastic daily prayer. Come before him with thanksgiving, extol him with music (v.2). "For the LORD is the great God, the great King above all gods" (v.3). The depths of the earth and the mountain peaks belong to him (v.4). "The sea is his, for he made it, and his hands formed the dry land" (v.5). "Come, let us bow down in worship, let us kneel before the LORD our Maker, for he is our God and we are the people of his pasture, the flock under his care" (vv.6–7a).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
95:1–2

"Come, let us sing for joy to the LORD." The triple invitation: sing, shout, come with thanksgiving. Worship is active, loud, and communal.

95:3–5

"The LORD is the great God, the great King above all gods. In his hand are the depths of the earth; the sea is his, for he made it." Total ownership: depths, heights, sea, land. All created, all held.

95:6–7a

"Come, let us bow down in worship, let us kneel before the LORD our Maker, for he is our God and we are the people of his pasture." Posture matches theology: the great King deserves kneeling. The shepherd owns the flock.

Verses 7b–11 — “Today, If Only You Would Hear His Voice”
891011

Hebrew Word Study

הַיּוֹם hayyōm — today: "Today (hayyōm), if only you would hear his voice, ‘Do not harden your hearts as you did at Meribah, as you did that day at Massah in the wilderness’" (vv.7b–8). The psalm’s dramatic turn: from joyful worship to urgent warning. Hebrews 3:7–11 quotes this extensively: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts." The wilderness generation tested God for forty years (v.9); God was angry and swore: "They shall never enter my rest" (v.11). The warning: worship without obedience leads nowhere. "Today" is urgent — there is a deadline.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
95:7b–8

"Today, if only you would hear his voice, do not harden your hearts." The most pastorally urgent verse in the Psalter. Hebrews makes it the key to the new covenant warning: TODAY. Not tomorrow. Not eventually.

95:9–10

"For forty years I was angry with that generation. I said, ‘They are a people whose hearts go astray, and they have not known my ways.’" Forty years of testing; forty years of anger. They saw the works but missed the ways.

95:11

"‘So I declared on oath in my anger: They shall never enter my rest.’" The irrevocable consequence: exclusion from rest. Hebrews 3–4 develops this into a theology of entering God’s rest through faith.
