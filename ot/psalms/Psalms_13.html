Psalms 13

How Long, LORD?

Verses 1–4 — “How Long Will You Forget Me?”
1234

Hebrew Word Study

עַד אָנָה ʿad-ʾānâ — how long?: "How long (ʿad-ʾānâ), LORD? Will you forget me forever?" (v.1). Four times "how long" in two verses — the most compressed lament in the Psalter. The repetition is desperation: how long forgotten, how long hidden, how long sorrow, how long enemy triumph? The psalm is only six verses but contains the entire lament arc: complaint, petition, trust.

Context

The shortest complete lament. David asks four "how long" questions (vv.1–2), makes two petitions (vv.3–4: look at me, answer me, give light to my eyes lest I sleep in death, lest my enemy triumph). Six verses, complete emotional arc.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 89:46"How long, LORD? Will you hide yourself forever?" — the same cry at a national level.
  • Rev 6:10"How long, Sovereign Lord?" — the martyrs’ cry echoes David’s.

Verses 5–6 — “I Trust in Your Unfailing Love”
56

Hebrew Word Study

חֶסֶד ḥesed — unfailing love / steadfast love / loyal love: "But I trust in your unfailing love (ḥesed); my heart rejoices in your salvation" (v.5). The pivot: from "how long" to "but I trust." Two verses of trust balance four questions of despair. Ḥesed is God’s covenant loyalty — love that endures when feelings fail.

Context

The pivot is the word "but" (v.5). After four questions and two petitions, David declares trust: "but I trust in your ḥesed." His heart rejoices in God’s salvation. He will sing because God has been good (v.6). The psalm moves from darkness to song in one verse. The shift is not based on changed circumstances but on chosen trust.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 52:8"I trust in God’s unfailing love for ever and ever" — the same ḥesed-trust declaration.
  • Lam 3:22–23"Because of the LORD’s great love we are not consumed" — the same theology in deeper darkness.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:5

"But I trust in your unfailing love." The single most important word in lament psalms: "but." Everything before it is complaint; everything after it is faith.

13:6

"I will sing the LORD’s praise, for he has been good to me." The psalm ends in song. From tears to music in six verses.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:5–6

Alter: the compression is remarkable: two verses of confidence after four of despair. The disproportion is the point — a little trust outweighs much suffering.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:5

Calvin: "but I trust" is the heart of the Christian life. Calvin: the "but" is not denial of pain but refusal to let pain have the last word.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:5

The NET: ḥesed (covenant love/loyalty) is the Psalter’s most important theological term. It describes God’s self-imposed obligation to love his covenant people. David trusts this, not his feelings.

Kidner Notes

kidner
13:5–6

Kidner: the "but" of v.5 is the hinge of every lament. Without it, the psalm is despair. With it, despair becomes trust. The turn is a choice, not a feeling.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
13:5–6

VanGemeren: the shift from lament to praise happens without any change in David’s circumstances. Nothing external changed. The decision to trust changed everything internal.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
13:5

Goldingay: ḥesed-trust is the bedrock. When feelings say "forgotten," covenant love says "held." The psalm chooses covenant over experience.
