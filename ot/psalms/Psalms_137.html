Psalms 137

By the Rivers of Babylon

Verses 1–4 — “How Can We Sing the Songs of the LORD in a Foreign Land?”
1234

Hebrew Word Study

בָבֶל Bābel — Babylon: "By the rivers of Babylon (Bābel) we sat and wept when we remembered Zion. There on the poplars we hung our harps" (vv.1–2). The exile psalm. The captors demanded entertainment: "Sing us one of the songs of Zion!" (v.3). The response: "How can we sing the songs of the LORD while in a foreign land?" (v.4). The harps hung on trees: worship silenced by displacement. Music requires home.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
137:1–2

"By the rivers of Babylon, we sat and wept when we remembered Zion. We hung our harps on the poplars." The image: harps silent, tears falling, Zion remembered from exile. The most poignant scene of displacement in the OT.

137:3–4

"Our captors asked us for songs: ‘Sing us one of the songs of Zion!’ How can we sing the LORD’s song in a foreign land?" The impossibility: Zion’s songs require Zion. Worship cannot be performed on command for entertainment.

Verses 5–9 — “If I Forget You, Jerusalem”
56789

Hebrew Word Study

יְמִינִי yəmînî — my right hand: "If I forget you, Jerusalem, may my right hand (yəmînî) forget its skill. May my tongue cling to the roof of my mouth if I do not consider Jerusalem my highest joy" (vv.5–6). The oath of remembrance: may the musician’s hand forget how to play and the singer’s tongue stick — the instruments of worship destroyed — if Jerusalem is forgotten. Then the devastating imprecation (v.9): "Happy is the one who seizes your infants and dashes them against the rocks." The most disturbing verse in the Psalter. The raw cry of a people who watched their own children killed. Not a command but a grief-scream.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
137:5–6

"If I forget you, Jerusalem, may my right hand forget its skill." The self-imprecation: the musician curses his own hands and tongue if he forgets Zion. The instruments of worship become the instruments of the oath.

137:8–9

"Daughter Babylon, doomed to destruction... happy is the one who repays you according to what you have done to us." The imprecation is retributive: what you did to us, may someone do to you. The verse is horrifying because the original atrocity was horrifying. The psalm does not celebrate violence; it screams in the language of its own suffering.

Prophet, lawgiver, and covenant mediator
Moses stands at the centre of the Pentateuch’s narrative — the one man who spoke with God face to face (Exod 33:11; Num 12:8) and mediated the Sinai covenant to Israel. In Leviticus he receives the priestly legislation; in Numbers he leads, intercedes, and ultimately fails at Meribah (Num 20). The writer of Hebrews calls him "faithful in all God’s house" (Heb 3:5) while distinguishing him from Christ, the Son over the house.
