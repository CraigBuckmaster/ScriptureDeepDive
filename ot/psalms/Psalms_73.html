Psalms 73

Surely God Is Good to Israel

Verses 1–16 — “My Feet Had Almost Slipped”
12345678910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

קִנְאָה qinʾâ — jealousy / envy: "I envied (qinʾâ) the arrogant when I saw the prosperity of the wicked" (v.3). BOOK III OPENS (Psalms 73–89, mostly Asaph psalms). The Psalter’s greatest wisdom psalm. Asaph nearly lost his faith over the prosperity of the wicked: they have no struggles (v.4), they are free from common burdens (v.5), they scoff and speak with malice (v.8), they say "How would God know?" (v.11). "Surely in vain I have kept my heart pure" (v.13). The crisis: if the wicked prosper and the righteous suffer, what’s the point of obedience?

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
73:1–3

"Surely God is good to Israel, to those who are pure in heart. But as for me, my feet had almost slipped; I had nearly lost my foothold. For I envied the arrogant." The psalm opens with theology (God is good) then immediately confesses doubt (my feet almost slipped). Both are true.

73:12–14

"This is what the wicked are like — always free of care, they go on amassing wealth. Surely in vain I have kept my heart pure." The most honest statement of doubt in the Psalter. Asaph questions the entire project of faithfulness.

73:16

"When I tried to understand all this, it troubled me deeply." Human reasoning cannot resolve the problem of unjust prosperity. The mind reaches its limit.

Verses 17–28 — “Till I Entered the Sanctuary of God”
171819202122232425262728

Hebrew Word Study

מִקְדְשֵׁי miqdashē — sanctuary / holy places: "Till I entered the sanctuary (miqdashē) of God; then I understood their final destiny" (v.17). The PIVOT. Not new information but new perspective. In God’s presence, Asaph sees what observation alone could not: the wicked stand on slippery ground (v.18), suddenly destroyed (v.19), dismissed like a dream on waking (v.20). Then the confession: "I was senseless and ignorant; I was a brute beast before you" (v.22). And the psalm’s climax: "Yet I am always with you; you hold me by my right hand. You guide me with your counsel, and afterward you will take me into glory" (vv.23–24). "Whom have I in heaven but you? And earth has nothing I desire besides you. My flesh and my heart may fail, but God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever" (vv.25–26).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
73:17

"Till I entered the sanctuary of God; then I understood their final destiny." The turning point of the entire psalm — and arguably the entire Psalter. Worship provides what reason cannot: eschatological perspective.

73:23–24

"Yet I am always with you; you hold me by my right hand. You guide me with your counsel, and afterward you will take me into glory." From near-fall (v.2) to permanent holding (v.23). From doubt to glory. The three-stage promise: present holding, ongoing guidance, future glory.

73:25–26

"Whom have I in heaven but you? And earth has nothing I desire besides you. My flesh and my heart may fail, but God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever." The Psalter’s most complete statement of God-sufficiency. Heaven: only God. Earth: nothing desired besides God. Body failing: God is the portion. Forever.

73:28

"But as for me, it is good to be near God. I have made the Sovereign LORD my refuge." The psalm’s final word: nearness to God IS the good. Not God’s gifts but God’s presence is the answer to the prosperity problem.
