Psalms 18

David’s Great Victory Psalm

Verses 1’24 — “The LORD Is My Rock” — Cosmic Rescue
123456789101112131415161718192021222324

Hebrew Word Study

סַלְעִי salʿî — my rock / my crag: "I love you, LORD, my strength. The LORD is my rock (salʿî), my fortress and my deliverer" (vv.1–2). Six metaphors for God in two verses: rock, fortress, deliverer, God, refuge, shield, horn of salvation, stronghold. The stack is overwhelming: God is everything David needs.

רָעַשׁ rāʿash — to quake / to tremble: "The earth trembled and quaked" (v.7). God’s rescue is described as theophany: earthquake, smoke from nostrils, fire from mouth, dark clouds, cherubim-riding, thunder, hail, lightning. This is the most dramatic divine-intervention description in the Psalter.

Context

The longest psalm so far (50 verses, duplicated in 2 Samuel 22). David praises God for rescue from all enemies. The theophany (vv.7–19) is cosmic: the earth trembles, mountains quake, God rides on a cherub, darkness surrounds him, hailstones and lightning blast the enemy. God reaches down, draws David from deep waters (v.16), sets him in a wide place (v.19). The psalm is retrospective: David reviews his entire career of divine rescue.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Sam 22The parallel text — David’s victory song with minor variations.
  • Exod 15:1–18The Song of the Sea — the exodus rescue that Psalm 18’s theophany echoes.
  • Hab 3:3–15Habakkuk’s theophany echoes Psalm 18’s cosmic imagery.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
18:1–2

"I love you, LORD, my strength. The LORD is my rock, my fortress, my deliverer." Six metaphors stacked. Overwhelming protection.

18:7–15

The theophany: earthquake, smoke, fire, darkness, cherubim, thunder, hail, lightning. God enters the battle personally. Creation itself becomes his weapon.

18:16–19

"He reached down from on high and took hold of me; he drew me out of deep waters. He brought me out into a spacious place." From drowning to wide-open space. Rescue is spatial: narrow → broad.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:7–15

Alter: the theophany is the psalm’s literary peak. Nature participates in God’s anger: mountains shake, skies split, fire and hail descend. This is not metaphor but mythic-poetic truth — creation responds to its Creator’s emotion.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:16–19

Calvin: "he drew me out of deep waters" — Calvin: the waters of death, the waters of despair, the waters of impossible odds. God’s arm reaches into every depth.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
18:2

The NET: salʿî (my rock/crag) is different from ṣūr (rock). Salʿî is a cliff or crag — high, inaccessible, defensive. God is the elevation that enemies cannot climb.

18:10

The NET: "he rode on a cherub and flew" — the cherubim are God’s throne-bearers (Ezek 1, 10). God rides his own throne into battle.

Verses 25–50 — “You Light My Lamp” — David’s Testimony

Hebrew Word Study

נֵרִי nērî — my lamp: "You, LORD, light my lamp (nērî); my God turns my darkness into light" (v.28). The lamp is life, hope, guidance. God illuminates what enemies tried to extinguish. The lamp that the wicked’s death snuffs (Prov 13:9), God reignites for the righteous.

Context

David reflects on God’s faithfulness: to the faithful God is faithful, to the pure he is pure (vv.25–26). God lights his lamp, enables him to leap walls, provides a perfect way (vv.28–30). God arms him for battle, gives him a wide path so his feet don’t slip (vv.32–36). David pursues enemies, shatters them, stamps them like dust (vv.37–42). God made him head of nations (v.43). The psalm closes with a messianic declaration: "He gives his king great victories; he shows unfailing love to his anointed, to David and his descendants forever" (v.50).

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Sam 7:12–16"Your throne will be established forever" — the Davidic covenant Psalm 18:50 echoes.
  • Rom 15:9Paul quotes Psalm 18:49 for Gentile praise: "I will praise you among the nations."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
18:28–30

"You light my lamp; my God turns my darkness into light. With your help I can advance against a troop; with my God I can scale a wall." God provides light, strength, and ability beyond David’s natural capacity.

18:46–50

"The LORD lives! Praise be to my Rock! He gives his king great victories; he shows unfailing love to his anointed, to David and his descendants forever." The psalm’s finale: from personal rescue to dynastic promise. The ḥesed to David’s line is forever.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:50

Alter: the final verse shifts from first person to third: "his anointed... David and his descendants." The personal becomes dynastic, the testimony becomes prophecy.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:50

Calvin: "to David and his descendants forever" is the Davidic covenant in psalm form. Calvin: every promise to David’s line is ultimately a promise to Christ’s kingdom.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
18:28

The NET: nēr (lamp) symbolises life and dynasty in the OT (2 Sam 21:17; 1 Kgs 11:36). God keeps the lamp burning when enemies try to extinguish it.

18:50

The NET: "his anointed" (məshîḥō) is the same word as Psalm 2:2. The messianic thread connects the Psalter’s introduction to its first great victory psalm.

Kidner Notes

kidner
18:50

Kidner: "to David and his descendants forever" extends the psalm from personal testimony to messianic promise. Christ is the ultimate descendant who receives eternal ḥesed.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
18:25–50

VanGemeren: the second half is David’s testimony — retrospective gratitude for a lifetime of divine faithfulness. The messianic close (v.50) ensures the testimony outlasts David.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
18:28

Goldingay: "you light my lamp" — in a world without electricity, a lamp is survival. God provides what darkness cannot take: visibility, warmth, hope.
