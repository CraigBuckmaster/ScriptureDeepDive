Psalms 32

The Joy of Forgiveness

Verses 1–7 — “Blessed Whose Transgressions Are Forgiven”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

נְשׂוּי פֶּשַׁע nəsûy feshaʿ — forgiven of transgression: "Blessed is the one whose transgressions are forgiven (nəsûy feshaʿ), whose sins are covered" (v.1). The second penitential psalm. Three words for sin: transgression (rebellion), sin (missing the mark), iniquity (crookedness). Three words for forgiveness: forgiven (carried away), covered (concealed), not counted (not reckoned). Paul quotes vv.1–2 in Romans 4:7–8 for justification by faith.

יָדִיתִי yādîṭî — I made known / I acknowledged: "Then I acknowledged (yādîṭî) my sin to you and did not cover up my iniquity" (v.5). The turning point: confession. While David was silent, his bones wasted (vv.3–4). When he confessed, God forgave. The contrast: concealment produces decay; confession produces freedom.

Context

David describes the misery of unconfessed sin: groaning, bones wasting, strength sapped (vv.3–4). Then the liberation of confession: "I said, ‘I will confess,’ and you forgave the guilt of my sin" (v.5). God is a hiding place, a protector, surrounded by songs of deliverance (v.7). The emotional arc: guilt → silence → decay → confession → forgiveness → joy.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 4:7–8Paul quotes Psalm 32:1–2 as proof of justification by faith apart from works: "Blessed are those whose transgressions are forgiven."
  • 1 John 1:9"If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just and will forgive us" — the NT’s version of David’s experience.
  • Ps 51The companion penitential psalm, more explicitly tied to David’s adultery with Bathsheba.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
32:1–2

"Blessed whose transgressions are forgiven, whose sins are covered. Blessed the one whose sin the LORD does not count against them." Three sins, three removals. Comprehensive forgiveness.

32:3–4

"When I kept silent, my bones wasted away through my groaning all day long. Day and night your hand was heavy on me." Unconfessed sin produces physical deterioration. The body carries what the mouth refuses to speak.

32:5

"Then I acknowledged my sin to you. I said, ‘I will confess.’ And you forgave the guilt of my sin." The confession is instantaneous; so is the forgiveness. No penance, no delay.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
32:1

Alter: three words for sin paired with three words for removal. The catalogue of sin is matched by a catalogue of grace. Nothing is left uncovered.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
32:1–2

Calvin: Paul’s use of this text in Romans 4 makes it foundational for justification. Calvin: forgiveness is not moral improvement but legal declaration: sin is not counted.

32:5

Calvin: the speed of forgiveness shatters every notion of earned pardon. David confessed; God forgave. The interval between confession and forgiveness is zero.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
32:1

The NET: three sin terms: peshaʿ (rebellion/transgression), ḥaṭṭāʾâ (sin/missing), ʿāwōn (iniquity/crookedness). Three forgiveness terms: nāsûy (lifted/carried away), kəsûy (covered), lōʾ yaḥshōb (not reckoned).

32:5

The NET: the instant forgiveness — "you forgave the guilt of my sin" — uses the verb nāsāʾ (to lift/carry away). God removes sin as completely as lifting a load off someone’s back.

Kidner Notes

kidner
32:3–4

Kidner: the physical symptoms of unconfessed sin — wasting bones, groaning, drained strength — are not divine punishment but the natural consequence of internal dishonesty. The body revolts against the soul’s lie.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
32:1–5

VanGemeren: this is the psalm Paul uses for imputed righteousness (Rom 4:6–8). Forgiveness is not earned by confession; it is declared by God. Confession is the occasion, not the cause.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
32:5

Goldingay: the three-stage movement (silence → confession → forgiveness) takes place in one verse. The breakthrough is instantaneous. The suffering was in the delay, not in the process.

Verses 8–11 — “I Will Instruct You and Teach You”
891011

Hebrew Word Study

סָמַךְ sāmak̅ — to surround / to encompass: "The LORD’s unfailing love surrounds (sāmak̅) the one who trusts in him" (v.10). The psalm’s summary: trust produces surrounding love. The image is of being enclosed by ḥesed on every side. The wicked have many woes; the trusting are wrapped in love.

Context

God speaks: "I will instruct you and teach you in the way you should go; I will counsel you with my loving eye on you" (v.8). The warning: "Do not be like the horse or the mule, which have no understanding but must be controlled by bit and bridle" (v.9). The contrast (v.10): woes for the wicked, surrounding love for the trusting. The psalm closes: "Rejoice in the LORD and be glad, you righteous; sing, all you who are upright in heart!" (v.11).

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 3:5–6"Trust in the LORD... he will make your paths straight" — the same guidance theology.
  • Jas 5:16"Confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed" — the community application.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
32:8

"I will instruct you and teach you in the way you should go." God becomes personal tutor. The eye of God guides, not just observes.

32:9

"Do not be like the horse or the mule." Animals require external control because they lack understanding. God wants willing followers, not controlled beasts.

32:10–11

"Many are the woes of the wicked, but the LORD’s unfailing love surrounds the one who trusts. Rejoice!" The psalm ends in celebration: trust produces surrounded love, and surrounded love produces joy.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
32:9

Alter: the horse-and-mule image is earthy and comic. God compares the stubborn person to a pack animal requiring hardware. The sarcasm is loving.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
32:10

Calvin: the contrast is binary: woes vs love, wicked vs trusting. Calvin: there is no third category. You are either inside the ḥesed-perimeter or outside in woe.

32:11

Calvin: the command to rejoice follows naturally from forgiveness. Calvin: joylessness in the Christian life is often unconfessed sin blocking the experience of declared pardon.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
32:8

The NET: "with my eye on you" (ʿēynî) can also be translated "I will counsel you, my eye upon you" — God watches over the guided person like a parent watching a child learn to walk.

32:10

The NET: sāmak̅ (surrounds/encompasses) is the same verb used for an army surrounding a city. Ḥesed besieges the trusting person with love.

Kidner Notes

kidner
32:8–9

Kidner: the horse and mule proverb is God’s gentle humour. The bit and bridle are for animals; God prefers to guide by eye contact. Choose the higher relationship.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
32:8–11

VanGemeren: the psalm moves from confession (vv.1–5) to instruction (vv.8–9) to celebration (vv.10–11). The forgiven person becomes the taught person, then the joyful person.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
32:10

Goldingay: "surrounds" is comprehensive. Not love beside you or ahead of you but encircling you. The trusting person is inside a perimeter of ḥesed.
