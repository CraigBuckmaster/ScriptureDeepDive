Psalms 15

Who May Dwell in Your Sanctuary?

Verses 1–3 — “Who May Live on Your Holy Mountain?”
123

Hebrew Word Study

גּוּר gūr — to sojourn / to dwell as guest: "LORD, who may dwell (gūr) in your sacred tent? Who may live on your holy mountain?" (v.1). The entrance question: who qualifies for God’s presence? The answer is ethical, not ritual: blameless conduct, righteous action, truthful speech, no slander, no wrong to neighbour.

Context

A Torah entrance liturgy — the worshipper asks at the gate: who may enter? The answer (vv.2–5): the one whose walk is blameless, who does right, speaks truth, does no wrong to a neighbour, keeps oaths even when it hurts, and does not accept bribes. Character, not credentials.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 24:3–4"Who may ascend the mountain of the LORD?" — the companion entrance liturgy.
  • Isa 33:14–16"Who can dwell with the consuming fire?" — the prophetic version.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
15:1

"Who may dwell in your sacred tent?" The question assumes not everyone qualifies. Access to God requires character.

15:2–3

"Whose walk is blameless, who does what is righteous, who speaks the truth from their heart." Three categories: conduct, action, speech. All internal → external.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:1

Alter: the "sacred tent" and "holy mountain" are Zion’s temple. The question is both literal (who enters the sanctuary?) and theological (who lives in God’s presence?).

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:2

Calvin: the ethical requirements show that faith and morality are inseparable. Calvin: you cannot worship God on Sunday and wrong your neighbour on Monday.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:1

The NET: gūr means to sojourn as a guest. Dwelling with God is guest-status, not ownership. We are guests in God’s house, admitted by his grace and expected to honour his rules.

Kidner Notes

kidner
15:1

Kidner: the entrance question was asked at the temple gate. The priests answered with the ethical requirements. This psalm preserves that liturgy.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
15:1–3

VanGemeren: the entrance requirements are entirely moral, not ceremonial. No mention of sacrifice, purity rituals, or priestly requirements. Character is the admission ticket.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
15:2

Goldingay: "speaks the truth from their heart" — not just accurate speech but sincere speech. The heart and the tongue are aligned. No double heart (cf. Ps 12:2).

Verses 4–5 — “The One Who Does These Things Will Never Be Shaken”
45

Hebrew Word Study

לֹא יִמּוֹט lōʾ yimmōṭ — will never be shaken / will not be moved: "Whoever does these things will never be shaken (lōʾ yimmōṭ)" (v.5). The psalm’s reward: stability. The person of integrity has a foundation that does not collapse. This connects to Psalm 1’s tree and Psalm 11’s foundations — the righteous are rooted, unshakable, permanent.

Context

More requirements: despise the vile, honour the God-fearing, keep oaths even at cost, lend without usury, refuse bribes against the innocent (vv.4–5a). The promise: "whoever does these things will never be shaken" (v.5b). The psalm is 5 verses of character and one sentence of reward. The emphasis is clear: the life matters; the stability follows.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 7:24–25"The wise man built his house on the rock... it did not fall" — the same unshakable-foundation promise.
  • Ps 112:6"The righteous will never be shaken" — the identical promise.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
15:4

"Who keeps an oath even when it hurts." Integrity means fulfilling commitments when the cost is high, not just when it is convenient.

15:5

"Whoever does these things will never be shaken." The one-sentence promise. Character produces stability.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:4

Alter: "keeps an oath even to their own hurt" is the most demanding requirement. It distinguishes genuine integrity from convenient morality.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:5

Calvin: "never shaken" is conditional on character, but the character itself is a gift of grace. Calvin: we do not earn stability; we receive the grace that produces the character that results in stability.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:4

The NET: "swears to their own hurt" means fulfilling a vow even when circumstances change and the vow becomes costly. Integrity is tested in the gap between promise and fulfilment.

15:5

The NET: lōʾ yimmōṭ (not be moved/shaken) uses the mot root — the same root in Psalm 10:6. The righteous share the REAL stability the wicked only imagined.

Kidner Notes

kidner
15:5

Kidner: "never shaken" is the psalm’s payoff: not prosperity or fame but stability. The person of integrity stands when everything around them falls.

Vangemeren Notes

vangemeren
15:4–5

VanGemeren: the requirements escalate: keeping costly oaths, refusing profitable bribes. Integrity is tested by what you give up, not what you gain.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
15:5

Goldingay: "never be shaken" echoes the wicked’s boast in 10:6 ("I will never be shaken"). The difference: the wicked’s confidence is self-generated; the righteous person’s is God-grounded.
