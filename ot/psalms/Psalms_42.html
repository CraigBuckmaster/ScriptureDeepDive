Psalms 42

As the Deer Pants for Streams of Water

Verses 1–5 — “My Soul Thirsts for God”
12345

Hebrew Word Study

צָמְאָה ṣāməʾâ — to thirst: "As the deer pants for streams of water, so my soul pants for you, my God. My soul thirsts (ṣāməʾâ) for God, for the living God. When can I go and meet with God?" (vv.1–2). Book II opens (Psalms 42–72, the "Elohistic Psalter" where God is called Elohim rather than YHWH). A Son of Korah psalm. The psalmist is exiled from the temple, thirsting for God’s presence. "My tears have been my food day and night, while people say to me all day long, ‘Where is your God?’" (v.3). The refrain: "Why, my soul, are you downcast? Put your hope in God" (v.5).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
42:1–2

"As the deer pants for streams of water, so my soul pants for you, my God." The most famous thirst-image in Scripture. Physical dehydration as metaphor for spiritual longing.

42:3

"Where is your God?" The taunt of the enemy. The absent God is the psalmist’s deepest wound.

42:5

"Why, my soul, are you downcast? Put your hope in God, for I will yet praise him, my Saviour and my God." The refrain (repeated in 42:11 and 43:5): self-counsel. The psalmist preaches to his own soul.

Verses 6–11 — “Deep Calls to Deep”
67891011

Hebrew Word Study

תְהוֹם təhōm — deep / abyss: "Deep calls to deep (təhōm) in the roar of your waterfalls; all your waves and breakers have swept over me" (v.7). The image of being overwhelmed: wave upon wave. The deeps of suffering call to each other across the psalmist’s soul. Yet: "By day the LORD directs his love, at night his song is with me" (v.8). Day-love and night-song — even in the flood, God’s love and music persist.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
42:7

"Deep calls to deep." The waves are God’s — "YOUR waves and breakers." Even the overwhelming flood belongs to God. The suffering is not random; it is under divine jurisdiction.

42:8

"By day the LORD directs his love, at night his song is with me." The most remarkable claim: in the deepest water, God sends both love and music. Day and night, the provisions arrive.

42:11

"Why, my soul, are you downcast?" The refrain returns. The psalmist has not been rescued; he is still in exile. But the self-counsel continues: hope in God.
