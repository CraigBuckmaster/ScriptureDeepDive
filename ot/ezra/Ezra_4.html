Ezra 4

Opposition and the Work Halted

Verses 1–5 — Adversaries Offer Help; Rejected; They Undermine
12345

Hebrew Word Study

צָר ṣār — adversary / enemy: The opponents are called ṣārê (adversaries) — the same root as "Satan." Their offer to help build is framed as opposition from the start. The narrator removes any ambiguity about their motives.

רָפָה rāpâ — to weaken / to slacken / to discourage: They "weakened the hands" (mərappîm yəḏê) of the builders — an idiom for discouragement. The same phrase describes the effect of bad news in war (Jer 38:4). Words as weapons.

Historical Context

The "adversaries" are the mixed population settled in the northern territory by Assyria after 722 BC (2 Kgs 17:24–41). They worshipped YHWH alongside other gods — syncretism. Their offer to help build was likely genuine in their own eyes but would have compromised the temple’s exclusively Yahwistic character. The rejection mirrors the later Samaritan-Jewish split.

Context

Zerubbabel’s refusal (“You have no part with us in building a temple to our God”) sounds exclusionary but is theologically principled. The Cyrus decree authorised the Jewish community specifically. Accepting help from syncretistic neighbours would have diluted the restoration’s covenant character from the foundation up.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 17:24–41The origin of the mixed population — Assyrian resettlement with syncretistic worship.
  • Neh 2:20Nehemiah’s identical rejection: "You have no share in Jerusalem or any claim or historic right to it."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1–2

The enemies’ claim to worship the same God since Esarhaddon’s time (681–669 BC) is historically accurate (2 Kgs 17:33) but theologically inadequate. Worshipping YHWH alongside other gods is not worshipping YHWH.

4:3

Zerubbabel’s refusal is firm, clear, and grounded in the Cyrus decree. He cites the king’s authority rather than making a theological argument — politically shrewd.

4:4–5

The adversaries hire counsellors (lobbyists) to frustrate the work throughout the entire reign of Cyrus until Darius. The opposition is organised, funded, and sustained over years.

4:5

"All the days of Cyrus... until the reign of Darius" spans approximately 538–520 BC — eighteen years of sustained opposition before the work resumes.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1–3

Calvin: "Satan’s subtlety is seen in this: he first tries to corrupt the work by joining it. When that fails, he opposes it openly. Believers must be as wise in refusing false allies as in resisting open enemies."

4:5

The sustained opposition teaches patience. Calvin: "God’s work is rarely accomplished without delay and setback. The righteous must learn to wait on God’s timing, not their own."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:2

The NET notes Esarhaddon (681–669 BC) is named specifically, anchoring the claim in verifiable Assyrian history. The settlers’ worship is described in 2 Kgs 17:33 as dual-allegiance.

4:4

"Weakened the hands" (mərappîm) uses the same piel form found in Jer 38:4, where officials accuse Jeremiah of weakening soldiers’ hands. The idiom implies deliberate demoralisation.

Williamson Notes

williamson
4:2

Williamson identifies the self-description as historically plausible: Esarhaddon’s deportation and resettlement policies are well-attested in Assyrian records. These were genuine settlers, not imposters — which makes the rejection harder and more principled.

4:4–5

The "counsellors" (yōāṣîm) are Persian bureaucrats hired to influence imperial policy. Williamson: this is lobbying, not violence — administrative warfare.

Kidner Notes

kidner
4:1–3

4:4–5

The shift from friendly overture to active sabotage happens in one verse. Kidner: "Rejected generosity turns to enmity — a pattern as old as Cain."

Verses 6–24 — Letters to Persian Kings; Work Halted
6789101112131415161718192021222324

Hebrew Word Study

שִׂטְנָה śiṭnâ — accusation / charge: The opponents write a "accusation" (śiṭnâ) — a formal legal charge. The Aramaic section (4:8–6:18) begins here, reflecting the imperial administrative language. The shift to Aramaic signals that we are reading Persian court documents.

בְּטֵל bəṭēl — to cease / to stop: Artaxerxes orders the work to "cease" (bəṭēl) by force. The same verb describes the creation-rest of Gen 2:2 (God ceased). Here the cessation is forced, not voluntary — a perversion of Sabbath.

Historical Context

The narrative in 4:6–23 is chronologically complex. The letters to Ahasuerus (v.6, = Xerxes, 486–465 BC) and Artaxerxes (vv.7–23, 465–424 BC) postdate the temple completion (516 BC). The narrator inserts them here thematically — grouping all opposition together regardless of chronology. Verse 24 returns to the Darius-era temple halt, the main narrative thread.

Context

The Aramaic section (4:8–6:18) is one of the most remarkable features of Ezra. The author preserves official Persian correspondence in its original administrative language. This is not translation — it is documentary evidence embedded in the biblical text. The letters reveal how imperial bureaucracy could be weaponised against the covenant community.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:6

The letter to "Ahasuerus" (= Xerxes I) at the beginning of his reign shows the opposition was persistent across multiple Persian administrations. Each new king was lobbied.

4:11–16

The letter to Artaxerxes frames the returnees as rebels: "a rebellious and wicked city" with a history of revolt. The accusation is technically accurate — Jerusalem had rebelled against Babylon — but weaponises history against restoration.

4:21–22

Artaxerxes orders a halt "until I so order." The qualifier leaves the door open — a detail the adversaries conveniently ignore. They enforce the decree "by force and power" (v.23), exceeding their authority.

4:24

The narrator returns to the main timeline: the temple work ceased until Darius’s second year (520 BC). The thematic digression (vv.6–23) is over; the Darius-era story resumes in chapter 5.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:11–16

Calvin: "The enemies of the church often use the weapons of law and government against God’s people. Let us not be surprised when civil authority is turned against true religion — it has always been so."

4:24

Calvin reads the halt as divine testing: "God permitted the delay to try the faith of his people. He could have prevented it but chose instead to teach them patience and trust."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:8

The NET notes the shift to Aramaic (4:8–6:18) and explains that Aramaic was the lingua franca of the Persian empire, used for all official correspondence west of the Euphrates.

4:14

"We are salted with the salt of the palace" is an Aramaic idiom meaning "we eat the king’s salt" — i.e., we are loyal subjects obligated to report threats to the crown.

Williamson Notes

williamson
4:8–16

Williamson analyses the letter’s Aramaic as authentic Persian chancellery language. The formulae (greeting, background, request, warning) match attested Persian administrative correspondence from Elephantine and elsewhere.

4:6–23

Williamson identifies the chronological displacement and argues it serves a literary purpose: all opposition episodes are collected to create a comprehensive picture of resistance before the resolution in chapters 5–6.

Kidner Notes

kidner
4:8–16

4:23–24

Kidner: "The work stopped. Sometimes God’s people must simply endure delay. Eighteen years of nothing is a long test of faith — but it was not the end of the story."

Scholarly Debates

The Chronological Displacement of 4:6–23
Thematic arrangement
Williamson, Kidner, most moderns
The letters to Xerxes (v.6) and Artaxerxes (vv.7–23) postdate the temple completion. They are inserted here to collect all opposition episodes before the resolution in chapters 5–6.
Strictly chronological
Some older commentators
The text should be read as sequential. Ahasuerus and Artaxerxes may be alternative names for earlier Persian kings.
The thematic reading is overwhelmingly preferred by modern scholarship. The narrator groups opposition to make a theological point: resistance is constant, but God’s purpose prevails.
