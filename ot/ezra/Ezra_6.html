Ezra 6

Temple Completed and Dedicated

Verses 1–12 — Darius Finds the Decree; Orders Full Support
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

דָּרְיָוֶשׁ Dārəyāweš — Darius: Darius I (522–486 BC) searched the archives and found Cyrus’s original decree at Ecbatana. The discovery transforms Tattenai’s investigation from a potential obstruction into a royal endorsement with imperial funding.

אֶכְבַּתְנָא ʾEkḇatnāʾ — Ecbatana: The decree was found at Ecbatana (modern Hamadan, Iran) — the Median summer capital, not in Babylon where Tattenai suggested searching. The Persian court moved seasonally; records followed the king.

Historical Context

Ecbatana was the old Median capital, used as a summer residence by Persian kings. Royal archives were maintained at multiple capitals (Babylon, Susa, Ecbatana, Persepolis). The fact that the decree was found at Ecbatana, not Babylon, shows Cyrus may have issued it during a summer stay. The archival discovery is entirely consistent with known Persian administrative practice.

Context

Darius goes beyond confirming Cyrus’s decree — he expands it. He orders imperial funding from Trans-Euphrates tax revenues, commands Tattenai to stay away, and threatens death to anyone who interferes. The investigation that could have ended the project instead secures it with greater resources than before. God turns bureaucratic threat into bureaucratic blessing.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezra 1:1–4The original Cyrus decree — now confirmed by Darius from the archives.
  • Prov 21:1"The king’s heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD" — demonstrated again through Darius.
  • Isa 45:13"He will rebuild my city and set my exiles free" — fulfilled through successive Persian kings.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1–2

The scroll (megîllâ) was found at Ecbatana. MacArthur notes that Cyrus issued the decree early in his reign, possibly before the court moved to Babylon. The archival system of Persia preserved it for nearly twenty years.

6:3–5

The Aramaic memorandum specifies temple dimensions (90 feet high, 90 feet wide) and construction materials (three courses of stone, one of timber). These are administrative specifications, not liturgical instructions.

6:8–10

Darius orders imperial funding from provincial taxes. The Persian treasury pays for a Jewish temple. He also requests prayer for the royal family — a standard Persian-period quid pro quo.

6:11–12

The death penalty for anyone altering the decree: "May God, who has caused his Name to dwell there, overthrow any king or people who lifts a hand to change this decree." Darius invokes YHWH’s protection of the temple — a pagan king safeguarding Jewish worship.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1–2

Calvin: "God disposed all things so that the very search which the enemies intended for destruction became the means of deliverance. So it ever is: the counsel of the wicked is turned against themselves."

6:10

The prayer request: Calvin notes the pragmatic wisdom — "They prayed for the king who funded them. Gratitude and intercession are fitting responses to earthly benefactors."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:2

The NET notes that Ecbatana (Aramaic ʾAḥmətāʾ) is identified with modern Hamadan in western Iran, at an elevation of 6,000 feet — an ideal summer capital.

6:11

"Let a beam be pulled from his house" — the NET observes this punishment is attested in Assyrian and Persian legal texts: the offender’s own property becomes the instrument of execution.

Williamson Notes

williamson
6:3–5

Williamson analyses the Aramaic memorandum’s format as a dikrona (memorandum) — an internal record, not a public proclamation. This explains why it differs from the Hebrew public version in 1:2–4: different document type, different audience.

6:11

The impalement threat is attested in Persian legal practice. Williamson notes Darius’s Behistun inscription uses similar language for violators of royal decrees.

Verses 13–22 — Temple Dedicated; Passover Celebrated
13141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

חֲנֻכַּה ḥănukkâ — dedication: The temple dedication (ḥănukkâ) uses the same word as the later Hanukkah celebration (165 BC). Every temple dedication in Israel is a ḥănukkâ — the word means "initiation" or "consecration." The festival of Hanukkah commemorates the Maccabean re-dedication but the word is anchored here.

פֶּסַח pesaḥ — Passover: The Passover celebration (pesaḥ) immediately follows the dedication. The sequence is deliberate: the temple is dedicated, then the founding festival of Israel’s identity is celebrated within it. Worship and redemption history converge.

Historical Context

The temple was completed on 3 Adar (March 516 BC) — exactly 70 years after its destruction in 586 BC. The precision of the Jeremiah fulfilment is striking: from destruction to completion, seventy years to the month. The dedication was modest compared to Solomon’s (100 bulls vs 22,000) but the joy was genuine.

Context

The Aramaic section ends at v.18, and the text returns to Hebrew for the Passover narrative (vv.19–22). The language shift marks a transition from imperial-administrative to covenant-liturgical. The Passover is described in Hebrew because it belongs to Israel’s internal life, not to Persian administration.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 8:62–66Solomon’s temple dedication — the model this celebration echoes on a smaller scale.
  • Exod 12:1–14The original Passover — now celebrated in a rebuilt temple by a restored people.
  • 2 Chr 35:1–19Josiah’s great Passover — the last before the exile. Ezra’s Passover resumes the tradition.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:14–15

The temple was completed "by command of the God of Israel and the decrees of Cyrus, Darius and Artaxerxes." Three Persian kings are credited alongside God’s command. The narrator sees no tension: God’s sovereignty works through imperial decrees.

6:16–17

The dedication offerings (100 bulls, 200 rams, 400 lambs, 12 goats) are modest next to Solomon’s (22,000 cattle, 120,000 sheep). But the 12 goats "for the twelve tribes of Israel" are theologically crucial: the restored community still claims to represent all Israel, not just Judah.

6:19–20

The priests and Levites had purified themselves — all of them "were ceremonially clean." The restored community takes holiness seriously from the start.

6:21–22

Gentile converts participate: those "who had separated themselves from the unclean practices of their Gentile neighbours." The community is not ethnically sealed; it is covenantally defined. Outsiders who choose YHWH are welcomed.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:16–17

Calvin: "Though the number of sacrifices was small compared to Solomon’s, the devotion was no less. God does not measure worship by quantity but by sincerity."

6:22

Calvin on joy: "This joy was not human excitement but a gift of God. He who had turned the heart of a Persian king could also fill the hearts of his people with gladness."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:15

The NET notes a textual variant: some manuscripts read "3 Adar" while 1 Esdras 7:5 reads "23 Adar." The Masoretic "3 Adar" is generally preferred.

6:21

The NET observes that "all who had separated themselves" likely refers to proselytes — non-Jews who abandoned idolatry and joined the covenant community. The restoration is not purely ethnic.

Williamson Notes

williamson
6:15

Williamson dates the completion to 12 March 516 BC (3 Adar, Darius year 6). He notes the seventy-year interval from 586 BC matches Jeremiah’s prophecy precisely when counted temple-to-temple.

6:19–22

The shift from Aramaic to Hebrew at v.19 signals a genre change: from administrative narrative to liturgical celebration. Williamson: the language change is deliberate and meaningful.

Kidner Notes

kidner
6:16–17

Kidner on the 12 goats: "A tiny detail with enormous theological weight. Twelve goats for twelve tribes — though only two and a half tribes stood there. The other tribes were lost, scattered, absorbed. But God’s people still numbered twelve. Faith counted what sight could not."

6:22

Kidner: "The LORD had filled them with joy — the same word used of the king of Assyria, whose heart the LORD had 'turned.' God fills hearts with joy as readily as he turns them to action."
