Ezra 5

The Prophets and Tattenai’s Investigation

Verses 1–5 — Haggai and Zechariah Prophesy; Work Restarts
12345

Hebrew Word Study

נְבִיא nəḇîʾ — prophet: Haggai and Zechariah are called nəḇîʾē — prophets who speak "in the name of the God of Israel." Their intervention breaks the eighteen-year silence. Prophecy restarts the work that bureaucracy halted.

עַיִן ʿayin — eye: "The eye of their God was watching over" (ʿayin ʾelāhăhōn) the elders. The anthropomorphism is deliberately intimate: God does not merely know — he watches. Protective surveillance.

Historical Context

The prophetic books of Haggai (520 BC) and Zechariah (520–518 BC) provide the companion narrative to Ezra 5. Haggai’s message was blunt: "Is it a time for you yourselves to be living in your panelled houses, while this house remains a ruin?" (Hag 1:4). Zechariah offered visionary hope: "Not by might nor by power, but by my Spirit" (Zech 4:6). Together they broke the community’s paralysis.

Context

Eighteen years of silence end with two prophets. The pattern is consistent throughout Scripture: when human effort stalls, God sends a word. The prophets do not bring new instructions — the Cyrus decree still stands. They bring renewed motivation. Sometimes the only thing needed is a voice that says: start again.

Cross-Reference

  • Zech 4:6–10"Not by might nor by power, but by my Spirit" — and "Who dares despise the day of small things?"
  • Hag 2:3–9"The glory of this present house will be greater than the glory of the former."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1

Haggai and Zechariah prophesy "in the name of the God of Israel, who was over them." The phrase establishes divine authority behind human speech. The prophets speak; God acts.

5:1–2

Zerubbabel and Jeshua respond immediately to the prophetic word. Eighteen years of inaction end in a single verse. The power of the prophetic word is measured by the response it produces.

5:3–4

Tattenai, governor of Trans-Euphrates, investigates. Unlike the earlier adversaries, Tattenai is not hostile — he is doing his administrative duty. The question is legitimate: who authorised this construction?

5:5

"The eye of their God was watching over the elders of the Jews" — divine protection expressed through imperial procedure. Tattenai does not halt the work pending investigation, which was within his authority. God restrains him.

Williamson Notes

williamson
5:1

Williamson dates Haggai’s ministry precisely to August–December 520 BC (Hag 1:1; 2:1, 10, 20). Zechariah began October 520 BC (Zech 1:1). The prophetic intervention is historically anchored.

5:3–5

Tattenai’s title is "governor of Trans-Euphrates" (paḥat ʿAḇar Naharâ) — attested in Babylonian administrative texts. Williamson notes he reports to the satrap, not directly to the king. His investigation follows proper Persian procedure.

Verses 6–17 — Tattenai’s Letter to Darius
67891011121314151617

Hebrew Word Study

נִשְׁתְוָן ništəwān — letter / written document: The Aramaic term for an official letter (ništəwān) is a Persian loanword, reflecting the administrative culture. The letter’s tone is notably neutral — Tattenai reports facts without editorial hostility, unlike the letter in chapter 4.

Historical Context

Tattenai’s letter is a model of Persian administrative procedure: factual, neutral, requesting verification. He reports what the Jews told him (Cyrus authorised the work) and asks Darius to search the archives. The contrast with the hostile letter of chapter 4 is striking — not all imperial officials are enemies.

Context

The Jews’ response to Tattenai (vv.11–16) is a compressed theological history: they are servants of the God of heaven; their ancestors angered him and were handed over to Nebuchadnezzar; Cyrus decreed the temple’s rebuilding. They take responsibility for the exile (it was their sin) while affirming God’s sovereignty (he used Nebuchadnezzar, he stirred Cyrus).

Cross-Reference

  • Ezra 1:1–4The Cyrus decree the Jews cite as their authorisation — Tattenai asks Darius to verify it.
  • 2 Chr 36:15–21The exilic summary the Jews’ response echoes: sin → warning → judgment → exile.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:6–7

Tattenai writes "To King Darius: Cordial greetings." The formula is respectful, professional. He is a bureaucrat, not a zealot. His letter is remarkably fair.

5:11–12

The Jews’ theological self-description: "We are the servants of the God of heaven and earth." They do not claim innocence — "our ancestors angered the God of heaven." Honest confession is their strongest argument.

5:14–16

The Jews cite Cyrus’s decree and Sheshbazzar’s role. They have institutional memory: even after eighteen years of inaction, they remember the authorisation and name the appointee.

5:17

Tattenai asks Darius to "search the royal archives of Babylon." He does not prejudge; he asks for evidence. The letter is a model of fair administrative practice — which God will use to vindicate his people.

NET Bible Notes

Williamson Notes

williamson
5:6–7

Williamson analyses the letter’s Aramaic formulae and finds them consistent with attested Persian epistolary conventions. The structure (address, greeting, report, request) is standard.

5:14–16

Williamson notes the Jews name Sheshbazzar (not Zerubbabel) as the original appointee. This suggests Sheshbazzar is the historically earlier figure, now no longer active, with Zerubbabel as his successor.

5:11–16

5:17

Kidner: "Tattenai’s letter might have been a threat; it turned out to be a lifeline. The search he requested would uncover the very decree that authorised everything."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Tattenai
Governor of Trans-Euphrates; neutral investigator
Unlike the hostile adversaries of chapter 4, Tattenai conducts a fair investigation. He reports facts, does not halt the work, and asks for archival verification. His neutrality becomes the mechanism through which God vindicates the restoration.
Haggai
Prophet; breaks the eighteen-year silence
Prophesied August–December 520 BC. His message is blunt and practical: stop building your own houses and build God’s house. His oracles are preserved in the book that bears his name.
Zechariah
Prophet; visionary companion to Haggai
Began prophesying October 520 BC. Where Haggai is practical, Zechariah is visionary — his night visions and messianic oracles provide the theological framework for the restoration.
