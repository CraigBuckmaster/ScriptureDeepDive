Ezra 10

The Public Assembly and the Covenant

Verses 1–17 — Shecaniah’s Proposal; Assembly in the Rain
1234567891011121314151617

Hebrew Word Study

תִּקְוָה tiqwâ — hope: Shecaniah: "In spite of this, there is still hope (tiqwâ) for Israel." The word tiqwâ carries the sense of expectation, a cord stretched toward the future. Hope exists not because the sin is minor but because God’s character is greater than the sin.

מַעַל maʿal — unfaithfulness / treachery: The sin is called maʿal — covenant treachery. The same word describes Achan’s theft (Josh 7:1) and Saul’s disobedience (1 Chr 10:13). It is the vocabulary of high betrayal, not minor transgression.

Historical Context

The assembly is called for the twentieth day of the ninth month (December 458 BC). The text notes "all the people sat in the square before the house of God, greatly distressed by the matter and because of the heavy rain" (v.9). December rains in Jerusalem are cold and heavy. The physical discomfort mirrors the spiritual discomfort.

Context

Shecaniah’s proposal (v.2–4) is remarkable: he proposes putting away the foreign wives "according to the law" — though no Torah law explicitly mandates divorce in cases of existing intermarriage. The prohibition is against contracting such marriages, not dissolving them. The community innovates within Torah principles to address a crisis the Torah did not directly anticipate.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 7:1–4The prohibition against intermarriage that grounds the crisis.
  • Mal 2:13–16"I hate divorce, says the LORD" — the tension. God hates both the intermarriage and the dissolution.
  • Neh 13:23–27Nehemiah confronts the same issue but uses confrontation rather than assembly.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1

While Ezra prays, "a large crowd of Israelites — men, women and children — gathered around him. They too wept bitterly." Ezra’s grief becomes the community’s grief. Leadership by lament.

10:2–3

Shecaniah proposes a covenant to "send away" the foreign wives and children. The proposal comes from a layman, not from Ezra. The community takes ownership of the solution.

10:9

The assembly sits in heavy rain, "trembling because of this matter and because of the rain." Physical misery compounds spiritual anguish. The narrator captures both without editorialising.

10:12–14

The assembly agrees but requests time: "This matter cannot be taken care of in a day or two." A three-month investigation process is established. Urgency tempered by due process.

10:15

Four men oppose the plan — the text records their dissent without condemning them. Williamson reads this as procedural objection, not moral disagreement.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1

Calvin: "The tears of the people were the best evidence of repentance. Not their words but their weeping showed that God’s Spirit was at work."

10:2–3

Calvin on Shecaniah’s proposal: "Hope is the indispensable companion of repentance. Without hope, confession leads to despair. With hope, even the hardest obedience becomes possible."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:3

The NET notes "according to the counsel of my lord" (baʿaṣat ʾadonî) refers to Ezra, not God. Shecaniah appeals to Ezra’s authority as the expert in Torah interpretation.

10:9

The ninth month (Kislev) corresponds to November–December. The NET observes Jerusalem’s winter rains average 5–6 inches in December — substantial rainfall for an outdoor assembly.

Williamson Notes

williamson
10:3

Williamson discusses the legal basis: the Torah prohibits forming these marriages (Deut 7:3) but does not prescribe divorce for existing ones. The community’s action is an extension of Torah principle, not a direct Torah command. This makes it a case of applied halakhah.

10:15

The four objectors: Williamson argues they oppose the procedure (too hasty, investigate case by case) rather than the principle. Their dissent is recorded, not suppressed — a sign of legitimate process.

Kidner Notes

kidner
10:1

Kidner: "Ezra’s tears produced more tears. A leader’s grief, when genuine, is more persuasive than any sermon."

10:2

Shecaniah: "There is still hope." Kidner: "The most important sentence in the chapter. Without hope, confession is despair. Hope is what makes repentance possible rather than pointless."

10:9

The rain: Kidner notes the narrator’s understatement. "The people are soaked, shivering, and facing the dissolution of their families. The physical world mirrors the spiritual one."

Verses 18–44 — List of Those Who Had Married Foreign Women

Hebrew Word Study

אָשֵׁם ʾāšēm — guilty: The list begins with those who "acknowledged their guilt" (ʾāšēmû). The public listing is not punitive shaming but transparency: the community records its failures as honestly as its genealogies.

Context

The list names 111 men — 17 priests, 6 Levites, 1 singer, 3 gatekeepers, and 84 laypeople. The priests are listed first because their guilt is greatest: they are the guardians of holiness. The book ends abruptly with this list, without resolution narrative. We are not told whether all divorces occurred, how the women and children fared, or whether the reforms held. The open ending is deliberate: the work of faithfulness is never finished.

Cross-Reference

  • Neh 13:28–29Even the high priest’s family intermarried — Nehemiah confronts Joiada’s son. The problem persists.
  • Mal 2:10–16Malachi addresses the same era — the prophet’s perspective on the crisis.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:18–19

The priests listed first "pledged themselves to put away their wives, and being guilty, presented a ram from the flock as a guilt offering." Confession + sacrifice = restoration. The priestly families model the complete process.

10:20–43

The list is arranged by class (priests, Levites, singers, gatekeepers, laypeople) — not by severity. Every tier of the community is represented. No class is exempt from accountability.

10:44

"All these had married foreign women, and some of them had children by these wives." The final sentence acknowledges the human cost. Children are mentioned — the text does not pretend this was painless.

10:44

The book ends without a triumphant conclusion. No celebration, no vindication. Just a list and a fact. The open ending is the narrator’s final theological statement: faithfulness is a process, not a destination.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:18–19

Calvin: "The priests’ guilt offering is the right response: not merely words but sacrifice. Repentance that costs nothing is worth nothing."

10:44

Calvin on the ending: "The book does not conclude with triumph but with obedience. The church’s life is not a series of victories but a daily discipline of faithfulness."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:19

The NET notes the guilt offering ram parallels Lev 5:14–19 — the āšām sacrifice for inadvertent covenant violation.

10:44

The NET acknowledges the textual difficulty of the final clause and offers the reading: "some of them had wives by whom they had children" — underscoring the human complexity.

Williamson Notes

williamson
10:18–43

Williamson analyses the list’s structure and finds it geographically organised within each class — suggesting an administrative investigation that proceeded district by district.

10:44

The Hebrew of the final clause is notoriously difficult. Williamson translates: "and they sent them away, women together with children." The abruptness is a literary choice: the narrator refuses to smooth the edges.

Kidner Notes

kidner
10:18–19

Kidner notes the guilt offering (ram) is the prescribed sacrifice for covenant violation (Lev 5:14–19). The priests follow Torah procedure for their own failure — which is both ironic and appropriate.

10:44

Kidner on the abrupt ending: "The book closes with a list, not a hymn. There is no fanfare, no celebration. Obedience is quiet, costly, and incomplete. That is what faithfulness looks like in a fallen world."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Shecaniah
Layman who proposes the covenant
Son of Jehiel — whose family appears in the guilt list (v.26). Shecaniah proposes the solution even though his own family is implicated. His word "there is still hope" is the chapter’s theological key.

Scholarly Debates

The Ethics of the Mass Divorce
Justified by covenant priority
Kidner, MacArthur, most evangelical readings
The survival of the covenant community required radical action. Syncretism had caused the exile; preventing its recurrence justified the painful measure. The marriages themselves violated Torah.
Morally troubling
Eskenazi, Williamson, many critical scholars
The text itself hints at discomfort: children are mentioned, four men object, the book ends without celebration. The narrator does not whitewash the cost. Malachi 2:16 ("I hate divorce") stands in tension with Ezra 10.
Both readings have textual support. The narrator presents the action as necessary without pretending it was costless. The abrupt ending and the mention of children suggest the author is aware of the moral complexity. Faithfulness sometimes involves choosing between imperfect options.

Theological Themes Tracker

Covenant peaks at 10: the entire chapter is about covenant faithfulness and its cost. Holiness and justice are high — accountability is the theme. The open ending refuses false closure.
