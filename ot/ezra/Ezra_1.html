Ezra 1

The Decree of Cyrus; The Temple Vessels Returned

Verses 1–4 — The Decree of Cyrus
1234

Hebrew Word Study

רוּחַ rûaḥ — spirit / wind / breath: The LORD "stirred up the spirit" (hēʿîr ʾet-rûaḥ) of Cyrus — the same verb used of God stirring Samson (Judg 13:25) and the exiles’ desire to return (Hag 1:14). God moves pagan kings by moving their spirits.

פָּקַד pāqaḏ — to appoint / visit / attend to: Cyrus says God "appointed" (pāqaḏ) him to build the temple — the same word used for God "visiting" his people in the Exodus (Exod 3:16). A Persian emperor uses covenantal vocabulary.

קוֹל qôl — voice / proclamation: Cyrus caused a "voice" to pass through the kingdom and put it in writing. The dual proclamation (oral + written) is the ANE standard for royal decrees, echoing the dual proclamation at Sinai.

Historical Context

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC), discovered in Babylon in 1879 and now in the British Museum, confirms Cyrus’s policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands and restoring their temples. The cylinder credits Marduk for choosing Cyrus — exactly paralleling Ezra 1:1’s claim that YHWH stirred Cyrus’s spirit. Isaiah 44:28–45:1 had named Cyrus by name generations earlier as God’s "shepherd" and "anointed."

Context

2 Chronicles 36:22–23 and Ezra 1:1–3 are virtually identical — the Chronicler ends with the decree, Ezra begins with it. The books form a continuous narrative: judgment (2 Chr 36:15–21) followed by restoration (Ezra 1:1). The decree fulfils Jeremiah’s seventy-year prophecy (Jer 25:11–12; 29:10): from 605 BC (first deportation) to 538 BC is approximately 67 years; from 586 BC (temple destruction) to 516 BC (temple completion) is exactly 70.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

The opening phrase anchors the return in secular history. Cyrus conquered Babylon in 539 BC; the decree went out in 538. God uses world empires to accomplish covenant purposes — Assyria for judgment (Isa 10:5), Persia for restoration.

1:1

"The LORD moved the heart of Cyrus" — the verb "stirred up" (hēʿîr) indicates divine initiative operating through human decision. Proverbs 21:1: "The king’s heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD."

1:2

Cyrus attributes his commission to "the LORD, the God of heaven." Whether Cyrus believed this personally or was diplomatically accommodating Jewish theology is debated; what matters is that the text affirms God’s sovereignty over the decree regardless.

1:3–4

The command for neighbours to provide silver, gold, goods, and livestock echoes the Exodus plunder (Exod 12:35–36). The second exodus pattern is deliberate.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

Calvin: "God holds the hearts of kings in his hand and turns them whithersoever he pleases. Cyrus was the instrument; God was the agent. Let us learn that no monarchy is so powerful that it can resist the decree of God."

1:3–4

The freewill offering of neighbours alongside the return echoes the Exodus. Calvin: God demonstrates that the second deliverance follows the pattern of the first.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:1

The NET notes that "stirred up the spirit" (hēʿîr ʾet-rûaḥ) is used elsewhere for God’s providential direction of human motivation (1 Chr 5:26; 2 Chr 21:16; Hag 1:14). The construction implies internal motivation, not external compulsion.

1:2

"The God of heaven" (ʾelāhê haššāmayim) became the standard Jewish designation for YHWH in the Persian period — a title acceptable in polytheistic administrative contexts while preserving monotheistic content.

Williamson Notes

williamson
1:1

Williamson notes the "first year" is Cyrus’s first regnal year over Babylon (538 BC), not over Persia (559 BC). The Jeremiah fulfilment is calculated from 605 BC, the first deportation.

1:2–4

The Aramaic form of the decree (preserved in 6:3–5) differs from the Hebrew version here. Williamson argues both are authentic: 1:2–4 is the public proclamation for Jewish consumption; 6:3–5 is the administrative memo filed in the archives.

Kidner Notes

kidner
1:1

Kidner notes the stunning theological claim: the mightiest empire on earth is set in motion by one clause — "the LORD stirred up the spirit of Cyrus." The entire Persian imperial apparatus becomes a delivery mechanism for a Jewish homecoming.

1:2–3

Cyrus’s decree is cast in language the Jews could embrace: "the LORD, the God of heaven, has given me all the kingdoms." Yet the Cyrus Cylinder credits Marduk. Kidner: "God could use this ambiguity; it served his people without requiring a confession from the king."

Verses 5–11 — The Temple Vessels Returned to Sheshbazzar
567891011

Hebrew Word Study

שֵׁשְׁבַּצַּר šēšbaṣṣar — Sheshbazzar: The "prince of Judah" who receives the temple vessels. Identity debated: some identify him with Zerubbabel, others with Shenazzar son of Jehoiachin (1 Chr 3:18). The Babylonian name suggests someone raised in court culture.

כֵּלִים kēlîm — vessels / articles: The temple vessels taken by Nebuchadnezzar (2 Kgs 25:14–15) are now returned. Their preservation through 70 years of exile is a theological statement: God preserved the instruments of worship even when the temple was ashes.

Historical Context

The inventory of 5,400 vessels (v.11) is remarkably specific. The individual counts in vv.9–10 total only 2,499 — the discrepancy suggests the detailed list covers major items while the total includes smaller pieces. Persian administrative practice required meticulous inventories, attested in records from Persepolis and Susa.

Context

The return of the temple vessels closes a narrative arc from Daniel 1:2 ("the Lord delivered some of the articles") through Daniel 5:2–4 (Belshazzar drinks from them at his feast). The vessels’ journey — Jerusalem → Babylon → Jerusalem — mirrors the people’s journey. Both are restored.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 25:14–15Nebuchadnezzar takes the temple vessels — the beginning of the arc Ezra 1 closes.
  • Dan 1:2"The Lord delivered some of the articles from the temple" — even the exile was under God’s control.
  • Dan 5:2–4Belshazzar desecrates the vessels at his feast — the night Babylon falls to Cyrus.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:5

The response is led by "family heads of Judah and Benjamin, and the priests and Levites." The leadership structure is tribal-priestly — even after 70 years, the covenant community organises itself around its original institutions.

1:7–8

Nebuchadnezzar had placed these vessels in the temple of his gods. Now Cyrus brings them out. The gods of Babylon could not keep what belonged to the God of Israel.

1:9–11

The precise inventory (gold dishes: 30, silver dishes: 1,000, knives: 29, gold bowls: 30, silver bowls: 410, other articles: 1,000) demonstrates that not one article was lost.

1:11

Sheshbazzar "brought all these along with the exiles." The vessels and the people travel together — both restored, both going home.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:7–8

Calvin: "God watched over the vessels in the midst of Babylon, that they might be restored at the appointed time. So he watches over his church: though she seems lost, not one member perishes."

1:11

Before the temple was rebuilt, the instruments were secured. Calvin: "God begins restoration with the tools of worship, for worship is the purpose of the return."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:8

"Sheshbazzar the prince of Judah" uses the title naśîʾ — the term Ezekiel uses for the restored Davidic leader (Ezek 34:24; 37:25). The title may carry messianic overtones.

1:11

The NET observes the numerical discrepancy without resolving it, noting that ancient inventories commonly listed major categories while providing a total that included minor items.

Williamson Notes

williamson
1:8

Williamson identifies Mithredath as the Persian treasurer (gizbar, an Iranian loanword). The name means "given by Mithra" — Persian administrative machinery serving YHWH’s purposes.

1:11

The discrepancy between the itemised count (2,499) and total (5,400) has generated much discussion. Williamson suggests the list covers only the largest ceremonial items.

Kidner Notes

kidner
1:5–6

Kidner: "The neighbours’ generosity, like that of the Egyptians at the Exodus, was more than human goodwill: ‘everyone whose heart God had moved.’ Once again the divine initiative underlies the human response."

1:9–11

The careful inventory answers the question the exiles must have asked: "Is anything left?" Yes — everything. Not one piece missing. Kidner sees in this a parable of God’s faithfulness.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Cyrus
King of Persia; God’s anointed instrument
Named by Isaiah 150 years before his birth as God’s "shepherd" and "anointed" (Isa 44:28; 45:1). Conquered Babylon in 539 BC. His decree to return captive peoples is confirmed by the Cyrus Cylinder.
Sheshbazzar
Prince of Judah; carries the vessels home
Leads the first wave of returnees. Called "the prince of Judah" (naśîʾ). Some scholars identify him with Zerubbabel; others distinguish them. He laid the temple foundation (5:16) but Zerubbabel completed the work.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder
Discovered in Babylon (1879), now in the British Museum. Records Cyrus’s conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples and rebuilding temples. Credits Marduk for choosing Cyrus — exactly paralleling Ezra’s claim that YHWH stirred Cyrus.
Josephus, Antiquities XI.1
Josephus reports that Cyrus was shown the Isaiah prophecy and was "seized by a desire and ambition to fulfil what was written." Whether historical or legendary, the tradition shows how deeply Jews connected the decree to prophetic fulfilment.

Reception History

Second Exodus Theology
Jewish and Christian interpreters read Ezra 1 through the lens of a "second exodus": neighbours’ gifts echo Egyptian plunder (Exod 12:35–36); the journey from Babylon parallels Egypt to Canaan; the return culminates in temple-building, just as the first exodus culminated in the tabernacle.

Literary Structure

Decree (Divine Initiative) — 1:1–4God moves the empire. The sovereign act that makes everything else possible.
Response (Human Obedience) — 1:5–6"Everyone whose heart God had moved" — Spirit-prompted action.
Inventory (Covenant Continuity) — 1:7–11The meticulous vessel count proves nothing was lost. What God consecrates, he preserves.

Scholarly Debates

Who Was Sheshbazzar?
Sheshbazzar = Zerubbabel
Some Talmudic sources, Berger
Both are called "governor" and credited with laying the foundation. Possibly the same person with different names.
Sheshbazzar ≠ Zerubbabel
Williamson, Japhet, most moderns
Different genealogies. Sheshbazzar may be Shenazzar (1 Chr 3:18). He disappears; Zerubbabel completes the work. Two generations, two leaders.
The majority view distinguishes them: Sheshbazzar led the first wave; Zerubbabel led the completion after a hiatus.

Theological Themes Tracker

Ezra 1 is dominated by sovereignty (God moves Cyrus) and prophecy fulfilled (Jeremiah’s seventy years). Mercy scores high — the exile ends because God remembers his covenant.
