Ezra 3

The Altar and the Foundation

Verses 1–6 — The Altar Rebuilt; Sacrifices Resume
123456

Hebrew Word Study

מִזְבֵּחַ mizbēaḥ — altar: The altar is rebuilt first — before the temple, before the walls, before houses. Worship precedes infrastructure. The altar was built "on its foundation" (mekōnō) — the same spot, the same God, the same purpose.

עוֹלָה ōlâ — burnt offering / whole offering: The tāmîd (daily) burnt offerings resume immediately. The ōlâ is the "ascending offering" — it goes up entirely to God. Nothing is kept. Total consecration.

Historical Context

The seventh month (Tishri) is the most sacred month in the Jewish calendar: it contains the Feast of Trumpets (1st), the Day of Atonement (10th), and the Feast of Tabernacles (15th–21st). The returnees’ first act in the seventh month — rebuilding the altar and celebrating Tabernacles — reestablishes the entire liturgical calendar in one month.

Context

The altar before the temple is theologically deliberate. In the tabernacle system, the bronze altar stood in the courtyard outside — it was the first point of contact between God and worshippers. You could have an altar without a temple (patriarchs did), but not a temple without an altar. The returnees prioritised correctly: access to God through sacrifice comes first.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 40:6The tabernacle altar placed first in the courtyard — the same priority the returnees follow.
  • Gen 8:20Noah’s first act after the flood: build an altar. The pattern recurs.
  • Lev 23:33–43The Feast of Tabernacles instructions that the returnees now follow.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1–2

The seventh month gathering echoes Neh 8:1. Jeshua and Zerubbabel lead — the priestly-royal partnership. They build the altar "despite their fear of the peoples around them" (v.3). Worship resumed under threat, not in safety.

3:3

"Despite their fear" — the returnees are surrounded by hostile populations. The altar is an act of defiance as much as devotion. They worship in the open before they have walls.

3:4–5

The Feast of Tabernacles is celebrated "as written" — the Torah is the authority, not tradition or convenience. The daily burnt offerings, New Moon sacrifices, and appointed feasts all resume.

3:6

Offerings began "on the first day of the seventh month, though the foundation of the LORD’s temple had not yet been laid." Worship does not wait for perfect conditions.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:2–3

Calvin: "They began with the altar because sacrifice is the ground of all worship. Without atonement there is no approach to God. The order is not accidental but theological."

3:6

Calvin praises those who worship before the temple is built: "They did not wait for perfection but began with what they had. God honours beginnings as well as completions."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:2

The NET notes that Jeshua (= Joshua) the high priest is distinct from the Joshua of the conquest. The name itself means "YHWH saves" and is the Hebrew form of "Jesus."

3:3

"Despite their fear" (bəʾemâ ālêhem) — the preposition āl with ʾemâ indicates fear "upon" them, i.e., weighing on them externally. They acted against their fear, not without it.

Williamson Notes

williamson
3:2

Williamson notes "as it is written in the Law of Moses" signals a Torah-based restoration. The community defines itself not by innovation but by return to the written standard.

3:4

The Tabernacles celebration fulfils Deut 16:16: all males appear before the LORD three times yearly. The first pilgrimage feast in the rebuilt community.

Kidner Notes

kidner
3:2–3

Kidner: "The altar before the temple is the right order. You do not need a building to worship — you need a sacrifice. The patriarchs knew this; the returnees rediscovered it."

Verses 7–13 — The Temple Foundation Laid; Weeping and Shouting
78910111213

Hebrew Word Study

יִסַּד yissaḏ — he founded / he laid the foundation: The foundation-laying (yāsaḏ) is a theological act: the temple’s foundation is also the community’s foundation. The wordplay is intentional — to found the temple is to re-found the covenant community.

בָּכָה bākâ — to weep: The old men "wept with a loud voice" while the young "shouted for joy." The same event produces opposite responses. The Hebrew ēyn makkîr ("no one could distinguish") is remarkable — grief and joy became one sound.

Historical Context

Solomon’s temple (destroyed 586 BC) had been one of the wonders of the ancient world. The second temple’s foundation was modest by comparison. Haggai 2:3 confirms the disparity: "Who of you is left who saw this house in its former glory? How does it look to you now? Does it not seem to you like nothing?" The old men’s weeping was for what was lost.

Context

The weeping-and-shouting paradox (v.12–13) is one of the most psychologically truthful moments in Scripture. Both responses are valid; the narrator refuses to judge either. The community contains both grief for what was lost and joy for what is beginning. This is what restoration actually feels like — not pure triumph, but complicated hope.

Cross-Reference

  • Hag 2:3–9"The glory of this present house will be greater than the glory of the former" — Haggai’s answer to the weepers.
  • Ps 126:5–6"Those who sow with tears will reap with songs of joy" — the theological resolution of Ezra 3’s paradox.
  • Zech 4:10"Who dares despise the day of small things?" — Zechariah’s rebuke to those who compare the new temple unfavourably.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:7

Cedar from Lebanon — the same source Solomon used (1 Kgs 5:6). Cyrus’s authorisation funds it. The Persian empire bankrolls a Jewish temple — the irony of providence.

3:8–9

Levites from age twenty and upward supervise. The age lowered from thirty (Num 4:3) reflects the personnel shortage — the community adapts within Torah parameters.

3:10–11

The foundation ceremony mirrors Solomon’s dedication (2 Chr 5:13): priests with trumpets, Levites with cymbals, antiphonal praise. The liturgy is identical; only the scale differs.

3:12–13

The weeping of the old and the joy of the young are not in competition. Both are acts of faith. The old weep because they remember; the young shout because they hope. Neither is wrong.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:11

Calvin: "‘His love endures forever’ — this was the sum of their theology. After exile, after loss, after seventy years, the one thing they affirmed was God’s steadfast love. This is the heart of faith."

3:12–13

Calvin reads the old men’s weeping charitably: "They did not weep from unbelief but from a righteous grief that the glory of God’s house was diminished. Both weeping and joy proceeded from godly affection."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:10

The NET notes that "with praise and thanksgiving" follows the Chronicler’s theology: true worship requires both sacrifice (altar) and praise (music). The second temple includes both from the first day.

3:12

The NET observes that "many of the older priests and Levites" who had seen Solomon’s temple must have been at least 70–80 years old — children when the first temple fell, now witnessing its replacement.

Williamson Notes

williamson
3:10

Williamson notes the deliberate echoes of 2 Chr 5:13 and 1 Chr 16:34 in the liturgical formula. The second temple consciously replicates the first temple’s worship forms.

3:12–13

The phrase "no one could distinguish" (ēyn makkîr) is a striking narrative comment. Williamson: the narrator makes a literary point about the indistinguishability of grief and hope in communal restoration.

Kidner Notes

kidner
3:10–11

Kidner: "The liturgy is straight from David’s playbook (1 Chr 16:34). The words ‘He is good; his love endures forever’ bridge the gap between Solomon’s temple and this one."

3:12–13

Kidner’s finest observation on Ezra: "Tears and cheers were inextricably mixed. This is how hope sounds when it arrives in a world of loss — not with a clean note but with a chord."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Jeshua
High priest; leads the altar reconstruction
Grandson of Seraiah, the last pre-exilic high priest. Jeshua’s leadership of the altar rebuilding establishes the priestly succession: the same family, the same altar site, the same God.
Zerubbabel
Governor; Davidic co-leader
Alongside Jeshua, leads the foundation ceremony. The dual leadership of governor and priest mirrors Moses and Aaron, Joshua and Eleazar.

Literary Structure

Sacrifice resumes before the temple exists. Access to God is the first priority.
Foundation Laid (Continuity Restored) — 3:7–11Cedar from Lebanon, Levitical musicians, Davidic liturgy — the forms of Solomon’s temple recreated.
Weeping and Shouting (Complicated Hope) — 3:12–13Both responses are valid. Restoration is not pure triumph but honest, mixed emotion.
