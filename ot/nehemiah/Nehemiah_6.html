Nehemiah 6

Sanballat’s Final Schemes; Wall Completed in 52 Days

Verses 1–9 — Four Invitations to Ono; All Refused
123456789

Hebrew Word Study

מְלְאכָה məlāʾkâ — work / task / business: "I am doing a great work (məlāʾkâ) and cannot come down." Nehemiah’s most quoted line. The word məlāʾkâ is the same word used for God’s work in creation (Gen 2:2). The wall is not a project but a calling.

Historical Context

The plain of Ono (modern Kafr Ana) was in neutral territory between Judah and Samaria — a natural meeting point. But it was also far from Jerusalem’s defences. An "invitation to negotiate" in open territory was an invitation to assassination. Nehemiah saw through it instantly.

Context

Four identical invitations, four identical refusals. Then a fifth attempt: an open letter accusing Nehemiah of planning rebellion (vv.5–7). Each escalation tests whether Nehemiah will leave the wall. He never does. Focus is his defining trait: "I am doing a great work and cannot come down."

Cross-Reference

  • Neh 2:19–20The first accusation of rebellion — the same weapon, recycled.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1–4

Four times: "Come, let us meet together." Four times: "I cannot come down." Nehemiah’s consistency is the victory. The first refusal is principle; the fourth is character.

6:2

"They were scheming to harm me." Nehemiah perceives the intent behind the invitation. Discernment is a leadership survival skill.

6:5–7

The open letter: "It is reported among the nations that you are about to become their king." The accusation is manufactured but dangerous — an open letter means it is public, designed to force a response.

6:8–9

"Nothing like what you are saying is happening; you are just making it up." Nehemiah denies the charge flatly, then prays: "Strengthen my hands." The prayer is one sentence. The work continues.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:3

Calvin: "The best defence against distraction is a clear sense of calling. Nehemiah did not argue; he simply stated his priority. The wall was greater than any meeting."

6:9

Calvin on "Strengthen my hands": "This one-sentence prayer sustained him through every scheme. He did not ask for the removal of enemies but for the strength to ignore them."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:2

The NET notes the "plain of Ono" is approximately 27 miles northwest of Jerusalem — a full day’s journey that would leave Nehemiah exposed.

6:5

An "open letter" (sepher pātûaḥ) was intentionally unsealed so intermediaries could read it. The NET observes this was a deliberate act of public pressure.

Williamson Notes

williamson
6:2

Williamson identifies Kephirim in the plain of Ono as a location controlled by neither Judah nor Samaria — ostensibly neutral but far from Nehemiah’s power base and security.

6:6–7

The mention of "prophets to proclaim about you" suggests Sanballat was fabricating evidence of a coronation plot. Williamson notes this is classic Persian-period political sabotage: accusing a vassal of royal ambitions was a capital charge.

Kidner Notes

kidner
6:3

Kidner: "I am doing a great work. The simple sentence is his shield against every distraction. When you know your calling, you can refuse every alternative without guilt."

6:5–8

Kidner: "An open letter was a calculated insult — diplomacy made public. It was designed to embarrass Nehemiah into responding. His response: ‘You made it up.’ Then back to work."

Verses 10–14 — Shemaiah’s False Prophecy
1011121314

Hebrew Word Study

נָבִיא nāḇîʾ — prophet: Shemaiah presents himself as a prophet with a divine warning. But Nehemiah perceives that "God had not sent him" — the test of Deut 18:22. False prophecy is the most insidious weapon because it wears God’s voice as a disguise.

Context

"Should a man like me run away? Should someone like me go into the temple to save his life? I will not go!" (v.11). Nehemiah refuses on two grounds: (1) running is beneath his calling, and (2) entering the temple sanctuary as a non-priest would violate Torah. The false prophet’s advice is designed to either kill him (assassination in the temple) or discredit him (entering a space reserved for priests).

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 18:20–22The test of a false prophet: a word that does not come from God.
  • 1 Kgs 13:1–26The man of God deceived by a false prophet — the cautionary tale.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:10

Shemaiah claims divine warning: "Let us meet in the house of God, inside the temple, and let us close the doors, because men are coming to kill you." The advice sounds spiritual but is designed to trap.

6:11

"Should a man like me run?" Nehemiah’s question is rhetorical and devastating. A governor does not hide. A man of God does not violate God’s law to save God’s servant.

6:12

"I realised that God had not sent him." Discernment. The advice sounded plausible; the source was false. Nehemiah tested the word against God’s character and found it wanting.

6:14

"Remember Tobiah and Sanballat... and also the prophet Noadiah and how they tried to intimidate me." A prophetess (Noadiah) is named among the opposition — false prophecy is not gender-specific.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:11

Calvin: "Nehemiah refused to save his life by sinning. A leader who purchases safety at the cost of integrity has lost both."

6:12

Calvin: "The greatest danger is a false word that comes dressed in God’s clothing. Nehemiah tested the prophet by the law and found him wanting."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:10

The NET notes "closed in" (āṣûr) could mean under prophetic restriction or house arrest. Either way, the setting is designed to create an atmosphere of urgency and secrecy.

6:11

The NET observes Nehemiah’s refusal to enter the temple reflects Num 18:7: only Aaronide priests could enter the sanctuary. Nehemiah, a layman, would have sinned by entering.

Williamson Notes

williamson
6:10

Williamson notes Shemaiah is "confined to his house" — possibly a symbolic prophetic act (cf. Ezek 3:24) designed to lend credibility to his warning. The theatrics make the deception more sophisticated.

6:14

Noadiah the prophetess: Williamson observes she is the only named female prophet in the post-exilic period. Her inclusion shows that prophetic opposition could come from multiple sources.

Kidner Notes

kidner
6:11

Kidner: "The twin refusals are magnificent: I will not run (because of who I am) and I will not enter (because of whose house it is). Courage and reverence in one breath."

6:12

Kidner: "He realised. Not ‘felt’ or ‘suspected’ but perceived with clarity. Discernment is a faculty, not a feeling."

Verses 15–19 — Wall Completed in 52 Days; Tobiah’s Network
1516171819

Hebrew Word Study

חֲמִשִּׁים ḥămiššîm — fifty: Fifty-two days. The wall of Jerusalem, ruined for over a century, rebuilt in less than two months. The speed itself is the testimony: this was done "with the help of our God" (v.16). Human effort at divine pace.

Context

The enemies’ response to the completion (v.16) is the chapter’s theological climax: "they were afraid and lost their self-confidence, because they realised that this work had been done with the help of our God." The wall is not merely defensive architecture — it is an argument. Its very existence testifies that God is real and active.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 127:1"Unless the LORD builds the house, the builders labour in vain" — the psalm the 52-day completion validates.
  • Zech 4:6"Not by might nor by power, but by my Spirit" — the prophecy the wall embodies.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:15

Fifty-two days: August 11 to October 2, 445 BC (25 Elul). The speed is miraculous not supernaturally but providentially — the entire community working under threat with total commitment.

6:16

"All the surrounding nations were afraid." The wall’s completion reverses the power dynamic. Those who mocked now fear. The wall is a witness.

6:17–19

Tobiah’s network of letters: he is connected by marriage to Jerusalem’s aristocracy. His opposition is not external but embedded within the community. "They kept reporting to me his good deeds" — internal propaganda.

6:16

"This work had been done with the help of our God." The narrator’s verdict. Not human engineering but divine enablement. The 52 days are the proof.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:15–16

Calvin: "In fifty-two days God accomplished what enemies said was impossible. The speed was not human; it was providential. Let all who doubt God’s power consider this wall."

6:16

Calvin: "Even the enemies recognised that God had done this. The wall silenced the mockers. Facts are more eloquent than arguments."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:15

The NET notes "twenty-fifth of Elul" corresponds to early October. The lunar date is precise — evidence of memoir-style record keeping.

6:16

The NET observes "this work had been done with the help of our God" (mēʾat ʾelōhēnû) is the narrator’s theological interpretation of the construction — the culmination of the "hand of God" theme.

Williamson Notes

williamson
6:15

Williamson dates the completion to 2 October 445 BC. The 52-day period has been questioned (Josephus says 2 years, 4 months) but Williamson defends the shorter period: the wall was repaired, not built from scratch. Existing foundations and partially standing sections reduced the work.

6:17–19

Williamson traces Tobiah’s family connections: his son Jehohanan married the daughter of Meshullam son of Berekiah (3:4, 30), a prominent builder. The opposition had allies among the very people who built the wall.

Kidner Notes

kidner
6:15–16

Kidner: "Fifty-two days. The enemies’ confidence collapsed because the calendar did not lie. No human explanation could account for the speed. The wall preached a sermon stone by stone."

6:17–19

Kidner on Tobiah’s letters: "The most dangerous enemy is the one with friends inside. Tobiah had a network of allies within Jerusalem who kept him informed and advocated for him. The wall could not keep out his influence."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Shemaiah
False prophet; hired by Tobiah and Sanballat
His temple-hiding advice was designed to either kill or discredit Nehemiah. Nehemiah perceived the deception and refused. Shemaiah represents the most dangerous form of opposition: false words wearing God’s authority.
