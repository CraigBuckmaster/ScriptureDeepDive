Nehemiah 13

Nehemiah’s Final Reforms

Verses 1–9 — Tobiah Expelled from the Temple Storeroom
123456789

Hebrew Word Study

לִשְׁכָה liškâ — chamber / room: Eliashib the priest gave Tobiah a large chamber (liškâ) in the temple courts — a storeroom previously used for offerings, incense, and vessels. The enemy inside the sanctuary. The high priest himself opened the door.

Historical Context

Nehemiah had returned to Artaxerxes in the thirty-second year (433 BC, cf. 13:6). During his absence, reforms collapsed. Eliashib, the same high priest who built the Sheep Gate (3:1), gave Tobiah — the arch-opponent — a room in the temple. Nehemiah returns to find the fox not just on the wall but inside the house.

Context

The chapter is structured as four reforms, each ending with "Remember me, my God." Nehemiah’s absence revealed how fragile the reforms were. Without sustained leadership, every gain reversed. The chapter is a warning: reformation requires maintenance, not just initiation.

Cross-Reference

  • Neh 2:10Tobiah first introduced as opponent — now he has a room in the temple.
  • Matt 21:12–13Jesus drives out the money-changers — the same cleansing impulse.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:1–3

Torah reading excludes Ammonites and Moabites (Deut 23:3–5). Tobiah is an Ammonite. The text itself demands his removal.

13:4–5

Eliashib "had been put in charge of the storerooms" and gave Tobiah "a large room." The betrayal is institutional, not merely personal. The high priest corrupted his office.

13:7–8

"I was greatly displeased and threw all Tobiah’s household goods out of the room." Physical, immediate, undiplomatical. Nehemiah does not negotiate with sacrilege.

13:9

"I gave orders to purify the rooms." Cleansing, then restoration. The equipment for offerings is returned to its proper place.

Verses 10–14 — Levites Recalled; Tithes Restored
1011121314

Hebrew Word Study

זָכְרָה־לִי zāk̅râ-lî — remember me: "Remember me for this, O my God" (v.14). The second "Remember me." Each reform is sealed with this prayer. Nehemiah builds his case before the only judge who matters.

Context

Without tithes, the Levites abandoned the temple and returned to their fields. Without Levites, worship collapsed. The causal chain: Tobiah in the storeroom → no space for tithes → no tithes → no Levites → no worship. One compromise cascades.

Cross-Reference

  • Neh 10:37–39"We will not neglect the house of our God" — the covenant promise already broken.
  • Mal 3:8–10"Will a mere mortal rob God? Yet you rob me — in tithes and offerings."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:10–11

Levites had "gone back to their own fields." Worship staff abandoned their posts because the community stopped supporting them. The covenant promise of 10:39 lasted less than a decade.

13:12–13

Nehemiah restores the system: tithes brought in, trustworthy administrators appointed. "They were considered trustworthy" — character as the criterion for stewardship.

13:14

"Remember me for this, O my God, and do not blot out what I have so faithfully done." Not self-righteousness but appeal to divine witness. Nehemiah wants his faithfulness noted.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase

Calvin: "He says ‘remember’ — not ‘reward.’ He wants God’s recognition, not compensation."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press

The NET notes "gone back to their fields" implies the Levites had no independent income. Without tithes, they had no choice but to farm.

Williamson Notes

williamson
13:13

Williamson notes the four trustees represent priest, Levite, scribe, and lay leader — a cross-section preventing any single group from controlling the funds.

Kidner Notes

Verses 15–22 — Sabbath Enforcement
1516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

שַׁבָּת šabbāt — Sabbath / rest: The Sabbath (šabbāt) is being violated: treading winepresses, loading donkeys, buying from Tyrian merchants. Nehemiah shuts the gates at sunset Friday and stations guards. He personally threatens the merchants: "If you do this again, I will arrest you."

Context

The covenant of 10:31 promised Sabbath observance. Like the tithes, it has collapsed. Nehemiah’s enforcement is physical: gates shut, guards posted, merchants confronted. He does not merely teach Sabbath theology — he enforces Sabbath practice.

Cross-Reference

  • Neh 10:31The covenant promise: "We will not buy from them on the Sabbath."
  • Isa 58:13–14"If you keep your feet from breaking the Sabbath" — the prophetic ideal.
  • Jer 17:19–27Jeremiah’s Sabbath gate warning — the prophetic precedent for Nehemiah’s action.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:15–16

Judahites treading winepresses on the Sabbath; Tyrian merchants selling fish and goods. The violation is both internal (Jewish workers) and external (foreign traders).

13:17–18

"What is this wicked thing you are doing?" Nehemiah confronts the nobles: "Didn’t your ancestors do this, and didn’t our God bring all this calamity on us?" History as argument.

13:19–21

Gates shut at sunset Friday. Merchants camp outside the walls once or twice, hoping for entry. Nehemiah warns them: "If you do this again, I will arrest you." They stop.

13:22

"Remember me for this also, O my God." Third "Remember me." The Sabbath, like the tithes, requires a prayer seal.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:18

Calvin: "He reminds them of the exile. The best cure for present sin is the memory of past judgment."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:16

The NET notes Tyrian merchants traded Mediterranean fish — a luxury item in landlocked Jerusalem. Economic temptation drove Sabbath violation.

Williamson Notes

williamson
13:16

Williamson notes the Tyrian fish merchants reflect the cosmopolitan economic pressure on post-exilic Jerusalem. The Sabbath prohibition requires resisting market forces — an economic sacrifice.

13:19

The gate-closing at "the beginning of the shadows before the Sabbath" confirms Jewish reckoning of the day from sunset — the earliest evidence of this practice in narrative.

Kidner Notes

kidner
13:17–18

Kidner: "He points to the exile and says: this is what Sabbath-breaking costs. History is his strongest argument. The community that forgets its past will repeat it."

13:21

Kidner on threatening the merchants: "Nehemiah is not subtle. His theology has fists. Sometimes the appropriate pastoral response is a threat."

Verses 23–31 — Intermarriage Confronted; "Remember Me"
232425262728293031

Hebrew Word Study

וָאֶרִיבָה wāʾērîḇâ — I contended / I rebuked: "I rebuked them and called curses down on them. I beat some of them and pulled out their hair" (v.25). Nehemiah’s response to intermarriage is more physical than Ezra’s. Ezra pulled his own hair (9:3); Nehemiah pulls theirs.

זָכְרֵנִי zāk̅rēnî — remember me: "Remember me with favour, O my God" (v.31). The fourth and final "Remember me." The book’s last word is not triumph but prayer. Nehemiah’s closing is not "I succeeded" but "God, remember."

Historical Context

Intermarriage has returned despite Ezra’s reform (Ezra 10). Half the children speak Ashdodite, not Hebrew. Language loss is identity loss. If the next generation cannot read Torah, the community dies from within.

Context

The book ends not with victory but with prayer. Four reforms, four collapses, four "Remember me" prayers. The open ending is the narrator’s final theological statement: human faithfulness is fragile; only God’s remembering endures. The book began with a prayer (1:5–11) and ends with one (13:31). Prayer brackets everything.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezra 9–10Ezra’s earlier reform on the same issue — now repeated.
  • 1 Kgs 11:1–8Solomon’s intermarriage — the case Nehemiah explicitly cites (v.26).
  • Mal 2:10–16Malachi addresses the same crisis from the prophetic angle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:23–24

"Half their children spoke the language of Ashdod." Linguistic assimilation is the canary in the mine. When children lose the language, they lose the texts, the liturgy, the identity.

13:25

"I rebuked them, cursed them, beat some, pulled out their hair." Nehemiah’s methods are not Ezra’s. Where Ezra wept and prayed, Nehemiah acts physically. Both approaches reflect genuine pastoral outrage; neither is gentle.

13:26

"Was it not because of marriages like these that Solomon king of Israel sinned?" Nehemiah uses Solomon as the paradigm. If the wisest king fell through intermarriage, no one is safe.

13:30–31

The book’s final two verses: "I purified the priests and Levites... and assigned duties... Remember me with favour, O my God." Purification, organisation, prayer. Then silence. No resolution. No triumph. Just "Remember me."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:26

Calvin: "He holds up Solomon as a mirror. If the wisest man fell, who can stand? The argument is irresistible: no one is above the danger of compromise."

13:31

Calvin on the ending: "He does not end with a victory speech but with a prayer. This is the mark of a true servant: the work is done, but the final word belongs to God, not to the worker."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:24

The NET discusses "Ashdodite" as an indicator of cultural assimilation. Speaking a foreign mother-tongue meant Jewish education at home had ceased.

13:31

The NET observes the book’s four "Remember me" prayers (5:19; 13:14, 22, 31) form a structural marker for each reform section. The prayer is a literary device as well as a genuine petition.

Williamson Notes

williamson
13:24

Williamson identifies "the language of Ashdod" as a Philistine dialect, not Aramaic. The children spoke the language of their non-Jewish mothers. Linguistic identity was the battleground.

13:31

Williamson reads the final "Remember me" as a deliberate literary closure: the book ends where it began, with prayer. Everything between the first prayer (1:5) and the last (13:31) is bracketed by dependence on God.

Kidner Notes

kidner
13:25

Kidner: "Ezra pulled his own hair; Nehemiah pulled theirs. The contrast tells you everything about the two men. Both were right to be outraged; they expressed it differently."

13:31

Kidner on the ending: "The book closes not with a fanfare but with a prayer. ‘Remember me.’ That is all any servant of God can finally say. The rest is in God’s hands."

Scholarly Debates

Nehemiah’s Methods
Justified by context
Kidner, MacArthur, most evangelical readings
The severity of the crisis demanded severe action. Hair-pulling and threats were within the cultural norms of ancient Near Eastern judicial authority. Nehemiah acted as governor with legitimate coercive power.
Troublingly violent
Some modern readings
Physical violence against community members raises ethical questions regardless of cultural context. Ezra’s approach (personal grief, prayer) may model a more sustainable reform pattern.
Both readings acknowledge the severity. The narrator presents Nehemiah’s actions without criticism, suggesting the original audience found them appropriate. The contrast with Ezra’s gentler approach shows the canon contains multiple models of reform leadership.
