Nehemiah 1

Nehemiah’s Prayer

Verses 1–3 — The Report: Jerusalem’s Walls Broken Down
123

Hebrew Word Study

חוֹמָה ḥōmâ — wall: The wall (ḥōmâ) of Jerusalem is "broken down" (məpōrāṣōt). A city without walls in the ancient world was not merely unprotected — it was not a city. It had no legal status, no defensibility, no dignity. The broken wall is a theological symbol: the covenant community has no visible form.

שַׂעַר šaʿar — gate: The gates (šəʿārîm) are "burned with fire." Gates were both defensive and judicial — elders judged at the gate (Ruth 4:1; Prov 31:23). Burned gates mean destroyed justice infrastructure.

Historical Context

The date is Kislev (November–December) of Artaxerxes’ twentieth year (445 BC). Nehemiah is in Susa, the Persian winter capital. The report comes from Hanani, Nehemiah’s brother, who has visited Jerusalem. The walls had been partially rebuilt but recently destroyed again (Ezra 4:23 records an order to halt construction).

Context

Nehemiah hears the news and immediately weeps, mourns, fasts, and prays "for some days." His first response is not a plan but grief, and his grief leads to prayer, not action. The entire book’s architecture flows from this sequence: news → grief → prayer → action. Nehemiah never acts without praying first.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezra 4:23The forced halt to wall-building that left Jerusalem exposed.
  • Ps 137:1–6"If I forget you, Jerusalem" — the exile’s longing that Nehemiah embodies.
  • Dan 9:1–3Daniel’s response to Jerusalem’s state: also mourning, fasting, and prayer.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

Nehemiah son of Hakaliah — not a priest, not a prophet, but a layman. A cupbearer. God’s work is not limited to religious professionals.

1:2–3

Nehemiah asks specifically about "the Jewish remnant that had survived the exile, and also about Jerusalem." He asks about people first, city second. His priorities are correct.

1:3

"Great trouble and disgrace" (raʿâ gəḏōlâ ûḥerpâ) — the disgrace is not merely physical but covenantal. A ruined Jerusalem shames the name of the God who chose it.

1:4

Nehemiah’s four responses: sat down, wept, mourned, fasted, prayed. The sequence is deliberate: emotional response precedes spiritual discipline, which precedes petition.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:3–4

Calvin: "Nehemiah’s grief was not for his own comfort but for God’s glory. The disgrace of Jerusalem was the disgrace of its God. True zeal begins with grief over what dishonours God."

1:4

Calvin on fasting before praying: "Fasting is the handmaid of prayer. It empties the body that the soul might be full. Nehemiah disciplined his flesh to sharpen his spirit."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:1

The NET notes "Kislev" corresponds to November–December in the modern calendar. The month name is Babylonian, adopted during the exile — a linguistic trace of captivity.

1:3

The NET observes "broken down" (məpōrāṣōt) uses the pual participle, indicating a state resulting from past destructive action — the walls remain in ruins.

Williamson Notes

williamson
1:1

Williamson dates the report to November–December 445 BC. The "twentieth year" is Artaxerxes I’s regnal year. Susa (modern Shush, Iran) was the winter capital; the court moved to Ecbatana in summer.

1:3

Williamson argues the destruction described is recent, not the original Babylonian destruction of 586 BC. Ezra 4:23 records a forced halt; the walls may have been partially rebuilt and then torn down again under Artaxerxes’ earlier order.

Kidner Notes

kidner
1:2–3

Kidner: "The news was not new — the walls had been down for over a century. But news can break a heart at any time. Nehemiah heard old facts with fresh grief."

1:4

Kidner on the mourning "for some days": "Not a flash of emotion but a sustained engagement with reality. The greatest actions are preceded by the deepest griefs."

Verses 4–11 — Nehemiah’s Prayer of Confession and Appeal
4567891011

Hebrew Word Study

אָנָּא ānnāʾ — please / I pray: The prayer opens with ānnāʾ YHWH — an intensely personal, pleading address. The same particle Moses uses (Exod 32:31). It expresses not formality but urgency — the desperate petition of someone who knows he has no leverage.

מַשְׁקֶה mašqê — cupbearer: Nehemiah identifies himself as "cupbearer to the king" (mašqê lammelek). The cupbearer was a position of immense trust — he tasted the king’s wine for poison. Nehemiah has direct, daily access to the most powerful man on earth. His prayer asks God to use this access.

Historical Context

The cupbearer (mašqê) in Persian courts was a high-ranking official with direct access to the king. Herodotus and Xenophon both describe the position as one of extraordinary trust and influence. Nehemiah’s request to leave this position for a ruined city is an enormous personal sacrifice.

Context

Nehemiah’s prayer follows the pattern of the great post-exilic confessions (Ezra 9; Dan 9; Neh 9): invocation of God’s covenant character, confession of national sin, appeal to covenant promises, specific petition. Like Ezra, Nehemiah uses "we" throughout — identifying with the sin of his ancestors though he personally was not guilty. The prayer ends with the practical note: "I was cupbearer to the king" — theology meets strategy.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 30:1–5The covenant promise Nehemiah appeals to: if you return, God will gather you — even from the farthest horizon.
  • Dan 9:4–19Daniel’s prayer of confession — nearly identical structure and theology.
  • Ezra 9:5–15Ezra’s prayer — the immediate predecessor in the canon.
  • 1 Kgs 8:46–50Solomon’s prayer anticipated this exact situation: praying toward Jerusalem from exile.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:5

"LORD, the God of heaven, the great and awesome God, who keeps his covenant of love" — Nehemiah begins with God’s character, not his own need. The order is crucial: who God is determines what God will do.

1:6–7

"I confess the sins we Israelites, including myself and my father’s family, have committed." Nehemiah includes himself in the guilt. Like Ezra and Daniel, he does not stand above the people’s sin but within it.

1:8–9

He quotes Deuteronomy 30:1–5 back to God: "Remember the instruction you gave your servant Moses." This is covenant logic: God made promises; Nehemiah holds him to them. Not presumption but faith.

1:11

"Give your servant success today by granting him favour in the presence of this man" — "this man" is the king of Persia. The world’s most powerful human is demoted to "this man" in prayer. Perspective.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:5

Calvin: "He begins with God’s greatness and covenant faithfulness. This is the foundation of all prayer: not our worthiness but God’s character."

1:8–9

Calvin on quoting Scripture in prayer: "Nehemiah reminds God of his own words. This is not impertinence but faith. God delights when his children hold him to his promises."

1:11

Calvin: "He calls the king ‘this man’ — not from contempt but from proportion. In the light of God’s sovereignty, every human authority is subordinate."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:5

The NET notes the combination "great and awesome God" (ēl haggāḏōl wəhannrāʾ) echoes Deut 7:21 and Dan 9:4. It is a confessional formula, not casual speech.

1:11

The NET observes the closing "I was cupbearer to the king" shifts abruptly from prayer to memoir. The narrative seam shows where the prayer source ends and Nehemiah’s personal account resumes.

Williamson Notes

williamson
1:5–11

Williamson analyses the prayer as a classic "penitential prayer" genre: invocation (v.5), confession (vv.6–7), appeal to Torah (vv.8–9), petition (vv.10–11). Every element draws from Deuteronomy. The prayer is theologically derivative but personally authentic.

1:11

The self-identification as "cupbearer" is unusual in a prayer. Williamson suggests Nehemiah is reminding himself (and God) of the strategic position he occupies. His access to the king is not coincidence but providence.

Kidner Notes

kidner
1:5–11

Kidner on the prayer’s structure: "Adoration, confession, appeal to Scripture, petition — in that order. Nehemiah prayed by the book, and his book was Deuteronomy."

1:11

Kidner: "‘This man’ — two words that put the Persian empire in its place. In the presence of God, Artaxerxes is merely ‘this man.’ Prayer is the great leveller."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Nehemiah
Cupbearer to Artaxerxes; future governor of Judah
A layman in a position of immense trust. His response to bad news — grief, fasting, prayer, then action — defines the book’s pattern. He is a man of prayer and a man of action, and the prayer always comes first.
Hanani
Nehemiah’s brother; brings the report
Arrives from Judah with the devastating news. He reappears in 7:2 as one of the gatekeepers Nehemiah appoints. The messenger becomes a guardian.

Scholarly Debates

What Destruction Does 1:3 Describe?
Original Babylonian destruction (586 BC)
Older view
The walls destroyed by Nebuchadnezzar had never been rebuilt. Nehemiah’s grief is at the ongoing state of affairs.
Recent destruction under Artaxerxes (c.448 BC)
Williamson, Kidner, most moderns
Ezra 4:23 records a forced halt. A partial rebuilding was torn down again. Nehemiah’s grief is at a fresh setback, not century-old ruins.
The recent-destruction view better explains Nehemiah’s surprise and urgency. If the walls had been down for 140 years, his shock would be less comprehensible.

Theological Themes Tracker

Covenant dominates: Nehemiah’s prayer appeals entirely to covenant promises. Faith is high — he resolves to act before knowing whether the king will agree.
