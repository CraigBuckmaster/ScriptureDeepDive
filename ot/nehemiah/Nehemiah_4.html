Nehemiah 4

Opposition: From Ridicule to Armed Threat

Verses 1–6 — Sanballat and Tobiah Mock
123456

Hebrew Word Study

לָעַג lāʿag — to mock / ridicule / scorn: Sanballat "ridiculed" (lāʿag) the Jews. Mockery is the first weapon. It costs nothing and demoralises without risk. Tobiah adds: "Even a fox would break down their wall." The insult targets the quality of the work — and by extension, the God behind it.

Historical Context

Sanballat mocks "in the presence of his associates and the army of Samaria" (v.2). This is a public political performance: the ridicule is designed to maintain his coalition’s confidence that the Jerusalem project will fail. The mockery is strategic, not spontaneous.

Context

Nehemiah’s response to ridicule is prayer, not retaliation: "Hear us, our God, for we are despised" (v.4). Then: "So we rebuilt the wall till all of it reached half its height, for the people worked with all their heart" (v.6). The best answer to mockery is progress.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 123:3–4"We have endured no end of contempt and ridicule" — the psalm Nehemiah’s prayer echoes.
  • 2 Kgs 18:19–35Rabshakeh’s mockery of Jerusalem — the Assyrian precedent for psychological warfare against the city.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1–2

Sanballat’s five mocking questions: Can they rebuild? Will they sacrifice? Will they finish? Can they revive burned stones? What are they doing? Each question targets a different anxiety.

4:3

Tobiah: "Even a fox climbing on their wall would break it down." The insult is memorable because it’s clever. Tobiah has a gift for the cutting phrase.

4:4–5

Nehemiah’s prayer is imprecatory: "Turn their insults back on their own heads." He does not pray for their conversion but for their defeat. Imprecatory prayer expresses righteous anger before God rather than through retaliation.

4:6

The wall reaches half height "for the people worked with all their heart." Morale is the decisive factor. Mockery failed because the hearts were willing.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase

4:4–5

Calvin on imprecatory prayer: "Nehemiah did not take revenge but committed his cause to God. This is the only lawful response to persecution: prayer, not retaliation."

4:4–5

The imprecatory prayer is standard biblical lament form (cf. Ps 79:12; 109:6–15). Williamson notes Nehemiah channels anger through prayer rather than military action.

Kidner Notes

Verses 7—14 — Conspiracy to Attack; Nehemiah Arms the Builders
7891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

לָחַם lāḥam — to fight / to do battle: The opposition escalates from mockery to conspiracy to "fight against Jerusalem" (lāḥam). The shift from words to weapons marks a new phase. Nehemiah’s response also escalates: from prayer alone (v.4) to prayer plus military preparation (v.9).

זָכַר zāk̅ar — to remember: "Remember the Lord, who is great and awesome, and fight" (v.14). The verb zāk̅ar (remember) is Nehemiah’s characteristic word — it appears throughout the book. Memory is the foundation of courage.

Historical Context

The coalition expands: Sanballat (north), Tobiah and Ammonites (east), Arabs (south), Ashdodites (west). Jerusalem is surrounded on all four sides. The conspiracy is coordinated — a multi-front military threat designed to make defence impossible.

Context

"We prayed to our God and posted a guard" (v.9). The sentence contains Nehemiah’s entire theology of action: pray AND act. Not prayer instead of guards. Not guards instead of prayer. Both. This is not a lack of faith but an expression of it — faith engages the means God provides.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Chr 20:12Jehoshaphat: "We do not know what to do, but our eyes are on you" — the prayer-then-fight pattern.
  • Eph 6:10–18The armour of God — the NT theological framework for Nehemiah’s practical principle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:7–8

Four-directional coalition: north, east, south, west. The geography is total encirclement. Nehemiah is surrounded but not contained.

4:9

"We prayed to our God and posted a guard." Eleven words that contain an entire theology of practical faith. Prayer without action is presumption; action without prayer is pride.

4:13

Nehemiah stations armed families "by clans, with their swords, spears and bows." The unit is the family. They fight for what they love — their homes, their children, their section of wall.

4:14

"Remember the Lord, who is great and awesome, and fight for your families." Theology and tactics in one sentence. Remember God; fight for your people. Both commands are necessary.

Verses 15–23 — Half Build, Half Guard
151617181920212223

Hebrew Word Study

חֲנִית ḥănît — spear / lance: "Half did the work while the other half held spears, shields, bows and armour" (v.16). The image of armed builders is Nehemiah’s signature: construction as combat, building as battle. The ḥănît (spear) in one hand and the trowel in the other.

Context

The armed builder is the chapter’s defining image and one of the most iconic in the OT. "Those who carried materials did their work with one hand and held a weapon in the other, and each of the builders wore his sword at his side" (vv.17–18). The wall is built under threat. This is what faith looks like when the environment is hostile: you don’t stop building, but you don’t pretend there’s no danger.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Cor 16:13"Be on your guard; stand firm in the faith; be courageous; be strong" — the NT echo of Nehemiah’s armed builders.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:15

"When our enemies heard that we were aware of their plot, God had frustrated it." The conspiracy fails not through battle but through disclosure. Sometimes defence is simply alertness.

4:16–18

The division of labour: half build, half guard. Those who carry loads do so one-handed, weapon in the other. The builders wear swords at their sides. Every worker is also a soldier.

4:19–20

The trumpet system: because the builders are spread along a long wall, they need communication. Nehemiah establishes a rally point: wherever the trumpet sounds, gather there. Military communication protocol for a construction project.

4:21–23

"We worked from dawn till the stars came out." Extended hours under threat. "Neither I nor my brothers nor my men nor the guards with me took off our clothes." They slept in their work clothes, ready for action at any moment.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:17–18

Calvin: "To build with one hand and hold a weapon with the other is the calling of every believer in this world. We are always building and always defending. Peace and war are simultaneous."

4:23

Calvin: "They did not undress. This total dedication teaches us that the work of God demands not convenience but commitment."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:17

The NET notes "with one hand" is literally "one of his hands was doing the work while the other held a weapon." The image is literary — some tasks require both hands — but the principle is clear.

4:23

The NET observes "each had his weapon, even when he went for water" suggests the threat was continuous, not intermittent. No moment was safe.

Williamson Notes

williamson
4:16–18

Williamson analyses the military arrangements as a combination of reserve force (half guarding) and individual readiness (each builder armed). The dual system is professional, not improvised.

4:20

The trumpet (shōpār) as rally signal follows the military pattern from Numbers 10:9. Nehemiah applies Torah’s warfare protocols to his construction project.

Kidner on sleeping in clothes: "They lived in a state of permanent readiness. Comfort was sacrificed for vigilance. This is what serious work looks like."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Sanballat
Governor of Samaria; leads the mockery and conspiracy
His tactics escalate across the chapter: ridicule (vv.1–3), conspiracy (vv.7–8), then... the threat collapses (v.15). Each escalation is met by a combination of prayer and practical action.
Tobiah
Ammonite official; master of the cutting remark
"Even a fox would break down their wall" — the most quoted insult in the book. His wit makes him the more memorable opponent, though Sanballat holds the military power.
