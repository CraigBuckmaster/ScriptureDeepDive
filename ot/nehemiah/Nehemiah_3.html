Nehemiah 3

The Builders List

Verses 1–14 — Northern and Western Sections: Sheep Gate to Valley Gate
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

בָנָה bānâ — to build: The verb bānâ (to build) appears over twenty times in this chapter. Each assignment is "N built the section next to them." The repetition creates a rhythm: build, build, build. The wall rises one section at a time, one family at a time.

קִדְּשׁוּהוּ qiddəšûhû — they consecrated it: Eliashib the high priest and the priests "consecrated" (qiddəšû) the Sheep Gate after building it. They did not merely construct — they dedicated. The wall is not just a defensive structure but a sacred boundary.

Historical Context

The list assigns 42 construction sections proceeding counterclockwise from the Sheep Gate (northeast corner). Builders include the high priest, district rulers, perfume-makers, goldsmiths, merchants, daughters (3:12), and temple servants. The cross-section of society is remarkable: no class is exempt from physical labour.

Context

The chapter reads like a roster but functions as theology: the wall is built by the entire community, each family responsible for a section. There is no professional construction crew. The wall belongs to everyone because everyone builds it. This is covenant participation made physical.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Cor 12:12–27The body metaphor: each part contributes. Nehemiah 3 is the OT architectural equivalent.
  • Exod 35–36The tabernacle built by skilled volunteers from every family — the same pattern.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1

Eliashib the high priest builds the Sheep Gate with the other priests. The religious leadership builds first, setting the example. The Sheep Gate is near the temple — appropriate for priests.

3:5

"The nobles of Tekoa would not put their shoulders to the work." The only negative note in the chapter. Their refusal stands out precisely because everyone else participates. Class privilege resists communal labour.

3:8

"Goldsmiths and perfume-makers" repair sections. Artisans with no construction experience do construction. The wall requires not specialists but willingness.

3:12

"Shallum and his daughters" repair a section. Women participate in the construction — noted without comment, as though it were unremarkable.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1

Calvin: "The high priest laid the first stone. When leaders work, the people follow. When leaders exempt themselves (v.5), the work is weakened."

3:5

Calvin on the Tekoite nobles: "They refused the yoke of common labour. But God records their shame alongside the honour of those who served. The page of Scripture is their only monument."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:1

The NET locates the Sheep Gate at the northeastern corner of the Old City, near the later Pool of Bethesda (John 5:2). The gate served the temple sacrificial system — sheep entered here.

3:8

The NET notes "goldsmiths" (ṣōrəpî) and "perfume-makers" (raqqāḥîm) are trade guild names, not individual designations.

Williamson Notes

williamson
3:1

Williamson traces the route counterclockwise from the Sheep Gate: the first section covers the northeastern corner, the most vulnerable to attack. The high priest builds the most exposed section.

3:5

The Tekoite nobles’ refusal uses the phrase "would not put their necks" (lōʾ hēḇîʿû ṣawwārām) — literally, they would not bend their necks. Pride, not inability, prevented their service.

Kidner Notes

kidner
3:1–14

Kidner: "The list is a roll of honour. It names those who built and shames those who refused. The Tekoite nobles (v.5) are remembered for exactly one thing: they wouldn’t work."

Kidner on Shallum’s daughters: "No comment, no surprise. The narrator records their work alongside priests and rulers as though it were perfectly natural. It was."

Verses 15–32 — Southern and Eastern Sections: Fountain Gate to Sheep Gate

Hebrew Word Study

חֲזִיק ḥăzîq — to repair / strengthen: Several sections use ḥēzîq ("repaired/strengthened") rather than bānâ ("built"), suggesting these sections needed reinforcement rather than complete reconstruction. The distinction implies careful assessment — some sections were salvageable.

Context

The list completes the circuit: Fountain Gate → Dung Gate → Water Gate → Horse Gate → back to the Sheep Gate (v.32). The wall is a circle, and the chapter traces the full perimeter. The literary structure mirrors the physical structure: a complete circuit of names completing a complete circuit of stone.

Cross-Reference

  • Neh 12:31–40The dedication procession later follows the same route in reverse — walking on what they built.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:16

Nehemiah son of Azbuk repairs "as far as the tombs of David." The mention of David’s tombs anchors the construction in Israel’s royal heritage. They are rebuilding the city of David.

3:20

"Baruch son of Zabbai zealously repaired another section." The word "zealously" (heḥĕrâ) appears only here. One builder’s extraordinary effort is individually noted.

3:28

Priests repair "each in front of his own house." Some builders work on the section nearest their home — personal stake in communal defence. What you build protects what you love.

3:32

The list returns to the Sheep Gate: a complete circle. Every gap is assigned, every section has a name.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:28

Calvin: "Each man built in front of his own house. This principle is the foundation of all community: take care of what is nearest, and the whole is secured."

3:32

Calvin on the completed circuit: "From Sheep Gate to Sheep Gate — the wall is one. So the church: diverse members, one body, one purpose."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:16

The NET locates the "tombs of David" on the southeastern ridge, consistent with archaeological evidence of royal burial chambers in the City of David.

3:26

The NET identifies "Ophel" as the narrow ridge between the City of David and the Temple Mount — the administrative quarter where temple servants resided.

Williamson Notes

williamson
3:15–32

Williamson maps the eastern wall sections along the steep Kidron Valley slope. This section required the most engineering skill due to the terrain. Several builders are district rulers (sar peleḵ), indicating administrative districts contributed as units.

3:26–27

The temple servants (nətînîm) living on Ophel build their section. Williamson notes their location near the Water Gate put them closest to the water supply — strategic placement.

Kidner Notes

kidner
3:20

Kidner on Baruch’s zeal: "Among all the builders, one man’s passion is singled out. The narrator honours not just participation but enthusiasm."

3:28–32

Kidner: "The list ends where it began — at the Sheep Gate. The circle is closed. In the text as on the ground, the wall is complete."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Eliashib
High priest; builds the Sheep Gate
Sets the example by building first. However, he later compromises by giving Tobiah a room in the temple precincts (13:4–7), showing that initial zeal does not guarantee sustained faithfulness.
