Ecclesiastes 1

Everything Is Meaningless

Verses 1–11 — “What Does Anyone Gain?” — The Cycles of Nature
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

הֲבֵל hebel — breath / vapour / meaningless / absurd: "Meaningless! Meaningless!" (hebel habalîm). The book’s signature word, occurring 38 times. Literally "breath" or "vapour" — something real but insubstantial, present but passing. Fox translates it "absurd": not that life is empty but that it defies rational expectation.

יִתְרוֹן yitrōn — profit / gain / advantage: "What does anyone gain (yitrōn) from all their labours?" (v.3). A commercial term: what is the net profit? Qoheleth applies business accounting to existence itself. The answer he keeps finding: zero.

Historical Context

The "Teacher" (Qoheleth) is introduced as "son of David, king in Jerusalem" (v.1) — traditionally identified with Solomon, though most scholars see this as a literary persona. The royal fiction gives Qoheleth the resources to test every human pursuit.

Context

The poem of natural cycles (vv.4–11) establishes the framework: sun rises and sets, wind blows in circuits, rivers flow to the sea but the sea is never full, and no one remembers what came before. Nature’s endless repetition is the metaphor for human futility. Nothing is new under the sun.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 8:20"The creation was subjected to frustration" — Paul’s theology of a groaning creation echoes Qoheleth.
  • Ps 39:5–6"Each person’s life is but a breath" — the same hebel theology.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1–2

"Meaningless! Meaningless! Utterly meaningless! Everything is meaningless." The thesis statement. Qoheleth will spend 12 chapters demonstrating this before the epilogue reframes it.

1:3

"What does anyone gain from all their labours at which they toil under the sun?" The key question. "Under the sun" is Qoheleth’s frame: life viewed from a purely earthly, horizontal perspective.

1:4–7

"Generations come and go, but the earth remains forever. The sun rises and the sun sets... the wind blows to the south and turns to the north... all streams flow into the sea, yet the sea is never full." Nature’s cycles: repetitive, endless, never arriving.

1:9–11

"There is nothing new under the sun." The denial of progress. History repeats; memory fades; what seems new is merely forgotten.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:2

Alter: the superlative "hebel of hebels" mirrors "song of songs" and "king of kings." This is the ultimate hebel — the breath of breaths.

1:4–7

Alter: the poem’s power is in its rhythm: each element (sun, wind, streams) moves endlessly without arriving. The syntax mimics the cycles it describes.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:2–3

Calvin: Qoheleth does not deny that God gives good things. He denies that earthly goods can satisfy the soul. The "gain" question has no positive answer because the soul was made for eternity, not for temporary profit.

1:9

Calvin: "nothing new under the sun" applies to the human condition, not to God’s providence. God does new things (Isa 43:19); humans repeat themselves.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:2

The NET discusses the translation options for hebel: "meaningless," "futile," "ephemeral," "enigmatic," "absurd." No single English word captures the full range.

1:3

The NET: yitrōn (profit/gain) is a commercial term found only in Ecclesiastes. Qoheleth treats life as a balance sheet and finds the bottom line is zero.

Longman Notes

longman
1:2

Longman: hebel is the book’s thematic keyword. He translates it "meaningless" but notes the range: temporary, ephemeral, incomprehensible, absurd. Qoheleth uses it to describe everything "under the sun."

1:3

Longman: "under the sun" (taḥat hashemesh) occurs 29 times and defines Qoheleth’s perspective: life without reference to God’s eternal purposes. The epilogue will supply what Qoheleth lacks.

Fox Notes

fox
1:2

Fox: hebel means "absurd" — not that life is empty but that it is irrational. The expected correlation between effort and result does not hold. Life mocks human planning.

1:4–7

Fox: the nature poem is not pessimistic but observational. Qoheleth sees the same patterns repeating and asks: if nature goes nowhere, does human life?

Verses 12–18 — “I Applied My Mind” — Wisdom Is Chasing After Wind
12131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

רְעוּת רוּחַ rəʿūṭ rûaḥ — chasing after wind / shepherding wind: "Chasing after wind" (rəʿūṭ rûaḥ). The image recurs throughout the book: every human pursuit is like trying to catch the wind. You can feel it; you cannot hold it. The phrase is Qoheleth’s verdict on wisdom, pleasure, toil, and wealth.

Context

Qoheleth establishes his credentials: "I, the Teacher, was king over Israel in Jerusalem" (v.12). He devoted himself to study and exploration — and concluded that the pursuit of wisdom, while better than folly, cannot resolve life’s fundamental absurdity. "With much wisdom comes much sorrow; the more knowledge, the more grief" (v.18). Wisdom sees more clearly, and what it sees is painful.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 4:29–34Solomon’s wisdom surpassed all others — the persona Qoheleth claims.
  • 1 Cor 1:19–20"Where is the wise person?" — Paul’s critique of human wisdom echoes Qoheleth.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:12–14

"I applied my mind to study and to explore by wisdom all that is done under the heavens. What a heavy burden God has laid on mankind!" The pursuit of understanding is divinely imposed but humanly frustrating.

1:15

"What is crooked cannot be straightened; what is lacking cannot be counted." The world is broken, and wisdom cannot fix it. This is realism, not despair.

1:17–18

"I applied myself to the understanding of wisdom, and also of madness and folly. For with much wisdom comes much sorrow." Knowledge increases pain because it reveals how much is wrong.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:15

Alter: "the crooked cannot be straightened" is a proverb Qoheleth adopts to express permanent limitation. Some things cannot be fixed, no matter how wise you are.

1:18

Alter: the parallelism (wisdom/sorrow, knowledge/grief) is tight and bitter. Each gain in understanding is matched by a gain in pain.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:13

Calvin: the "heavy burden" is the restless human mind. God made us to seek; the fall ensures we cannot fully find. The frustration is theological, not merely psychological.

1:18

Calvin: wisdom’s grief is the wise person’s awareness of sin, death, and injustice that the fool ignores. Better to grieve with clear eyes than to be happy in delusion.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:14

The NET: rəʿūṭ rûaḥ is debated: "chasing after wind" (pursuit) or "shepherding wind" (futile attempt to control). Both images convey impossibility.

1:15

The NET: "what is crooked" may refer to the moral disorder of the world, not just intellectual puzzles. Some wrongs cannot be righted by human effort.

Longman Notes

longman
1:13

Longman: "a heavy burden God has laid" — Qoheleth recognises the drive to understand as God-given but unsatisfying. Humanity was made to seek meaning but cannot find ultimate meaning "under the sun."

1:18

Longman: "the more knowledge, the more grief" — this is Qoheleth’s version of the tree of knowledge. Awareness increases suffering. Ignorance was a kind of bliss.

Fox Notes

fox
1:13–14

Fox: Qoheleth’s method is empirical. He does not reason from premises but from observation. He studied, explored, and concluded: the data does not support the claim that wisdom produces happiness.

1:18

Fox: grief through knowledge is not anti-intellectual. Qoheleth values wisdom (7:12). His point: wisdom’s reward includes seeing truths that hurt.
