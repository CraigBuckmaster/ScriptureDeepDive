Ecclesiastes 5

Stand in Awe of God

Verses 1–7 — “Let Your Words Be Few”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

יְרָא yərāʾ — to fear / to revere: "Stand in awe (yərāʾ) of God" (v.7). The same word as "fear of the LORD" throughout the OT. Qoheleth’s theology of worship: approach carefully, speak sparingly, fulfil what you vow. God is in heaven; you are on earth. The distance demands reverence.

Context

The first passage in Ecclesiastes about worship. Guard your steps (v.1), do not be hasty with your mouth (v.2), fulfil your vows (vv.4–6). The theology is simple: God is transcendent, humans are small, and careless words before God are dangerous. This connects to Job 28:28 ("the fear of the LORD, that is wisdom") and to Proverbs 1:7.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 10:19"When words are many, sin is not absent" — the same caution about speech.
  • Matt 6:7"Do not keep on babbling" — Jesus’ teaching on prayer echoes Qoheleth’s restraint.
  • Job 28:28"The fear of the Lord, that is wisdom" — the wisdom tradition’s shared conclusion.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1–2

"Guard your steps when you go to the house of God... do not be quick with your mouth, do not be hasty in your heart to utter anything before God." Approach God with caution, not casualness. God is in heaven; you are on earth.

5:4–6

"When you make a vow to God, do not delay to fulfil it... it is better not to make a vow than to make one and not fulfil it." Vows are serious. Better silence than broken promises.

5:7

"Much dreaming and many words are meaningless. Therefore fear God." The conclusion: less talk, more awe.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:2

Alter: the heaven/earth contrast is spatial theology: the vertical distance between God and humanity mirrors the epistemic distance between divine wisdom and human understanding.

5:7

Alter: "fear God" closes the worship section as it will close the entire book (12:13). The refrain bookends the central teaching.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1–2

Calvin: the temple instruction is not anti-worship but anti-presumption. Come ready to listen before you speak. The posture of worship is receptivity, not performance.

5:7

Calvin: "fear God" is Qoheleth’s bridge to the epilogue’s conclusion. Within all the hebel, this remains: God is real, God is holy, and the appropriate response is awe.

Longman Notes

longman
5:1–7

Longman: this is Qoheleth’s most explicitly theological passage so far. The God who hid his purposes (3:11) now demands reverence. The inscrutability that frustrates Qoheleth also demands humility before it.

5:2

Longman: "God is in heaven and you are on earth" is the theological distance that governs all of Qoheleth’s epistemology. We cannot see the whole because we are not in heaven.

Fox Notes

fox
5:1–7

Fox: Qoheleth’s worship theology is minimalist: go, listen, don’t talk too much, keep your promises. No mention of sacrifice, liturgy, or praise. Fox: Qoheleth strips worship to its core: reverent attentiveness.

5:2

Fox: "a dream comes with many cares" — the connection between anxiety and verbosity. Worried people talk too much, including in prayer.

Verses 8–20 — “Whoever Loves Money Never Has Enough”
891011121314151617181920

Hebrew Word Study

כֶּסֶף kesef — silver / money: "Whoever loves money (kesef) never has enough; whoever loves wealth is never satisfied with their income" (v.10). The insatiability of greed. Wealth grows, so do the mouths to feed (v.11). The rich person’s sleep is troubled while the labourer sleeps soundly (v.12). And the kicker: you arrived naked and you leave naked (v.15). You cannot take it with you.

חֵלֶק ḥēleq — portion: "To accept their lot (ḥēleq) and be happy in their toil — this is a gift of God" (v.19). The third "enjoy" passage. Contentment is not earned but received. The ability to enjoy what you have is itself God’s gift.

Context

Qoheleth turns to wealth’s futility. Money breeds desire for more money (v.10). As goods increase, so do those who consume them (v.11). A "grievous evil": wealth hoarded, then lost, leaving the owner with nothing to pass on (vv.13–17). The response: enjoy what God gives. Eat, drink, find satisfaction in your portion. Happiness is a gift, not an achievement (vv.18–20).

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Tim 6:6–10"The love of money is a root of all kinds of evil" — Paul’s direct echo of Qoheleth.
  • Luke 12:15"Life does not consist in an abundance of possessions" — Jesus’ teaching parallels Qoheleth’s observations.
  • Job 1:21"Naked I came from my mother’s womb, and naked I will depart" — the same theology.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:10

"Whoever loves money never has enough." The psychology of greed: acquisition increases appetite rather than satisfying it.

5:12

"The sleep of a labourer is sweet, whether they eat little or much, but as for the rich, their abundance permits them no sleep." Wealth destroys the simple pleasure of rest.

5:15

"Everyone comes naked from their mother’s womb, and as everyone comes, so they depart." The ultimate equaliser: you leave with exactly what you arrived with — nothing.

5:18–20

"This is what I have observed to be good: that it is appropriate to eat, to drink and to find satisfaction... this is a gift of God." The third "enjoy" passage. Contentment is grace, not achievement.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:15

Alter: the naked-arrival/naked-departure motif echoes Job 1:21 directly. Both wisdom books share the same theology of radical dispossession at death.

5:18

Alter: "it is appropriate" — Alter notes the Hebrew carries a sense of beauty or fitness. It is not merely permitted to enjoy; it is fitting.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:10

Calvin: the insatiability of greed proves that money is not the soul’s true good. Augustine: "Our hearts are restless until they rest in you." Qoheleth discovers the same truth from the opposite direction.

5:19–20

Calvin: "this is a gift of God" elevates daily contentment to the level of divine grace. The person who eats with gratitude receives more than the miser who hoards with anxiety.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:10

The NET: kesef (silver/money) is the general term for currency. The proverb applies to all forms of wealth, not just coinage.

5:15

The NET connects this to Job 1:21 and notes the universal tradition across cultures: you can’t take it with you.

Longman Notes

longman
5:10–12

Longman: the wealth section is not anti-wealth but anti-worship-of-wealth. The problem is not having money but loving it. The insatiability proves the idol is broken.

5:19–20

Longman: "God keeps them occupied with gladness of heart" — the occupied person does not dwell on life’s absurdity because God fills their days with joy. This is grace, not distraction.

Fox Notes

fox
5:10

Fox: the insatiability of desire is Qoheleth’s psychological insight. The problem is not the object (money) but the orientation (love). Loving any finite thing infinitely produces frustration.

5:18–20

Fox: "this is a gift of God" — even the capacity for enjoyment is not self-generated. Fox: Qoheleth’s God is a giver, but an inscrutable one. You cannot predict when or to whom the gift will come.
