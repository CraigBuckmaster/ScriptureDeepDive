Ecclesiastes 3

A Time for Everything

Verses 1–15 — “A Time to Be Born and a Time to Die”
123456789101112131415

Hebrew Word Study

עֵת ʿēṭ — time / appointed time / season: "There is a time (ʿēṭ) for everything, and a season for every activity under the heavens" (v.1). The 14 pairs (28 activities) in vv.2–8 encompass all of life: birth/death, planting/uprooting, killing/healing, tearing down/building, weeping/laughing, mourning/dancing. The poem says: every human experience has its appointed moment.

עוֹלָם ʿōlām — eternity / the distant past or future: "He has also set eternity (ʿōlām) in the human heart; yet no one can fathom what God has done from beginning to end" (v.11). The book’s most profound verse. Humans sense eternity but cannot comprehend it. The ache for the infinite is built into finite creatures. This is the theological root of all hebel: we were made for more than "under the sun" can deliver.

Context

The time-poem (vv.2–8) is perhaps the most famous passage in Ecclesiastes. But Qoheleth’s purpose is not comfort but provocation: if God has appointed every time, what profit does the worker gain? (v.9). God has made everything beautiful "in its time" (v.11) but hidden the big picture from human eyes. We sense eternity but cannot see it. The result: enjoy the good, know that God’s work endures, and stand in awe (vv.12–14).

Cross-Reference

  • Acts 1:7"It is not for you to know the times or dates the Father has set" — the same limited access to divine timing.
  • Rom 8:28"In all things God works for the good" — the NT confidence that Qoheleth reaches for but cannot quite grasp.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1–8

The 14 pairs encompass all human experience. The structure is deliberate: birth/death bookend the list. Between them, every activity has its appointed season.

3:10–11

"He has made everything beautiful in its time. He has also set eternity in the human heart." The tension: beauty exists, eternity is sensed, but comprehension is withheld. This is not cruelty but design.

3:14

"Everything God does will endure forever; nothing can be added to it and nothing taken from it. God does it so that people will fear him." Divine permanence vs human impermanence. The intended response: awe, not frustration.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:2–8

Alter: the pairs create a totality through opposites (merism). Birth-death, plant-uproot, kill-heal — everything between the extremes is included.

3:11

Alter: "beautiful in its time" is a concession: individual moments have beauty. But the whole pattern remains hidden. Beauty is local; mystery is global.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:11

Calvin: eternity in the heart is the imago Dei — the image of God that makes humans unique among creatures. We long for the eternal because we were made by the Eternal. The longing is evidence of our origin.

3:14

Calvin: "God does it so that people will fear him" — the inscrutability of God’s timing is pedagogical. It teaches dependence. If we could comprehend fully, we would not need faith.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:1

The NET: ʿēṭ means an appointed time, not just any moment. The poem asserts divine appointment, not random occurrence.

3:11

The NET discusses ʿōlām at length: it can mean "eternity," "long duration," or "the unknown future." The ambiguity itself mirrors the verse’s point: we sense something we cannot define.

Longman Notes

longman
3:1–8

Longman: the poem is not about human control of timing but divine sovereignty over it. Humans do not choose when to be born or when to die. God sets the times; we live within them.

3:11

Longman: ʿōlām (eternity) in the heart is what makes humans restless. Animals live in the moment; humans sense something beyond. This awareness is both gift and burden.

Fox Notes

fox
3:1–8

Fox: the time-poem is deterministic. Every event has its appointed slot. The implication: human agency is limited. You cannot make it harvest time by wanting it to be.

3:11

Fox: "eternity in the heart" means humans are haunted by awareness of the whole but denied sight of it. We know the puzzle exists; we cannot see the picture on the box.

Verses 16–22 — Humans and Animals Share the Same Fate
16171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

מִקְרֶה miqreh — fate / chance / what happens: "Humans have the same fate (miqreh) as the animals; the same fate awaits them both: as one dies, so dies the other" (v.19). Miqreh means what befalls, what happens. It is not destiny but outcome. Qoheleth observes: biologically, humans die like animals. Both return to dust. If there is a difference, it is not visible "under the sun."

Context

After the time-poem, Qoheleth turns to injustice (v.16: "in the place of judgment, wickedness was there") and mortality (vv.18–21). His provocative question: "Who knows if the human spirit rises upward and if the spirit of the animal goes down into the earth?" (v.21). Qoheleth does not deny the afterlife; he says the evidence is insufficient to prove it from observation alone. His conclusion: enjoy your work, because that is your portion (v.22).

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 3:19"Dust you are and to dust you will return" — the creation theology Qoheleth draws on.
  • 2 Cor 5:1–5"We have a building from God, an eternal house in heaven" — Paul’s answer to Qoheleth’s uncertainty.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:16

"In the place of judgment, wickedness was there." Qoheleth observes what Job observed: injustice exists where justice should be. Courts are corrupt.

3:18–21

"As for humans, God tests them so that they may see that they are like the animals." Humbling anthropology: physically, we die identically. "Who knows?" is not denial but honest uncertainty.

3:22

"So I saw that there is nothing better than that a person should enjoy their work, because that is their lot." The second "enjoy" passage. When you cannot answer ultimate questions, attend to the present good.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:19–21

Alter: the dust-to-dust echo of Genesis 3:19 is deliberate. Qoheleth locates human mortality within creation theology. The curse applies equally to all creatures.

3:22

Alter: "who can bring them to see what will happen after them?" — the future is closed. This is not fatalism but the boundary of human knowledge.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:18–21

Calvin: the comparison humbles human pride. In death, kings and cattle share the same biology. The soul’s immortality, which Calvin affirms, is known by revelation, not by Qoheleth’s empiricism.

3:22

Calvin: "enjoy your work" is not resignation but wisdom within limits. Do what is before you; trust God with what is beyond you.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:19

The NET: miqreh (fate/chance) does not imply randomness but shared outcome. The same event (death) befalls both.

3:21

The NET: "who knows?" is a rhetorical question expecting no confident answer. Qoheleth is epistemically humble, not doctrinally nihilistic.

Longman Notes

longman
3:19–21

Longman: Qoheleth’s comparison of human and animal death is deliberately shocking. He does not deny that humans have souls; he says the difference is invisible from an empirical standpoint. The afterlife requires revelation, not observation.

3:22

Longman: "that is their lot (ḥēleq)" — the portion theology again. You cannot earn eternity; you can receive today.

Fox Notes

fox
3:19–21

Fox: Qoheleth is not a materialist. He asks "who knows?" — a question, not a denial. His epistemology is limited to observation, and observation cannot verify the afterlife. The question is honest.

3:22

Fox: "nothing better than to enjoy" is Qoheleth’s ethical conclusion: when the ultimate is inaccessible, maximise the penultimate.
