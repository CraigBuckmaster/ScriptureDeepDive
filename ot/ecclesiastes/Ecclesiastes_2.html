Ecclesiastes 2

Pleasures, Projects, Wisdom — All Tested

Verses 1–16 — “I Denied Myself Nothing”
12345678910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

שִׂמְחָה simhāh — joy / gladness / pleasure: "Come now, I will test you with pleasure (simhāh)" (v.1). Qoheleth conducts a controlled experiment: if wisdom produces grief, perhaps pleasure produces satisfaction. He tests laughter, wine, building projects, gardens, slaves, silver, gold, entertainment. The result: hebel.

Context

This is the great experiment. Qoheleth has unlimited resources (the Solomonic persona) and tries everything: pleasure (vv.1–2), wine (v.3), building projects (vv.4–6), wealth and entertainment (vv.7–8). He denies himself nothing (v.10). His verdict: "when I surveyed all that my hands had done, everything was meaningless" (v.11). Then wisdom is tested against folly — wisdom is better (v.13), but both wise and fool share the same fate: death (v.14–16).

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 12:16–21The rich fool who built bigger barns — Jesus’ parable echoes Qoheleth’s experiment.
  • 1 Tim 6:7–9"We brought nothing into the world, and we can take nothing out" — Paul’s version of Qoheleth’s conclusion.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1–2

"Come now, I will test you with pleasure." Qoheleth is systematic: he tested wisdom (1:12–18), now pleasure. Neither satisfies.

2:4–8

"I undertook great projects: I built houses, planted vineyards, made gardens, acquired slaves, amassed silver and gold." The catalogue of achievement. Everything Solomon was famous for.

2:10–11

"I denied myself nothing... yet when I surveyed all, everything was meaningless." The verdict after the experiment: maximum resources, maximum effort, zero lasting satisfaction.

2:14–16

"The wise have eyes in their heads, while the fool walks in the darkness; but I came to realise that the same fate overtakes them both." Death equalises. Wisdom’s advantage is real but temporary.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:8

Alter: "the delights of the sons of men" — the Hebrew is obscure but likely refers to sexual pleasure. Qoheleth tested every category of human enjoyment.

2:11

Alter: "a chasing after wind" closes the experiment. The phrase is the stamp Qoheleth presses on every category: tested and found insufficient.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:10–11

Calvin: Qoheleth enjoyed his pleasures — "this was the reward for all my toil." But the reward is temporary. Calvin: earthly goods are gifts, not goals. Enjoyment is real but finite.

2:14–16

Calvin: death’s equalising power is the argument that drives Qoheleth to look beyond "under the sun." If death ends everything, wisdom is pointless. The epilogue will supply the missing dimension: eternity.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:3

The NET: "guiding myself with wisdom" — Qoheleth experiments with pleasure while maintaining rational control. This is not drunken abandon but a disciplined test.

2:16

The NET: "the wise person, like the fool, will not be long remembered" connects to 1:11 ("no one remembers"). Mortality erases both wisdom’s and folly’s legacy.

Longman Notes

longman
2:1–11

Longman: the pleasure experiment is not hedonism but empiricism. Qoheleth is a researcher with unlimited funding. His conclusion is not "pleasure is bad" but "pleasure doesn’t last."

2:14–16

Longman: death as the great equaliser is Qoheleth’s most persistent theme. It undermines every distinction wisdom can draw. The wise advantage evaporates at the grave.

Fox Notes

fox
2:10

Fox: "my heart took delight" — Qoheleth genuinely enjoyed the process. He is not a killjoy. His point is that enjoyment, while real, does not constitute a permanent gain.

2:15–16

Fox: "the wise person, like the fool, is not long remembered" — the absurdity is not that the fool dies but that the wise person dies identically. The system does not reward its best participants.

Verses 17–26 — “So I Hated Life”; God’s Gift of Enjoyment
17181920212223242526

Hebrew Word Study

חֵלֶק ḥēleq — portion / share / lot: "A person can do nothing better than to eat and drink and find satisfaction (ḥēleq) in their own toil" (v.24). ḥēleq is Qoheleth’s positive word: the portion God assigns. You cannot secure permanent gain (yitrōn), but you can receive a temporary portion (ḥēleq) of enjoyment. The gift is real; it is just not permanent.

Context

After the despair ("I hated life," v.17; "I hated all the things I had toiled for," v.18), Qoheleth pivots to the first of seven "enjoy life" passages (2:24; 3:12–13; 3:22; 5:18–19; 8:15; 9:7–10; 11:7–10). These are not contradictions but the positive underside of hebel: since you cannot secure permanent gain, receive the temporary gifts God gives. Eat, drink, find satisfaction in toil. This is from God’s hand.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Tim 4:4"Everything God created is good, and nothing is to be rejected if it is received with thanksgiving" — Paul’s affirmation of Qoheleth’s "from God’s hand."
  • Matt 6:25–34"Do not worry about tomorrow" — Jesus’ teaching parallels Qoheleth’s "enjoy today."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:17–19

"So I hated life... I hated all the things I had toiled for, because I must leave them to the one who comes after me." The existential crisis: everything you build will be managed by someone you can’t control.

2:24–26

"A person can do nothing better than to eat and drink and find satisfaction in their own toil. This too, I see, is from the hand of God." The first "enjoy" passage. Enjoyment is not earned but given. It is God’s gift, not humanity’s achievement.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:17

Alter: "I hated life" is Qoheleth at his most extreme. He will moderate this in later chapters, but the despair is real and unfeigned.

2:24

Alter: the "eat and drink" advice is not the counsel of despair but the wisdom of limitation. Accept what you can receive; stop reaching for what you cannot hold.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:24–26

Calvin: enjoyment "from the hand of God" means every good meal, every satisfying day, every moment of contentment is a divine gift. Calvin: ingratitude is worse than Qoheleth’s despair, because it refuses even the portion God gives.

2:17

Calvin: "I hated life" is honest, not blasphemous. The wisest person on earth surveyed everything and found it insufficient. The insufficiency drives the reader toward God, which is the book’s purpose.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:24

The NET: the Hebrew may read "there is nothing better for a person than to eat and drink" or "it is not good for a person that he should eat and drink." The textual variant is debated; most translations follow the positive reading.

2:26

The NET: ḥēleq (portion) contrasts with yitrōn (profit). You cannot earn profit from life, but you can receive a portion as gift.

Longman Notes

longman
2:24–26

Longman: the "enjoy life" passages are Qoheleth’s only positive counsel. They are not hedonism but surrender: stop trying to secure permanent gain and receive the daily portion God gives. This is the wisdom of open hands.

2:26

Longman: "to the person who pleases him, God gives wisdom, knowledge and happiness" — Qoheleth admits divine favouritism but calls even that hebel. The gift is real; the system is inscrutable.

Fox Notes

fox
2:24

Fox: "from the hand of God" is the theological key. Enjoyment is not self-generated but divinely given. The absurdity is that you cannot plan when or whether it will come. You can only receive it when it does.

2:26

Fox: "this too is hebel" applied even to God’s giving. The absurdity extends to divine distribution itself: why does God give to some and not others? Qoheleth has no answer.
