Ecclesiastes 6

Wealth Without Enjoyment

Verses 1–6 — “A Stillborn Child Is Better Off”
123456

Hebrew Word Study

נֵפֶל nēfel — stillborn / miscarriage: "A stillborn child (nēfel) is better off" (v.3). Qoheleth’s most extreme statement about wealth without enjoyment. A person with everything — wealth, honour, long life, many children — who cannot enjoy any of it is worse off than a child who was never born. The stillborn at least has rest.

Context

The chapter addresses a "grievous evil" (v.1): God gives someone wealth and honour but does not grant the ability to enjoy them. A stranger enjoys them instead (v.2). Even a hundred children and two thousand years of life are worthless without enjoyment (v.3–6). The implication: enjoyment is not automatic. It is a separate gift from wealth itself. You can have everything and still have nothing.

Cross-Reference

  • Job 3:11–16Job’s wish to have been stillborn — the same theology of unborn rest.
  • Luke 12:20–21"This very night your life will be demanded" — wealth without enjoyment in Jesus’ parable.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1–2

"God gives someone wealth, possessions and honour... but God does not grant the ability to enjoy them." The critical distinction: having and enjoying are separate gifts. God gives one without the other.

6:3–6

"A man may have a hundred children and live many years... but if he cannot enjoy his prosperity and does not receive proper burial, I say that a stillborn child is better off." The stillborn, who "comes without meaning and departs in darkness" (v.4), at least has rest (v.5).

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:3–6

Alter: the stillborn comparison recalls Job 3 where Job wished he had died at birth. Both wisdom books share the conviction that existence without quality is not a gift.

6:4

Alter: "comes in hebel and goes in darkness" — the stillborn’s life is the purest hebel: breath that never took shape.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:2

Calvin: God’s withholding of enjoyment is a mystery, not a cruelty. Calvin: sometimes the withholding drives the person to seek God rather than goods. The inability to enjoy earthly things can be the beginning of heavenly desire.

6:3–6

Calvin: the stillborn comparison is extreme but makes the point: bare existence is not blessing. Life requires both provision and the capacity to receive it gratefully.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:2

The NET: the inability to enjoy is described as God’s decision, not the person’s failure. This raises the theodicy question within wisdom literature.

6:3

The NET: "a hundred children" is hyperbolic, emphasising that quantity of life (years, offspring) does not compensate for quality (enjoyment, burial).

Longman Notes

longman
6:1–2

Longman: the inability to enjoy is God’s withholding. Qoheleth attributes it to divine decision, not personal failure. This is troubling theology: God gives gifts and then prevents their enjoyment.

6:3–6

Longman: the stillborn comparison is deliberately shocking. Qoheleth pushes the logic: if wealth without enjoyment is meaningless, then existence without enjoyment is worse than non-existence.

Fox Notes

fox
6:2

Fox: "God does not grant the ability to enjoy" — the absurdity peaks here. The same God who gives wealth withholds enjoyment. Fox: this is not theology of punishment but of inscrutable distribution. There is no pattern.

6:6

Fox: "Do not all go to the same place?" Death is the equaliser again. The wealthy non-enjoyer and the stillborn end in the same ground.

Verses 7–12 — “Who Knows What Is Good?”
789101112

Hebrew Word Study

צֵל ṣēl — shadow: "Who knows what is good for a person in life, during the few and meaningless (hebel) days they pass through like a shadow (ṣēl)?" (v.12). Human life is a shadow: present but insubstantial, real but fleeting. The double image (hebel + shadow) compounds the brevity. And the chapter’s closing question: who can tell anyone what will happen after them? The future is sealed.

Context

The chapter’s second half is a series of rhetorical questions that expect no answer. All human toil is for the mouth, yet appetite is never satisfied (v.7). What advantage has the wise over the fool? (v.8). Better what the eye sees than the roving of appetite (v.9). The closing questions (vv.11–12) summarise Qoheleth’s epistemological humility: we do not know what is good, we pass through like shadows, and no one can tell us what comes next.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 144:4"They are like a breath; their days are like a fleeting shadow" — the identical shadow imagery.
  • Jas 4:14"You are a mist that appears for a little while" — James’s version of the shadow life.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:7

"Everyone’s toil is for their mouth, yet their appetite is never satisfied." The treadmill: work to eat, eat to work, never arriving.

6:9

"Better what the eye sees than the roving of desire." Present reality beats future fantasy. Contentment with what you have is better than longing for what you lack.

6:11–12

"The more the words, the less the meaning. Who knows what is good? Who can tell anyone what will happen after them?" The chapter closes with questions that dissolve certainty. More talk does not equal more meaning.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:9

Alter: "what the eye sees" is the concrete present; "roving of appetite" is the abstract future. Qoheleth counsels: live where you are, not where you wish you were.

6:12

Alter: the chapter ends mid-question. No answer is given. The literary effect mirrors the epistemological point: we are left without resolution.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:7–9

Calvin: insatiable appetite is the result of misdirected desire. The soul seeks the infinite in the finite and is always disappointed. Redirect the soul to God, and the appetite finds its proper object.

6:12

Calvin: "who knows?" is the honest cry of every thinking person. Calvin: the answer is available — in revelation, not in observation. Qoheleth’s limitation is methodological: he looks only "under the sun."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:9

The NET: "better what the eye sees" is a proverb favouring present enjoyment over restless desire. The bird in hand beats two in the bush.

6:12

The NET: "like a shadow" (ṣēl) emphasises both reality and transience. Shadows are real optical phenomena but have no substance of their own.

Longman Notes

longman
6:7–9

Longman: appetite (nephesh, literally "soul") is insatiable. The human soul was made for the infinite; finite goods cannot fill it. Augustine’s "restless heart" is Qoheleth’s observation stated theologically.

6:12

Longman: "who knows what is good?" is not nihilism but honest limitation. Qoheleth admits the boundaries of his own wisdom. The answer to "who knows?" is: God knows, but he hasn’t told us everything.

Fox Notes

fox
6:7–9

Fox: the appetite/satisfaction paradox is the micro-version of hebel. Each individual desire recurs: you eat, you hunger again. The cycle is internal, not just cosmic.

6:12

Fox: "pass through like a shadow" — the shadow is real (it exists) but insubstantial (it has no weight). Human life is exactly this: real but unable to hold its own shape.
