Ecclesiastes 10

Wisdom and Folly Proverbs

Verses 1–10 — Dead Flies and the Fool’s Heart
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

זְבוּבֵי מָוֶת zəbūbê māwet — flies of death / dead flies: "As dead flies (zəbūbê māwet) give perfume a bad smell, so a little folly outweighs wisdom and honour" (v.1). The asymmetry again: a tiny impurity ruins a great good. One dead fly ruins an entire batch of perfume. One foolish act can destroy a lifetime of wisdom. The ratio is disproportionate and unfair — which is precisely the point.

Context

A collection of loosely connected wisdom proverbs. The fool’s heart inclines to the left (v.2). Fools broadcast their foolishness (v.3). The one who digs a pit falls into it; the one who breaks through a wall is bitten by a snake (v.8–9). These are traditional proverbs Qoheleth gathers, not all original to him. Their placement here suggests that even practical wisdom has limits: you can know these truths and still be caught by chance.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 26:1–12The "fool" collection in Proverbs — the same tradition Qoheleth draws from.
  • Gal 5:9"A little yeast works through the whole batch of dough" — Paul’s version of the dead-fly principle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1

"Dead flies give perfume a bad smell." The ratio: a vast quantity of good ruined by a tiny quantity of bad. This is the mathematics of folly.

10:2

"The heart of the wise inclines to the right, but the heart of the fool to the left." Not political commentary but the traditional association of the right hand with skill and favour.

10:8–9

"Whoever digs a pit may fall into it; whoever breaks through a wall may be bitten by a snake." Actions have consequences, but the consequences are not always proportionate or predictable.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:1

Alter: the proverb’s power is in its sensory concreteness. You can picture the flies, smell the ruined perfume. Wisdom literature at its best is vivid before it is abstract.

10:8–9

Alter: the pit-digger and wall-breaker proverbs describe the world’s unpredictable recoil. Every action disturbs the environment, and the environment pushes back.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1

Calvin: the dead-fly principle applies to reputation, ministry, and character. A lifetime of integrity can be undone by a single public failure. The disproportion is the warning.

10:8–9

Calvin: "whoever digs a pit may fall into it" — the proverb applies to the one who sets traps for others. God’s justice sometimes operates through natural consequences.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:1

The NET: zəbūbê māwet literally "flies of death" — putrefying flies. The image is of decay contaminating something precious.

10:8–9

The NET: these proverbs describe occupational hazards. Each trade carries its own risk. The point: no activity is risk-free.

Longman Notes

longman
10:1

Longman: the dead-fly proverb is 9:18 in miniature: "one sinner destroys much good." The theme of asymmetric destruction runs through Qoheleth’s thought.

10:8–10

Longman: the pit-and-snake proverbs are traditional wisdom about unintended consequences. Even careful action carries risk. You cannot eliminate danger by being wise.

Fox Notes

fox
10:1

Fox: the perfume/flies image is olfactory — you smell the ruin before you see it. A little folly announces itself immediately and permanently.

10:2

Fox: "right" and "left" are skill/weakness pairs, not moral categories. The fool’s heart goes toward incompetence; the wise person’s goes toward capability.

Verses 11–20 — Words of the Wise; Do Not Curse the King
11121314151617181920

Hebrew Word Study

בַעַל הַלָּשׁוֹן baʿal hallāshōn — master of the tongue / snake charmer: "If a snake bites before it is charmed, the charmer (baʿal hallāshōn, "master of the tongue") receives no fee" (v.11). A proverb about timing: even skill is useless if exercised too late. The snake charmer who charms after the bite has wasted his expertise. Wisdom must be timely to be useful.

Context

The proverbs continue: the fool’s words consume him (vv.12–14), fools exhaust themselves because they don’t know the way to town (v.15), woe to the land whose king is a child (v.16) but blessed is the land whose king is noble (v.17). The chapter closes with two warnings: laziness leads to a leaking roof (v.18), and do not curse the king even in thought, because a bird may carry the report (v.20). Practical wisdom for surviving in a world of power and folly.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 18:21"The tongue has the power of life and death" — the same speech-theology.
  • Jas 3:5–6"The tongue is a small part of the body, but it makes great boasts" — James’s development of the speech proverbs.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:12–14

"Words from the mouth of the wise are gracious, but fools are consumed by their own lips. The beginning of their words is folly; their end is wicked madness." The fool’s speech degenerates: starts foolish, ends insane.

10:16–17

"Woe to the land whose king was a servant and whose princes feast in the morning! Blessed is the land whose king is of noble birth and whose princes eat at a proper time." Leadership quality determines national destiny. Discipline at the top produces order below.

10:20

"Do not revile the king even in your thoughts, or curse the rich in your bedroom, because a bird in the sky may carry your words." Prudence about speech — especially about the powerful. Nothing is truly private.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:11

Alter: "master of the tongue" is literally baʿal hallāshōn. The double meaning: the snake charmer uses his tongue (incantation), and the proverb is about the tongue (speech). The tongue that charms too late is the tongue that speaks too late.

10:20

Alter: the bird-as-informant image is vivid and paranoid — appropriate for anyone living under absolute monarchy.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:16–17

Calvin: godly leadership is a national blessing; ungodly leadership is a national curse. The king proverbs apply to every form of governance: character at the top determines conditions below.

10:20

Calvin: the caution about speech is not cowardice but prudence. Speaking truth to power has a time and place; cursing power in private is neither wise nor brave.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:11

The NET: baʿal hallāshōn literally "possessor of the tongue" — one who masters speech, specifically incantation. The applied meaning: any expertise must be timely.

10:20

The NET: "a bird in the sky may carry your words" is proverbial, not literal. Walls have ears; secrets travel. The wise person assumes surveillance.

Longman Notes

longman
10:12–15

Longman: the fool’s speech proverbs reinforce 5:1–7: speech matters, and the fool’s speech reveals and accelerates their foolishness.

10:20

Longman: "a bird may carry your words" is probably the origin of the English idiom "a little bird told me." The proverb warns: assume everything you say will be reported.

Fox Notes

fox
10:11

Fox: the snake-charmer proverb is about the timing of expertise. Skill deployed too late is worthless. This applies to wisdom itself: wisdom after the disaster has already occurred helps no one.

10:16–17

Fox: the king proverbs are political observation, not advice. Qoheleth describes what he has seen: immature rulers produce disorder; disciplined rulers produce stability.
