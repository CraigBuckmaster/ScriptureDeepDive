Ecclesiastes 7

Wisdom and Its Limits

Verses 1–14 — “Sorrow Is Better Than Laughter”
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

כַעַס kaʿas — sorrow / grief / vexation: "Sorrow (kaʿas) is better than laughter, because a sad face is good for the heart" (v.3). Counter-intuitive wisdom: grief teaches what laughter cannot. The house of mourning is a better school than the house of feasting (v.2). Qoheleth values what is painful but instructive over what is pleasant but shallow.

Context

"Better" proverbs dominate vv.1–12: a good name is better than fine perfume (v.1), the day of death than the day of birth (v.1), mourning than feasting (v.2), rebuke than song (v.5), patience than pride (v.8). These are deliberately provocative — inversions of common sense that reveal deeper wisdom. Then the caution: do not say "why were the old days better?" (v.10). Nostalgia is foolishness, not wisdom.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 27:6"Wounds from a friend can be trusted" — the same preference for painful truth over pleasant flattery.
  • 2 Cor 7:10"Godly sorrow brings repentance that leads to salvation" — Paul’s theological development of Qoheleth’s insight.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1–2

"A good name is better than fine perfume, and the day of death better than the day of birth." The paradox: death is better because a completed life can be assessed. Birth is promise; death is verdict.

7:3–4

"Sorrow is better than laughter. The heart of the wise is in the house of mourning." Mourning confronts reality; feasting avoids it. Wisdom goes where it hurts.

7:8–10

"The end of a matter is better than its beginning, and patience is better than pride. Do not say, ‘Why were the old days better?’ It is not wise to ask such questions." Nostalgia is lazy; patience is wise.

7:13–14

"Consider what God has done: who can straighten what he has made crooked? When times are good, be happy; when times are bad, consider: God has made the one as well as the other." The theological conclusion: both good and bad come from God. Accept both.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:1

Alter: "day of death better than day of birth" is the chapter’s most provocative claim. Alter: at birth, everything is potential; at death, everything is actual. You can evaluate a completed life.

7:13

Alter: "who can straighten what he has made crooked?" echoes 1:15. Some things cannot be fixed. Wisdom is knowing the difference between what you can change and what you cannot.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:2–3

Calvin: the house of mourning teaches what the house of feasting cannot: that life is finite, judgment is real, and preparation matters more than pleasure.

7:14

Calvin: "God has made the one as well as the other" — prosperity and adversity both serve God’s purposes. Job learned this through suffering; Qoheleth teaches it through observation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:1

The NET: "good name" (shēm) and "fine perfume" (shemen) are a wordplay in Hebrew — shēm/shemen. Character outlasts fragrance.

7:14

The NET: "God has made the one as well as the other" — literally "God has made this corresponding to that." The alternation of good and bad is designed, not random.

Longman Notes

longman
7:1–12

Longman: the "better" proverbs are Qoheleth’s counter-cultural wisdom. Each inverts natural preference: death over birth, mourning over feasting, rebuke over song. The wise person gravitates toward what is instructive, not what is pleasant.

7:13–14

Longman: "God has made the one as well as the other" is determinism tempered by pragmatism. You cannot change what God does; you can choose how to respond.

Fox Notes

fox
7:3

Fox: "sorrow is better than laughter" is not masochism but realism. Sorrow strips illusions; laughter maintains them. The wise person prefers the uncomfortable truth.

7:10

Fox: the anti-nostalgia proverb is sociologically acute. Every generation believes the past was better. Qoheleth: this is not wisdom but selective memory.

Verses 15–29 — “No One on Earth Is Righteous”
151617181920212223242526272829

Hebrew Word Study

צַדִּיק ṣaddîq — righteous one: "There is not a righteous person (ṣaddîq) on earth who does what is good and never sins" (v.20). The universal sinfulness declaration — the OT’s clearest statement of total depravity outside the Psalms. Paul will quote this tradition in Romans 3:10. No one is exempt.

יָשָׁר yāshār — upright / straight: "God created mankind upright (yāshār), but they have gone in search of many schemes" (v.29). The fall narrative in one sentence. Humanity was made straight; they chose crooked. The plural "schemes" suggests not one fall but a thousand small deviations.

Context

Qoheleth warns against extremes: do not be overrighteous or overwise (v.16), but do not be overwicked or a fool either (v.17). The middle way. Then the bleak observation about women (vv.26–28) — widely debated: is this misogyny or a report on Qoheleth’s experience of courtly seduction? The chapter ends with the anthropological verdict: humans were made upright but chose complexity (v.29).

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 3:10–12"There is no one righteous, not even one" — Paul’s quotation of this tradition.
  • Gen 3:6"She took some and ate it" — the narrative version of Qoheleth’s "gone in search of many schemes."
  • 1 Kgs 11:1–3Solomon’s foreign wives turned his heart — the autobiographical subtext of the Solomonic persona.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:16–18

"Do not be overrighteous, do not be overwise. Do not be overwicked, do not be a fool." The balanced life: avoid both self-righteous perfectionism and reckless licence. "Whoever fears God will avoid all extremes."

7:20

"Indeed, there is no one on earth who is righteous, no one who does what is good and never sins." The universal diagnosis. The friends told Job to repent; Qoheleth says everyone needs to.

7:29

"This only have I found: God created mankind upright, but they have gone in search of many schemes." The creation-fall summary. Simplicity was the gift; complexity was the choice.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:20

Alter: the universality is emphatic — "not one" (ʾēyn). Alter connects this to the realism running through Ecclesiastes: since no one is sinless, the self-righteous friend is as guilty as the suffering sufferer.

7:29

Alter: "many schemes" carries the flavour of ingenuity turned wrong. The human capacity for invention, given for good, was redirected toward self-serving complexity.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:20

Calvin: this verse is foundational for the doctrine of total depravity. Every person sins; no one achieves righteousness by works. The Reformation stands on texts like this one.

7:29

Calvin: God made humans upright; the corruption is entirely human in origin. God cannot be blamed for the fall. The "many schemes" are the countless ways humanity has deviated from the original design.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:16

The NET: "overrighteous" may mean self-righteous or may warn against the extreme asceticism that destroys rather than builds. Context favours the self-righteousness reading.

7:29

The NET: hishshəbōnōṭ means reckonings, calculations, or devices. The plural emphasises multiplied deviation from the original simplicity.

Longman Notes

longman
7:16–18

Longman: "overrighteous" and "overwise" refer to self-righteous legalism, not to genuine piety. Qoheleth warns against the kind of religion the friends practised: rigid, judgmental, and ultimately wrong.

7:20

Longman: universal sinfulness is the theological basis for the "enjoy your portion" counsel: since perfection is impossible, gratitude for what God gives is the wise response.

7:29

Longman: "upright" (yāshār) is the same word used of Job (1:1). God made humans with the same integrity he praised in Job. Humans corrupted it themselves.

Fox Notes

fox
7:16–18

Fox: the "overrighteous" warning is Qoheleth at his most pragmatic. Excessive piety is as dangerous as excessive wickedness. Both are forms of extremism that ignore reality.

7:29

Fox: "many schemes" (hishshəbōnōṭ) is related to the word for "reckoning" or "calculation." Humans calculated their way out of simplicity into complexity. The fall is intellectual as much as moral.
