Ecclesiastes 11

Cast Your Bread; Rejoice, Young Person

Verses 1–6 — “Cast Your Bread on the Waters”
123456

Hebrew Word Study

שַׁלַּח shallaḥ — to send / to cast: "Cast (shallaḥ) your bread on the waters, for after many days you will find it again" (v.1). The image is of maritime trade: send grain overseas and the profit will return eventually. Qoheleth’s counsel: invest broadly, act generously, take risks — because you cannot predict which ventures will succeed.

Context

After chapters of observation and warning, Qoheleth shifts to counsel for action. Cast your bread (v.1), distribute to seven or eight (v.2), sow in the morning and evening (v.6). The logic: since you cannot predict the future, act broadly rather than narrowly. Do not let uncertainty paralyse you. The wind and clouds are unpredictable (vv.4–5), but the farmer who waits for perfect conditions never sows.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Cor 9:6"Whoever sows generously will also reap generously" — Paul’s sowing theology echoes Qoheleth.
  • Gal 6:9"Let us not become weary in doing good, for at the proper time we will reap" — sowing with patience.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:1–2

"Cast your bread on the waters... invest in seven ventures, yes, in eight." Diversify. Generosity and prudence combined: give broadly because you don’t know which investment will pay off.

11:4–5

"Whoever watches the wind will not plant; whoever looks at the clouds will not reap." Waiting for certainty prevents action. Faith means acting without complete information.

11:6

"Sow your seed in the morning, and at evening let your hands not be idle, for you do not know which will succeed." Work persistently because outcomes are unpredictable. Effort is your responsibility; results are God’s.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:1

Alter: "bread on the waters" is commercial: sending grain by ship. The return is delayed but expected. Faith bridges the gap between sending and receiving.

11:4

Alter: the wind-watcher and cloud-gazer are paralysed by analysis. Qoheleth’s counter: imperfect action beats perfect inaction.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:1–2

Calvin: generosity as investment is God’s economy. What is given away returns; what is hoarded rots. Calvin: the stingy person trusts their own management more than God’s provision.

11:5–6

Calvin: "you do not know the work of God" is the same epistemological limit as 3:11 and 8:17, but now applied positively: since you don’t know, sow everywhere and let God choose which prospers.

NET Bible Notes

11:5

Longman Notes

longman
11:1–2

Longman: the bread-on-water image combines generosity with business acumen. Qoheleth counsels bold action precisely because the future is unknown. Hebel drives generosity, not paralysis.

11:6

Longman: "you do not know which will succeed" — the ignorance that frustrated Qoheleth in earlier chapters now motivates him. Since you cannot know, try everything.

Fox Notes

fox
11:1–6

Fox: this section reverses Qoheleth’s earlier tone. After chapters of "you cannot know," he now says: "therefore act." The practical response to epistemological limits is not withdrawal but broad engagement.

11:5

Fox: "the work of God who makes everything" — Qoheleth grounds his counsel in theology: God is at work in the unknown. Your ignorance is not God’s absence.

Verses 7–10 — “Light Is Sweet”; Rejoice, Young Person
78910

Hebrew Word Study

בַחוּרוֹתֶיךָ baḥūrōṭek̅ā — in your youth: "Rejoice, young person, in your youth (baḥūrōṭek̅ā), and let your heart give you joy in the days of your youth" (v.9). The seventh and final "enjoy" passage. Directed specifically at the young: use your strength, follow your heart, enjoy your eyes — but know that God will bring you to judgment. The joy is real; the accountability is also real.

Context

The book’s tone lifts. "Light is sweet" (v.7) — simply being alive is good. Then the direct address to youth: rejoice, follow your heart, walk in the sight of your eyes. But: "know that for all these things God will bring you into judgment" (v.9). The joy is encouraged but framed by accountability. And: "banish anxiety from your heart and cast off the troubles of your body, for youth and vigour are hebel" (v.10). Even the best days are fleeting. This prepares for the aging allegory in chapter 12.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Tim 4:12"Don’t let anyone look down on you because you are young" — the same affirmation of youth.
  • Ps 37:4"Take delight in the LORD, and he will give you the desires of your heart" — the Psalm that complements Qoheleth’s "follow your heart."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:7–8

"Light is sweet, and it pleases the eyes to see the sun. However many years anyone may live, let them enjoy them all." Simple gratitude for existence. Even Qoheleth, after all his hebel, says: being alive is good.

11:9

"Rejoice, young person... follow the ways of your heart and whatever your eyes see, but know that for all these things God will bring you into judgment." The balance: enjoy fully AND live accountably. Joy and judgment are not opposites.

11:10

"Banish anxiety from your heart and cast off the troubles of your body, for youth and vigour are meaningless." Youth itself is hebel — not because it is bad but because it is brief. Therefore: don’t waste it on worry.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:7

Alter: "light is sweet" — monosyllabic, unqualified, beautiful. After 10 chapters of complexity, Qoheleth’s simplest sentence is his most memorable.

11:9

Alter: "follow your heart and your eyes" inverts the warning of Numbers 15:39 ("do not follow after your own heart and your own eyes"). Qoheleth is more permissive than Torah — but the judgment clause restores the boundary.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:9

Calvin: "God will bring you into judgment" is the eschatological frame that redeems the carpe diem counsel. Enjoy freely, but not as if there were no Judge. The balance is freedom within accountability.

11:10

Calvin: "banish anxiety" is a command, not a suggestion. Anxiety dishonours the God who gives today’s portion. Calvin: worry is the opposite of faith, and Qoheleth condemns it alongside folly.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:9

The NET: "God will bring you into judgment" is debated — is it Qoheleth’s own caveat or a later editorial addition? The text as it stands frames joy within divine accountability.

11:10

The NET: "youth and vigour" (yaldūṭ) literally means "dawn of life." The metaphor: youth is the morning of existence, bright but brief.

Longman Notes

longman
11:9

Longman: "follow your heart" is not antinomian permission but death-aware urgency. Enjoy deeply because the window is closing. The judgment clause prevents the counsel from becoming licence.

11:10

Longman: "youth and vigour are hebel" is the transition to chapter 12’s aging allegory. The best years pass fastest. What follows — aging and death — comes for everyone.

Fox Notes

fox
11:7–8

Fox: "light is sweet" is Qoheleth’s simplest positive statement. No qualification, no "but." Life is good. This is not inconsistent with hebel — the transient can still be sweet.

11:9–10

Fox: the judgment clause is either Qoheleth’s own caveat or the epilogue narrator’s addition. Either way, it frames the joy within accountability. Fox: the tension is the book’s final word on enjoyment — real, encouraged, and answerable.
