Ecclesiastes 4

Oppression, Toil, Companionship

Verses 1–8 — Oppression, Envy, and the Lonely Toiler
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

עֹשֶק ʿōsheq — oppression / extortion: "I saw the tears of the oppressed (ʿōsheq) — and they have no comforter" (v.1). The word appears three times in v.1, hammering the theme. Oppression exists, the oppressed weep, and no one comforts them. The power is on the side of the oppressors. Qoheleth’s social observation is as sharp as Amos’s.

Context

Qoheleth turns from existential reflection to social observation. Oppression (vv.1–3): the dead are happier than the living, and the unborn are happiest. Envy (vv.4–6): all toil is driven by envy; better one handful with tranquillity than two with chasing wind. The lonely toiler (vv.7–8): a person alone, no companion, endless work — "for whom am I toiling?" The question of purpose without community.

Cross-Reference

  • Amos 4:1"You cows of Bashan who oppress the poor" — the same social critique.
  • Job 3:11–13"Why did I not perish at birth?" — Job’s parallel conclusion that the dead are better off.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1–3

"I saw the tears of the oppressed — and they have no comforter." Repeated twice for emphasis. The oppressed have power against them and no advocate. Qoheleth’s conclusion: better to be dead; better still never to have been born.

4:4

"All toil and all achievement spring from one person’s envy of another." A devastating social analysis: the engine of productivity is comparison, not purpose.

4:6

"Better one handful with tranquillity than two handfuls with toil and chasing after wind." The proverb of enough. One handful, held in peace, beats two grasped in anxiety.

4:7–8

"There was a man all alone... yet there was no end to his toil. ‘For whom am I toiling and why am I depriving myself of enjoyment?’" The question of purpose without relationship.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:1

Alter: the triple repetition of oppression (ʿōsheq) in v.1 is rhetorical force: the word itself oppresses the ear.

4:6

Alter: the handful proverb has the economy of a haiku. One image (the open hand holding enough) against another (two fists grasping and chasing). The wisdom is in the contrast.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1

Calvin: "no comforter" is the worst feature of oppression. Pain with comfort is bearable; pain without comfort is desolation. The church’s calling is to be the comforter the oppressed lack.

4:6

Calvin: contentment is not laziness but spiritual maturity. The person with one handful and peace has more than the person with two handfuls and anxiety.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:1

The NET: the triple occurrence of ʿōsheq emphasises systemic, ongoing exploitation, not a single incident.

4:6

The NET: "tranquillity" (naḥat) means rest, quietness, composure. The contrast is between peaceful sufficiency and anxious excess.

Longman Notes

longman
4:1–3

Longman: the "no comforter" refrain is devastating. In a just world, the oppressed would have advocates. In Qoheleth’s world, power belongs to the oppressor. This is the social dimension of hebel.

4:6

Longman: the one-handful proverb is Qoheleth’s ethic of sufficiency. Enough is better than more. This challenges both ancient and modern cultures of accumulation.

Fox Notes

fox
4:1–3

Fox: Qoheleth’s observation of oppression is not cynicism but clear-eyed sorrow. He does not blame the victims or excuse the oppressors. He simply notes: this is how it is.

4:4

Fox: "envy of another" as the root of toil is a psychological insight. Competition, not necessity, drives most human effort. Remove comparison and the motivation collapses.

Verses 9–16 — “Two Are Better Than One”
910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

הַחוּט הַמְשֻׁלָּשׁ haḥūṭ haməshullāsh — the threefold cord: "A cord of three strands (haḥūṭ haməshullāsh) is not quickly broken" (v.12). The most quoted verse in the chapter. Two are better than one; three are stronger still. The image is of rope: individual strands break easily; braided together, they hold. Community is not optional but structural.

Context

After the loneliness of v.7–8, Qoheleth affirms companionship. Two are better: they can help each other up when one falls (v.10), keep each other warm (v.11), defend against attack (v.12). Then a political proverb (vv.13–16): a poor but wise youth is better than an old foolish king. But even the youth’s popularity fades. Popularity is hebel too.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 2:18"It is not good for the man to be alone" — the creation theology behind Qoheleth’s observation.
  • Matt 18:20"Where two or three gather in my name, there am I" — Jesus’ version of the threefold cord.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:9–10

"Two are better than one, because they have a good return for their labour: if either of them falls down, one can help the other up." The economic and practical case for companionship.

4:11–12

"If two lie down together, they will keep warm... though one may be overpowered, two can defend themselves. A cord of three strands is not quickly broken." Warmth, defence, strength — all require company.

4:13–16

"Better a poor but wise youth than an old but foolish king." Even political success is hebel: the crowds who cheered the new king will forget him too.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:12

Alter: the cord of three strands is a folk proverb Qoheleth adopts. The imagery is from rope-making: each strand added multiplies the breaking strength.

4:15–16

Alter: "those who came later were not pleased with the successor" — the fickleness of public opinion. Yesterday’s hero is tomorrow’s forgotten man.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:9–12

Calvin: the companionship passage is God’s design made visible. Humans were not created for isolation. The church, marriage, and friendship are all expressions of the cord’s strength.

4:13–16

Calvin: the political proverb teaches that no human institution provides lasting security. Kings rise and fall; popularity ebbs. Only what God establishes endures (3:14).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:12

The NET: "cord of three strands" is proverbial, not specifically theological. But the progression from one (v.8) to two (v.9) to three (v.12) emphasises that more connection means more strength.

4:16

The NET: "those who came later were not pleased" — each generation forgets the previous. Popularity is the most ephemeral form of gain.

Longman Notes

longman
4:9–12

Longman: after cataloguing loneliness and oppression, Qoheleth offers the book’s most positive social observation. Companionship is not hebel. It has tangible value: help, warmth, strength.

4:12

Longman: the "third strand" is often read as God, though Qoheleth does not say so explicitly. The principle: community stronger than the sum of its parts.

Fox Notes

fox
4:9–12

Fox: companionship is presented in pragmatic terms: help, warmth, defence. Qoheleth does not sentimentalise friendship. Its value is functional, which for him is the highest compliment.

4:13–16

Fox: the political proverb is another example of hebel: even the replacement king will be forgotten. Power is a revolving door.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Qoheleth
The Teacher; traditionally identified with Solomon
The royal observer who tested every human pursuit and found all of them hebel. His voice is not despair but honest empiricism: what can be verified "under the sun"? His positive counsel: enjoy the portion God gives.
