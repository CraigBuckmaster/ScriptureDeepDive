Job 15

Eliphaz’s Second Speech: Harsher

Verses 1–16 — “Your Own Mouth Condemns You”
123456789

Hebrew Word Study

דַעַת daʿat — knowledge / opinion: "Should the wise fill their bellies with the hot east wind (rūaḥ qādîm)?" (v.2). Eliphaz accuses Job of producing only hot air. The east wind (qādîm) is the sirocco — the dry, scorching desert wind that kills vegetation. Job’s words, Eliphaz says, are destructive, not wise.

Context

Eliphaz has dropped all gentleness. In his first speech (ch 4–5) he began with compliments. Now he leads with “your own mouth condemns you” (v.6). His argument: Job’s protests undermine piety itself, not just Job’s case. By challenging God, Job is discouraging others from faith.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 10:19"When words are many, sin is not absent" — the proverbial basis for Eliphaz’s suspicion of many words.
  • Job 4:2–4Compare Eliphaz’s gentle first opening with this aggressive second one.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
15:2–3

Eliphaz attacks Job’s speech as worthless wind. The tone shift from chapter 4 is dramatic.

15:4–6

"You even undermine piety and hinder devotion to God. Your sin prompts your mouth; you adopt the tongue of the crafty. Your own mouth condemns you." Eliphaz claims Job’s protests are evidence of guilt.

15:7–8

"Were you the first man born? Were you brought forth before the hills?" Eliphaz challenges Job’s wisdom. You are not primordial; you have no special knowledge.

15:14–16

"What are mortals, that they could be pure?" Eliphaz repeats his night vision theology (4:17): all humans are corrupt. This time without the vision — just the assertion.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:6

Calvin: “your own mouth condemns you” — Eliphaz treats Job’s protests as confessions. This is circular reasoning: complaining about unjust suffering proves you deserve it.

15:14–16

Calvin: the universal corruption argument is theologically valid (Rom 3:23) but pastorally useless. Telling a sufferer that everyone is guilty answers nothing.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:2

The NET: the “east wind” (qādîm) is the sirocco — the hot, dry wind from the Arabian desert that desiccates everything. Eliphaz says Job’s words are equally destructive.

15:7

The NET: “were you the first man born?” echoes the Primeval Man tradition — the first human was thought to have special wisdom by virtue of proximity to creation.

Clines Notes

clines
15:4

Clines: “you undermine piety” is Eliphaz’s most serious charge. Job’s protest, in the friends’ view, is not just personal error but a public danger — it teaches others to question God.

15:7–8

Clines: “were you the first man born?” may allude to the Primeval Man myth (cf. Ezek 28:12–15). Eliphaz denies Job any special access to divine wisdom.

Habel Notes

habel
15:1–16

Habel: the prosecution reopens its case. Eliphaz introduces new charges: Job’s words themselves are evidence of guilt. His protests undermine public religion.

Verses 17–35 — The Fate of the Wicked

Hebrew Word Study

רָשָׁע rāshāʿ — wicked / guilty: "The wicked person (rāshāʿ) writhes in pain all their days" (v.20). Eliphaz paints an elaborate portrait of the wicked person’s fate. The implication is unmistakable: this is your future, Job, unless you repent.

Context

The second half of Eliphaz’s speech is a long catalogue of horrors awaiting the wicked. The wicked live in terror (v.20–21), wander hungry (v.23), are crushed (v.24), defied God (v.25–26), and lose everything (vv.29–34). The description bears uncomfortable resemblance to Job’s actual situation — which is precisely Eliphaz’s rhetorical strategy.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 73:16–20The Psalmist saw the prosperity of the wicked and was troubled — until he entered the sanctuary. Eliphaz only sees the punishment side.
  • Prov 1:31–32"They will eat the fruit of their ways" — the proverbial tradition Eliphaz draws from.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
15:20–25

The wicked: tormented all their days, terrifying sounds in their ears, destruction waits. "He shakes his fist at God and vaunts himself against the Almighty."

15:27–30

Obesity, ruined cities, lost wealth. Eliphaz connects physical comfort with spiritual corruption. The rich wicked will lose everything.

15:34–35

"The company of the godless will be barren... they conceive trouble and give birth to evil." The speech ends with a retribution image: the wicked produce only destruction.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:20

Calvin: the wicked do suffer, and Eliphaz’s descriptions are not wrong as general observations. They are wrong as a diagnosis of Job’s specific case.

15:25–26

Calvin: accusing Job of shaking his fist at God is Eliphaz’s worst misreading. Job has not shaken his fist; he has cried for a hearing. Protest and rebellion are different things.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:20

The NET: “writhes in pain all their days” uses a word for the physical contortions of labour pains. The wicked’s entire life is agony.

15:35

The NET: the closing image of conceiving trouble and giving birth to evil (cf. Isa 59:4) frames wickedness as generative — it produces more of itself.

Clines Notes

clines
15:17–35

Clines: the catalogue of wicked-person horrors is aimed at Job. Each line is a mirror: you live in terror, you wander, you will lose everything. Eliphaz is too “polite” to say “you are wicked” directly — he paints the portrait and lets Job see himself in it.

15:25–26

Clines: “shakes his fist at God” — Eliphaz implies Job’s protests are rebellion against God. The legal complaint has been redefined as insubordination.

Habel Notes

habel
15:17–35

Habel: Eliphaz presents a precedent catalogue — here is what happens to the wicked. The unspoken conclusion: Job’s experience matches the pattern, therefore Job is wicked.
