Job 30

Job’s Present Misery

Verses 1–15 — “But Now They Mock Me”
123456789101112131415

Hebrew Word Study

עַתָּה ʿattâ — now: "But now (ʿattâ) they mock me, men younger than I" (v.1). The contrast with chapter 29 is built on this word: "now." Then: honour. Now: mockery. Then: respected elder. Now: laughingstock of outcasts. The temporal marker is a theological indictment: nothing in Job’s character changed; only his circumstances did.

Context

The contrast with chapter 29 is systematic. Then: young men stepped aside (29:8). Now: young men mock him (30:1). Then: chief men fell silent (29:9–10). Now: outcasts spit at him (30:10). Then: he chose their way (29:25). Now: terrors are turned against him (30:15). The reversal is total, and Job presents it as evidence that his suffering is disproportionate to any possible cause.

Cross-Reference

  • Lam 1:1"How deserted lies the city, once so full of people!" — the same contrast between former glory and present desolation.
  • Ps 22:6–7"I am a worm and not a man, scorned and despised" — the Psalmist’s parallel experience of social rejection.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
30:1–8

"But now they mock me, men younger than I, whose fathers I would have disdained to put with my sheep dogs." The social inversion: the lowest class of society now mocks the man who was their superior. The outcasts mock the outcast.

30:9–11

"And now those young men mock me in song; I have become a byword among them. They detest me and keep their distance; they do not hesitate to spit in my face." Total social rejection.

30:12–15

"On my right the rabble rises; they lay snares for my feet... terrors overwhelm me; my dignity is driven away as by the wind." Job’s honour has evaporated.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
30:1

Calvin: the mockery from social inferiors is the cruellest cut. Those Job would have helped now spit on him. Social reversal is a secondary suffering layered on top of the primary.

30:15

Calvin: "my dignity is driven away as by the wind" — dignity cannot be reclaimed by argument. It must be restored by God, which is exactly what Job awaits.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
30:1

The NET: the emphasis on "younger than I" highlights the social inversion. In a culture where age commanded respect, being mocked by youth was the ultimate indignity.

30:9

The NET: "byword" is māshāl — Job has become a proverbial example of ruin, the object lesson people cite.

Clines Notes

clines
30:1

Clines: the word "now" (ʿattâ) is the chapter’s structural hinge. Everything in chapter 29 is reversed by ʿattâ. The reversal is not moral but situational — the man hasn’t changed; his world has.

30:9–10

Clines: becoming a "byword" (māshāl) means Job’s name is now a proverb of ruin — the cautionary tale other people tell. The respected elder has become a joke.

Habel Notes

habel
30:1–15

Habel: Job presents evidence of damages. The contrast between chapters 29 and 30 quantifies the loss: from universal respect to universal contempt.

Verses 16–31 — “I Have Become a Brother of Jackals”
16171819202122232425262728293031

Hebrew Word Study

אַךְ תַנִּים ʾakh tannîm — brother of jackals: "I have become a brother of jackals, a companion of owls" (v.29). Job’s only companions are the animals of the wasteland. The jackals and ostriches inhabit the ruins; Job inhabits the ruins of his life. The animal imagery marks his exclusion from human community.

Context

Job’s lament reaches its absolute bottom. God has thrown him into the mud (v.19), his skin is blackened and peeling (v.30), his bones burn (v.30), his harp is tuned to mourning and his pipe to wailing (v.31). The chapter ends with music turned to dirge. This is the "present" panel of the triptych: past glory (29), present misery (30), future oath (31).

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 102:3–7"My bones burn like glowing embers... I am like a desert owl, like an owl among the ruins" — the identical animal-of-ruin imagery.
  • Isa 34:13–14"Jackals will occupy her palaces... desert creatures will meet with hyenas" — desolation imagery applied to Edom.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
30:16–19

"Now my life ebbs away... nights of misery have been assigned to me. God has thrown me into the mud, and I have been reduced to dust and ashes." Job addresses God directly: you did this.

30:20–23

"I cry out to you, God, but you do not answer; I stand up, but you merely look at me. You turn on me ruthlessly; you drive me before the driving wind." God’s silence is the worst suffering. Job speaks and receives no response.

30:26

"Yet when I hoped for good, evil came; when I looked for light, then came darkness." The expectation-reversal formula. Job’s theology predicted blessing; he received ruin.

30:29–31

"I have become a brother of jackals, a companion of owls. My skin grows black and peels; my body burns with fever. My lyre is tuned to mourning, my pipe to the sound of wailing." The speech ends in dirge. Music, the highest art, is perverted into lamentation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
30:20

Calvin: "you do not answer" is the cry that anticipates the whirlwind. God IS going to answer (ch 38). But the silence between complaint and response is the furnace where faith is tested.

30:31

Calvin: music turned to mourning is the cultural equivalent of creation turned to chaos. When the highest human art serves only grief, something fundamental has broken.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
30:19

The NET: "thrown me into the mud" uses a verb for violent casting down. God is the active agent of Job’s humiliation.

30:29

The NET: jackals and ostriches inhabit ruins and deserts. Their cries are mournful and eerie. Job’s companions are the sounds of desolation.

Clines Notes

clines
30:20–21

Clines: "I cry out and you do not answer" is the most devastating accusation. God’s silence is not mere absence but active rejection. Job stands up and God merely stares.

30:29–31

Clines: the brother of jackals, companion of ostriches — animals of ruins and desolation. Job is not merely suffering but has been relocated to the wasteland, expelled from human community.

Habel Notes

habel
30:16–31

Habel: Job presents the full extent of his suffering. The catalogue is comprehensive: physical (burning skin, fever), social (brother of jackals), emotional (mourning), and theological (God does not answer). The plaintiff’s damages are total.
