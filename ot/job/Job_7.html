Job 7

Job Addresses God Directly

Verses 1–11 — "My Days Are Swifter Than a Weaver’s Shuttle"
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

צָבָא ṣābāʾ — army / hard service / warfare: "Does not mortals have hard service (ṣābāʾ) on earth?" (v.1). Life is conscription — forced labour with a fixed term. The image is military: a soldier counting the days until discharge. Job’s life feels like involuntary service with no end in sight.

הֶבֶל hebel — breath / vapour / vanity: "Remember that my life is a breath (hebel)" (v.7). The same word that dominates Ecclesiastes. Human life is a puff of air — real but insubstantial, present but passing.

Context

Job shifts from addressing the friends (ch 6) to addressing God directly. The speech is a lament in the classic form: complaint about suffering (vv.1–6), appeal to God (vv.7–11), accusation against God (vv.12–21). Job is no longer talking about God — he is talking to God.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 39:4–5"Show me, LORD, my life’s end and the number of my days" — the same brevity theme.
  • Jas 4:14"You are a mist that appears for a little while and then vanishes" — echoing Job’s hebel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1–2

"Does not mortals have hard service on earth?" Life as forced labour. The hired worker longs for the shadows — the end of the shift. Job longs for the end of his.

7:5–6

"My body is clothed with worms and scabs." Physical description of his disease. "My days are swifter than a weaver’s shuttle, and they come to an end without hope."

7:7–8

"Remember, O God, that my life is a breath." The first direct address to God. Job wants God to remember his finitude — before it is too late.

7:11

"I will not keep silent; I will speak out in the anguish of my spirit." Job declares he will not be quiet. The complaint will continue.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1

Calvin: "hard service" — life after the fall is labour and struggle. Job states what all humans experience, but his experience intensifies the universal condition.

7:7

Calvin: addressing God as "remember" implies God may have forgotten. This is bold — Job treats God as someone who needs reminding.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:1

The NET notes ṣābāʾ means compulsory military service or corvée labour — not voluntary.

7:6

The NET: "swifter than a weaver’s shuttle" — the shuttle moves so fast the eye cannot track it. Life passes before it can be grasped.

Clines Notes

clines
7:1–6

Clines: the military and labour metaphors frame life as something endured, not enjoyed. Job’s experience has reduced existence to bare survival.

7:7

Clines: "Remember that my life is a breath" is an appeal to God’s compassion through God’s knowledge of human fragility. If God knows how short life is, why fill it with suffering?

Habel Notes

habel
7:1–11

Habel: Job opens his direct address to God with a plea for attention. The plaintiff has turned to the judge: "Remember that my life is a breath."

Verses 12–21 — "Am I the Sea, or the Monster of the Deep?"
12131415161718192021

Hebrew Word Study

יָם ... תַנִּין yām ... tannîn — sea ... sea monster: "Am I the sea (yām), or the monster of the deep (tannîn), that you put me under guard?" (v.12). Job compares himself to the primordial chaos forces that God restrained at creation. The implied protest: I am not a cosmic threat — why treat me like one?

פָקַד pāqad — to visit / attend to / examine: "What is mankind that you make so much of them, that you examine (pāqad) them every morning?" (v.17–18). A parody of Psalm 8. In the Psalm, God’s attention is glory; in Job, God’s attention is oppression.

Context

The chapter’s theological heart: Job takes Psalm 8 (“What is mankind that you are mindful of them?”) and inverts it. In the Psalm, divine attention is a gift. In Job, divine attention is surveillance. God watches every moment, tests every morning, refuses to look away even long enough for Job to swallow his spit (v.19). The parody is devastating: the same theology, opposite experience.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 8:4"What is mankind that you are mindful of them?" — the text Job parodies.
  • Ps 139:7–12"Where can I go from your Spirit?" — in the Psalm, comfort; in Job, torment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:12

"Am I the sea, or the monster?" Job protests: God treats him as a cosmic enemy, not a suffering servant. The restraint God uses on chaos forces is being applied to one fragile man.

7:17–18

The parody of Psalm 8. In the Psalm: "What is mankind that you are mindful of them?" (glory). In Job: "What is mankind that you make so much of them, that you test them every moment?" (surveillance).

7:19

"Will you never look away from me, or let me alone even for an instant?" God’s omniscience, which should be comfort, has become suffocation.

7:20–21

"If I have sinned, what have I done to you, O watcher of men?" Job does not deny sin in general — he asks what his sin could possibly do to God. And: "Why do you not pardon my offenses?" If God is so great, why can’t he simply forgive?

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:17–18

Calvin: the parody of Psalm 8 is bold but not blasphemous. Job is not denying God’s goodness but protesting its apparent reversal. The theology that should comfort has become the source of his distress.

7:20–21

Calvin: "why do you not pardon?" is the cry of a man who believes in God’s power to forgive and cannot understand why he doesn’t use it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:12

The NET notes the sea and tannîn are chaos symbols from ANE mythology, subdued by God at creation. Job sarcastically asks if he is such a threat.

7:17

The NET identifies this as a parody of Ps 8:4 and notes the shift from wonder to complaint — same words, opposite tone.

Clines Notes

clines
7:12

Clines: the sea/tannîn image draws on creation mythology. God restrained the sea at creation (cf. Ps 104:9; Jer 5:22). Job asks: am I that dangerous? The answer is obviously no — which is the point.

7:17–18

Clines: the Psalm 8 parody is the most theologically audacious move in the speech. Job takes Scripture and turns it against God. He uses God’s own word to accuse God.

Habel Notes

habel
7:12–21

Habel: Job escalates his legal argument. He is not merely complaining but building a case: God’s attention is disproportionate (v.12), oppressive (v.17–18), and unforgiving (v.20–21). The plaintiff demands relief.
