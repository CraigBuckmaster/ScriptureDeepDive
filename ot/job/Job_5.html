Job 5

Eliphaz Continues: Blessed Is the One Whom God Corrects

Verses 1–16 — "Man Is Born to Trouble"; God Rescues the Lowly
12345678910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

עָמָל ʿāmāl — trouble / toil / misery: "Man is born to trouble (ʿāmāl) as surely as sparks fly upward" (v.7). The proverb is memorable and partly true — suffering is universal. But Eliphaz uses it to normalise Job’s catastrophe: this is just what happens to mortals.

פֶלֶא peleʾ — wonder / marvel: "He performs wonders (peleʾ) that cannot be fathomed" (v.9). Eliphaz’s doxology is beautiful and orthodox. His theology of God’s greatness is not wrong — it is irrelevant to Job’s situation.

Context

Eliphaz moves from diagnosis (ch 4: the innocent don’t perish) to prescription (ch 5: appeal to God, accept his correction). His advice is textbook wisdom theology: God wounds but binds up (v.18), rescues the needy (v.15), brings down the proud (v.11). All true in general. All useless to Job in particular.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 3:11–12"Do not despise the LORD’s discipline" — the exact theology Eliphaz applies.
  • Heb 12:5–6The NT quotes Proverbs 3:11–12 approvingly — discipline theology is not wrong, just misapplied here.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1

"Call if you will, but who will answer?" Eliphaz implies Job has no advocate — no angel to intercede. Job will later cry for exactly this (9:33; 16:19; 19:25).

5:6–7

"Hardship does not spring from the soil... man is born to trouble." Suffering is not random but comes from the human condition. Eliphaz is partly right — but his implication is that Job brought it on himself.

5:8–16

The doxology: God does great things, gives rain, lifts the lowly, frustrates the crafty. Beautiful theology — wrong application. Job does not need a lecture on God’s general goodness.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:7

Calvin: "born to trouble" is the human condition after the fall. Eliphaz is right that suffering is universal. He is wrong to use universality as an explanation for Job’s specific case.

5:9–16

Calvin: every line of the doxology is true. God does rescue, heal, frustrate the wicked. The question is not whether God can do these things but whether he is doing them for Job right now.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:7

The NET discusses "sparks fly upward" vs "sons of Resheph" (fire-deity). Both readings support the same point: trouble is inherent in human existence.

5:9

The NET notes this doxology parallels Psalm 107 and other hymnic traditions — Eliphaz draws on established liturgical language.

Clines Notes

clines
5:7

Clines: "sparks fly upward" is often translated as "sons of Resheph" (a deity of plague). The proverb may mean: trouble is as natural as fire rising. Either way, Eliphaz normalises catastrophe.

5:8–16

Clines: the doxology is borrowed from hymnic tradition. Eliphaz is sincere. His error is pastoral, not theological: he offers doctrine when Job needs presence.

Habel Notes

habel
5:1–16

Habel: Eliphaz presents the case for divine discipline. His argument: suffering is corrective, therefore submit. Job will reject this because his suffering is not corrective — it is a test he knows nothing about.

Verses 17–27 — "Blessed Is the One Whom God Corrects"
1718192021222324252627

Hebrew Word Study

יָסַר yāsar — to discipline / correct / instruct: "Blessed is the one whom God corrects (yāsar)" (v.17). The discipline theology: suffering is God’s pedagogy. Do not resist it; learn from it. This is Proverbs 3:11 applied to Job — and misapplied, because Job’s suffering is not corrective.

שָׁלוֹם shālōm — peace / completeness / wholeness: "You will know that your tent is secure... you will come to the grave in full vigour" (vv.24–26). Eliphaz promises shalom — if Job submits. The conditional promise: accept correction, and prosperity returns. This is the friends’ if-then theology at its most seductive.

Context

Eliphaz closes with a beautiful promise: God will deliver from six troubles, no seven (v.19). No harm from famine, war, wild animals, or barren ground. You will die old and satisfied. The promise is a Deuteronomic ideal — covenant blessing for covenant obedience. The problem: Job has been obedient, and the blessing has been removed. The theology works in Deuteronomy; it fails in Uz.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 3:11–12The direct source of Eliphaz’s theology — discipline as blessing.
  • Heb 12:5–11The NT’s own application of discipline theology — valid in its context.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:17

"Blessed is the one whom God corrects." The theology is not wrong in principle (Heb 12:5–11). It is wrong in application: Job’s suffering is not correction.

5:18

"He wounds, but he also binds up." True as general theology. False as specific diagnosis. God did not wound Job to correct him; God permitted Satan to test him.

5:19–22

The numerical proverb: "from six calamities he will rescue you; in seven no harm will touch you." Eliphaz promises comprehensive protection — if Job repents. The conditional is the problem.

5:24–27

"You will know that your tent is secure." Eliphaz describes the restoration Job will eventually receive (ch 42). But the path to restoration is not through repentance from sin; it is through endurance of innocent suffering.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:17

Calvin: the principle of divine discipline is biblical and true. Eliphaz’s sin is not heresy but misdiagnosis. He has the right medicine for the wrong disease.

5:27

Calvin: "we have examined this" — the appeal to collective experience. But experience without revelation is inadequate. The friends have observed patterns; they have not heard from God.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:17

The NET connects this directly to Prov 3:11–12, noting Eliphaz quotes (or paraphrases) established wisdom tradition.

5:19

The NET identifies the "six... seven" formula as a graded numerical saying common in wisdom literature (cf. Prov 6:16; 30:15, 18, 21, 29).

Clines Notes

clines
5:17–27

Clines: Eliphaz’s closing is the most pastorally dangerous part of his speech. He promises exactly what Job will eventually get — but for the wrong reason. Job is restored not because he repents of sin but because he endures with integrity.

5:27

"We have examined this, and it is true." Clines: the friends’ appeal to experience. They have observed, verified, concluded. But their sample set excludes the case in front of them.

Habel Notes

habel
5:17–27

Habel: Eliphaz rests his case. The expert witness has testified: discipline theology explains everything. Job’s task in the next speech is to demolish it.
