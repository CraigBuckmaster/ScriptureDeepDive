Job 3

Job Curses the Day of His Birth

Verses 1–10 — "Let the Day Perish" — Anti-Creation
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

יוֹם yōm — day: "Let the day perish on which I was born." Job curses his birthday using the vocabulary of Genesis 1 in reverse. Where God said "Let there be light," Job says "Let that day be darkness." This is anti-creation language — Job wishes his existence undone.

חֹשֶׁךְ ḥōshek̅ — darkness: Darkness (ḥōshek̅) is the anti-creation force. In Genesis 1:2, darkness covered the deep before God spoke light. Job wants to return there — to the pre-creation void where he would never have existed.

Context

After seven days of silence, Job’s first words are not a prayer, not an accusation against God, and not a question about why. They are a wish to have never been born. The speech is the opposite of Genesis 1: systematic un-creation. Day by day, element by element, Job takes apart the world that produced him.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 1:3–5"Let there be light" — the creation language Job inverts.
  • Jer 20:14–18Jeremiah curses the day of his birth — the closest OT parallel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1–3

Job breaks the seven-day silence. His first word is a curse — not against God, but against his own existence. He curses the day and the night.

3:4–6

"Let that day be darkness." He wants creation reversed for the day he was born — let it be swallowed by chaos, erased from the calendar.

3:7–10

"May that night be barren." He wishes the night of his conception had produced nothing. No cry of joy, no new life. Because suffering exists, he wishes existence didn’t.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:3

Calvin: Job does not curse God but curses the day — a distinction the narrator will uphold. The despair is real but the line is not crossed.

3:4–5

Calvin: wishing the day were darkness is wishing creation undone. Only extreme suffering produces this kind of theological vertigo.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:3

The NET notes cursing one’s birthday is attested in Mesopotamian literature. Job’s lament draws on a shared ANE tradition of extreme grief expression.

3:8

The NET identifies "those who curse the day" (or "rouse Leviathan") as sorcerers believed to have power over cosmic forces — Job invokes even mythological chaos against his own existence.

Clines Notes

clines
3:3–10

Clines: the anti-creation motif is deliberate and systematic. Job uses the vocabulary of Genesis 1 (light, darkness, day, night, sea) in reverse order. He is not merely upset; he is theologically deconstructing his own existence.

Verses 11–26 — "Why Did I Not Die at Birth?" — Death as Rest
11121314151617181920212223242526

Hebrew Word Study

לָמָה lāmâ — why?: "Why did I not perish at birth?" (lāmâ). The question is repeated five times in the chapter (vv.11, 12, 16, 20, 23). It is not a request for information but a cry of protest. Job does not ask "why do I suffer?" but "why do I exist?"

נוּחַ nûaḥ — rest: In death, Job says, "I would be at rest" (nûaḥ). Kings, counsellors, prisoners — all are equal in death. The grave is the great equaliser. This is not nihilism but longing: death is described as the rest that life denies.

Context

The speech moves from wishing he’d never been born (vv.1–10) to wishing he’d died at birth (vv.11–19) to asking why God gives life to the suffering (vv.20–26). The trajectory narrows: from cosmic protest to personal accusation. By v.23, God is the implied target: "Why is light given to a man whose way is hidden, whom God has hedged in?"

Cross-Reference

  • Eccl 4:2–3"I declared that the dead are happier than the living" — the same grim calculus.
  • Phil 1:21–23Paul’s "to depart and be with Christ is far better" — the Christian reframing of Job’s longing.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:11–16

Stillborn children are envied: "I would be lying down in peace; I would be asleep and at rest." Death is portrayed as the sleep the living are denied.

3:17–19

"There the wicked cease from turmoil, and the weary are at rest." The democracy of death: slave and master, prisoner and guard — all equal.

3:20–23

"Why is light given to those in misery?" Now God is implicitly accused: he gives life to those who long for death. The "hedge" of v.23 inverts 1:10 — the Satan said God hedged Job with blessing; now Job says God hedges him with suffering.

3:24–26

"What I feared has come upon me." Job’s final words in the speech. The silence is broken, the debate is opened, the friends now have something to respond to.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:20–23

Calvin: Job does not accuse God directly but asks "why?" — which is the beginning of accusation. The line between lament and complaint is thin, and Job walks it.

3:26

Calvin: the triple negative — no peace, no quietness, no rest — is the cry of a soul that has lost every foothold. This is the darkest point before the debate begins.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:23

The NET connects "hedged in" to 1:10 where the Satan uses the same verb. The ironic reversal is intentional: protection becomes prison.

3:26

The NET notes the chapter ends abruptly with no resolution — a deliberate literary choice that forces the reader forward into the debate.

Clines Notes

clines
3:20–23

Clines: the inverted hedge is the speech’s sharpest irony. In 1:10 the Satan complained that God’s hedge protected Job too well. Now Job complains the hedge imprisons him. Same word, opposite meaning.

3:26

Clines on "I have no peace, no quietness, no rest" — the triple negative is the antithesis of the rest Job envied in vv.13–19. He can neither live nor die, neither rest nor escape.

Habel Notes

habel
3:20–26

Habel: the speech functions as the opening statement of the plaintiff. Job has established his standing (blameless), his suffering (total), and his protest (existence itself is the problem). The trial can begin.
