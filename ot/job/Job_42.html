Job 42

Job Repents; Restoration

Verses 1–6 — “My Ears Had Heard of You but Now My Eyes See You”
123456

Hebrew Word Study

רָאָה rāʾâ — to see: "My ears had heard of you but now my eyes see (rāʾâ) you" (v.5). The most important verse in the book. Job’s transformation is not from ignorance to knowledge but from hearing to seeing. He had correct theology (he feared God, 1:1). Now he has encounter. The difference between information about God and experience of God is the difference between hearing and seeing.

נִחַמְתִּי niḥamətî — I relent / I am comforted / I repent: "Therefore I despise myself and repent (niḥamətî) in dust and ashes" (v.6). The verb niḥam can mean repent, relent, be comforted, or change one’s mind. Job does not repent of sin (he had none) but of speaking beyond his knowledge. He retracts not his innocence but his demand for an explanation. He now has something better: encounter.

Context

Job’s second and final response. He acknowledges that God can do all things (v.2), that he spoke of things he did not understand (v.3), and that now — having seen God — he is transformed (v.5–6). The key: Job’s repentance is not the friends’ prescribed repentance (from hidden sin). It is repentance from demanding that God operate on Job’s terms. Job’s theological framework was too small for the God he has now encountered.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 6:5"Woe to me! I am ruined!" — Isaiah’s parallel response to divine encounter.
  • Luke 5:8"Go away from me, Lord; I am a sinful man!" — Peter’s response to encountering Jesus.
  • 1 John 3:2"When Christ appears, we shall see him as he is" — the ultimate seeing that Job’s encounter anticipates.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
42:2

"I know that you can do all things; no purpose of yours can be thwarted." Job affirms divine omnipotence and sovereignty without reservation. The complaint is withdrawn.

42:3

"You asked, ‘Who is this that obscures my plans without knowledge?’ Surely I spoke of things I did not understand, things too wonderful for me to know." Job quotes God’s own question (38:2) and accepts the rebuke. His words were not sinful but insufficient.

42:5

"My ears had heard of you but now my eyes see you." The book’s resolution. Job moved from theology (hearing about God) to encounter (seeing God). The whirlwind gave him not answers but the Answerer.

42:6

"Therefore I despise myself and repent in dust and ashes." Job repents — not of sin but of his theological presumption. He demanded explanations from a God whose ways transcend explanation. Now, having seen God, he needs no explanation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
42:5

Calvin: the difference between hearing and seeing is the difference between knowledge and fellowship. Job knew God before his suffering; he knows God differently after. Suffering was the corridor, not the destination. The destination is encounter.

42:6

Calvin: Job repents in dust and ashes — the same posture he began in (2:8). But the meaning has changed. Before, dust was despair. Now, dust is humility. Same posture, different person.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
42:5

The NET: rāʾâ (to see) in this context means direct experiential encounter, not physical sight. Job "sees" God in the way Moses saw God’s back (Exod 33:23) — as much as a mortal can bear.

42:6

The NET discusses the translation difficulties: "despise myself" may be "retract my words"; "repent" may be "am comforted." The Hebrew is deliberately ambiguous — the response to God cannot be captured in a single verb.

Clines Notes

clines
42:5

Clines: "now my eyes see you" is not visual perception but experiential knowledge. Job has encountered the living God, not merely the God of theology. This encounter is the book’s answer: not information but presence.

42:6

Clines: the verb niḥam may also mean "I am comforted" or "I change my mind" rather than "I repent." Some translations: "I relent concerning dust and ashes" — Job lets go of his mourning posture. Either way, the transformation is from demand to acceptance, from argument to awe.

Habel Notes

habel
42:1–6

Habel: the plaintiff withdraws his case. Not because God proved Job guilty — God never did. Not because the friends were right — God will rebuke them (v.7). Job withdraws because the encounter with God transcends the legal framework. The court dissolves into worship.

Verses 7–9 — God Rebukes the Friends: “You Have Not Spoken the Truth About Me”
789

Hebrew Word Study

נְכוֹנָה nək̅ōnâ — right / correct / truthful: "You have not spoken the truth (nək̅ōnâ) about me, as my servant Job has" (v.7). The most shocking sentence in the epilogue. God rebukes the friends — not Job. The man who accused God of injustice spoke truth about God. The men who defended God’s justice spoke falsely. How? Because honest protest is closer to the truth than dishonest defence.

Context

God addresses Eliphaz (and through him all three friends): "I am angry with you and your two friends, because you have not spoken the truth about me, as my servant Job has" (v.7). This is the book’s verdict. The friends’ theology was wrong not because retribution is always false but because they applied it mechanically, fabricated evidence, and defended God with lies. Job’s raw, anguished, sometimes excessive protest was closer to truth because it was honest. God prefers honest wrestling to dishonest flattery.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 51:6"You desire truth in the inward parts" — God values honesty over propriety.
  • Rev 3:15–16"Because you are lukewarm, I am about to spit you out" — God prefers hot or cold to lukewarm piety.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
42:7

"The LORD said to Eliphaz the Temanite: I am angry with you and your two friends, because you have not spoken the truth about me, as my servant Job has." The friends are rebuked; Job is vindicated. The retribution theology, applied mechanically, was false testimony about God.

42:8

"My servant Job will pray for you, and I will accept his prayer and not deal with you according to your folly." Job must intercede for the friends. The accused becomes the intercessor. The man they condemned now saves them from condemnation.

42:9

"The LORD accepted Job’s prayer." Job’s intercession is effective. He has become the mediator he longed for — between God and the friends. The irony is complete: Job prayed for an arbiter (9:33); Job becomes one.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
42:7

Calvin: God’s anger at the friends is not about their intentions but their theology. They meant well; they spoke falsely. Sincerity does not excuse inaccuracy when you claim to speak for God.

42:8

Calvin: Job’s intercession for the friends is a Christ-type: the righteous one prays for the guilty, and his prayer is accepted. The pattern of mediation that Job longed for, he now embodies.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
42:7

The NET: nək̅ōnâ means "what is correct, right, established." The friends’ theology was not established truth. Job’s protest — raw, sometimes excessive — was closer to the reality of God.

42:8

The NET: the sevenfold sacrifice (seven bulls and seven rams) is the most elaborate sacrifice specified for individuals in the OT, reflecting the severity of the friends’ offence.

Clines Notes

clines
42:7

Clines: "you have not spoken nək̅ōnâ about me" — the word means what is correct, established, firm. The friends’ theology was not established truth but wishful systematising. Job’s protest, for all its excess, was grounded in reality.

42:8

Clines: Job praying for his friends is the book’s most beautiful reversal. They came to comfort him and wounded him. He prays for them and saves them. The victim intercedes for his tormentors.

Habel Notes

habel
42:7–9

Habel: the verdict is rendered. The prosecution (friends) loses. The defendant (Job) is vindicated. But the vindication is not about Job’s innocence (which was established in ch 1–2) but about Job’s honesty. Honest complaint trumps false defence.

Verses 10–17 — Job Restored: Double Everything
1011121314151617

Hebrew Word Study

שְׁבוּת shəbūṭ — captivity / restoration / fortunes: "The LORD restored (shəbūṭ) Job’s fortunes after Job had prayed for his friends" (v.10). The restoration comes AFTER Job’s intercession, not after his repentance. The sequence matters: encounter with God (v.5) → repentance (v.6) → intercession for the friends (v.8) → restoration (v.10). Restoration is the fruit of the entire journey, not a reward for any single act.

מִשְׁנֶה mishneh — double / twice: "The LORD blessed the latter part of Job’s life more than the former part" (v.12). He receives double: 14,000 sheep (was 7,000), 6,000 camels (was 3,000), 1,000 yoke of oxen (was 500), 1,000 donkeys (was 500). But he receives the SAME number of children: seven sons and three daughters. Why not double? Because the first children are not lost — they are with God. Job has fourteen sons and six daughters, not seven and three.

Context

The prose epilogue restores what the prose prologue took away. But the restoration is not simple reversal. Job is not returned to his previous state; he has been transformed. He has seen God. He has prayed for his tormentors. He is not the same man who sat in ashes. The doubled wealth signifies divine generosity, not mere compensation. The equal number of children signifies that death is not loss — the first children live. Job lives 140 more years, sees four generations, and dies old and full of days.

Cross-Reference

  • Jas 5:11"You have heard of Job’s perseverance and have seen what the Lord finally brought about. The Lord is full of compassion and mercy" — the NT summary of Job’s story.
  • Isa 40:2"She has received from the LORD’s hand double for all her sins" — the same double-restoration theology.
  • Deut 21:17"He must acknowledge the firstborn son... by giving him a double share" — the firstborn’s double portion, applied to Job’s restored life.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
42:10

"After Job had prayed for his friends, the LORD restored his fortunes and gave him twice as much as he had before." The restoration follows intercession. Job receives back when he gives away.

42:11

"All his brothers and sisters and everyone who had known him before came and ate with him." The social restoration: the community that abandoned him (ch 19) returns. The alienation is reversed.

42:12–13

"The LORD blessed the latter part of Job’s life more than the former part." Double livestock. Same number of children. The children’s count is the subtlest theology in the epilogue.

42:14–15

"He named the first daughter Jemimah, the second Keziah, and the third Keren-Happuch. Nowhere in all the land were there found women as beautiful as Job’s daughters, and their father granted them an inheritance along with their brothers." The daughters are named (unusual), beautiful, and inherit alongside sons (radical for the period). Job’s restoration includes social justice.

42:16–17

"After this, Job lived a hundred and forty years; he saw his children and their children to the fourth generation. And so Job died, old and full of days." The patriarchal ending: a full life, many descendants, a good death. The Satan’s question is answered: Job feared God for nothing, and God gave him everything.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
42:10

Calvin: the restoration comes after intercession, not after repentance. This is crucial: Job’s suffering was not about his sin, and the restoration is not a reward for confessing sin. The entire cycle — test, endurance, encounter, intercession, restoration — is God’s design.

42:13

Calvin: the same number of children means the first ten are alive with God. Job’s "double" includes the resurrection of the dead. The patriarchal hope of reunion is embedded in the arithmetic.

42:15

Calvin: the daughters’ inheritance is Job’s final act of justice. Even in restoration, he extends righteousness to the vulnerable. The man has not changed in character; he has deepened in wisdom.

42:17

Calvin: "old and full of days" is the blessing the friends promised (5:26). It came true — but not through the path they prescribed. Restoration came through encounter with God, not through repentance from imaginary sin.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
42:10

The NET: shəbūṭ literally means "turning" — God turned Job’s situation. The same word describes the restoration of Israel from exile.

42:13

The NET: the number of children (7+3=10, not doubled to 20) is widely noted as theologically significant. The first children are not replaced but joined by new ones.

42:15

The NET: granting daughters an inheritance alongside sons was not legally required and was socially unusual. Job voluntarily extends justice beyond cultural norms.

42:17

The NET: "full of days" (səbaʿ yāmîm) is the ideal death in the OT: a long life fully lived, concluded in contentment. The phrase is used of Abraham (Gen 25:8), Isaac (Gen 35:29), and David (1 Chr 29:28).

Clines Notes

clines
42:12–13

Clines: the double livestock but same number of children is either a narrative oversight or the book’s most profound theological statement. If Job’s first ten children are with God, then Job has twenty children — ten in heaven, ten on earth. The first are not lost; they are ahead.

42:15

Clines: the daughters receiving inheritance alongside brothers is extraordinary for the ancient Near East. Job’s restoration includes a more just household. The man who was "eyes to the blind and feet to the lame" (29:15) now extends justice even within inheritance law.

42:17

Clines: "old and full of days" echoes the patriarchal language of Genesis (Gen 25:8; 35:29). Job is restored to the patriarchal ideal — the life the prologue described, now deepened by encounter with God.

Habel Notes

habel
42:10–17

Habel: the verdict is implemented. The plaintiff is restored, the prosecution rebuked, the case closed. But the restoration transcends legal categories. Job receives not justice (which would be merely getting back what was lost) but grace (double).

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Job
The man from Uz; blameless, upright, feared God
The entire book tested the Satan’s question: does Job fear God for nothing? The answer: yes. Job’s integrity survived the loss of everything. His theology grew from hearing to seeing. He interceded for those who tormented him. He died old and full of days.
