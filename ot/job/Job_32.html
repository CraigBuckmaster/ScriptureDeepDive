Job 32

Elihu Arrives

Verses 1–14 — “I Am Young and You Are Old”
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

אֲלִיהוּא ʾĕlîhûʾ — Elihu / my God is he: Elihu means "My God is He" — the most theocentric name in the book. He is young, angry at both Job and the friends, and has waited respectfully until the elders finished. He represents a fourth theological position: neither retribution (friends) nor protest (Job) but divine pedagogy — suffering as God’s teaching tool.

Context

Elihu appears without introduction in the prologue and disappears without mention in the epilogue. God never endorses or rebukes him. Scholars debate whether he is a later addition, an intentional bridge between the dialogue and the whirlwind, or both. His four speeches (ch 32–37) share some theology with God’s whirlwind speeches but lack their power.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Tim 4:12"Don’t let anyone look down on you because you are young" — the principle Elihu claims.
  • Eccl 12:12"Of making many books there is no end" — the weariness Elihu’s verbosity can provoke.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
32:1–5

The narrator: Job’s three friends stopped answering because Job was righteous in his own eyes. Then Elihu burned with anger — at Job for justifying himself rather than God, and at the friends for condemning Job without refuting him.

32:6–9

"I am young in years, and you are old; that is why I was fearful. I thought age should speak. But it is the spirit in a person, the breath of the Almighty, that gives them understanding." Elihu challenges the assumption that age equals wisdom.

32:11–14

"I waited while you spoke... but not one of you has proved Job wrong." Elihu claims he can do what the friends couldn’t: actually answer Job’s arguments.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
32:6–9

Calvin: Elihu’s point about age and wisdom is valid. The Spirit gives understanding regardless of age. But Elihu will need to demonstrate this with substance, not just assertion.

32:11–14

Calvin: "not one of you has proved Job wrong" is accurate. The friends failed to refute Job because Job’s evidence was stronger than their theology.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
32:2

The NET: Elihu son of Barakel the Buzite, of the family of Ram. His genealogy is given in unusual detail, suggesting he is a historical figure or is being presented as one.

32:8

The NET: the "spirit/breath of Shaddai" parallels the creation account where God breathes life into Adam. Elihu claims the same creative breath gives him insight.

Clines Notes

clines
32:1–5

Clines: Elihu’s anger at both parties is his distinctive position. The friends condemned without evidence; Job justified himself without acknowledging God’s right to test. Elihu thinks both sides are wrong.

32:8

Clines: "the breath of the Almighty gives understanding" — Elihu claims inspiration, not just experience. He appeals to the Spirit rather than tradition or observation.

Habel Notes

habel
32:1–14

Habel: Elihu is an uninvited advocate who enters the court after both prosecution and defence have rested. His role is ambiguous: friend of the court? New prosecutor? Bridge to the judge’s ruling?

Verses 15–22 — “I Must Speak or I Will Burst”
1516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

אֶפָּתֵחָה ʾefpāṭēḥâ — I will open: "I too will have my say; I too will tell what I know. For I am full of words, and the spirit within me compels me" (vv.17–18). Elihu’s self-introduction is enthusiastic to the point of comedy. He describes himself as a wineskin about to burst (v.19). The image is vivid and slightly ridiculous — deliberately so? Scholars disagree.

Context

Elihu promises impartiality: he will not flatter anyone or use titles (v.21–22). This is a young man’s boldness. Whether it is also wisdom remains to be seen. His speeches will contain genuine theological insights mixed with considerable verbosity.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 20:9"His word is in my heart like a fire, a fire shut up in my bones" — the same compulsion to speak that Elihu describes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
32:17–19

"I too will have my say. I am full of words; the spirit within me compels me. Inside I am like bottled-up wine, like new wineskins ready to burst." Elihu cannot contain himself. The energy is genuine, even if the self-awareness is limited.

32:21–22

"I will show no partiality. If I were to flatter, my Maker would soon take me away." Elihu pledges fairness and claims divine accountability for his words.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
32:18–19

Calvin: the compulsion to speak can be genuine prophetic impulse or mere youthful enthusiasm. Elihu may have both. His theology will be partly right and partly overconfident.

32:22

Calvin: the refusal to flatter is commendable but also self-serving. Elihu establishes his credentials before delivering his message.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
32:19

The NET: "new wineskins ready to burst" — new wine in new skins generates gas as it ferments. Without release, the skin ruptures. Elihu is fermenting with unspoken words.

32:22

The NET: the claim of divine accountability ("my Maker would take me away") elevates Elihu’s speech from advice to claimed prophecy.

Clines Notes

clines
32:18–19

Clines: the wineskin image is either passionate sincerity or pompous self-importance. Clines leans toward deliberate comedy: the narrator presents Elihu as a young man who thinks more highly of his own words than they deserve.

32:22

Clines: the promise not to flatter is followed by four chapters of unsolicited advice. The gap between promise and delivery is part of Elihu’s characterisation.

Habel Notes

habel
32:15–22

Habel: Elihu positions himself as an impartial voice. He is neither prosecution nor defence but claims to speak from the Spirit. His actual speeches will partially support both sides.
