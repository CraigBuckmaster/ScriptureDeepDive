Job 10

Job’s Lament: Why Did You Give Me Life?

Verses 1–7 — "Do Not Condemn Me — Tell Me Why"
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

רִיב rîb — contention / lawsuit / case: "I loathe my very life; therefore I will give free rein to my complaint (rîb)" (v.1–2). The word rîb is a technical legal term: a formal lawsuit or dispute. Job is not venting; he is filing a legal complaint against God.

Context

Job addresses God with increasing boldness. "Do not declare me guilty; tell me what charges you have against me" (v.2). He demands that God state the specific accusation. Then a piercing question: "Does it please you to oppress?" (v.3). Job implies God might enjoy causing pain — a charge he himself will later withdraw.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 139:13–16"You knit me together in my mother’s womb" — the creation theology Job uses, but with opposite emotion.
  • Isa 45:9"Does the clay say to the potter, What are you making?" — the question Job dares to ask.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1–2

"I will say to God: Do not declare me guilty — tell me what charges you have against me." Job demands due process. The accused has a right to know the charge.

10:3

"Does it please you to oppress, to spurn the work of your hands?" Job accuses God of taking pleasure in suffering. This is grief at its most raw.

10:4–7

"Do you have eyes of flesh? Do you see as a mortal sees?" Job asks whether God’s perspective is limited like a human’s. The implication: God should know Job is innocent because God sees everything.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:2

Calvin: the demand to know the charge is legitimate. Scripture itself teaches that God acts for reasons, not capriciously. Job’s error is not in asking but in assuming God owes him an answer.

10:3

Calvin: "does it please you to oppress?" goes too far. But Calvin notes that even this excess comes from a man who believes in God’s justice and cannot reconcile it with his experience.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:1

The NET notes the rîb is a formal lawsuit filed against God — the most adversarial legal term available.

10:3

The NET: "the work of your hands" reminds God that Job is God’s creation. To destroy your own creation is self-defeating.

Clines Notes

clines
10:2

Clines: "tell me what charges you have against me" is the language of a defendant demanding the indictment. Job’s legal strategy: force God to state the case or drop it.

10:3

Clines: "Does it please you to oppress?" pushes the accusation further than chapter 9. Job is now questioning God’s character, not just God’s actions.

Habel Notes

10:1–7

Verses 8–22 — "Your Hands Shaped Me — Yet You Destroy Me"
8910111213141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

עָשָה ... בִלְעָה āsāh ... billəʿāh — made ... swallowed up / destroyed: "Your hands shaped (āsāh) me and made me... yet you would destroy (billəʿāh) me" (v.8). The paradox at the heart of the lament: the Creator is destroying his own creation. The same hands that formed Job now crush him. This is the theology of Genesis 2 meeting the experience of Job 2.

Context

Job recounts his creation in tender terms — poured out like milk, curdled like cheese, clothed with skin and flesh, knit with bones and sinews, granted life and lovingkindness (vv.10–12). Then the turn: "But this is what you concealed in your heart; I know this was in your mind: if I sinned, you would be watching me" (vv.13–14). The creative love was a trap. God made Job beautiful only to destroy him.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 139:13–16The creation-of-the-individual theology — God forms the body in the womb.
  • Jer 18:1–10The potter and the clay — God’s sovereign right over what he makes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:8–9

"Your hands shaped me and made me. Will you now turn and destroy me? Remember that you moulded me like clay." Job appeals to God’s investment: you made me; don’t waste your own work.

10:10–12

"You poured me out like milk, curdled me like cheese, clothed me with skin and flesh, knit me together with bones and sinews. You gave me life and showed me kindness." The most beautiful creation theology in the dialogue.

10:13–17

"But this is what you concealed in your heart." Job accuses God of hidden malice: the kindness was a setup. God watched and waited for sin, and when it didn’t come, punished anyway.

10:18–22

"Why then did you bring me out of the womb?" Back to chapter 3’s theme: better never to have been born. The speech ends in darkness: "the land of gloom and utter darkness."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:10–12

Calvin: the creation imagery is among the most tender in Scripture. Job knows he is fearfully and wonderfully made. That knowledge makes his suffering worse, not better.

10:18–22

Calvin: the speech ends in near-despair but not in blasphemy. Job wishes he were dead but does not take his own life. Even at the bottom, a thread of faith holds.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:10–11

The NET discusses the milk/cheese/skin/sinew imagery as a poetic description of embryonic development — remarkably detailed for the ancient world.

10:21–22

The NET notes the piling up of darkness words — "gloom, utter darkness, deepest night, disorder" — creates an overwhelming sense of the grave as anti-creation.

Clines Notes

clines
10:8–12

Clines: the creation recollection is Job’s strongest argument. If God invested this much care in making him, destroying him is irrational. The Creator-becomes-Destroyer paradox is the book’s deepest theological problem.

10:18–22

Clines: the return to "why was I born?" closes the first cycle of Job’s speeches (ch 3 began the same way). The circle of despair is complete.

Habel Notes

habel
10:8–22

Habel: Job’s argument shifts from procedural (demand the charge) to substantive (God’s own actions are contradictory). The plaintiff presents evidence: God made him with care, then destroyed him without cause.
