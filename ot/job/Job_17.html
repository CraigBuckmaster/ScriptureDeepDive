Job 17

Job: My Days Are Over

Verses 1–16 — “My Spirit Is Broken”; Darkness as Companion
12345678910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

תִקְוָה tiqwâ — hope: "Where then is my hope (tiqwâ)? Who can see any hope for me?" (v.15). The word tiqwâ echoes Zophar’s promise of hope through repentance (11:18). Job takes the friends’ own word and declares it empty. The hope they offered has no substance because the premise (sin → repentance → restoration) is wrong.

Context

The shortest chapter in Job’s speeches and the darkest. Job’s spirit is broken, his days are cut short, mockery surrounds him (v.1–2). He asks God to post a pledge for him (v.3) since no human will. He describes the grave as “my father” and the worm as “my mother and sister” (v.14). Family has been replaced by death and decay.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 88:3–6"I am overwhelmed with troubles and my life draws near to death" — the closest Psalm to Job’s tone.
  • Ps 88:18"Darkness is my closest friend" — almost identical to Job’s closing sentiment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
17:1–2

"My spirit is broken, my days are cut short, the grave awaits me. Surely mockers surround me." The nadir. Job sees nothing ahead but death.

17:3

"Give me a pledge, O God. Who else will put up security for me?" Job asks God to be his guarantor — since no human will. The legal metaphor: he needs bail.

17:11–16

"My days have passed, my plans are shattered... if the only home I hope for is the grave, if I spread out my bed in the realm of darkness — if I say to corruption, ‘You are my father,’ and to the worm, ‘My mother’ or ‘My sister’ — where then is my hope?"

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:3

Calvin: asking God for a pledge shows that even at his lowest, Job still turns to God. He has no other recourse. This is faith in the form of desperation.

17:15–16

Calvin: “where then is my hope?” is a question, not a denial. Job asks because he cannot see hope, not because he has concluded it doesn’t exist. The question keeps the door open.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:3

The NET: "put up security" is a commercial/legal term — posting bail or a guarantee. Job needs someone to vouch for him before the divine court.

Clines Notes

clines
17:3

Clines: asking God for a pledge is paradoxical — Job asks his accuser to be his guarantor. But there is no one else. The friends refuse; humans can’t; only God can.

17:14–16

Clines: the corruption-as-family imagery is the chapter’s most disturbing move. The grave replaces community. Worms replace relatives. Darkness replaces home.

Habel Notes

habel
17:1–16

Habel: Job’s despair is the low point of the legal case. The plaintiff has exhausted his arguments and sees no path to a hearing. Hope descends with him to the dust.

Verses 10–16 — Death as Family; Where Is My Hope?

Hebrew Word Study

תִקְוָה tiqwâ — hope: "Where then is my hope (tiqwâ)? Who can see any hope for me?" (v.15). Job takes the friends’ own word and declares it empty.

Context

Job describes the grave as his new family: corruption is his father, the worm is his mother and sister (v.14). Every human relationship has been replaced by decay. Hope descends with him to the dust (v.16).
