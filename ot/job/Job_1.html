Job 1

The Test Begins

Verses 1–5 — Job Introduced: Blameless, Upright, Feared God
12345

Hebrew Word Study

תָּם tām — blameless / complete / whole: Job is tām — not sinless but whole, a man of integrity. The word means complete, sound, without hidden fracture. It is God’s own assessment (1:8), not Job’s self-description. The entire book tests whether this verdict will survive.

יָשָׁר yāshār — upright: Yāshār means straight, level, ethically aligned. Combined with tām, it describes a life oriented correctly in every dimension — complete in character and straight in conduct.

Historical Context

The land of Uz is likely in Edom or northern Arabia (cf. Lam 4:21; Jer 25:20). Job’s wealth is measured in livestock (7,000 sheep, 3,000 camels, 500 yoke of oxen, 500 donkeys) — patriarchal-era markers. He is "the greatest man among all the people of the East."

Context

The narrator establishes Job’s righteousness before the test begins. This is not a story about hidden sin exposed but about genuine righteousness tested. The reader knows what Job does not: his suffering has nothing to do with his behaviour.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezek 14:14Noah, Daniel, and Job — three legendary righteous men.
  • Gen 6:9Noah was "blameless" (tāmîm) — the same root word as Job’s tām.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

"Blameless and upright" — God’s verdict, confirmed twice (1:8, 2:3). Job’s integrity is the premise, not the question.

1:2–3

Seven sons, three daughters, 11,000 animals. The numbers are schematic — seven and three signal completeness. Job’s prosperity matches his character.

1:4–5

Job sacrifices for his children "just in case" they sinned. His piety is preventive, not reactive. He fears God on behalf of others.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

Calvin: the fourfold description (blameless, upright, feared God, shunned evil) leaves no gap for the friends’ later accusations. God himself will confirm it.

1:5

Calvin on the sacrifices: Job acts as priest for his family. This is patriarchal religion — the father mediates.

The NET observes 11,000 animals makes Job the wealthiest figure in the wisdom literature — deliberately superlative.

Clines Notes

clines
1:1

Clines notes tām is not a moral superlative but a wholeness term — Job is undivided, consistent. His outer life matches his inner life.

1:5

The burnt offerings "just in case" reveal Job’s theology: sin is possible even in celebration. Clines: this is scrupulous piety, not anxiety.

Habel Notes

habel
1:1–5

Habel reads the prologue as establishing Job’s legal standing. He is the blameless plaintiff — his case will be that he has done nothing to deserve what follows.

Verses 6–12 — The Heavenly Court: Satan’s Challenge
6789101112

Hebrew Word Study

הַשָּׂטָן haśśāṭān — the adversary / the accuser: Haśśāṭān — with the definite article. Not a proper name but a title: "the accuser." He is a member of the divine court whose function is prosecutorial. He challenges motives, not facts.

חִנָּם ḥinnām — for nothing / without cause / freely: "Does Job fear God for nothing (ḥinnām)?" The most important question in the book. It asks whether disinterested righteousness exists — whether anyone serves God without expecting reward.

Historical Context

The "sons of God" (bənê hā’ēlōhîm) presenting before the LORD resembles the divine council scene attested in Mesopotamian and Ugaritic literature. The Satan functions as a prosecuting attorney in the heavenly court.

Context

The reader sees what Job never sees: the backstage. The heavenly wager gives the reader information that no character in the story possesses. This dramatic irony is the engine of the entire book. Job’s friends will diagnose his suffering as punishment; the reader knows it is a test.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 22:19–22Micaiah sees the divine council — the same throne-room setting.
  • Zech 3:1–2The Satan accuses Joshua the high priest — the same prosecutorial role.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:6–7

The sons of God present themselves. The Satan comes among them. God initiates the conversation: "Have you considered my servant Job?" God puts Job forward.

1:8

"There is no one on earth like him." God’s assessment is superlative and unqualified.

1:9–10

"Does Job fear God for nothing?" The Satan’s argument: Job is righteous because it pays. Remove the payment, remove the righteousness.

1:12

"Everything he has is in your power, but on the man himself do not lay a finger." God sets limits. The test has boundaries.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:9

Calvin: the Satan’s question is the oldest temptation — to reduce faith to transaction. If God is good because he gives, what happens when he takes?

1:12

Calvin: God permits evil within fixed limits. The Satan can do nothing without permission, and nothing beyond the boundary set.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:6

The NET notes "sons of God" (bənê ēlōhîm) refers to angelic beings, members of the divine council (cf. Ps 89:6–7).

1:9

The NET translates ḥinnām as "for nothing" and notes it is the theological crux of the entire book.

Clines Notes

clines
1:9

Clines: the question is not about Job specifically but about the nature of piety itself. Is disinterested goodness possible? The book’s answer will be: yes, but barely.

1:12

God’s permission is not indifference. Clines reads the limit as evidence that God stakes his own reputation on Job’s response.

Habel Notes

habel
1:6–12

Habel: the heavenly court is a courtroom. The Satan is the prosecutor, God the judge, Job the defendant who doesn’t know he’s on trial. The legal drama begins before Job suffers.

Verses 13–22 — Four Messengers; Job Worships
13141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

נָתַן ... לָקָח nāṭan ... lāqāḥ — gave ... has taken away: "The LORD gave and the LORD has taken away; blessed be the name of the LORD." Job attributes both giving and taking to God. He does not mention the Satan. His theology is radically God-centred: there is no secondary cause worth naming.

Context

Four disasters in rapid succession: Sabeans take the oxen and donkeys, fire from heaven burns the sheep, Chaldeans take the camels, a wind kills the children. Each messenger arrives "while he was still speaking" — no recovery time. The architecture is devastating: livestock, livestock, livestock, children. The worst comes last.

Cross-Reference

  • Eccl 5:15"Naked a man comes from his mother’s womb, and as he comes, so he departs" — the same theology Job expresses.
  • 1 Tim 6:7"We brought nothing into the world, and we can take nothing out" — Paul’s echo of Job 1:21.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:13–19

Four messengers, four disasters. The pattern is literary: each announces total loss. "I am the only one who has escaped to tell you" — repeated four times. The lone survivor exists only to deliver the news.

1:20

Job tears his robe, shaves his head — the conventional mourning. Then he falls to the ground — and worships. The response is worship, not accusation.

1:21

"Naked I came from my mother’s womb, and naked I will depart." Job frames his loss within creation theology. Everything was gift; nothing was owed.

1:22

"In all this, Job did not sin by charging God with wrongdoing." The narrator’s verdict: Job passes the first test. The Satan’s hypothesis is wrong.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:21

Calvin: "Blessed be the name of the LORD" in the mouth of a man who has just lost everything is the highest form of worship. It costs nothing to bless God in prosperity; it costs everything in ruin.

1:22

Calvin: the narrator’s verdict is important because what follows will be more ambiguous. In chapter 1, Job’s response is beyond reproach.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:16

The NET notes "fire of God" could be lightning — natural disaster attributed to divine agency.

1:21

The NET observes the Hebrew is literally "the LORD gave, the LORD took; let the name of the LORD be blessed" — a chiastic structure centering on God’s name.

Clines Notes

clines
1:21

Clines: Job’s response is theologically correct but humanly extraordinary. The narrator tells us he did not sin — but does not tell us whether he was tempted.

1:22

The phrase "charge God with wrongdoing" (literally "give God unseemliness") is a legal term. Clines: Job does not file a complaint against God. Not yet.

Habel Notes

habel
1:20–22

Habel: Job’s first response is the model plaintiff — he absorbs the blow without counter-accusation. His legal restraint here will contrast with his later demand for a hearing.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Job
The greatest man in the East; blameless and upright
His integrity is God’s own assessment (1:8). His response to total loss — worship, not accusation — answers the Satan’s question: yes, someone can fear God for nothing.
The Satan
The accuser; member of the divine court
Not yet the devil of later theology but a prosecuting attorney. His question — "Does Job fear God for nothing?" — is the inciting premise of the entire book.
