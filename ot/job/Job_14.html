Job 14

Job on Human Mortality

Verses 1–12 — “At Least There Is Hope for a Tree”
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

יְלוּד אִשָּׁה yəlūd ʾîshāh — born of woman: "Mortals, born of woman, are of few days and full of trouble" (v.1). The universalising opening. Job moves from his specific case to the human condition. Everyone born of woman shares this fate: brief life, abundant trouble. The phrase “born of woman” emphasises human origin and fragility.

Context

Job’s meditation on mortality is the first cycle’s climax. He has argued with all three friends; now he steps back and considers the human condition itself. The central image: a tree can be cut down and sprout again (vv.7–9), but a person dies and does not rise (v.12). Nature has what humans lack: renewal.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 90:5–6"You sweep people away... they are like new grass" — the same brevity and fragility.
  • 1 Pet 1:24"All people are like grass" — Peter quoting Isaiah, echoing Job’s theme.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:1–2

"Mortals, born of woman, are of few days and full of trouble. They spring up like flowers and wither away." Job’s most universal statement. This is not complaint but observation.

14:5

"A person’s days are determined; you have decreed the number of their months." God has set the boundary. The sovereignty theme: life’s length is not accidental.

14:7–9

"At least there is hope for a tree: if it is cut down, it will sprout again... at the scent of water it will bud." The tree metaphor: vegetation has resurrection capacity that humans lack.

14:10–12

"But a man dies and is laid low... so he lies down and does not rise." The contrast is devastating: trees revive, humans don’t. Nature mocks human mortality.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:1

Calvin: "born of woman" emphasises not gender but origin — from weakness, through weakness, into weakness. The human condition is inherently fragile.

14:7–9

Calvin: the tree image is not idle poetry. Job is asking a theological question: why does God give vegetation what he denies to humans? Is the stump more favoured than the soul?

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:1

The NET: “born of woman” is used in the OT for emphasising human lowliness (cf. Matt 11:11).

14:7

The NET: the tree’s ability to sprout from a stump is well attested in olive and fig trees in the ANE — Job draws on observable nature.

Clines Notes

clines
14:7–12

Clines: the tree-vs-human contrast is the chapter’s structural centre. It is also an implicit accusation: God gave trees the ability to regenerate but denied it to the creatures made in his image.

14:12

Clines: “till the heavens are no more, people will not awake.” Job does not have a developed resurrection theology. He describes death as final. The longing in vv.13–17 is all the more poignant because he does not believe it will happen.

Habel Notes

habel
14:1–12

Habel: Job rests his case for the first cycle with a meditation on mortality. The argument: if human life is this short and this fragile, why does God waste it on suffering? The legal question becomes existential.

Verses 13–22 — The First Glimmer of Resurrection Hope
13141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

חָלִיפָתִי ḥalîfātî — my renewal / my relief / my replacement: "All the days of my hard service I will wait for my renewal (ḥalîfātî) to come" (v.14). The word ḥalîfâ means replacement, relief, or renewal — like a soldier’s tour of duty ending when the relief shift arrives. Job imagines waiting in Sheol for God to remember him and summon him back.

Context

After declaring death final (v.12), Job suddenly imagines an alternative: “If only you would hide me in the grave... and then remember me!” (v.13). This is not doctrine but longing. Job does not believe in resurrection; he wishes for it. The conditional “if” (lū) is a wish contrary to fact. But the wish itself is the seed. The trajectory: this longing will grow into “I know that my redeemer lives” (19:25).

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 16:10"You will not abandon me to the realm of the dead" — David’s hope, more developed than Job’s.
  • 1 Cor 15:42–44"The body that is sown is perishable, it is raised imperishable" — the full answer to Job’s longing, centuries later.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:13

"If only you would hide me in the grave and conceal me till your anger has passed! If only you would set me a time and then remember me!" The wish for temporary death — hidden until the anger passes, then summoned back.

14:14–15

"If someone dies, will they live again? All the days of my hard service I will wait for my renewal to come. You will call and I will answer you; you will long for the creature your hands have made." The most hopeful verse in the first cycle. Job imagines God missing him, wanting him back.

14:16–17

"Surely then you will count my steps but not keep track of my sin. My offences will be sealed up in a bag; you will cover over my sin." Job imagines a post-resurrection forgiveness — sins sealed away, covered, forgotten.

14:18–22

"But as a mountain erodes and crumbles... so you destroy a person’s hope." The vision collapses. The wish dissolves back into despair. The first cycle ends in darkness.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:14

Calvin: "will they live again?" is the question that Job cannot answer and cannot stop asking. The entire OT strains toward resurrection without fully grasping it. Job’s longing is part of that trajectory.

14:18–22

Calvin: the collapse of hope at the chapter’s end is honest. Job does not pretend to believe what he cannot see. His faith is a thread, not a rope. But the thread holds.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:13

The NET: the particle lū introduces a wish known to be impossible — "if only" with the knowledge that it will not happen.

14:14

The NET notes ḥalîfâ is a military term — the relief detachment that replaces exhausted soldiers. Job imagines death as a tour of duty with an end.

Clines Notes

clines
14:13–15

Clines: the wish-for-resurrection passage is the emotional peak of the first cycle. Job does not believe it will happen (the conditional is contrary to fact) but he longs for it with his entire being. The longing itself is theologically significant.

14:14

Clines: “you will call and I will answer” reverses 13:22 where Job asked God to call. Here Job imagines the call coming from God’s initiative. The relationship would be restored from God’s side.

14:18–22

Clines: the chapter’s ending is devastating. The hope of vv.13–15 is withdrawn. Water wears away stone; God destroys human hope. The first cycle ends where it began: in darkness.

Habel Notes

habel
14:13–22

Habel: the resurrection wish is the first appeal for a post-mortem hearing. If Job cannot get justice in this life, perhaps death is not the end. The legal metaphor extends beyond the grave — at least in imagination.
