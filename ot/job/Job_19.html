Job 19

Job: “I Know That My Redeemer Lives”

Verses 1–20 — The Catalogue of Alienation
1234567891011121314151617181920

Hebrew Word Study

חָנַנִי ḥānanî — be gracious to me / have pity: "Have pity (ḥānanî) on me, my friends, have pity, for the hand of God has struck me" (v.21). The verb is from the root ḥānan — to be gracious, to show mercy. Job begs for what the friends refuse to give: compassion without diagnosis.

Context

Before the famous declaration, Job catalogues his total alienation. God has wronged him (v.6), stripped his honour (v.9), torn down his hope like a tree (v.10). Then the social collapse: relatives avoid him, friends forget, servants ignore, wife finds his breath repulsive, children despise, intimate friends turn against him (vv.13–19). He is “nothing but skin and bones” (v.20). This is the lowest point before the highest moment.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 88:8"You have taken from me friend and neighbour — darkness is my closest friend" — the closest Psalm parallel to Job’s alienation.
  • Isa 53:3"Despised and rejected by mankind" — the Suffering Servant who experiences what Job describes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
19:6–7

"Know that God has wronged me and drawn his net around me. Though I cry, ‘Violence!’ I get no response; though I call for help, there is no justice." Job’s boldest accusation: God has wronged him.

19:8–12

God has blocked Job’s path, stripped his honour, uprooted his hope, kindled his anger, sent troops against him. The siege imagery: Job is a city under divine assault.

19:13–19

The alienation catalogue: estranged from brothers, acquaintances, relatives, guests, servants, wife, children, intimate friends. Every human relationship severed.

19:20

"I am nothing but skin and bones." The famous phrase. Job is physically and socially reduced to nothing. This is the nadir from which the next verse soars.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
19:6

Calvin: “God has wronged me” is Job’s most dangerous statement. Calvin: it is technically wrong (God cannot do wrong) but experientially understandable. Job describes what he feels, not what he knows.

19:20

Calvin: "skin and bones" — the body itself testifies to the severity of the test. God’s servant is reduced to a skeleton.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
19:6

The NET: the verb "wronged" (ʿāwat) means to twist, distort, pervert. Job accuses God of perverting justice — the same charge Bildad denied (8:3).

19:20

The NET: "escaped with only the skin of my teeth" (v.20b) — the origin of the English idiom. Teeth have no skin; Job has escaped with nothing.

Clines Notes

clines
19:6

Clines: “God has wronged me” is Job’s most direct legal accusation. Not “God seems unjust” but “God has committed injustice against me.” The plaintiff names the crime.

19:13–20

Clines: the alienation catalogue is structured in concentric circles: family → friends → household → wife → children. Each circle closer, each rejection more devastating.

Habel Notes

habel
19:1–20

Habel: Job presents his final evidence of injury. The catalogue of alienation establishes the full extent of damages. The plaintiff has lost everything — property, health, family, reputation, community. The case for vindication is at maximum strength.

Verses 21–29 — “I Know That My Redeemer Lives”
212223242526272829

Hebrew Word Study

גֹאֲלִי gōʾălî — my redeemer / my kinsman-redeemer: "I know that my Redeemer (gōʾălî) lives" (v.25). The gōʾél is the kinsman-redeemer — the nearest relative who has the legal duty to vindicate, rescue, or buy back. In Ruth, Boaz is the gōʾél. In Exodus, God himself is Israel’s gōʾél. Job claims a redeemer who is alive, who will stand on the earth, and who will vindicate him. This is the book’s christological climax.

חַי ḥay — living / alive: "My redeemer lives (ḥay)" — the word is emphatic. Not “I hope” or “I wish” but “I know” (yādaʿtî). And the redeemer is not dead, not absent, not hypothetical. He lives. The certitude is staggering after 18 chapters of darkness.

Context

The theological climax of the book. After total alienation (vv.1–20), Job makes the most audacious faith statement in the OT. He knows his redeemer lives. He knows the redeemer will stand on the earth. He knows that after his skin has been destroyed, yet in his flesh he will see God (v.26). The trajectory is complete: arbiter (9:33) → witness (16:19) → redeemer (19:25). Job’s faith has grown through suffering, not despite it.

Cross-Reference

  • Ruth 3:9"I am your servant Ruth... spread the corner of your garment over me, since you are a guardian-redeemer" — the gōʾél in action.
  • Isa 44:6"I am the first and the last; apart from me there is no God... Israel’s Redeemer" — God as gōʾél.
  • 1 Cor 15:51–53"We will all be changed... the dead will be raised imperishable" — the resurrection Job could only glimpse.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
19:21

"Have pity on me, my friends, for the hand of God has struck me." The last appeal to the friends. They will not respond.

19:23–24

"Oh, that my words were recorded, that they were written on a scroll, that they were inscribed with an iron tool on lead, or engraved in rock forever!" Job wants his case preserved for posterity. The irony: his words ARE recorded — in Scripture.

19:25

"I know that my Redeemer lives, and that in the end he will stand on the earth." The pinnacle. Three certainties: (1) the redeemer exists, (2) the redeemer is alive, (3) the redeemer will act.

19:26–27

"And after my skin has been destroyed, yet in my flesh I will see God; I myself will see him with my own eyes — I, and not another. How my heart yearns within me!" Job expects to see God personally, bodily, with his own eyes. This is either resurrection hope or post-mortem vision — either way, it transcends everything he has said before.

19:28–29

Job warns the friends: if you persecute the innocent, judgment awaits YOU. The tables will turn.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
19:25

Calvin: “I know that my Redeemer lives” is the gospel in the OT. Job, in the extremity of suffering, grasps the truth that will take centuries to fully reveal: there is a living redeemer who will vindicate the innocent.

19:26–27

Calvin: "in my flesh I will see God" points unmistakably to bodily resurrection. Job, without access to the NT, anticipates what Paul will teach in 1 Corinthians 15. The Spirit of prophecy speaks through Job’s suffering.

19:27

Calvin: “I myself will see him with my own eyes — I, and not another.” The personal pronoun is emphatic three times. Not a proxy, not a representative — Job himself. This is personal, embodied hope.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
19:25

The NET: gōʾél is the technical term for the kinsman-redeemer — the nearest relative obligated to vindicate, ransom, or avenge. In Lev 25 the gōʾél redeems property; in Num 35 the gōʾél avenges blood; in Ruth the gōʾél redeems family.

19:26

The NET discusses both readings at length. The Hebrew is genuinely ambiguous. The theological weight does not depend on the resolution: Job expects to see God.

Clines Notes

clines
19:25

Clines: the identity of the gōʾél is debated. Some say it is God himself (paradoxically: God vindicates Job against God). Some say it is a heavenly being distinct from God. Clines: the ambiguity may be deliberate — Job is reaching toward something he cannot fully name.

19:26

Clines: “in my flesh I will see God” is the most debated phrase in the book. “In my flesh” (Hebrew ambiguous: could mean “from my flesh” or “apart from my flesh”). If “in my flesh”: bodily resurrection. If “apart from my flesh”: post-mortem spiritual vision. Either way, Job expects to encounter God personally after death.

Habel Notes

habel
19:25–27

Habel: the gōʾél declaration is Job’s appeal to the highest court. When human courts fail, when the judge is also the accused, the kinsman-redeemer steps in. Job trusts that the system has a fail-safe: a redeemer who will vindicate him in the end.
