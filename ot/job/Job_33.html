Job 33

Elihu’s First Speech: God Speaks Through Suffering

Verses 1–18 — “God Speaks Through Dreams and Pain”
123456789101112131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

חֲלוֹם ḥălōm — dream: "In a dream (ḥălōm), in a vision of the night, when deep sleep falls on people... God may speak" (vv.15–16). Elihu introduces the theology of divine communication through suffering. God speaks to humans in two ways: dreams (vv.15–18) and pain (vv.19–22). Suffering is not punishment but communication — God’s alternative language when words are ignored.

Context

Elihu begins respectfully: he is made of clay like Job (v.6), he will not overwhelm him (v.7). He quotes Job’s own words back to him: "I am pure, I have done no wrong" (v.9). Then his counter-argument: God speaks in ways humans don’t perceive (v.14). Dreams and pain are divine messages. This is neither retribution theology (the friends) nor accusation of divine injustice (Job) but a third option: suffering as pedagogy.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 20:3–7God warns Abimelech in a dream — the dream as divine communication.
  • Heb 12:5–11"God disciplines those he loves" — the NT development of Elihu’s discipline theology.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
33:6–7

"I am the same as you in God’s sight; I too am a piece of clay." Elihu establishes solidarity. He is not speaking from superiority but from shared humanity.

33:8–11

Elihu quotes Job: "I am pure, without transgression; I am clean and free from guilt. Yet God has found fault with me." Elihu accurately summarises Job’s position.

33:14–18

"God does speak — now one way, now another — though no one perceives it. In a dream, in a vision of the night... he may speak in their ears and terrify them with warnings, to turn them from wrongdoing and keep them from pride." Suffering as communication: God’s pedagogy.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
33:14

Calvin: "God speaks — now one way, now another" is Elihu’s best insight. God is not silent in Job’s suffering; he is speaking in a language Job hasn’t recognised.

33:17–18

Calvin: "to turn them from wrongdoing and keep them from pride" — suffering as preventive, not punitive. This is closer to the truth than the friends’ theology.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
33:14

The NET: "though no one perceives it" — the problem is not divine silence but human deafness. God speaks; we don’t hear.

33:15–16

The NET: the dream as divine revelation is well attested in ANE culture and throughout the OT (Gen 28, 37, 40–41; Dan 2, 7).

Clines Notes

clines
33:9–11

Clines: Elihu quotes Job accurately, which is more than the friends did. He engages the actual argument rather than a caricature.

33:14–18

Clines: Elihu’s contribution is the category of divine communication. The friends said: suffering = punishment. Job said: suffering = injustice. Elihu says: suffering = message. This is a genuine theological advance in the dialogue.

Habel Notes

habel
33:1–18

Habel: Elihu introduces a new legal argument: God communicates through non-verbal means. The judge speaks not only through verdicts but through circumstances. Suffering is a form of testimony.

Verses 19–33 — “God Does All These Things to Turn Back from the Pit”
192021222324252627282930313233

Hebrew Word Study

מֵלִיץ mēlîṣ — mediator / interpreter / angel: "If there is an angel as his mediator (mēlîṣ), one out of a thousand, to vouch for a person’s uprightness" (v.23). Elihu describes a mediating angel — one who intercedes, translates suffering into meaning, and tells God: "Spare them from going down to the pit; I have found a ransom." This echoes Job’s cry for an arbiter (9:33), witness (16:19), and redeemer (19:25).

Context

Elihu describes the second form of divine speech: physical suffering (vv.19–22). Pain on the sickbed, bones barely visible, appetite gone, flesh wasting. Then the rescue: a mediating angel finds a ransom, and the person is restored (vv.23–26). The restoration includes public testimony: "I have sinned... but God redeemed me from the pit, and I shall live to enjoy the light" (vv.27–28). Elihu’s theology is that the cycle is: sin or danger → suffering as warning → repentance → rescue → testimony.

Cross-Reference

  • Job 9:33"If only there were an arbiter" — Job’s first cry for mediation, which Elihu now develops.
  • 1 Tim 2:5–6"One mediator between God and mankind, the man Christ Jesus, who gave himself as a ransom" — the NT fulfilment of Elihu’s mediating angel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
33:19–22

"Or someone may be chastened on a bed of pain... their bones barely visible... their soul draws near to the pit." Physical suffering as the second form of divine communication.

33:23–24

"If there is an angel as his mediator, one out of a thousand, to vouch for a person’s uprightness, and he is gracious and says: Spare them from the pit; I have found a ransom." The mediating angel finds a ransom — the key theological concept.

33:26–28

"That person prays to God and finds favour; they see God’s face and shout for joy. They say: I have sinned, but God redeemed me from the pit." The restored person testifies publicly. Suffering’s purpose was to produce this testimony.

33:29–30

"God does all these things to a person — twice, even three times — to turn them back from the pit, that the light of life may shine on them." Elihu’s conclusion: suffering is repeated divine pedagogy aimed at restoration, not destruction.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
33:23–24

Calvin: the mediating angel who finds a ransom points beyond Elihu’s understanding to Christ. "I have found a ransom" — the ransom is ultimately the cross.

33:29–30

Calvin: God’s repeated efforts to reach humans through suffering demonstrate not cruelty but persistence. The pedagogy requires the student to eventually listen.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
33:23

The NET: mēlîṣ means interpreter or mediator. The angel translates between God and human, making God’s purpose in suffering comprehensible.

33:24

The NET: "ransom" (kōfer) is the price paid for release. The same term is used for the atonement money in Exodus 30:12.

Clines Notes

clines
33:23–24

Clines: the "one out of a thousand" mediating angel is Elihu’s most important theological contribution. He gives a partial answer to Job’s mediator cry — there IS someone who intercedes. But the ransom concept is left undeveloped.

33:29–30

Clines: "twice, even three times" — God is patient and persistent. The pedagogy is not one-time but repeated. God keeps trying to get through.

Habel Notes

habel
33:19–33

Habel: Elihu offers a partial resolution to Job’s legal problem. If there is a mediating angel, the gap between God and human that Job identified (9:33) has a solution — at least in principle.
