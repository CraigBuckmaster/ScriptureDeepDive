Job 8

Bildad’s First Speech: The Tradition of the Fathers

Verses 1–10 — "Does God Pervert Justice?"
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

מִשְׁפָּט mishpāṭ — justice / judgment / right: "Does God pervert justice (mishpāṭ)?" (v.3). Bildad’s opening is a question he considers absurd. Of course God doesn’t pervert justice. Therefore Job’s suffering must be just. The logic is sound; the premise (that all suffering is judicial) is wrong.

Context

Bildad is blunter than Eliphaz. Where Eliphaz opened with compliments, Bildad opens with "How long will you say such things?" (v.2). His theology is simpler: if your children died, they must have sinned (v.4). If you are pure, God will restore you (v.6). The if-then logic is merciless when applied to specific tragedies.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 32:4"He is the Rock, his works are perfect, and all his ways are just" — the Deuteronomic foundation Bildad builds on.
  • Gen 18:25"Will not the Judge of all the earth do right?" — Abraham’s question, which Bildad assumes has only one answer.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:2

"How long will you say such things?" Bildad is impatient. Job’s speech offended his theology.

8:3–4

"Does God pervert justice? Does the Almighty pervert what is right? When your children sinned against him, he gave them over to the penalty of their sin." Bildad states the retribution principle in its harshest form: your children’s deaths were punishment.

8:5–6

"If you will seek God earnestly... if you are pure and upright, even now he will rouse himself on your behalf." The conditional: if you’re righteous, restoration follows. The implication: if restoration hasn’t come, you’re not righteous.

8:8–10

"Ask the former generation... will they not instruct you?" Bildad appeals to tradition. What the fathers taught must be true. This is theology by inheritance, not investigation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:3

Calvin: the question "Does God pervert justice?" is valid. God does not pervert justice. But Bildad’s error is assuming that all suffering is judicial. Some suffering is testative, not punitive.

8:4

Calvin: attributing the children’s deaths to their sin is cruel speculation. Bildad states as fact what he cannot know.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:3

The NET notes mishpāṭ is a legal term — "Does God bend the ruling?" Bildad frames the question as a legal impossibility.

8:4

The NET: "he gave them over to the penalty of their sin" is literally "he sent them into the hand of their transgression." The Hebrew is chillingly impersonal.

Clines Notes

clines
8:4

Clines: "When your children sinned" is the cruelest line in the dialogue. Bildad explains the deaths of Job’s children as judicial punishment — to their father’s face. The theology is monstrous in its application.

8:8–10

Clines: Bildad’s appeal to tradition is his signature move. He doesn’t argue from experience (Eliphaz) or from logic (Zophar) but from ancestral authority.

Habel Notes

habel
8:1–10

Habel: Bildad is the second expert witness. His testimony: traditional wisdom unanimously supports the retribution principle. The fathers confirm it. Departure from tradition is folly.

Verses 11–22 — "God Does Not Reject the Blameless"
111213141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

תָּם tām — blameless / complete: "Surely God does not reject the blameless (tām)" (v.20). The irony: Bildad uses the very word God used to describe Job (1:8; 2:3). If Bildad is right, God should not have rejected Job. But God permitted Job’s suffering. The theology contradicts the narrative the reader already knows.

Context

Bildad uses plant imagery: papyrus without water withers (vv.11–13), a spider’s web provides no support (v.14), a well-watered plant can still be uprooted (vv.16–19). The images all point to the same lesson: apparent prosperity without genuine righteousness collapses. His conclusion: God doesn’t reject the blameless (v.20) — which should be good news for Job, except Bildad implies Job is not actually blameless.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 1:3–4"Like a tree planted by streams of water... not so the wicked" — the same plant-and-prosperity theology.
  • Matt 7:24–27The wise and foolish builders — Jesus’s version of foundations that hold or collapse.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:11–15

Papyrus needs water; godless hope needs God. The metaphor: those who forget God have no root. Their confidence is a spider’s web.

8:20–22

"Surely God does not reject the blameless." Bildad’s conclusion is theologically correct in the abstract but wrong in application. God has not rejected Job — but Bildad cannot see that because Job is suffering.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:20

Calvin: "God does not reject the blameless" is true. But Bildad assumes that suffering = rejection. It doesn’t. God tests the blameless precisely because they are blameless (1:8).

8:22

Calvin: the threat at the end reveals Bildad’s real message: repent or be destroyed. This is pastorally bankrupt.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:14

The NET: "his confidence is a spider’s web" — fragile, easily destroyed. The image is of false security that cannot bear weight.

8:20

The NET notes tām here echoes 1:1 and 2:3 — the same word, creating dramatic irony the reader perceives but Bildad does not.

Clines Notes

clines
8:20

Clines: the word tām is devastating here. Bildad uses it meaning "if you are blameless, God won’t reject you." The reader knows God himself called Job tām. Bildad’s theology condemns God’s own assessment.

8:22

Clines: "The tent of the wicked will be no more." Bildad ends with a threat disguised as theology. The implication: if your tent is destroyed, you were wicked.

Habel Notes

habel
8:11–22

Habel: Bildad’s testimony rests on traditional evidence — proverbs, plant metaphors, inherited wisdom. The cross-examination in chapters 9–10 will dismantle it.
