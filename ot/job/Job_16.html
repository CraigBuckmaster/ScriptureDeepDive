Job 16

Job Replies: My Witness Is in Heaven

Verses 1–17 — “Miserable Comforters”; God’s Assault
1234567891011121314151617

Hebrew Word Study

מְנַחֲמֵי עָמָל mənaḥămê ʿāmāl — miserable comforters / comforters of trouble: "Miserable comforters (mənaḥămê ʿāmāl), all of you!" (v.2). The friends came to comfort (2:11). Job now diagnoses their comfort as additional misery. The mənaḥēm (“comforter”) has become ʿāmāl (“trouble”). The healers are the wound.

Context

Job’s response is his most violent description of God’s attack. God has “torn me in his anger” (v.9), “shattered me” (v.12), “pierced my kidneys” (v.13), “broken through me like a warrior” (v.14). The imagery is of divine warfare: God is not a passive sovereign permitting suffering but an active combatant assaulting Job.

Cross-Reference

  • Lam 3:1–6"I am the man who has seen affliction by the rod of his wrath" — Jeremiah’s parallel experience of divine assault.
  • Ps 22:1"My God, why have you forsaken me?" — the cry Jesus quotes on the cross, which echoes Job’s sense of abandonment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:2

"Miserable comforters, all of you!" Job returns the friends’ own label: they are the opposite of what they claim to be.

16:7–14

God as assailant: tears, gnashes teeth, sharpens eyes, slaps cheek, turns over to the ungodly, shatters, seizes by the neck, sets up as target, pierces kidneys, pours out gall, breaks through like a warrior. The most violent theophany-in-reverse in Scripture.

16:15–17

"I have sewed sackcloth over my skin... my face is red with weeping. Yet my hands have been free of violence and my prayer is pure." Job maintains innocence even while describing God’s assault.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:9–14

Calvin: Job’s description of God as attacker is painful but honest. He describes what he experiences, not what he believes God should be. The gap between his theology (God is good) and his experience (God attacks) is the book’s central tension.

16:17

Calvin: the maintenance of innocence amidst suffering is Job’s most consistent theme. He never claims to be sinless; he claims to be innocent of the specific sin the friends imply.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:9

The NET: “torn me in his anger” uses the verb for a predator tearing prey. God is cast as a wild animal.

16:12–13

The NET: "seized me by the neck, crushed me, set me up as his target, his archers surround me, he pierces my kidneys." Compound military imagery — God as general commanding an assault.

Clines Notes

clines
16:7–14

Clines: the attack sequence is deliberately escalating. Each image is worse: torn → shattered → seized → pierced → broken through. Job describes God as an enemy warrior, not a judge.

16:17

Clines: “my hands are free of violence and my prayer is pure” is Job’s oath of innocence within the speech. He is not in denial — he is on the record.

Habel Notes

habel
16:1–17

Habel: Job describes the attack as evidence of judicial misconduct. The judge has become the assailant. The trial has degenerated into violence. Habel: this is Job’s strongest argument that the legal system has failed.

Verses 18–22 — “My Witness Is in Heaven”
1819202122

Hebrew Word Study

עֵדִי ʿêdî — my witness: "Even now my witness (ʿêdî) is in heaven; my advocate is on high" (v.19). A dramatic escalation from 9:33 (arbiter). Job now believes he has a witness who will testify on his behalf in the heavenly court. Who is this witness? Not the friends. Not the Satan. The text does not say. But the trajectory is unmistakable: arbiter (9:33) → witness (16:19) → redeemer (19:25).

Context

Job cries out to the earth not to cover his blood (v.18) — an allusion to Abel’s blood crying from the ground (Gen 4:10). He then makes the astonishing claim: someone in heaven will testify for him. This is the book’s christological trajectory. Job needs someone who is both divine (in heaven) and sympathetic (his advocate). The NT identifies this as Christ (1 John 2:1).

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 4:10"Your brother’s blood cries out to me from the ground" — the Abel allusion: innocent blood demands hearing.
  • 1 John 2:1"We have an advocate with the Father — Jesus Christ, the Righteous One" — the NT fulfilment of Job’s witness/advocate.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:18

"Earth, do not cover my blood; may my cry never be laid to rest!" Like Abel’s blood, Job’s suffering cries out for justice. He appeals to creation itself as witness.

16:19

"Even now my witness is in heaven; my advocate is on high." The theological leap: from no arbiter (9:33) to a witness already present in the heavenly court. Job’s faith grows through suffering.

16:20–21

"My intercessor is my friend as my eyes pour out tears to God. On behalf of a man he pleads with God as one pleads for a friend." Job imagines his heavenly witness interceding for him. The friend the earthly friends failed to be exists in heaven.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:19

Calvin: the witness in heaven is, ultimately, Christ. Job did not know this, but the Spirit who inspired his words was pointing forward to the only mediator between God and man (1 Tim 2:5).

16:21

Calvin: the vision of someone pleading with God on behalf of a human is the definition of intercession. Hebrews 7:25: Christ "always lives to intercede for them."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:18

The NET connects the blood-not-covered motif to Gen 4:10 and Isa 26:21 — innocent blood that demands justice.

16:19

The NET: ʿêdî (my witness) and mêlîṣ (my advocate) are parallel legal terms. Someone in the divine court knows Job’s case.

Clines Notes

clines
16:19

Clines: the identity of the witness is debated. Some say it is God testifying against God (Job’s good God vs the God who attacks). Clines: the ambiguity is deliberate. Job grasps at something he cannot fully articulate.

16:21

Clines: “one pleads for a friend” — the human legal metaphor applied to heaven. Job wants what he had with his friends (advocacy) but from a competent source.

Habel Notes

habel
16:18–22

Habel: the witness in heaven is the pivotal development in Job’s legal strategy. He has moved from “I need an arbiter” to “I have a witness.” The case is no longer hopeless; there is an advocate in the court.
