Job 6

Job Replies to Eliphaz: My Complaint Is Just

Verses 1–13 — "If Only My Anguish Could Be Weighed"
12345678910111213

Hebrew Word Study

כַעַס kaʿas — vexation / anguish / grief: "If only my vexation (kaʿas) could be weighed and my misery placed on the scales" (v.2). Job demands that his pain be measured objectively. His complaint is not self-pity but a claim that his anguish is proportionate to his outcry. He is not overreacting.

חִצֵּי שַׁדַּי ḥiṣṣê shadday — arrows of Shaddai: "The arrows of the Almighty (Shaddai) are in me" (v.4). Job names God as the one attacking him. He uses the divine warrior image: Shaddai is an archer, and Job is the target. This is not the Satan’s work in Job’s understanding — it is God’s.

Context

Job’s first reply to Eliphaz. He does not engage the retribution principle directly but makes an emotional case: my suffering is heavier than sand (v.3), God’s arrows are in me (v.4), even animals don’t cry out without cause (v.5). The argument: my complaint is proportionate to my pain.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 38:2"Your arrows have pierced me" — the same divine-archer image.
  • Lam 3:12–13"He drew his bow and made me the target for his arrows" — Jeremiah’s parallel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:2–3

"If only my anguish could be weighed." Job wants objective measurement. His pain, he claims, would outweigh the sand of the seas.

6:4

"The arrows of the Almighty are in me." Job identifies God as his attacker. He has no concept of the Satan’s role — he sees only God.

6:8–9

"If only God would be willing to crush me, to let loose his hand and cut me off!" Job longs for death as mercy. He wants God to finish the job.

6:10

"Then I would still have this consolation — that I had not denied the words of the Holy One." Even wishing for death, Job’s integrity holds. He has not cursed God.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:4

Calvin: Job attributes his suffering to God, not to the Satan. This is theologically correct in one sense — God permitted it — but Job’s mistake is assuming God is hostile rather than testing.

6:10

Calvin: even in despair, Job clings to one thing: he has not denied God. This is the thread that holds through the entire book.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:4

The NET notes "arrows of Shaddai" portrays God as a warrior. The poison imagery suggests the arrows are dipped — the suffering intensifies over time.

6:5

The NET: "Does a wild donkey bray when it has grass?" Animals don’t cry without cause. Neither does Job. His outcry is warranted.

Clines Notes

clines
6:2–4

Clines: the scales image is legal — Job wants his case weighed by an impartial standard. If his anguish were measured, the court would rule his complaint justified.

6:10

Clines: this is the crux of Job’s self-defence. Even at his lowest, he has not "denied the words of the Holy One." The Satan’s prediction has failed.

Habel Notes

habel
6:1–13

Habel: Job’s reply is a legal rebuttal. He does not accept Eliphaz’s diagnosis. Instead he presents evidence: the weight of his suffering (proportionality) and his preserved integrity (innocence).

Verses 14–30 — "My Brothers Are Undependable as Streams"
1415161718192021222324252627282930

Hebrew Word Study

נַחַל naḥal — wadi / intermittent stream: "My brothers have been as treacherous as a wadi (naḥal)" (v.15). A wadi is a seasonal streambed — a torrent in winter, bone-dry in summer. The image is devastating: when Job needed his friends most (summer, drought), they dried up. The water was there when he didn’t need it.

חֶסֶד ḥesed — loyal love / kindness / faithfulness: "Anyone who withholds kindness (ḥesed) from a friend forsakes the fear of the Almighty" (v.14). Job connects the friends’ failure to love with a failure to fear God. Pastoral cruelty is not just unhelpful — it is unfaithful.

Context

Job turns from God to the friends. The wadi metaphor is drawn from desert geography: caravans depending on seasonal streams find nothing when they arrive. The friends are those empty streams. Job then challenges them: "Show me where I have been wrong" (v.24). He does not refuse correction — he refuses unsubstantiated accusation.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 15:18"You are to me like a deceptive brook, like a spring that fails" — Jeremiah uses the same wadi image for God himself.
  • Prov 17:17"A friend loves at all times, and a brother is born for adversity" — the standard the friends fail to meet.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:14–15

"Anyone who withholds kindness from a friend forsakes the fear of the Almighty. But my brothers are as undependable as intermittent streams." The theological connection: failing a friend is failing God.

6:21

"Now you too have proved to be of no help." Direct. The friends offered theology when Job needed presence.

6:24–25

"Teach me, and I will be quiet; show me where I have been wrong." Job is not closed to correction. He demands specifics, not generalities.

6:28–30

"Look at me! Would I lie to your face? Is there any wickedness on my lips?" Job’s final appeal: read my face, hear my words, judge for yourselves.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:14

Calvin: withholding kindness is connected to forsaking the fear of God. The friends’ theological error produces pastoral cruelty, and pastoral cruelty is a spiritual failure.

6:24

Calvin: Job’s openness to correction is important. He does not claim perfection — he claims innocence of the specific charge implied by his suffering.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:15

The NET explains the wadi (naḥal) — a seasonal streambed that fills during rains but dries completely in summer heat.

6:19

The NET: "caravans of Tema look" — Tema is in northwest Arabia. Real trade routes depended on water sources that sometimes failed.

Clines Notes

clines
6:15–20

Clines: the wadi image is the chapter’s masterpiece. Caravans from Tema and Sheba arrive at the wadi expecting water and find dust. They are "distressed" and "dismayed." The friends are that dry streambed.

6:24–25

Clines: Job’s willingness to be corrected is crucial. He is not defiant for defiance’s sake. He wants evidence, not assumptions.

Habel Notes

habel
6:14–30

Habel: Job cross-examines the witnesses. He challenges the friends to produce evidence: "Show me where I have been wrong" (v.24). They cannot, because there is no evidence. The prosecution’s case is circumstantial.
