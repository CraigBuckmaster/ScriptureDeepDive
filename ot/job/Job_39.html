Job 39

God Continues: The Wild Kingdom

Verses 1–12 — Mountain Goats, Wild Donkey, Wild Ox
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

פֶרֶא pereʾ — wild donkey: "Who let the wild donkey (pereʾ) go free? Who untied its ropes?" (v.5). God liberated the wild donkey. It roams free, laughs at the town, and scorns the driver’s shout (v.7). God made creatures that serve no human purpose and are not under human control. The wild donkey is free because God made it free.

Context

God’s second round of animal questions. Mountain goats give birth without human midwives (vv.1–4). The wild donkey roams free (vv.5–8). The wild ox cannot be domesticated (vv.9–12). Every animal challenges human centrality: these creatures exist outside the human economy. They serve God’s purposes, not ours. Job’s demand that the universe make sense to HIM is the wrong starting point.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 104:10–18"He makes springs pour water into the ravines... the wild donkeys quench their thirst" — God’s provision for wild creatures.
  • Matt 10:29"Are not two sparrows sold for a penny? Yet not one of them will fall to the ground outside your Father’s care" — the same divine attention to animals.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
39:1–4

"Do you know when the mountain goats give birth? Do you watch when the doe bears her fawn?" Wildlife reproduces without human assistance. The natural world has God as its obstetrician.

39:5–8

"Who let the wild donkey go free? He laughs at the commotion in the town; he does not hear a driver’s shout." Freedom from human control is God’s gift to this creature. Not everything that exists is for us.

39:9–12

"Will the wild ox consent to serve you? Will it stay by your manger at night?" The wild ox is untameable. Its power serves God’s design, not human agriculture.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
39:5–8

Calvin: the wild donkey’s freedom is a parable of grace. God gives some creatures freedom precisely because freedom is good, even when it serves no utilitarian purpose.

39:9–12

Calvin: the untameable ox teaches Job that power exists beyond human management. So does God’s sovereign action in Job’s suffering.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
39:5

The NET: pereʾ is the Asian wild ass (onager), famous for its speed and untameability. It lives in salt flats and barren land where domesticated donkeys cannot survive.

39:9

The NET: the wild ox (rēʾem) is likely the now-extinct aurochs — ancestor of domestic cattle but vastly larger and completely untameable.

Clines Notes

clines
39:5–8

Clines: the wild donkey is God’s most subversive creation in this speech. It laughs at civilisation. It mocks the human project of domestication. God is proud of its wildness.

39:9–12

Clines: the wild ox won’t serve Job. Its strength is real but not available for human use. The implied lesson: God’s power, like the wild ox’s, serves divine purposes, not human ones.

Habel Notes

habel
39:1–12

Habel: God presents evidence from the animal kingdom. Each creature represents a domain God governs without human consultation. The judge demonstrates jurisdictions the plaintiff never knew existed.

Verses 13–30 — The Ostrich, the Warhorse, the Eagle
131415161718192021222324252627282930

Hebrew Word Study

סוּס sūs — horse / warhorse: "Do you give the horse (sūs) its strength? Do you clothe its neck with a flowing mane?" (v.19). The warhorse is God’s masterpiece of designed ferocity. It paws the ground, charges into battle, laughs at fear, does not shy from the sword. It was not made for Job’s use but for its own terrible glory.

Context

The animal gallery concludes with three extraordinary creatures. The ostrich (vv.13–18): foolish by design, leaves eggs unattended, yet outruns the horse. The warhorse (vv.19–25): terrifying in battle, exulting in its own power. The hawk and eagle (vv.26–30): soaring beyond human reach, their young lapping up blood. God’s creation includes foolishness, violence, and predation — by design, not by accident.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 21:31"The horse is made ready for the day of battle, but victory rests with the LORD" — the horse imagery connecting battle to divine sovereignty.
  • Deut 32:11"Like an eagle that stirs up its nest and hovers over its young" — God compared to the eagle he describes here.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
39:13–18

"The wings of the ostrich flap joyfully, though they cannot compare with the wings of the stork. She lays her eggs on the ground... she is hardened toward her young. Yet when she spreads her feathers to run, she laughs at horse and rider." The ostrich: foolish by design, fast by design. Not everything in creation is optimised for wisdom.

39:19–25

"Do you give the horse its strength? It laughs at fear, afraid of nothing. It does not shy from the sword. It paws fiercely, rejoicing in its strength; it charges into the fray." The warhorse: designed for battle, exulting in violence. God made this. Not everything in creation is gentle.

39:26–30

"Does the hawk take flight by your wisdom? Does the eagle soar at your command? It dwells on a cliff... its young ones feast on blood." The eagle: predatory, high, feeding on death. God’s creation includes predation at the heights.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
39:19–25

Calvin: the warhorse’s joy in battle is God-given. Not all divine gifts are gentle. Some are terrible. God’s creativity includes power, wildness, and even what we call savagery.

39:30

Calvin: the eagle feeding on blood is not a flaw in creation but a feature. God’s ecosystem includes death. This is not a fallen world here; this is how God designed it. The implication for Job: suffering may be part of the design, not a departure from it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
39:13–18

The NET discusses the ostrich at length — the Hebrew is difficult and debated. The key point: God takes credit for a creature that lacks wisdom but has other gifts.

39:19–25

The NET: the warhorse passage is widely regarded as one of the finest examples of Hebrew poetry. The language mimics the horse’s energy — short, staccato phrases charging forward.

39:30

The NET: "its young ones feast on blood" is the speech’s final image before God demands Job’s response. The last picture is of death feeding life — the cycle of nature that transcends human moral categories.

Clines Notes

clines
39:13–18

Clines: the ostrich is God’s comedy. "God did not endow her with wisdom" (v.17). Yet she outruns horses. God deliberately created something foolish and fast. The design includes what we would call flaws.

39:19–25

Clines: the warhorse passage is the most exhilarating poetry in the book. God describes with obvious pleasure a creature that exists for violence. The divine aesthetic includes ferocity.

39:30

Clines: "where the slain are, there it is" — the eagle is drawn to death. God’s creation is not a nursery; it is a food chain. The whirlwind speeches present a wilder, more dangerous, more real God than the friends’ theology could accommodate.

Habel Notes

habel
39:13–30

Habel: God’s final animal witnesses include the foolish, the violent, and the predatory. The creation is not morally tidy. The retribution principle cannot account for an ostrich. The friends’ theology is too small for God’s world.
