Job 13

Job Demands a Hearing with God

Verses 1–19 — “Worthless Physicians”; “Though He Slay Me”
12345678910111213141516171819

Hebrew Word Study

רֹפְאֵי אֶלִל rōfʾê ʾĕlîl — healers of nothing / worthless physicians: "Worthless physicians (rōfʾê ʾĕlîl), all of you!" (v.4). The friends came as comforters; Job diagnoses them as incompetent doctors. Their medicine — retribution theology — does not heal because it misdiagnoses the disease.

הֵן יִקְטָלֵנִי hên yiqtālênî — though he slay me: "Though he slay me, yet will I hope in him" (v.15). The most famous verse in Job and one of the most debated. The Hebrew is ambiguous: the written text (Ketiv) reads "I will not hope" while the marginal reading (Qere) reads "I will hope." Both are grammatically valid. The ambiguity itself is the point: Job simultaneously trusts and protests.

Context

Job silences the friends (vv.1–12), then turns to God with what may be the bravest statement in Scripture. The tension: he will argue his case before God’s face (v.15) even though that confrontation might kill him. His integrity matters more than his life. He would rather die making his case than live in guilty silence.

Cross-Reference

  • Dan 3:17–18"Our God is able to deliver us... but even if he does not" — the same defiant conditional faith.
  • Hab 3:17–18"Though the fig tree does not bud... yet I will rejoice" — trust despite circumstances.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:3–4

"But I desire to speak to the Almighty and to argue my case with God. You, however, smear me with lies; you are worthless physicians." Job wants God, not the friends. Their theology is a plaster on a wound it cannot reach.

13:7–9

"Will you speak wickedly on God’s behalf? Will you show him partiality?" Job accuses the friends of lying for God. Bad theology in God’s defence is still bad theology.

13:15

"Though he slay me, yet will I hope in him — I will surely defend my ways to his face." Defiant faith. Job will not let go of either his God or his integrity.

13:18

"I have prepared my case; I know I will be vindicated." Legal confidence. Job believes that if he could get a fair hearing, he would win.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:7–9

Calvin: the accusation that the friends lie for God is profound. Defending God with false arguments is not piety but impiety. God does not need defenders who misrepresent the facts.

13:15

Calvin: this verse is the summit of faith in the OT. Job trusts God enough to confront God. He fears God enough to speak honestly. The two are not contradictory.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:4

The NET: ʾĕlîl means “of no value / worthless.” The friends’ theological medicine is inert.

13:15

The NET discusses both textual traditions and notes the KJV follows the Qere (“I will hope”) while many modern translations follow the Ketiv (“I have no hope”).

Clines Notes

clines
13:4

Clines: "worthless physicians" is Job’s final verdict on the friends as counsellors. From this point, he largely ignores them and addresses God directly.

13:15

Clines discusses the Ketiv/Qere ambiguity at length. The written text may mean "he will slay me — I have no hope" while the read text means "though he slay me, I will hope." Clines: the ambiguity captures Job’s psychology perfectly. He does both.

Habel Notes

habel
13:1–19

Habel: Job dismisses the expert witnesses and demands a direct hearing with the judge. He is willing to risk death (v.15) for the sake of due process. The plaintiff insists on his day in court.

Verses 20–28 — Job’s Terms for the Hearing

Hebrew Word Study

חַטָּאת ḥaṭṭāʾṭ — sin / offence: "How many wrongs and sins (chatṭāʾṭ) have I committed?" (v.23). Job demands an itemised indictment. General accusations are not enough. He wants specifics. This is the language of a defendant who knows the charges are baseless and challenges the prosecution to prove otherwise.

Context

Job sets the terms for his hearing: remove your hand, stop terrifying me, then call and I will answer (vv.20–22). The conditions are about power equalisation — Job cannot speak freely while God’s terror silences him. He demands a level playing field.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 51:4"Against you, you only, have I sinned" — David’s specific confession vs Job’s demand for specific charges.
  • Isa 43:26"State your case, that you may be proved right" — God’s own invitation to legal argument.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:20–22

"Only grant me these two things... withdraw your hand far from me, and stop frightening me. Then summon me and I will answer, or let me speak, and you reply." Job’s procedural demands: remove the terror, then begin the trial.

13:23–24

"How many wrongs have I committed? Show me my offence and my sin. Why do you hide your face?" Job demands discovery — the legal right to see the evidence against him.

13:25–28

"Will you torment a windblown leaf?" Job’s self-description: fragile, decaying, wasting away. The contrast between his vulnerability and God’s power underlines the unfairness.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:21–22

Calvin: the demand for fair hearing conditions is not arrogance but justice. Every court system recognises the right of the accused to speak without intimidation.

13:24

Calvin: the hidden face of God is the deepest suffering. Physical pain and social rejection are secondary to the experience of divine absence. Job’s real agony is theological, not physical.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:23

The NET: "make me know my transgression" uses legal vocabulary — the demand for formal charges, not vague accusations.

13:27

The NET: "you put my feet in shackles" — Job describes himself as a prisoner under surveillance. God monitors every step.

Clines Notes

clines
13:20–22

Clines: the two conditions — remove the hand, stop the terror — are about procedural justice. A defendant cannot make a case under torture. Job demands what any court provides: the right to speak without coercion.

13:24

Clines: "why do you hide your face?" is the deepest question. God’s silence is worse than God’s wrath. An enemy you can see is better than a judge who won’t appear.

Habel Notes

habel
13:20–28

Habel: Job stipulates the terms of the hearing. He wants due process: fair conditions, specific charges, the right to speak. The legal metaphor reaches its fullest expression in this passage.
