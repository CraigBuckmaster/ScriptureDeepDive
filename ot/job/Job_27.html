Job 27

Job’s Oath: I Will Not Deny My Integrity

Verses 1–6 — “I Will Never Admit You Are in the Right”
123456

Hebrew Word Study

תֻמָּה tummâ — integrity / completeness / blamelessness: "I will not deny my integrity (tummâ)" (v.5). The same root as tām (blameless) from 1:1. Job swears an oath on his integrity. He uses God’s own assessment of him (1:8; 2:3) as the basis of his legal defence. The character God praised in heaven, Job defends on earth.

Context

Job swears a formal oath: “As surely as God lives, who has denied me justice... till I die, I will not deny my integrity” (vv.2–5). This is a legal oath — the most solemn form of testimony. Job swears by the God who has (in his view) wronged him. The paradox: he trusts God enough to swear by God while accusing God of injustice.

Cross-Reference

  • Job 1:1"Blameless and upright" — God’s own verdict, which Job now defends.
  • Job 2:3"He still maintains his integrity" — God’s assessment, which this oath fulfils.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:2–3

"As surely as God lives, who has denied me justice, the Almighty, who has made my life bitter — as long as I have life within me." Job swears by the God who has wronged him. The oath formula is the most binding available.

27:4–5

"My lips will not say anything wicked, and my tongue will not utter lies. I will never admit you are in the right; till I die, I will not deny my integrity." Job refuses to confess sins he has not committed. The friends’ demand for repentance is rejected.

27:6

"I will maintain my innocence and never let go of it; my conscience will not reproach me as long as I live." Job’s conscience is clear. He will not manufacture guilt to satisfy a theology.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
27:5–6

Calvin: the refusal to confess false guilt is not pride but honesty. To admit sins one has not committed would be lying — the very thing Job refuses to do.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
27:2

The NET: "denied me justice" is literally "turned aside my right/judgment." Job accuses God of procedural injustice.

27:5

The NET: "I will not deny my integrity" uses the same root (t-m-m) as 1:1. Job defends God’s own assessment of him.

Clines Notes

clines
27:2

Clines: the oath formula "as God lives" is extraordinary. Job swears by the very God he accuses of injustice. He denies God’s justice but not God’s existence or sovereignty.

27:5–6

Clines: "I will never admit you are in the right" is addressed to the friends. Job will not accept their diagnosis. His integrity is worth more to him than their approval — or their theology.

Habel Notes

habel
27:1–6

Habel: Job takes the oath. In the ancient legal system, an oath of innocence was the defendant’s final legal instrument. If no one can disprove it, the case is decided.

Verses 7–23 — The Fate of the Godless
7891011121314151617181920212223

Hebrew Word Study

חָנֵף ḥānēf — godless / profane / hypocrite: "What hope has the godless (ḥānēf) when he is cut off, when God takes away his life?" (v.8). Job now describes the fate of the wicked using the friends’ own language. The reversal is deliberate: Job appropriates retribution theology not to apply it to himself but to his accusers.

Context

In a surprising move, Job describes the wicked’s destruction using language identical to the friends’ speeches. Scholars debate whether this section is Job speaking, a displaced fragment of Zophar’s missing third speech, or Job deliberately adopting the friends’ language to demonstrate he knows their theology — and still rejects its application to himself.

Cross-Reference

  • Job 20:29"Such is the lot God allots the wicked" — Zophar’s conclusion, which Job now echoes.
  • Ps 73:27"Those who are far from you will perish" — the standard retribution theology.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:7–8

"May my enemy be like the wicked, my adversary like the unjust! For what hope has the godless when he is cut off?" Job turns the friends’ theology against them: if anyone is godless here, it is the accuser, not the accused.

27:13–23

The catalogue of wicked-person destruction mirrors Zophar’s and Bildad’s speeches: treasure hoarded for the righteous, east wind sweeps the wicked away, God hurls the storm against them. Job knows this theology. He just doesn’t accept it as his diagnosis.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
27:13

Calvin: Job agrees that the wicked ultimately face judgment. His quarrel is not with retribution as a final reality but with retribution as an immediate, this-life diagnosis.

27:19–23

Calvin: the east wind sweeping the wicked away is poetic justice: the same God who sent the east wind against Job’s children (1:19) will send it against the truly wicked.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
27:8

The NET: ḥānēf means one who is profane or polluted — not merely sinful but fundamentally corrupt. Job applies this to his accusers.

27:13

The NET notes the similarity to Zophar’s 20:29 and flags the scholarly debate about this section’s authorship within the dialogue.

Clines Notes

clines
27:7–23

Clines: whether this is Job or a displaced Zophar speech, the rhetorical effect is the same: Job demonstrates he can argue retribution theology as well as the friends. His rejection of it for his own case is based on experience, not ignorance.

27:13

Clines: "this is the fate God allots the wicked" uses Zophar’s exact wording from 20:29. Job quotes his opponent’s closing argument and turns it around.

Habel Notes

habel
27:7–23

Habel: Job uses the friends’ own rhetoric as a counter-weapon. By describing the fate of the wicked, he implicitly argues: I know what happens to the wicked — and it hasn’t happened to me in the way you claim.
