Job 31

Job’s Oath of Innocence

Verses 1–23 — Oath Against Lust, Deceit, Adultery, Injustice
1234567891011121314151617181920212223

Hebrew Word Study

בְרִית bərîṭ — covenant / agreement: "I made a covenant (bərîṭ) with my eyes not to look lustfully at a young woman" (v.1). Job’s oath begins with the eyes — the entry point of temptation. He made a formal agreement with his own senses. This anticipates Jesus’ teaching that lust in the heart equals adultery (Matt 5:28).

אִם ʾam — if (conditional oath): The entire chapter is built on the word "if" (ʾam): if I have done X, then let Y happen to me. This is the ancient Near Eastern oath of clearance — the most solemn legal instrument available. Each "if" clause is a sworn denial of a specific sin.

Context

Job’s oath of innocence is the longest and most comprehensive in the OT. He swears against: lust (v.1), deceit (v.5), adultery (v.9), injustice to servants (v.13), neglect of the poor (v.16), withholding from orphans and widows (v.17–21), idolatry (v.24–28), gloating over enemies (v.29), hidden sin (v.33–34). The structure is “if I have done X, then let the curse fall on me.” Each clause invites God to verify. The oath is Job’s final legal instrument: if no one can refute it, the case is decided in his favour.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 5:28"Anyone who looks at a woman lustfully has already committed adultery" — Jesus’ teaching parallels Job’s eye-covenant.
  • Ps 24:3–4"Who may ascend the mountain of the LORD? The one who has clean hands and a pure heart" — the entrance requirements that Job claims to meet.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:1

"I made a covenant with my eyes not to look lustfully at a young woman." The oath begins with the most private sin: the thought life. Job swears against internal sin, not just external behaviour.

31:5–8

"If I have walked with falsehood... let what I have sown be eaten by another." Deceit: Job invites crop failure as the penalty for lying.

31:9–12

"If my heart has been enticed by a woman... then may my wife grind another man’s grain." Adultery: the penalty matches the crime. If I violated another’s marriage, let mine be violated.

31:13–15

"If I have denied justice to any of my servants... did not he who made me in the womb make them?" Job grounds servant-rights in creation theology: all humans share the same Maker.

31:16–23

"If I have denied the desires of the poor or let the eyes of the widow grow weary... then let my arm fall from the shoulder." Neglect of the vulnerable: Job swears his entire social justice record.

Calvin: the eye-covenant shows Job’s righteousness was not passive but actively maintained. He fought temptation at its point of entry — the gaze.

31:13–15

Calvin: "did not he who made me make them?" is the most radical social ethic in the OT before the prophets. Job derives human dignity from creation, not status.

Verses 24–40 — Oath Against Idolatry; “Let the Almighty Answer Me!”

Hebrew Word Study

תָו tāw — mark / signature / sign: "Here is my signature (tāw) — let the Almighty answer me!" (v.35). Tāw is the last letter of the Hebrew alphabet. Job signs his legal document. The case is filed. The defendant (God) must now respond. Everything that follows — Elihu’s speeches, the whirlwind — is the response to this demand.

Context

Job’s oath concludes with the most dramatic moment in the dialogue. He has sworn against every conceivable sin. Now he signs the document: "Here is my tāw — let the Almighty answer me!" He wants God’s written response (v.35). He would wear the indictment on his shoulder like a crown (v.36). The legal metaphor reaches its climax: the case is filed, the signature affixed, the court must now convene.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 50:8"He who vindicates me is near. Who will bring charges against me?" — the same legal confidence.
  • Rom 8:33–34"Who will bring any charge against those whom God has chosen?" — Paul’s answer to Job’s question.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:24–28

"If I have put my trust in gold... if my heart has been secretly enticed by the sun or the moon... these would be sins to be judged, for I would have been unfaithful to God on high." Idolatry: Job swears against worship of both wealth and celestial objects.

31:29–32

"If I have rejoiced at my enemy’s misfortune... no stranger had to spend the night in the street, for my door was always open to the traveller." Hospitality and non-vindictiveness: positive and negative righteousness combined.

31:33–34

"If I have concealed my sin as people do, hiding my guilt in my heart because I so feared the crowd." Job denies hypocrisy: he has not hidden sin out of fear of public opinion.

31:35–37

"Here is my signature — let the Almighty answer me! Let my accuser put his indictment in writing. I would wear it on my shoulder; I would put it on like a crown." The climactic demand. Job would display God’s charges as a badge of honour because he is confident they would prove his innocence.

31:38–40

"If my land cries out against me... then let briers come up instead of wheat." The final oath covers even the land itself. "The words of Job are ended." The case rests.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
31:35

Calvin: "let the Almighty answer me" is the bravest sentence in the OT. Job demands a response from the Creator of the universe. He gets one (ch 38) — but not the one he expected.

31:40

Calvin: "the words of Job are ended" — the legal case is complete. Job has nothing more to say. The silence that follows is the space in which God’s answer forms. The whirlwind is on its way.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
31:35

The NET: tāw is literally the final letter of the Hebrew alphabet and was written as a cross or X — the equivalent of a signature mark in the ancient world.

31:36

The NET: "wear it on my shoulder" — like a medal of honour or insignia of office. The indictment, Job believes, would prove his innocence.

31:40

The NET: "the words of Job are ended" is an editorial colophon marking the conclusion of Job’s speeches. Only God and Elihu will speak after this.

Clines Notes

clines
31:35

Clines: the tāw — the last letter of the Hebrew alphabet. Job has said everything he can say, from aleph to tāw. The signature is both a legal act and a literary closure.

31:35–37

Clines: "I would wear the indictment on my shoulder like a crown" — this is not arrogance but legal confidence. If God stated the charges, they would vindicate Job rather than condemn him.

31:40b

Clines: "The words of Job are ended" — the narrator’s note. The dialogue is over. What follows (Elihu, the whirlwind) is response, not continuation.

31:35–40
