Job 29

Job’s Past Glory

Verses 1–11 — “How I Long for the Months Gone By”
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

נֵר nēr — lamp / light: "When his lamp (nēr) shone on my head and by his light I walked through darkness" (v.3). God’s lamp guided Job. The image contrasts with the present: where the friends said the wicked’s lamp is snuffed (18:5), Job says his righteous lamp has been extinguished without cause.

Context

Job begins his three-chapter final defence (29–31). Chapter 29 is the “before” picture: honour, influence, respect. He was the elder at the gate, the arbiter of disputes, the one who rescued the poor and made the widow’s heart sing. This is not nostalgia; it is evidence. Job presents his former life as proof of the character the friends deny.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 1:1–3"Blessed is the one... whose delight is in the law" — the righteous person’s life that Job describes having lived.
  • Prov 31:23"Her husband is respected at the city gate" — the same social honour Job enjoyed.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
29:2–4

"How I long for the months gone by, for the days when God watched over me, when his lamp shone on my head." The nostalgia is theological, not merely personal. Job misses God’s manifest presence.

29:7–10

"When I went to the gate of the city... the young men stepped aside, the old men rose. The chief men refrained from speaking." Job was the most respected man in his community. His word settled disputes, his presence commanded silence.

29:11

"Whoever heard me spoke well of me, and those who saw me commended me." Universal respect. This is not boasting; it is evidence for the defence.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
29:2–6

Calvin: Job’s longing for God’s presence is the deepest layer of his suffering. The loss of wealth and health are secondary to the loss of God’s manifest friendship.

29:7–11

Calvin: the respect of the community confirms what God already said in 1:8. Job was genuinely righteous, and everyone knew it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
29:4

The NET: "intimate friendship" (sōd) means a council or inner circle. God’s sōd was over Job’s tent — God confided in him.

29:7

The NET: "the gate" was the public square where legal and civic business was conducted. Job’s presence there marks him as a community leader.

Clines Notes

clines
29:2–6

Clines: Job’s memory is saturated with divine presence. "God watched over me" (v.2), "his lamp shone" (v.3), "God’s intimate friendship blessed my house" (v.4), "the Almighty was still with me" (v.5). The past tense is devastating: was, was, was.

29:7–10

Clines: the gate-scene establishes Job’s social status. He was not merely rich but influential — an elder, a judge, a community pillar. His fall is total: from gate to ash-heap.

Habel Notes

habel
29:1–11

Habel: Job presents character evidence. The defence calls its first witness: Job’s own past. The testimony establishes his social standing and moral reputation.

Verses 12–25 — Job the Deliverer of the Poor
1213141516171819202122232425

Hebrew Word Study

צֶדֶק ṣedeq — righteousness / justice: "I put on righteousness (ṣedeq) as my clothing; justice (mishpāṭ) was my robe and turban" (v.14). Job wore justice like clothing. It was not an occasional act but a permanent identity. He was clothed in it as completely as a priest in vestments.

Context

Job catalogues his social justice record: rescued the poor (v.12), the dying blessed him (v.13), he was eyes to the blind and feet to the lame (v.15), he investigated cases on behalf of strangers (v.16), broke the fangs of the wicked (v.17). This directly refutes Eliphaz’s fabricated charges (22:6–9). Point by point, Job’s actual life contradicts the friends’ accusations.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 61:10"He has clothed me with garments of salvation" — the same clothing-as-righteousness metaphor.
  • Jas 1:27"Religion that God our Father accepts: to look after orphans and widows" — the definition of religion that Job lived.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
29:12–13

"I rescued the poor who cried for help, and the fatherless who had none to assist them. The one who was dying blessed me." Job’s social justice record refutes Eliphaz’s charges (22:6–9).

29:14–16

"I put on righteousness as my clothing. I was eyes to the blind and feet to the lame. I was a father to the needy." Job was not passively good but actively righteous — seeking out injustice and correcting it.

29:18–20

"I thought, ‘I will die in my own house, my days as numerous as the grains of sand.’" Job expected a long, blessed life. The expectation was reasonable; the reversal was not.

29:21–25

"People listened to me expectantly, waiting in silence for my counsel. I chose the way for them and sat as their chief." Job was king, counsellor, and comforter. He sat among them "as one who comforts mourners." The irony: the comforter of others cannot be comforted by his friends.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
29:14–16

Calvin: the catalogue of righteous deeds is not self-righteousness but evidence. In a trial, the accused must present their record. Job does so with specificity and passion.

29:18

Calvin: "I will die in my own house" — the expectation of a blessed death was based on the same theology the friends hold. Job believed in retribution too, until his experience disproved it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
29:14

The NET: the clothing metaphor is sustained: righteousness was his garment, justice his robe and turban. The dressed self is the public self — Job was publicly, visibly just.

29:17

The NET: "broke the fangs of the wicked" — a predator image. Job rescued prey from the predator’s mouth.

Clines Notes

clines
29:14

Clines: "I put on righteousness as my clothing" is not metaphor — it is identity. Job’s entire public persona was constructed on justice. Stripping him of prosperity strips away the visible evidence, but not the reality.

29:25

Clines: "as one who comforts mourners" — the final irony. Job, who comforted others in grief, is surrounded by comforters who only add to his grief.

Habel Notes

habel
29:12–25

Habel: Job’s testimony directly rebuts the prosecution’s fabricated charges. Every accusation Eliphaz made (22:6–9) is contradicted by Job’s own record. The defence presents counter-evidence.
