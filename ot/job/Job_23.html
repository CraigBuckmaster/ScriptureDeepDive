Job 23

Job’s Reply: If Only I Could Find God

Verses 1–7 — “If Only I Knew Where to Find Him”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

מָצָא māṣāʾ — to find / to reach / to encounter: "If only I knew where to find (māṣāʾ) him; if only I could go to his dwelling!" (v.3). The verb māṣāʾ means to reach, encounter, arrive at. Job wants physical access to God — a face-to-face hearing. The frustration: God is both the one Job needs and the one Job cannot locate.

Context

Job ignores Eliphaz’s fabricated charges entirely. He doesn’t defend himself against the specific accusations because they are beneath response. Instead he returns to his core longing: a direct encounter with God. If only he could find God, he would lay out his case (v.4) and hear what God would say (v.5). He is confident God would not crush him but would listen (v.6).

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 42:1–2"As the deer pants for streams of water, so my soul pants for you, my God" — the same longing for divine presence.
  • Isa 55:6"Seek the LORD while he may be found" — the assumption that God CAN be found. Job questions this.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
23:2–3

"Even today my complaint is bitter; his hand is heavy in spite of my groaning. If only I knew where to find him!" The persistent longing: Job wants God, not theology about God.

23:4–5

"I would state my case before him and fill my mouth with arguments. I would find out what he would answer me." Job’s confidence: in a fair hearing, truth would prevail.

23:6–7

"Would he vigorously oppose me? No, he would give me a hearing. There the upright could establish their innocence before him." Faith in God’s fairness survives Job’s experience of God’s silence.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:3

Calvin: the longing to find God is the deepest form of faith. Job does not want to escape God but to meet God. His quarrel is not with God’s existence but with God’s absence.

23:6–7

Calvin: "he would give me a hearing" — Job trusts God’s justice even while protesting God’s current actions. This is the paradox of Job’s faith: he trusts the one he accuses.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:3

The NET: māṣāʾ implies physical arrival at a destination. Job wants to walk to God’s door and knock.

23:7

The NET: "establish their innocence" is literally "be delivered forever from my judge" — permanent acquittal.

Clines Notes

clines
23:3

Clines: the cry to “find” God is Job’s most basic need. Not understanding, not vindication — encounter. He wants God’s presence more than God’s explanation.

23:6–7

Clines: Job’s confidence that God would listen is remarkable after 20 chapters of complaint. His faith in God’s character has not been destroyed by his experience of God’s actions.

Habel Notes

habel
23:1–7

Habel: Job reiterates his demand for a hearing. Despite the prosecution’s failure to prove guilt, the judge has not appeared. The plaintiff cannot get the court to convene.

Verses 8–17 — “He Knows the Way That I Take”
891011121314151617

Hebrew Word Study

בְחָנַנִי bəḥānanî — when he tests me: "He knows the way that I take; when he has tested (bəḥānanî) me, I will come forth as gold" (v.10). The metallurgical image: suffering as the refiner’s fire. Job compares himself to gold being purified. He will emerge tested and proven. This is the highest expression of faith since the Redeemer declaration.

Context

Job searches in all directions — east, west, north, south — and cannot find God (vv.8–9). But then a pivot: even though Job cannot find God, God knows where Job is (v.10). And when the test is over, Job will come forth as gold. The chapter’s two halves are in tension: searching for a hidden God (vv.1–9) and trusting a God who sees everything (vv.10–17). The tension is unresolved — which is the point.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Pet 1:6–7"These have come so that the proven genuineness of your faith — of greater worth than gold — may result in praise" — Peter’s commentary on Job’s metaphor.
  • Mal 3:2–3"He will sit as a refiner and purifier of silver" — the same refining image.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
23:8–9

"But if I go to the east, he is not there; if I go to the west, I do not find him." God is absent in every direction. The search is comprehensive and futile.

23:10

"He knows the way that I take; when he has tested me, I will come forth as gold." The chapter’s summit. Job trusts that his suffering is a test with an outcome: proven purity.

23:11–12

"My feet have closely followed his steps; I have kept to his way without turning aside. I have not departed from the commands of his lips; I have treasured the words of his mouth." Job’s clearest claim of consistent obedience.

23:13–17

"He stands alone, and who can oppose him? He does whatever he pleases... he carries out his decree against me." The tension: God is sovereign and terrifying. Job trusts and fears simultaneously.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:10

Calvin: this verse is the key to Job’s theology of suffering. It is not punishment but testing. The furnace does not create the gold; it reveals it. Job’s suffering reveals character that was always there.

23:14

Calvin: "he carries out his decree against me" — God’s sovereignty is both comfort and terror. Comfort because there IS a plan. Terror because the plan includes suffering without explanation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:10

The NET: the metallurgical metaphor is precise. Gold is refined by heating to separate impurities. The process requires extreme heat but the result is purity.

23:13–14

The NET: "he stands alone" — literally "he is in one" (unique, self-determined). No one can change his mind or redirect his purpose.

Clines Notes

clines
23:10

Clines: “come forth as gold” is Job’s most confident prediction about his own vindication. He does not know when or how, but he knows the testing will prove his integrity.

23:13–17

Clines: the ending is deliberately ambiguous. God is sovereign (good) but does whatever he pleases (terrifying). Job’s faith is not comfortable certainty but trust in the dark.

Habel Notes

habel
23:8–17

Habel: Job accepts that the judge is hidden but trusts the eventual verdict. He has done everything right (vv.11–12); the test will confirm it (v.10). But the judge’s absolute sovereignty remains frightening (vv.13–17).
