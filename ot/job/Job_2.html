Job 2

Satan’s Second Challenge

Verses 1–6 — "Skin for Skin"
123456

Hebrew Word Study

עוֹר בְעַד עוֹר ʿōr bəʿad ʿōr — skin for skin: "Skin for skin" — a proverbial expression meaning: a person will trade anything to save their own life. The Satan’s argument: Job’s worship in chapter 1 was self-preservation, not genuine piety. He lost possessions and children but kept his body. Take his health, and the mask falls.

Context

The heavenly court reconvenes. God points out that Job "still maintains his integrity, though you incited me against him to ruin him without any reason (ḥinnām)." God uses the Satan’s own word back at him. The test was without cause — God admits it. This is the most theologically explosive verse in the prologue.

Cross-Reference

  • Job 1:9The Satan’s first challenge — the second escalates from property to body.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:3

"You incited me against him to ruin him without any reason." God himself calls Job’s suffering causeless. This verse demolishes the friends’ entire theology before they speak.

2:4–5

"Skin for skin! A man will give all he has for his own life. But stretch out your hand and strike his flesh and bones, and he will surely curse you."

2:6

"He is in your hands; but you must spare his life." The second limit. The Satan can torment but not kill. God maintains control.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:3

Calvin: God says "without any reason" — meaning Job’s suffering is not retribution. This should silence all who assume suffering proves guilt.

2:6

Calvin: God’s sovereignty is shown in the limit. Evil operates on a leash.

Verses 7–13 — Job in Ashes; the Three Friends Arrive
78910111213

Hebrew Word Study

בְרֵךְ bārēk̅ — bless / curse (euphemism): Job’s wife: "Curse (bārēk̅) God and die." The verb is bārak — "bless." The narrator uses a euphemism: the word for "bless" stands in for "curse" because the actual phrase is too terrible to write. This is the very thing the Satan predicted Job would do (1:11; 2:5).

שִׁבְעַת יָמִים shivʿat yāmîm — seven days: The friends sit with Job seven days and seven nights without speaking. This is the most profound act of pastoral care in the Bible. Their failure begins when they open their mouths.

Context

Job’s wife speaks one line and disappears from the book. Her advice is exactly what the Satan expected: "Curse God and die." Job refuses. Then silence — seven days of it. The friends’ wordless presence is their finest moment. When they begin to explain, they begin to fail.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 50:10Seven days of mourning for Jacob — the standard mourning period.
  • Ezek 3:15Ezekiel sits among the exiles "overwhelmed, for seven days" — the same stunned silence.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:9

Job’s wife: "Curse God and die." She voices what the Satan predicted. Job’s refusal to comply is the second vindication of his integrity.

2:10

"Shall we accept good from God, and not trouble?" Job’s theology is consistent: God is the source of both. The narrator confirms: "In all this, Job did not sin in what he said."

2:11–12

Three friends arrive: Eliphaz from Teman, Bildad from Shuah, Zophar from Naamah. They weep, tear their robes, sprinkle dust. They barely recognise him.

2:13

Seven days and nights of silence. "No one said a word to him, because they saw how great his suffering was." This is the only truly helpful thing the friends do.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:10

Calvin: "Shall we accept good from God, and not trouble?" This is the mature theology that holds God’s sovereignty over both blessing and suffering. Job does not pretend God is uninvolved.

2:13

Calvin: the silence of the friends was their best sermon. When they began to speak, they began to wound.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:9

The NET discusses the euphemistic use of bārak — scribes substituted "bless" for "curse" when God was the object, to avoid writing blasphemy.

2:13

The NET notes the seven-day silence corresponds to the standard mourning period and demonstrates the friends’ genuine grief.

Clines Notes

clines
2:9

Clines: Job’s wife is not the villain tradition makes her. She sees her husband’s agony and offers the only escape she can imagine. Her advice is wrong but not malicious.

2:13

Clines on the silence: the friends do everything right until they speak. Their seven-day vigil is a model of solidarity. The disaster is not their presence but their theology.

Habel Notes

habel
2:11–13

Habel: the friends arrive as "expert witnesses" for the prosecution. They will argue that suffering proves guilt — the retribution principle. Their seven-day silence is the court’s recess before testimony begins.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Job’s wife
Speaks one line: "Curse God and die"
She voices the Satan’s expectation. Tradition is harsh on her, but she has lost the same children Job lost. Her advice is despair, not malice.
Eliphaz
The Temanite; first and gentlest friend
From Teman (Edom), known for wisdom (Jer 49:7). The most sophisticated theologian of the three. His arguments are not stupid — they are the best case for retribution theology.
Bildad
The Shuhite; appeals to tradition
His theology relies on ancestral wisdom: what the fathers taught must be true. He is the conservative voice — tradition over experience.
Zophar
The Naamathite; bluntest of the three
The least diplomatic friend. Where Eliphaz hints, Zophar accuses. His theology is the same but his manner is harsher.
