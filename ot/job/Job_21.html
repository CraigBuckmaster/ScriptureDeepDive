Job 21

Job Demolishes the Retribution Principle

Verses 1–16 — “Why Do the Wicked Live On?”
12345678910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

רְשָׁעִים rəshāʿîm — wicked ones: "Why do the wicked (rəshāʿîm) live on, growing old and increasing in power?" (v.7). The question that demolishes the friends’ theology. If suffering proves guilt, what does prosperity prove? The wicked grow old, their children are established, their homes are safe, their livestock multiply. Reality contradicts the retribution principle.

Context

Job’s most devastating rebuttal. He asks the friends to actually look at the world instead of reciting theology. The wicked prosper: they are powerful (v.7), their children dance (v.11), they spend their days in prosperity and go to the grave in peace (v.13). They even say to God, “Leave us alone!” (v.14) — and God apparently does. Job is not defending wickedness; he is destroying the premise that suffering = sin.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 73:3–5"I envied the arrogant when I saw the prosperity of the wicked. They have no struggles; their bodies are healthy and strong" — the Psalmist’s identical observation.
  • Jer 12:1"Why does the way of the wicked prosper?" — Jeremiah’s same question to God.
  • Mal 3:14–15"It is futile to serve God... the arrogant are blessed" — the same complaint in Malachi.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
21:7–9

"Why do the wicked live on? Their homes are safe and free from fear; the rod of God is not on them." Direct contradiction of everything the friends have said. The wicked are NOT punished.

21:11–13

"Their children dance about. They sing to the music of timbrel and lyre... they spend their years in prosperity and go down to the grave in peace." The wicked die peacefully. Where is the retribution?

21:14–16

"They say to God, ‘Leave us alone! We have no desire to know your ways.’" The wicked explicitly reject God — and prosper anyway. Job’s point: the retribution principle does not describe reality.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:7

Calvin: the prosperity of the wicked is a genuine theological problem. Job is not wrong to raise it. The friends’ error is not that retribution exists but that it operates perfectly in this life.

21:14–15

Calvin: the wicked say "leave us alone" and God does. This is the mystery of divine patience — not approval but forbearance. But Job rightly notes that the friends’ theology cannot explain it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:7

The NET: the question is rhetorical but empirically grounded. Job has observed what the friends refuse to see.

21:13

The NET: "in peace" (bəshalom) — the wicked die with the very shalom the friends promised to the repentant. The irony is devastating.

Clines Notes

clines
21:7

Clines: this is Job’s most empirical argument. He is not philosophising but observing. The data contradicts the theory. The friends have a theology; Job has evidence.

21:13

Clines: "go down to the grave in peace" is the death the friends promised Job IF he repented (5:26; 11:18). The wicked receive it WITHOUT repenting. The promise is worthless.

Habel Notes

habel
21:1–16

Habel: Job presents counter-evidence that destroys the prosecution’s case. The retribution principle predicts the wicked will suffer. The evidence shows they prosper. The theory fails.

Verses 17–34 — “How Often Does the Lamp of the Wicked Go Out?”
171819202122232425262728293031323334

Hebrew Word Study

הֶבֶל hebel — breath / vanity / emptiness: "So how can you console me with your nonsense (hebel)?" (v.34). Job uses the Ecclesiastes word — hebel, vanity, breath, emptiness — to characterise the friends’ entire theology. Their comfort is vapour. Their answers are empty air.

Context

Job quotes the friends back to them: “How often is the lamp of the wicked snuffed out?” (v.17 — echoing Bildad 18:5). His answer: not often enough. He then dismantles the “punishment passes to the children” theory (v.19): what good is that to the dead person? They don’t feel it. The speech ends with Job’s harshest verdict on the friends: your comfort is falsehood (v.34).

Cross-Reference

  • Eccl 8:14"Righteous people who get what the wicked deserve, and wicked people who get what the righteous deserve" — Qoheleth’s identical observation.
  • Eccl 9:2"The same destiny overtakes all" — the same collapse of moral distinction.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
21:17–18

"How often is the lamp of the wicked snuffed out? How often does calamity come upon them?" Job throws Bildad’s own image (18:5) back. The implied answer: not often.

21:19–21

"It is said, ‘God stores up the punishment for their children.’ Let him repay the wicked, so that they themselves will experience it!" Delayed justice is not justice. The wicked person should bear their own punishment.

21:23–26

"One person dies in full vigour... another dies in bitterness of soul, never having enjoyed anything good. Side by side they lie in the dust, and worms cover them both." Death equalises. Prosperity and misery lead to the same worms.

21:34

"So how can you console me with your nonsense? Nothing is left of your answers but falsehood!" Job’s closing verdict on the friends’ entire theological enterprise.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:23–26

Calvin: the levelling of death is a powerful argument. If the righteous and wicked share the same grave, the difference must lie elsewhere — in judgment after death, not in this life.

21:34

Calvin: Job’s verdict on the friends is harsh but earned. They have offered theology instead of compassion, theory instead of truth, and their theory is wrong.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:17

The NET: "how often" expects the answer "rarely" — turning Bildad’s confident assertion into an embarrassing question.

21:34

The NET: hebel here means "emptiness" or "delusion" — the friends’ answers are not just unhelpful but actively false.

Clines Notes

clines
21:22

Clines: "Can anyone teach knowledge to God, since he judges even the highest?" Job implies: if God judges differently from how you expect, maybe God knows something you don’t.

21:34

Clines: hebel — the friends’ answers are vapour. This is not just disagreement but dismissal. Job has weighed their theology and found it empty.

Habel Notes

habel
21:17–34

Habel: Job rests his counter-case. The prosecution’s theory (retribution) fails the empirical test. The defence’s evidence (wicked prosper, righteous suffer) is unanswerable. The friends have lost.
