Job 20

Zophar’s Second Speech: The Short Triumph of the Wicked

Verses 1–11 — “The Mirth of the Wicked Is Brief”
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

רֶגַע regaʿ — moment / instant: "The mirth of the wicked is brief (regaʿ)" (v.5). Zophar’s core claim: whatever prosperity the wicked enjoy is momentary. They may rise to the heavens but they perish like their own dung (v.7). The vulgarity is deliberate — Zophar is angry.

Context

Zophar’s second speech is driven by emotion, not argument. Job’s redeemer declaration (ch 19) has not moved him — he is “greatly disturbed” (v.2). His response: double down on retribution. The wicked prosper briefly, then collapse spectacularly. Every image is of reversal: height to nothing, feast to vomit, wealth to poverty.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 37:35–36"I have seen a wicked and ruthless man flourishing like a native tree, but he soon passed away" — the same brief-prosperity theology.
  • Ps 73:18–20"You place them on slippery ground; you cast them down to ruin" — sudden reversal of the wicked.

"His children must make amends to the poor... the youthful vigour that fills his bones will lie with him in the dust." The wicked’s children inherit their punishment.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:5

Calvin: the observation that wicked prosperity is brief is often true. But “often” is not “always,” and Zophar treats it as a universal law.

20:7

Calvin: the dung comparison reveals Zophar’s contempt. He is no longer counselling — he is expressing disgust.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:5

The NET: regaʿ means a blink, an instant. Zophar’s point: wicked prosperity is measured in moments, not years.

20:7

The NET notes the comparison to dung is deliberately degrading — the wicked’s ultimate destiny is refuse.

Clines Notes

clines
20:2–7

Clines: Zophar’s agitation shows. His speech lacks the structure of Eliphaz’s or even Bildad’s. He is reacting emotionally to Job’s audacity in claiming a redeemer.

20:7

Clines: "perish like their own dung" — the vulgarity is unprecedented in the dialogue. Zophar has descended from theology to insult.

Habel Notes

habel
20:1–11

Habel: Zophar’s second speech is the prosecution’s desperate response to Job’s redeemer claim. Unable to refute it, he reasserts the retribution principle with greater force.

Verses 12–29 — Wealth Consumed; God’s Wrath
121314151617181920212223242526272829

Hebrew Word Study

חֵלֶק ḥéleq — portion / allotment / inheritance: "Such is the lot (ḥéleq) God allots the wicked" (v.29). The word ḥéleq means the portion assigned by God — the inheritance the wicked receive. For Zophar, suffering IS the wicked person’s divinely appointed share. The logic is circular: if you suffer, suffering is your allotment, which proves you’re wicked.

Context

Grotesque imagery dominates: the wicked person’s food turns to venom in his stomach (v.14), he vomits up riches (v.15), sucked cobra poison (v.16), cannot enjoy rivers of honey (v.17), must give back unenjoyable earnings (v.18). Then divine wrath: fire consumes his tent (v.26), the heavens expose his guilt (v.27), flood waters carry off his house (v.28). Every image is of consumption reversed: what was swallowed is vomited back.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 12:20"This very night your life will be demanded from you" — Jesus’ parable echoes Zophar’s theology of sudden reversal.
  • Prov 20:17"Food gained by fraud tastes sweet, but one ends up with gravel" — the proverbial basis for Zophar’s imagery.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
20:12–16

The wicked’s enjoyment of evil is like sweet food that becomes poison: "though evil is sweet in his mouth... his food will turn sour in his stomach; it will become the venom of serpents."

20:20–22

"He will have no respite from his craving... in the midst of plenty, distress will overtake him." The paradox: abundance without satisfaction. Full stomach, empty soul.

20:27–29

"The heavens will expose his guilt; the earth will rise up against him. A flood will carry off his house. Such is the lot God allots the wicked." Creation itself conspires against the wicked. Heaven and earth testify.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:15

Calvin: “God will make him vomit up riches” — the image is powerful: ill-gotten wealth cannot be retained. But Calvin notes Zophar offers no evidence that Job’s wealth was ill-gotten.

20:29

Calvin: “such is the lot God allots the wicked” — Zophar pronounces sentence. He has judged his friend without trial, without evidence, without mercy.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:14–16

The NET: the food/poison/venom progression uses three different words for toxin — escalating lethality. The metaphor is of slow poisoning from within.

20:29

The NET: ḥéleq is a technical term for the divinely assigned portion. Zophar claims to know what God has allotted Job.

Clines Notes

clines
20:12–20

Clines: the vomiting imagery is sustained and visceral. Zophar describes the wicked’s entire life as a meal that cannot be digested. Everything consumed must be returned.

20:29

Clines: the closing verdict is pure retribution theology in summary form. This is Zophar’s last word — he will not speak again. His theology has reached its limit.

Habel Notes

habel
20:12–29

Habel: Zophar presents his final evidence: the wicked always lose everything. But his own speech undermines the prosecution — if the wicked always fall, why hasn’t Job been shown to be wicked by any specific evidence?
