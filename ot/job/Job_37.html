Job 37

Elihu: The Storm Arrives

Verses 1–13 — “God’s Voice Thunders”
12345678910111213

Hebrew Word Study

קוֹל qōl — voice / sound / thunder: "God’s voice (qōl) thunders in marvellous ways" (v.5). The same word for voice and thunder. When God speaks in the storm, nature and revelation merge. The thunder IS God’s voice — not a metaphor but an identity.

Context

Elihu’s final speech climaxes in the storm itself. His heart pounds (v.1), thunder rolls (v.2–5), snow and rain fall (v.6), animals take shelter (v.8), the tempest comes (v.9), ice forms (v.10), clouds scatter lightning (v.11). The storm is both meteorological event and theophanic prelude. Elihu is describing what he sees and hears — and what he sees and hears is God approaching.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 29:3–4"The voice of the LORD is over the waters... the voice of the LORD is powerful" — the thunder psalm.
  • Exod 19:16–19"Thunder and lightning, with a thick cloud over the mountain" — the Sinai theophany, the template for all storm-theophanies.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
37:1–2

"At this my heart pounds and leaps from its place. Listen! Listen to the roar of his voice." Elihu is physically affected by the approaching storm. The theology has become experience.

37:5–6

"God’s voice thunders in marvellous ways; he does great things beyond our understanding. He says to the snow, ‘Fall on the earth,’ and to the rain shower, ‘Be a mighty downpour.’" God commands weather as a general commands an army.

37:8–10

"The animals take cover; they remain in their dens. The tempest comes from its chamber, the cold from the driving winds. The breath of God produces ice." Even animals respond to God’s power. The storm shuts down all activity.

37:11–13

"He loads the clouds with moisture; he scatters his lightning through them... whether for correction, or for his land, or for love, he sends it." Three purposes of the storm: correction, provision, love. This trinity of purposes applies to suffering as well.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
37:5

Calvin: "beyond our understanding" is the proper conclusion of all theology. Every systematic statement about God ends here: there is more that we do not know than what we do.

37:13

Calvin: "for correction, or for his land, or for love" — God’s actions have multiple possible purposes. The friends assumed only correction. Elihu opens the categories.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
37:5

The NET: qōl (voice) is the standard word for both human voice and thunder. The ambiguity is theological: God’s speech and nature’s roar are the same event.

37:10

The NET: "breath of God produces ice" — God’s breath (neshamâ) created life (Gen 2:7) and also creates ice. The same divine energy takes opposite forms.

Clines Notes

clines
37:5

Clines: "does great things beyond our understanding" is the chapter’s key phrase. Elihu admits the limit. He has been explaining God for four chapters; now he concedes God is beyond explanation. The admission is his wisest moment.

37:13

Clines: the three purposes — correction, land (provision), and love (hesed) — are Elihu’s final theological contribution. Not all storms are punishment. Some feed the earth. Some are expressions of love. The same may be true of suffering.

Habel Notes

habel
37:1–13

Habel: Elihu’s speech dissolves into witness. He is no longer arguing but testifying to what he sees: the storm is coming. The advocate yields the floor to the judge.

Verses 14–24 — “Out of the North He Comes in Golden Splendour”
1415161718192021222324

Hebrew Word Study

זָהָב zāhāb — gold / golden splendour: "Out of the north God comes in golden splendour (zāhāb); he comes in awesome majesty" (v.22). The final image before the whirlwind. God approaches from the north — the traditional direction of divine appearance in Canaanite and Israelite theology — in blinding golden light. Elihu’s last words describe the arrival. The next voice will be God’s.

Context

Elihu’s closing questions anticipate God’s: "Do you know how God controls the clouds? Do you know how the lightning flashes?" (vv.15–16). These are warm-up questions for the 77 questions God will ask in chapters 38–41. Elihu’s final sentence: "The Almighty is beyond our reach and exalted in power; in his justice and great righteousness, he does not oppress" (v.23). Then silence. Then the whirlwind.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezek 1:4"A windstorm coming out of the north — an immense cloud with flashing lightning and surrounded by brilliant light" — Ezekiel’s theophany parallels Elihu’s description.
  • Hab 3:3–4"His glory covered the heavens... his splendour was like the sunrise" — the divine approach.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
37:14–18

"Listen to this, Job; stop and consider God’s wonders. Do you know how God controls the clouds and makes his lightning flash?" Elihu’s questions foreshadow God’s. Both ask Job to contemplate nature as evidence of divine wisdom.

37:19–20

"Tell us what we should say to him; we cannot draw up our case because of our darkness." Elihu admits human inadequacy: we cannot prepare a case against God.

37:22–24

"Out of the north God comes in golden splendour; he comes in awesome majesty. The Almighty is beyond our reach... in his justice and great righteousness, he does not oppress. Therefore, people revere him." Elihu’s final words. The conclusion: reverence, not argument. Then the storm breaks, and God speaks.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
37:22

Calvin: the golden splendour from the north is the Shekinah glory approaching. Elihu sees it before Job does. His speeches, for all their faults, have turned Job’s face toward the horizon where God appears.

37:23–24

Calvin: "in his justice and great righteousness, he does not oppress" is Elihu’s parting theological statement. God will confirm the first part (justice and righteousness) while redefining what it means. The whirlwind is the definition.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
37:22

The NET: zāhāb (gold) here means golden light, not the metal. The storm clears from the north and the sky blazes with God’s approaching glory.

37:24

The NET: Elihu’s final word is about reverence. This is the same posture the wisdom hymn demanded (28:28): "the fear of the LORD, that is wisdom." The circle closes.

Clines Notes

clines
37:22

Clines: "out of the north he comes in golden splendour" is the book’s most beautiful transition. Elihu describes what he literally sees: a golden storm front approaching from the north. Behind it, or within it, is God. The next chapter opens: "Then the LORD spoke out of the storm."

37:24

Clines: Elihu’s last word is "he does not regard any who are wise in their own conceit." The final dig at Job. But it is also Elihu’s own undoing: he too has been wise in his own conceit for four chapters.

Habel Notes

habel
37:14–24

Habel: Elihu’s final words are stage directions. He describes the approach of the judge. The curtain rises. The human speeches are over. "Then the LORD answered Job out of the storm."
