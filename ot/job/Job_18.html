Job 18

Bildad’s Second Speech: The Fate of the Wicked

Verses 1–21 — Terrors, Snares, Disease, Death
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

נֵר nēr — lamp / light: "The lamp of the wicked is snuffed out" (v.5). The lamp represents life, prosperity, and hope. Bildad says the wicked’s lamp goes dark — their apparent blessing is temporary.

Context

Bildad opens aggressively: “When will you end these speeches?” (v.2). He then launches into the imagery of the wicked’s destruction: their lamp is snuffed, their steps are narrowed, their own schemes trip them up. Snares, traps, and nets — the wicked are caught by their own devices.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 13:9"The lamp of the wicked is snuffed out" — the same image Bildad draws on.

18:5–10

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:5–7

Calvin: the lamp imagery is proverbial truth. The wicked’s prosperity does end. But Bildad applies it specifically to Job, which is a misapplication.

Verses 11–21 — The King of Terrors
1112131415161718192021

Hebrew Word Study

בַלְהוֹת balhōṭ — terrors / sudden horror: "Terrors (balhōṭ) startle him on every side and dog his every step" (v.11). Bildad’s speech is a single sustained portrait of the wicked person’s destruction. The terrors are comprehensive: physical, social, familial. The wicked person loses everything — and Bildad intends Job to see himself in the mirror.

מָלֵך בַלָּהוֹת melek̅ balhōṭ — king of terrors: "The king of terrors (melek̅ balhōṭ)" (v.14) — a personification of death itself. Death is the ultimate monarch before whom all others bow. The phrase may allude to Mot, the Canaanite god of death, or Nergal, the Mesopotamian ruler of the underworld.

Context

Bildad’s second speech is shorter and harsher than his first. No appeal to tradition this time — just a relentless catalogue of the wicked person’s destruction. The structure: terrors pursue (vv.5–12), disease consumes (vv.13–14), tent destroyed (vv.15–16), name erased (vv.17–18), no offspring survive (v.19), all who see it are appalled (vv.20–21).

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 49:14"Like sheep they are destined for the grave, and death will be their shepherd" — death as ruler over the wicked.
  • Prov 13:9"The lamp of the wicked is snuffed out" — the same image Bildad uses (v.5).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
18:1–4

"When will you end these speeches?" Bildad is exasperated. He accuses Job of treating the friends like animals.

18:5–7

"The lamp of a wicked man is snuffed out; the flame of his fire stops burning." The lamp image: the wicked’s prosperity is a candle that will be extinguished.

18:11—15

Terrors on every side, disease eating his skin, marched before the king of terrors, sulphur scattered on his dwelling. The imagery is apocalyptic in its comprehensiveness.

18:16–21

Roots dry up below, branches wither above. No offspring, no survivor. His name vanishes. Everyone is appalled. Bildad ends: "Surely such is the dwelling of an evil man; such is the place of one who does not know God." Direct accusation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:14

Calvin: the “king of terrors” is death personified. Bildad uses the image to frighten Job into repentance. But Job has already said he longs for death (ch 3). The threat has no power over someone who welcomes its fulfilment.

18:21

Calvin: “one who does not know God” is the harshest charge a believer can make against another. Bildad has crossed from theological disagreement to personal damnation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
18:13

The NET: “the firstborn of death” devours his skin — possibly a reference to a particularly virulent disease, personified as death’s offspring.

18:14

The NET: “king of terrors” may allude to the ANE death-deity. The phrase occurs nowhere else in the OT.

Clines Notes

clines
18:5–21

Clines: Bildad’s speech is notable for what it lacks: argument. There is no theology, no reasoning, no appeal to evidence. It is pure assertion — a verbal avalanche aimed at burying Job.

18:21

Clines: “the place of one who does not know God” is Bildad’s final verdict. He has moved from “does God pervert justice?” (8:3) to an outright accusation that Job does not know God.
