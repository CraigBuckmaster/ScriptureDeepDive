Job 36

Elihu’s Fourth Speech: The Storm Approaches

Verses 1–15 — “God Uses Suffering to Open Ears”
123456789101112131415

Hebrew Word Study

גָלָה gālâ — to reveal / uncover / open: "He opens (gālâ) their ears to correction" (v.10). The pedagogy theme from chapter 33 returns. God uses suffering to unclog deaf ears. The afflicted are students; pain is the teacher; the lesson is humility. If they listen, they spend their days in prosperity (v.11). If not, they perish (v.12).

Context

Elihu’s fourth and longest speech. He begins with his strongest theology: God is mighty but does not despise anyone (v.5). He disciplines the righteous to keep them from arrogance (vv.7–9). He opens ears through suffering (v.10). Those who listen are restored; those who refuse perish (vv.11–12). "He delivers the afflicted by their affliction; he opens their ears by adversity" (v.15) — the summary of Elihu’s entire theology.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 119:71"It was good for me to be afflicted so that I might learn your decrees" — the Psalmist’s version of Elihu’s pedagogy.
  • Heb 12:11"No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but later it produces a harvest of righteousness" — the NT development.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
36:5–6

"God is mighty, but despises no one; he is mighty, and firm in his purpose." Elihu’s opening is his best: God’s power is not contempt. Divine strength is purposeful, not arbitrary.

36:8–10

"If people are bound in chains, held fast by cords of affliction, he tells them what they have done — that they have sinned arrogantly. He makes them listen to correction." Suffering as divine communication: the chains are not punishment but pedagogy.

36:11–12

"If they obey and serve him, they will spend the rest of their days in prosperity... but if they do not listen, they will perish." The if-then structure: response determines outcome.

36:15

"He delivers the afflicted by their affliction, and opens their ears by adversity." The summary: affliction itself is the delivery vehicle. Suffering is not the obstacle to God but the means of God.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
36:5

Calvin: "mighty but despises no one" is crucial. God’s power is not contemptuous. The friends implied that human suffering doesn’t matter to God; Elihu says it matters enough for God to use it as a teaching tool.

36:15

Calvin: "delivers by affliction" is the theology of the cross in seed form. God’s deliverance comes through suffering, not around it. This is Elihu’s closest approach to the gospel.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
36:10

The NET: gālâ means to uncover, expose, reveal. God uncovers ears that were stopped — the suffering removes the blockage.

36:15

The NET: the paradox is deliberate: affliction IS the deliverance, not the obstacle to it. The means and the end are the same.

Clines Notes

clines
36:15

Clines: "delivers the afflicted BY their affliction" is Elihu’s theological centre. Not despite suffering but through it. The instrument of distress is the instrument of rescue. This is genuinely profound — and partially correct.

36:5

Clines: "mighty but despises no one" corrects Bildad’s humanity-as-worms theology (25:6). Elihu dignifies the sufferer even while insisting on divine purpose in suffering.

Habel Notes

habel
36:1–15

Habel: Elihu’s final argument is his strongest. Suffering is not evidence of guilt (friends) or evidence of injustice (Job) but evidence of divine attention. God afflicts because he cares enough to teach.

Verses 16–33 — The Storm Builds: God in Thunder
161718192021222324252627282930313233

Hebrew Word Study

רַעַם raʿam — thunder: "God’s voice thunders (raʿam) in marvellous ways; he does great things beyond our understanding" (37:5, previewed here). The storm imagery begins in 36:26 and builds through chapter 37. Elihu is not just arguing; he is narrating a physical storm that arrives as he speaks. The theophany approaches.

Context

Elihu’s speech transitions from theology to meteorology. He describes God’s mastery over weather: rain (vv.27–28), clouds (v.29), lightning (v.30–32), thunder (v.33). The description becomes increasingly vivid and present-tense. Scholars suggest Elihu is describing an actual storm building on the horizon as he speaks — the very storm from which God will speak in chapter 38. Elihu’s words bridge the human dialogue and the divine theophany.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 29:3–9"The voice of the LORD is over the waters; the God of glory thunders" — the thunder psalm that Elihu’s imagery parallels.
  • 1 Kgs 19:11–12The LORD passes by in wind, earthquake, fire — and then a gentle whisper. The theophany tradition.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
36:22–26

"God is exalted in his power. Who is a teacher like him?... How great is God — beyond our understanding!" Elihu’s doxology builds toward the whirlwind.

36:27–28

"He draws up the drops of water, which distil as rain to the streams; the clouds pour down their moisture and abundant showers fall." The water cycle described with remarkable precision.

36:29–33

"See how he scatters his lightning about him, bathing the depths of the sea. This is the way he governs the nations and provides food in abundance." Weather as governance: the same God who sends rain also rules nations.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
36:26

Calvin: "how great is God — beyond our understanding" is the right posture before the whirlwind. Elihu prepares Job (and the reader) for an encounter that will transcend all previous theology.

36:27–28

Calvin: the water-cycle description is remarkably accurate for the ancient world. Elihu perceives God’s wisdom in nature — the same perception God will demand of Job in chapter 38.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
36:27–28

The NET: the evaporation-condensation-precipitation cycle is described in terms consistent with modern meteorology. Whether the poet understood the physics or simply observed the pattern is debated.

36:33

The NET: the final verse is textually very difficult. It may mean "even the cattle know the approaching storm" — nature perceives what humans miss.

Clines Notes

clines
36:26–33

Clines: the storm description is the bridge between human speech and divine speech. Elihu describes the approaching tempest from which God will speak. His role is that of a warm-up act — preparing the audience for the main event.

36:33

Clines: the final line is difficult but may mean: the thunder announces what is coming. Elihu’s own words have been a kind of theological thunder; now the real thunder arrives.

Habel Notes

habel
36:16–33

Habel: Elihu’s speech merges with the approaching storm. The advocate’s closing argument dissolves into the judge’s entrance. The theophany is at the door.
