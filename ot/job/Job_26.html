Job 26

Job’s Reply: God’s Power over Chaos

Verses 1–4 — “How You Have Helped the Powerless!”
1234

Hebrew Word Study

מַה mah — what / how: "How (mah) you have helped the powerless! How you have saved the arm that is feeble!" (v.2). Pure sarcasm. Job mocks the friends’ entire contribution: you came to help and you failed. Your theology strengthened no one.

Context

Job opens with biting sarcasm directed at Bildad. The four questions (vv.2–4) all expect the answer "not at all." Who gave you these words? Whose spirit spoke through you? The implication: your wisdom came from nowhere divine.

Cross-Reference

  • Job 13:4"Worthless physicians, all of you!" — Job’s earlier verdict, now reinforced.

Verses 5–14 — God Hangs the Earth on Nothing
567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

תֹהוּ tōhû — void / formlessness / emptiness: "He stretches out the north over the void (tōhû) and hangs the earth on nothing" (v.7). Tōhû is the chaos-word from Genesis 1:2 — formless and void. God’s creative power is displayed in his mastery over primordial chaos. He does not eliminate chaos; he governs it.

רַהַב rahab — Rahab / sea monster / chaos: "By his power he churned up the sea; by his understanding he cut Rahab to pieces" (v.12). Rahab is the mythological sea monster representing primordial chaos. God defeats chaos not once but continually. The creation is sustained by ongoing divine combat.

Context

Job delivers his own doxology of divine power — and it surpasses anything the friends said. The dead tremble beneath the waters (v.5), Sheol is naked before God (v.6), God hangs the earth on nothing (v.7), wraps up the waters in clouds (v.8), covers the face of the moon (v.9), marks the horizon between light and darkness (v.10). This is creation theology at its most magnificent. Job’s closing line: "And these are but the outer fringe of his works; how faint the whisper we hear of him! Who then can understand the thunder of his power?" (v.14). Everything the friends said about God’s power was a fraction of the truth. Job knows more of God’s power than they do — and is still baffled by his suffering.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 1:2"The earth was formless and void (tōhû)" — the same chaos-word.
  • Ps 104:2–9"He wraps himself in light... he set the earth on its foundations" — the creation hymn Job echoes.
  • Col 1:17"In him all things hold together" — the NT answer to how the earth hangs on nothing.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
26:5–6

"The dead are in deep anguish, those beneath the waters. The realm of the dead is naked before God; Destruction lies uncovered before him." Even Sheol cannot hide from God. His knowledge penetrates death itself.

26:7

"He stretches out the north over empty space; he suspends the earth over nothing." The most scientifically prescient verse in the OT — the earth suspended in space, held by nothing visible.

26:12–13

"By his power he churned up the sea; by his understanding he cut Rahab to pieces. By his breath the skies became fair; his hand pierced the gliding serpent." God defeats chaos: sea monster, serpent, primordial disorder — all subdued.

26:14

"And these are but the outer fringe of his works; how faint the whisper we hear of him!" Job’s conclusion: everything we know of God is a whisper. The thunder of his power is beyond comprehension. This verse anticipates the whirlwind speeches (ch 38–41).

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
26:7

Calvin: the earth on nothing — Calvin takes this as evidence of God’s sustaining power. The universe requires continuous divine maintenance, not just initial creation.

26:14

Calvin: "how faint the whisper" — all theology is a whisper. The full thunder of God awaits the eschaton. Job has more theological humility in this one verse than the friends showed in their combined speeches.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
26:7

The NET: "nothing" (bəlî-mâ) is emphatic — absolutely nothing. The earth has no visible support.

26:12

The NET: Rahab is a mythological sea monster symbolising chaos. God’s defeat of Rahab represents his ongoing mastery over disorder.

Clines Notes

clines
26:7

Clines: "hangs the earth on nothing" is remarkable for its cosmological accuracy. Ancient cosmologies typically placed the earth on pillars, turtles, or water. Job’s poet knew better.

26:14

Clines: "the outer fringe" — literally the edges, the hem of his garment. What we know of God is like touching the hem of a king’s robe in a dark room. The king is there; we perceive almost nothing of him.

Habel Notes

habel
26:5–14

Habel: Job presents his own testimony about God’s power. It exceeds anything the friends said. The irony: the man who accuses God of injustice knows God’s power better than his defenders do.
