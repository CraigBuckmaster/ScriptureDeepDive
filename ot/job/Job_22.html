Job 22

Eliphaz’s Third Speech: Direct Accusation

Verses 1–11 — Fabricated Charges
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

חָבַל ḥābal — to take a pledge / to bind: "You demanded security (ḥābal) from your relatives for no reason; you stripped people of their clothing, leaving them naked" (v.6). Eliphaz invents specific sins. Having failed to convince Job through theology, he fabricates charges: exploitation of the poor, withholding food and water, mistreating widows and orphans. None of this is supported by the narrative.

Context

Eliphaz abandons theological argument and resorts to invention. He accuses Job of specific social crimes (vv.6–9) that directly contradict Job’s character as established in chapters 1–2 and confirmed by God himself. This is the friends’ lowest point: when the theology fails, manufacture the evidence.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 58:7"Share your food with the hungry" — the social justice standard Eliphaz accuses Job of violating.
  • Matt 25:42–43"I was hungry and you gave me nothing to eat" — Jesus’ description of judgment for social neglect — the charges Eliphaz invents.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:2–4

"Can a man be of benefit to God? Can even a wise person benefit him?" Eliphaz’s opening: human righteousness has no value to God. The implication: God gains nothing from punishing or rewarding. Therefore Job’s suffering must have a cause in Job.

22:5–9

"Is not your wickedness great? Are not your sins endless? You stripped people naked, gave no water to the weary, withheld food from the hungry, sent widows away empty-handed, broke the arms of the fatherless." Fabricated charges. Every one contradicts the prologue.

22:10–11

"That is why snares are all around you, why sudden peril terrifies you." Eliphaz completes the circle: these invented sins explain Job’s suffering. The diagnosis is total fiction.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:6–9

Calvin: Eliphaz has no evidence for these charges. He deduces them from Job’s suffering: if he suffers this much, he must have sinned this specifically. The reasoning is circular and cruel.

22:5

Calvin: “are not your sins endless?” — Eliphaz has condemned his friend on the basis of a theological system, not evidence. This is the danger of all theological systems applied without grace.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:6

The NET: ḥābal means to take a pledge as security for a debt. Eliphaz accuses Job of exploiting his relatives through predatory lending.

22:9

The NET: "you broke the arms of the fatherless" is a metaphor for removing their only support. Eliphaz accuses Job of the most vulnerable forms of oppression.

Clines Notes

clines
22:6–9

Clines: the fabricated charges are Eliphaz’s most desperate move. When theology fails, invent evidence. The friends’ system requires Job to be guilty; since he isn’t, they must create guilt.

22:5

Clines: “Is not your wickedness great?” The question expects “yes.” Eliphaz has moved from “perhaps you sinned” (ch 4) to “your sins are endless.” The escalation is the measure of his desperation.

Habel Notes

habel
22:1–11

Habel: the prosecution, having lost on the evidence, resorts to fabrication. Eliphaz invents crimes that fit the punishment. This is judicial corruption — precisely what Job accused God of (9:24).

Verses 12–30 — “Submit to God and Be at Peace”
12131415161718192021222324252627282930

Hebrew Word Study

שָׁלוֹם shālōm — peace / wholeness: "Submit to God and be at peace (shālōm) with him" (v.21). Eliphaz’s final appeal. The promise of shalom through submission. If Job were guilty, this would be good advice. Since he isn’t, it is a demand to confess to crimes he didn’t commit.

Context

After the fabricated charges, Eliphaz returns to the conditional promise: repent and be restored. His closing is beautiful as theology and devastating as pastoral care. “Assign your nuggets to the dust... then the Almighty will be your gold” (vv.24–25). Eliphaz imagines a restored Job who has surrendered wealth for God. The vision is attractive. The premise is false.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 55:6–7"Seek the LORD while he may be found" — the same call to repentance, but in Isaiah the speaker has authority; Eliphaz does not.
  • Jas 4:8"Come near to God and he will come near to you" — the NT version of Eliphaz’s counsel, valid in its context.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:12–14

"Is not God in the heights of heaven? Yet you say, ‘What does God know?’" Eliphaz misquotes Job. Job never said God doesn’t know — he said God doesn’t care.

22:21–25

"Submit to God and be at peace... assign your nuggets to the dust, and the Almighty will be your gold." The most seductive promise yet: trade earthly wealth for divine relationship. Beautiful — if the premise were right.

22:27–30

"You will pray to him, and he will hear you... he will deliver even those who are not innocent." The last line is accidentally prophetic: God WILL deliver Job, who IS innocent. Eliphaz speaks better than he knows.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:21

Calvin: “submit to God and be at peace” is excellent advice in the right context. But telling an innocent sufferer to submit to false charges is not peace — it is injustice.

22:30

Calvin: Eliphaz unknowingly prophesies. Job WILL intercede for the friends (42:8–10), and God WILL deliver them because of Job’s prayer. The irony is complete.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:21

The NET: "submit" (sakan) means to be familiar with, to be at peace with. Eliphaz calls for restored relationship, not mere compliance.

22:30

The NET: this verse is textually difficult. Some read "he delivers the innocent" vs "he delivers even the not-innocent." Both readings point to grace exceeding strict justice.

Clines Notes

clines
22:21

Clines: the call to submit is Eliphaz’s last word in the book. He will not speak again. His entire contribution reduces to: repent. The theology never evolved.

22:30

Clines: "he will deliver even those who are not innocent" may be Eliphaz’s most truthful line — delivered accidentally. God’s grace does extend beyond the innocent. But Eliphaz means it as: even YOUR uncleanness can be pardoned if you submit.

Habel Notes

habel
22:12–30

Habel: the prosecution’s final closing argument. Eliphaz appeals for a plea deal: confess and receive mercy. But Job has nothing to confess. The prosecution has failed.
