Genesis 46

The Descent to Egypt; Jacob Reunited with Joseph

Hebrew Word Study

al-tira al-tira — do not be afraid: V.3: God's reassurance to Jacob at Beersheba echoes the "do not be afraid" formula spoken to Abraham (15:1) and Isaac (26:24). The fear of leaving the promised land for Egypt is real -- Egypt represents the world, slavery, danger. The divine word meets the fear at the threshold: I am going with you, and I will bring you back.

shivim shivim — seventy: V.27: the number seventy carries covenantal weight in the OT. Seventy nations in the Table of Nations (Gen 10); seventy elders of Israel (Exod 24:1); seventy years of exile (Jer 25:11). The seventy who go down to Egypt become the nation that comes up. The number is a promise embedded in a count.

Historical Context

The descent to Egypt is the most significant migration in Genesis — more significant than Abraham's move from Ur or even from Haran, because this migration will define Israel for the next four centuries. God appears to Jacob at Beersheba, the southernmost point before the Sinai wilderness, to confirm that the descent is divinely sanctioned. The genealogical list of the seventy who descend with Jacob is the most complete family census of Israel in the OT before the Exodus numbering.

Context

Genesis 46 opens the Egypt section with the theological frame that governs all that follows: this descent is under divine commission, not merely human desperation. The Beersheba vision (vv.1-4) is the pivot: Jacob hesitates at the border of the promised land; God appears and authorises the journey. The genealogy (vv.8-27) is not a bureaucratic list but a theological statement: the seventy who descend are the seed of the promise on the verge of becoming a nation.

Cross-Reference

Places

Beersheba
Southern gateway to Canaan / Egypt route
Jacob stops at Beersheba on the way to Egypt — the southern anchor of the Promised Land. God appears to him here for the last time in Canaan: "I am God, the God of your father. Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt." The last theophany in the Promised Land occurs at its southern gate.
Goshen / Egypt
Nile Delta; eastern frontier of Egypt
Goshen is the land assigned to Israel in Egypt — fertile, in the eastern Nile Delta, positioned close to the border. It is both inside Egypt (under Pharaoh's administration) and at its edge (allowing eventual exit). The geography of Goshen is the geography of tolerated foreignness.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
46:1-4

God's appearance to Jacob at Beersheba is the last direct divine speech to Jacob in the narrative. "I am God, the God of your father. Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt, for I will make you into a great nation there." Three promises: presence ("I will go down to Egypt with you"), return ("I will surely bring you back again"), and consolation ("Joseph's own hand will close your eyes"). The descent is divinely commanded and divinely accompanied.

46:3

The fear God addresses — "do not be afraid to go down to Egypt" — reflects Jacob's awareness of the danger of Egypt. Abraham had been warned not to go to Egypt (implicitly, by the narrative of ch.12). The Abrahamic covenant was associated with Canaan. To leave Canaan is to risk losing the covenant geography. God's specific reassurance that the descent is his will is necessary precisely because the departure from the land of promise requires explicit divine warrant.

46:26-27

The number seventy who descended with Jacob is theologically significant. Seventy is the number of the nations in Genesis 10 (the Table of Nations). Israel descends as a mirror of the nations — seventy into Egypt, matching the seventy of the world. The parallel is not incidental: Israel in Egypt will become the nation through whom all the seventy nations will eventually be blessed.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
46:1

Jacob's stop at Beersheba before the descent is significant. Sarna: "Beersheba was associated with Isaac (26:23-25) and Abraham (21:31-33) — it is a major patriarchal covenant site. Jacob pauses here not merely logistically but theologically: he is at the southern frontier of the promised land, about to cross out of it for the first time since his return from Haran. He offers sacrifice and receives divine assurance before the crossing."

46:4

The divine promise "I will bring you back again" is fulfilled not in Jacob's lifetime but through his descendants — the Exodus and the conquest. Sarna: "The 'bringing back' is the Exodus. God is promising Jacob not a personal return but a national one. The covenant with Abraham — the land, the people, the blessing — will be fulfilled through the Egyptian sojourn, not despite it."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
46:1-4

The appearance of God at Beersheba creates a formal parallel with the earlier covenant appearances at the beginning of major journeys. Alter: "Jacob received a divine appearance at Bethel when he left Canaan for Haran (28:12-15); he received one at the Jabbok when he returned (32:24-32). Now, at the third major transition of his life — the descent to Egypt — he receives another. The pattern of divine appearance at life's transitions is consistent."

46:4

The promise "Joseph's own hand will close your eyes" is one of the most tenderly personal details in all the divine speeches of Genesis. Alter: "God does not speak abstractly of covenant and promise here — he speaks of the specific, embodied comfort of a beloved son's hand at the moment of death. The divine promise is simultaneously cosmic (a great nation) and intimate (Joseph's hand)."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
46:2-3

Calvin on God's encouragement: "God here appears to Jacob, both to animate his mind for so great a journey, and to give him the assurance that his going into Egypt was not contrary to the purpose of God. The command not to fear was necessary because Jacob might have reasoned: God gave this land to my fathers and to me; if I leave it I leave the covenant. God specifically addresses this fear by promising his presence in Egypt."

46:27

Calvin on the number seventy: "Though the Lord permitted his people to go down into Egypt, he wished them to remain as a separate nation, not to be absorbed into the Egyptian population. The genealogy of seventy serves this purpose: it is a census of a distinct people, counted and named, whose identity is to be maintained through the Egyptian sojourn. They go as a family; they will emerge as a nation."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
46:3

The NET note on "do not be afraid to go down to Egypt" addresses the geographical theology of the descent. The note: "The promised land was the geographical expression of the covenant. To leave it was to leave the covenant geography. God's specific reassurance — 'I am El, the God of your father' — grounds the permission in the covenant relationship itself. The covenant does not end at the border of Canaan; the covenant God travels with his people."

46:27

The NET note on the number seventy discusses the textual tradition: the MT has 70, the LXX has 75 (cited in Acts 7:14). The note surveys the counting methods that produce each number. The note also observes the significance of 70 as the number of nations in Genesis 10, suggesting the parallel between Israel as a 70-person family entering Egypt and the 70 nations of the earth is intentional.

Verses 28–34 — Reunion with Joseph; The Goshen Strategy
28293031323334

Hebrew Word Study

גֹּשֶׁן Goshen — Goshen: V.28: The region of Goshen in the northeastern Nile Delta is identified as the settlement area for Jacob's family. The region is suitable for grazing (v.34) and is geographically separated from the Egyptian population centres, allowing Israel to maintain its distinctive identity. The separation is providential: proximity to Joseph but distinctiveness from Egypt.

Historical Context

Judah is sent ahead to arrange the meeting with Joseph in Goshen — a striking detail. Judah, who once proposed selling Joseph for silver, is now the family's advance agent securing Joseph's reception. Joseph harnesses his chariot and goes to Goshen to meet his father — a mark of honour; in Egyptian protocol, the subordinate travels to the superior. The meeting of Jacob and Joseph after twenty-two years is one of the most emotionally charged reunions in Scripture. Joseph's practical advice about what to say to Pharaoh is the chapter's final movement: the family is transitioning from pastoral wanderers to Goshen residents.

Context

The reunion of Jacob and Joseph (vv.28-30) is the emotional climax of the Joseph cycle. Twenty-two years of grief resolved in a single embrace. Jacob's words are the words of Simeon in Luke 2:29 -- "now I am ready to die, since I have seen for myself that you are still alive." The satisfaction is complete. The Goshen strategy (vv.31-34) is Joseph's practical wisdom: by identifying as shepherds (detestable to Egyptians), Israel will be allowed to live separately in Goshen, preserving their identity.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 1:7The Israelites were fruitful and multiplied greatly — the fulfilment of the "great nation" promise of 46:3 begins immediately after the descent.
  • Heb 11:21By faith Jacob, when he was dying, blessed each of Joseph's sons — the faith that began at Beersheba reaches to the deathbed.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
46:28

Judah sent ahead to Joseph to get directions to Goshen — the man who once sold Joseph now goes ahead to prepare the way for the family's reception by Joseph. The reversal is quietly noted by the narrative: no comment, no editorial irony. But the attentive reader notices: the Judah who led the proposal to sell Joseph now leads the advance party to receive him.

46:29-30

Joseph harnesses his chariot and goes to Goshen to meet Israel his father — and presents himself to him; he fell on his neck and wept on his neck a long time. Jacob said to Joseph, "Now I can die, since I have seen your face and know that you are still alive." The reunion is rendered with the same restraint as the revelation in ch.45: five verbs, one speech, two truths — the reunion is real; Jacob is ready to die content.

46:33-34

Joseph's instruction about how to answer Pharaoh — "say that your servants have always been shepherds" — is both practical and significant. Egyptians regarded shepherds as offensive (v.34). Locating the family in Goshen, at the edge of the Delta, preserves their separateness from Egyptian society. Joseph understands that the preservation of Israel's identity requires geographic separation from the culture he has successfully navigated.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
46:29

The reunion scene — Joseph presenting himself, falling on his father's neck, weeping at length — is the most extended physical contact scene in the Joseph cycle. Sarna: "Five verbs describe Joseph's approach: he harnessed his chariot, he went, he presented himself, he fell on his neck, he wept. The narrative slows for the first time since the weeping in ch.45. This is the moment Jacob has been dead for since the sons brought the blood-stained coat."

46:34

The cultural note — "all shepherds are detestable to the Egyptians" — is confirmed by Egyptian records and reliefs. Sarna: "The Egyptians' distaste for the pastoral lifestyle reflects the settled agricultural culture's disdain for nomadic peoples. The same cultural distance that made Egyptians and Hebrews eat separately (43:32) will keep Israel geographically separate in Goshen. Joseph uses Egyptian cultural prejudice as the mechanism of Israel's preservation."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
46:29-30

Jacob's "I can die now, since I have seen your face and know that you are still alive" deliberately echoes his earlier deathward statements: "I will go to my grave mourning my son" (37:35). Alter: "Jacob has said twice that he will die — once in grief (37:35), once in readiness (46:30). The first death-speech was the death of hope; the second is the death of a fulfilled man. The same idiom used for grief is now used for completion."

46:28

The detail of Judah sent ahead is the narrative's quiet final irony on Judah. Alter: "No comment is made. No connection is drawn. But the reader who has followed Judah's arc — from the proposal to sell Joseph, through the Tamar episode, through the surety and the great speech — recognises the silent symmetry. The man who facilitated Joseph's departure to Egypt is now the advance man for the family's arrival."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
46:29-30

Calvin on the reunion: "When Joseph embraces his father and weeps upon his neck, we see the full force of filial affection... After such a long period of separation, in which Jacob had mourned Joseph as dead, the meeting is the reversal of his greatest grief. And Jacob, in saying that he can now die content, shows that the dearest desire of his heart has been granted."

46:33-34

Calvin on Joseph's strategic advice: "Joseph shows the prudence of a wise man. He knows the Egyptians are proud and disdainful; he arranges that the Israelites shall live apart, so that they may be kept free from Egyptian corruptions. Providence uses even Egyptian cultural prejudice as the mechanism of covenant preservation. What the Egyptians regard as social inferiority becomes Israel's protective separation."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
46:4

The NET note on "Joseph's own hand will close your eyes" observes that this was the specific duty of the eldest son — closing the eyes of the deceased father. By promising this act to Jacob, God is promising that Joseph will outlive Jacob, be present at his death, and honour him with the closing act. The promise is fulfilled in 50:1.

46:34

The NET note on "shepherds are detestable to Egyptians" discusses the evidence from Egyptian sources. The note observes that wall paintings and administrative texts confirm the lower social status of pastoral peoples in Egyptian eyes. The note: "Joseph is not creating this prejudice; he is exploiting an existing cultural reality for a providential purpose. Israel's despised social status becomes the guarantee of their geographical and cultural separation."

Reception History

The descent as typology
The church fathers read the 70 who descend to Egypt as a type of the 70 disciples sent out by Jesus (Luke 10:1). Israel going down and being multiplied into a nation typifies the church going out and multiplying into the nations.
The Goshen strategy
Joseph's use of Egyptian prejudice to maintain Israelite distinctiveness has been cited in discussions of minority community preservation and the wisdom of accepting social separation for the sake of communal identity.

Textual Notes

46:27שִׁבְעִים — seventy persons: MT vs LXX
MT shivim nefesh — seventy persons. LXX ἑβδομήκοντα πέντε — seventy-five, counting additional descendants of Joseph (Manasseh and Ephraim's descendants). Acts 7:14 (Stephen's speech) follows the LXX: "seventy-five."
The MT-LXX variation (70 vs 75) reflects a different counting method, not a different tradition. The NT's use of the LXX number (Acts 7:14) is not an error — Stephen follows the LXX text known in his context. The theological significance (Israel as mirror of the 70 nations of Gen 10) applies to the MT count; the LXX adds generations Joseph saw in Egypt.

Scholarly Debates

God's Appearance at Beersheba: The Last Patriarchal Theophany
Covenant boundary moment
Sarna, Wenham, Hamilton
Beersheba is the last covenant site before the wilderness. God appears here to authorise a crossing that would otherwise seem to violate the covenant geography. The appearance is necessary because Jacob is crossing out of the promised land for the first time since his return from Haran.
Narrative closure
Alter
The appearance at Beersheba is the last direct divine speech to Jacob and the last patriarchal theophany in Genesis. It closes the series of Bethel/Peniel/Beersheba divine appearances that have defined Jacob's life. The covenant is complete; the patriarch can now rest.
Both readings are correct and complementary. The appearance authorises the descent (covenant geography concern) and marks the end of the patriarchal theophany series (narrative closure). The two functions together make the Beersheba appearance a pivotal moment in both the Jacob narrative and the structure of Genesis as a whole.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 46 is the pivot chapter between the patriarchal era and the Egyptian era of Israel's history. The descent is divinely commanded and divinely accompanied. The seventy who descend are a distinct covenant people — counted, named, preserved. Joseph's Goshen strategy uses cultural separation to maintain covenant identity. The reunion of Jacob and Joseph is the personal miracle within the national miracle.
