Genesis 11

The Tower of Babel; From Shem to Abram

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–9 — The Tower of Babel
123456789

Places

Plain of Shinar
Mesopotamia; region of modern Iraq
Shinar is the plain between the Tigris and Euphrates — the same region where Nimrod built his empire (10:10). The location is significant: Babel is built in the territory of the first empire-builder, fulfilling and extending the pattern established there.
Tower of Babel
Babylon; ziggurat architecture
Ancient Babylon featured massive stepped temple-towers (ziggurats) with their tops reaching toward the sky. The excavated ziggurat at Ur demonstrates the architectural form. The narrative addresses the religious logic of such structures: reaching the divine through human construction.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
11:1-4

MacArthur: The Tower of Babel narrative reveals three sinful motivations: to make a name for themselves (pride), to build a tower reaching to heaven (presumption), and to avoid being scattered (disobedience to the mandate to fill the earth). The tower is not merely architectural ambition but organised rebellion.

11:6-8

MacArthur: God's observation -- 'If as one people speaking the same language they have begun to do this, then nothing they plan to do will be impossible for them' (v.6) -- is not alarm but the recognition that united human rebellion against God can produce extraordinary evil. The confusion of language is an act of mercy that prevents greater judgment.

Hebrew Word Study

śāpāh ʾeḥāt sa-FAH e-KHAT — one language / one lip: "The whole world had one language (śāpāh ʾeḥāt) and a common speech" (v.1) — the unity of language enables the unity of purpose that makes Babel possible. Śāpāh ("lip") is the bodily organ of speech — language as the instrument of human coordination and community. The same term will be inverted at Pentecost (Acts 2), where the Spirit restores communication across language barriers not by re-imposing uniformity but by enabling each to hear in their own language — a different kind of unity.

hābāh ha-VAH — come, let us: The repeated hābāh ("come, let us") of vv.3-4 is the human voice of corporate ambition: "let us make bricks... let us build... let us make a name for ourselves." The same construction in v.7 — "Come, let us go down" — is God's response, mimicking the form of human speech to demonstrate that human plans, however unified, fall within divine purview. The ironic echo: humanity's "let us build" is met by God's "let us confuse" (v.7).

šēm SHAYM — name / reputation: The builders' goal — "let us make a name (šēm) for ourselves" (v.4) — contrasts directly with what God will promise Abraham in 12:2: "I will make your name (šēm) great." The Babel project is self-generated fame; Abraham's greatness is covenant gift. Babel seeks to secure human identity through tower and city; the covenant promises identity through divine calling and promise. The two paths to šēm — human construction vs. divine gift — are the alternatives the narrative places before every reader.

Historical Context

The Babel narrative reflects Mesopotamian ziggurat culture. The Etemenanki ("house of the foundation of heaven and earth") in Babylon may be the specific referent — a massive 7-stage ziggurat dedicated to Marduk. Cuneiform texts describe these structures as cosmic mountains connecting heaven and earth. The use of fired brick and bitumen (rather than stone and mortar, as in Canaan) is archaeologically accurate for the Mesopotamian plain, confirmed by excavations at Ur and Uruk.

Context

The Babel story concludes the primeval history (Gen 1–11) by explaining the origin of language diversity. It stands in ironic contrast to Pentecost (Acts 2), where the Spirit reverses the confusion. The "one language" echoes the pre-fall unity of creation; the tower project is humanity's corporate attempt at self-deification — a societal Adam-and-Eve rebellion.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:1-4

The Tower of Babel narrative is the narrative explanation of the linguistic diversity already described in chapter 10. Sarna: "The Table of Nations (ch.10) presupposes the dispersal; Babel (11:1-9) narrates it. The two passages are in logical order, but reversed chronological order — a common biblical technique." The tower is described as a ziggurat — the stepped temple-towers of Mesopotamia. The name "Babel" connects to Babylon; the builders are in Mesopotamia.

11:4

The dual motivation — "a tower that reaches to the heavens" and "let us make a name for ourselves lest we be scattered" — is the classic anatomy of hubris. Sarna: the first goal is vertical (reach heaven, breach the divine-human boundary); the second is horizontal (prevent dispersal, remain concentrated in defiance of the "fill the earth" mandate). Both motivations are inversions of the creation commission.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:1-4

Alter notes the irony of the narrative's opening: "the whole earth had one language" — and then immediately uses that unity for the most unified act of human rebellion. The single language is not an achievement to celebrate but a dangerous condition that concentrates human pride. The building project announces itself: "Come, let us build." The first word is an invitation — the same "come" that God used to Noah. Now humanity invites itself to build against God.

11:5

Vayered YHWH lirot — "the LORD came down to see." The divine condescension is exquisitely ironic: humanity builds a tower to reach heaven; God has to come down to see it. The tower so impressive to its builders is invisible from heaven without divine effort to notice it. The gap between human ambition and divine perspective could not be expressed more economically.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:4

The desire to "make a name" is the essence of pride — the desire for glory that belongs only to God. Calvin: "They wished to be celebrated to the utmost limits of the world, and to transmit their fame to the latest posterity. Thus insatiable is the ambition of the human mind." The tower is not primarily a building project but a naming project — the fallen human drive to establish one's own significance independent of God.

11:7

Calvin on the divine council ("Come, let us go down"): "God is not held in council with himself; but since the plural is used, he expresses something of the mystery of the Trinity. He is not speaking to angels — he speaks as the Father to the Son and Spirit." The "let us" of Babel mirrors the "let us make" of 1:26 — the Triune God acts in both creation and judgment.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:1

The NET note on "the whole earth had one language" discusses whether this means literally one language (pre-Babel) or one dialect (unified speech). The note favours the literal reading — a single human language before the confusion. The note acknowledges the linguistic and anthropological questions this raises but reads the text as a claim about a specific historical moment.

11:4

The NET note on "a tower that reaches to the heavens" observes this is an idiom for extreme height, not a claim that the tower literally reached the sky. The note identifies the building as a ziggurat — a terraced temple-tower — common in Mesopotamia. The "heavens" language may reflect the religious function of ziggurats as connections between heaven and earth, which makes the project an act of religious overreach as well as architectural ambition.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
11:10-26

MacArthur: The Shemite genealogy from Shem to Abram narrows the narrative focus from all humanity to one family. The lifespans progressively shorten compared to the pre-Flood genealogy of ch.5, reflecting the changed conditions. The genealogy ends with Terah and his three sons, focusing on Abram.

11:26-32

MacArthur: The detail that Sarai was barren (v.30) is placed before the call narrative of ch.12 to establish the central obstacle to the covenant promises. The covenant heir will be born not to natural fertility but to supernatural provision -- establishing from the beginning that the promise is God's achievement, not Abraham's.

Hebrew Word Study

tôledôt to-le-DOT — genealogy / account of generations: "This is the account of Shem's family line" (v.10) uses the tôledôt formula that structures all of Genesis — the sixth of ten. The Shemite genealogy connects the Table of Nations (ch.10) to the call of Abram (ch.12) in ten carefully counted generations, just as the genealogy of ch.5 connected Adam to Noah in ten generations. The pattern signals theological intentionality: these are not random family records but the selected chain of covenant transmission.

šānāh sha-NAH — year: The lifespans in ch.11 are shorter than ch.5 (Shem: 600 years; Terah: 205 years) but longer than post-patriarchal lifespans. Whatever their hermeneutical status, the decreasing lifespans across chs. 5, 11, and the patriarchal narratives mark a movement toward the ordinary human lifespan of Ps 90:10 ("seventy years... eighty if strong"). The reduction of lifespan is the Flood's secondary consequence: humanity is not destroyed but diminished, pressing toward the "threescore and ten" that becomes the baseline of ordinary human existence.

Arpakšad / Šelaḥ / ʿEber ar-pak-SHAD / she-LAKH / AY-ver — Arphaxad / Shelah / Eber: The genealogy from Shem to Abram traces the Semitic lineage that the Table of Nations had broadly sketched. The name Eber (v.14-17) grounds "Hebrew" identity within this post-Flood genealogy — Abraham will be called "Abraham the Hebrew" (ʿibrî, 14:13), connecting his ethnic designation to this ancestor. The genealogy is the documentary bridge that legitimises Abram's claim to be the heir of both the Noahic covenant and the pre-Flood promises to the human race.

The divine "Let us go down" (v.7) echoes "Let us make man" (1:26), suggesting a divine council. God's response is not angry destruction but dispersal — the very thing the builders feared (v.4). Alter — Literary Reading

11:10-26

The post-Babel genealogy is the most abbreviated in Genesis — no character notes, no stories, only the formulaic ages and deaths. Alter: "After the Tower of Babel, language is confused and humanity is dispersed. Sarai's barrenness is the first thing we learn about Abraham's wife. Alter notes this establishes the central narrative tension immediately: the childless couple through whom nations will be blessed. The story of Abraham begins with the announcement of its greatest obstacle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
11:27-32

MacArthur: Terah began the journey to Canaan but stopped in Haran and died there (v.31-32). The incomplete obedience of the father becomes the background against which the son's complete obedience (12:4: 'so Abram went') is highlighted. God's call accomplishes what human initiative left incomplete.

11:31

MacArthur: Ur of the Chaldeans was one of the most sophisticated cities of the ancient world. Abraham's call to leave Ur was a call to leave security, culture, and social standing. The NT (Heb 11:8) emphasises that he went 'even though he did not know where he was going' -- faith is demonstrated by movement without full information.

Hebrew Word Study

Terakh TEH-rakh — Terah: Terah's departure from Ur of the Chaldeans (v.31) begins the journey that Abraham will complete — Terah intended to go to Canaan but "settled" in Harran. Josh 24:2 records that "your ancestors, including Terah the father of Abraham... lived beyond the Euphrates River and worshipped other gods." Terah is the initiator of the journey; the call to Abraham (ch.12) is what transforms migration into covenant vocation. The destination (Canaan) is already named in v.31 — the journey of faith is already oriented before the call is given.

Ûr Kaśdîm oor kash-DEEM — Ur of the Chaldeans: Ur was one of the great Sumerian city-states — by 2000 BC a metropolis of perhaps 65,000 people, with a ziggurat (the very architectural type the Babel narrative describes), elaborate temples, and sophisticated administrative culture. Abraham's departure from Ur is a departure from one of the most cosmopolitan urban centres of the ancient world for a nomadic life in Canaan. The call to "go from your country" (12:1) is a call from urban civilisation to covenant pilgrimage.

Ḥārān kha-RAN — Harran: Harran (modern southeastern Turkey) was a major caravan city and cult centre for the moon-god Sin — the same deity worshipped at Ur. Terah's settlement there (v.31) and death there (v.32) suggests a migration that stalled, perhaps at a culturally familiar waystation. Abraham will leave Harran when his father dies (12:4; Acts 7:4), completing the journey Terah began. The delay at Harran is the narrative's way of showing that covenant completion requires divine initiative beyond mere human migration.

Historical Context

Archaeological excavations at Ur (modern Tell el-Muqayyar, Iraq) by Leonard Woolley (1920s–30s) revealed a sophisticated city of 65,000+ people with advanced art, writing, and ziggurat worship of the moon-god Nanna. The patriarchal migration from Ur to Haran to Canaan follows documented trade and migration routes of the early 2nd millennium BC. Haran (Harran) served as a major node on the Fertile Crescent trade network.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Terah
Patriarch; Father of Abram, Nahor, Haran
Initiated the journey from Ur but stopped short in Haran, dying there at 205. His name may connect to the Hebrew word for moon worship (yārēaḥ-related). He is the last patriarch of the old world and the first of the new.
Abram (Abraham)
Son of Terah; Patriarch of Israel
Introduced here without fanfare — just one more name in a genealogy. The abruptness sets up the explosive call of chapter 12. His wife Sarai is noted as barren, raising the question that will drive Genesis 12–25.
Sarai
Wife of Abram; Later Sarah
Introduced with her barrenness as the defining fact: "Sarai was barren; she had no child" (v.30). This is not incidental detail — it is the theological problem that chapter 12 onward will address.
Lot
Son of Haran; Nephew of Abram
Orphaned early (father Haran died in Ur), Lot will travel with Abram to Canaan and become a foil to Abraham's faith throughout Genesis 13–19.

Ancient Sources

Enuma Elish (Babylonian Creation Epic)
The Babylonian creation myth describes Marduk building Babylon and the Esagila temple as a cosmic center — precisely the kind of "name-making" city building that Genesis 11 critiques. The biblical account inverts the Babylonian theology: God, not Marduk, controls the ordering of humanity.
The parallel is deliberately polemical — Israel's scripture answers Babylon's self-aggrandizing mythology.
Jubilees 10:18–26 (2nd c. BC)
The Book of Jubilees elaborates the Babel story: Mastema (the adversary) assists the builders; the confusion of languages involves angels speaking different tongues to different groups.
Shows how Second Temple Judaism found the Babel story generative for angelology and the theology of the nations.
Josephus, Antiquities 1.4.3
Josephus identifies Nimrod (Gen 10:8–10) as the instigator of the Babel project, building the tower as a monument against another flood. He describes the tower as being made of baked brick with bitumen for mortar — matching the Genesis account precisely.
Josephus's retelling shows the story was read historically in the 1st century, not as myth.

Reception History

Augustine, City of God XIX
Augustine saw Babel as the foundational text for understanding the earthly city — humanity organized around self-love rather than love of God. Babylon becomes his paradigm for all human civilization oriented away from the divine.
John Calvin, Commentary on Genesis
Calvin emphasized God's providential use of the scattering: what humanity feared (dispersion) became the mechanism by which the earth was populated per Gen 1:28. Judgment and blessing intertwine.
Modern Scholarship
Claus Westermann and others see the Babel story as an etiology for language diversity with no necessary historical claim. Evangelical scholars like Kenneth Mathews and Bruce Waltke argue the narrative is historically grounded while carrying profound theological weight about human rebellion.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

Genesis 1-11: Primeval History and Historical Narrative
Historical narrative throughout
Mathews, Collins, Wenham
Genesis 1-11 is intended as historical narrative — the author presents these events as real history, which the genealogies (connecting Adam to Abraham) confirm. The genre is "primeval history" — history of a different kind than what follows, but still history.
Myth/Legend in chs. 1-11, history from ch. 12
von Rad, Brueggemann
Genesis 1-11 is "primeval history" in the form of myth/legend — theological reflection on universal human experience using ancient narrative conventions. Chapter 12 marks the transition to historical narrative with the call of Abraham.
Theological narrative with historical core
Longman, Enns, Sparks
Genesis 1-11 uses ancient Near Eastern literary conventions (similar to surrounding literature) to convey theological truths. Whether it is "historical" in the modern sense is less important than whether it is true.
The genre of Genesis 1-11 remains one of the most contested questions in OT scholarship. The genealogical connections to historical Abraham (with chronological data) support a historical framework while the literary similarities to ANE myth suggest a different kind of historical presentation than modern biography.
Historical and Literary Status of the Tower Narrative
Historical-geographical
Speiser, Wenham, Sarna
The city-tower description matches Mesopotamian ziggurats. Plausible historical memory of early Mesopotamian urbanisation and the cultural prestige of Babylon.
Aetiological
Gunkel, von Rad
An etiology explaining language diversity and the origins of Babel/Babylon. Historical kernel shaped as theological reflection on hubris.
Theological polemic
Sarna, Hamilton, Walton
Structured as counterpoint to Babylonian pride. Bab-il = "gate of God"; the narrator's bavel = "confusion." Complete theological inversion.
The three readings are complementary. Most contemporary scholarship accepts all three: historical reference point (Mesopotamian urbanism), aetiological function (language diversity), and theological purpose (indicting pride, asserting divine sovereignty).
