Genesis 23

The Death of Sarah; the Purchase of Machpelah

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–9 — Sarah Dies; Abraham Negotiates for a Burial Place
123456789

Places

Hebron / Kiriath Arba
Southern Canaan hill country
Hebron (also called Kiriath Arba) is where Sarah dies at 127 years old. Abraham is "a foreigner and stranger" here — he owns no ground in the Promised Land at this point. The death of the covenant matriarch in a land the family doesn't possess dramatises the gap between promise and possession.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
23:2

MacArthur: Sarah died at 127 years old -- the only woman whose age at death is recorded in Scripture. Abraham mourned and wept for her. The covenant man's grief is not suppressed but expressed. The weeping is followed immediately by practical action to honour her burial -- grief and responsibility together.

23:7-9

MacArthur: Abraham bows to the Hittites and requests a burial site 'among you' -- he identifies himself as 'a foreigner and stranger among you' (v.4). Despite the covenant promise of the land, Abraham lived in Canaan as an alien throughout his lifetime (Heb 11:13). His faith was in the promise, not in present possession.

Hebrew Word Study

ʾereṣ mĕgurîm EH-rets me-gu-REEM — land of sojournings: Sarah's death (v.2) in Kiriath Arba/Hebron begins the process of covenant land acquisition. The problem is stark: Abraham owns nothing — he is a ger wĕtôšāb ("stranger and sojourner," v.4) in the land God has promised him. The first legal transaction of Genesis will be for a burial ground — a small piece of the promised land, legally purchased at full price, so that covenant family can be buried in it. The burial ground is the down payment on the full land promise.

ʾadōnî a-do-NEE — my lord: The elaborate social negotiation of vv.5-16 — the "no, you honour us too much" and counter-offers of Hittite hospitality protocol — reflects the high-context culture of ancient Near Eastern land transactions. The Hittites address Abraham as "a mighty prince (nĕśîʾ ʾĕlōhîm) among us" (v.6) — literally "a prince of God," acknowledging his special status. Abraham must navigate both the social requirement of accepting hospitality and the legal necessity of outright ownership, not a gift that could be reclaimed by any heir.

šāqal sha-KAL — to weigh / to pay silver: "Abraham agreed to Ephron's terms and weighed out for him the price he had named" (v.16) — the commercial language of weighing silver is precise legal terminology. The transaction is public (witnessed at the city gate, v.18), at full market value, permanently recorded ("according to the current commercial standard"). Abraham refuses a gift specifically to prevent future legal challenges to ownership. The cave of Machpelah, legally purchased, becomes Israel's first title deed to the promised land — a covenant-in-miniature of the full land promise.

Historical Context

The legal detail of Genesis 23 closely mirrors Hittite and Mesopotamian property transaction documents. The public negotiation at the city gate, the witnesses, the careful recording of the price, and the formula "in the hearing of the sons of Heth" correspond exactly to ANE legal practice for real property transfer. Scholars like M.R. Lehmann have noted that the Hittite legal code (c. 1300 BC) required the seller to pay feudal obligations if he sold only part of his land — which explains why Ephron insists on selling the whole field (v.11), not just the cave. Abraham wisely accepts.

Context

The death of Sarah is recorded with deliberate economy — one verse (v.2) covering her death, Abraham's grief, and the transition to the business of burial. The chapter's length is not callousness but theological purpose: the real estate transaction is the point. Abraham is purchasing the first permanent title to the promised land — a small cave and a field, but the beginning of possession. He dies owning only this plot, yet died in faith.

    Verses 10–20 — The Purchase of Machpelah

    Places

    Machpelah / Cave of Machpelah
    East of Mamre, near Hebron
    Machpelah is the first piece of the Promised Land Abraham legally owns — purchased at full market price from Ephron the Hittite. The cave becomes the family tomb: Abraham, Sarah, Isaac, Rebekah, Jacob, and Leah are all buried here. The patriarchal claim on Canaan is rooted in this legally acquired burial ground.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    23:15-16

    MacArthur: The 400 shekels of silver at the going merchant rate establishes a legal, documented purchase -- not a gift or a loan. Abraham insists on paying full market value. The first piece of land in the covenant inheritance is acquired through a legally binding commercial transaction that cannot be disputed or reclaimed.

    23:19-20

    MacArthur: 'The field and the cave in it were deeded to Abraham by the Hittites as a burial site' -- the legal conclusion of the transaction. The cave of Machpelah at Hebron becomes the burial site for Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Sarah, Rebekah, and Leah (49:31; 50:13). The tiny plot of legally-owned land is the first fulfilment of the land promise.

    Hebrew Word Study

    mĕʿārāh me-a-RAH — cave: The cave (mĕʿārāh) of Machpelah will become the family burial place of the covenant line: Sarah (23:19), Abraham (25:9), Isaac and Rebekah (35:27-29), Leah (49:31), and Jacob (50:13). The cave functions as the family's covenant marker in the land — their bodies are buried there because they hold the promise of resurrection and return to the land. Jacob's insistence from Egypt that he be buried in Machpelah (49:29-32) and Joseph's anticipation of an Exodus (50:24-25) both depend on this theology: the covenant land is where covenant bodies belong.

    ʾāḥūzat qeber a-khu-ZAT KE-ver — property for a burial site / burial holding: Abraham's request is for ʾăḥûzat qeber — "a burial holding" (v.4, 9, 20). The term ʾăḥûzāh (holding/property) appears in the land legislation of Leviticus 25 as the permanent family landholding that cannot be permanently alienated. By purchasing burial ground, Abraham is establishing an ʾăḥûzāh — a permanent covenant landholding — in the land of promise before any of the larger promise is fulfilled. The burial ground is the eschatological guarantee: the covenant family will rise and possess this land.

    bĕnê Ḥēt be-NAY KHAYT — Hittites / sons of Heth: The Hittites of Hebron are listed among the Canaanite peoples whose land Israel will eventually inherit (10:15; 15:20). Their respectful treatment of Abraham — "a mighty prince among us" (v.6) — demonstrates that the covenant patriarch has earned a standing even among the resident peoples of the land. The commercial transaction at the gate of Hebron is witnessed by the very people who will ultimately be displaced by Abraham's descendants — a legal irony embedded in the covenant narrative.

      Chapter-Level Scholarship

      People of the Chapter

      Sarah
      Covenant matriarch; first to die
      The only woman in the Bible whose age at death is recorded (127 years). She is also the only woman in the Bible to have a chapter devoted to her burial — a mark of her status as covenant matriarch. Her death occasions the only piece of land Abraham legally owns.
      Abraham
      Patriarch; grieving widower; legal purchaser
      Mourns genuinely ("went in to mourn...and to weep"), then rises to handle practical necessity with legal precision. His insistence on paying full price rather than accepting a gift ensures uncontestable title — a model of integrity in commercial dealings.
      Ephron the Hittite
      Property owner; negotiator
      His initial generosity ("I give it to you") is standard ANE negotiating courtesy — not a genuine offer. When Abraham persists, Ephron names a high price. The transaction is entirely realistic and documented in accordance with Hittite legal practice.

      Ancient Sources

      Hittite Legal Code (c. 1300 BC)
      The Hittite law code required that when an entire property was sold, all feudal obligations transferred to the buyer. Ephron's insistence on selling the whole field (not just the cave) legally transferred all obligations to Abraham — his phrasing "I give it to you" followed by quoting the full price follows standard Hittite negotiating protocol exactly.
      M.R. Lehmann (1953) argued this match between Genesis 23 and Hittite law is evidence for the antiquity and historical accuracy of the text.

      Reception History

      Hebrews 11:13
      The author of Hebrews reads Abraham's status as a "stranger and foreigner" (his own words in Gen 23:4) as a theological statement about the nature of faith: "These all died in faith, not having received the things promised, but having seen them and greeted them from afar, and having acknowledged that they were strangers and exiles on the earth."

      Literary Structure

      The Negotiation: Covenant Faith as Legal Diplomacy23:7-16Abraham bows to the Hittites and conducts elaborate negotiations for a single cave. The diplomacy is covenant faith enacted: he acts as heir of the land by formally purchasing his first legal foothold in it.
      Machpelah: Dead Buried in Unpossessed Land23:19-20Abraham buried Sarah in Machpelah -- the first covenant-family burial in the Promised Land. The dead are laid down in the land they did not possess alive. Faith claims the future by burying its dead in it.

      Scholarly Debates

      Hittites in Canaan: Historical Problem?
      Anachronistic / literary
      Gurney, some scholars
      The Hittite empire was centred in Anatolia; no evidence of Hittites in Hebron in the patriarchal period. "Hittites" (bene-het) may be a later retroactive designation.
      Local Hittites / Neo-Hittites
      Kitchen, Hoffmeier, Sarna
      The term "Hittite" is used broadly in the OT for Canaanite sub-groups (cf. Uriah the Hittite in Hebron's region). Smaller Hittite-related groups may have been present in Canaan earlier than the main empire. The legal procedures in ch.23 match Hittite land-purchase texts of the 2nd millennium BCE.
      Kitchen and Hoffmeier have demonstrated that the legal formulae in Genesis 23 (the offer of free land, the insistence on purchase, the precision of the full price) match Hittite land-purchase conventions of the Middle Bronze Age. Whatever the precise ethnic label, the cultural-legal background is authentic to the period.
