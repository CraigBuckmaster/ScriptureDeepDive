Genesis 15

The Abrahamic Covenant Ratified

Verses 1–6 — Abram's Faith Credited as Righteousness
123456

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
15:1

MacArthur: 'Do not be afraid, Abram. I am your shield; your very great reward.' God speaks after Abraham's refusal of Sodom's king's offer. The divine assurance addresses both the vulnerability created by military action and the financial cost of refusing the plunder. God himself replaces what Abraham refused.

15:5-6

MacArthur: God takes Abraham outside and says 'Look up at the sky and count the stars -- if indeed you can count them. So shall your offspring be.' Abraham believed the LORD, and he credited it to him as righteousness. This is the most theologically significant verse in the patriarch narratives -- Paul's foundation text for justification by faith (Rom 4:3; Gal 3:6).

Hebrew Word Study

māgēn ma-GAYN — shield / protector: "I am your shield (māgēn), your very great reward" (v.1) — God's self-presentation after the battle of ch.14 addresses Abram's implicit anxiety: with four defeated kings and their allies somewhere in the region, Abram has reason to fear reprisal. The shield metaphor is one of the OT's most common images for divine protection (Ps 3:3; 18:2; 28:7; 33:20). God is both the protector (māgēn) and the reward (śākār) — the relationship itself is the compensation, not any derived benefit.

ʾĕmunāh e-mu-NAH — faith / faithfulness / firm trust: "Abram believed the LORD, and he credited it to him as righteousness" (v.6) — the verse Paul quotes as the foundation of justification by faith (Rom 4:3; Gal 3:6). The word ʾĕmunāh (from ʾāman — to be firm, reliable, faithful) means more than intellectual assent: it is the orientation of one's whole being toward God as trustworthy. The hiphil form (heʾĕmîn) suggests causing oneself to stand firm in another — leaning one's full weight on the divine promise.

ṣĕdāqāh tse-da-KAH — righteousness / right standing: "He credited it to him as righteousness" (v.6) — ṣĕdāqāh is forensic, relational, and covenantal: the right standing of a party who has fulfilled covenant obligations. Here, remarkably, it is credited (ḥāšab — counted/imputed) to Abram not on the basis of performance but of faith. The theological revolution of this verse is that covenant standing can be received through trust before any law is given or any act performed — the basis of Paul's entire argument in Romans 3-5.

Historical Context

None

Context

Genesis 15:6 is arguably the most theologically significant verse in the entire Old Testament. Paul quotes it three times (Rom 4:3, 9, 22; Gal 3:6) to establish that justification has always been by faith, not works. This is not a NT innovation — it is the foundational principle of how God has always related to humanity. Abram's faith is the pattern; his righteousness is the prototype of what Paul calls "the righteousness of God."

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 4:1–25Paul's full exposition: Abraham was justified before circumcision (ch.15 precedes ch.17), proving that faith, not law or circumcision, is the basis of righteousness.
  • Gal 3:6–9The Abrahamic faith-righteousness is the basis of Gentile justification: "those who are of faith are blessed along with Abraham, the man of faith."
  • Heb 11:11–12Abraham's faith in the impossible promise is the model for faith as "the assurance of things hoped for."
  • Jas 2:21–23James (using the same verse!) argues that Abraham's faith was "completed" by his works — not contradicting Paul but emphasizing genuine faith produces action.

15:1

The divine word comes to Abram in a vision: "Do not be afraid, Abram. I am your shield; your very great reward." Sarna notes this is the first vision formula in the Bible — "the word of the LORD came to Abram in a vision." The promise of divine protection ("shield") may respond to anxiety after the battle of chapter 14 — having made enemies of the eastern kings, Abram faces potential retaliation.

15:6

Ve'he'emin baYHWH vayyachshoveha lo tsedaqah — "and he believed the LORD, and he counted it to him as righteousness." Sarna: "This is one of the most theologically momentous verses in the entire Old Testament. Faith — trust in God's word — is credited as righteousness. The theological weight Paul places on this verse (Romans 4; Galatians 3) is already present in the Hebrew: Abram did not earn righteousness by works but received it through faith."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:1-3

Abram's response to the divine promise is remarkably direct: "You have given me no children; so a servant in my household will be my heir." Alter: "Abram does not simply receive the promise with silent faith; he argues with God. The patriarch's response to divine address is frank, even bold — he names the problem that makes the promise seem empty. This kind of covenantal frankness characterises the patriarchal dialogues with God."

15:6

The verse is a narrative comment, not direct speech. Alter: "The narrator's statement — 'he believed' — is deceptively simple. The Hebrew word heemin (believed/trusted) has the root aman — the firmness, stability, reliability from which 'amen' derives. Abram's belief is not an emotion but a settled, stable trust in God's word against all visible evidence."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:6

Romans 4 and this verse together are the foundation of Calvin's doctrine of justification by faith. Calvin: "Moses declares that Abram obtained righteousness by faith. Righteousness is not imputed to him because of his own merit, but because he embraced the grace of God by faith. Paul correctly deduces from this that righteousness is not of works but of faith alone." The doctrine of imputed righteousness is grounded in this single narrative statement.

15:2-3

Abram's complaint — "you have given me no children" — is not unbelief but a frank statement of the problem that makes the promise seem impossible. Calvin: "We see that Abram did not rashly pretend to believe more than he felt; but, just as he was perplexed about the manner of the fulfilment, so he honestly uttered what was pressing upon him." Honest wrestling with God's promises is not opposed to faith but is a form of it.

Verses 7–11 — The Covenant Ceremony Prepared
7891011

Places

Between the pieces
A ritual space, no specific location given
The covenant ceremony uses the ancient vassal-treaty form: both parties pass between slaughtered animals, invoking the same fate if the covenant is broken. But only God (as fire and smoke) passes through. The place is sanctified by the presence of the divine, not by geography.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
15:7-11

MacArthur: The covenant ceremony uses the standard ancient Near Eastern ritual of cutting animals in half so that covenant parties walk between the pieces -- invoking a self-curse: 'may this happen to me if I break the covenant.' God initiates and commands the ceremony; Abraham prepares it but does not yet pass through.

15:11

MacArthur: Abraham drove away the birds of prey from the carcasses -- a detail that signals his watchful attention during the covenant preparation. The birds of prey attempting to consume the sacrifices may symbolise the hostile forces that will threaten the covenant promise during the coming centuries of Egyptian bondage (vv.13-16).

Hebrew Word Study

yāraš ya-RASH — to possess / to inherit: "How can I know that I will gain possession (ʾîrāšennāh) of it?" (v.8) — Abram's question about the land uses the verb yāraš (to inherit/dispossess), which will become the technical term for Israel's conquest of Canaan (Num 33:53; Deut 1:8). Abram is asking for a legal guarantee of what has been promised verbally. God's response is not rebuke but a formal covenant ceremony — the divine commitment is made with the solemnity of a binding legal rite.

bāthar ba-TAR — to cut in two / to halve: The covenant ceremony of vv.9-11 involves cutting animals in two (bāthar) — the standard ancient Near Eastern covenant-ratification rite, from which the Hebrew idiom "to cut a covenant" (kārat bĕrît) derives. The contracting parties would walk between the divided carcasses, symbolically saying: "If I break this covenant, may what happened to these animals happen to me." The remarkable element in v.17 is that only God (as the smoking firepot and blazing torch) passes between the pieces — making it an unconditional divine commitment.

ʿayiṭ a-YIT — birds of prey: "Then birds of prey came down on the carcasses, but Abram drove them away" (v.11). The ʿayiṭ (birds of prey / vultures) descending on the covenant sacrifice are understood by some as threats to the covenant — nations and forces that will attempt to devour it — which Abram actively repels. His role as guardian of the covenant ceremony anticipates Israel's ongoing struggle to maintain covenant integrity against external and internal threats that seek to "pick at" the divine promises.

Historical Context

The covenant ceremony of Genesis 15 has direct parallels in ANE suzerainty treaties. In Mari texts and Hittite treaty documents, parties to a covenant would walk between the halves of slain animals, invoking the curse: "May what happened to these animals happen to the one who breaks this covenant." The stunning innovation in Genesis 15 is that GOD alone passes through — taking the covenant curse upon himself, making it an unconditional divine commitment.

Context

The one-sided nature of this covenant is theologically explosive. Abram is put into a "deep sleep" — he cannot pass through the pieces. Only the divine presence (fire pot and torch) passes. This means the covenant's fulfillment depends entirely on God, not on Abram's faithfulness. It is an unconditional promise, a suzerain's self-imposed oath. Theologians see this as the basis for the "new covenant" of Hebrews, where God's own Son bears the covenant curse on the cross.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 34:18–20When Israelites "cut a covenant" and then broke it by re-enslaving freed servants, God invokes the curse of walking between the pieces — exactly the Genesis 15 ceremony.
  • Gal 3:15–17Paul argues the Mosaic law cannot annul the Abraham covenant, which was confirmed 430 years earlier — an unconditional promise cannot be made conditional later.
  • Heb 6:13–18God swore by himself because there was no greater (v.13) — the basis of "two unchangeable things" (promise + oath) giving "strong encouragement" to those who flee to Christ.

15:7-11

The covenant ceremony of the animal halves is an ancient Near Eastern ratification ritual — a self-maledictory oath in which the parties pass between the divided animals, invoking the fate of the animals on themselves if they break the covenant. Sarna: "Archaeological evidence from Mari and other sites confirms that the 'cutting' of animals in covenant ceremonies was a standard ancient Near Eastern practice. The Hebrew idiom for making a covenant — karat berith (cut a covenant) — reflects this practice."

15:12-17

The deep sleep (tardemah) that falls on Abram echoes Adam's deep sleep in 2:21. Sarna notes both accompany a divine creative act: in ch.2, the creation of woman; here, the creation of the covenant. The human is passive while the divine acts — the covenant's unilateral character is dramatised in Abram's unconsciousness.

15:8-11

Abram asks "how can I know that I will gain possession of it?" — the same frank questioning as v.2. Alter: "Abram wants a sign, a confirmation of the promise. God provides one — but the sign is entirely divine: a ritual that Abram prepares but does not participate in (he falls into a deep sleep). The covenant is confirmed unilaterally. Calvin’s Commentary

15:9-11

The preparation of the covenant animals is an act of obedience — Abram follows the divine instruction precisely. Calvin: "Abram did not require a further sign because he was destitute of faith, but that he might more certainly confirm it to himself." MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
15:12-16

MacArthur: In a deep sleep, Abraham receives a prophetic vision of the 400-year Egyptian bondage. The precision of the prophecy -- nation, slavery, affliction, judgment on the oppressor, fourth generation return -- establishes God's sovereign foreknowledge of the entire redemption history before it occurs.

15:17-18

MacArthur: God alone passes between the pieces as 'a smoking firepot with a blazing torch' -- divine symbols throughout the OT (Ex 13:21; 19:18). The covenant is unconditional and unilateral: God incurs the self-curse, not Abraham. On that day the LORD made a covenant with Abram -- the formal covenant ratification is now complete.

Hebrew Word Study

tardēmāh tar-day-MAH — deep sleep / trance: A "thick and dreadful darkness came over him" (v.12) — the same deep sleep (tardēmāh) that fell on Adam before the formation of Eve (2:21), a state of prophetic receptivity to divine revelation. The combination of sleep and dread (ʾêmāh) marks the encounter as a theophanic experience of the holy — God's presence is not casually encountered but experienced as awesome and overwhelming. The covenant ratification will occur at this threshold between human consciousness and divine initiative.

gēr / ʿābad / ʿinnāh GAYR / a-VAD / ee-NAH — alien / serve / oppress: The prophecy of v.13 — "your descendants will be strangers in a country not their own, and they will be enslaved and mistreated four hundred years" — uses three keywords that describe Egypt: gēr (alien/sojourner — cf. Exod 22:21), ʿābad (to serve/slave — cf. Exod 1:13), ʿinnāh (to oppress/afflict — cf. Exod 1:11-12). The Exodus is not an unplanned rescue but the fulfilment of a precise covenant prophecy given to Abraham four centuries earlier.

ʾăbōt hāʾĕmōrî a-VON ha-e-mo-REE — iniquity of the Amorites: "The sin of the Amorites has not yet reached its full measure" (v.16) — the delay in the land's inheritance is theologically motivated: God will not dispossess the Canaanites until their covenant-breaking and moral corruption has reached a threshold that justifies displacement. The divine patience toward the Amorites for four generations is an act of mercy. When Israel eventually takes the land, it is not conquest for conquest's sake but the execution of a covenant that has been patient and just.

15:17

The smoking firepot and blazing torch passing between the animal pieces is the climax of the covenant ceremony. Alter: "The divine presence manifests as fire — not to consume but to covenant. The deep sleep and the "thick and dreadful darkness" that settles on Abram are appropriate responses to the divine presence. People of the Chapter

Abram
Patriarch; First justified-by-faith believer
His faith in the face of an impossible promise — childless at advanced age — becomes the paradigmatic act of faith in the Bible. Paul calls him "the father of all who believe" (Rom 4:11).
Eliezer of Damascus
Abram's servant; default heir
Mentioned only here in the Bible. As Abram's chief servant and potential heir under the custom of adoption-for-inheritance (attested in Nuzi texts), his existence represents the human "solution" that God overrules.

Ancient Sources

Nuzi Tablets (15th c. BC)
Cuneiform tablets from Nuzi (northeastern Iraq) document the custom of adopting a servant as heir when a man had no son — exactly Abram's situation with Eliezer. The tablets show that a subsequently born natural son would supersede the adopted heir.
Provides direct cultural background for Abram's anxiety about Eliezer and the legal framework God's promise overrides.
LXX (Septuagint) Genesis 15:6
The Greek translation renders "counted/reckoned" (ḥāšaḇ) as elogisthē — the same word Paul uses throughout Romans 4. This linguistic bridge directly enabled Paul's theological argument about imputed righteousness.
The LXX translation choice becomes a key link in the NT's theological use of this verse.

Reception History

Martin Luther, Lectures on Genesis (1535–45)
Luther called Genesis 15:6 "the foremost passage in the entire Old Testament" and the foundation of his reformation doctrine of justification sola fide. He argued it proved that righteousness before God has always been by faith alone, not merit.
N.T. Wright, Paul and the Faithfulness of God
Wright nuances the traditional reading: Abraham's faithfulness (as well as God's) is in view — the verse is about covenant loyalty, not merely individual forensic justification. His reading sparks the "New Perspective on Paul" debate, one of the most significant theological controversies of the late 20th century.

Literary Structure

Faith Credited as Righteousness15:6One of the OT's most theologically concentrated sentences. Paul (Rom 4) and James (2:23) both expound this verse -- the ground of justification by faith.

Scholarly Debates

The "400 Years" of Oppression (15:13): Chronology
Literal 400 years
Sarna, Wenham
Exod 12:40 gives 430 years from the entry into Egypt (or from Abraham's call, per LXX and Gal 3:17). The 400 years is approximate; the 430 is more precise.
Schematic round number
Kitchen, Hoffmeier
400 is a schematic number in ancient Near Eastern texts for a very long but not precisely defined period. The theological point is "four generations of affliction" rather than a precise 400 solar years.
The chronological details of the Egyptian sojourn have generated sustained scholarly debate. The key synchronisms (the Exodus date, pharaoh identification) remain disputed. Most evangelical scholars accept a 15th century Exodus (early date) or 13th century (late date). The 400/430 year figure is genuine historical memory regardless of the resolution.
