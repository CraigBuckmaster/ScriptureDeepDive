Genesis 39

Joseph in Potiphar's House; False Accusation

Verses 1–10 — Joseph in Potiphar's House; the Lord with Joseph
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

יְהוָה הָיָה עִם YHWH hāyāh ʿim — the LORD was with: The phrase "the LORD was with Joseph" is the chapter's theological anchor (vv.2,3,21,23). The same formula appeared at Bethel (28:15: "I will be with you") and at Jacob's departure (31:3). It is the covenantal presence formula — God's personal accompaniment of the chosen servant.

מַצְלִיחַ maṣlîaḥ — successful / prosperous: From the root ṣālaḥ, "to advance, prosper, succeed." Prosperity in the Joseph narrative is always attributed to divine presence, not human skill — though Joseph's skill is real and visible.

מְשָׁרְתֵהוּ mĕšārĕtēhû — attended him / served him: The verb šārat (serve, minister) is used later for priestly service (Exod 28:35). Joseph's service of Potiphar is rendered with the full care and competence that would later characterize priestly ministry — he serves a pagan master as unto the LORD.

Historical Context

Genesis 39 is the chapter of divine presence. The phrase "the LORD was with Joseph" (vv.2,3,5,21,23) is the theological refrain that organises the entire chapter — appearing in both the Potiphar section and the prison section. The LORD's presence is what distinguishes Joseph's time in Egypt from ordinary slavery. It is not Joseph's native ability (though he is capable), but the divine presence attending him, that produces the visible prosperity. Potiphar recognises this (v.3) even as a pagan; the prison warden recognises it (v.23).

Context

The fourfold "LORD was with Joseph" in this chapter is the interpretive key to the entire Joseph narrative. Joseph does not explain his success through intelligence or initiative — the text consistently attributes it to divine presence. This presence is not conditional on circumstances: it operates in the house of a pagan, in the prison of a corrupt official, and eventually in the court of Pharaoh. The phrase links back to God's promise to Jacob at Bethel (28:15) and forward to the Exodus commission to Moses (Exod 3:12: "I will be with you").

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 28:15God's promise: "I will be with you." Fulfilled first in Jacob's life, now operating through Joseph.

Places

Nile delta region
Joseph enters Egyptian domestic space — Potiphar's house in Egypt. The narrative's geography contracts from the vast Canaan-Egypt axis to a single household interior. His rise and fall happen within the bounded space of an Egyptian official's compound.
Egypt (context)
Northeastern Africa; Nile Valley
Egypt in the Joseph story is characterized by its bureaucratic hierarchy: household, prison, Pharaoh's court. The geography is vertical (social ladder) rather than horizontal. Joseph's journey through these spaces is the covenant's passage through Egyptian power structures.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
39:2-3

The LORD was with Joseph, and he prospered — the most direct statement of providential presence in the Joseph cycle. The prosperity is immediate and visible: Potiphar sees it and draws the correct conclusion about its source. What is remarkable is that the divine blessing is visible to a pagan Egyptian official before Joseph himself articulates it. God's presence is so tangible in Joseph's work that it requires no theological explanation from Joseph — it is simply evident.

39:5-6

Potiphar entrusts everything to Joseph — the entirety of his household except his own food. The exception is a legal/ritual detail: an Egyptian official could not have his personal food managed by a foreigner because of purity concerns. But within those limits, Joseph has complete authority. The blessing that has been flowing to Joseph now flows outward to Potiphar's entire household: "The LORD blessed the household of the Egyptian because of Joseph."

39:7-9

Potiphar's wife's repeated proposition and Joseph's refusal constitute one of the most sustained ethical tests in the OT. Joseph's response is not "I will be caught" or "this is culturally wrong" but a theological argument: "how could I do such a wicked thing and sin against God?" The ethical reasoning is God-centred, not consequence-centred. This is the heart of Joseph's moral character.

39:10

The proposition is repeated "day after day" — this is not a single temptation but a sustained campaign. Joseph does not merely resist the initial approach; he refuses continuous pressure. He refuses not only her advances but her company — he avoids being near her. This is the practical wisdom of sustained moral resistance: the refusal of proximity as the refusal of temptation.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
39:2

The phrase "the LORD was with Joseph" (wayyehi YHWH et-Yosef) is a covenant-presence formula that appears elsewhere for Isaac (26:3,24), Jacob (28:15), and Joshua (Josh 1:5,9). Sarna: "The formula signals that the covenant established with Abraham is active in Joseph's Egypt. Joseph is not abandoned by the God of his fathers when he is sold into slavery — the covenant presence travels with him. Egypt is within the reach of the covenant God."

39:9

Joseph's refusal speech — "how can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" — contains the word ra'ah gedolah (great wickedness), the same root used in the divine assessment of the pre-flood generation (6:5: "every inclination of the thoughts of the human heart was only evil all the time"). Joseph instinctively categorises the proposed act in the most serious terms. Sarna: "Joseph's ethical instinct is calibrated to divine standards, not social conventions."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
39:6

The description of Joseph as "well-built and handsome" (yefeh-to'ar vifeh mareh) uses the identical phrase used for his mother Rachel (29:17). Alter: "The physical beauty is a genetic inheritance from his mother. But in Joseph's case the beauty becomes a liability rather than an asset. Rachel's beauty attracted Jacob's love; Joseph's beauty attracts Potiphar's wife's desire. The same quality that secured Jacob's affection for the mother now creates danger for the son."

39:10

The sustained nature of the temptation — "day after day she spoke to Joseph" — is rendered without elaboration. Alter notes the narrative economy: the reader does not need to know what she said each time. The refrain of daily repetition and daily refusal is itself the content. Joseph's consistency under sustained pressure is the characterisation. No single dramatic refusal but the accumulation of daily nos against daily yeses.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
39:2-3

Calvin on the LORD's presence: "Let the reader observe that the favour of God, though for a time it may be concealed from us, yet never fails to appear openly at length. Joseph, when reduced to the condition of a slave, seems to have been cast off by God; but now it appears that God was with him, even in his slavery." The delay between the appearance of abandonment and the manifestation of presence is the structure of faith: God is present before the presence becomes visible.

39:9

Calvin on Joseph's refusal: "Joseph does not put forward, as the ground of his refusal, that the deed would be disgraceful and dishonest before the world; but he takes the higher ground, that it would be a sin against God... Hereby Joseph shows that he regards not his own honour or interest, but only God's glory." The God-centred nature of Joseph's ethical reasoning is what distinguishes it from mere social convention or self-interest.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
39:2

The NET note on "the LORD was with Joseph" observes this is the first time since the Jacob narrative that the divine name YHWH has been used in a non-speech context. The note: "The narrator's use of YHWH — the covenant name — rather than Elohim signals that what is happening to Joseph is covenant history. The God of the Abrahamic covenant is actively present in the slave quarters of an Egyptian house." The divine name is a narrative theological marker.

39:9

The NET note on Joseph's words discusses the phrase "sin against God" (chata'ti le'Elohim). The note observes this is the foundational statement of Joseph's ethical theology: wrongdoing is ultimately against God, not merely against other humans. The note compares Psalm 51:4 ("against you, you only, have I sinned") and observes that Joseph's God-centred ethics anticipates the Psalms' and Prophets' insistence that all human wrongdoing is ultimately theocentric.

Verses 11–23 — False Accusation; Joseph in Prison; God with Him

Hebrew Word Study

חֶסֶד ḥesed — steadfast love / lovingkindness: In v.21 the LORD shows Joseph ḥesed — the covenantal loyalty that undergirds all of God's dealings with his people. This is the first use of ḥesed in connection with Joseph — it will sustain him through prison.

הָרָעָה הַגְּדֹלָה hārāʿāh haggĕdōlāh — this great wickedness: Joseph's refusal frames the issue as theological: "how can I sin against God?" He does not primarily appeal to loyalty to Potiphar (though he mentions that too) — the ultimate ground of his refusal is the fear of God.

בִּגְדוֹ bigdô — his garment: The garment motif continues: Joseph's robe of many colors was used to deceive Jacob (ch.37); now his garment is used to falsely accuse him. The garment is again both evidence and instrument of his apparent undoing.

Historical Context

Potiphar's wife's false accusation and Joseph's imprisonment is the nadir of the Joseph cycle — the righteous man is punished for righteousness. The chapter's theology of divine presence (v.21,23) is its answer to the injustice: the LORD does not remove the injustice but attends Joseph through it. The prison becomes the next stage of Joseph's training, not the end of his story. The warden who trusts Joseph mirrors Potiphar's trust — the pattern of divine blessing attracting human recognition continues even in the deepest confinement.

Context

Joseph's refusal is framed in explicitly theological terms: "How can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" (v.9). He does not primarily say "it would betray Potiphar" — though that is mentioned — he says "it would be sin against God." This is the theology of integrity: behavior is governed by the vertical relationship, not only the horizontal. The prison (v.20) is Joseph's reward for righteousness — and God's "steadfast love" (ḥesed, v.21) meets him there. The pattern: integrity leads to suffering leads to elevation, repeated twice (house → prison → court).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
39:12-13

Potiphar's wife's seizure of Joseph's garment — and his flight leaving it in her hand — is the third garment scene in the Joseph cycle (coat of many colours, 37:23; this garment, 39:12; the royal garments to come, 41:42). The garment left behind becomes the false evidence against him. What was a sign of his integrity (he fled) becomes the weapon against him (she displays what he left). Righteousness becomes the instrument of persecution.

39:16-19

The accusation is constructed with artistry: Potiphar's wife tells the household servants first, framing the story before Potiphar hears it, building a coalition of witnesses. When Potiphar arrives she tells him the same story with one addition: "The Hebrew slave you brought us came to me to make sport of me" — she implicates Potiphar in bringing the danger into his own house. The narrative reveals her rhetorical skill in making her victim the aggressor.

39:21-23

The LORD was with Joseph even in prison — the refrain of divine presence is repeated at the lowest point. The prison warden's trust mirrors Potiphar's trust; the pattern of blessing and recognition repeats at the new level. What looked like God's abandonment — the innocent man in prison — is instead God's preparation of the next platform from which Joseph will serve. The prison is not a punishment but a preparation.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
39:14

Potiphar's wife's accusation — "that Hebrew slave you brought us" — uses the ethnic designation Hebrew (Ivri) as a term of contempt. Sarna: "'Hebrew' in this context is not a neutral ethnic term but a social marker for a foreigner of low status — literally a 'crosser' (from the root avar = to cross over). The woman deploys Joseph's foreignness as part of the accusation: the alien has violated the Egyptian household. The ethnic slur is part of the rhetorical attack."

39:20

Joseph is put in "the place where the king's prisoners were confined" — a royal prison, not an ordinary jail. Sarna notes the providential placement: the same prison where Pharaoh's officials are held is where Joseph will encounter the cupbearer and baker (ch.40), which will lead directly to his appearance before Pharaoh (ch.41). The prison that looks like the end of Joseph's story is the specific location Providence has chosen for the next chapter of it.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
39:12

The repeated garment motif — Joseph's coat stripped by the brothers (37:23), his garment seized by Potiphar's wife — creates what Alter calls a structural pattern of the Joseph cycle. "Each time Joseph loses a garment, he loses a position. The stripping is a loss of status. But each loss is followed by a rise to a new, higher position. The garment-loss/rise pattern organises the entire narrative trajectory." Joseph will receive royal garments (41:42) at the apex.

39:20

The irony of the prison is multiple. Alter notes: "The 'king's prison' is exactly where Joseph needs to be — not the general slave quarters but the facility for royal officials. His righteous refusal of Potiphar's wife lands him, with precise providential irony, in the place from which he will reach the king." The injustice is real; its providential placement is equally real. Both truths coexist.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
39:17-18

Calvin on the false accusation: "The impudence of the woman is beyond all description; she turns the tables with perfect coolness. The crime which she had herself committed she transfers to the man who was innocent." Calvin reads this as a description of the mechanism of all false accusation: the aggressor claims victim status; the victim is framed as aggressor. The pattern repeats in every generation.

39:21

Calvin on divine presence in prison: "Whereas it might seem that Joseph had been forsaken by God, he now has fresh proof of divine grace... He is not afflicted by despair or overwhelmed by grief, because he knew that God was still his Father. The consciousness of divine favour serves him as a shield against all assaults of adversity." Calvin identifies the consciousness of God's presence — not the removal of the circumstance — as the source of Joseph's stability.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
39:14

The NET note on "Hebrew" (Ivri) discusses the etymology (possibly from Eber, ancestor of the Semitic peoples, Gen 10:21; or from the root avar = to cross over) and its social connotations in the ancient Near East. The note observes that "Hebrew" in Genesis is consistently used by non-Israelites when referring to Israelites with a degree of social distance or contempt (40:15, 41:12, 43:32). The self-designation "Israelite" is used internally; "Hebrew" marks the outsider's perspective.

39:21

The NET note on "the LORD was with Joseph" as the prison refrain observes this is the fourth use of the formula in the chapter (vv.2,3,21,23). The note: "The repetition is emphatic — it is the chapter's theological thesis. Joseph's circumstances change dramatically (slave → senior slave → prisoner); the divine presence is constant. The reader is meant to understand that 'with him' is not an incidental detail but the explanation for everything that follows in the Joseph narrative."

Ancient Sources

Tale of Two Brothers (Egypt, c.1200 BC)
An Egyptian story where a wife falsely accuses her husband's brother of attempted rape after he rejects her advances — strikingly parallel to Genesis 39.
The parallel demonstrates that the social dynamic of Genesis 39 was recognized in Egyptian culture; it does not imply literary dependence but cultural realism.
Egyptian Prison Records
Papyri from the Middle Kingdom (c. 2000–1700 BC) document royal prison facilities where high-status prisoners were held — consistent with the "place where the king's prisoners were confined" (v.20).
The administrative detail of v.20 is consistent with Egyptian records.

Reception History

Chrysostom, Homilies on Genesis
Joseph's flight from temptation is the model of Christian chastity — the refusal to remain in the presence of temptation, the literal fleeing. "He left his garment and fled."
Calvin, Commentary
The fourfold "LORD was with Joseph" is for Calvin the chapter's sermon: divine presence is the explanation for both prosperity and survival in adversity. The believer is never alone.
Joseph as type of Christ
Patristic and Reformed interpreters note the parallel: the beloved son, falsely accused, imprisoned, raised to rule and save. Joseph's integrity in the face of false accusation prefigures Christ before Pilate.

Textual Notes

39:2וַיְהִי יְהוָה אֶת יוֹסֵף — "the LORD was with Joseph"
MT wayyehi YHWH et-Yosef — "and YHWH was with Joseph." The formula appears vv.2,3,21,23. All ancient versions agree. The fourfold repetition is unique in the patriarchal narratives.
The formula "YHWH was with X" appears for Isaac (26:3,24), Jacob (28:15; 31:3), Joshua (Josh 1:5), Samuel (1 Sam 3:19), and David (1 Sam 16:18; 18:14). It is the formula of covenant presence — the assurance that the covenant God accompanies his servant through every circumstance. Its fourfold concentration in one chapter (39) is the theological signature of the Joseph cycle.

Scholarly Debates

Potiphar's Wife: Ancient Near Eastern Parallels
Tale of Two Brothers (Egypt, c.13th c. BCE)
Most OT scholars
The Egyptian "Tale of Two Brothers" has the same basic plot: a wife propositions her husband's younger relative; he refuses; she accuses him falsely; he is punished. The parallel is widely cited.
Independent tradition
Kitchen, Sarna
The parallel shows that this narrative type was known in the ancient world, but Genesis 39 is not dependent on the Egyptian tale. The theological content (God's presence, Joseph's God-centred refusal) is entirely distinctive. The parallel illuminates the cultural context without establishing literary dependence.
The "Tale of Two Brothers" parallel is genuine but proves cultural background rather than literary borrowing. The key differences are theological: Joseph refuses because of his relationship with God, not merely social convention. The Egyptian parallel has no equivalent to "how could I do this great evil and sin against God?" (39:9). The parallel illuminates; it does not undermine.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 39 is organised by the repeated assertion of divine presence under every circumstance. Slave quarters, position of trust, temptation, prison — the LORD accompanies Joseph through each stage. The chapter's central ethical moment — Joseph's God-centred refusal of Potiphar's wife — is the moral foundation of the entire Joseph cycle: the man who says "how can I sin against God?" is the man God can trust with the salvation of Egypt and Israel.
