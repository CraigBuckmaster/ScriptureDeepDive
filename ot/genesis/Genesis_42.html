Genesis 42

Brothers Arrive in Egypt; Joseph Tests Them

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–17 — Brothers Arrive in Egypt; Joseph Recognizes Them

Hebrew Word Study

וַיִּשְׁתַּחֲווּ wayyištaḥăwû — bowed themselves: The fulfilment of Joseph's first dream (37:7) — ten brothers prostrate before the grain-governor. The same verb used in the dream narrative now appears in the narrative of its fulfilment, twenty-two years later.

וַיִּזְכֹּר wayyizkōr — remembered: Joseph remembered the dreams. The zkr root (remember) ties together the cupbearer's forgetting (40:23), Pharaoh's need (41:1), and now the divine commission that has shaped Joseph's entire life.

Historical Context

The arrival of the ten brothers in Egypt fulfils the first of Joseph's dreams (the sheaves bowing, 37:7) — they bow before him without recognising him. Joseph's recognition of them without their recognising him creates the narrative tension that will drive chapters 42-45. His decision to test them — accuse them of espionage, demand Benjamin — is complex: is it revenge, self-protection, or a deliberate test of whether his brothers have changed? The chapter reveals the first movement of conscience in the brothers: their guilt about Joseph begins to surface (v.21-22).

Context

Joseph's immediate recognition and the brothers' failure to recognize him is psychologically realistic — he is dressed as an Egyptian ruler after twenty-two years; they are the same Canaanite shepherds he knew as a boy. His harsh speech and the spy accusation are not cruelty but tests. He must know: have they changed? Would they sacrifice another favoured son to save themselves?

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 37:7First dream fulfilled: ten brothers bow with faces to the ground.
  • Gen 43:26Second fulfilment: all eleven brothers bow when Benjamin arrives.
  • Ps 105:17–19The Psalm reads the entire Joseph story as God sending "a man before them" — the sale as divine commission.

Places

Egypt (famine centre)
Nile Valley; only source of grain
The famine creates a gravitational pull toward Egypt as the food source. Jacob sends all sons except Benjamin to Egypt — the journey from Canaan to Egypt is roughly 400km. This repeated journey (chs.42-45) prefigures Israel's longer sojourn.
Canaan (famine periphery)
Southern Canaan and Negev
While Egypt has grain, Canaan has famine. The covenant land is uninhabitable without Egypt's supply — a spatial paradox that will define Israel's history: the Promised Land's fertility depends on seasons; Egypt's irrigation is more reliable.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
42:6

They came and bowed down before him with their faces to the ground — the exact fulfilment of the dream they had mocked: "will your sheaves bow down to mine?" (37:8). Joseph recognises them immediately. They do not recognise him: he has been transformed — Egyptian dress, Egyptian name, Egyptian authority. Twenty years of separation and transformation have made him unrecognisable.

42:7-9

Joseph's decision to treat them as strangers and speak harshly is a deliberate test. He "remembered his dreams" — the narrative flags the connection. What follows is not revenge but a staged process of moral examination: are these the same men who threw him into a pit? Or have twenty years changed them? The cup-in-the-sack episode, the Benjamin requirement, and Judah's great speech (ch.44) will reveal the answer.

42:13-17

The accusation of espionage is the pretext for the test. Egypt's granary operations would have been sensitive intelligence; the accusation is plausible. The demand that one brother return to Canaan and bring the youngest is the mechanism for testing: will the brothers sacrifice Benjamin to escape, as they once sacrificed Joseph?

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
42:6

The fulfilment of the dream is noted with characteristic brevity. Sarna: "The narrative does not pause to celebrate the dream's fulfilment. The moment passes quickly, buried in the action of the scene. Joseph remembers; the reader remembers. But the brothers are oblivious — they do not connect the bowing grain of the dream to the bowing men of the present scene. The very ignorance that prevented them from seeing Joseph's destiny in his dreams now prevents them from seeing him before them."

42:9

The word used for Joseph's remembering — wayyizkor — is the same divine-remembering verb used throughout the Joseph cycle. Sarna: "Joseph does not merely recall a past event; he perceives its providential significance in the present moment. The dreams are not memories of childhood presumption but prophecies now in the process of fulfilment. His testing of the brothers is driven by this recognition."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
42:7

Joseph "recognised his brothers but pretended to be a stranger." Alter: "The concealment is Joseph's deliberate choice, made in an instant. He has full power; they are entirely in his hands. He could reveal himself immediately. Instead he chooses to test them. The test requires time, pressure, and the specific conditions he will create. Joseph is managing a process of moral examination that will take three chapters."

42:14-16

The espionage accusation is a test disguised as an official procedure. Alter: "Pharaoh's second-in-command does not need to accuse grain-buyers of espionage. The accusation is generated; the demand for Benjamin is engineered. Joseph is creating the conditions under which his brothers' moral character will be revealed. The pressure of the false accusation is a crucible."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
42:7-9

Calvin on Joseph's harsh treatment: "Joseph was not moved by private revenge... His intention was to bring his brothers to repentance; and he saw that this could not be done except by temporal chastisement... He wished to prove whether his brothers were the same men they had formerly been, or whether they had corrected their cruelty and their wickedness by repentance."

42:15

Calvin on the oath "by the life of Pharaoh": "Although he swore by Pharaoh, he was still ultimately appealing to God, who presides over all oaths. The form of the oath accommodates itself to Egyptian custom while the substance remains a call on divine judgment."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
42:6

The NET note on "he bowed down before him with faces to the ground" flags this as the explicit fulfilment of the first dream (37:7). The note observes that the narrative does not editorially point to the connection — it simply records the action, allowing the attentive reader to make the connection. This is the characteristic restraint of biblical narrative: fulfilment without fanfare.

42:9

The NET note on Joseph "remembering his dreams" observes that this is the first explicit mention of the dreams since chapter 37. The narrator's note signals that Joseph's subsequent actions are dream-driven: he is not acting randomly but with the prophetic script in mind, watching the fulfilment unfold and choosing how to manage its completion.

Verses 18–38 — The Brothers' Guilt; Silver in the Sacks; Jacob's Refusal
181920212223242526272829303132333435363738

Hebrew Word Study

אֲשֵׁמִים ʾăšēmîm — guilty: Collective guilt-acknowledgement — the first genuine moral reckoning in the brotherhood since the sale of Joseph.

שְׁאֹל šeʾôl — Sheol: Jacob's threat: "you would bring down my gray hairs with sorrow to Sheol" (v.38). The same phrase returns in 44:29 — repeated paternal grief driving the narrative toward crisis.

Historical Context

The brothers' awakening conscience — "surely we are being punished because of our brother" — is the first movement of moral awareness in the ten since the sale. Their guilt has been suppressed for twenty years; the pressure of Joseph's test brings it to the surface. Reuben's retrospective "I told you so" (v.22) reveals that the brothers have been carrying private guilt even without discussing it. The silver found in their sacks on the way home deepens the terror: they believe God is punishing them for the original crime.

Context

The return of the silver (v.28) produces another theological response from the brothers: "What is this that God has done to us?" They are beginning to read their distress through a divine lens — exactly what Joseph needs to see. The chapter ends with Jacob refusing to release Benjamin, setting up the famine-pressure that will force his hand in ch.43.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 37:22Reuben urged the brothers not to kill Joseph (37:22); now he says "I told you so" — moral concern real, decisive action too little.
  • Prov 28:1The wicked flee when no one pursues — the brothers' terror at the silver illustrates the psychology of unresolved guilt.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
42:21-22

The brothers' guilt-speech is the chapter's most important moment: "Surely we are being punished because of our brother. We saw how distressed he was when he pleaded with us for his life, but we would not listen; that's why this distress has come on us." Joseph is standing there — they do not know it — hearing them describe the scene he experienced twenty years ago. The narrative detail (Joseph understood what they said; he turned away and wept, v.24) is one of the most moving in Genesis.

42:24

Joseph weeping — and then controlling himself and having Simeon bound — is a scene of extraordinary emotional complexity. He is simultaneously the wronged party hearing his brothers' confession and the official conducting a test. He must not reveal himself too soon. So he weeps privately — the first of five times he weeps in the Joseph cycle (42:24; 43:30; 45:2,14-15; 50:1) — and then returns to the official performance.

42:27-28

The silver found in the sack — "their hearts sank and they turned to each other trembling, saying, 'What is this that God has done to us?'" — is the brothers' instinctive theological interpretation of their situation. They attribute the silver (which Joseph put there as a grace) to divine judgment. Their guilt has sensitised them to interpret every unexpected event as divine retribution. The mechanism of guilty conscience: grace read as accusation.

42:36-38

Jacob's response — "you have deprived me of my children: Joseph is no more and Simeon is no more, and now you want to take Benjamin" — is the most prolonged grief speech in the Joseph cycle. He refuses to allow Benjamin to go to Egypt. Reuben's offer to allow Jacob to kill his two sons if Benjamin does not return (v.37) is rejected as absurd. The family crisis deepens. The famine will force a resolution Jacob cannot prevent.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
42:21

The brothers' retrospective acknowledgment — "we saw his distress when he pleaded with us for his life" — contains the only reference in the narrative to Joseph's pleading in the pit. The main narrative in 37:24-28 records the sale without Joseph's speech. Here the brothers remember what the narrator withheld: he begged. The retrospective revelation is devastating — it fills in a detail the reader was not given before, making the original scene retroactively more harrowing.

42:28

The silver in the sack creates a theological puzzle for the brothers. Sarna: "They interpret the silver as divine accusation because their consciences have been activated by Joseph's pressure. The same silver that Joseph intends as hospitality is received as judgment. Guilty conscience distorts grace into accusation — the silver that should comfort them terrifies them."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
42:23-24

The moment of Joseph's private weeping is one of the most carefully constructed scenes in the cycle. Alter: "Joseph is present throughout the brothers' guilt speech. He hears himself described — his distress, his pleading. And then: he turned away from them and wept. The turning away is both physical (he could not face them) and emotional (the control required to continue the test). He weeps and then returns and has Simeon bound. The movement from tears to official action is the chapter's most psychologically complex moment."

42:36

Jacob's speech — "me have you bereaved" (othi shikkaltem) — begins with the direct object pronoun in emphatic position. Alter: "Jacob leads with himself: it is he who has been bereaved. Joseph gone, Simeon bound, Benjamin demanded. The self-centred grief is not wrong — Jacob has suffered immensely — but it contrasts with the brothers' emerging awareness of others' suffering (v.21). Jacob is still in grief; the brothers are beginning to convert guilt into action."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
42:21-22

Calvin on the brothers' guilty conscience: "They acknowledge that God is the author of their punishment, and they confess their sin with their own mouth. This is a work of God — that after twenty years their buried wickedness is brought to light. How many men carry secret crimes through long years until the pressure of circumstance forces the truth to surface."

42:37-38

Calvin on Reuben's absurd offer: "Reuben proposed an absurd compensation — if I do not bring Benjamin back, kill my two sons. Jacob rightly rejects this as foolish. But the offer reveals that Reuben is trying to make amends; he understands that he failed Joseph and is determined not to fail again. His method is wrong; his direction is right."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
42:21

The NET note on the brothers' reference to Joseph's pleading observes this is new information not present in the main narrative of chapter 37. The note: "The brothers' speech fills a gap the narrator deliberately left. The reader learns retrospectively that Joseph begged, that his brothers watched unmoved. The information comes not from the narrator but from the guilty parties themselves, which makes it more devastating — it is self-incriminating testimony."

42:28

The NET note on "What is this that God has done to us?" discusses the theological grammar of the statement. The brothers invoke Elohim (generic divine name) rather than YHWH (covenant name) — reflecting their uncertain relationship with the covenant God. The note: "Their question is genuinely theological: they believe they are in the grip of a divine retribution. They are not wrong about the divine involvement; they are wrong about its character — this is grace, not judgment."

People of the Chapter

Joseph
The unrecognized ruler
Tests from a position of total power. His private weeping (v.24) shows the test costs him emotionally even as it is necessary.
The Ten Brothers
Minus Benjamin

Reuben
The failed elder

Jacob

Ancient Sources

Egyptian Grain Administration
Middle Kingdom papyri document elaborate grain-distribution bureaucracies. Viziers controlled supply; foreigners came to Egypt during Levantine droughts.
The scenario of Genesis 42 — Canaanites buying Egyptian grain — is historically attested.
Ration Lists
Egyptian texts record Semitic visitors receiving grain rations, confirming the historical plausibility of Jacob's sons travelling to Egypt for food.
The administrative detail is culturally authentic.

Reception History

Origen, Homilies on Genesis
The brothers' journey and failure to recognise Joseph typifies Israel's failure to recognise Christ. The second visit (ch.43) corresponds to the second coming.
Chrysostom
Joseph's weeping in private while speaking harshly in public shows the tension of loving discipline — true love sometimes requires allowing distress to produce repentance.

Hebrew-Rooted Reading

42:21 אֲשֵׁמִים אֲנַחְנוּ ashemin anachnu — we are guilty (42:21; the first corporate confession of the brothers — twenty years of suppressed guilt spoken aloud)  |  וַיֵּבְךְּ wayyevk — he wept (42:24; Joseph turns away and weeps — first of five weeping scenes; tears controlled, test continues)

Textual Notes

42:21אֲשֵׁמִים אֲנַחְנוּ — "we are guilty"
MT ashemin anachnu — "we are guilty/bear guilt." The verb asham covers both guilt and its consequence (guilt-feeling, guilt-offering). LXX ἔνοχοί ἐσμεν — "we are liable/guilty."
The brothers' confession is the most explicit guilt-acknowledgment in the Joseph cycle. The word asham is the same root used for the guilt-offering (asham) in Leviticus. The connection is probably deliberate in the final form of the text: the brothers' guilt (asham) requires the equivalent of a guilt-offering before reconciliation can occur. Judah's substitute-offer in ch.44 functions narratively as that offering.

Scholarly Debates

Joseph's Testing: Revenge, Righteousness, or Redemption?
Revenge
Some popular readings
Joseph, now powerful, makes his brothers suffer as they made him suffer. The tests are payback dressed up as official procedure.
Moral testing for repentance
Wenham, Hamilton, Sarna, most scholars
Joseph designs the tests to determine whether his brothers have changed. The cup-in-sack tests whether they will abandon Benjamin as they abandoned him. Only when Judah offers himself as substitute does Joseph know the transformation is complete.
Providential orchestration
Calvin
Joseph is not acting from personal revenge or even primarily from moral pedagogy — he is an instrument of God's design to bring his family to Egypt and fulfil the covenant promise. The tests are the means by which God's purpose is accomplished.
The revenge reading is textually unsupported — the narrative presents Joseph as restrained, weeping privately, and acting purposefully. The moral-testing reading is primary: the cup-in-sack test is specifically designed to recreate the conditions of the original betrayal. The providential reading adds the theological frame. All three are present; the moral-testing reading is the most prominent.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 42 begins the moral reversal of the Joseph cycle. The brothers who showed no compassion toward Joseph (37:24-25) now display guilt, fear, and retrospective awareness of his suffering. The test Joseph applies is not revenge but diagnosis: are these men capable of moral transformation? Chapter 42 gives the first evidence that they might be.
