Genesis 13

Abram and Lot Separate; the Land Promise Renewed

See on Map — Abraham's Call
Verses 1–7 — Return from Egypt; Conflict Between Herdsmen
1234567

Places

Negev to Bethel
Southern Canaan back to central hills
Abram retraces his steps from Egypt: Negev → the place between Bethel and Ai where he had first camped. Returning to the first altar is a act of covenant renewal — he calls on the name of the LORD at the same spot as before (12:8).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
13:1-7

MacArthur: Abraham's return from Egypt (v.1) marks a resumption of the covenant journey after the detour of ch.12. His wealth is noted (v.2) -- not as a sign of blessing through deception but as material with which he will now demonstrate covenant generosity. The conflict with Lot creates the occasion for the covenant character to be shown.

13:7

MacArthur: 'The Canaanite and Perizzite were also living in the land at that time' -- an editorial note that underscores the significance of Abraham's behaviour. The watching world of Canaan is the audience before which Abraham and Lot conduct their dispute. How the covenant family behaves in conflict matters for witness.

Hebrew Word Study

ʿāšîr a-SHEER — wealthy / rich: "Abram had become very wealthy in livestock and in silver and gold" (v.2). The covenant blessing of 12:2 is already producing material fruit — precisely the kind of flourishing that covenant faithfulness generates in the OT. The prosperity, however, creates the conflict of v.7: the land "could not support them" if they stayed together. Material blessing thus generates the occasion for the separation that will clarify each man's character — Abram's generosity vs. Lot's self-interest.

rîb REEV — quarrel / dispute / legal contention: The conflict (rîb) between Abram's and Lot's herdsmen (v.7) uses a word that carries legal resonance — a dispute that requires resolution through judgment or agreement. The narrator adds that "the Canaanites and Perizzites were also living in the land at that time" — noting that the covenant people's internal dispute is being conducted in front of the watching Canaanites. Abram's generous resolution of the rîb is thus a covenantal witness as well as a family settlement.

ʾaḥ AKH — brother / kinsman: Abram's appeal — "we are close relatives (ʾanāšîm ʾaḥîm)" (v.8) — invokes kinship obligation. The word ʾaḥ (brother) in Hebrew covers a range of relatedness from biological brother to close cousin to covenantal ally. Abram's priority of relationship over territory — offering Lot first choice of the land — models the covenant principle that the relationship matters more than the inheritance. His willingness to receive the worse portion demonstrates that he trusts the divine promise more than human negotiation.

Historical Context

None

Context

Abram's generosity to Lot is striking — as the patriarch and elder, he had every right to choose first. Instead he defers, trusting God to fulfill the promise regardless of which portion of land he receives. This is a model of faith in action: holding the promise loosely, letting God provide. Lot's choice will prove catastrophically poor.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 3:5–6Trusting in the LORD rather than leaning on one's own sight — the principle Abram embodies and Lot violates.
  • 2 Pet 2:7–8Peter calls Lot "righteous" — he was not wicked, but his choice to live near Sodom shows the danger of choosing by sight rather than faith.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:1-7

The conflict between Abram's and Lot's herdsmen is the first recorded instance of the land's inability to support both men simultaneously. Sarna: "The quarrel is not between Abram and Lot personally but between their servants — a socially important distinction. The narrator adds that 'the Canaanites and Perizzites were then living in the land' (v.7), which serves two functions: it notes that the land is occupied, making the future gift more remarkable, and it implies that the quarrel among God's people is a poor witness to their neighbours."

13:2-5

The parallel description of Abram's and Lot's wealth sets up the separation narrative. Sarna notes the blessing that produces conflict is itself a sign of the covenant's working. The very prosperity that requires separation is evidence of God's faithfulness to his promise.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:5-7

Lot's possessions have grown in parallel with Abram's — "Lot, who was moving about with Abram, also had flocks and herds and tents." Alter: "The abundance is the problem: the very blessing God has given (12:2-3) has made cohabitation impossible. The land cannot hold both of them. The blessing creates the necessity of separation — a recurring pattern in Genesis where the abundance of God's gifts generates the need for division."

13:7-8

The conflict between Abram's and Lot's herdsmen is immediately contextualised by the Canaanites and Perizzites present in the land. Alter notes this double exposure: the covenant-land conflict between the two Hebrews is observed by its current occupants. The promise is always complicated by the already-resident.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:6-7

The necessity of separation arises from abundance, not scarcity — the blessing itself creates the conflict. Calvin: "We see that even good men are sometimes entangled in contentions, not through any fault of their own, but through the malice and perverseness of others." The quarrel begins with the servants; Abram and Lot are not personally at fault. But the separation is still necessary.

13:5-7

Calvin on the conflict arising from prosperity: blessing itself produced the occasion for strife. This teaches that prosperity is not always the pure good it appears — it may be the testing of character. Abram's response will show whether the covenant has shaped his character or merely his circumstances.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:7

The NET note on the Canaanites and Perizzites "dwelling in the land" observes this is an important narrative detail: the land is occupied, not empty. The promise of land to Abraham's descendants must be understood in light of the fact that others already live there — the fulfilment will involve displacement, which the Mosaic narratives will later address theologically.

13:5-7

The NET note observes that the parenthetical comment about Canaanites and Perizzites heightens the tension: Abram and Lot are quarrelling over land they do not own, in front of its current inhabitants. The irony is structural.

Verses 8–13 — Abram's Generous Offer; Lot Chooses the Plain
8910111213

Places

Jordan Plain / Sodom region
Dead Sea basin; the kikkar (circle) of the Jordan
Lot chooses the "well-watered plain of the Jordan" — the kikkar, the circular basin around the Dead Sea. "Like the garden of the LORD" and "like Egypt" are both double-edged comparisons: the paradise comparison anticipates the destruction; the Egypt comparison signals the moral danger.
Sodom and Gomorrah
Southern Dead Sea region
Lot pitches his tents near Sodom (v.12) — a gradual approach that will become full urban residence by ch.19. The geography of compromise is traced in steps.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
13:8-11

MacArthur: Abraham's generous offer -- 'let's part company; if you go to the left, I'll go to the right' -- is the covenant character in practice. As the senior partner and the covenant heir, Abraham had the right to choose first. He surrendered that right. The generosity is possible because he trusted the divine promise, not the visible landscape.

13:12-13

MacArthur: Lot's choice of the well-watered Jordan plain is described immediately with the ominous editorial: 'the people of Sodom were wicked and were sinning greatly against the LORD.' The beauty of the land concealed the corruption of the culture. Lot chose by sight; the sight was deceiving.

Hebrew Word Study

nāśāʾ ʿayin na-SAH AY-yin — lifted his eyes: Lot "looked up and saw that the whole plain of the Jordan was well watered" (v.10) — the same gesture (wayyiśśāʾ lôṭ ʾet-ʿênāyw) that Abraham will perform after Lot's departure (v.14) when God tells him to "look from where you are." Lot's gaze is horizontal and calculating — he sees the land "like the garden of the LORD, like the land of Egypt." Abraham's gaze will be vertical and covenantal — he sees the land as divine gift. How one looks reveals the orientation of one's heart.

Sĕdom se-DOM — Sodom: The narrator's aside — "the people of Sodom were wicked and were sinning greatly against the LORD" (v.13) — is a warning embedded in the geography. Lot chooses the Jordan plain for its fertility, gravitating toward Sodom without knowing (or perhaps without heeding) what its character will mean for his family. The rightward orientation of Lot's choice (v.11 — "Lot journeyed east") echoes the east-of-Eden geography of sin and exile (3:24; 4:16; 11:2). The compass direction is morally freighted in Genesis.

pārad pa-RAD — to separate / to part from: The separation (pārad) of Abram and Lot (v.11) is the moment of narrative divergence: Lot's story will end in the ashes of Sodom; Abram's will proceed to the stars of the covenant promise. The same root appears in the creation narrative (1:4, 6, 7, 14, 18) where God separates (yabdēl) the elements of creation. Separation in Genesis is often the condition of new beginnings: light from darkness, waters above from waters below, Abram from his homeland, and now Abram from Lot.

Historical Context

None

Context

Lot's choice is described in Eden-like language — the Jordan Valley "like the garden of the LORD." This echoes 2:8–9, but the paradise is illusory: Sodom lurks at the end of the road. The text tells us immediately that Sodom's men were "wicked, great sinners" — information Lot apparently did not consider. He chose by sight, Abram by faith.

    Verses 14–18 — God Reaffirms the Promise to Abram
    1415161718

    Places

    Hebron / Mamre
    Southern Canaan hill country; modern Hebron
    Abram settles at the great trees of Mamre near Hebron. This will become the primary covenant camp — the site of the three visitors (ch.18), Sarah's burial negotiations (ch.23), and the family tomb at Machpelah. Hebron is the covenant's domestic centre.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    13:14-17

    MacArthur: Immediately after Lot's departure, God reaffirms the covenant with its most expansive territorial promise to date: all the land in every direction, as far as you can see (v.14-15). The loss of Lot occasions the renewal of the promise. God's covenant purpose expands precisely when human circumstances contract.

    13:18

    MacArthur: Abraham built an altar at Hebron (Mamre) -- worship marks each significant stage of the covenant journey. The altars at Shechem (12:7), Bethel (12:8), and Hebron (13:18) are the punctuation marks of Abraham's progressive possession of the land through worship. He worships before he fully possesses.

    Hebrew Word Study

    qûm hithallek KOOM hit-ha-LEKH — arise, walk through: God's instruction after Lot's departure — "Go, walk through the length and breadth of the land" (v.17) — is a covenant land-grant formula. Walking through territory was an ancient legal act of possession-taking. The command hithallek ("walk about in it") uses the same Hithpael stem as the "walking with God" of Enoch (5:22, 24) and Noah (6:9) — Abram's walking through the land is simultaneously a legal claim and a spiritual act of covenant obedience.

    ṣāpōn wānegeb wāqedem wayyāmāh tsa-FON ve-NEH-gev ve-KE-dem ve-YA-mah — north and south and east and west: God's instruction to look "north and south, east and west" (v.14) and "the land you see I will give to you and your offspring forever" is a four-directional land-grant covering the entire visual field. The gesture is comprehensive and symbolic — the entire compass of the covenant land falls within the scope of God's promise. The same four-directional formula appears in the tribal allotments of later Israel.

    ʿāpar a-FAR — dust / fine soil: "I will make your offspring like the dust of the earth" (v.16) — the ʿāpar (dust) image is drawn from the creation of Adam (2:7: "formed from the dust of the ground"). The promise of innumerable descendants is thus grounded in the creation metaphor of humanity's materiality — as uncountable as the particles of the earth itself. The same promise will be elaborated with the stars of heaven (15:5) and the sand of the seashore (22:17), each image emphasising a different dimension of the covenant's scope.

    Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    Abram
    Patriarch; model of generosity and faith
    By surrendering the first choice to Lot, Abram demonstrates the counter-intuitive economics of faith: release what you can see and trust what God has promised. The promise is immediately renewed after Lot departs (v.14).
    Lot
    Nephew; contrast figure to Abram
    Lot's choice is tragic in slow motion — he moves toward Sodom by stages: the valley (v.11), among the cities (v.12), then to Sodom itself (13:12 → 14:12 → 19:1, sitting in the gate). Each chapter he is deeper in.

    Ancient Sources

    Josephus, Antiquities 1.8.1–2
    Josephus notes that the region of Sodom was exceptionally fertile before divine judgment, and that traces of divine fire could still be seen in his day (1st c. AD) — possibly referring to the sulfur-rich terrain around the Dead Sea.
    Shows the text was read geographically/historically in the 1st century.

    Reception History

    Augustine, City of God XI.1
    Augustine uses the Abram/Lot separation to illustrate the two cities: Abram represents the City of God (ordered by love of God), Lot drifts toward the earthly city (ordered by self-interest).
    John Chrysostom
    Chrysostom praised Abram's yielding to Lot as a supreme act of virtue: "He gave up the whole earth to his nephew" — despite being the patriarch with prior claim.

    Literary Structure

    The Generous Offer: Covenant Character Displayed13:8-9The elder gives the choice to the younger. The generosity is possible because Abram trusts the divine promise will hold regardless of which land he takes. Covenant faith enables the generous gesture.
    Land Renewed After Separation13:14-17The divine reaffirmation comes immediately after Lot's departure -- filling the relational vacuum with the most expansive statement of the land promise to date. The loss of Lot occasions the renewal of the greatest promise.

    Scholarly Debates

    Lot: Righteous or Compromised?
    Righteous alien
    2 Pet 2:7-8, Wenham
    Peter explicitly calls Lot "a righteous man who was distressed by the depraved conduct of the lawless." Lot's righteousness is apostolically attested even if his choices were unwise.
    Progressively compromised
    Hamilton, Sarna, Alter
    The narrative traces a downward trajectory: Lot chooses the Jordan plain (ch.13), moves near Sodom (13:12), lives in Sodom (14:12), is at the city gate (19:1 — a position of civic standing). Each step represents deeper integration.
    Both readings capture something true. Peter's testimony should not be dismissed; Lot was genuinely different from Sodom's inhabitants. But the narrative's trajectory is one of gradual compromise — choosing well-watered land over covenant community, choosing civic standing in Sodom over separation. Righteousness and compromise can coexist in one person.
