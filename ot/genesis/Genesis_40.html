Genesis 40

Joseph Interprets the Dreams of the Cupbearer and Baker

Verses 1–8 — The Cupbearer and Baker in Prison; Their Dreams
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

פִּתְרֹן pitrôn — interpretation: A word of Egyptian origin appearing only in the Joseph story. Its use grounds the narrative in authentic Egyptian cultural context — dream interpretation was a specialized priestly function in Egypt.

הֲלוֹא לֵאלֹהִים פִּתְרֹנִים hălôʾ lēʾlōhîm pitrōnîm — do not interpretations belong to God?: Joseph's response is theologically crucial: he does not claim personal skill but attributes the capacity for dream interpretation entirely to God. He is a conduit, not a practitioner. This is the same self-understanding Joseph maintains before Pharaoh (41:16).

Historical Context

The encounter with Pharaoh's cupbearer and baker in prison is the penultimate step in Joseph's rise to power. The two officials are imprisoned during an apparent investigation (the cause is unspecified), and Joseph is assigned to attend them. Their dreams — dreamed on the same night — create the occasion for Joseph's first display of interpretive gift in Egypt. Joseph's question to them — "Do not interpretations belong to God?" — is the theological claim that will define his role: he is the conduit of divine revelation, not the source of it.

Context

Chapter 40 is structurally a hinge. It connects Joseph's prison period to his court period, and it prepares Pharaoh's need for an interpreter (ch.41). The dreams of the cupbearer and baker mirror each other in form but diverge in outcome — the same motifs (three) yield opposite results (life/death). Joseph's pastoral attention to troubled men (v.6-7: "he saw that they were troubled... why are your faces downcast?") shows a quality of care that will characterize his leadership throughout.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
40:1-3

The imprisonment of Pharaoh's chief cupbearer and chief baker places Joseph in the same facility as two high-ranking royal officials. The providential precision is characteristic of the Joseph cycle: the man who needs access to Pharaoh is imprisoned with the two men who have direct access to Pharaoh's table. Joseph's path to Pharaoh runs through the prison and through these two men — neither of which he could have arranged.

40:5-7

Both officials dream on the same night — a detail that Joseph will later use to argue the divine significance of Pharaoh's own paired dreams (41:25-32). The dreaming is itself significant; the troubled response to the dreams is what draws Joseph's attention. Joseph asks about their troubled faces — he is attending to them pastorally even while imprisoned himself. The attentiveness to others' welfare in the midst of one's own suffering is a mark of Joseph's character.

40:8

"Do not interpretations belong to God? Tell me your dreams." Joseph's answer is a theological claim before it is an interpretive offer. He does not say "I can interpret dreams" — he says "God can interpret dreams; therefore tell me." The offer of divine interpretation is grounded in God's sovereignty over revelation. This is precisely the claim he will make to Pharaoh (41:16: "I cannot do it, but God will give Pharaoh the answer").

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
40:1

The crimes of the cupbearer and baker are not specified. Sarna notes this deliberate vagueness: the narrative does not need the cause of their imprisonment, only the fact of it. The specific situation — high-ranking officials in the same facility as Joseph — is what matters providentially. The cause of their imprisonment is irrelevant to the story; their position in prison is essential.

40:8

Joseph's declaration — "Do not interpretations belong to God?" — is the most theologically explicit statement in the chapter. Sarna: "Joseph's claim locates dream interpretation within the framework of divine revelation. He is not claiming personal ability but divine access. The statement distinguishes Joseph from the Egyptian professional interpreters who later fail before Pharaoh (41:8) — they claim technical skill; Joseph claims divine mediation."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
40:5-6

Both officials dream on the same night — simu mutton echad — a phrase Alter notes is literarily significant. The symmetry of the two dreams (one morning, one fatal outcome) creates the narrative structure of the entire episode. The dreams are paired; their interpretations are paired; their outcomes are paired. Alter: "The symmetry is architecturally perfect — the narrative matches form to content. Joseph the dreamer becomes Joseph the interpreter of paired dreams, a skill he will deploy again with Pharaoh."

40:8

Alter on Joseph's theological claim: "Joseph does not volunteer an interpretation; he reframes the interpretive enterprise. Before interpreting, he establishes the epistemological ground: interpretations come from God. This is not false modesty — it is a genuine repositioning of what dream interpretation means. The professional interpreters who fail before Pharaoh (41:8) have no such ground. Joseph's interpretations succeed because they are grounded in divine revelation, not technical expertise."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
40:5-7

Calvin on Joseph's pastoral attentiveness: "Although Joseph was in prison, yet he does not neglect the duties of humanity, but compassionately inquires into the cause of the sadness of his companions in captivity. By this example we are taught that we ought to perform acts of kindness to our neighbours, even when in circumstances of difficulty ourselves." Joseph's suffering does not excuse him from attending to others' suffering. The imprisoned man still has pastoral capacity.

40:8

Calvin on divine interpretation: "Joseph, when asked for an interpretation, does not seek praise for himself, as if he possessed some natural gift, but transfers all to God. He declares that it is not his own work, but that of God alone, to give the interpretation. Hereby he professes his faith, and declares that he is the servant of the living God, who has the knowledge of things hidden."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
40:1

The NET note on "chief cupbearer" (sar hamashqim) and "chief baker" (sar ha'ofim) explains these were senior palace officials responsible for food and drink preparation and service to Pharaoh. The cupbearer in particular was a position of intimate trust — Nehemiah 1:11 provides the most famous biblical example. The note observes that both officials had access to the most politically sensitive zone of the palace: Pharaoh's personal food supply.

40:8

The NET note on "do not interpretations belong to God?" observes this is the first time in the Egyptian section that Joseph explicitly invokes divine authority. The note compares Daniel 2:28 (Daniel's similar claim before Nebuchadnezzar: "there is a God in heaven who reveals mysteries") and observes that both Joseph and Daniel establish the same principle: divine revelation, not human technique, is the source of prophetic interpretation.

Verses 9–23 — Joseph Interprets the Dreams; the Forgotten Promise

Hebrew Word Study

נָשָׂא רֹאשׁ nāśāʾ rōʾš — lift up the head: The idiom means to restore someone to favor (v.13) — and also to execute someone (v.19). The exact same phrase carries opposite meanings for the two men, a dark pun that only becomes clear in v.20.

יוֹם הֻלֶּדֶת yôm hulledet — birthday / day of birth: Pharaoh's birthday feast is the only birthday celebration mentioned in the Torah. Birthdays as royal celebrations are well attested in Egyptian papyri.

שָׁכַח šākaḥ — forgot: The cupbearer "forgot" (šākaḥ) Joseph. The word echoes through the Joseph narrative as a contrast to God's remembering (41:1: "after two whole years"). Human forgetting sets the stage for divine remembering.

Historical Context

The two dream interpretations are structurally mirrored: the cupbearer's three branches = three days until restoration; the baker's three baskets = three days until execution. The symmetry is precise and devastating. Joseph's request to the cupbearer — "remember me when it goes well with you" — is the most vulnerable moment in the Joseph narrative: the man of faith asking for human help. The chapter ends with the cupbearer forgetting — the bleakest verse in the cycle before the turn of chapter 41.

Context

The parallel dreams (three branches/three baskets) with opposite outcomes demonstrate that the same divine gift of interpretation serves both good and difficult news. Joseph does not soften the baker's interpretation — he delivers the death sentence clearly. This integrity in hard words is consistent with Joseph's character throughout. The final verse — "the chief cupbearer did not remember Joseph, but forgot him" — is the low point of the chapter and the setup for ch.41. Two more years will pass before the moment arrives. The delay is part of the plan.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 41:1After two whole years, Pharaoh dreams — the cupbearer's forgetting is ended by Pharaoh's need.
  • Ps 105:19The LORD's word tests Joseph — the years of waiting are not abandonment but refining.
  • Gen 50:20God's purpose operates through every delay and every forgetfulness: "you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
40:9-13

The cupbearer's dream — three branches, blossoms, grapes pressed into Pharaoh's cup — is dense with the imagery of productive natural process: budding, blossoming, bearing fruit, pressing wine. Joseph's interpretation unpacks the temporal code: three branches = three days. The positive interpretation is given with confidence and completeness. There is no hesitation or hedging in Joseph's reading of the good news.

40:14-15

Joseph's request — "remember me when it goes well with you... mention me to Pharaoh" — is followed immediately by the only autobiographical statement he makes in the entire cycle: "I was forcibly carried off from the land of the Hebrews, and even here I have done nothing to deserve being put in this dungeon." The complaint is factually accurate and theologically significant: Joseph names the injustice plainly. He is not passive; he asks for help.

40:16-19

The baker, hearing the positive interpretation, offers his own dream. Joseph's reading reverses the outcome: three baskets = three days until execution. The interpretive principle (three = three days) is the same; the outcome is opposite. Joseph delivers the bad news without softening. The interpreter of God's word does not adjust the message for palatability.

40:23

"The chief cupbearer did not remember Joseph; he forgot him." The two-clause statement is the chapter's devastating ending. Two years will pass (41:1) before the memory returns. The man of faith who asked for human help is failed by human memory. Providence uses even the forgetting: Joseph must wait two more years in prison so that he appears before Pharaoh at precisely the right moment. The cupbearer's failure is Providence's timing.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
40:14-15

Joseph's request — "but when all goes well with you, remember me and show me kindness; mention me to Pharaoh" — is the only time in the Joseph cycle that he explicitly asks for human intervention on his behalf. Sarna: "The request does not undermine Joseph's trust in God; it is a legitimate use of human means alongside divine trust. Joseph does not refuse human help as a test of faith; he asks for it and then waits. The refusal of human means in the name of faith is not biblical wisdom."

40:23

The cupbearer's forgetting is narrated with stark simplicity. Sarna: "Wayyishkacheihu — and he forgot him. The single word ends the chapter. Two years of Joseph's life are contained in this one word. The verb is the opposite of wayyizkor — the divine remembering that turned the tide for Noah (8:1), for Rachel (30:22), and that will turn the tide for Joseph when God moves Pharaoh to seek a dream interpreter. Human forgetting and divine remembering are the two poles of the chapter."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
40:20-22

The fulfillment of the two interpretations is narrated in rapid succession: the cupbearer is restored, the baker is hanged. The narrative speed is calibrated: the reader does not need dwelling on outcomes the interpretation already announced. The point of the rapid fulfillment is the validation of Joseph as an accurate interpreter. When Pharaoh's dreams come (ch.41), the reader already knows: Joseph's interpretations come true.

40:23

The chapter ends on wayyishkacheihu — "he forgot him." Alter: "The single-word ending is narratively and theologically perfect. Joseph, who has maintained extraordinary faithfulness through every injustice, is left at the mercy of human forgetfulness. But the narrative has already established that 'the LORD was with Joseph' (39:21). The human forgetting is real; it does not override the divine remembering that will move the story forward in chapter 41."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
40:14-15

Calvin on Joseph's request: "In seeking the aid of the chief butler, Joseph did not transgress the bounds of duty. He was not wrong to use lawful means for his deliverance... But seeing that the butler was ungrateful and forgetful, the Lord suffered Joseph to be kept in prison two years longer, that, having been sufficiently humbled, he might come forth, in his own time, to the light which God had prepared for him." The delay is disciplinary, not punitive.

40:23

Calvin on the forgetting: "So it often happens with the saints of God, that when they seem to themselves to be on the threshold of deliverance, they are again frustrated. Yet we must not therefore conclude that God has forgotten them; for he allows them to struggle with difficulties that they may learn to hang on him alone." The cupbearer's forgetting is not God's forgetting but God's timing — Joseph must learn to lean on God alone before he is trusted with the salvation of nations.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
40:14

The NET note on "remember me and show kindness to me" observes that the phrase "show kindness" (asah chesed) uses the covenant-loyalty term chesed — the same word used for God's steadfast love to the patriarchs. Joseph asks the cupbearer for chesed — the same quality God has shown Joseph. The irony is that the cupbearer will fail to show chesed (v.23) while God will continue to show it throughout.

40:23

The NET note on "he forgot him" discusses the two-year gap that follows (41:1). The note observes that the Hebrew simply says "two full years" (shnatayyim yamim) — two complete years of waiting after the cupbearer's restoration. The note compares the parallel delay in Daniel's story and observes that in both cases the delay ensures that when the man of God appears before the king, the king's need is urgent enough to create maximum impact.

Ancient Sources

Chester Beatty Papyrus III
The Egyptian Dream Book (c. 1279–1213 BC) catalogs dream symbols and their meanings — vines, birds, bread all appear as interpretive categories.
The papyrus confirms that Joseph's context (professional dream interpretation, specific symbols like the vine and baskets) reflects authentic Egyptian dream culture.
Egyptian Birthday Feasts
Papyri document royal birthday celebrations as significant court events with pardons and appointments — consistent with v.20's use of Pharaoh's birthday as the occasion for the cupbearer's restoration.
The historical detail of v.20 is culturally accurate.

Reception History

Calvin, Commentary
The cupbearer's forgetting is not a setback to God's plan — it is part of it. Calvin argues that divine timing is always better than the timing we would choose for ourselves. Joseph's waiting produces the exact positioning needed for Pharaoh's moment.
Luther, Lectures
The chapter demonstrates that God uses even human ingratitude and forgetfulness as instruments of his purpose. The cupbearer's failure to remember Joseph leads, two years later, to the greatest possible outcome.

(the theological claim that grounds all of Joseph's interpretive work)  |  וַיִּשְׁכָּחֵהוּ wayyishkacheihu — he forgot him (40:23; the single word that contains two years; opposite of wayyizkor — divine remembering)

Textual Notes

40:8הֲלוֹא לֵאלֹהִים פִּתְרֹנִים — "do not interpretations belong to God?"
MT halo leElohim pitronim — "is it not to God that interpretations belong?" The word pitronim (interpretations) is from pathar (to interpret dreams) — a word appearing only in the Joseph cycle (40:8; 40:16,22; 41:8,11-13,15). LXX οὐχὶ διὰ τοῦ θεοῦ ἡ σύγκρισις αὐτῶν ἐστιν — "is not the interpretation of them through God?"
The word pathar (interpret) occurs only in the Joseph cycle in the OT and is possibly an Egyptian loanword (cf. Egyptian ptr = to see/observe). The distribution is significant: dream interpretation vocabulary is confined to the Joseph-in-Egypt chapters, reflecting the Egyptian cultural setting where dream interpretation was a professional discipline.

Scholarly Debates

Joseph and Daniel: Parallel Narratives?
Deliberate typological parallel
Most commentators
Both Joseph and Daniel are faithful Israelites in a foreign court; both interpret dreams for foreign kings; both are unjustly imprisoned and then exalted; both credit God as the true source of interpretation. The parallels are too numerous to be coincidental.
Independent historical events
Conservative scholars
The parallels reflect the recurring pattern of God using faithful exiles in foreign courts — a real historical phenomenon. Both Joseph and Daniel are historical figures whose stories naturally share features because they faced similar situations.
Both readings are correct and not in conflict. The historical situations genuinely parallel each other, and the biblical author(s) shaped the Daniel narrative with awareness of the Joseph tradition. The typological connection is intentional at the literary level while both figures are historical. Daniel is presented as a "new Joseph" for the Babylonian exile.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 40 is the pivot chapter — the moment at which Joseph's gifts are first deployed in Egypt, and the moment at which human failure (the cupbearer's forgetting) extends the wait. The chapter's theology is the theology of divine timing: the right man with the right gift at the wrong human moment must wait for the right divine moment. Two years of prison after the cupbearer's restoration are not abandonment but preparation.
