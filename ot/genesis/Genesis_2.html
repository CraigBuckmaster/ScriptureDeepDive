Genesis 2

The Garden of Eden and the Making of Woman

See on Timeline — Creation
Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–3 — The Seventh Day: God Rests
123

Hebrew Word Study

שָׁבַת shavat — ceased / rested: V.2: The root means to cease, to stop — not primarily to recuperate from exhaustion but to complete and withdraw from a finished work. God's rest is not recovery; it is the royal declaration that creation is complete and good.

וַיְקַדֵּשׁ wayyeqaddesh — consecrated / declared holy: V.3: The first thing God declares holy in Scripture is not a person, a place, or an object — it is a time. The sanctification of the seventh day is the first act of consecration in the Bible. All subsequent Sabbath theology flows from this moment.

Historical Context

The Sabbath pattern established here — six days of work, one of rest — became the structural backbone of Israel's weekly calendar (Exod 20:8-11). The connection to creation theology means the Sabbath is not merely a social institution but a participation in the divine rhythm embedded in creation itself. Ancient Near Eastern calendars often had irregular rest days tied to lunar cycles or ominous days; Israel's seven-day week tied to creation was distinctive.

Context

The Sabbath commandment grounds Israel’s weekly rest directly in Genesis 2:2–3 — “for in six days the LORD made the heavens and the earth.”

Cross-Reference

  • Ex 20:8–11The Sabbath commandment grounds Israel’s weekly rest directly in Genesis 2:2–3 — “for in six days the LORD made the heavens and the earth.”
  • Heb 4:1–11The author of Hebrews reads Sabbath rest as pointing forward to an eschatological rest that still remains for God’s people.
  • John 5:17Jesus claims “My Father is always at his work to this very day, and I too am working” — a provocative reinterpretation of the divine Sabbath rest.
  • Rev 21:3“God’s dwelling place is now among the people” — the cosmic temple imagery of Genesis 2 reaches its fulfilment in the new creation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1–3

The seventh day is the first "holy" thing named in Scripture — not a place, not a person, but a time. God's blessing and sanctification of the seventh day establishes the theological basis for all subsequent Sabbath observance. The Sabbath is holy because God made it so; it carries his own rest as its content.

2:2

God "rested" — not from exhaustion but in the sense of completion. The Hebrew shavat means to cease, to stop. Creation is finished; God withdraws from creative activity and surveys a completed work. This is the posture of the craftsman who sets down his tools and looks at what he has made.

2:3

The Sabbath principle is grounded in creation, not in the Mosaic covenant — which is why it appears before the fall, before sin, before Israel. When God commands Sabbath observance at Sinai (Exod 20), he is not inventing a new institution but directing Israel to participate in a rhythm woven into the fabric of creation itself.

2:1–3

The forming/filling pattern of creation days 1-6 finds its crown in day 7. Days 1-3 formed spaces; days 4-6 filled them. But the culmination is not the creation of humanity (day 6) but the rest of God (day 7). Creation's goal is not productivity but Sabbath — communion with the Creator in the completeness of a finished work.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:1–3

Shavat — to cease — has no object in the MT: "God ceased on the seventh day from all his work." The absence of an object is significant: God did not cease from something incomplete but from a complete work. Sarna notes this is the seventh use of "created" in the chapter — the seventh-day holiness crowns the sevenfold pattern. The Sabbath is not an appendage to creation but its climax and interpretive key.

2:3

Wayyeqaddesh oto — "he made it holy / set it apart." This is the first occurrence of the root q-d-sh (holy) in the Bible. Before any altar, any priest, any law — holiness first attaches to time. The Jewish tradition's insistence that Shabbat is the most sacred institution in the entire Torah finds its root here: God himself declared time holy before he declared anything else holy.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:1–3

The literary movement from chapter 1 into these three verses is a deliberate deceleration. Chapter 1 drives forward with relentless energy: "and God said... and there was... and God saw... evening and morning." Here the pace halts. Three verses, three statements about the seventh day, three repetitions of "seventh day" — the chapter ends by lingering on completion. The formal symmetry tells the reader: this is the point toward which everything has been building.

2:3

The phrase "which God created to do" (asher bara elohim laasot) at the end of v.3 is grammatically awkward — literally "which God created by doing." Alter notes this may be intentional: the phrase suggests creation as ongoing act, the world made as something that goes on making itself under God's governance. Creation is not a static artifact but a dynamic work.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1–3

The Sabbath is the seal of creation — God's declaration that his work is complete and good. Calvin reads the Sabbath as a sacrament of creation: as circumcision was a sign of the Abrahamic covenant and baptism is a sign of the new covenant, the Sabbath is the sign of the creation covenant. It assures us that God completed his work and invites us to rest in what he has done rather than in our own labours.

2:3

That God "blessed" the seventh day — not merely sanctified it — shows the Sabbath is meant to be a gift, not a burden. Calvin: "God invites and allures us to himself by the sweetness of this blessing, that the whole of our life may be conformed to his eternal Sabbath." The Sabbath is not a restriction but an invitation to share in the divine rest that crowns all of creation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:2

The NET note on wayyishbot ("rested / ceased") observes that the LXX reads "sixth day" here, likely an early scribal correction to avoid the reading that God was still working on the seventh day. The MT reads "seventh day" in both v.2 and v.3, which is the lectio difficilior — the harder reading is more likely original. God ceased from his work on the seventh day, having completed it.

2:3

The NET note on wayyeqaddesh oto ("he consecrated it") explains that the Piel of q-d-sh means to declare or treat as sacred, to set apart from ordinary use. The note draws the connection to Exod 20:8 and the Mosaic Sabbath commandment, observing that Matthew's Gospel (12:1-8) reads the Sabbath in light of Jesus as "Lord of the Sabbath" — the one who embodies what the seventh day always pointed toward.

Verses 4–7 — The Second Account: Man Formed from Dust
4567

Hebrew Word Study

תּוֹלְדוֹת toledoth — generations / account: V.4: The formula "these are the generations of" (elleh toledoth) appears eleven times in Genesis as a structuring device. Each use introduces a new section of the narrative. The toledoth of the heavens and earth (2:4) transitions from the cosmic account of chapter 1 to the human-scale account of chapter 2.

יִצֶר yatsar — formed / shaped: V.7: Used of a potter shaping clay. God forms man as a craftsman shapes a vessel — intimate, hands-on, direct. The word contrasts with bara (created, ch.1) which is effortless sovereign speech. Here the emphasis is on the personal, relational character of human creation.

נִשְׁמַת חַיִּים nishmat chayyim — breath of life: V.7: Nishmat — from neshamah, breath / spirit. The divine breath animated clay into a living being. The same root appears in Job 32:8 and 33:4: "the breath of the Almighty gives him understanding." Humanity is not merely biological creature but breath-bearer — the one in whom the divine neshamah dwells.

Historical Context

The "second creation account" (2:4-25) uses a different name for God — YHWH Elohim (the LORD God) — and narrates creation from a human perspective rather than a cosmic one. It zooms in on what chapter 1 summarised: the formation of the man from the ground. The use of "ground" (adamah) and "man" (adam) creates a wordplay that defines human identity: we are adamah-creatures, earth-beings, formed from the same substance we cultivate.

Context

Chapter 2 is not a contradiction of chapter 1 but a close-up lens. Chapter 1 gives the architecture; chapter 2 gives the relational texture. The shift from Elohim (cosmic transcendence) to YHWH Elohim (covenant intimacy) marks the shift in perspective. God who spoke galaxies into being now stoops to the ground and forms a single human being with hands-on care.

Cross-Reference

  • Job 33:4The Spirit of God has made me; the breath of the Almighty gives me life — the neshamah of Gen 2:7 recurs throughout wisdom literature as the ground of human consciousness.
  • 1 Cor 15:45The first man Adam became a living being; the last Adam a life-giving spirit — Paul reads Gen 2:7 as the type of which Christ is the antitype.

Places

The Garden of Eden
Location debated; likely Mesopotamian region
The garden is placed "in the east" — a directional marker that will carry theological weight when humanity is expelled eastward (3:24). Its rivers (Pishon, Gihon, Tigris, Euphrates) ground the garden in recognisable geography, making it historical space, not mythological space.
Tigris and Euphrates
Modern Iraq/Turkey border region
Two of the four rivers are the Tigris and Euphrates — the major waterways of ancient Mesopotamia. Their presence anchors the garden in the same geography as Ur (Abram's city) and Babel. The garden and the later covenant history share the same watershed.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:4

The "toledoth formula" — "these are the generations of" — is Genesis's structural marker. It appears eleven times and introduces a new stage in the narrative. Here it transitions from the cosmic creation account to the human-scale account. The two accounts are complementary, not contradictory: chapter 1 gives God's view from above; chapter 2 gives the human view from within.

2:7

The formation of man from the dust of the ground is one of the most theologically freighted statements in the entire Bible. Humanity is formed from the earth — we share our material substance with the ground we cultivate. The divine breath (nishmat chayyim) is what makes the difference: without it, we are merely dust; with it, we are nefesh chayah — living beings bearing the breath of God.

2:4

The change from "God" (Elohim) in chapter 1 to "the LORD God" (YHWH Elohim) in chapter 2 is not a sign of two authors but of two perspectives. Elohim emphasises transcendence and sovereignty; YHWH emphasises covenant relationship and personal presence. As the narrative zooms in from cosmos to garden to human, the name for God zooms in as well.

2:7

Nefesh chayah — "living being" — is the same phrase used for the animals in 1:20,24. What distinguishes humanity is not a different grade of life but the image of God (1:26) and the divine breath (2:7). The image and the breath together constitute what it means to be human: a creature of earth who bears the imprint of heaven.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:4

Elleh toledoth hashamayim veha'aretz — "these are the generations of the heavens and the earth." Sarna: the toledoth formula does not introduce a parallel account of creation but a sequel — what followed from the creation of heavens and earth. The "generations" of the cosmos are the human story that emerges from it. Genesis 2 is not a second cosmogony but the first human chapter of the cosmic story.

2:7

Wayyitser YHWH Elohim et haAdam afar min haadamah — "the LORD God formed the man, dust from the ground." The wordplay adam/adamah (man/ground) is untranslatable in English. Sarna notes this wordplay defines human nature structurally: adam is the creature of adamah, bound to the earth, dependent on it, shaped from it. The breath of God does not make humanity alien to the earth but a living bridge between earth and heaven.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:4

The second creation account begins in medias res — with a landscape of non-growth: "no shrub of the field was yet in the earth... for the LORD God had not sent rain... and there was no man to till the ground." Alter notes the narrative technique: the story sets up a landscape of lack — no vegetation, no rain, no man — so that when God creates the man and the garden, they appear as answers to specific needs. Creation responds to lack; the man's vocation (to till) is announced before his creation.

2:7

Wayyipach beappav nishmat chayyim — "he blew into his nostrils the breath of life." The physical intimacy of this image is extraordinary: God bending close, breath-to-nostril, animating clay. This is creation as an act of intimacy, not distance. The verb naphach (to blow) is used of the action of a flute player or a smith blowing on coals — a skilled, careful, directed act.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:4

Moses here begins to explain more fully what he had briefly touched upon in chapter 1. The repetition is not redundancy but amplification: the Spirit of God desires that we should not only know that the world was created but should understand the manner and order of that creation, and above all the dignity of mankind. The second account is given so that we might see ourselves as God's special care.

2:7

God formed man from the dust to humble human pride. We are not made from some noble material but from clay — the lowest substance. And yet into this clay God breathed his own breath. Calvin: "Moses first describes the ignoble original of our body, to repress the pride of the flesh; and then he adds the divine inspiration of the soul, to prevent us from lying torpid in the earth." The tension between our dusty origin and our divine breath-bearing is the defining tension of human existence.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:4

The NET note on toledoth explains this is a narrative marker that introduces the "account" or "story" of what follows, not merely a genealogy. The note discusses the literary structure of Genesis as organised by these toledoth formulae — eleven in total — and argues they are the editorial seams of the book rather than the work of different authors.

2:7

The NET note on nishmat chayyim ("breath of life") distinguishes this from ruach (spirit/wind) in 1:2. Neshamah specifically refers to the breath that animates — the vital force that makes a creature alive. The note observes that in 1:20,24 animals are called nefesh chayah without receiving the divine breath; 2:7 specifies the unique source of human life: not merely the creative word but the direct divine exhalation.

Verses 8–17 — The Garden of Eden; the Command
891011121314151617

Hebrew Word Study

גַּן gan — garden / enclosure: V.8: Not a wild paradise but a cultivated enclosure — the word implies a walled or fenced space, tended and ordered. Eden is not wilderness but a managed garden, a place of purposeful cultivation. The same root appears in the Arabic janna (garden/paradise), which entered English as "garden."

עֵץ הַדַּעַת טוֹב וָרָע ets hadaat tov vara — tree of the knowledge of good and evil: V.9: Daat — knowledge — is not merely intellectual information but experiential knowledge, the deep knowing that comes from participation. "Good and evil" as a merism (two opposites representing the whole) means "everything" — complete wisdom, the full range of moral experience. The tree offers total self-determination: to know all, to judge all, to need no outside authority.

לְעָבְדָהּ וּלְשָׁמְרָהּ leovdah uleshomrah — to work it and keep it: V.15: Two verbs that define the human vocation before the fall: abad (to work, serve, till) and shamar (to keep, guard, preserve). The same two verbs appear in Numbers 3:7-8 and 18:5-6 as the duties of the Levitical priests. Adam in the garden is a priestly figure serving and guarding the sacred space of God's dwelling.

Historical Context

The four rivers of Eden (Pishon, Gihon, Tigris, Euphrates) have been the subject of extensive geographical speculation. The Tigris and Euphrates are identifiable; the Pishon and Gihon are not certainly known. Some scholars locate Eden in Mesopotamia (where the Tigris and Euphrates originate); others read the geography as cosmic/symbolic. What is clear is that Eden is presented as the source of the world's great rivers — the centre from which blessing flows outward. The gold and precious stones mentioned (vv.11-12) suggest Eden as a place of extraordinary abundance.

Context

The garden narrative establishes three things simultaneously: the human vocation (to work and keep the garden), the human provision (every tree for food), and the human limit (not the tree of the knowledge of good and evil). The prohibition is not arbitrary cruelty but the structural definition of what it means to be a creature rather than the Creator. To eat would be to claim the Creator's prerogative of self-determination — to become one's own god.

  • Rev 22:1-2The river of life flowing from the throne — Eden's river pattern restored in the new creation.
  • Ezek 28:13You were in Eden, the garden of God — Eden as the archetype of the holy mountain.

Places

The Garden of Eden
East of Eden; trees at its centre
The tree of life and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil are placed "in the middle of the garden." Their central position is spatial theology: access to life and the boundary of human knowledge are both located at the covenant centre, not the periphery.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:8–9

The garden of Eden is not merely a pleasant landscape; it is a cosmic sanctuary — the place where God dwells with his creatures, the prototype of all subsequent sacred spaces (tabernacle, temple). The tree of life at the centre of the garden corresponds to the lampstand at the centre of the tabernacle: both are symbols of divine life-giving presence. Eden is the first temple.

2:16–17

The prohibition is the definition of creaturely limits. God gives the man "every tree" — abundance beyond measure — and withholds only one. The one prohibition is not about restriction for its own sake but about the boundary between Creator and creature. To eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil would be to claim the divine prerogative of being the ultimate judge of good and evil — to usurp God's position as moral sovereign.

2:15

The Hebrew verbs "work" (abad) and "keep" (shamar) define the human vocation before the fall. These are not merely agricultural duties: they are priestly functions. The same two verbs appear in the Torah for the work of the Levites in the tabernacle (Num 3:7-8). Adam is the first priest, tending the first sanctuary. The fall will be a priestly failure.

2:9

The tree of life and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil stand at the centre of the garden. They are not merely botanical features but theological symbols. The tree of life represents unending communion with God; the tree of knowledge represents the claim to autonomous self-determination. Between them the human choice is posed: dependence or independence, life or death.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:8

The garden is placed "in Eden, in the east." The Hebrew miqqedem can mean "in the east" or "of old / from ancient times." Both readings are defensible. The garden is located in primordial time and space — at the origin point of the world's rivers, in the ancient east. Sarna notes the four rivers function as a cosmic geography: Eden is the source of the world's nourishment, the headwaters of blessing that flow outward to all lands.

2:16–17

The command uses the strongest form of prohibition: lo tochel mimenu — "you shall not eat from it." The penalty — "in the day you eat of it you shall surely die" (mot tamut, an emphatic Hebrew idiom) — is absolute. Sarna: the tree of knowledge represents neither science nor moral discernment (humans already have moral capacity as image-bearers) but rather total autonomy — the wisdom to function without reference to God. The prohibition preserves the fundamental distinction between Creator and creature.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:9

The two central trees are named together in a curious symmetry: "the tree of life in the midst of the garden, and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil." Both are in the middle; both are withholding-and-offering. Alter notes that the tree of life disappears from the narrative after this mention until the very end (3:22,24) — it is the silent other option, the path not taken. The tree of knowledge becomes the focus of the drama; the tree of life becomes what is lost.

2:15

Wayyannachehu beghan Eden leovdah uleshomrah — "he put him in the garden of Eden to work it and keep it." The syntax is significant: the purpose clause ("to work and keep") is built into the act of placement. The human being is not placed in the garden for rest or consumption but for vocation. Purpose precedes the fall; work is not a curse but a creation-given calling. The fall will not introduce work but will corrupt it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:8–9

God planted the garden as a token of his paternal benevolence toward humanity. The abundance of provision — every tree pleasant and good for food — shows that God's first disposition toward his creature is generosity, not restriction. Calvin against those who read the prohibition as the dominant note of the garden: the prohibition is a single limit within an overwhelming abundance. The garden is primarily a place of gift, not of test.

2:16–17

The prohibition is a test of obedience and a definition of humanity's place. Calvin: "God had given man freedom to eat of all the fruits of the garden except one, and had added a threatening, that if he should eat of that one he would die. This was a trial of obedience, that Adam might know he had a lord and governor." The tree of knowledge is not intrinsically evil; what is forbidden is the act of seizing what God has reserved for himself.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:9

The NET note on ets hadaat tov vara ("tree of the knowledge of good and evil") discusses the merism "good and evil" — two polar opposites representing the totality. The note surveys interpretive options: moral knowledge, sexual knowledge, universal wisdom, judicial authority. The NET favours "wisdom that encompasses all things" — the capacity to determine for oneself what is right and wrong, functioning as one's own moral authority. This is what is forbidden to creatures.

2:16–17

The NET note on mot tamut ("you will surely die") explains this as an emphatic Hebrew construction using the infinitive absolute followed by the finite verb — literally "dying you will die." The note notes the apparent delay between the eating (ch.3) and physical death, and surveys interpretive options: spiritual death immediately, eventual physical death, or both. The NET reads the threatened death as comprehensive — spiritual estrangement from God (immediate) and physical death (eventual).

Verses 18–25 — The Making of Woman; One Flesh
1819202122232425

Hebrew Word Study

עֵזֶר כְּנֶגְדּוֹ ezer kenegdo — helper corresponding to him: V.18: Ezer — helper — is not a word for subordination; it is used eighteen times in the OT, sixteen of which refer to God as Israel's helper (Ps 121:2, 124:8). The word connotes strength that comes alongside, not from beneath. Kenegdo — "corresponding to him," "face to face," "as his counterpart" — means an equal who faces him and corresponds to his nature.

צֵלָע tsela — rib / side: V.21: Tsela appears 40 times in the OT, almost always meaning "side" — the side of a building, the side of the ark. The building of woman from the man's side (not his head to rule him, not his foot to be ruled by him — as the rabbis noted) signals that she is constituted from his very substance, taken from his side to stand beside him.

בָּשָׂר אֶחָד basar echad — one flesh: V.24: The climactic formula of the chapter. Marriage is not the achievement of union but the recognition of an original unity — man and woman were one before they were two. The reunion in marriage restores what was divided in the making of woman. Basar (flesh) means the whole embodied person — not merely the physical.

Historical Context

The ancient Near Eastern world had numerous creation myths featuring the making of woman from the body of a man, but none with the theological weight Genesis places on the event. The naming of the animals (vv.19-20) established Adam's capacity for language, culture, and discernment — and its insufficiency. No animal corresponds to him. The making of woman from his side answers a need that nothing else in creation can meet. The institution of marriage as "leaving and cleaving" in v.24 is the most foundational social institution in the Bible.

Context

The making of woman is the climax of the creation narrative. The verdict "not good" in v.18 is the first negative assessment in the entire creation account — the only thing in a good creation that is not yet good. Everything has been building toward this: the Sabbath crowned day 6, but day 6 was incomplete without the completion of the human pair. "One flesh" is not just a description of marriage; it is a statement about what humanity fundamentally is.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:18

The declaration that it is "not good" for man to be alone is the first negative assessment in the creation account. Not because solitude is sinful but because humanity in its fullness requires the complementary relationship of man and woman. The image of God — which in 1:27 is explicitly "male and female" — cannot be fully expressed in an individual. Human nature is fundamentally relational.

2:23–24

Adam's declaration — "bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh" — is the first recorded human speech in the Bible. Significantly, the first words spoken by the first human are words of recognition, of belonging, of joyful identification with another person. The capacity for language is first exercised in the direction of relationship. And the narrator's comment in v.24 institutes marriage as the foundational human social structure: leaving father and mother (priority of the marriage bond), cleaving to his wife (permanence), one flesh (union).

2:18

Ezer kenegdo — "a helper who corresponds to him." The word "helper" (ezer) does not imply inferiority. God himself is called Israel's ezer (Ps 121:2). The word describes function (coming alongside) not status (subordination). Kenegdo adds the crucial nuance: corresponding, face-to-face, an equal counterpart who mirrors and completes.

2:21–22

The building of woman from the man's side is charged with theological significance. Not from his head (to rule over him), not from his foot (to be ruled by him) — as later rabbinic tradition noted — but from his side, to stand beside him. The intimacy of this origin is the basis for the "one flesh" of marriage: she is literally constituted from his substance. Marriage is not the creation of a new union but the recognition of an original one.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:18

Lo tov heyot haAdam levaddo — "it is not good for the man to be alone." This is the only negative verdict in the creation account. Sarna notes this establishes sociality as intrinsic to human nature: the human being is not complete as an individual. The creation of woman is not an afterthought or a supplement but the completion of humanity. Humanity in its fullness is male-and-female together.

2:23–24

The wordplay ish/ishah (man/woman) — she shall be called woman (ishah) because she was taken from man (ish) — mirrors the adam/adamah wordplay of 2:7. As the human is defined by his relation to the ground, so woman is defined by her relation to man (and man to woman). The relational definition of human identity runs throughout the chapter.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:19–20

The naming of the animals is a remarkable literary episode. God brings the animals to the man to see what he will call them — a staging of human creative authority, but also, Alter notes, a dramatic technique of elimination. Each naming increases the sense of Adam's isolation: the animals parade before him, he demonstrates his mastery of language, and yet not one corresponds to him. The effect of "but for Adam no suitable helper was found" is like the resolution of a chord that has been building through dissonance.

2:24

Ve'al ken yaazov ish et aviv ve'et immo — "therefore a man leaves his father and his mother." The verse is the narrator's comment, not God's speech. Alter notes the remarkable boldness of this insertion: the narrator interrupts the primeval narrative to offer an etiological comment about contemporary marriage practice. The precedent set in Eden (a man and a woman, leaving family, becoming one) is presented as the basis for all subsequent human marriage.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:18

That God saw it was not good for man to be alone teaches us that humans are social beings by nature and creation, not merely by convention or necessity. Calvin: "Man has been created for the purpose of cohabiting with men. Whence it follows that solitude is not good for man, because he has been framed for the ordinary course of life, and not for the life of a hermit." The creation of woman is the perfection of the human social nature.

2:23–24

The one-flesh union of marriage is the human analogy of the union between Christ and the church. Calvin anticipates Paul's reading in Ephesians 5: "Moses intimates that marriage is a divine institution, and that it cannot be dissolved at the pleasure of men, since it is subject to God's government." The permanence of marriage is grounded not in human law but in the creation order — "what God has joined together."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:18

The NET note on ezer kenegdo ("helper suitable for him") gives extensive treatment to ezer — noting it is used of God as Israel's helper in Deuteronomy 33:7, Psalms 121:2, and elsewhere, which shows the word carries no implication of inferiority. The note on kenegdo explains the preposition neged means "in front of / corresponding to" — a helper who is his equal counterpart, face-to-face rather than underneath.

2:24

The NET note on basar echad ("one flesh") observes this is more than a physical statement — basar encompasses the whole person. The note also addresses the textual variant at the end of v.25 ("both naked, and they felt no shame") as the chapter's closing word — innocence before the fall. The nakedness without shame contrasts with the shame-driven clothing of 3:7,21, creating a structural bracket around the fall narrative.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Adam
First human; priest and cultivator of Eden
Formed from the ground (adamah) and animated by divine breath, Adam is simultaneously a creature of earth and a bearer of heaven. His vocation — to work and keep the garden — is priestly. His naming of the animals demonstrates linguistic creativity and cultural authority. His aloneness is the only thing declared "not good" in all creation, which defines him as essentially relational. He is the image of God who cannot be fully expressed without the corresponding image in woman.
Eve / Woman
First woman; taken from the man's side
Built from the man's side (tsela), she is constituted from his substance and stands as his equal counterpart — ezer kenegdo. The building of woman is the completion of humanity, not its supplement. She is the one for whom the man leaves father and mother, with whom he becomes one flesh. Her later role in the temptation narrative (ch.3) stands in tragic contrast to her creation-given dignity and vocation.

Ancient Sources

Enki and Ninhursag (Sumerian, c.2000 BCE)
In the Sumerian myth, the god Enki eats forbidden plants in the paradise of Dilmun and falls ill. The goddess Ninhursag creates eight healing deities to cure the eight ailing organs. One of these is created to heal his "rib" (Sumerian ti = rib / to live). The wordplay on "rib" and "life" in Sumerian has often been compared to the Genesis rib narrative.
The comparison illuminates a possible ancient Near Eastern background for the rib motif, but the theological differences are total. In the Sumerian myth, the rib-creature heals a god's illness; in Genesis, the rib-creature is the companion and equal of humanity. The purposes are entirely different.
The "Two-Faced" Human (Plato, Symposium, c.385 BCE)
Aristophanes' speech in the Symposium describes the original human as a double-being, split by Zeus into two halves, each of which constantly seeks its other half. The myth explains erotic longing as the search for one's original wholeness.
The Platonic myth and Genesis 2 share the structural intuition that the human pair were originally one and that marriage is a reunion of what was divided. But the theological frameworks are opposite: in Plato, division is punishment; in Genesis, the making of woman from man is gift. The "one flesh" of v.24 is not restoration of a damaged state but fulfilment of a created purpose.

Reception History

Philo of Alexandria, On the Creation of the World (c.20 BCE)
Philo reads the two creation accounts allegorically: the first account describes the creation of the ideal/intelligible human (the Logos-image); the second describes the creation of the material human (the earthly Adam). For Philo, "breath of life" means the rational soul (nous) that makes humanity capable of knowing God. The garden is the allegory of wisdom; the rivers are the cardinal virtues.
Augustine, The Literal Meaning of Genesis (c.401-415)
Augustine wrestled with Genesis 2 throughout his career. His mature position: the account is literal history, not allegory, but its literal meaning includes spiritual significance. The "one flesh" of marriage is literally true and simultaneously a figure of Christ and the church (following Paul in Eph 5:31-32). Augustine is the source of the reading that Eve was taken from the side while Adam slept, just as the church was born from the side of Christ on the cross (water and blood, John 19:34).
Karl Barth, Church Dogmatics III/1 (1945)
Barth reads Genesis 2 as the foundation of his relational anthropology: the human being is constituted as "I-and-Thou" from the beginning. "Male and female" is not incidental to the image of God but definitive of it. The co-humanity of man and woman — face-to-face relationship — is the creaturely analogy of the I-Thou relation within the Trinity. Marriage is thus a sign of the divine co-humanity within God.

Literary Structure

Toledoth — 2:4Structural marker: "these are the generations of the heavens and the earth" — transitions from cosmic to human scale
Landscape of Lack — 2:5-6No vegetation, no rain, no man — creates narrative expectation: what will fill these lacks?
Formation — 2:7God forms man from ground (adam/adamah wordplay), breathes life: earth-being animated by divine breath
Garden Provision — 2:8-16Setting: garden, trees, rivers, abundance. Prohibition: one tree only. Provision precedes limit.
Naming — Insufficiency — 2:19-20Animals named but none corresponds to man. Demonstrates human capacity and human need simultaneously.
Tsela — from his side. First human speech: "bone of my bones." Recognition of original unity.
Institution of Marriage — 2:24Narrator's comment: leave/cleave/one flesh. Creation-precedent for all subsequent marriage.
Genesis 2 is structured as a movement from incompleteness to completion. The landscape of lack (no vegetation, no rain, no man) generates narrative expectation; the garden provides the setting; the prohibition defines the limit; the naming sequence demonstrates human capacity but reveals human need; the making of woman resolves the "not good" and crowns the human creation with the union of "one flesh." The chapter ends with innocence — naked and unashamed — which the following chapter will shatter.

Scholarly Debates

Two Creation Accounts: Complementary or Contradictory?
Source-critical (J + P)
Wellhausen, von Rad, Speiser
Gen 1 (Elohim) and Gen 2–3 (YHWH-Elohim) are independent documents combined by a redactor; minor contradictions (creation order, divine name) reflect distinct traditions.
Literary-canonical
Alter, Wenham, Sailhamer, Hamilton
The two accounts form a unified composition: ch.1 provides the cosmic overview, ch.2 zooms into the human-garden focus. No contradiction, only change of perspective and narrative register.
Literary-canonical reading has gained significant ground since the 1980s and is the majority position in evangelical and many mainline commentaries.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 2 is the intimacy chapter of creation. Where chapter 1 gives the architecture of the cosmos, chapter 2 gives the relational texture of human existence: a man formed from earth and animated by divine breath, placed in a garden with a vocation (to work and keep), a provision (every tree), a limit (one prohibition), and a companion (built from his own side). The chapter closes with the institution of marriage as the foundational human relationship — leaving, cleaving, one flesh. The "not good" of aloneness becomes the "very good" of union.
