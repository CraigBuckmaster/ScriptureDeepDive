Genesis 24

The Marriage of Isaac and Rebekah

Verses 1–9 — Abraham's Commission: A Wife for Isaac
123456789

Places

Aram Naharaim / Nahor's city
Upper Mesopotamia; modern SE Turkey/NW Syria
The servant is sent to "my country and my own relatives" — Aram Naharaim ("Aram of the two rivers"), the region around Harran. Nahor's city is the family's Mesopotamian base. The servant's journey is a geographical reversal of Abram's original call: back to Mesopotamia to bring the covenant forward.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
24:2-4

MacArthur: Abraham's instruction to his servant -- swear by the LORD God of heaven and earth that you will not get a wife for my son from the Canaanites but will go to my own relatives -- reflects the covenant principle of separation. The wife of the covenant heir must come from the covenant line, not from the cursed Canaanite culture.

24:7

MacArthur: Abraham's confidence -- 'He will send his angel before you so that you can get a wife for my son from there' -- is not presumption but covenant faith. The same God who called him from Ur, who gave him Isaac, who provided the ram, will guide the servant to the right woman. The covenant God governs the details of covenant succession.

Hebrew Word Study

šālaḥ sha-LAKH — to send: Abraham "sent his servant" (šālaḥ, v.2) to find a wife for Isaac — the same verb used of God "sending" his covenant agents throughout Scripture. The servant is unnamed (possibly Eliezer of Damascus, mentioned in 15:2) but given a solemn oath-commission. The entire chapter is the longest narrative in Genesis, with the servant's story told twice — once as it happened, once as he recounts it. The doubling is theologically significant: covenant mission requires testimony, and testimony requires witness.

yārēk ya-RAYKH — thigh: "Put your hand under my thigh" (v.2) — the oath-gesture of placing the hand under the patriarch's thigh (near the circumcision/covenant-organ) signals the solemnity of the commission. The same gesture appears at Jacob's deathbed (47:29). The thigh-oath invokes the covenant sign of circumcision: the servant is bound by the covenant itself, not merely by social obligation. Breaking this oath would be a breach of covenant-oath — one of the most serious offences in the ancient Near East.

malʾāk mal-AKH — messenger / angel: "He will send his angel before you" (v.7) — Abraham's confidence that God will send a malʾāk to guide the servant echoes the angel who guided Hagar (16:7) and will guide Israel in the wilderness (Exod 23:20). The conviction that divine guidance accompanies covenant mission is not optimism but theology: the covenant God is the one who sends, sustains, and guides those sent on his behalf. The servant's subsequent prayer (vv.12-14) and its immediate answer (vv.15-27) confirm that the angel's guidance is real and specific.

Historical Context

None

Context

Abraham's insistence that Isaac not marry a Canaanite woman is the first explicit concern for covenant continuity in the next generation. The oath sworn on the patriarch's thigh (an ancient oath gesture, possibly relating to the "seed" — descendants) is the most solemn form of commitment in the ANE. The concern for endogamy (marriage within the kinship group) preserves the covenant line and echoes forward to the Mosaic prohibition of intermarriage with Canaanites (Deut 7:3).

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 26:34–35; 28:1–2Isaac's sons will face the same issue: Esau's Canaanite wives "make life bitter" for Isaac and Rebekah; Jacob is specifically sent to Paddan-aram to find a wife from the family line.
  • 2 Cor 6:14Paul's "do not be unequally yoked with unbelievers" echoes the same theological principle — covenant integrity requires covenant partnership.

24:1-9

The marriage commission is the longest chapter in Genesis and the most elaborate narrative in the Abraham cycle. Sarna: "The servant's commission is given with extraordinary solemnity: the oath taken with the hand under the thigh (the reproductive organs), the double prohibition (no wife from Canaan, Isaac must not go back to Mesopotamia), and the servant's careful request for divine confirmation. The entire enterprise is structured as a test of divine providence."

24:7

Abraham's statement of faith — "the LORD will send his angel before you" — is a declaration of confidence in divine guidance. Sarna: "Abraham, who began his journey to an unknown land (12:1), now speaks with complete confidence about the divine guidance of a mission to the same region he left. The faith that was tested in the Akedah expresses itself in confident delegation."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:2-4

The oath under the thigh. Alter: "The gesture is unique to this passage and to 47:29 (Jacob and Joseph). Its connection to the reproductive organs suggests an oath sworn on the power of procreation — swearing by the very faculty through which the covenant promise is transmitted. It is the most intimate and physically specific oath in the Bible."

24:6-9

The servant's question and Abraham's answer establish the double constraint: no returning to Mesopotamia with Isaac, and no forcing the woman. Alter notes these two constraints shape the narrative throughout: the servant must find a willing woman who will come to a land she has never seen.

Verses 10–27 — The Servant Prays; Rebekah Appears
101112131415161718192021222324252627

Places

Spring outside the city (Harran area)
Upper Mesopotamia
The servant stops at the well outside the city in the evening — the standard meeting point for travellers and residents. His prayer is answered before it is finished: Rebekah appears at the exact moment his petition ends. The ordinary geography of a watering point becomes the site of providential encounter.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
24:12-14

MacArthur: The servant's prayer is a model of specific, faith-filled intercession. He asks for a very specific sign -- the woman who offers water to both him and his camels -- and receives it immediately (v.15-17). The prayer aligns the servant's request with the covenant purpose (finding a wife for Isaac) and trusts God to show his covenant faithfulness.

24:27

MacArthur: 'Praise be to the LORD, the God of my master Abraham, who has not abandoned his kindness and faithfulness to my master. As for me, the LORD has led me on the journey to the house of my master's relatives.' The servant's doxology is the narrative's theological comment: the entire chain of events -- prayer, sign, meeting, response -- is the LORD's guidance.

Hebrew Word Study

ḥesed weʾĕmet KHEH-sed ve-EH-met — steadfast love and faithfulness: "The LORD... has not abandoned his kindness and faithfulness (ḥasdô weʾămittô) to my master" (v.27) — the servant's exclamation upon meeting Rebekah combines ḥesed ("covenant love") and ʾemet ("faithfulness/truth") in one of the OT's great theological compounds. The pairing ḥesed weʾĕmet appears in the revelation of God's name at Sinai (Exod 34:6: "abounding in love and faithfulness") and throughout the Psalms as the signature of divine character. Providence experienced in covenant terms is always both loving and faithful.

ʿênayim ay-NAY-im — eyes: The servant had prayed: "let the girl to whom I say, 'Please let down your jar that I may have a drink,' and who says, 'Drink, and I'll water your camels too' — let her be the one you have chosen" (v.14). The sign-prayer is answered before it is finished (v.15: "before he had finished praying, Rebekah came out"). The "eyes" of covenant discernment — seeing what God is doing through what appears coincidental — are the servant's great gift. His testimony to Rebekah's family (vv.34-49) is one of Scripture's most elegant accounts of providential guidance narrated in faith.

pāgaʿ pa-GAH — to meet / to encounter: The account of the servant's mission turns on three "meetings" — the servant meets Rebekah at the well, the servant meets Rebekah's family, and Rebekah will meet Isaac (v.65). The verb pāgaʿ ("to encounter/fall upon") is used of providential meetings — Elijah and the angel (1 Kgs 19:5), Jacob and the angels (32:1). The entire chapter is a theology of providential encounter: the servant prays; God arranges; the right person appears. The meeting at the well is not coincidence but the answer to covenant prayer.

Historical Context

None

Context

The literary technique of Genesis 24 is deliberate repetition. The servant's journey is described (vv.10–21), then narrated again in his speech to Laban (vv.34–49) with small but significant variations. This double narration slows the reader, creates suspense, and confirms the providential interpretation. In the servant's retelling, what appeared coincidental in the first account now appears as divinely orchestrated. Providence is often only visible in retrospect.

    Places

    Canaan / Negev (return journey)
    Upper Mesopotamia back to southern Canaan
    The servant's return traverses the full route from Aram back to Canaan — the same route Abram first travelled. Isaac is at Beer Lahai Roi in the Negev when Rebekah arrives: the covenant son is at Hagar's well, the place named for God's sight, when he first sees his covenant wife.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    24:58

    MacArthur: 'Will you go with this man?' 'I will go.' Rebekah's single-word decision mirrors Abraham's response to the call of God (12:1-4). Both left family and homeland for an unknown destination in response to a word from the LORD. The covenant pattern of radical obedient departure marks each generation's entry into the covenant inheritance.

    24:67

    MacArthur: Isaac took Rebekah into his mother Sarah's tent, and she became his wife. He loved her. The simple, direct statement of covenant love is significant: Isaac's love for Rebekah is the first explicit statement of marital love in Scripture. The covenant is passed through a love that has its ground in God's covenant faithfulness, not romantic circumstance.

    Hebrew Word Study

    ʾāh / ʾēm AKH / AYM — brother / mother: Rebekah's family — Laban her brother and Bethuel her father (v.50), with her mother separately consulted (vv.53-58) — demonstrate the complex social structures of covenant negotiation. Laban's prominent role alongside Bethuel suggests he has significant household authority — a role that will characterise him in the Jacob narratives (ch.29-31). The mother's separate voice in the negotiation (v.55: "Let the young woman remain with us ten days or so") reflects that women's kinship networks have real, if informal, power in covenant family decisions.

    šālaḥ sha-LAKH — to send / to let go: The covenant negotiation climaxes in the family's consent: "Take her and go, and let her become the wife of your master's son, as the LORD has directed" (v.51). The consent formula reflects their recognition of divine direction (YHWH yāṣāʾ haddābār — "the matter has gone out from the LORD"). Even outside the formal covenant community, the family of Nahor recognises that the servant's mission has divine mandate. The consent is not merely social permission but theological recognition of providential direction.

    nāḥam na-KHAM — to comfort / to be comforted: "Isaac... married Rebekah. So she became his wife, and he loved her; and Isaac was comforted after his mother's death" (v.67). The verb nāḥam — the root of Noah's name ("he will comfort us") — appears here as the emotional resolution of the chapter and of the grief implied since Sarah's death in ch.23. The marriage covenant is the personal embodiment of the covenant promise: the line will continue, the comfort comes in the form of a wife prepared by divine providence, and the covenant's next generation begins in love.

    Context

    Rebekah's two-word answer — "I will go" — parallels Abraham's departure from Ur in its radical willingness to leave everything and go to an unknown land to a man she has never seen. Her family blesses her with words that echo the Abrahamic promise: "May you increase to thousands upon thousands; may your offspring possess the gate of their enemies" (v.60). She enters the story already carrying the covenant blessing.

    Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    The Servant (likely Eliezer)
    Abraham's chief servant; model of faithful stewardship
    Prays before acting, credits God immediately, refuses to eat before completing his report, does not deviate from his mission. A model of servant leadership — completely self-effacing, entirely focused on his master's purpose. His name is never given in this chapter.
    Rebekah
    Daughter of Bethuel; future matriarch
    Identified by extraordinary generosity before her identity is known. Her "I will go" is one of the most consequential two-word decisions in the entire Bible. She becomes the mother of Jacob and Esau, the next generation of covenant carriers.
    Isaac
    Son of Abraham; bridegroom
    Largely passive in this chapter — the subject of everyone's efforts. His role is to receive. He is "comforted after his mother's death" — the grief of ch.23 finds healing in ch.24. Marriage becomes a vehicle of divine comfort.

    Ancient Sources

    ANE Marriage Customs
    Marriage negotiation in the ancient world involved family representatives, bride price, and parental consent. Genesis 24 follows this pattern while uniquely emphasizing the bride's own consent (v.58 — "Will you go?"). This element of Rebekah's choice is notable in an ancient context.

    Reception History

    The Providence Theme
    Commentators from Augustine to Chrysostom to Calvin have used Genesis 24 as the primary biblical text on divine providence in ordinary life. No angel appears, no miracle occurs — just a prayer answered by the first young woman at a well. God's guidance operates through natural means and human character.
    Type of the Church
    Patristic interpreters read the chapter allegorically: Abraham = God the Father; Isaac = Christ; the servant = the Holy Spirit; Rebekah = the Church called from the nations to be Christ's bride. The allegorical reading, while not controlling, captures genuine theological resonances.

    Literary Structure

    The Willingness to Go: Covenant Pattern in a New Generation24:57-58Will you go with this man? I will go. The one-word answer is the narrative's hinge. Rebekah makes the same unconditional commitment Abraham made in 12:1 -- leaving everything for an unknown destination.
    The Meeting at Evening: Meditation and Movement24:63-67Isaac meditates in the field at evening; he lifts his eyes and sees the camels coming. Rebekah lifts her eyes and sees Isaac. The mutual seeing at the threshold of the meeting is the patriarchal narrative's most tender encounter.

    Scholarly Debates

    The Servant's Prayer: Model for Providential Guidance?
    Specific sign-seeking (questionable)
    Some pastoral traditions
    The servant sets up a specific test (the girl who offers water to the camels). This is analogous to "fleece" requests (Judg 6:36-40). Such specific sign-seeking may reflect limited faith rather than mature trust.
    Legitimate prayer for wisdom
    Calvin, Wenham, Waltke
    The servant prays in complete dependence, gives God complete freedom to act, and responds with immediate worship when God answers. The prayer is a model of humble, specific, grateful intercession — not manipulative sign-seeking.
    The servant's prayer is not presented negatively by the narrative. The distinction between the servant's prayer and Gideon's fleece is that the servant prays for a sign that will reveal character (hospitality, generosity) rather than merely a mechanical confirmation. The narrative presents it as exemplary faith and grateful providence.
