Genesis 37

Joseph's Dreams; Sold into Egypt

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–11 — Joseph's Dreams; His Brothers' Hatred
1Jacob lived in the land where his father had stayed, the land of Canaan.2This is the account of Jacob's family line. Joseph, a young man of seventeen, was tending the flocks with his brothers, the sons of Bilhah and the sons of Zilpah, his father's wives, and he brought their father a bad report about them.3Now Israel loved Joseph more than any of his other sons, because he had been born to him in his old age; and he made an ornate robe for him.4When his brothers saw that their father loved him more than any of them, they hated him and could not speak a kind word to him.5Joseph had a dream, and when he told it to his brothers, they hated him all the more.6He said to them, "Listen to this dream I had:7We were binding sheaves of grain out in the field when suddenly my sheaf rose and stood upright, while your sheaves gathered around mine and bowed down to it."8His brothers said to him, "Do you intend to reign over us? Will you actually rule us?" And they hated him all the more because of his dream and what he had said.9Then he had another dream, and he told it to his brothers. "Listen," he said, "I had another dream, and this time the sun and moon and eleven stars were bowing down to me."10When he told his father as well as his brothers, his father rebuked him and said, "What is this dream you had? Will your mother and I and your brothers actually come and bow down to the ground before you?"11His brothers were jealous of him, but his father kept the matter in mind.

Hebrew Word Study

כְּתֹנֶת פַּסִּים ketōnet passîm — robe of many colors / long robe: The exact meaning of passîm is debated — it may mean "long-sleeved" or "ornamented." The same phrase appears only in 2 Sam 13:18 for the garment of royal daughters. This is a garment marking status and favoritism — and it will become the instrument of Joseph's apparent death (v.31).

חָלַם ḥālam — dream: Dreams are the primary vehicle of divine revelation in the Joseph story. The same root is used for Pharaoh's dreams (ch.41) — God speaks through Joseph and through Pharaoh using the same medium.

קָנָא qānāʾ — jealous / zealous: The brothers' jealousy (qin'āh) is the same root as God's own jealousy for his covenant people (Exod 20:5). Misdirected passion for honor drives them to sell their own brother.

Historical Context

The Joseph cycle (ch.37-50) is the longest sustained narrative in Genesis, with many features of a novella: psychological complexity, plot reversals, ironic symmetry, and a theological argument built through accumulated plot events rather than explicit divine speech. Joseph is the pivotal figure who bridges the patriarchal era and the Egyptian era of Israel's history. His dreams and their fulfillment drive the plot; his character — capable, faithful, wronged, and ultimately forgiving — is the moral centre of the cycle.

Context

The Joseph narrative opens with the same family dynamics that structured the Jacob narrative: parental favoritism (Isaac preferred Esau; Jacob prefers Joseph), sibling rivalry, and the younger son's elevation. But where Jacob stole his position through deception, Joseph's elevation comes through divine dreams and providential suffering. The two dreams (sheaves bowing, heavenly bodies bowing) frame the entire narrative — they will not be fulfilled until 42:6 and 43:26 when the brothers bow before Joseph in Egypt.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 42:6; 43:26The dream fulfillments: brothers bow before Joseph the governor.
  • Psalm 105:17–19The Psalm retrospectively identifies Joseph's suffering as God's testing: "He had sent a man before them — Joseph, sold as a slave... until what he foretold came to pass."
  • Acts 7:9–10Stephen's sermon: "The patriarchs, jealous of Joseph, sold him into Egypt. But God was with him."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
37:2

The narrative opens with Joseph at seventeen bringing a bad report about his brothers to Jacob. Before the coat and the dreams, there is the informer's role: Joseph is already in conflict with his brothers over their conduct. The bad report may have been accurate and conscientious — or it may have been the self-righteous reporting of a favourite son. Either way, it establishes the brothers' pre-existing grievance before Jacob's favouritism inflames it further.

37:3-4

The coat of many colours — or the long-sleeved robe, as the Hebrew ketonet passim more precisely means — is the tangible symbol of paternal favouritism. Joseph receives what Reuben, the firstborn, should have received. The brothers see it and draw the correct inference: Israel loved Joseph more than any of his other sons. The hatred follows immediately: "they hated him and could not speak a kind word to him."

37:5-8

Joseph's first dream — sheaves bowing to his sheaf — is not a theological vision but a domestic one: the grain fields, the labour, the family economy of a pastoral household. But its meaning is unmistakable. The brothers understand it immediately: "Do you intend to reign over us?" The anger of the interpretation is the anger of men who can read a power dynamic when they see one. The dream is not humble.

37:9-11

The second dream expands the scope: sun, moon, and eleven stars bowing to Joseph. Now even Jacob is included. Jacob rebukes Joseph publicly — "will your mother and I and your brothers actually come and bow down to the ground before you?" — but the text adds: "his father kept the matter in mind." Jacob knows the language of divine revelation. He does not dismiss the dream even while he criticises its presumption.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:3

The ketonet passim (coat of many colours or long-sleeved robe) is mentioned in only one other place: 2 Samuel 13:18, where it describes the garments worn by the king's virgin daughters. Sarna: "The garment may signify either royalty or exemption from manual labour — in either case it marks Joseph as set apart from his brothers' working life. The same garment appears in the rape of Tamar, another story of family violation and its consequences. The connection is not accidental."

37:5-9

The two dreams are structurally parallel: one domestic (sheaves), one cosmic (celestial bodies). Sarna notes that in the ancient world, doubled dreams were understood as divinely confirmed predictions — a principle explicitly stated in 41:32 (Pharaoh's dreams). Joseph's two dreams carry the same double-confirmation: what God has purposed will certainly come to pass. The dreams are not wishful thinking but prophetic disclosure.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:3

The coat is the first in a series of garments that structure the entire Joseph cycle. Alter: "The ketonet passim is the first of the story's garment-markers. Joseph's coat will be stripped from him by his brothers (37:23), Potiphar's wife will seize his garment (39:12-13), and the Joseph who appears before Pharaoh will be dressed in royal garments (41:42). Clothing is identity in this narrative — what you wear signals your status and what is done to your clothing tells you what has been done to you."

37:10-11

Jacob's dual response to the second dream — public rebuke, private retention — is a psychologically precise detail. Alter: "Jacob speaks for public consumption and thinks privately. He knows the language of revelation (he has lived with it his whole life — Bethel, Peniel, the Haran dreams). He cannot simply dismiss what sounds like his father's blessing extended to the next generation. He rebukes the presumption while keeping the content."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
37:3

Calvin on parental favouritism: "Jacob acted improperly in making this distinction among his children. For, although love is not equally due to all children, parents ought prudently to beware of exciting jealousy." Calvin places the first responsibility on Jacob: the coat is a parental failure. He then addresses Joseph: the sin is compounded by Joseph's willing reception of the distinction and his apparent delight in his superior status.

37:5-9

Calvin on the dreams: "God wished to condemn the wickedness of his brothers before the judgment seat of their own conscience... Although Joseph does not yet comprehend the full meaning of his dreams, he has no doubt that they come from God. The faithfulness of the dreamer is itself part of the divine method: God uses the dreamer's confidence in revelation to bring about the revelation's fulfilment."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
37:3

The NET note on ketonet passim surveys the translation history: "coat of many colours" (KJV, following LXX and Vulgate) vs. "richly ornamented robe" (NIV) vs. "long-sleeved robe" (NRSV). The note observes that the cognate Akkadian kuttinu patinnu may mean a robe reaching to the hands and feet — i.e., a long-sleeved, full-length garment. The note connects this to 2 Sam 13:18 and notes the common element is a garment of special status.

37:9

The NET note on "the sun and moon and eleven stars were bowing down to me" observes that eleven stars correspond exactly to eleven brothers (Benjamin not yet present). The inclusion of sun and moon (Jacob and Rachel, or Jacob and Leah) is noted with the parenthetical that Rachel is already dead (35:19), which creates an interpretive puzzle that the narrative does not resolve — possibly the moon represents Leah, or the dream is not a precise allegory.

Verses 12–28 — The Plot; Joseph Sold into Slavery
12Now his brothers had gone to graze their father's flocks near Shechem,13and Israel said to Joseph, "As you know, your brothers are grazing the flocks near Shechem. Come, I am going to send you to them." "Very well," he replied.14So he said to him, "Go and see if all is well with your brothers and with the flocks, and bring word back to me." Then he sent him off from the Valley of Hebron. When Joseph arrived at Shechem,15a man found him wandering around in the fields and asked him, "What are you looking for?"16He replied, "I'm looking for my brothers. Can you tell me where they are grazing their flocks?"17"They have moved on from here," the man answered. "I heard them say, 'Let's go to Dothan.'" So Joseph went after his brothers and found them near Dothan.18But they saw him in the distance, and before he reached them, they plotted to kill him.19"Here comes that dreamer!" they said to each other.20"Come now, let's kill him and throw him into one of these cisterns and say that a ferocious animal devoured him. Then we'll see what comes of his dreams."21When Reuben heard this, he tried to rescue him from their hands. "Let's not take his life," he said.22"Don't shed any blood. Throw him into this cistern here in the wilderness, but don't lay a hand on him." Reuben said this to rescue him from them and take him back to his father.23So when Joseph came to his brothers, they stripped him of his robe—the ornate robe he was wearing—24and they took him and threw him into the cistern. The cistern was empty; there was no water in it.25As they sat down to eat their meal, they looked up and saw a caravan of Ishmaelites coming from Gilead. Their camels were loaded with spices, balm and myrrh, and they were on their way to take them down to Egypt.26Judah said to his brothers, "What will we gain if we kill our brother and cover up his blood?27Come, let's sell him to the Ishmaelites and not lay our hands on him; after all, he is our brother, our own flesh and blood." His brothers agreed.28So when the Midianite merchants came by, his brothers pulled Joseph up out of the cistern and sold him for twenty shekels of silver to the Ishmaelites, who took him to Egypt.

Hebrew Word Study

בֹּר bōr — pit / cistern: The same word used in Psalms for Sheol/the grave (Ps 28:1; 30:3). Joseph's descent into the pit is a death-and-resurrection pattern: he is removed from the land of the living, then raised up to rule. The motif recurs in the prison (another pit, matṣōr).

עֶשְׂרִים כֶּסֶף ʿesrîm kesep — twenty pieces of silver: The price of a slave aged 5–20 according to Leviticus 27:5. Joseph was seventeen — the brothers received the legal minimum for a young male slave. This price is later echoed in Zechariah's thirty pieces (Zech 11:12–13) and Judas's betrayal.

Historical Context

The plot to kill Joseph and its modification into sale is a carefully constructed scene of moral deterioration and partial restraint. Reuben's plan to rescue Joseph (v.22) and Judah's alternative of selling rather than killing (v.26-27) both represent partial virtue — they cannot save Joseph from harm but they save him from death. The Ishmaelite/Midianite traders who carry Joseph to Egypt are themselves descendants of Abraham — the irony that the covenant people is sold into slavery through Abraham's descendants is one of Genesis's most pointed.

Context

The pit scene is layered with irony: the dreamer is thrown into an empty pit — the same Hebrew word (bōr) used for the grave. The brothers sit down to eat while Joseph is in the pit (v.25) — an echo of Esau's selling his birthright for food. Reuben's plan to rescue Joseph (v.21) is frustrated by his absence (v.29) — he bears the guilt of failing to act decisively. Judah's compromise (sell him rather than kill him, v.26) is presented as the "better" option, but it still amounts to betrayal. No brother emerges with moral credit.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 50:20Joseph's definitive theological interpretation: "You intended to harm me, but God intended it for good."
  • Psalm 105:17God "sent a man before them — Joseph, sold as a slave." The Psalm reads the sale as divine sending.
  • Matt 27:3–10Judas's thirty pieces of silver echo Joseph's twenty — both are betrayals of the innocent for money.
  • Acts 7:9Stephen cites the brothers' jealousy as the mechanism of God's providential plan for Israel's salvation.

Places

Dothan
Northern Canaan; Jezreel Valley area
The brothers are grazing at Dothan — about 130km north of Hebron, in the Jezreel Valley. The Midianite/Ishmaelite traders passing through are on the major trade route from Gilead to Egypt, which passes through Dothan. The geography of commerce intersects the family tragedy.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
37:18-20

The brothers see Joseph coming from a distance — still wearing the coat of many colours — and the plot crystallises immediately. The coat that marked his status is now the trigger for their violence. They plan his death with chilling economy: "Come now, let's kill him and throw him into one of these cisterns... Then we'll see what becomes of his dreams." The last phrase is savage irony: the dreams they want to kill are the dreams God has already set in motion.

37:21-22

Reuben's intervention to save Joseph — "let's not take his life... throw him into this cistern here in the wilderness but don't lay a hand on him" — is a moment of partial virtue. Reuben intends to return and rescue Joseph later (v.29-30). But partial virtue leaves Joseph in the cistern long enough for the Ishmaelite traders to appear. Good intentions that do not act decisively fail to protect.

37:26-27

Judah's proposal — "what will we gain if we kill our brother and conceal his blood? Come, let's sell him to the Ishmaelites and not lay our hands on him" — is pragmatic rather than compassionate. He argues against murder not on moral grounds but on economic ones: there is no profit in killing. The twenty pieces of silver paid for Joseph is the exact sum paid for a male slave aged 5-20 in Leviticus 27:5 — the brothers price their brother at the minimum legal value.

37:28

The transition — Midianite merchants pull Joseph up out of the cistern and sell him to the Ishmaelites for twenty pieces of silver — passes over Joseph's experience in the cistern without a word. The chapter never describes his terror, his pleading (revealed retrospectively in 42:21), or his response to being sold. The narrator's silence is not indifference but restraint: the reader's imagination must supply what the text withholds.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:21-22

Reuben's intervention is the first positive act of the brothers toward Joseph and the first moral distinction within the group. Sarna: "The differentiation of the brothers — Reuben objects, Judah modifies, the rest acquiesce — begins here and will develop throughout the Joseph cycle. The brothers are not a monolithic villain but a group with individual moral gradations. The same gradations will appear in the Egyptian encounters."

37:27-28

The twenty pieces of silver paid for Joseph creates a connection forward to Zechariah 11:12-13 and Matthew 26:15 — the thirty pieces of silver paid for Jesus. Sarna notes the typological connection without pressing it: Joseph sold by his brothers for silver anticipates the Son sold by his disciple. The numbers differ; the pattern is consistent. Joseph is the most fully developed messianic type in Genesis.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:23-24

The stripping of Joseph's coat and the throwing into the cistern are narrated with the same verbs used for ritual sacrifice: stripped (vayafshitu), cast (vayashlikhu). Alter: "The narrative language subtly suggests that what the brothers are doing is a kind of murder-sacrifice — they are offering Joseph up to the pit while staging a counter-narrative (the goat's blood) for their father. The sacrificial vocabulary is not accidental; it indicts the brothers' action at the deepest level."

37:28

The passive construction — "Joseph was lifted out of the cistern" — withholds Joseph's agency and experience. Alter: "The entire sale is narrated from the outside. We are not told what Joseph said, felt, or did. The brevity is itself a statement about power: Joseph is acted upon throughout this scene; his subjectivity is irrelevant to the transactors. The reader will not hear Joseph's voice again until chapter 39."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
37:20

Calvin on the brothers' "let's see what becomes of his dreams": "They thought that by destroying him they could prove God a liar. This is the height of impiety — to set oneself against the decrees of heaven by violence. But God shows, in the event, how foolish and vain is the audacity of those who think to resist him." The brothers' scorn of the dreams is the scorn of men who do not believe God can accomplish what he has spoken.

37:26-27

Calvin on Judah's proposal: "Judah had more authority than the rest, and prevailed with them. How base was the source of this counsel — not that they might spare their brother's life, but that they might make gain of him. We see then that avarice sometimes restrains men from the perpetration of great crimes, not from any feeling of humanity, but for the sake of their own interest." Partial virtue from impure motives is still providence-enabling.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
37:25

The NET note on the Ishmaelites/Midianites discusses the apparent inconsistency: v.25 says Ishmaelites, v.28 says Midianites. The note surveys the main solutions: (1) the terms are used interchangeably (cf. Judg 8:24 where Midianites are called Ishmaelites); (2) two groups are present; (3) source variation. The note favours (1) — a tribal confederation in which both names applied — observing that Judges 8:24 explicitly equates them.

37:28

The NET note on twenty pieces of silver notes this is the price of a male slave in Leviticus 27:5 for ages 5-20. The note observes the irony: the brothers assess Joseph at the legal minimum for a young slave. The covenant heir — the one whose dreams have already been confirmed as prophetic — is sold at the minimum market rate. The note also anticipates the typological connection to Matthew 26:15 (thirty pieces for Jesus).

Verses 29–36 — Reuben's Distress; Jacob's Grief
29When Reuben returned to the cistern and saw that Joseph was not there, he tore his clothes.30He went back to his brothers and said, "The boy isn't there! Where can I turn now?"31Then they got Joseph's robe, slaughtered a goat and dipped the robe in the blood.32They took the ornate robe back to their father and said, "We found this. Historical Context

The double deception at the end of Genesis 37 — the goat's blood on the coat presented to Jacob, and Potiphar buying Joseph in Egypt — is the chapter's final irony. Jacob, who deceived his father with goat skins (27:16), is now deceived by his sons with goat's blood. The same animal, the same father-son relationship, the same mechanism of deception. The coat that was the symbol of Jacob's unjust favouritism is now the instrument of the retribution.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
37:29-30

Reuben's return to the empty cistern — "he tore his clothes" — is the chapter's most poignant moment. He had intended to return and rescue Joseph; he has failed. His response is not guilt over participation (he tried to prevent it) but grief over failure to act decisively when he had the chance. The space between good intention and effective action is where Joseph was lost.

37:31-33

The coat soaked in goat's blood is the most precise irony in the Jacob narrative. Jacob had deceived Isaac with goat skins; his sons deceive him with goat's blood. The same animal, the same patriarch, the same pattern of deception using animal material. The man who lived by deception is deceived by the exact method he himself employed. The justice of the symmetry is devastating.

37:34-35

Jacob's mourning — tearing clothes, sackcloth, refusing all comfort — is the most extreme grief response in Genesis. "I will continue to mourn until I join my son in the grave." Jacob will mourn for Joseph for at least twenty-two years. He will be wrong about his son's death for all of them. The grief he refused to be comforted from was grief over a fiction. Providence was concealing a mercy he could not imagine.

37:36

The last verse of the chapter is transitional: "Meanwhile, the Midianites sold Joseph in Egypt to Potiphar, one of Pharaoh's officials, the captain of the guard." The "meanwhile" is the chapter's pivot: what looks like catastrophe from Canaan is, in Egypt, the beginning of the fulfilment of the dreams. The chapter ends in two locations simultaneously: Jacob mourning in Canaan; Joseph arriving in the house of a powerful Egyptian official.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:31-33

The blood-soaked coat presented to Jacob is the chapter's most elaborate irony. Sarna: "The brothers ask their father: 'Examine it to see whether it is your son's robe.' They use the same verb haker (examine, recognise) that appears in 38:25 when Tamar presents Judah's pledge to him. The recognition scenes are linked. In both cases the haker forces the one who deceived to face the consequence of their action."

37:36

The closing verse — Joseph sold to Potiphar — is both endpoint and beginning. Sarna notes the precision of Potiphar's title: sar hatabbachim (captain of the guard) literally means "chief of the slaughterers/butchers" — a title for the commander of the royal bodyguard whose duties included execution. Joseph has been delivered from his brothers' murderous intention into the hands of the royal executioner. Providence works through alarming particulars.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:33

Jacob's recognition of the coat — "my son's robe! A ferocious animal has devoured him!" — is structured as an exclamation followed by a conclusion. Alter: "Jacob does not ask questions or express doubt. He accepts the evidence immediately and completely. The man who manufactured false evidence for his father (ch.27) is now the victim of manufactured evidence. The recognition he forces on Isaac ('Is it really you, my son Esau?') is now forced on him."

37:35

The refusal of comfort — "in mourning will I go down to the grave to my son" — is Jacob's most extended speech in the chapter. Alter notes the phrase "go down to Sheol" uses the same verb (yarad) that will describe Joseph's going down to Egypt, Judah's going down to Adullam, and multiple other descents in the subsequent chapters. The chapter inaugurates a structural pattern of descent and ascent that will organise the entire Joseph cycle.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
37:32-33

Calvin on Jacob's grief: "Let us notice in this passage, that holy men are not so divested of human affection as to be free from all sorrow. It becomes us not to be altogether free from grief on account of the dead... The moderation required of us is that we should not give way to grief so far as to murmur against God. Jacob is blameable in this, that being too credulous, he had drunk in sorrow, without any discrimination, before the truth was ascertained."

37:36

Calvin on Providence: "The Lord, in order that he might raise his servant from the depths of the pit to the highest degree of honour, leads him, as it were by a circuitous path, through various afflictions. But of these things Jacob is ignorant; he lies at home in despondency, while his son, stripped and sold, is nevertheless, by God's secret guidance, conducted towards the object of his destination."

37:33

Ancient Sources

Egyptian Slave Records
Egyptian administrative papyri record Semitic slaves (ʿāpiru/Habiru) bought and sold in Egypt from c. 1800 BC. The price of twenty shekels matches Middle Bronze Age slave prices documented in Mesopotamian tablets.
The economic details of the Joseph sale are archaeologically consistent with the period.
Potiphar's Title
The title "captain of the guard" (śar haṭṭabbāḥîm, lit. "chief of the slaughterers/executioners") appears in Egyptian records as a high court official. The title marks Potiphar as a significant figure in Pharaoh's household.
The specificity of Joseph's owner's title is consistent with authentic Egyptian administrative terminology.

Joseph is the clearest type of Christ in the OT: the beloved son, betrayed by his own brothers for silver, apparently dead, raised to rule and save. Augustine develops the typology extensively.
Thomas Mann, Joseph and His Brothers (1943)
Mann's four-volume novel is the great literary reception of the Joseph narrative — an exploration of myth, psychology, and the way archetypal patterns repeat through history.

Literary Structure

Favouritism — 37:1-4Jacob loves Joseph most; the coat signals it publicly. Brothers hate Joseph — they cannot speak a kind word to him. Conflict pre-exists the dreams.
Sheaves bow; sun, moon, stars bow. Brothers furious; Jacob rebukes but keeps the matter in mind. Divine prophecy in domestic imagery.
The Plot — 37:12-22Brothers at Shechem/Dothan. Reuben tries to save Joseph — the cistern plan. Good intention; insufficient action.
Coat stripped. Cistern. Ishmaelites/Midianites. Twenty pieces of silver. Joseph sold without a word of his own recorded.
The Deception — 37:29-36Goat's blood on coat. haker na. Jacob deceived with the same mechanism he used on Isaac. Grief refused; Joseph arrives at Potiphar's.
Genesis 37 launches the Joseph cycle with the full weight of the Jacob narrative behind it. The coat of many colours — Jacob's partiality made visible — triggers a catastrophe. The dreams — God's plan made audible — guarantee the catastrophe's ultimate reversal. Between the two, the brothers' hatred, Reuben's failed virtue, Judah's pragmatic modification, and Jacob's deceived grief create the darkest chapter since the fall. Joseph goes down to Egypt; God's plan goes with him.

Textual Notes

37:28מִדְיָנִים / יִשְׁמְעֵאלִים — Midianites or Ishmaelites?
MT v.25,27,28: Ishmaelites; v.28,36: Midianites. The same caravan is described with both names. LXX reflects the same ambiguity. Judges 8:24 explicitly equates Midianites with Ishmaelites.
The alternation of names reflects either (1) two groups present simultaneously; (2) a confederation in which both names applied; (3) source variation (J/E). The Judges 8:24 equation supports (2). The variation is not a contradiction but reflects the fluid ethnic terminology of the period.
37:36סְרִיס — "official" or "eunuch"?
MT saris — both "court official/chamberlain" and "eunuch." The word's meaning shifted over time; in some contexts it clearly means a eunuch (Isa 56:3-5; Esth 2:14-15); in others it simply means a royal official.
The meaning here is likely "court official" rather than eunuch — Potiphar has a wife and a household (ch.39). The ambiguity disappears in context. The LXX (σπάδων) can mean either.

Scholarly Debates

The Joseph Cycle: History or Novella?
Historical narrative
Kitchen, Hoffmeier, Wenham
The Egyptian details (names, titles, practices) are accurate to the Second Intermediate Period or New Kingdom. The slave price (20 shekels), the titles (sar hatabbachim), the social customs — all are historically verified. The narrative has the texture of historical memory.
Theological novella
von Rad, Westermann, Coats
The Joseph story has the literary characteristics of a Wisdom narrative or novella: psychological complexity, ironic reversals, a clear moral thesis. It may have been composed as theological literature rather than historical chronicle.
Historically grounded literary art
Sarna, Alter, Hamilton
The two options are not mutually exclusive. The narrative is historically grounded (accurate Egyptian details) and literarily sophisticated. Ancient historiography was not opposed to literary artistry.
The historically-grounded literary art reading is most satisfying. Kitchen's demonstration of the authentic Egyptian background settles the question of historical grounding. Alter's literary analysis demonstrates the narrative's sophisticated artistry. Both are true simultaneously — exactly as one would expect from an ancient author of skill writing about real events.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 37 is the pivot chapter of Genesis. The Joseph cycle begins with the same themes that have driven the patriarchal narrative — favouritism, sibling rivalry, deception — but now with a scale and complexity that will carry the story all the way to Egypt. The dreams are the theological guarantee: what God has spoken cannot be stopped by hatred, a cistern, or a slave market. Providence uses the brothers' violence as its instrument.
