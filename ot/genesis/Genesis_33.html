Genesis 33

Jacob Meets Esau; Arrival at Shechem

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–11 — Jacob Meets Esau; Reconciliation
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

חָנַן ḥānan — find favor: Jacob uses the language of a petitioner before a sovereign: "find favor in the sight of my lord" (vv.8,10,15). He addresses Esau as "my lord" six times in this chapter — the linguistic embodiment of "the older shall serve the younger" (25:23) being voluntarily fulfilled.

בְּרָכָה berākāh — blessing: In v.11 Jacob calls his gift "my blessing" (birkatî) — a deliberate echo of the stolen blessing of ch.27. He is now returning a blessing to Esau rather than stealing one from him. The word transforms the context.

חִנַּנִי ḥinnannî — dealt graciously with me: From the same root as "find favor" (ḥānan). Jacob's explanation for his prosperity: "God has dealt graciously (ḥānannî) with me." Grace explains everything.

Historical Context

The meeting of Jacob and Esau after twenty years is the most emotionally charged reconciliation scene in Genesis. Esau's running to meet Jacob, his embrace, his kissing and weeping (v.4) mirrors the father's reception of the prodigal son in Luke 15:20. The elaborate protocol Jacob had prepared — bowing seven times, the gift procession — is swept aside by Esau's immediate embrace. The grace of the encounter far exceeds the strategy Jacob had deployed.

Context

The reconciliation scene inverts chapter 27: Jacob now voluntarily bows seven times (the sign of royal homage, attested in Amarna letters) before Esau, addressing him as "my lord." The stolen blessing is symbolically returned via the gift. Esau's "I have enough" (v.9) and Jacob's "I have enough" (v.11) — same Hebrew word — suggest that God has blessed both brothers. The fearful meeting Jacob anticipated (ch.32) becomes a tearful embrace. "I have seen your face, which is like seeing the face of God" (v.10) — Jacob connects the Esau meeting with Peniel: both encounters could have been lethal, both resulted in grace.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 15:20The prodigal son's father "ran to meet him, fell on his neck and kissed him" — the same actions as Esau toward Jacob (v.4). The reconciliation with Esau shapes the parable.
  • Gen 27:29Isaac's blessing: "Your brothers will bow down to you." Jacob fulfills this by bowing — the blessing is real but its direction is reversed by grace.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
33:1-3

Jacob's arrangement of his household for the encounter with Esau — maidservants and their children first, Leah and her children second, Rachel and Joseph last — reveals his continued hierarchy of affection, even in mortal danger. The most beloved are placed farthest back, given the most protection. The arrangement is both strategy and spiritual symptom: Jacob cannot yet value people equally.

33:4

Esau ran to meet his brother, embraced him, fell on his neck, kissed him, and they wept together. This is the most unexpected verse in the Jacob narrative. Nothing has prepared the reader for this. The 400 men, the twenty years of estrangement, Jacob's elaborate preparations — all of it dissolved in a single act of extravagant welcome. Esau has done what Jacob could not predict: he has simply forgiven him.

33:9

Esau's "I already have plenty, my brother" is among the most spiritually striking statements in Genesis. The man who sold his birthright for a bowl of soup now says he has enough. Either God has materially blessed Esau despite his spiritual carelessness (as the promise to Ishmael indicates nations would come from Esau, 17:20), or Esau has genuinely arrived at contentment. Either way, his abundance frees him from resentment.

33:10

Jacob's interpretation of the encounter — "to see your face is like seeing the face of God, and you have received me favourably" — is a theological reading of the reconciliation. The night before, Jacob saw the face of God (Peniel, 32:30). Now, in Esau's unexpected grace, he sees the same face. The face of God encountered in divine wrestling and the face of God encountered in human forgiveness are the same face.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
33:4

Esau's embrace is described with five verbs in sequence: ran, fell upon his neck, kissed, embraced (the order in MT), wept. Sarna notes the narrative acceleration: five actions compressed into a single verse. The emotional intensity of the reunion is conveyed by the density of physical action rather than by explanatory comment. The MT has a series of dots over the word "kissed" in the scribal tradition (a masoretic qere/ketib matter) indicating special attention — some rabbis read the dots as indicating exceptional intensity, others as uncertainty.

33:10-11

Jacob's gift and his speech about seeing Esau's face as seeing God's face create a theological commentary on the Peniel experience. Sarna: "The encounter with Esau is a direct extension of the wrestling match at the Jabbok. The same divine face of unexpected grace appears in both. Jacob received a blessing at Peniel; he gives a blessing (the gift, minchah) at the Esau encounter. The two meetings are theologically continuous."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
33:4

The masoretic dots over "he kissed him" — a scribal device for uncertain or specially significant text — have generated extensive rabbinic commentary. Alter notes that the scribal marking is likely ancient and signals that the word carries exceptional weight. Some rabbis read it as insincere kissing; others as the most sincere kiss in the patriarchal narratives. Alter himself reads the five verbs as describing genuine reconciliation, with the dots marking the narrative's own sense of the extraordinary quality of the moment.

33:10

Jacob's equation of Esau's face with the face of God is narratively audacious. Alter: "This is Jacob himself providing the theological interpretation of what has happened. The night at the Jabbok was a preparation for this morning — the wrestling with God prepared him for the embracing of his brother. Grace encountered vertically (in the divine wrestling) is now encountered horizontally (in the human reconciliation)."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
33:4

Calvin on Esau's reception: "God had so subdued the ferocious mind of Esau, as to cause him to run to meet his brother, whom he had previously thirsted to destroy. We see therefore that the hearts of men, however fierce and cruel, are under the secret government of God." The unexpected grace of Esau is not a character virtue but a divine restraint. God has changed Esau's heart — the same God who restrained Laban in the dream now restrains Esau in his affections.

33:10

On Jacob's "like seeing the face of God": Calvin reads this as a theological reflection born of the Peniel experience: "Jacob, having seen the face of God in a wonderful manner, now, by a kind of comparison, says that a similar joy had been afforded him by the sight of his brother's face. He does not say that Esau was like God, but that the kindness which Esau had shown was an image, as it were, of divine favour." The created grace of human forgiveness reflects the uncreated grace of God.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
33:4

The NET note on the dots in the MT over "he kissed him" surveys the traditional interpretations: unusual emotion (some traditions) vs. scribal uncertainty about the word. The note observes that the five-verb sequence is authentic and that the dots likely indicate special scribal attention rather than doubt. The note connects the reconciliation scene to Luke 15:20, where the father's reception of the prodigal uses the same physical vocabulary: ran, fell on neck, kissed, wept.

33:10

The NET note on "seeing your face is like seeing the face of God" connects this to the Peniel naming in 32:30. The note observes the irony: Jacob, who feared Esau's face for twenty years, now equates it with the face of God he had sought at Peniel. The reversal is the theological point: the expected face of wrath has become the unexpected face of grace.

Verses 12–20 — The Parting; Jacob Arrives at Shechem
121314151617181920

Hebrew Word Study

שָׁלֵם šālēm — safely / in peace: Could be a place name (Salim/Salem) or an adverb: "Jacob came safely (šālēm) to the city of Shechem." Both meanings resonate: peace and completeness after a dangerous journey.

אֵל אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל ʾēl ʾĕlōhê yiśrāʾēl — El, God of Israel: Jacob's altar name — "El [is] the God of Israel" — the first time the name Israel is used of the people rather than just the patriarch. Jacob names God in relation to his new identity.

Historical Context

Jacob's decision not to follow Esau to Seir but to go to Succoth and then Shechem has generated debate: is this faithfulness (he is going toward Canaan as commanded) or diplomatic evasion of Esau's companionship? The excuse given — his children and flocks are weak — may be genuine or may be a polite way of separating from Esau without confrontation. Either way, the outcome is that Jacob establishes himself at Shechem in Canaan proper, erects an altar, and names it "God, the God of Israel" — the first act of formal worship on Canaanite soil with his new name.

Context

Jacob does not follow Esau to Seir — his direction is Canaan, not Edom. His polite deflection (v.14: "I will come to my lord in Seir") was apparently unfulfilled; Esau and Jacob next meet at Isaac's death (35:29). Jacob's settlement at Shechem is theologically significant: Shechem is where Abram first camped in Canaan and received the land promise (12:6). Jacob buys the land legally (echoing Abraham's purchase of Machpelah in ch.23) and builds an altar — the same sequence as Abraham. The land claim is established by purchase and worship.

Cross-Reference

  • Josh 24:32Joseph's bones buried at Shechem in the plot of ground Jacob bought — the purchase of land at Shechem foreshadows Israel's future there.
  • John 4:5-6Jesus meets the Samaritan woman at Jacob's well near Shechem — the land of Genesis 33 becomes the setting of a major NT encounter.

Places

Shechem
Central Canaan; between Ebal and Gerizim
After the reunion with Esau, Jacob settles in Shechem and buys a parcel of ground — the second legal land purchase in Genesis after Machpelah. He erects an altar called El Elohe Israel. Shechem is also where the covenant family is dangerously concentrated when Dinah is violated in ch.34.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
33:13-14

Jacob's refusal to match Esau's pace — "the children are tender and the flocks and herds are nursing; if they are driven hard for even one day, all the animals will die" — is practically sound but narratively ambiguous. Jacob will ultimately go to Succoth (v.17) rather than Seir. He may be tactfully redirecting without confronting. After twenty years of living by his wits with Laban, Jacob's diplomatic deflection is a practised skill.

33:18-20

Jacob's arrival at Shechem "in peace" (shalem) and his purchase of land from the sons of Hamor is a significant moment: Jacob is the first patriarch to purchase land in Canaan proper (Abraham purchased Machpelah in 23:1-20). The altar he erects is named El Elohe Yisrael — "God, the God of Israel" — the first use of his new name in a worship context. The wrestling match at Peniel is embodied here in the altar's name.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
33:17

Succoth — "booths" — is named for the temporary shelters Jacob built for his livestock. Sarna notes the irony: the man who has just experienced the most profound divine encounter of his life (Peniel) and the most gracious human encounter (Esau's reception) now builds booths — impermanent, pragmatic structures. The sacred and the domestic coexist in the Genesis narrative without tension.

33:20

The altar name El Elohe Yisrael — "God, the God of Israel" — is the formal appropriation of the new name in worship. Sarna: "For the first time Jacob worships under his new name. The wrestling match has created a new worshipper. The man who left Canaan with only a staff (28:22) returns as Israel and builds an altar in the land." The two-decade journey from Bethel to Peniel to Shechem is completed in this act of naming the altar.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
33:12-15

The exchange between Esau and Jacob in vv.12-15 is a masterpiece of diplomatic indirection. Alter notes that neither man says directly what he means: Esau offers to accompany or precede Jacob; Jacob declines politely with reasons that are plausible but not necessarily true; Esau offers men as escort; Jacob declines again. Both men are navigating the residue of twenty years of estrangement with the tools of courtesy. The reconciliation is genuine but the distance is maintained.

33:20

The altar name El Elohe Yisrael encodes a transformation: no longer "the God of my father Abraham" or "the God of Isaac" (as in the prayer of 32:9) but "the God of Israel" — the God of Jacob himself, under his new name. Alter: "Jacob has moved from the faith of his fathers to a personal faith with a personal name. The altar is both memorial and claim — this is my God, and this is my land."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
33:13-14

Calvin defends Jacob's deflection: "Jacob did not dare to trust himself entirely to his brother... the recent reconciliation was not sufficiently confirmed to induce Jacob confidently to place himself and his whole family under his brother's protection." Calvin reads caution, not deception: after twenty years of estrangement and the night at Peniel, Jacob is still learning to trust. The fear has been diminished but not entirely removed.

33:18-20

Calvin on the altar: "The altar was a testimony of his gratitude. Jacob, having returned to his country after a long banishment, and having obtained possession of a part of the land, confesses that it is owing to the divine favour that he enjoys so much tranquility, and that he still retains a footing in the land." The purchase of land and the altar together are Jacob's dual act of settlement — material and spiritual — in the land of promise.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
33:18

The NET note on "he arrived safely" discusses the Hebrew shalem — the same root as shalom (peace/wholeness). The note observes the word also appears in the name Salem (Jerusalem) and connects Jacob's arrival in "peace/wholeness" to the divine promise of 28:21 ("I will return safely [beshalom] to my father's house"). The promise made at Bethel is being fulfilled: Jacob has returned to Canaan in peace.

33:20

The NET note on El Elohe Yisrael surveys the translation options: "God, the God of Israel" (ESV, NIV) vs. "El is the God of Israel" (using El as the proper name of the Canaanite deity, claiming him for Israel's God). The note favours the former but acknowledges the ambiguity. The name is significant as the first instance of Jacob using the new name given at Peniel in a formal religious act.

Ancient Sources

Midrash Rabbah
Rabbinic tradition debates whether Esau's kiss (v.4) was sincere or deceptive — some rabbis suggested he intended to bite Jacob's neck rather than kiss it, but was miraculously prevented.
This tradition reflects rabbinic discomfort with Esau's positive portrayal and the identification of Esau with Rome in later tradition.
Amarna Letters (14th c. BC)
The seven-fold prostration of v.3 appears in the Amarna tablets as the standard greeting formula of Canaanite vassals to Pharaoh: "I bow seven times before my lord."
The social gesture Jacob uses is authentic to the period and region.

Reception History

Origen, Homilies on Genesis
Esau's running embrace is a type of God's reception of the repentant soul — the magnanimity of grace toward one who had wronged the giver.
Luther, Lectures on Genesis
Luther sees Jacob's voluntary submission (calling Esau "my lord") as the fruit of genuine humility born at Peniel — the wrestler who prevailed with God now willingly yields to his brother.

Genesis 33 is the resolution of the 20-year crisis that has driven the Jacob narrative. The feared encounter becomes a grace-filled reconciliation. The wrestler who named God's face (Peniel) now sees God's face in his brother's forgiveness. The chapter ends with the first act of worship under the name Israel — the Peniel transformation embodied in an altar.

Textual Notes

33:10רָאִיתִי פָנֶיךָ כִּרְאֹת פְּנֵי אֱלֹהִים — "as if I have seen the face of God"
MT ki re'iti fanekha kir'ot penei Elohim — "I have seen your face as one sees the face of God." The comparison is to the Peniel experience (32:30: "I have seen God face to face").
Jacob's comparison of Esau's face to God's face is disorienting unless read as Jacob's unexpected grace experience: at Peniel he received unexpected divine grace; now from Esau he receives unexpected human grace. Both encounters were with one who had every reason to destroy him; both showed him mercy instead. The parallel is the narrative's theological comment on forgiveness.

Scholarly Debates

Is the Jacob-Esau Reconciliation Genuine?
Genuine reconciliation
Hamilton, Wenham
Esau runs to meet Jacob, embraces him, weeps, and insists on accompanying him. The language mirrors the prodigal son parable (Luke 15:20). The reconciliation is real.
Surface diplomacy
Alter, von Rad, some readings
Jacob declines Esau's offer to travel together (v.14-15) and then goes in a different direction than he said he would (v.17: "Esau returned to Seir; Jacob went to Succoth"). Jacob's evasion suggests residual distrust and strategic self-protection rather than full reconciliation.
Esau's response is clearly genuine — the narrative presents it as such. Jacob's response is more complex: real gratitude and relief mixed with lingering caution. The reconciliation is genuine at the level of Esau's forgiveness; Jacob's wariness reflects his character rather than Esau's insincerity. Full trust takes longer than forgiveness.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 33 is the grace chapter of the Jacob narrative. After twenty years of flight and wrestling, the feared encounter with Esau becomes the most unexpected scene in the entire story: the wronged brother runs to embrace the deceiver. Jacob interprets the encounter theologically: Esau's face is like God's face — the same grace that met him in the night wrestling meets him in his brother's embrace. The chapter ends with an altar and a new name.
