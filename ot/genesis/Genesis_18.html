Verses 1–15 — The Three Visitors; the Promise of Isaac

Places

Great Trees of Mamre / Hebron
Southern Canaan, near modern Hebron
The three visitors appear at Mamre — Abram's long-term dwelling, now fully established as the covenant camp. The scene unfolds in the domestic space: tent entrance, the heat of the day, water brought for feet. The most significant theophany in the Abraham story happens in the most ordinary domestic setting.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
18:1-3

MacArthur: The three visitors appear as ordinary men; only the narrative's opening (v.1: 'the LORD appeared to Abraham') reveals their identity. The hospitality Abraham shows -- rushing to meet them, bowing low, providing water, preparing a meal -- reflects the ancient code of hospitality and, unknowingly, the entertainer of angels (Heb 13:2).

18:14

MacArthur: 'Is anything too hard for the LORD?' (v.14) is the theological climax of the visit. Sarah's laughter at the impossible promise is met with the divine question that defines the covenant relationship: the God who made the promise is not limited by human biology. The question becomes the foundation of all subsequent covenant faith.

Hebrew Word Study

niplāʾôt nif-la-OT — wonderful / too difficult / miraculous things: "Is anything too hard (yippālēʾ) for the LORD?" (v.14) — the rhetorical question uses a root (pālāʾ) that means to be extraordinary, surpassing, wonderful — the same root used for the "wonders" (niplāʾôt) of the Exodus (Exod 3:20; 34:10). The question is the answer: nothing is pālāʾ for God — no biological impossibility, no human-assessed impossibility overrides divine capability. The question will be echoed at the angel's announcement to Mary: "Nothing will be impossible (adunateō) with God" (Luke 1:37).

śĕḥōq se-KHOK — laughter: Sarah "laughed to herself" (v.12) and then denied it (v.15) — a moment of very human, very understandable disbelief in the impossible. The LORD's response — "Did you not laugh? ... No, but you did laugh" — is neither harsh judgment nor endorsement of unbelief but the gentle, accurate perception of what happened. The name Isaac (ṣāḥaq — "he laughs") will carry this moment forever: the laughter of impossibility becomes the laughter of fulfilled promise.

ʾadonāy a-do-NAI — my Lord / the LORD: Abraham's greeting of the three visitors — "If I have found favour in your eyes, my Lord (ʾadonāy), do not pass your servant by" (v.3) — and the subsequent narrative oscillation between "the men/angels" and "the LORD" reflects the theological complexity of the encounter. The three visitors are divine messengers, but one of them is, or represents, YHWH himself. Theophany in the OT is often mediated — God present but characterised — and this encounter is one of the most dramatic instances.

Historical Context

None

Context

Abraham's hospitality to the three strangers (running to meet them, bowing, preparing a lavish meal) is a paradigm of Near Eastern hospitality. Hebrews 13:2 uses this episode to commend hospitality to strangers: "some have entertained angels unawares." The dramatic irony — Abraham and Sarah hosting divine visitors without initially recognizing them — builds the tension of the announcement beautifully.

Cross-Reference

  • Heb 13:2The explicit NT application: Abraham unknowingly hosted divine messengers. The episode becomes a basis for the Christian virtue of hospitality to strangers.
  • Luke 1:37Gabriel echoes the divine question to Sarah: "For nothing will be impossible with God" — the Annunciation repeats the Mamre promise structure.
  • Rom 9:9Paul quotes Gen 18:10 ("At the appointed time I will return, and Sarah shall have a son") to ground the promise of Isaac in divine faithfulness rather than human descent.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:1-2

Three men appear to Abraham at Mamre — the narrative begins with YHWH but shifts to three men. Sarna: "The identification of the three visitors has been much debated. The text moves fluidly between the singular (YHWH, 'he') and the plural ('they', 'the men') — suggesting that the divine presence manifests through or as the three visitors without fully resolving the theological question of their nature."

18:12-14

Sarah's laughter at the promise is within herself — the divine visitor hears it anyway. The divine question — "Is anything too hard for the LORD?" — is one of the most frequently quoted verses in the Old Testament. Sarna: "The Hebrew 'too hard' (yippaleh) means 'too wonderful / too extraordinary' — the question asks whether the category of the miraculous exceeds God's capacity. The answer is rhetorical: nothing exceeds it."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:1-8

The hospitality scene is described with meticulous attention to its speed and abundance: Abraham runs, Sarah hastens, the servant hurries. Alter: "The series of rapid verbs — ran, hurried, ran again, took — conveys the urgency of hospitality. This is not merely social convention but covenantal welcome: Abraham throws everything he has at these strangers. The hospitality is excessive, and the promise it elicits is correspondingly excessive."

18:13-15

Sarah denies laughing — "I did not laugh" — and is directly corrected: "Yes, you did laugh." Alter: "The divine visitor's insistence — 'No, but you did laugh' — is remarkable for its ordinary domestic quality. God corrects a lie in the same way a parent corrects a child. The narrator does not moralize; the correction is simply reported, and the conversation moves on. Faith, laughter, and embarrassment are woven together in the same breath."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:1-3

The divine appearance in human form accommodates divine condescension to human capacity. Calvin: "It was necessary that God should descend to us, since we cannot ascend to him. He therefore assumed a human form, that he might familiarly converse with Abraham." The divine visits in Genesis are preparation for the Incarnation — God practicing the form of human presence that will be fully realized in Christ.

18:14

"Is anything too hard for the LORD?" is the epistemological ground of all covenant promise. Calvin: "When God promises anything which surpasses the order of nature, and which seems incredible to us, he always proves his power from this consideration — that nothing is impossible with him." The question is the answer to every human objection to divine promise.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
18:1

The NET note on the appearance at Mamre discusses the ambiguity between YHWH and the three men. The note surveys three interpretive positions: (1) three angels, one of whom speaks as YHWH; (2) a theophany of the pre-incarnate Christ accompanied by two angels; (3) a divine manifestation that the narrative deliberately leaves ambiguous. The note observes that the NT's use of this passage (Heb 13:2 — "entertained angels without knowing it") supports the angelic interpretation.

18:9-15

The NET note on Sarah's laughter traces the tsachaq root into Isaac's name. The same laughter of incredulity (v.12) becomes the name of the child (21:3) which becomes the source of Sarah's joyful laughter (21:6). The narrative's wordplay seeds a trajectory from doubt to delight.

Places

Between Mamre and Sodom
The view down toward the Dead Sea basin
The men look toward Sodom from the hill country — the narrative's spatial logic is visible: from the high ground of the covenant dwelling, the doomed city of the plain is visible below. The intercession happens between the two spaces.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
18:17-19

MacArthur: God's internal monologue -- 'shall I hide from Abraham what I am about to do?' -- reveals the intimacy of the covenant relationship. Abraham is God's friend (Isa 41:8; Jas 2:23); friends share what they are doing. The covenant relationship includes divine transparency about divine purposes.

18:32

MacArthur: Abraham stops at ten righteous persons. The intercession does not reach further, perhaps because Abraham assumes ten is a sufficient community to preserve a city. The point of the intercession is not the number but the principle it establishes: God will spare the wicked for the sake of the righteous among them -- the principle fulfilled ultimately in the righteous One who saves the wicked.

Hebrew Word Study

ṣĕʿāqāh tse-a-KAH — outcry / cry for help: "The outcry (ṣĕʿāqāh) against Sodom and Gomorrah is so great" (v.20) — the same word is used for Israel's cry under Egyptian oppression (Exod 2:23: "their cry for help because of their slavery went up to God"). The ṣĕʿāqāh in the OT is almost always the cry of the oppressed seeking justice — which means Sodom's victims, not Sodom's citizens, are crying out. Sodom's sin is not merely sexual but includes the violence and inhospitality toward the vulnerable that will be graphically demonstrated in ch.19.

mišpāṭ / ṣĕdāqāh mish-PAT / tse-da-KAH — justice and righteousness: Abraham's intercession rests on the theological appeal to God's character: "Will not the Judge of all the earth do right (mišpāṭ)?" (v.25). The pairing of mišpāṭ (justice, right judgment) and ṣĕdāqāh (righteousness, right relationship) in the divine character (v.19 — "Abraham will direct his children to keep the way of the LORD by doing what is right and just") is the foundational OT description of divine moral character — the standard that all social ethics in Israel must reflect.

palal pa-LAL — to intercede / to intervene / to pray: Abraham's extended negotiation with God (vv.23-32) is one of the OT's most remarkable intercession texts. The verb hithpallel (the reflex of pālal, "to judge/intervene") is the standard word for prayer — literally "to judge oneself," to place oneself under divine scrutiny on behalf of another. Abraham intercedes not for himself but for any righteous persons who might be in Sodom. His intercession is the model for all prophetic-intercessory prayer: based on God's character, tenacious, and calibrated to the reality of divine mercy.

Historical Context

None

Context

Abraham's intercession for Sodom is one of the most remarkable scenes in the OT — a human pressing divine justice, negotiating down from fifty to ten. The boldness is breathtaking: "Shall not the Judge of all the earth do what is just?" Abraham appeals to God's own character. This is not defiance but deep theological understanding: Abraham knows God is both just and merciful, and grounds his appeal in that knowledge. The intercession fails (ten righteous are not found), but the method — bold, persistent prayer rooted in divine character — becomes the model for biblical prayer.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 32:11–14Moses intercedes for Israel after the golden calf — the same pattern: bold appeal to divine character and covenant promise, God "relenting" from judgment.
  • Luke 18:1–8Jesus' parable of the persistent widow echoes Abraham's persistence in intercession — keep asking, God will answer.
  • Rom 8:34Christ at God's right hand "intercedes for us" — the ultimate fulfillment of Abraham's intercessory role.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:16-21

God deliberates whether to reveal the Sodom plan to Abraham. Sarna: "The divine soliloquy of vv.17-21 is unique in the Abraham narrative — God reasons with himself about how much to tell Abraham. The reasoning — 'should I hide this from Abraham?' — is the theological basis for prophetic revelation: God reveals his plans to his servants (Amos 3:7). Abraham is treated as a friend privy to divine counsel."

18:23-25

Abraham's intercession — "will you sweep away the righteous with the wicked?" — is the first sustained prayer of intercession in the Bible. Sarna: "Abraham does not question God's right to judge; he questions the justice of collective punishment. The argument is theological: the Judge of all the earth must do justice. God's own character is invoked as the standard against which divine action must be measured."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:23-32

The intercession is a negotiation — descending by tens from 50 to 10. Alter: "The decreasing numbers create comic tension: Abraham pushes the divine hospitality to its limit, each time offering a deferential apology before pressing further. The humor is deliberate: the great patriarch haggling with God over the price of Sodom. But the stakes are ultimate — life and death for a city."

18:32

Abraham stops at ten. Alter: "Why does Abraham not go lower? Perhaps he believes ten righteous people could be found. Perhaps he reaches the social minimum for a viable community. Perhaps, knowing what he knows about Sodom, he recognizes that even ten cannot be found. The intercession stops not because Abraham loses nerve but because the logic of justice reaches its limit."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:23

Abraham's intercession establishes the propriety and power of prayer for the wicked. Calvin: "Abraham, in interceding for the Sodomites, shows us an example of that charity which extends even to the unworthy, in hope that some may be saved. He does not plead their merits but the general principle of divine justice." The intercession is grounded in theology, not sentiment.

18:25

"Will not the Judge of all the earth do right?" Calvin: "Abraham dares to measure God's actions by the standard of justice — not because he presumes to judge God, but because he has learned from God himself what justice requires. This is the confidence of faith: God's own character is the warrant for petitioning him." The boldness of the prayer is grounded in the character of the one being addressed.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
18:20-21

The NET note on "the outcry against Sodom and Gomorrah is great" (zaaqat) observes that zaaqah is a legal term for a cry for justice — the same root used for the blood of Abel (4:10) and Israel's cry in Egypt (Exod 2:23). The note observes that God's statement — "I will go down and see" — echoes the Babel narrative (11:5) and establishes the pattern of divine investigation before judgment.

18:22-33

The NET note on Abraham's intercession discusses the legal form: Abraham is functioning as covenant advocate before the divine judge. The decreasing numbers (50 to 10) represent the minimum righteous population required for communal preservation — Abraham is testing the mercy principle's lower limit.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The Three Visitors
Angels / Divine Presence
Identified with YHWH; received as honored guests. The mysterious "three" has fascinated interpreters — Trinitarian readings appear as early as Justin Martyr and are prominent in Orthodox iconography (Rublev's famous Trinity icon depicts this scene).
Abraham
Patriarch; model of hospitality and intercession
Runs to serve strangers, lavishes hospitality, then boldly intercedes for a wicked city. The full range of covenant faithfulness — love of neighbor and passionate prayer — is on display.
Sarah
Matriarch; laughs at the promise
Her hidden laughter is caught by the divine visitor ("Why did Sarah laugh?"). Her denial ("I did not laugh") and the divine insistence ("No, but you did laugh") is the most human exchange in the patriarchal narratives.

Ancient Sources

Rublev's Trinity Icon (c. 1411 AD)
The Russian icon painter Andrei Rublev depicted the three visitors of Genesis 18 as the Holy Trinity — three angelic figures seated around a table. This icon became one of the most reproduced and theologically discussed works of Christian art in history.
The icon visualizes the Patristic reading that Abraham hosted the pre-incarnate Trinity.
Philo, On Abraham §107–132
Philo allegorizes the three visitors as God and his two divine powers (creative and royal). His philosophical reading influenced both Jewish and Christian allegorical traditions.

Reception History

Justin Martyr, Dialogue with Trypho §56
Justin (2nd c.) argued that the "LORD" who appeared at Mamre and who later rained fire on Sodom was the pre-incarnate Christ — distinct from the Father but equally divine. This is the first extended Christian Christophany argument from Genesis 18.
Islamic tradition
The Quran does not directly narrate the Mamre visit but affirms Abraham's hospitality to divine messengers and the announcement of Isaac's birth (Quran 11:69–74; 51:24–30). The visitors are described as angels, not divine figures.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

Abraham's Intercession: Bargaining with God?
Progressive legal argument
Sarna, Alter, Hamilton
Abraham does not bargain — he argues from a legal principle: "Will you sweep away the righteous with the wicked?" He establishes the principle of divine justice (the Judge of all the earth must do right) and then tests its implications.
Model for intercessory prayer
Calvin, MacArthur, Brueggemann
The passage is the OT's paradigm for intercessory prayer: persistence, specificity, grounding in divine character, humility ("I am but dust and ashes"). God welcomes the argument; Abraham stops asking before God stops granting.
Both readings are correct. The passage establishes the legal/theological ground of intercession (divine character as the basis for appeal) and the personal/devotional practice (persistent, humble, specific prayer). The two are not in conflict.
