Genesis 25

Abraham's Death; the Birth of Jacob and Esau

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–11 — Abraham's Death and Burial
1Abraham had taken another wife, whose name was Keturah.2She bore him Zimran, Jokshan, Medan, Midian, Ishbak and Shuah.3Jokshan was the father of Sheba and Dedan; the descendants of Dedan were the Ashurites, the Letushites and the Leummites.4The sons of Midian were Ephah, Epher, Hanok, Abida and Eldaah. All these were descendants of Keturah.5Abraham left everything he owned to Isaac.6But while he was still living, he gave gifts to the sons of his concubines and sent them away from his son Isaac to the land of the east.7Abraham lived a hundred and seventy-five years.8Then Abraham breathed his last and died at a good old age, an old man and full of years; and he was gathered to his people.9His sons Isaac and Ishmael buried him in the cave of Machpelah near Mamre, in the field of Ephron son of Zohar the Hittite,10the field Abraham had bought from the Hittites. There Abraham was buried with his wife Sarah.11After Abraham's death, God blessed his son Isaac, who then lived near Beer Lahai Roi.

Places

Cave of Machpelah
Hebron; family tomb
Isaac and Ishmael bury Abraham together at Machpelah — the first act of the two divided sons after their separation. The shared burial act in the family tomb is the geography of reconciliation: together at the covenant's designated resting place.

The covenant promises were not fully possessed in his lifetime (Heb 11:13) but he died in faith, seeing them from a distance.

MacArthur: Isaac and Ishmael buried their father together at Machpelah. The two sons who represent the covenant division -- one chosen, one not -- are united at their father's grave. The burial of Abraham reconciles what the covenant had divided. It anticipates the final reconciliation when all things are gathered under Christ (Eph 1:10).

Hebrew Word Study

gāwaʿ ga-VAH — to expire / to breathe one's last: Abraham "breathed his last (yiggawaʿ) and died at a good old age, an old man and full of years" (v.8). The verb gāwaʿ ("to expire") is more solemn than the ordinary word for death (mût) — it describes the cessation of vital breath. The phrase "full of years" (śābēaʿ yāmîm — satisfied/sated with days) is a covenantal evaluation: Abraham lived out the full measure of his days in covenant faithfulness. The same phrase describes Isaac (35:29), Job (42:17), and David (1 Chr 29:28).

qeber / māʿārāh KE-ver / me-a-RAH — grave / cave: Abraham's burial in the cave of Machpelah (v.9-10) — by both Isaac and Ishmael together — is the first time the two half-brothers are presented together in the narrative after the expulsion of Hagar (21:14). The shared burial of their father is a moment of covenant reconciliation — the two streams of Abraham's descendants uniting to honour their common ancestor in the covenant ground of Machpelah. The grave is the one place where the divided family temporarily becomes one.

bērek / bērak be-RAYKH / bey-RAK — to bless / blessing: "After Abraham's death, God blessed his son Isaac" (v.11). The covenant blessing transfers from generation to generation — Abraham's death does not interrupt the covenant promise but releases it to the next recipient. Isaac lives near Beer Lahai Roi — the well associated with Hagar and Ishmael (16:14) — a geographical detail that keeps the two covenant streams in view simultaneously even as the narrative focus shifts to the covenant line through Isaac.

Historical Context

None

Context

Abraham died "in a good old age, an old man and full of years, and was gathered to his people" — one of the most gracious death notices in Scripture. The phrase "gathered to his people" implies belief in an afterlife beyond the grave, a gathering of the dead. Most significantly: Isaac and Ishmael bury their father together. After years of separation, the two sons are reunited in grief and honor. It is the last recorded moment of reconciliation in the Abraham story.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 49:33; 50:13Jacob also "gathered to his people" and is buried in Machpelah — the patriarchal death formula repeating.
  • Heb 11:13Abraham "died in faith, not having received the things promised" — the seemingly incomplete life is actually a life of faith completed, awaiting future fulfillment.

Alter — Literary Reading

25:8

Abraham died "at a good old age, an old man and full of years." Alter: "The death notice is one of the most peaceful in the Bible — three qualifications of completeness: good age, old, full. Abraham's life has been the story of promise, testing, and faith. The conclusion is not triumph or drama but the quiet fullness of a life completely lived. 25:9-11

Isaac and Ishmael burying their father together is one of Genesis's most moving scenes. Alter notes the narrative economy: no dialogue, no explanation of how they came to meet. The silence speaks: whatever divided them in life, Abraham's death is answered by his sons' shared act of honour.

Verses 12–18 — The Descendants of Ishmael
12This is the account of the family line of Abraham's son Ishmael, whom Sarah's slave, Hagar the Egyptian, bore to Abraham.13These are the names of the sons of Ishmael, listed in the order of their birth: Nebaioth the firstborn of Ishmael, Kedar, Adbeel, Mibsam,14Mishma, Dumah, Massa,15Hadad, Tema, Jetur, Naphish and Kedemah.16These were the sons of Ishmael, and these are the names of the twelve tribal rulers according to their settlements and camps.17Ishmael lived a hundred and thirty-seven years. He breathed his last and died, and he was gathered to his people.18His descendants settled in the area from Havilah to Shur, near the eastern border of Egypt, as you go toward Ashur. And they lived in hostility toward all the tribes related to them.

Places

From Havilah to Shur
Arabian Peninsula to Egyptian border
Ishmael's twelve sons settle from Havilah (eastern Arabia) to Shur (the Egyptian border) "in the vicinity of all his brothers." The territory is vast — the entire Arabian peninsula and Sinai. The covenant's outside has its own geography of fulfilment.

Hebrew Word Study

Yišmāʿēl yish-ma-EL — Ishmael — God hears: The twelve princes of Ishmael (vv.13-16) fulfil the promise of 17:20: "I will make him into a great nation." The twelve-tribe structure of Ishmael's descendants mirrors the twelve tribes of Israel — a structural parallel suggesting that God's covenant purposes for Abraham's entire lineage have a symmetry: both Ishmael and Jacob/Israel generate twelve-tribe confederacies. The fulfilment of the Ishmael promise occurs within the text that is transitioning to the Jacob narrative — a reminder that God's faithfulness is not narrowed to one covenant line.

nāpal na-FAL — to fall / to settle / to camp: "And they lived in hostility toward all the tribes related to them" (v.18) — or more literally, "he settled (nāpal) to the east of all his relatives." The word nāpal ("fell upon/settled") in territorial contexts means to settle in a region — Ishmael's descendants occupy the territory from Egypt to Assyria (v.18). The "hostility" (literally "before the face of") translates the same phrase used of Ishmael in 16:12 — a perpetual orientation of independence and resistance that has characterised his descendants throughout history.

tôledôt to-le-DOT — account of generations: "This is the account of the family line of Abraham's son Ishmael" (v.12) — the tôledôt formula for Ishmael is brief (vv.12-18) before the narrative transitions to "the account of the family line of Abraham's son Isaac" (v.19). The structural contrast is deliberate: Ishmael's tôledôt is quickly summarised; Isaac's (the covenant line) will unfold over the next thirty-five chapters. The brevity is not theological dismissal but narrative focus — the covenant story must be told fully; the peripheral story is acknowledged and closed.

Historical Context

None

Context

The oracle to Rebekah (v.23) overturns the natural birth order before the boys are born — "the older shall serve the younger." The entire Jacob story unfolds under this reversal. The birthright sale (vv.29–34) shows Jacob acquiring by cunning what God had already promised by oracle — creating a character study of someone who grasps at what God would have given anyway. The pattern will cost Jacob decades of exile.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 9:10–13Paul uses the Esau/Jacob reversal as his foundational text for divine election: "Though they were not yet born and had done nothing either good or bad...she was told, 'The older will serve the younger.'" God's choice precedes human action.
  • Heb 12:16–17Hebrews uses Esau as a warning against being "sexually immoral or unholy like Esau, who sold his birthright for a single meal" — a type of those who trade the spiritual for the immediate.
  • Mal 1:2–3"Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated" — the Malachi reference cited by Paul in Romans 9 takes the Genesis narrative into prophetic and theological territory.

Verses 19–34 — The Birth of Jacob and Esau; the Birthright Sold
19This is the account of the family line of Abraham's son Isaac. Abraham became the father of Isaac,20and Isaac was forty years old when he married Rebekah daughter of Bethuel the Aramean from Paddan Aram and sister of Laban the Aramean.21Isaac prayed to the Lord on behalf of his wife, because she was childless. Isaac was sixty years old when Rebekah gave birth to them.27The boys grew up, and Esau became a skillful hunter, a man of the open country, while Jacob was content to stay at home among the tents.28Isaac, who had a taste for wild game, loved Esau, but Rebekah loved Jacob.29Once when Jacob was cooking some stew, Esau came in from the open country, famished.30He said to Jacob, "Quick, let me have some of that red stew! MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
25:21-23

MacArthur: Isaac prayed for his barren wife (v.21) -- 25 years after God had promised the covenant line through Isaac, the barrenness continues. The pattern of barrenness followed by miraculous conception recurs: Sarah, Rebekah, Rachel. Each birth is a demonstration that the covenant continues by divine provision, not human fertility.

25:29-34

MacArthur: Esau's sale of his birthright illustrates the spiritual principle that the person who lives for immediate gratification despises eternal inheritance. 'He ate and drank, and then got up and left. So Esau despised his birthright' (v.34) -- the narrative's verdict is theological, not just biographical. The writer of Hebrews calls Esau 'godless' (Heb 12:16) for this reason.

Hebrew Word Study

ʿāqār a-KAR — barren: Rebekah is ʿăqārāh — barren (v.21). The pattern is now established: each covenant woman in Genesis is barren until God opens her womb (Sarah: ch.21; Rebekah: ch.25; Rachel: ch.30). The barrenness is not accidental but theological — it creates the condition in which the divine gift character of the covenant child is unmistakable. Each birth is a demonstration of divine power over the biological impossibility that would otherwise close the covenant line. Paul reads this pattern in Gal 4:23-27: "the son of the free woman was born through the promise."

dārash da-RASH — to seek / to enquire / to resort to: Isaac "prayed (wayyeʿtar) to the LORD on behalf of his wife" and Rebekah "went to enquire (lidrōš) of the LORD" (vv.21-22). Two verbs of seeking God: ʿātar ("to pray urgently/to burn incense before") and dāraš ("to seek/enquire" — the technical term for consulting an oracle or prophet). The covenant couple's response to barrenness is to pray and seek divine revelation — the model for all covenant problem-solving. Prayer precedes and accompanies the promise's fulfilment.

bĕkōr / ṣāʿîr be-KOR / tsa-EER — firstborn / younger: "The older (rab) will serve the younger (ṣāʿîr)" (v.23) — the divine oracle overturns the natural order of primogeniture before the twins are born. The bĕkōr (firstborn) convention — Ishmael, Esau, and Reuben all forfeited or were bypassed in their firstborn status — runs through Genesis as a theological statement: covenant election is not natural hierarchy but divine choice. Paul reads the oracle in Rom 9:10-13: "before the twins were born or had done anything good or bad — in order that God's purpose in election might stand."

Sarna — JPS Commentary

25:21-26

The birth narrative of Jacob and Esau contains the three elements of a birth oracle: the divine consultation (Rebekah inquires of the LORD), the oracle (two nations in your womb; the older will serve the younger), and the fulfilment (the twins born in characteristic postures — Esau red and hairy, Jacob grasping Esau's heel). Sarna: "The oracle of the elder serving the younger reverses the expected social order — it is the divine right of primogeniture over human convention."

25:29-34

The birthright trade is one of the most economically compressed moral narratives in Genesis. Sarna: "Esau's dismissal of his birthright — 'what good is the birthright to me?' — is the defining characterisation of his life. He is the man who lives for the present moment ('I am about to die') at the expense of the future. The writer's summary — 'so Esau despised his birthright' — passes judgment without moralising."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Abraham
Patriarch; "full of years"; gathered to his people
His death is described with the most gracious language in the OT — no tragedy, no regret, a life completed. He sees some of the promise fulfilled (Isaac born, married, blessed by God) though not the nation or the full land. He dies as Hebrews 11 describes: in faith, not sight.
Esau
Firstborn son; hunter; man of the present moment
His identity is appetite and immediacy: "I am about to die; of what use is a birthright to me?" He values present hunger over future inheritance. The narrative does not hate Esau — he is vigorous, real, understandable — but it marks him as one who lives for today at the expense of tomorrow.
Jacob
Second-born; craftsman; man of the future
His name means "he supplants/grasps the heel" — a portent of his character. He acquires by calculation what God had already promised by oracle. His grasping nature will require decades of divine discipline to transform him into "Israel" (he who struggles with God).
Rebekah
Mother; receives the oracle
She alone knows the oracle — "the older shall serve the younger." Her later favoritism of Jacob (ch.27) must be read in this light, even if her methods are wrong. She is trying to fulfill what God has promised.

Ancient Sources

Mari Prophetic Texts
The oracle to Rebekah during her difficult pregnancy ("She went to inquire of the LORD") parallels ANE practices of seeking oracles at sanctuaries during pregnancy. Mari texts describe women seeking divine guidance for difficult pregnancies.

Reception History

Paul, Romans 9:10–13
Paul's use of the Esau/Jacob election as his key text for divine sovereignty is one of the most contested passages in NT theology. He reads the prenatal election as proof that God's choice is based on "him who calls, not on works or merit." This generates the Calvinist/Arminian debate about predestination that has shaped Western Christianity.
Hebrews 12:15–17
The warning against "Esau-type" faith — selling eternal inheritance for immediate gratification — has made Esau a pastoral warning figure across church history. His regret (v.34 LXX: he was "rejected" though he "sought the blessing with tears") is a sobering portrait.

Literary Structure

Together at the Tomb: Division Resolved25:9Isaac and Ishmael bury their father together. No dialogue, no explanation. Whatever divided them in life, Abraham's death is answered by his sons' shared act of honour.
The Birthright Sold: Measured in a Bowl25:29-34He ate, drank, rose, and left -- so Esau despised his birthright. The narrative's verdict is in the verb sequence. The birthright is measured in a bowl of stew and dismissed with the rising from the table.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

Romans 9 and the Jacob-Esau Election: Corporate or Individual?
Individual election to salvation
Augustine, Calvin, Reformed tradition
Paul's use of Jacob/Esau in Rom 9:10-13 is about individual predestination to salvation. The prenatal choice establishes God's sovereign freedom in election apart from works.
Corporate/national election
Cranfield, Dunn, Wright, many NT scholars
Paul's primary reference is to the corporate election of Israel-over-Edom — the choice of Jacob's line as the covenant people. Election in Genesis 25 is about covenant headship, not necessarily eternal destiny.
Historical then typological
Wenham, Hamilton, Sarna
The Genesis narrative is about covenant succession, not eternal salvation. Paul draws on it for a broader argument about God's sovereign freedom in the new covenant. Both the historical and theological applications are valid.
The debate is extensive and ongoing. The corporate reading of Genesis 25 is clearly correct — the twins represent nations (v.23: "two nations are in your womb"). Paul's application in Romans 9 may extend this to individual election, but the Genesis text itself is about covenant succession. The two applications are not in conflict.
