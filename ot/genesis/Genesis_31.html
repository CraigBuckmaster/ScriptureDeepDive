Genesis 31

Jacob's Flight; Laban's Pursuit; the Mizpah Covenant

Verses 1–16 — Jacob Resolves to Leave; God Commands It
12345678910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

תְּרָפִים terāpîm — household gods / teraphim: Small figurines used for divination and domestic cult. Their exact function is debated; they appear in Judges 17–18, 1 Samuel 19:13, and Ezekiel 21:21 as objects of illicit religious practice.

גָּנַב gānab — steal: The same root used for the 8th commandment. Rachel's theft echoes Jacob's own history of taking what belonged to another; the family pattern continues.

שָׁמַר šāmar — watch / guard: Laban accuses Jacob of guarding his heart in secret (v.20 lit. "stole the heart of Laban"). The idiom means to deceive — again the family lexicon of deception.

Historical Context

The divine command to return is given in the context of Laban's changed disposition (vv.1-2) and Rachel and Leah's confirmation (vv.14-16). The consultation with the wives before departing is significant: Jacob is not making a unilateral decision but building a family consensus. Rachel and Leah's response — "all the wealth God has taken from our father belongs to us and our children" — indicates they have arrived at the same conclusion about Laban's exploitation and God's just redistribution.

Context

Jacob's departure is divinely commanded (v.3) and confirmed by the Bethel angel (v.13) — he is not fleeing in fear but obeying God. The twenty-year arc is complete: God kept his Bethel promise (28:15: "I will be with you and will watch over you wherever you go, and I will bring you back"). Rachel's theft of the teraphim introduces a subplot of unresolved idolatry that Jacob will address in 35:2–4 when he buries all the foreign gods under the oak at Shechem.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 28:15God's Bethel promise: "I will bring you back" — now fulfilled.
  • Gen 35:2–4Jacob commands his household to put away foreign gods; the teraphim are finally buried.
  • Deut 26:5The liturgical confession: "My father was a wandering Aramean" — Jacob's Paddan-aram sojourn remembered.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:1-2

The change in Laban's household is visible and atmospheric: Laban's sons are resentful, Laban's face is no longer friendly. Jacob reads the signs correctly. Twenty years of service, ten changes of wages, constant manipulation — and the prosperity that God has given Jacob despite all of it has made him a threat in Laban's eyes. The providential blessing that should have created gratitude has instead created jealousy.

31:5-9

Jacob's review of Laban's ten wage changes is both a complaint and a testimony. The complaint: Laban has been consistently dishonest, changing the terms whenever Jacob was prospering. The testimony: "God did not allow him to harm me." Jacob credits the divine protection, not his own ingenuity, for his survival. This is a spiritually mature reading of the previous twenty years.

31:11-13

The angel of God in the dream reveals the theological explanation for the breeding strategy: "I have seen all that Laban has been doing to you." God has been watching the exploitation and responding to it — not by removing Jacob from the situation but by using it to accomplish his enrichment. The revelation reframes the entire Haran sojourn: what looked like human cunning was divinely guided strategy.

31:14-16

Rachel and Leah's declaration — "do we still have any share in our father's estate?" — is a remarkable act of daughters breaking with their father in favour of their husband. Laban has treated them as commodities: he sold them, used their bride price, and treated the proceeds as his own. Their solidarity with Jacob against Laban is both a practical decision and a theological alignment with the God who has been Jacob's protector.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:1

The shift in Laban's disposition is encoded in the single phrase "Laban's face was not favorable toward him as before." Sarna notes the Hebrew idiom for social relationship — the face turned toward or away — reflects the ancient Near Eastern understanding of relationship as visibility. Laban has turned his face away: the social bond is fracturing. Jacob reads the body language of the household before God speaks explicitly.

31:14-16

Rachel and Leah's response to Jacob's consultation is extraordinary. Sarna notes they speak with a single voice ("they replied" is plural in Hebrew, then a single speech follows) — an unusual narrative device signaling unusual agreement. The women who have competed bitterly for a decade unite against their father. Their accusation — "He has sold us and devoured our purchase price" — is a legal charge: Laban has violated the marriage contract by retaining the bride price for himself.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:3

God's command to return is embedded in reported speech (Jacob reports it to his wives in v.3 and to the reader through the wives in v.13). Alter notes the narrative indirection: we do not see the divine communication directly but hear it twice refracted — first as Jacob's report to his wives, then as the content of a dream. The technique creates a layered sense of how divine revelation penetrates into the domestic and practical decisions of ordinary life.

31:14-16

The wives' speech is one of the most detailed instances of female speech in Genesis, and it is legally precise. Alter notes: they do not appeal to sentiment (we love Jacob) or emotion (we miss Canaan) but to economic justice (Laban sold us and consumed the proceeds). Their theological conclusion — "all the wealth God took from our father and gave to us" — interprets Jacob's prosperity as divine redistribution of what Laban owed them.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
31:3

Calvin reads the divine command to return as the resolution of Jacob's twenty-year exile: "God, after having long delayed, now commands Jacob to return. For Jacob had not rashly left his father's house, but had obeyed the commandment of God; and now, after a course of twenty years, God, who had sent him away, recalls him." The departure and the return are both under divine command. Jacob is not a fugitive but a servant of God under orders.

31:11-13

Calvin on the dream revelation: "God here confirms what he had before promised at Bethel; as if he would say, 'I am the God who appeared to thee in a vision.' He purposely reminds Jacob of the vision, to show that he had not in vain made that solemn engagement." The angel in the dream connects the Haran sojourn back to its starting point at Bethel. Twenty years of promise-keeping are summarised in the command to return.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
31:3

The NET note on "return to the land of your fathers" observes this echoes the Abrahamic call in 12:1 (leave your country, your people, your father's household) in reverse: the journey outward in 12:1 is now matched by the journey homeward in 31:3. The covenant land that Abram entered is the land Jacob is being recalled to. The note highlights this as a sign that the Abrahamic promises are being progressively fulfilled in the next generation.

31:13

The NET note on "I am the God of Bethel" identifies this as a divine self-identification formula (similar to Exodus 20:2) that grounds the command in the prior covenant relationship. The note observes that the angel claims to be God — one of the theophanies of the Angel of the LORD that the NT and Christian tradition identify with the pre-incarnate Son.

Verses 17–35 — The Flight; Rachel Steals the Household Gods

Hebrew Word Study

פַּחַד יִצְחָק paḥad yitsḥāq — Fear of Isaac: A divine name found only in v.42 and v.53. Not fear that Isaac experienced, but the Fearsome One whom Isaac worshipped — an archaic epithet for God as the Awesome Sovereign.

רֵיקָם rêqām — empty-handed: The opposite of "blessing." Laban sent nothing; God provided everything. The word contrasts with the abundant flocks Jacob takes — all of it, Jacob insists, is God's provision.

Context

God's warning to Laban in the dream (v.24) is not protection of Jacob's deception but intervention to prevent Laban from harming Jacob — God restrains the powerful uncle from doing violence to the patriarch. Jacob's speech (vv.36–42) is the first time in Genesis he speaks with full moral clarity: he lists twenty years of faithful service and directly attributes his prosperity to God. The man who once grabbed his brother's heel now stands before his accuser and invokes "the Fear of Isaac."

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 20:3God warns Abimelech in a dream — same pattern of divine protection via nocturnal warning.
  • Gen 28:20–22Jacob's Bethel vow: "if God gives me bread to eat and clothing to wear" — v.41 shows God kept exactly this promise.

Places

Flight route from Paddan Aram toward Canaan
Upper Mesopotamia to Gilead
Jacob flees while Laban is shearing his sheep three days' journey away. Laban pursues and catches Jacob in the hill country of Gilead (east of the Jordan) after seven days. The flight traces the northern route from Harran down through Transjordan toward Canaan.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:17-20

The flight is immediate upon God's word. Jacob waits for the right moment (shearing time, when Laban is occupied and distant) and moves his entire household — two wives, two concubines, eleven sons, his flocks and possessions — without informing Laban. The scale of the departure is enormous; the secrecy is total. Jacob has learned from twenty years of Laban's manipulations that transparency is not a viable strategy.

31:22-24

Laban pursues and overtakes Jacob after seven days. The pursuit is cut short before it begins: God appears to Laban in a dream warning him not to say anything to Jacob — either good or bad. The phrase "good or bad" is a merism for "anything at all." God restrains Laban preemptively. The divine protection Jacob has been promised (28:15, 31:3) is enacted precisely when the threat is greatest.

31:34-35

Rachel's deception — sitting on the camel saddle containing the teraphim and pleading "the custom of women is upon me" — is an audacious act in the ancient world, where menstruation created ritual impurity that would be transmitted by contact. Laban cannot search the saddle without contamination. Rachel has used the structure of ancient purity law to protect her theft. The narrative does not endorse the deception but records it with a certain narrative relish.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:19

The teraphim are mentioned three times in the narrative (vv.19,34,35), each time in an increasingly ironic register. Sarna notes that the word teraphim appears in contexts of divination (Ezek 21:21; Zech 10:2), inheritance rights (as attested in Nuzi texts), and household religion. Rachel's motivation is unclear — the narrative deliberately withholds explanation. What is clear is that her theft of the household gods ironically renders Laban's pursuit futile: his oracular objects are in the saddlebag of the woman he cannot touch.

31:24

Laban's dream — God warns him directly — is the only time in the patriarchal narratives that God communicates with a non-covenant figure as the main recipient (cf. Pharaoh's dreams in ch.41, where the communication is indirect). Sarna: "The divine intervention on Jacob's behalf extends even to restraining his adversary through direct revelation. The God of Jacob is not contained by the boundaries of covenant relationship."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:19

Rachel's theft of the teraphim is described with no editorial comment and no explanation of motive. Alter notes this is a consistent narrative strategy: Genesis does not explain what it does not need to explain for the plot. The reader is left to infer motive (inheritance security? religious attachment? spite?). The absence of explanation creates a more interesting character — Rachel acts from reasons the text will not provide.

31:34-35

The scene of Rachel sitting on the saddle, lying to her father about her condition, while the household gods are literally beneath her — is one of the most pointed ironies in Genesis. Alter: the objects of Laban's religious practice are being protected by Laban's daughter through his own purity customs. The household gods cannot help him find themselves; the purity system he observed cannot be overridden; and Rachel, the most desired object of Jacob's affections, has outmanoeuvred the cleverest man in Haran.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
31:22-24

God's appearance to Laban in a dream is an act of preemptive grace toward Jacob. Calvin: "It was necessary that Laban should be restrained by a divine interposition, for Jacob was far from being his equal in cunning and craft." Calvin acknowledges Jacob's relative vulnerability despite his cleverness: without divine restraint, Laban would have found a way to harm him. The divine warning is not a supplement to Jacob's strategy but its foundation.

31:35

Calvin on Rachel's lie: "The holy patriarch's wife, following the common custom of women, does not scruple to tell a falsehood." He does not excuse the lie but contextualises it: desperation, cultural norms, the high stakes of the pursuit. He uses the episode as a reminder that the saints of Scripture are not perfect examples to be imitated in every particular but flawed humans sustained by grace.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
31:20

The NET note on "Jacob deceived Laban" (lit. "Jacob stole the heart of Laban the Aramean") explains the Hebrew idiom ganav et lev (steal the heart) means to deceive, to prevent from knowing one's intentions. The note notes the irony of the verb ganav appearing for Jacob's deception immediately after Rachel's physical theft in v.19 — two members of the same household stealing from the same man in two different registers.

31:35

The NET note on "the way of women" explains this is a euphemism for menstruation, widely understood in the ancient Near East to create ritual impurity transmitted by contact. The note observes that Rachel's use of this convention is strategically precise: she invokes a purity category that prevents physical search without revealing the actual object of the search. The note leaves open whether the reader is meant to admire or deplore her ingenuity.

Verses 36–55 — Jacob's Accusation; the Covenant at Galeed
3637383940414243444546474849505152535455

Hebrew Word Study

מִצְפָּה miṣpāh — Mizpah / Watchtower: From the root ṣāpāh, "to watch." The name means "watchtower" — here a place where God watches over the boundary between two parties. Later Mizpah is an important religious site in Judges and Samuel.

גַּלְעֵד gal'êd — Galeed: Means "heap of witness" in Hebrew. Laban also calls it Jegar-sahadutha in Aramaic (v.47) — the bilingualism reflects the boundary between Israelite and Aramean spheres.

בְּרִית berît — covenant: The treaty between Jacob and Laban follows recognizable ancient Near Eastern treaty forms: monument, witnesses (v.50: God as witness), oath, meal (v.54).

Context

The Mizpah benediction ("The LORD watch between you and me") is often quoted as a blessing between friends. In context it is a warning: "since we distrust each other, may God watch to ensure neither of us crosses this line." The heap marks the end of the Jacob-Laban cycle. From this point Jacob moves toward Esau and toward Canaan — the land of promise finally in view. Laban disappears from the narrative. The blessing he speaks over his daughters (v.55) is his only recorded act of genuine fatherly love.

Cross-Reference

  • Josh 24:25-27A stone as witness at Shechem — the covenant-stone motif from Gen 31 reappears in Israel's covenant renewal.
  • Isa 43:10You are my witnesses — the witness-language of covenant from Gen 31 pervades the prophetic covenant lawsuits.

Places

Galeed / Mizpah
Hill country of Gilead; Transjordan
The covenant stone is called Galeed ("heap of witness" in Hebrew) and Jegar Sahadutha (Aramaic equivalent) — named in two languages because two peoples are dividing territory. Mizpah ("watchtower") is the covenant's geographical boundary: "the LORD watch between you and me when we are absent from one another."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:36-42

Jacob's sustained speech of accusation (vv.36-42) is the longest recorded speech he makes in the Genesis narrative. After twenty years of patient endurance, Jacob enumerates the injustices systematically: twenty years of work, ten changes of wages, daytime heat and night cold, no compensation for animals lost to wild beasts. The speech ends with the decisive testimony: "if the God of my father had not been with me, you would certainly have sent me away empty-handed."

31:43-44

Laban's response is remarkable: he does not rebut the accusations. He claims the daughters and animals are his — a claim that is legally arguable in his cultural context — but immediately shifts to proposing a covenant. The proposal of a treaty is an implicit acknowledgment that he cannot prevail. The covenant is Laban's face-saving measure after losing the argument.

31:49-50

The Mizpah covenant is structured as mutual surveillance under divine watch. The God who has seen Jacob's twenty years of service, who has watched over him at Bethel and throughout the Haran sojourn, is now invoked as the treaty witness. The "blessing" — "may the LORD watch between you and me" — is a warning to both parties: God sees what humans hide. The covenant is sealed by sacrifice and a shared meal — the ancient form of treaty ratification.

31:54-55

The shared meal ratifies the covenant. The next morning, Laban kisses his grandchildren and daughters and departs. The kisses are the last physical act between Laban and Jacob's household. Twenty years of service, deception, and divine overruling end with a formal covenant, a meal, and a farewell. Jacob is now free to continue toward Canaan — and toward the more difficult encounter with Esau.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:42

Jacob's climactic declaration — "if the God of my father, the God of Abraham and the Fear of Isaac, had not been with me..." — introduces the unusual divine title "Fear of Isaac" (pahad Yitzhaq). Sarna notes this title appears only twice in Scripture (vv.42,53) and may reflect the specific aspect of God that Isaac experienced at the near-sacrifice: the God before whom one trembles in awe. The title is Jacob's testimony to the ancestral God who has protected him.

31:47

The two names for the stone heap — Laban's Aramaic Yegar-Sahadutha and Jacob's Hebrew Galeed — are the only instance in the patriarchal narratives of direct Aramaic speech. Sarna: "The bilingual naming underscores the ethnic and linguistic frontier being established." Jacob has been in Aram for twenty years; he returns as a Hebrew to Canaan. The stone heap marks the boundary of his identity.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:36-42

Jacob's speech is the chapter's rhetorical climax. Alter notes the structure: a rebuke (what is my crime?), a review (these twenty years), and a theological verdict (God saw my affliction). The speech is not boasting — Jacob explicitly credits God, not himself, for his survival. But it is assertive: after twenty years of calculated submission, Jacob speaks his full mind. The relationship with Laban is over; Jacob can finally say what he has known throughout.

31:44-50

The covenant scene — stone heap, stone pillar, bilingual naming, the Mizpah formula — is one of the most formally structured treaty scenes in Genesis. Alter notes the solemnity of the bilateral witnessing: God, the stone heap, and both parties together. The Mizpah formula, so often used as a benediction, is a surveillance clause: divine watching ensures compliance. The treaty makes explicit what the entire Haran sojourn has dramatised: God watches, and his watching produces outcomes.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
31:36-42

Calvin on Jacob's indignation: "Jacob was moved, not by private resentment for the injuries he had received, but by a holy zeal. He saw that, if he suffered himself to be thus treated, not only would his own affairs be ruined, but that the covenant of God would be trampled under foot." Jacob's anger is righteous because it is finally in defence of the divine promise, not merely his own interest. Calvin distinguishes this from carnal anger.

31:49-50

On the Mizpah formula: "Moses calls this place Mizpah, that is, a Watch-tower, because Laban said, let God see between us... These words are to be taken as the words of an execration or malediction, not of a blessing." Calvin is the classic source for the correct interpretation: the Mizpah is a mutual warning under divine surveillance, not a prayer for divine protection of the separated parties. The covenant is sealed by the threat of divine judgment for violation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
31:42

The NET note on pahad Yitzhaq ("Fear/Dread of Isaac") surveys the three main interpretive options: (1) the God whom Isaac feared; (2) the God who is the object of Isaac's reverence; (3) a divine title meaning "Kinsman of Isaac" (from a proposed Akkadian cognate). The note acknowledges options 1 and 2 as most linguistically defensible. The title captures the experiential quality of Isaac's encounter with God — specifically the near-sacrifice of Genesis 22.

31:49

The NET note on the Mizpah formula confirms Calvin's interpretation against the devotional use: "May the LORD keep watch between you and me when we are away from each other" — the context is a covenant enforcement clause. The note observes that the formula is invoked specifically in vv.50-52 with treaty stipulations (do not mistreat my daughters, do not marry other women), making clear that the divine watching is contractual surveillance, not tender care.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Jacob
Patriarch, son of Isaac
Standing at the threshold of his return to Canaan after 20 years in Haran. His speech in vv.36–42 marks a new spiritual maturity — attributing all prosperity to God.
Laban
Aramean patriarch, Jacob's uncle
The great antagonist of Jacob's Haran years. An exploiter who changed Jacob's wages ten times yet whose prosperity derived from God's blessing on Jacob.
Rachel
Jacob's beloved wife, Laban's daughter
Her theft of the teraphim introduces a spiritual danger that Jacob unknowingly curses; her premature death (35:16–20) haunts the rest of the Jacob narrative.
Leah
Jacob's first wife
Though less loved, Leah loyally follows Jacob. Her sons — Reuben, Simeon, Levi, Judah — will carry the weight of the tribal narrative.

Ancient Sources

LXX (Septuagint)
The Greek translates teraphim (v.19) as ta eidōla, "the idols" — making explicit the religious dimension that the Hebrew term leaves partly ambiguous.
The LXX interpretation influenced early Christian readings of Rachel's theft as a type of the church taking spiritual treasure from paganism — an allegory found in Origen.
Josephus, Antiquities 1.19
Josephus emphasizes Rachel's intelligence in hiding the teraphim under a camel saddle and inventing a menstrual excuse — he finds her deception admirable where the text is neutral.
Josephus consistently humanizes patriarchal figures by filling in psychological motivation.

Reception History

Augustine, City of God XVIII
The Mizpah covenant functions as a type of the partition between the city of God and the city of man — two peoples dwelling side by side under divine surveillance.
Calvin, Commentary on Genesis
Calvin notes that Jacob's twenty-year inventory of faithful service is not boasting but legitimate witness against a false accusation. The righteous may defend themselves with facts.
Modern Missions Movement
The Mizpah benediction was adopted by 19th-century missionaries sending partners out as a prayer for mutual accountability and protection across distance.

Literary Structure

Divine Command — 31:1-16Laban's changed face, God's command to return, Rachel and Leah's consent. Three confirmations: circumstance, divine word, family unity.
Secret Flight — 31:17-21Jacob departs secretly during shearing. Rachel steals the teraphim — motive unexplained. The deceiver family flees by night.
Pursuit and Dream — 31:22-35Laban pursues, dreams, is warned by God. Overtakes Jacob. Searches for teraphim — foiled by Rachel's ruse.
Twenty years of grievances articulated. The longest sustained speech of Jacob in Genesis. Culminates in: "God has seen."
Mizpah Covenant — 31:43-55Bilateral treaty under divine witness. Bilingual stone heap. The "blessing" that is a surveillance clause. Farewell.
Genesis 31 is structured as the formal conclusion of the Haran sojourn. Every thread is tied off: God's command returns Jacob to the land of promise; Rachel's theft closes the teraphim subplot; Jacob's speech delivers the delayed verdict on twenty years of exploitation; the Mizpah covenant formalises the separation of two peoples. The chapter ends with a departure — not flight this time, but a formal farewell between equals under divine witness.

Textual Notes

31:19תְּרָפִים — teraphim: household gods
MT teraphim — plural form (possibly singular referent). Household gods used for divination and as property-title markers in some ancient Near Eastern contexts. Only here and Judg 17-18; 1 Sam 19:13,16; Hos 3:4; Zech 10:2.
The teraphim's significance in Laban's household may be legal as well as religious: Nuzi texts show that possession of household gods could establish inheritance rights. Rachel's theft may be a claim to Laban's inheritance as well as a religious act. This explains Laban's extreme agitation at their loss.

Scholarly Debates

Mizpah Covenant (31:44-55): Boundary or Blessing?
Mutual surveillance / distrust
Most modern readings
"The LORD watch between you and me when we are absent from one another" (v.49) is a mutual accountability agreement between two parties who do not trust each other. The Mizpah benediction is a covenant of separation, not friendship.
Genuine blessing
Traditional use in Christian practice
Popularised as a farewell blessing, the Mizpah benediction is used by Christians departing from each other as a prayer for divine protection. The use is not illegitimate but the context (distrust between Jacob and Laban) should temper the sentiment.
The Mizpah covenant is primarily a boundary agreement — "this heap of stones is a witness that neither of us will cross to harm the other." The invocation of divine surveillance (v.49) reflects the mutual distrust, not mutual affection. The modern use as a blessing between friends is a secondary application that loses the adversarial context but is not theologically wrong.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 31 brings the Haran episode to a close with a formal covenant that demarcates Jacob from Laban, Hebrew from Aramean, covenant people from the nations. The divine presence that was promised at Bethel (28:15) and reiterated at the departure (31:3) has been active throughout: restraining Laban (v.24), guiding Jacob's breeding strategy (vv.10-12), and overseeing the Mizpah covenant (v.49). The twenty-year sojourn was under God's watch from beginning to end.
