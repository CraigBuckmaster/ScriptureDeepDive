Genesis 6

The Corruption of the Earth; Noah Commissioned

See on Timeline — Flood
Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–8 — Corruption of the Earth; Noah Finds Favor
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

בְּנֵי הָאֱלֹהִים bene haElohim — sons of God: V.2,4: One of the most debated phrases in the entire OT. Three main interpretations: (1) divine beings / angelic figures who intermarried with human women (cf. Job 1:6; 2:1; Ps 82 — where bene Elohim clearly means heavenly beings); (2) the righteous line of Seth (sons of God) intermarrying with Cain's line (daughters of men); (3) kings or rulers claiming divine status. The NT seems to favour interpretation (1): Jude 6-7 and 2 Pet 2:4 connect fallen angels with sexual sin.

נְפִילִים nefilim — Nephilim: V.4: From the root naphal (to fall) — "fallen ones" or possibly "those who cause others to fall." Described as the heroes of old, men of renown. They appear again in Numbers 13:33, where the spies report giants in Canaan. Whether the Nephilim are the offspring of the bene Elohim unions or a pre-existing group is grammatically ambiguous in v.4.

נִחַמְתִּי nichamti — I am grieved / I repent: V.6: Not a change of divine mind in the sense of correction of an error. The word naham (in Niphal) means to grieve, to be deeply moved, to relent. God's grief over human evil is anthropopathic language — it expresses genuine divine sorrow over the creature's condition in terms accessible to human understanding. It does not imply God was surprised or that his eternal purposes are altered.

Historical Context

The pre-flood corruption narrative has close parallels in Mesopotamian flood literature (Atrahasis, Gilgamesh) where the gods also become distressed by the noise or corruption of humanity and decide to destroy it. Genesis's account inverts the Mesopotamian logic: the gods in Atrahasis are disturbed by the clamour of an overpopulated humanity; God in Genesis is grieved by the moral corruption — violence and wickedness. The difference is fundamental: the flood in Genesis is moral judgment, not divine irritation.

Context

The contrast between Genesis 5's closing hope (Noah) and Genesis 6's opening darkness is stark. The world that chapter 5's genealogy traversed in rapid summary was, it turns out, a world sliding toward catastrophe. The filling of the earth with humanity (5:4) has become the filling of the earth with violence (6:11). The divine image has not prevented the spread of evil; if anything, the multiplication of image-bearers has multiplied the capacity for harm.

Cross-Reference

  • Jude 6-7The angels who did not keep their positions of authority — Jude connects the Gen 6 bene Elohim with fallen angels.
  • 2 Pet 2:4-5God did not spare angels when they sinned and did not spare the ancient world but protected Noah — the two events linked.
  • Matt 24:37-38As it was in the days of Noah, so it will be at the coming of the Son of Man — Jesus uses the pre-flood corruption as a type of end-time conditions.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1-4

The "sons of God" and "daughters of men" passage is the most debated text in Genesis after chapter 3. The most exegetically defensible reading identifies the "sons of God" as angelic/divine beings (cf. Job 1:6; 2:1; 38:7, where the same phrase clearly refers to heavenly beings). This reading makes the passage an account of supernatural transgression of the Creator-creature boundary — a cosmic escalation of the original fall.

6:5-6

The divine assessment — "every inclination of the thoughts of the human heart was only evil all the time" — is the most totalising description of human depravity in the entire Bible. This is not saying that some people were very wicked; it is saying that the moral condition of the entire human species had reached a point of comprehensive corruption. The grief of God (v.6) is the appropriate divine response to the destruction of everything he had declared good.

6:1-4

The bene Elohim passage has generated enormous interpretive literature from ancient times. Sarna notes that the Septuagint renders bene Elohim as "angels of God" in some manuscripts, following the supernatural interpretation. The Targums (Aramaic translations) often rationalise to "sons of nobles" or "sons of judges." The rabbinic tradition was divided; the supernatural interpretation was popular in Second Temple Judaism (1 Enoch 6-16 elaborates it extensively).

6:5

Kol yetser machshevot libbo rak ra kol hayyom — "every inclination of the thoughts of his heart was only evil all the time." Sarna: "This is perhaps the most thoroughgoing description of human depravity in biblical literature." Five separate intensifiers — every, inclinations, thoughts, only, all the time — pile up to create a total picture. The decision to send the flood is not arbitrary but is the appropriate response to a species that has become comprehensively self-destructive.

6:5-6

The paired statements — human evil is total (v.5), God is grieved (v.6) — create a striking emotional tableau. Alter: "The God of Genesis is not a cold judicial machine delivering verdict on an antiseptic calculation. He grieves. The verb naham (be grieved) and the phrase 'it pained his heart' (wayyitatsev el libbo) — the same root as the pain in childbirth (itsabon, 3:16) and the pain of labour (itsabon, 3:17) — connects the divine grief to the human grief of the fall's consequences. God shares in the pain of what sin has produced."

6:8

"But Noah found favour in the eyes of the LORD" — the word chen (favour, grace) appears here for the first time in the Bible. It is the Hebrew word that will give us the NT concept of grace (charis in Greek). In the darkest moment of human history — God grieving over total corruption — one man finds grace. The entire flood narrative pivots on this one verse.

6:5

Calvin on "every inclination of the thoughts of the human heart was only evil all the time": "This passage, therefore, stands as a firm and invincible defence against those who hold that men are able, by their own natural strength, to be obedient to God." For Calvin, Genesis 6:5 is the foundational OT text for the doctrine of total depravity — the comprehensive corruption of the human will, not just its partial weakening. Every faculty, not just some, has been affected by the fall.

6:6-7

That God "repented" of making man does not imply change in the divine being. Calvin: "The repentance which is here ascribed to God does not properly belong to him, but has reference to our understanding of him. For since we cannot comprehend him as he is, it is necessary that, for our sake, he should be described as we conceive of him." The language of divine grief is accommodated to human capacity — a genuine emotional reality expressed in terms we can understand.

6:2

The NET note on bene haElohim devotes extensive treatment to the three major interpretive options, concluding that the angelic/supernatural interpretation has the strongest support from the immediate context (where the term clearly refers to divine beings in other OT uses), from ancient Jewish interpretation (LXX, 1 Enoch, Dead Sea Scrolls), and from the NT (Jude 6-7; 2 Pet 2:4). The note acknowledges the Sethite interpretation is widely held among evangelical scholars but finds the textual evidence stronger for the supernatural reading.

6:6

The NET note on wayyinnachem (grieved/repented) distinguishes between God's repentance here (a genuine emotional response to human sin, expressed in human terms) and the theological claim that God does not repent (Num 23:19; 1 Sam 15:29) — which refers to divine reliability in keeping promises. The note identifies this as anthropopathism (attributing human emotions to God) rather than anthropomorphism (attributing human physical form).

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:1-4

The "sons of God" (bene haElohim) passage is one of the most debated in the entire Torah. Sarna surveys the three main ancient interpretations: (1) divine beings / angels who took human wives (followed by the LXX and most ancient Jewish sources); (2) nobles or rulers ("sons of the gods" = great men); (3) the line of Seth (godly men) marrying the line of Cain. Sarna himself does not resolve the debate but notes that the passage functions narratively to show the complete corruption of the created order before the flood.

6:8

Veynoach matsa chen beyne YHWH — "but Noah found favour in the eyes of the LORD." The Hebrew chen (favour, grace) appears here for the first time in the Bible. The wordplay on Noah's name (noach/chen) is deliberate. Sarna: this verse is the pivot of the entire pre-flood narrative — everything that precedes it establishes universal corruption; this single verse establishes the exception. The flood narrative begins and ends with divine favour toward Noah.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:1-4

The sons of God passage is deliberately mysterious. Alter: "the writer exploits the mythological background without quite endorsing it." The divine beings take human wives; gigantic offspring result; God limits human lifespan. The passage reads like a fragment of a larger myth deliberately compressed, its full meaning left opaque. Its function is to show the cosmic scope of the corruption: even the boundary between divine and human has been transgressed.

6:5-8

The divine soliloquy of vv.5-7 is among the most anthropomorphic passages in Genesis: "the LORD was grieved... his heart was deeply troubled." God regrets making humanity. Alter: "The writer is willing to attribute to God the full weight of disappointment and sorrow a parent might feel over children who have gone terribly wrong. The God of Genesis is not the unmoved mover of Greek philosophy but a God who can be pained by the creatures he loves."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1-4

Calvin reads the "sons of God" as the Sethite line — the children of the covenant — intermarrying with the Cainite line. The sin is the mixing of the holy and the profane: the people of God abandoning their distinctiveness by taking wives from those who did not know God. Calvin: "The narration of Moses was intended to show that at this time the world had become so corrupt that there was no longer any distinction between the holy seed and the reprobate." The boundary between covenant and world had collapsed.

6:6-7

God's repentance (wayyinnachem) is not a change in God's eternal purpose but an accommodation to human understanding. Calvin: "Repentance, attributed to God, does not properly belong to him, but has reference to our understanding of him." God is expressing, in terms comprehensible to human minds, the full weight of his response to human wickedness. The grief is real; the metaphysical form of expression is accommodated to human capacity.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:2

The NET note on "sons of God" (bene haElohim) gives the most extensive treatment of the three interpretive options. The note observes that the same phrase in Job 1:6, 2:1, and 38:7 unambiguously refers to divine/heavenly beings. The note concludes that the most natural reading of the phrase is heavenly beings, consistent with ancient Near Eastern usage, and that this reading best explains the narrative function (cosmic transgression before the flood).

6:8

The NET note on chen (favour/grace) marks this as the first occurrence of the grace word-group in the Bible. The note observes the passive form: Noah did not earn favour but found it — the initiative is entirely divine. This becomes the theological template for all subsequent divine-human relationship in Scripture: grace precedes and enables human response.

Verses 9–13 — Noah's Character; God's Verdict
910111213

Hebrew Word Study

צַדִּיק תָּמִים tsaddiq tamim — righteous and blameless: V.9: Tsaddiq = righteous (conforming to the covenant standard); tamim = whole, complete, unblemished (the same word used for unblemished sacrificial animals). Together they describe a man of integrity and moral wholeness. The qualification "in his generation" has been debated: does it mean he was righteous by comparison with a corrupt generation (only), or righteous in absolute terms during a corrupt generation (emphasis on context)?

חָמָס chamas — violence: V.11,13: The word encompasses all forms of violent injustice — murder, oppression, robbery, cruelty. The earth is described as "full of chamas" — the perfect inversion of 1:28's blessing to fill the earth. The earth has been filled, as commanded, but filled with violence rather than the image-bearing, God-honouring humanity that was intended.

Historical Context

Noah's characterisation as "righteous and blameless" sets him apart from the universal corruption of vv.5-7. The ark (tevah in Hebrew) is the same word used for the basket in which Moses was placed in the Nile (Exod 2:3,5) — a deliberate verbal connection between the two deliverers. Both Noah and Moses are placed in a tevah and carried through waters of judgment to a new beginning. The ark's dimensions (300 × 50 × 30 cubits, with a cubit approximately 18 inches) produce a vessel of approximately 450 × 75 × 45 feet — large enough to carry the stated cargo.

Context

Noah's righteousness is presented as the reason for his selection — but the text does not explain whether his righteousness preceded God's grace or was the product of it. The Reformed tradition reads "Noah found grace" (8) as primary: grace precedes righteousness. The Arminian tradition reads Noah's righteousness as the basis of selection. Both agree that Noah's character stands in absolute contrast to the corrupt generation around him — and that this contrast is what the narrator intends to establish.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 17:9“The heart is deceitful above all things” — the prophet echoes Genesis 6:5’s diagnosis of the human heart.
  • Eph 2:3–5“By nature deserving of wrath… but God, who is rich in mercy” — Paul’s great “but” structurally mirrors Gen 6:5–8.
  • Matt 24:37–39Jesus compares the days before his return to “the days of Noah” — life proceeding as normal until sudden judgment.
  • Heb 11:7“By faith Noah, when warned about things not yet seen, in holy fear built an ark” — his righteousness (Gen 6:9) was the fruit of faith.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:9

Noah is described as "righteous and blameless among the people of his time." The phrase "among the people of his time" does not limit his righteousness but contextualises it: he maintained integrity in the most corrupt generation in history. That he "walked with God" (the same phrase used of Enoch, 5:24) establishes him as a man of genuine covenant relationship, not merely moral respectability.

6:11-13

The earth is described as "corrupt" (shachat) and "full of violence" (chamas). These two words appear three times in four verses — the narrator is insisting on the totality of the corruption. God's assessment is not harsh; it is accurate. The flood is not arbitrary divine wrath but the appropriate response to a species that has comprehensively destroyed the good creation. The judge of all the earth will do right (18:25).

6:9

Tsaddiq tamim hayah bedorotav — "he was righteous and blameless in his generations." Sarna notes the plural "generations" (dorotav) — suggesting his righteousness was maintained across multiple generations, a sustained quality rather than a momentary achievement. The same combination of tsaddiq and tamim will be used of Job (Job 1:1) — the two great pre-Abrahamic figures of righteousness in the Hebrew Bible.

6:11-12

Vattishshachet haarets — "the earth was corrupt." The verb shachat (corrupt, destroy) appears here in the niphal (passive: was corrupted) and will reappear in v.13 in the piel (active: I will destroy it). The same verb describes both what humanity has done to the earth and what God will do to it. Sarna: "The punishment fits the crime with terrible precision: the earth that humanity has corrupted will be corrupted by God."

6:8

Chen — grace, favour. Alter notes this is the first occurrence of the word in the Bible and one of the most pivotal. In a passage of comprehensive condemnation, one brief sentence pivots the entire narrative: "but Noah found favour in the eyes of the LORD." The contrast between "all" (every inclination, all flesh, all the earth) and "one" (Noah) is the structural heart of the passage. Grace is always particular before it is universal.

6:12

Vayar Elohim et haarets — "God saw the earth." The divine seeing of 1:10,12,18,21,25,31 ("God saw that it was good") is now inverted: God sees that the earth is corrupt. The vocabulary of the creation account is being systematically reversed: the good creation seen by God has become a corrupt creation seen by God. The flood will be a de-creation followed by a re-creation.

6:9

Calvin on "Noah walked with God": "This is the highest praise which can be given to any man — that his whole life was ordered according to God's will. It does not mean that Noah was free from all sin, but that he sincerely endeavoured to serve God, and that he framed his life according to the rule of holiness." The righteousness of Noah, like all OT righteousness, was faith-based and imputed — he was counted righteous because he trusted God, not because he achieved perfection.

6:11-13

Calvin on the violence filling the earth: "When injustice, violence, and cruelty prevail in the world, the corruption of mankind is so great that God himself cannot bear to look upon it. He is not constrained by any external force but by his own nature, which cannot endure iniquity, to destroy what he had made." The flood is not divine temper but divine justice responding to comprehensive injustice.

6:9

The NET note on "blameless among the people of his time" discusses whether this relativises his righteousness (he was blameless only by comparison) or contextualises it (he maintained blamelessness despite the context). The note favours the contextual reading: the phrase emphasises the difficulty of his righteousness — he maintained it in the worst possible moral environment — rather than limiting it. The same phrase structure in Psalm 1 and Proverbs describes absolute, not relative, righteousness.

6:11

The NET note on chamas (violence) describes its semantic range: physical violence, wrongdoing, injustice, oppression. The word occurs 60 times in the OT and almost always describes unjust violence. The note observes that the earth being "full of chamas" directly inverts the blessing of Genesis 1:28 to fill the earth — the earth has been filled, but with the opposite of what was intended.

6:9

Tsaddiq tamim hayah bedorotav — "righteous, blameless in his generation." Sarna notes the two words: tsaddiq (righteous, in right covenant relationship) and tamim (blameless, whole, complete — the same word used for unblemished sacrificial animals). The phrase "in his generation" has been read both positively (he stood out in a corrupt age) and relatively (he was righteous by the low standard of his generation). The flood is the earth receiving back what it had put into itself.

6:9

The toledoth of Noah opens with a character note rare in biblical narrative: Noah is described before he acts. Alter: "The portrait of Noah in three terms — righteous, blameless, walking with God — is the Bible's most compressed characterisation of ideal human behaviour. Each term adds a dimension: tsaddiq (relational rectitude), tamim (inner wholeness), hithalech et haElohim (continuous fellowship with God)."

The repetition of "corrupt" and "violence" in vv.11-12 creates a dense verbal texture. Alter: "The earth was corrupt... God saw the earth was corrupt... the earth was full of violence." The three-fold statement functions as a formal legal charge — the prosecution's case against the world. The use of "God saw" echoes the creation narrative's "God saw that it was good" — now inverted: God sees that it is irredeemably bad.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:9

Noah walking with God echoes Enoch (5:22-24). Calvin: "The same form of expression is used concerning Enoch. By this mode of speaking, Moses intends to show that Noah regulated all his life and all his actions by the will of God." Walking with God is not occasional piety but the continuous orientation of a life — every step taken in reference to the divine presence and will.

6:12

The corruption of "all flesh" (kol basar) extends the diagnosis from humanity to the whole created order. Calvin: "The whole world was implicated in Adam's fault. For since the nature of man was corrupted, all his offspring partook of that corruption, and therefore the dominion of sin extended even over those creatures which should have been subject to man." The flood is not merely a moral judgment on human sin but a cosmic reset of a creation gone wrong.

The NET note on "blameless" (tamim) observes this does not mean sinless — the word means complete, whole, without defect. Verses 14–22 — Instructions for the Ark; Noah Obeys
141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

תֵּבָה tevah — ark / chest: V.14: The tevah is not a boat in the conventional sense — it has no rudder, no sail, no means of propulsion or steering. It is a floating container, designed to survive, not to navigate. The word appears only twice in the OT: here (the ark of Noah) and in Exodus 2:3,5 (the basket of Moses). Both are floating vessels carrying a deliverer through waters of judgment. The connection is deliberate.

בְּרִית berit — covenant: V.18: The first occurrence of "covenant" (berit) in the Bible. God establishes a covenant with Noah before the flood — not after it. The covenant is announced as the framework within which the flood occurs: Noah is not merely a survivor but the recipient of God's first formal covenant. The pattern of covenant-before-judgment sets the template for all subsequent covenant theology.

Historical Context

The ark's dimensions (300 × 50 × 30 cubits) give it a displacement tonnage and volume sufficient to carry the stated cargo — approximately 1.5 million cubic feet. Naval architects have noted that the 6:1 length-to-width ratio is optimal for stability in rough seas. Whether read literally or theologically, the ark's design reflects practical maritime wisdom. God provides blueprints for the salvation he commands.

Context

God's detailed instructions for the ark are a theological statement: salvation is God's initiative, God's design, and God's provision. Noah does not design the ark; he builds what God specifies. The covenant announced in v.18 frames the entire construction project: the ark is not merely a survival vehicle but the physical embodiment of God's covenant faithfulness. What God covenants, he provides the means to fulfill.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:14-16

The detailed specifications for the ark reveal something important about God's provision: when God calls to a task, he equips for it. Noah is not left to improvise a survival vessel; God provides the blueprints. The ark is God's design from beginning to end. The window (tsohar), the door, the three decks — each element is specified. This is the pattern of all divine provision: the God who calls is the God who equips.

6:18

The first use of "covenant" (berit) in Scripture occurs here, before the flood, in a context of judgment. The covenant is God's commitment to bring Noah through the judgment — not merely to preserve him from it. The covenant is the ground of the ark: Noah builds because God has covenanted. Faith acts on the basis of the divine word, not on the basis of seen evidence. "Things not yet seen" (Heb 11:7) — Noah had never seen a flood.

6:22

"Noah did everything just as God commanded him." This simple statement is among the most theologically significant in Genesis: in a world where "every inclination was only evil all the time" (6:5), one man did everything God commanded. Comprehensive obedience as the response to comprehensive grace. The contrast between the world's comprehensive disobedience and Noah's comprehensive obedience is the chapter's final word.

6:14

Tsohar — the window or opening in the ark — is a hapax legomenon (appears only here in the OT). Its precise meaning is uncertain: some translate "roof," others "window," others "skylight." The word may be related to tsohorayim (noon), suggesting an opening that admits light. Whatever its precise form, it represents divine provision of light within the ark — a small but significant detail of care.

6:18

Vahaqimoti et beriti ittach — "and I will establish my covenant with you." This is the first occurrence of berit (covenant) in the Bible. Sarna notes that the covenant is announced here before the flood and ratified after it (9:8-17) — the entire flood narrative is bracketed by covenant. God's judgment occurs within the framework of his covenant commitment, not outside it. The flood is not the abandonment of creation but its renewal within a covenant framework.

6:14-22

The construction instructions read like a building specification — precise measurements, materials, design features. Alter: "The detailed specification serves a narrative purpose: it establishes Noah as a man of meticulous, sustained obedience. To build an ark of these dimensions in a world that had never seen rain (2:5) required years of labour and public ridicule. The instructions are the measure of the faith required to carry them out."

6:22

"Noah did everything just as God commanded him." The final verse of the chapter echoes the creation account's refrain: "And it was so" (wayyehi ken, repeated in Genesis 1). What God commands happens — in creation through divine speech, in the ark through human obedience. Noah's "so he did" (ken asah) mirrors the "and it was so" of creation: the faithful creature enacts the divine word.

6:14

The detailed ark specifications show that God condescends to human weakness by providing practical means for his commands. Calvin: "God, in condescending to the capacity of men, prescribes the form of the ark, lest Noah, in his perplexity, might object that the thing was impossible. For it would have been absurd to burden him with so great a work without any definite plan." The God who commands also equips — a principle Calvin applies throughout his theology.

6:18

Calvin on the first covenant: "God now, for the first time, uses the word covenant, to teach Noah that there is a mutual obligation between them — God will be faithful, and Noah must trust him. The covenant includes both promise and demand. God promises to preserve Noah through the flood; Noah is bound to obey God's commands." The covenant is not merely a divine promise but a bilateral relationship of faithfulness.

6:14

The NET note on tevah (ark) discusses the word's restricted usage (only here and Exod 2) and its distinction from the more common word for ship (oniyah, sefinah). The note observes that the tevah is a floating container, not a navigable vessel — designed for passive survival, not active sailing. This distinguishes the Genesis ark from the Mesopotamian flood hero's boats (Utnapishtim's ark in Gilgamesh is described as having a pilot).

6:18

The NET note on berit (covenant) marks this as the covenant's first occurrence and notes that the verb used (haqim = to establish, confirm) suggests this covenant had antecedents — God is establishing/confirming a pre-existing relationship rather than creating an entirely new one. The note connects this to the Noahic covenant of chapter 9, where the same verb is used (9:9,11,17).

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:14

Teva — ark. The word teva appears only twice in the Hebrew Bible: here (Noah's ark) and in Exodus 2:3-5 (Moses's basket in the Nile). Both are vessels of salvation for those through whom God will preserve his redemptive purposes. Sarna notes the deliberate echo: as Moses was drawn out of the water in a teva, so Noah was preserved through the water in a teva. Both are types of the same divine preservation.

The specifications for the ark are given with unusual technical detail: wood type, pitch coating, dimensions (300 x 50 x 30 cubits), rooms, roof, door, three decks. Alter notes the contrast with the creation narrative: there God speaks and things appear; here God speaks and precise technical instructions follow. The ark is not miraculous but constructed — human labour in response to divine command. The detailed instructions signal the importance of exact obedience.

6:22

Vayya'as Noach kechol asher tsivvah oto Elohim — "and Noah did all that God commanded him." Seven Hebrew words that summarise perfect obedience. Alter: the closing statement is the structural complement to 6:5 ("every inclination of the thoughts of the human heart was only evil all the time"). Against total human corruption, Noah's total obedience. The contrast defines the theological stakes of the flood.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:14-16

Calvin on the ark dimensions: the construction of the ark was itself an act of faith and a public testimony. For one hundred years (by some calculations) Noah built the ark in plain sight of his contemporaries — a standing rebuke to their unbelief and a public declaration of his own. Calvin: "The building of the ark was to Noah a kind of visible sermon, by which he proclaimed the approaching flood to his generation."

6:18

The covenant announced here is the first covenant in Scripture — preceding Sinai, preceding Abraham. Calvin: God's covenant with Noah is the basis for all subsequent covenant theology. The covenant form — promise of preservation, requirement of obedience — establishes the pattern that Abraham, Moses, David, and ultimately Christ will fulfil. The flood narrative is not merely a story about water; it is the first act of covenant history.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:14

The NET note on gopher wood (ets gopher) acknowledges that the species is unknown — this is the only occurrence of the word in the Bible, and no ancient source identifies it with certainty. The note surveys the main suggestions (cypress, cedar, laminated wood) without resolving the question. The note on "rooms" (qinnim, literally "nests") is also discussed — the domestic connotation of the word suggesting compartments rather than merely structural cells.

6:18

The NET note on berith (covenant) marks this as the word's first occurrence in the Bible. The note discusses the form of the covenant here: it is unilateral (God commits to preserve Noah), not bilateral (Noah is commanded to obey but the covenant itself is a divine promise). This places the Noahic covenant in the category of "grant covenants" (like the Abrahamic and Davidic) rather than "suzerainty covenants" (like the Mosaic).

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Noah
Righteous and blameless; builder of the ark
In a world of comprehensive corruption, Noah alone is characterised as righteous, blameless, and walking with God — the same characterisation as Enoch (5:24) but in a context of universal evil. He receives the first formal covenant in Scripture (6:18) and becomes the new Adam of the post-flood world. The NT treats him as the paradigm of faith-based obedience (Heb 11:7), individual righteousness in a corrupt world (Ezek 14:14), and as the type of final judgment and salvation (Matt 24:37-38; 1 Pet 3:20).
The Nephilim
Heroes of old; men of renown
The most mysterious figures in Genesis. Their identity is disputed (offspring of angelic-human unions, or a pre-existing group of warriors?), but their role in the narrative is clear: they represent the apex of pre-flood human power and glory — the world at its most impressive, at the moment of God's decision to destroy it. Power and glory without righteousness lead to the flood. They reappear in Numbers 13:33 as the giants of Canaan.

Ancient Sources

The Atrahasis Epic (Babylon, c.1700 BCE)
The Atrahasis Epic describes gods who create humanity to do their labour, then become disturbed by humanity's overpopulation and noise. They send plagues, famines, and finally a flood to reduce human population. A warning is given to one righteous man (Atrahasis) who builds a boat and survives.
The structural parallels — divine disturbance, flood decision, warning to one man, survival by boat — show that Genesis and the Mesopotamian traditions share a common cultural memory of a catastrophic flood. The theological differences are absolute: the Mesopotamian gods are irritated by noise; the God of Genesis is grieved by moral corruption. The Mesopotamian flood is arbitrary; the Genesis flood is moral judgment.
The Epic of Gilgamesh, Tablet XI (Babylon, c.1200 BCE)
The flood account in Gilgamesh presents Utnapishtim (the Babylonian Noah) who is warned by the god Ea, builds a great boat, takes animals aboard, and survives a flood of seven days. After the flood, he offers a sacrifice; the gods smell it and gather "like flies." He is granted immortality.
The Gilgamesh parallel is the closest to Genesis of all the ANE flood texts — some details are remarkably similar (the sending of birds to test receding waters). The key theological inversion: the Babylonian flood is arbitrary (Enlil, who caused it, is even rebuked by Ea afterwards); the Genesis flood is just. The Babylonian gods are unreliable and divided; the God of Genesis acts with purpose and covenant faithfulness.

Reception History

Origen, Homilies on Genesis (c.245 AD)
Origen reads the ark allegorically as the church: "The ark is the church, Noah is Christ. As Noah saved those in the ark, so Christ saves those in the church. The dimensions of the ark correspond to the body of Christ." The allegorical tradition of reading the ark as a type of the church runs from Origen through Augustine and into the medieval period.
Augustine, The City of God, Book XV (c.413 AD)
Augustine reads the flood as the type of the final judgment: the flood destroyed the city of man (the corrupt world) while preserving the city of God (Noah's family). He also reads the ark's dimensions as mystically significant: the 300-cubit length corresponds to the Greek letter chi (Christ's cross), and the 6:1 ratio relates to the human body. Augustine establishes the exegetical tradition of reading the ark's measurements christologically.
Peter Lombard, Sentences (c.1150 AD)
The Sentences became the standard theological textbook of the medieval university, and its treatment of Genesis 6 established the scholastic discussion of whether the flood was global or regional, whether the ark was sufficient to hold all species, and how the bene Elohim should be interpreted. These questions, first systematically addressed by Lombard's students (Aquinas, Bonaventure), continue to be debated.

Literary Structure

Sons of God Prologue — 6:1-4Cosmic transgression: divine-human boundary violated. The world's corruption reaches its maximum before the flood.
"Every inclination of the thoughts of the human heart was only evil all the time." The most comprehensive indictment in the Bible. God grieves.
Noah Exception — 6:8But Noah found favour (chen — first grace-word in the Bible). Single verse pivot against the universal indictment.
Noah's Character — 6:9Righteous, blameless, walking with God. Three terms; character before action.
Flood Commission — 6:13-22God announces judgment, gives ark specifications, announces covenant (berith — first in the Bible), commands total obedience.
Total Obedience — 6:22"Noah did all that God commanded him." Seven words against 6:5's indictment.
Genesis 6 is the hinge between the primeval history and the flood narrative. Its theological architecture is precise: universal human corruption (vv.1-7), one exception (v.8), the character of that exception (vv.9-13), and the commission of the instrument of salvation (vv.14-22). The first grace-word in the Bible appears here (chen, v.8), and the first covenant-word (berith, v.18). The flood story is simultaneously a story of judgment and a story of grace.

Intertextual Threading

Gen 6:5→Rom 3:10-12Echo
"There is no one righteous, not even one" — Paul's description of universal human sin in Rom 3 reads Gen 6:5 as the permanent human condition, not merely a pre-flood reality.
Gen 6:8→Eph 2:8-9Echo
Grace as the basis of salvation, not human merit — Noah finding grace anticipates the NT's salvation-by-grace-through-faith structure.
Gen 6:18→Gen 9:9-17Fulfilment
The covenant announced in 6:18 is ratified and detailed in 9:9-17 — the rainbow covenant. The structure is promise before fulfillment.
Gen 6:14→Exod 2:3Echo
The tevah (ark) of Noah and the tevah (basket) of Moses — both floating containers carry a deliverer through waters of judgment to a new beginning.

Textual Notes

6:2,4bene elohim — sons of God: divine beings or human rulers?
MT bene ha-elohim (with definite article). In Job 1:6; 2:1; 38:7 and Ps 29:1, bene elohim/bene elim clearly refers to members of the divine court/heavenly beings. LXX translates "angels of God" in some MSS, "sons of God" in others. The Sethite reading requires "sons of God" = "sons of the godly line" — which has no support in the text itself.
Critical for understanding the cause of the flood, the nature of the Nephilim, and the background of Jude 6 and 2 Pet 2:4 which allude to angelic sin.
6:3My Spirit shall not abide/strive in man
MT lo-yadon ruachi ba-adam — the verb yadon (or yadun) is a hapax legomenon of uncertain meaning. Proposals: "abide/remain" (most translations), "contend/strive" (KJV), "rule." The 120-year limit: 120 years until the flood, or 120 years as the new human lifespan?
The meaning affects the nature of divine patience before judgment and the significance of the 120-year figure. Most modern scholars read it as a 120-year period before the flood.
6:6YHWH was grieved/repented
MT wayyinnachem YHWH (Niphal of nacham) — translated variously as "was sorry" (KJV, ESV), "regretted" (NIV, NET), "repented" (older translations), "was grieved" (NRSV). The word connotes emotional distress leading to changed course. Num 23:19 says God does not repent/change his mind; 1 Sam 15:11,29 uses the same verb of God in tension with Num 23:19.
The divine "repentance" or "grief" raises the theological question of divine impassibility and the compatibility of divine emotion with divine immutability. The tension is real and intentional: God is genuinely affected by human evil without being mutable in his essential nature.

Scholarly Debates

The Flood: Global or Regional?
Global
Whitcomb, Morris, Wenham, Mathews
The text uses universal language (all flesh, all the earth, under the heavens) without qualification. The NT references (Matt 24:38-39; 2 Pet 2:5; 3:6) treat the flood as a global event. The theological purpose — the judgment of all humanity — requires universal scope.
Regional/Mesopotamian
Millard, Kidner, Walton, Longman
The universal language reflects the author's perspective (the known world, not the entire globe) and the covenant community (all people descended from Adam in the Mesopotamian heartland). Archaeological evidence supports a massive regional flood in Mesopotamia (Woolley's excavations at Ur); the text can be read with this scope without linguistic violence.
Tranquil Flood
Some literary scholars
The flood is primarily a theological statement about divine judgment and covenant — its precise historical or geographical scope is beside the narrative point.
The global reading is the more natural reading of the text's universal language and the NT's usage. The regional reading attempts to harmonise with geology and archaeology. The debate remains significant for inerrancy discussions.
The Nephilim: Giants, Heroes, or Fallen Beings?
Giants / mighty warriors
Most modern translations and commentators
Nephilim = "fallen ones" or giants (cf. Num 13:33 where the Nephilim are described as making the Israelites feel like grasshoppers). They are the offspring of the sons-of-God union.
Fallen angels or their offspring
1 Enoch, Book of Jubilees, some Church Fathers
The Nephilim are the Gibborim (mighty men) produced by the union of divine beings and human women — a hybrid race whose violence precipitates the flood.
Heroic/legendary figures
Sarna, Westermann
The Nephilim and Gibborim are the legendary heroes of antiquity — the figures whom later cultures celebrated as demigods. Genesis domesticates them as the products of a transgressive pre-flood era.
The "mighty warriors/heroes" reading is most supported contextually. The offspring of illicit unions between divine beings and humans are the legendary heroes of ancient memory — presented here in their proper context as products of the pre-flood transgression.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 6 is the darkest chapter of the primeval history and simultaneously the beginning of the Bible's most fundamental theological structure: covenant. In the midst of universal corruption and divine grief, God establishes a covenant with a single righteous man and provides the means of salvation. The chapter introduces the key theological concepts of grace (chen, v.8), covenant (berit, v.18), and comprehensive obedience (ken asah, v.22) that will shape the entire subsequent biblical narrative.
