Genesis 26

Isaac in Gerar: the Covenant Continued

Verses 1–11 — Isaac in Gerar; the Covenant Reaffirmed
1234567891011

Places

Gerar / Philistine territory
Coastal plain of Canaan
Isaac goes to Gerar — the same Philistine city where Abraham used the wife-sister deception. The covenant pattern recurs in the same place. Abimelech's name suggests either the same king as Abraham's encounter or a dynastic title.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
26:2-5

MacArthur: God appears to Isaac in Gerar and commands him to stay -- not to go to Egypt as Abraham had done. The Abrahamic covenant is formally reaffirmed to Isaac for the first time: the same land, seed, and blessing promises. The explicit ground: 'because Abraham obeyed me and did everything I required' (v.5). Parental covenant faithfulness benefits subsequent generations.

26:7-9

MacArthur: Isaac's wife-sister deception in Gerar is a near-verbatim repetition of Abraham's deception -- the same location, the same king, the same failure. The sin pattern is transmitted generationally. Abimelech's rebuke (v.9-10) is essentially the same as his predecessor's of Abraham. The covenant family's recurring failure requires recurring divine intervention.

Hebrew Word Study

yārad / ʿālāh ya-RAD / a-LAH — go down / go up: God's instruction to Isaac — "Do not go down to Egypt; live in the land where I tell you to stay" (v.2) — uses the directional verbs that pattern the patriarchal narrative. Egypt is always "down" (moral and physical descent); Canaan is always "up" (covenant ascent). The prohibition on going to Egypt prevents Isaac from repeating Abraham's Egypt-sojourn and keeps him within the covenant land through the famine. The covenant line is tested in the land, not protected from it by going elsewhere.

ḥāyāh kha-YAH — to live: "Stay in this land for a while, and I will be with you (ʾehyeh ʿimmĕkā) and will bless you" (v.3) — the divine promise ʾehyeh ʿimmĕkā ("I will be/I am with you") is the covenantal presence-formula that sustains the patriarchs and ultimately becomes the divine name revealed at Sinai (Exod 3:14: "I AM who I AM" — ʾehyeh ʾăšer ʾehyeh). The promise of divine presence is the content of the covenant blessing — what makes "I will bless you" real is "I will be with you."

ʿāqab a-KAV — because / on account of: "I will make your descendants as numerous as the stars in the sky... because (ʿēqeb ʾăšer) Abraham obeyed me and kept my requirements" (v.4-5). The ʿēqeb ("because/in the consequence of") grounds Isaac's covenant blessing in Abraham's prior obedience — the covenant passes through Abraham's faithfulness to his descendants. This is not merit-theology but covenantal solidarity: the covenant community shares in the consequences of its representative's obedience, just as it will share in the consequences of Adam's disobedience (Rom 5:12-21).

Historical Context

None

Context

The covenant renewal to Isaac is structurally parallel to the original call of Abraham — famine triggers migration, God intervenes with the promise, the patriarch obeys. The repetition is not redundancy but theological insistence: the covenant is not inherited automatically. Each generation must receive it afresh. God appears to Isaac directly, confirming that the relationship is personal, not merely dynastic.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 12:1–3The original covenant to Abraham — Isaac now receives the same three elements: land, offspring, and universal blessing.
  • Gen 22:18The blessing through the offspring "because you obeyed" — now explicitly cited as the reason Isaac inherits the same promise.
  • Heb 11:20Isaac "by faith blessed Jacob and Esau" — his role in the covenant story is that of the middle link in the chain of promise.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
26:1-5

God appears to Isaac during a famine and renews the Abrahamic covenant explicitly. Sarna: "This is the only extended divine speech addressed to Isaac in Genesis. The promise is articulated in the same terms as the Abraham covenant (land, descendants, universal blessing) and is grounded explicitly in Abraham's obedience — 'because Abraham obeyed me and kept my requirements.' The covenant passes through obedience, not mere biological descent."

26:7-11

The third wife-sister episode — Isaac claiming Rebekah as his sister — is a repetition of Abraham's pattern. Sarna: "The repetition is both realistic (the strategy worked before; why not again?) and theological: the covenant heir inherits not only the promises but the failures. The pattern of human compromise within divine covenant faithfulness characterises the entire patriarchal narrative."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
26:1

A famine in the land. The same opening phrase appeared in 12:10, sending Abraham to Egypt. Alter: "The famine tests whether Isaac will repeat his father's pattern of seeking human solutions (Egypt) or trust the divine word. God intervenes before Isaac departs, directing him to stay in Canaan. The test Isaac passes by obedience is the test Abraham failed — or, rather, the test God prevents Isaac from even reaching."

26:2-5

The covenant renewal formula is repeated almost verbatim from the Abraham episodes. Alter notes the intentional repetition: Isaac is not receiving a new covenant but the continuation of his father's covenant.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
26:2-5

God appears to forbid the Egyptian escape. Calvin: "God permits no occasion to the faithful of forgetting his promises. Isaac was about to go down to Egypt; God says 'stay in this land, I will be with you.' The promise is renewed precisely at the point of temptation to abandon it. This is the divine pastoral care — not allowing his people to stray far before calling them back."

26:4-5

Calvin: The covenant rested on free grace, not Abraham's obedience. But faithful obedience is the means by which the covenant's benefits are actually received in history. The promise is unconditional; the enjoyment of it is shaped by faithfulness.

The note observes that the covenant was given unconditionally (15:17-18) but that Abraham's obedience is cited here as the occasion for its renewal to Isaac — consistent with the pattern in Genesis 22 where the Akedah triggers the divine oath.

Verses 12–22 — Isaac Prospers; the Wells of Contention
1213141516171819202122

Places

Wells of the Negev / Beersheba valley
Southern Canaan
Esek (contention), Sitnah (opposition), Rehoboth (room) — the well names trace Isaac's progress from conflict to space. The final well is undisputed. Each name preserves the history of the encounter: the geography of the Negev becomes a record of divine provision through conflict.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
26:17-22

MacArthur: The wells of Esek (contention), Sitnah (opposition), and Rehoboth (room) trace Isaac's response to conflict: he re-digs, encounters opposition, moves on, and eventually finds a place where there is room. The progressive withdrawal is not weakness but wisdom -- and God opens the space that fighting could not have secured.

26:22

MacArthur: 'Now the LORD has given us room and we will flourish in the land' -- Isaac interprets the opened well as covenant provision. The name Rehoboth (broad places/room) is Isaac's theological commentary on the event. God's method of blessing is to open space, not to win contests. The covenant community's posture is to receive what God opens, not to seize what opposition withholds.

Hebrew Word Study

meʾāh šĕʿārîm me-AH she-a-REEM — a hundredfold: "Isaac planted crops in that land and the same year reaped a hundredfold (meʾāh šĕʿārîm), because the LORD blessed him" (v.12). The hundredfold yield is extraordinary — typical yields in Palestinian agriculture were tenfold at best. The surplus is the visible, material form of covenant blessing: the prosperity is so unmistakable that "the Philistines envied him" (v.14) and Abimelech requests that he leave "because you have become too powerful for us." Covenant faithfulness produces a flourishing that the surrounding world cannot ignore.

bĕʾēr / māyim be-AYR / MA-yim — well / water: The well-disputes of vv.19-22 (Esek — quarrel; Sitnah — enmity; Rehoboth — broad places) trace Isaac's progressive movement away from Philistine territory toward the space where God will reaffirm the covenant (v.24). Water in a semi-arid land is life itself — the wells are not merely economic assets but covenant survival markers. Each well dispute is a choice between standing one's ground and moving on; Isaac consistently moves on, and God responds with the covenant reaffirmation he needs.

rāḥab ra-KHAV — wide / spacious / broad: "He named it Rehoboth, saying, 'Now the LORD has given us room (rāḥab) and we will flourish in the land'" (v.22). The name Rehoboth (from rāḥab — spacious) expresses the covenant theological principle: God makes space for his people when they respond to opposition with trust rather than resistance. The same root describes the covenant promise in Exod 3:8 — a land "large (rĕḥābāh) and good." The covenant promises spaciousness as the fruit of patient faith.

Historical Context

None

Context

Isaac's wife-as-sister deception is the third iteration of this pattern in Genesis (cf. 12:10–20; 20:1–18). The deliberate repetition invites comparison: Abraham lied twice; now Isaac lies once. The verb used for Isaac and Rebekah — "laughing" (meṣaḥēq) — is the piel of the same root as Isaac's name. The very name "Laughter" catches the patriarch in his lie. Abimelech again proves more righteous than the covenant man.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 12:10–20; 20:1–18The two Abrahamic precedents. The pattern's third occurrence suggests a structural theme: the covenant patriarch's fear vs. trust in God's protection.
  • Rom 8:31The theological answer to the patriarch's recurring fear: "If God is for us, who can be against us?" — the promise of divine protection that the patriarchs grasped imperfectly.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
26:12-16

Isaac's agricultural success — "a hundredfold" in the same year — is a direct fulfilment of the covenant blessing. Sarna: "The Philistine envious response — 'you have become too powerful for us' — is the first instance in Isaac's narrative of the blessing creating hostility. The same pattern will appear in Egypt (Exod 1:9). The covenant people's prosperity provokes the envy of their neighbours, a recurring theme in the patriarchal and Exodus narratives."

26:18-22

Isaac re-digs Abraham's wells and names the new wells Esek (contention), Sitnah (opposition), and Rehoboth (room). Sarna: "The well-digging narrative is more than a pastoral dispute — it is a progressive claim on Canaan. Each well is an act of habitation; the contentions are the resistance of the current occupants; the final name Rehoboth ('room') marks the moment when the land begins to yield to the covenant people."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
26:20-22

The progression of well names — contention, opposition, room — traces a narrative arc of gradual establishment. Alter: "Isaac does not fight for the first two wells; he simply moves away and digs again. The patience is not cowardice but the posture of a man whose security does not depend on any particular piece of land but on the divine promise that undergirds his presence in Canaan."

26:14-18

The well-dispute sequence traces Isaac's movement from conflict toward the open land God provides. Alter notes each well's name is a station on a journey from human hostility to divine provision.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
26:12-14

The hundredfold yield is a divine sign of covenant faithfulness even in the land of the Philistines. Calvin: "God blessed Isaac not according to his desert but according to his covenant with Abraham. The blessing came even in a foreign country, under the suspicion and envy of strangers — to show that the promise of God is not confined to any particular location."

26:14-17

Calvin: Isaac trusted God to give him room rather than seizing it by force. The man who fights for space often loses it; the man who waits for God to provide it receives more than he fought for.

Verses 23–35 — The Lord Appears at Beersheba; Treaty with Abimelek
23242526272829303132333435

Places

Beersheba
Southern anchor of the Promised Land
The LORD appears to Isaac at Beersheba — the same site where Abraham made his treaty. Isaac builds an altar here and digs a well. Beersheba becomes the double-covenant site: Abraham's treaty and now Isaac's theophany both occur here. The city will later mark the southern boundary of Israel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
26:24-25

MacArthur: The LORD appeared to Isaac at Beersheba and reaffirmed the Abrahamic covenant: 'I am the God of your father Abraham.' The divine self-identification grounds the current promise in the prior covenant. Isaac built an altar and called on the name of the LORD -- worship precedes all other activities at the significant covenant sites.

26:28-29

MacArthur: Abimelech's treaty proposal acknowledges what the pagan king can observe from outside the covenant: 'We saw clearly that the LORD was with you.' The covenant presence of God with Isaac is perceptible to those who watch from outside. The visible blessing of the covenant community is a witness to the nations -- the fulfilment of the 'blessing to all peoples' promise has this dimension.

Hebrew Word Study

bārak bārak ba-RAKH ba-RAKH — to bless repeatedly: The divine reaffirmation at Beersheba — "I am the God of your father Abraham. Do not be afraid, for I am with you; I will bless you and will increase the number of your descendants" (v.24) — uses the standard covenant-renewal formula. The divine self-identification (ʾĕlōhê ʾābîkā ʾabrāhām) grounds the present promise in the past covenant. Isaac is not receiving a new promise but the continuation of the one already given to Abraham. The covenant is not re-negotiated at each generation but renewed and amplified.

šālôm sha-LOM — peace / wholeness / well-being: Abimelech's treaty request — "Let there be a sworn agreement between us... a treaty of peace (ûniktĕrāh berît ʿimmāk)" (v.28) — recognises that Isaac's prosperity is covenant-sourced: "We saw clearly that the LORD was with you" (v.28). The non-covenant king seeks peace (šālôm — covenant wholeness) with the one blessed by God. The covenant blessing generates a šālôm that extends beyond Israel's borders — the fulfilment beginning of the universal blessing promised in 12:3.

ʾālaṣ/kāʿas a-LATS / ka-AS — grief / bitterness: Esau's marriages to Hittite women "were a source of grief (mōrat rûaḥ — bitterness of spirit) to Isaac and Rebekah" (v.35). The term mōrat rûaḥ ("bitterness of spirit") uses the same root as Marah (bitter water) and Naomi's "call me Māraʾ." The Hittite wives represent Esau's practical disregard for the covenant line's endogamy — the requirement that covenant descendants marry within the family rather than among the Canaanites. It is this grief that drives Rebekah's plan for Jacob in ch.27.

Historical Context

None

Context

Isaac re-digging Abraham's wells is an act of both practical necessity and theological continuity. The wells are covenant markers — stopping them was an act of erasure. Isaac's restoration of the wells, using Abraham's original names, is a claim of covenant continuity. Each well dispute (Esek = contention, Sitnah = accusation, Rehoboth = room) traces a spiritual geography of persecution → persistence → provision.

    Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    Isaac
    Covenant's second link; passive but faithful
    The only patriarch with no major moral failing — his deception is the least harmful of the three sister-wife episodes, and he is caught rather than confronted. His re-digging of Abraham's wells and his Beersheba altar show genuine covenant continuity.
    Rebekah
    Matriarch; largely silent in this chapter
    She is the object of the deception and the cause of its unraveling — "Isaac laughing with Rebekah" (v.8). Her character will emerge forcefully in the next chapter.
    Abimelech
    King of Gerar; recurring Philistine figure
    Whether this is the same Abimelech as in ch.20–21 is debated — the name may be a royal title rather than a personal name. His moral clarity again exceeds the covenant man's. His confession ("the LORD has been with you") is the chapter's theological verdict.

    Ancient Sources

    Abimelech as throne title
    Semitic scholars note that "Abimelech" (ʾāḇî + melek = "my father is king") functions as a Philistine royal title throughout Genesis, rather than a personal name. This explains why the same name appears across multiple generations. Similar ANE examples: "Pharaoh" as a title, not a personal name.

    Reception History

    Calvin, Commentary on Genesis
    Calvin used Isaac's hundredfold harvest as a key proof text for divine providence in economic life: God blesses the labor of the faithful, not because of merit but as covenant faithfulness made visible. He warned against the opposite error of attributing all prosperity to skill.
    The Isaac Paradox
    Commentators from Chrysostom onward have noted Isaac's peculiar role: he is the least developed patriarch, yet the most essential link. Without Isaac, the Abraham → Jacob chain breaks. His very passivity is a theological statement: the covenant's survival does not depend on heroic individuals.

    Literary Structure

    The Covenant Renewed: Obedience and Transmission26:3-5God appears to Isaac in Gerar. The Abrahamic covenant is explicitly transmitted because Abraham obeyed -- the obedience is retrospective (Abraham's) and the blessing is prospective (Isaac's).
    Well Names: Conflict to Space26:20-22Esek (contention), Sitnah (opposition), Rehoboth (room): the progression from dispute to withdrawal to the space God provides is a character arc in three place names.

    Scholarly Debates

    Isaac: Character or Cipher?
    Passive/secondary figure
    Alter, von Rad
    Isaac is the most passive of the patriarchs — he is nearly sacrificed, his wife is chosen for him, his sons deceive him, he is never called by God to go anywhere. He inherits and passes on the covenant but rarely acts.
    Sustained covenant faithfulness
    Wenham, Hamilton
    Isaac's passivity may reflect faithfulness: he stays where God sends him (26:2-3), he digs wells, he makes peace. His lack of dramatic narrative reflects steady covenant obedience rather than the crises of Abraham and Jacob.
    Isaac's narrative function is clearly transitional — he bridges Abraham and Jacob. But the theological point of his passivity may be deliberate: the covenant does not depend on dramatic heroism but on quiet obedience. The same well-digging that seems undramatic is the covenant presence extending into Philistine territory.
