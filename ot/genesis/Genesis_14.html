Genesis 14

The Battle of the Kings; Melchizedek

Verses 1–12 — The War of the Kings; Lot Captured
123456789101112

Places

Five Cities of the Plain
Southern Dead Sea basin
Sodom, Gomorrah, Admah, Zeboyim, and Zoar are the five city-states whose king coalition is defeated by the four kings of Mesopotamia and Elam. The geography is entirely the Dead Sea region — a self-contained geopolitical world that will be destroyed in ch.19.
Battle Route
From Mesopotamia through Transjordan to the Negev
The four kings march via the King's Highway down the eastern side of Transjordan, sweeping through Ashteroth Karnaim (Bashan), Ham, Shaveh Kiriathaim, and the hill country of Seir to El Paran by the desert. The route traces the entire length of the Levant.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase

MacArthur: The Battle of the Nine Kings is the most politically complex episode in the Abraham narrative. The four kings from the East defeat the five kings of the cities of the plain, taking Lot captive. The historical specificity -- named kings, identified territories -- grounds the account in real geopolitical events.

14:12

MacArthur: 'They also carried off Abram's nephew Lot' -- the capture of Lot is the occasion for Abraham's military action. The covenant man who showed generosity to Lot now shows loyalty. Covenant loyalty and covenant generosity are the same character expressed in different circumstances.

Hebrew Word Study

melekhîm me-la-KHEEM — kings: The "war of the kings" (vv.1-11) introduces Abram's world as geopolitically complex — four eastern kings against five kings of the Jordan valley. The political background is historically plausible for the Middle Bronze Age. More important theologically is what the narrative establishes: Abram inhabits a real world of power-politics, military conflict, and captivity — yet the covenant promise operates within and through this world, not in isolation from it.

šābāh sha-VAH — to take captive: "They also carried off Abram's nephew Lot and his possessions, since he was living in Sodom" (v.12). Lot's capture (šābāh) is the consequence of his choice of the Jordan plain in ch.13. The same verb will describe Israel's captivity (Deut 21:10; 2 Kgs 24:14). Abram's rescue of Lot thus foreshadows on a personal scale what God will do for his people Israel when they are taken captive — the kinsman-redeemer motif operating at national scale.

ʾānōkî ḥānîtî a-no-KHEE kha-NEE-tee — escaped and told: "A man who had escaped came and reported this to Abram the Hebrew" (v.13) — the term hāʿibrî ("the Hebrew") appears here for the first time, linking Abram to the ʿEber genealogy of ch.11 and establishing his ethnic-social designation. The fugitive's report triggers Abram's military response — 318 trained men from his own household. Abram is not merely a seminomadic patriarch but the leader of a substantial household capable of military action.

Historical Context

The four-against-five-kings war is set in an era of Mesopotamian hegemony over Canaan, consistent with historical patterns in the early 2nd millennium BC. Chedorlaomer of Elam is particularly notable — Elamite power extended westward during the Isin-Larsa period. The cities of the plain (Sodom, Gomorrah, Admah, Zeboiim, Zoar) are also listed in Deuteronomy 29:23, confirming an established tradition.

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    14:1-12

    The battle of the four kings against five is the most politically complex narrative in the Abraham story. Sarna identifies the historical background: the kings of Mesopotamia (east) versus the kings of the Jordan valley (west). The names of the eastern kings have been the subject of extensive historical research — none have been definitively identified with known ancient Near Eastern rulers. Sarna: "The narrative has the character of historical memory, though direct archaeological confirmation has not been found."

    14:12

    Lot is captured by the eastern coalition — "he was living in Sodom." The narrative quietly notes Lot's location without comment. The choice of 13:10-13 has placed Lot exactly where he will be at risk. The consequences of sight-based choosing continue to unfold.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    14:1-9

    The battle narrative in chapter 14 is unique in the Abraham stories — no other passage gives Abraham a military role or sets his story in a geopolitical context. Alter: "The chapter seems to come from a different narrative tradition — its archaic language, its list of kings, its formal description of battle lines, all suggest an ancient source. Whatever its origin, it serves here to show Abram in the broadest possible context: not just a wandering pastoralist but a figure of regional significance."

    14:9-12

    The battle narrative's style — rapid, list-heavy, geographically specific — is unlike anything else in Genesis. Alter notes the effect: the sudden shift to military chronicle makes the Abraham narrative more historically credible. Abraham exists in a real political world of competing powers.

Calvin: "God permitted this battle to take place and Lot to be captured, in order to exercise the faith and courage of Abram, and to give him occasion to show the force of his piety." The historical event is simultaneously a divine stage-setting for Abram's character to be revealed.

    Verses 13–16 — Abram Rescues Lot
    13141516

    Places

    Dan
    Far northern Canaan; source of the Jordan River
    Abram pursues to Dan — the northernmost point of the later Promised Land. The pursuit covers the entire length of Canaan from the Dead Sea to the headwaters of the Jordan, demonstrating that the covenant heir has military reach across the whole land.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    14:14-16

    MacArthur: Abraham deployed 318 trained men born in his household -- a significant private military force. The precision of 'Dan' (v.14) and 'north of Damascus' (v.15) reflects accurate geographical knowledge. Abraham's victory is complete: he recovered all the goods, Lot, the women, and the other people.

    14:16

    MacArthur: Abraham recovered everything -- not just Lot but all the goods and all the people. The comprehensiveness of the rescue reflects covenant thoroughness: he did not do the minimum but fully restored what the enemy had taken. The covenant man's generosity in rescue mirrors his generosity in offering the best land to Lot.

    Hebrew Word Study

    wayyradeP vai-yir-DOF — and he pursued: Abram's pursuit (rādap) of the four kings to Dan (v.14) and beyond Damascus (v.15) is a night attack — "he and his men divided against them" (v.15). The military precision and the distance of pursuit (from Hebron to Dan to Hobah north of Damascus — over 200km) present Abram as a serious military commander, not a fortunate amateur. The same root rādap will describe Israel's pursuit of enemies in holy war (Josh 10:10; 1 Sam 14:22).

    wayāšeb vai-ya-SHEV — and he brought back: Abram "recovered all the goods and brought back his relative Lot and his possessions, together with the women and the other people" (v.16). The recovery is comprehensive — not just Lot but all the captives and all the goods. The scope of Abram's rescue foreshadows the comprehensiveness of the Exodus: God brings back "his people" — not partially but wholly, with all their possessions. The pattern of full redemption is embedded in Abram's military action.

    ḥālāl kha-LAL — slain / pierced / to pierce: The victory is described in military terms but the narrative quickly shifts to the covenant encounter with Melchizedek. The juxtaposition is deliberate: the military strength that enables rescue is placed alongside the priestly blessing that consecrates it. Abram's power is bracketed by two encounters — with Melchizedek (vv.17-20) and with the king of Sodom (vv.21-24) — and his response to each reveals his true allegiance.

    Historical Context

    Context

    Abram's military rescue of Lot reveals a man of action and loyalty, not merely a passive recipient of promises. His 318 trained men ("born in his house") indicate he is a significant chieftain, not merely a wandering shepherd. Yet after the victory, he refuses any personal profit — setting up the Melchizedek scene as the proper response to military success.

Sarna: "The narrative emphasises Abram's speed and decisiveness — he takes what he needs and acts immediately. The rescue demonstrates that the man of faith is also a man of action, capable of engaging the world on its own terms when the welfare of his household demands it."

    Abram's night attack is a tactically sophisticated manoeuvre — dividing forces and striking at night to maximise surprise. Sarna notes the three-phase operation is described with the precision of a military communique, suggesting a historical source of unusual reliability behind the narrative.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    14:14-16

    Abram's mobilisation — 318 "trained men born in his household" — suggests a household of considerable size. Alter: "The patriarch is not a simple nomad but the leader of what amounts to a small private army. The rescue narrative briefly reveals the social scale of the promise-bearer's household." The success of the night attack validates Abram's standing as a military figure in the region.

    14:15

    The brevity of the rescue account — one verse for the military action — is characteristic biblical technique. The story is not interested in military heroics but in what they set up theologically: the Melchizedek encounter and the rejection of the king of Sodom's offer. The battle is the setup; the aftermath is the point.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    14:16

    Abram recovers Lot, the women, and "all the goods." Calvin: "Abram did not only liberate his nephew, but restored to all their property those who had been despoiled. This is the moderation and equity of holy men — they do not seek their own things only, but extend their care to all who need their help." The rescue is not merely familial but humanitarian.

    14:14

    Calvin on the 318 trained men: Abram was not a man without resources but had built a substantial household capable of military action. He had not used this power for conquest but for peace. When necessity required it, he acted decisively. Faith that waits patiently and faith that acts decisively are the same faith in different circumstances.

    Verses 17–24 — Melchizedek Blesses Abram
    1718192021222324

    Places

    Valley of Shaveh / King's Valley
    Near Jerusalem; possibly Kidron Valley
    The king of Sodom meets Abram in the Valley of Shaveh — later called the King's Valley, associated with the area near Jerusalem. Melchizedek comes from Salem (widely identified with Jerusalem) to bless Abram here.
    Salem
    Jerusalem; "city of peace"
    Melchizedek is king of Salem — the pre-Israelite name for Jerusalem. Psalm 76:2 equates Salem with Zion. Abram's encounter with the priest-king of Jerusalem anticipates David's capture of the city and the Melchizedekian priesthood of Psalm 110 and Hebrews 5-7.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    14:18-20

    MacArthur: Melchizedek is identified as both king of Salem (Jerusalem) and priest of God Most High (El Elyon) -- the first priest mentioned in Scripture. He brings bread and wine and blesses Abraham. Abraham gives him a tenth of everything, recognising the higher priestly order Melchizedek represents.

    14:22-23

    MacArthur: Abraham's refusal to take any plunder from the king of Sodom -- 'not even a thread or the strap of a sandal' (v.23) -- demonstrates that his wealth will come from God alone and not from association with Sodom. The oath by the LORD God Most High validates the Melchizedekian blessing and refuses contamination from Sodom's king.

    Hebrew Word Study

    Malkî-ṣedeq mal-kee-TZEH-dek — king of righteousness / Melchizedek: The sudden appearance of Melchizedek, "king of Salem and priest of God Most High" (v.18), is one of Genesis's most theologically pregnant moments. His name combines melek ("king") and ṣedeq ("righteousness") — a name meaning "king of righteousness." His city Salem (šālēm, later Jerusalem) adds "king of peace." He has no genealogy, no predecessors, no successors — appearing fully formed as priest-king, which the Letter to the Hebrews reads as typologically pointing to Christ's eternal priesthood (Heb 7:1-17).

    ʾEl ʿElyôn EL el-YON — God Most High: Melchizedek's title for God — ʾEl ʿElyôn, "God Most High, Creator of heaven and earth" (v.19) — is a Canaanite divine name here applied to Israel's God. Abram immediately accepts this identification (v.22: "I have sworn an oath to the LORD, God Most High"), equating YHWH with Melchizedek's ʾEl ʿElyôn. The episode demonstrates that the covenant God is not merely a tribal deity but the universal sovereign recognised even by non-Israelite priest-kings, "Creator of heaven and earth."

    maʿăśēr ma-a-SAYR — tithe / tenth: Abram gave Melchizedek "a tenth of everything" (v.20) — the first tithe in Scripture. The tithe (maʿăśēr) will be legislated in the Mosaic law (Lev 27:30-32; Num 18:26; Deut 14:22-29) as the recognition that God owns the totality of the land's produce. Abram's spontaneous tithe to the priest-king of Salem demonstrates his acknowledgment of divine sovereignty over his military victory: the spoils belong ultimately to "the LORD, God Most High," not to Abram's own strength.

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    Melchizedek appears from nowhere, has no genealogy (a significant omission in the genealogy-obsessed world of Genesis), pronounces blessing, receives a tithe, and disappears. He is simultaneously king of a city and priest of God Most High — a combination later forbidden in Israel (Uzziah was struck with leprosy for attempting it, 2 Chr 26:16–21). His appearance anticipates the royal-priestly office that Israel's system cannot sustain but that Christ embodies.

    Cross-Reference

    • Ps 110:4God swears to David's Lord: "You are a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek" — the Davidic king as eternal priest.
    • Heb 5:6–10; 7:1–28The most extensive NT treatment: Melchizedek's priesthood is superior to Aaron's because Abraham (ancestor of Levi) paid tithes to him. Christ is the fulfillment of this superior priesthood.
    • Heb 7:3Melchizedek is described as "without father or mother or genealogy, having neither beginning of days nor end of life" — not that he was supernatural, but that Scripture records none of these, making him a type of Christ's eternal priesthood.

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    14:18-20

    Melchizedek is one of the most mysterious figures in the Old Testament. Sarna identifies him as priest-king of Jerusalem (Salem = Jerusalem). His name means "my king is righteous" or "king of righteousness." He brings bread and wine — sustenance after the battle. His blessing of Abram by "God Most High" (El Elyon) and his tithe from Abram indicate that Abram recognises his priestly authority.

    14:22-24

    Abram's refusal of the king of Sodom's offer is a second act in the same scene — contrasting sharply with his reception of Melchizedek's gift. Sarna: "Abram accepts bread and wine from the priest-king of Jerusalem; he refuses wealth from the king of Sodom. The two responses define his orientation: he receives from the righteous and refuses from the corrupt." The refusal preserves the integrity of the divine blessing — no one can say a pagan king made Abram rich.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    14:18-20

    Melchizedek appears without introduction, blesses Abram, and disappears. Alter: "He is the most enigmatic figure in the Abraham narrative — a pagan priest-king who worships the same God as Abram, who blesses Abram with a divine blessing, and who receives Abram's tithe. His appearance is brief, its significance immense." The author of Hebrews will exploit this brevity — Melchizedek appears and disappears without genealogy, making him a type of the eternal priesthood.

    14:22-24

    Abram's refusal of the king of Sodom's offer is one of the most theologically charged rejections in Genesis. Alter notes the oath sworn by 'the LORD, God Most High' (YHWH El Elyon) — the same title Melchizedek used to bless Abram — identifies the covenant God and the cosmic God as one. Abram will not let the king of Sodom say he made Abram rich.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    14:18

    Melchizedek as a type of Christ: Calvin affirms the typological reading but is careful about its limits: "The Scripture does not teach us to consider Melchizedek as one who had neither beginning nor end, but only that his origin is not recorded. The type of Christ lies not in the absence of genealogy but in the permanence of his priesthood — he combines kingship and priesthood in a single person, which the Mosaic law will separate and which Christ will reunite."

    14:22-23

    Abram's refusal of the king of Sodom's wealth is a declaration of dependence on God alone. Calvin: "Abram thought it would be more profitable to have God alone for his debtor. He did not wish to be enriched through the munificence of the king of Sodom, lest it should seem that God had failed him in his promise." The refusal is an act of faith in the sufficiency of the Abrahamic promise.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    14:18

    The NET note on El Elyon (God Most High) discusses whether this refers to the same God as YHWH or a different deity. The note observes that Abram's explicit identification of YHWH with El Elyon in v.22 — "I have raised my hand to the LORD, God Most High" — indicates that Abram understood Melchizedek's God and his own God to be the same.     14:20

    The NET note on the tithe Abram gives Melchizedek observes this is the first tithe in the Bible — predating the Mosaic tithe system by centuries. The note connects this to Hebrews 7:4-10, where the author uses Abram's tithe to demonstrate Melchizedek's (and therefore Christ's) superiority to the Levitical priesthood: Abraham tithed to Melchizedek, and Levi was "in the loins of Abraham" when this happened.

    Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    Melchizedek
    King of Salem; Priest of God Most High
    The most theologically significant figure in the chapter. He appears without introduction, blesses Abram, receives a tithe, and vanishes — his lack of genealogy (unusual for Genesis) becomes the basis for Hebrews' typological argument about Christ's eternal priesthood.
    Abram
    Patriarch; Warrior; Man of integrity
    Revealed here as a military leader with 318 trained men. His refusal of the king of Sodom's offer demonstrates that his wealth will come from God alone — an act of faith that Hebrews reads as foundational.
    King of Sodom
    Unnamed; Morally corrupt city's ruler
    Offers Abram the spoils of war. His offer, rejected by Abram, is a test: will the patriarch allow a wicked king to claim credit for his prosperity? Abram's refusal protects the theological purity of his witness.

    Ancient Sources

    Dead Sea Scrolls — 11QMelchizedek
    A fragmentary Qumran text casts Melchizedek as a heavenly being who will execute judgment on the Day of Atonement. He is identified with the "elohim" of Psalm 82 and given a quasi-angelic status.
    Shows how Second Temple Judaism elevated Melchizedek far beyond the Genesis text, making the Hebrews author's careful typological reading a measured response to existing speculation.
    Josephus, Antiquities 1.10.2
    Josephus identifies Salem with Jerusalem and Melchizedek as the first to build a temple there. He translates Melchizedek as "righteous king" and notes his priestly role.
    Josephus' geographical identification with Jerusalem became standard in Jewish and Christian tradition.

    Reception History

    Letter to the Hebrews, chapters 5–7
    The NT's most extensive use of the OT interprets Melchizedek as the type whose antitype is Christ. Key moves: (1) his name = "king of righteousness"; (2) Salem = Jerusalem = "king of peace"; (3) no recorded genealogy = eternal priesthood; (4) Levi paid tithes through Abraham = Melchizedekian order superior to Levitical.
    Psalm 110 and David
    Psalm 110:4 ("a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek") is the most-cited OT verse in the NT. Jesus uses it to stump the Pharisees (Matt 22:41–46); Hebrews builds a theology of Christ's priesthood on it.

    Literary Structure

    Abram the Warrior: Covenant Loyalty Activates Capability14:14-16318 trained men, a night attack, pursuit to Dan: Abram has military capability he has never used for conquest. He uses it once -- for Lot. Covenant loyalty activates what personal ambition never did.
    Melchizedek: Priest-King of Salem14:18-20Melchizedek appears without genealogy, blesses Abram, and receives the tithe. The encounter is theologically inexhaustible: its full significance unfolds across Psalm 110:4 and Hebrews 5-7.

    Scholarly Debates

    Melchizedek: Historical Figure, Type, or Angel?
    Historical king-priest
    Sarna, Wenham, Waltke
    Melchizedek is a historical figure — the king-priest of pre-Israelite Jerusalem. The chapter is a historical narrative. His priesthood and blessing of Abraham are real historical events that have later theological significance.
    Christophany / pre-incarnate Christ
    Some Church Fathers, Calvin partially
    The complete absence of genealogy and the exalted priestly role suggest a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ. Ambrose and others read the bread and wine as foreshadowing the Eucharist administered by Christ.
    Typological figure
    Hebrews 5-7, most evangelical scholars
    Melchizedek is a historical figure whose particular features (priesthood, righteousness, peace, no recorded genealogy) make him a type of Christ's eternal priesthood. He is not Christ himself but the pattern Christ fulfils.
    The typological reading of Hebrews 5-7 is the NT's own interpretation and carries apostolic authority. The historical and typological readings are not in conflict. Most scholarship reads Melchizedek as a real historical king-priest whose lack of recorded genealogy (in the Genesis text) becomes the basis for typological comparison with Christ's eternal priesthood.
