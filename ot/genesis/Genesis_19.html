Genesis 19

The Destruction of Sodom; Lot's Escape

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–11 — The Angels at Sodom; Lot's Hospitality
1The two angels arrived at Sodom in the evening, and Lot was sitting in the gateway of the city. When he saw them, he got up to meet them and bowed down with his face to the ground.2"My lords," he said, "please turn aside to your servant's house. You can wash your feet and spend the night and then go on your way early in the morning." "No," they answered, "we will spend the night in the square."3But he insisted so strongly that they did go with him and entered his house. He prepared a meal for them, baking bread without yeast, and they ate.4Before they had gone to bed, all the men from every part of the city of Sodom—both young and old—surrounded the house.5They called to Lot, "Where are the men who came to you tonight? Bring them out to us so that we can have sex with them."6Lot went outside to meet them and shut the door behind him7and said, "No, my friends. Don't do this wicked thing.8Look, I have two daughters who have never slept with a man. Let me bring them out to you, and you can do what you like with them. But don't do anything to these men, for they have come under the protection of my roof."9"Get out of our way," they replied. "This fellow came here as a foreigner, and now he wants to play the judge! We'll treat you worse than them." They kept bringing pressure on Lot and moved forward to break down the door.10But the men inside reached out and pulled Lot back into the house and shut the door.11Then they struck the men who were at the door of the house, young and old, with blindness so that they could not find the door.

Places

Sodom — City Gate
Southern Dead Sea basin
Lot is at the city gate when the angels arrive — the gate being the civic and judicial centre of ancient cities. His position there suggests full civic integration, not peripheral residence. His hospitality mirrors Abraham's but in an urban rather than pastoral setting.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
19:1-3

MacArthur: Lot's hospitality to the two angels (v.2-3) mirrors Abraham's (18:1-8) but with differences: Lot is sitting at the gate (a position of civic authority), he bows with his face to the ground, and he urges them strongly after they initially decline. The hospitality is genuine; but Lot's position in Sodom is the source of the coming complication.

19:9-11

MacArthur: The Sodomites' demand to 'know' (yada -- sexual knowledge) the visitors is unambiguous. Lot's offer of his daughters to protect his guests, while following ancient hospitality customs, is morally reprehensible by any standard. The angelic blinding of the crowd (v.11) is the divine protection that overrides the human failure.

Hebrew Word Study

ʾanāšîm a-na-SHEEM — men / human beings / the men (angels): The two "angels" who arrive at Sodom are called malʾākîm in v.1 but ʾanāšîm ("men") throughout the rest of the chapter — the same ambiguity as in ch.18. The narrative oscillates between their angelic identity and human appearance, creating the theological irony of Sodom's attempted violation: the town literally attempts to assault the messengers of God. The contrast with Abraham's hospitality (18:1-8) is stark — the same divine messengers receive radically different treatment.

rāʿāh / ḥāṭāʾt ra-AH / kha-TAHT — wickedness / sin: The men of Sodom are described as "evil, sinning greatly against the LORD" (13:13), and ch.19 demonstrates the content: violent assault on guests (vv.4-9), contempt for the protective obligations of hospitality, and blindness after being struck (v.11) that is both literal and metaphorical. Ezekiel 16:49-50 identifies Sodom's sin comprehensively: "pride, excess of food and prosperous ease... she did not aid the poor and needy... they did detestable things." Sexual violence is the culmination, not the whole, of Sodom's moral disorder.

ʿiwwēr ee-VAYR — blind / blindness: The angels "struck the men who were at the door of the house, young and old, with blindness (sannĕwērîm)" (v.11) — a blindness so complete that "they wore themselves out trying to find the door." The word sannĕwērîm (a rare Aramaic loanword, also in 2 Kgs 6:18) describes total incapacitation. The blindness is both the immediate punishment and a symbol of the spiritual blindness that has characterised Sodom throughout. Those who pursue the darkness become blind — they literally cannot find the door.

Historical Context

None

Context

Ezekiel 16:49–50 provides the most detailed biblical analysis of Sodom's sin: "arrogance, overfed and unconcerned; they did not help the poor and needy. They were haughty and did detestable things before me." Homosexual gang rape is the culminating expression of a society organized around pride, comfort, and contempt for the vulnerable. The hospitality violation — attacking guests — is the specific act that triggers judgment in the narrative.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezek 16:49–50Ezekiel's summary of Sodom's sins: pride, excess, neglect of the poor — with the sexual violence as the culmination of a corrupt society.
  • Jude 7Jude describes Sodom and Gomorrah as undergoing "the punishment of eternal fire" as an example for those who pursue "sexual immorality and strange flesh."
  • Luke 10:12Jesus uses Sodom as the epitome of judgment, saying it will be "more tolerable for Sodom" than for cities that reject the gospel — making the Sodom account about reception of divine messengers.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
19:1-11

The assault of the men of Sodom on the two visitors is the confirmation of the wickedness announced in 13:13 and 18:20. Sarna: "The narrative does not linger on the sin's specifics — the point is the totality of the corruption: 'all the men from every part of the city of Sodom' (v.4). The society has no remnant of righteousness; even the young and old participate. The judgment is therefore complete and just."

19:8

Lot's offer of his daughters to the mob is morally scandalous to modern readers. Sarna: "The offer reflects the extreme importance of hospitality obligations in ancient culture — the guest under one's roof must be protected even at great personal cost. The narrative does not endorse Lot's offer; it records it as the desperate measure of a man who has not yet found the exit. The angels resolve the crisis by blinding the mob (v.11), superseding Lot's morally compromised solution."

Alter — Literary Reading

19:1-3

Lot sitting at the gate of Sodom signals his civic integration — he is a judge or elder of the city. Alter: "Lot has completed his journey from outsider (13:12: 'pitched his tents near Sodom') to resident to city official. The man who chose by sight and moved toward Sodom has become, by degrees, one of Sodom's own. The hospitality he extends to the angels is admirable; the city he represents is irredeemable."

The Sodomites turn on Lot: "this fellow came here as a foreigner, and now he wants to play the judge!" Alter: "The men of Sodom expose the contradiction in Lot's position: he has integrated enough to sit at their gate but retains enough moral sense to object to their behavior. He is neither fully theirs nor fully apart from them — the ambiguous position that will mark him through the rest of the chapter."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
19:4-5

The sin of Sodom in this passage is the attempted sexual assault of the divine visitors — a sin of violent domination, not merely of sexual immorality. Calvin: "The men of Sodom wished to offer violence to the angels. This is the ultimate degradation of the image of God — to treat divine messengers as objects of violent lust." The sin is simultaneously against creation order, against hospitality, and against the divine presence.

19:11

The blinding of the men of Sodom is a mercy as well as a judgment — it gives them an opportunity to stop, which they do not take ("they wore themselves out trying to find the door"). Calvin: "Even when struck blind, they persist in their wickedness. This shows the extent of the corruption: not even divine intervention from outside can redirect a will entirely given over to evil."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
19:5

The NET note on "know them" (neda) discusses whether the verb implies sexual knowledge here or simply acquaintance. The note concludes that the context — the contrast with Lot's offer of his daughters — makes the sexual meaning unmistakable. The note also observes that Ezekiel 16:49-50 gives a broader characterisation of Sodom's sins (pride, excess food, unconcern for the poor) that complements rather than replaces the sexual sin described here.

19:8-11

The NET note on Lot's offer of his daughters discusses the legal protection of guests in the ancient Near East: the host was obligated to protect his guests at significant personal cost. The note also discusses the blindness inflicted on the crowd as divine intervention protecting the messengers.

Verses 12–22 — Lot's Family Warned; the Escape to Zoar
12The two men said to Lot, "Do you have anyone else here—sons-in-law, sons or daughters, or anyone else in the city who belongs to you? Get them out of here,13because we are going to destroy this place. The outcry to the Lord against its people is so great that he has sent us to destroy it."14So Lot went out and spoke to his sons-in-law, who were pledged to marry his daughters. He said, "Hurry and get out of this place, because the Lord is about to destroy the city!" But his sons-in-law thought he was joking.15With the coming of dawn, the angels urged Lot, saying, "Hurry! Take your wife and your two daughters who are here, or you will be swept away when the city is punished."16When he hesitated, the men grasped his hand and the hands of his wife and of his two daughters and led them safely out of the city, for the Lord was merciful to them.17As soon as they had brought them out, one of them said, "Flee for your lives! Don't look back, and don't stop anywhere in the plain! Flee to the mountains or you will be swept away!"18"No, my lords, please!" Lot said.19"Your servant has found favor in your eyes, and you have shown great kindness to me in sparing my life. But I can't flee to the mountains; this disaster will overtake me, and I'll die.20Look, here is a town near enough to run to, and it is small. Let me flee to it—it is very small, isn't it? Then my life will be spared."21He said to him, "Very well, I will grant this request too; I will not overthrow the town you speak of.22But flee there quickly, because I cannot do anything until you reach it." (That is why the town was called Zoar.)

Places

Zoar
Small city south of the Dead Sea
Lot names Zoar ("small") while fleeing — the place is saved from destruction at his request. Zoar is the escape valve: the small place spared so the covenant margins are preserved. Lot's retreat to Zoar and then to the mountains traces the withdrawal from civilization.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
19:14

MacArthur: Lot's sons-in-law thought he was joking -- a tragic detail. The man who had chosen Sodom for its prosperity had so compromised his witness that those closest to him could not take his warning seriously. Lot's ineffective warning to his sons-in-law illustrates how worldly compromise destroys prophetic credibility.

19:22

MacArthur: 'I cannot do anything until you reach it' -- the divine limitation on judgment is imposed by the covenant obligation to rescue the righteous before executing judgment on the wicked. The timing of Sodom's destruction is governed by Lot's safety, not by any independent divine timetable.

Hebrew Word Study

māhar ma-HAYR — hurry / make haste: The angels' repeated command to "hurry" (māhar, vv.15, 17, 22) and Lot's delay (v.16 — "he hesitated") creates the chapter's central irony: the rescuers must drag the rescued toward their own salvation. "The LORD was merciful to them" (v.16) — the causative of the verb leads them by the hand even when they are too spiritually attached to Sodom to flee voluntarily. The same dynamic characterises God's patient leading of Israel toward what they would not independently choose.

himlēṭ him-la-LAYT — escape / flee for your life: "Flee for your lives! Don't look back, and don't stop" (v.17) — the command has the same urgency as the Exodus departure instructions: "Leave Egypt in haste" (Exod 12:11). The specification "don't look back" (ʾal-tabiṭ ʾaḥărêkā) is the prohibition of nostalgia for what is being left behind. Lot's wife looks back (v.26 — watabbēṭ ʾiššāh ʾaḥărāyw) and becomes a "pillar of salt" — petrified in the act of return. Jesus cites her as a warning against attachment to what God is calling us to leave (Luke 17:32).

ʾammônî / môʾābî am-mo-NEE / mo-a-VEE — Ammonite / Moabite: The chapter ends with the birth of Moab and Ben-Ammi from Lot's daughters (vv.37-38) — the ancestors of the Moabites and Ammonites, nations that will have complex relationships with Israel throughout its history. Ruth is a Moabite; Naamah the Ammonite is a wife of Solomon. The genealogical note is not merely historical but theological: even these nations, born of desperate and compromised circumstances, fall within the scope of God's historical purposes.

Historical Context

None

Context

Lot's rescue is explicitly credited to Abraham's intercession in v.29 — "God remembered Abraham and sent Lot out." The intercession of ch.18 had its effect not in sparing Sodom but in sparing Lot. The divine mercy operates through the covenant relationship even when the direct prayer is not answered as asked. Lot's wife's backward look is interpreted by Jesus as a warning: "Remember Lot's wife" (Luke 17:32) — clinging to what God has condemned means sharing its fate.

19:14

Lot's sons-in-law think he is joking — "he seemed to his sons-in-law to be jesting." Sarna: "The irony is devastating: the man who has the divine warning, who knows the truth, is not believed by those closest to him. His credibility in Sodom has been entirely consumed by his assimilation. He can no longer speak with prophetic authority to those who know him."

Sarna: "Lot's request for Zoar ('little one') is granted. The irony of Lot's career is contained in this small negotiation: even at the moment of rescue, he seeks the smallest possible accommodation rather than full separation from the cities of the plain."

Alter — Literary Reading

19:16

Lot lingers — the angel seizes him, his wife, and his daughters by the hand. Alter: "The physical seizure of Lot's family is one of the most tender moments in the judgment narrative: divine grace literally dragging the saved out of the path of destruction. Lot, who has been unable to flee on his own, is bodily carried out by angelic hands. The salvation is entirely from outside."

19:17-22

Lot's negotiation for Zoar — 'let me flee there' — is characteristic of his half-obedience throughout the narrative. He was told to flee to the mountains; he bargains for a city instead. Alter notes Lot trusts the divine rescue but not the divine instruction about where to go.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
19:16

The angel's seizure of Lot's family despite Lot's hesitation is the acting out of divine mercy toward the undeserving. Calvin: "Lot, notwithstanding the revelation of God and the proximity of the judgment, was so seized with torpor that he had to be forcibly dragged away. Calvin: Lot had received a clear command but substituted his own judgment. He was afraid of the mountains and asked for a city instead. God grants the request out of mercy — not because Lot's judgment was right but because divine condescension extends even to those who comply imperfectly.

Verses 23–38 — Sodom Destroyed; Lot's Wife; Lot and His Daughters
23By the time Lot reached Zoar, the sun had risen over the land.24Then the Lord rained down burning sulfur on Sodom and Gomorrah—from the Lord out of the heavens.25Thus he overthrew those cities and the entire plain, destroying all those living in the cities—and also the vegetation in the land.26But Lot's wife looked back, and she became a pillar of salt.27Early the next morning Abraham got up and returned to the place where he had stood before the Lord.28He looked down toward Sodom and Gomorrah, toward all the land of the plain, and he saw dense smoke rising from the land, like smoke from a furnace.29So when God destroyed the cities of the plain, he remembered Abraham, and he brought Lot out of the catastrophe that overthrew the cities where Lot had lived.30Lot and his two daughters left Zoar and settled in the mountains, for he was afraid to stay in Zoar. He and his two daughters lived in a cave.31One day the older daughter said to the younger, "Our father is old, and there is no man around here to give us children—as is the custom all over the earth.32Let's get our father to drink wine and then sleep with him and preserve our family line through our father."33That night they got their father to drink wine, and the older daughter went in and slept with him. He was not aware of it when she lay down or when she got up.34The next day the older daughter said to the younger, "Last night I slept with my father. Let's get him to drink wine again tonight, and you go in and sleep with him so we can preserve our family line through our father."35So they got their father to drink wine that night also, and the younger daughter went in and slept with him. Again he was not aware of it when she lay down or when she got up.36So both of Lot's daughters became pregnant by their father.37The older daughter had a son, and she named him Moab; he is the father of the Moabites of today.38The younger daughter also had a son, and she named him Ben-Ammi; he is the father of the Ammonites of today.

Places

Sodom and Gomorrah
Southern Dead Sea basin
The destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah is one of the most geologically dramatic events in the OT — fire and brimstone from the sky, the smoke rising like a furnace. The Dead Sea region's sulphur and bitumen deposits make this the most plausible geographic candidate for the cities.
Lot's Cave / Mountains of Moab
East of the Dead Sea
Lot and his daughters retreat to the mountains east of Zoar — the territory that will become Moab and Ammon. The cave-birth of Moab and Ammon in the aftermath of Sodom's destruction ties these nations' origins to judgment's margins.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
19:26

MacArthur: Lot's wife looked back and became a pillar of salt. Jesus cites this in Luke 17:32 ('Remember Lot's wife') as a warning about attachment to the world when the call to escape is given. The backward look was the physical expression of a heart that had not fully left Sodom.

19:30-38

MacArthur: The Moabites and Ammonites, Israel's persistent neighbours and adversaries, descend from Lot's incestuous unions with his daughters. The origin story explains both the kinship (they are Abraham's relatives) and the tension (their origin in moral compromise shapes their character as peoples). Ruth the Moabitess will later demonstrate that this ancestry is not destiny.

Hebrew Word Study

gāprît gaf-REET — sulphur / brimstone: "The LORD rained down burning sulphur (gāprît) on Sodom and Gomorrah" (v.24) — gāprît is volcanic/geological material associated with divine judgment throughout Scripture (Deut 29:23; Ps 11:6; Isa 34:9; Rev 19:20; 21:8). The destruction is total — "dense smoke, like smoke from a furnace" (v.28). Archaeological and geological evidence around the Dead Sea region suggests a catastrophic geological event in the Middle Bronze Age. The narrative presents the event as direct divine action — mediated through natural processes but theologically purposive.

neṣib melaḥ ne-TZEEV MEH-lakh — pillar of salt: Lot's wife "became a pillar of salt" (v.26) — a detail so specific that it preserved in local memory the landscape feature it supposedly explained. But the theological function is more important than the geological: she looked back and was destroyed by what she looked back at. Salt in the OT has dual significance: it is the covenant mineral (Num 18:19 — "a covenant of salt forever") and the symbol of desolation (Deut 29:23; Judg 9:45). She is petrified in covenant violation — the permanent monument to the failure of her faith.

wayizkōr vai-yiz-KOR — and he remembered: "God remembered Abraham, and he brought Lot out of the catastrophe" (v.29) — the same divine zākar that opened the Flood's turning point (8:1: "God remembered Noah"). Divine remembering in the OT is active, purposive covenantal faithfulness. Lot is saved not because of his own merit — the narrative has repeatedly shown his moral ambiguity — but "because God remembered Abraham." The salvation of the marginal figure through the merit of the covenant patriarch is a theological pattern that runs from Lot through Ruth through every sinner sheltered under Christ's covenant.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
19:24-25

The destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah by "burning sulfur" — brimstone and fire — is the most dramatic divine judgment in the pre-Mosaic narrative. Sarna: "Archaeological surveys of the Dead Sea region have identified evidence of violent geological activity and conflagration that may be consistent with the narrative. Lot's wife looking back and becoming a pillar of salt is an etiological element — explaining a feature of the Dead Sea landscape. Sarna: "The story is not merely punitive; it illustrates the fatal attachment to what is being left behind. She looked back not merely to see but to remain — her whole orientation was still toward Sodom, and she became what she was already turning back toward."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
19:26

"But Lot's wife looked back, and she became a pillar of salt." Alter: "One of the most economical sentences in Genesis. No motive is given, no explanation, no moral judgment — only the action and its consequence. The reader is left to interpret: curiosity? longing? disobedience? The refusal to explain is part of the horror. She looked back; she is salt. The terseness is the judgment."

19:30-38

The Lot-daughters episode is deeply uncomfortable. Alter: "The daughters' plan — to preserve their father's line by making him drunk and conceiving — is presented without direct moral evaluation. Their motivation ('there is no man anywhere to sleep with us') is based on the false assumption that all men have perished. The children born — Moab and Ben-Ammi — are the ancestors of Israel's persistent adversaries. The episode is the dark comedy at the end of Lot's story."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
19:26

Lot's wife as a warning. Calvin: "She perished in punishment of her unbelief. Therefore, when the Lord calls us to leave the world and to be separated from it, let us not look back; for, though the Lord is willing to pardon many faults in his people, yet he is severe in punishing the treachery of those who, after they have been delivered, look back toward the destruction from which they have been rescued."

19:30-38

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Lot
Nephew of Abraham; "righteous" but compromised
Called "righteous" by 2 Peter 2:7–8, yet he lingered (v.16), offered his daughters to the mob (v.8), and needed to be seized by angels to escape. His righteousness is real but barely adequate — he is saved "as through fire" (1 Cor 3:15).
Lot's Wife
Unnamed; became a pillar of salt
Her backward glance (v.26) has generated enormous interpretive attention. Jesus warns: "Remember Lot's wife" (Luke 17:32). The salt pillar may be historical (the Lisan Peninsula area has salt formations) or symbolic of petrification in the moment of disobedience.
Two Angels
Messengers; executors of judgment
The same figures as two of the three Mamre visitors. They execute divine judgment with decisive efficiency — and merciful persistence in rescuing Lot despite his hesitation.

Ancient Sources

Tall el-Hammam Archaeological Evidence (2021)
A 2021 Nature Scientific Reports article by Bunch et al. described evidence of a catastrophic cosmic airburst (similar to the Tunguska event) at Tall el-Hammam c. 1650 BC — melted pottery, shocked quartz, platinum spike consistent with an extraterrestrial impact. The city showed sudden total destruction.
The findings do not prove the biblical account but provide a possible natural mechanism for a catastrophic destruction event in exactly the right geographical and chronological range.

Reception History

Throughout OT Prophecy
Sodom becomes the stock image of divine judgment: Isaiah 1:9–10 calls Jerusalem's leaders "rulers of Sodom"; Jeremiah 23:14 says Jerusalem's prophets are "like Sodom"; Amos 4:11 uses Sodom as a benchmark of God's past warnings. The city's destruction permeates prophetic imagination.
Revelation 11:8
The great city where the two witnesses are killed is called "spiritually...Sodom and Egypt, where their Lord was crucified" — apocalyptic Sodom becomes a symbol of any anti-God civilization.

Literary Structure

Hospitality vs Violation at the Door19:1-11Lot's hospitality and the city's violation demand confront each other at the door. The divine blinding of the crowd is the direct divine intervention that protects the messengers -- and reveals the city's judgment.
The Backward Look: Salt as Monument to Attachment19:26She looked back. The brevity of the sentence is the economy of judgment. She could not leave Sodom behind; the city she could not release became her monument. Attachment that cannot release the past becomes the past.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

The Sin of Sodom: Sexual or Social?
Sexual/homosexual
Traditional interpretation, Wenham, Waltke
The mob demands to "know" (yada) the men sexually (v.5). The same verb is used in v.8 for the daughters "who have not known a man." The sexual connotation is explicit. The sin includes attempted homosexual gang rape.
Inhospitality / violence
Bailey (1955), some scholars
The verb yada (know) can mean "become acquainted with" (Gen 29:5). The sin is violation of hospitality norms, not sexual. Ezekiel 16:49 identifies Sodom's sin as pride, gluttony, and neglect of the poor — with no sexual reference.
Both/comprehensive
Sarna, Hamilton, Jude 7
Jude 7 ("gave themselves up to sexual immorality and perversion") confirms a sexual element. But the broader prophetic condemnation (Ezek 16:49; Isa 1:10-17; Amos 4:11) emphasises social sins. Sodom's wickedness was comprehensive.
The narrative clearly includes attempted sexual violence (the verb yada in context, Jude 7). The broader prophetic condemnation adds pride, injustice, and inhospitality. The two are not alternatives — the sexual violence is the acute expression of a deeper social and moral catastrophe. Modern attempts to reduce the sin to inhospitality alone do not adequately account for Jude 7 and 2 Pet 2:6-7.
