Isaiah 12

Songs of Praise: In That Day You Will Say

Verses 1–3 — “Surely God Is My Salvation”
123

Hebrew Word Study

יְשׁוּעָה yəshūʿâ — salvation: "Surely God is my salvation (yəshūʿâ); I will trust and not be afraid. The LORD, the LORD himself, is my strength and my defence; he has become my salvation" (v.2). Chapter 12 is a two-song epilogue to the Immanuel section (chs.7–11). The first song (vv.1–3) is individual thanksgiving: anger turned away, comfort given, salvation as a well from which to draw water with joy (v.3).

Context

Chapter 12 closes the first major division of Isaiah (chs.1–12) with praise, just as the Psalter’s five books each end with doxology. The name yəshūʿâ (salvation) shares the same root as the name Yeshua (Jesus). "With joy you will draw water from the wells of salvation" (v.3) was sung during the water-pouring ceremony at the Feast of Tabernacles — the context in which Jesus stood and cried "If anyone is thirsty, let him come to me" (John 7:37–38).

Cross-Reference

  • Ex 15:2The LORD is my strength and my defence; he has become my salvation — Moses’ song after the Red Sea, quoted verbatim in Isa 12:2.
  • John 7:37–38If anyone is thirsty, let him come to me and drink — Jesus at Tabernacles, echoing Isa 12:3’s wells of salvation.
  • Ps 118:14The LORD is my strength and my song; he has become my salvation — the psalm parallel.
  • Rev 7:17The Lamb will lead them to springs of living water — the fulfilment of drawing from salvation’s wells.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
12:1

"In that day you will say: I will praise you, LORD. Although you were angry, your anger has turned away." The sequence: anger first, then comfort. Praise comes AFTER judgment, not instead of it.

12:2

"The LORD is my strength and my defence; he has become my salvation." Quoting Moses’ exodus song. The second exodus produces the same praise.

12:3

"With joy you will draw water from the wells of salvation." Water = life. Joy = the manner of drawing. Wells = inexhaustible supply. Jesus echoes this at Tabernacles.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:2

Calvin notes the quotation of Exodus 15:2 makes the second-exodus theology explicit: the praise that followed the Red Sea will follow the return from exile. Every deliverance recapitulates the exodus.

12:3

Calvin reads the wells metaphor as the gospel’s inexhaustible supply: salvation is not a single event but an ongoing source. You draw from it daily, with joy.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:2

The NET notes yəshūʿâ appears twice in v.2, framing the verse. The root yshʿ gives us both "salvation" and the name "Jesus" (Yeshua). The wordplay is embedded in the Hebrew.

12:3

The NET connects the water-drawing to the Tabernacles ceremony (Sukkot) described in the Mishnah (Sukkah 4:9). The joy of the water-pouring was legendary.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
12:1–3

Oswalt reads ch.12 as the structural conclusion to chs.1–12: judgment opened the section (ch.1); praise closes it. The same pattern governs the whole book: chs.1–39 end in judgment; chs.40–66 end in praise.

12:2

Oswalt: the quotation of Ex 15:2 is the hermeneutical key — every deliverance in Isaiah is a new exodus. The exodus is not just history but pattern.

Childs Notes

childs
12:1–3

Childs reads the chapter as a canonical doxology: it gathers the themes of chs.1–11 (judgment, Immanuel, remnant, shoot) and responds with worship. The reader is taught that the proper response to prophecy is praise.

12:2

Childs notes the doubled yəshūʿâ in v.2 is a wordplay on the name Isaiah (yəshaʿyāhū = "YHWH saves"). The prophet’s name IS the chapter’s message.

Verses 4–6 — “Shout Aloud, for Great Is the Holy One of Israel”
456

Hebrew Word Study

קְדוֹשׁ qədōsh — Holy One: "Shout aloud and sing for joy, people of Zion, for great is the Holy One (qədōsh) of Israel among you" (v.6). The second song (vv.4–6) is communal: "Make known among the nations what he has done. Proclaim that his name is exalted" (v.4). "Sing to the LORD, for he has done glorious things; let this be known to all the world" (v.5). The "Holy One of Israel" — Isaiah’s signature title for God (26 times in Isaiah, 6 elsewhere) — appears in the section’s final verse.

Context

The communal song shifts from personal praise (vv.1–3) to missionary proclamation (vv.4–6): "make known among the nations," "let this be known to all the world." The praise is not private but public and universal. The title "Holy One of Israel" links back to the trisagion of 6:3 and forward to its 25 remaining uses throughout the book. The chapter’s six verses serve as a hinge between the Immanuel section (chs.7–11) and the Oracles Against Nations (chs.13–23).

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 105:1–2Give praise to the LORD, proclaim his name; make known among the nations what he has done — nearly identical language.
  • 1 Chr 16:8–10Give praise to the LORD, call upon his name; make known among the nations — David’s song of the same pattern.
  • Isa 6:3Holy, holy, holy — the throne-room trisagion that generates the "Holy One of Israel" title.
  • Zeph 3:14–17Sing, Daughter Zion; the LORD is with you — the same joy-in-God’s-presence motif.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
12:4–5

"Make known among the nations what he has done." The praise is missionary: not just worship but witness. The nations must hear.

12:6

"Great is the Holy One of Israel AMONG you." Not distant but present. The holiness of 6:3 has come to dwell with the people. Immanuel theology in worship-form.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:4–6

Calvin reads the transition from personal to communal praise as the pattern of all genuine worship: it begins in the individual heart and overflows to the nations. Private gratitude becomes public proclamation.

12:6

Calvin: "the Holy One among you" is the answer to the entire Immanuel section. God is WITH his people, holy and present. The terror of 6:5 becomes the joy of 12:6.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:4

The NET notes the imperative verbs: "give praise, call, make known, proclaim." Four commands, all outward-facing. Praise is not passive reception but active declaration.

12:6

The NET observes "Holy One of Israel" is Isaiah’s signature phrase. Its placement at the end of the first major section is programmatic: the book’s entire theology flows from this name.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
12:4–6

Oswalt reads the missionary dimension as essential: Isaiah’s vision is never just about Israel. From 2:2–4 (nations streaming) to 11:10 (banner for peoples) to 12:4–5 (make known to the world), the scope is always universal.

12:6

Oswalt: the Holy One "among you" closes the circle opened by "Holy, holy, holy" in 6:3. The God who is transcendently holy is also intimately present.

Childs Notes

childs
12:4–6

Childs reads the chapter as a canonical bridge: the praise closes chs.1–12 and the missional imperatives ("make known among the nations") anticipate the Oracles Against Nations in chs.13–23.

12:6

Childs notes the "Holy One of Israel" is Isaiah’s theological centre. It holds together holiness (God’s transcendence) and Israel (God’s particular relationship). Neither alone is sufficient.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
