Isaiah 3

When Leadership Fails

Verses 1–15 — “The LORD Takes Away the Supply and the Support”
123456789101112131415

Hebrew Word Study

מַשְעֵן mashʿēn — support / supply: "The LORD is about to take from Jerusalem and Judah both supply and support (mashʿēn): all supplies of food and water, the hero and the warrior, the judge and the prophet" (vv.1–2). The leadership vacuum: boys become rulers, the people oppress each other, the young rise against the old (vv.4–5). "My people, your guides lead you astray" (v.12). God takes his place in court to judge the elders and leaders who have ruined the vineyard and ground the faces of the poor (vv.13–15).

Context

The removal of leadership is presented as divine judgment: God TAKES AWAY competent leaders, leaving chaos. The list of removed figures (vv.2–3) spans military, judicial, religious, and even occult leadership — the entire social infrastructure collapses. The vineyard metaphor (v.14) anticipates the Song of the Vineyard in ch.5. The phrase "grinding the faces of the poor" (v.15) is among the most vivid social-justice images in the prophets.

Cross-Reference

  • Judg 21:25Everyone did as they saw fit — the anarchy Isa 3 describes.
  • Ezek 34:2–4Woe to the shepherds who only take care of themselves — the same failed-leadership indictment.
  • Matt 9:36Jesus saw the crowds harassed and helpless, like sheep without a shepherd — the NT version of Isa 3’s leadership vacuum.
  • Jas 5:1–6Weep and wail, you rich people — the NT’s version of Isa 3:14–15.

MacArthur Study Notes

The supply list: food, water, heroes, warriors, judges, prophets, diviners, elders, captains, counsellors, artisans, enchanters. Every category removed.

Boys as rulers. The strong oppressing the weak. Youth disrespecting age. Social order inverted.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1–4

Calvin reads the leadership-removal as the worst divine judgment: not famine or war but the removal of competent governance. A society can survive drought; it cannot survive the absence of justice.

3:14–15

Calvin applies "grinding the face of the poor" directly: any economic system that enriches leaders at the expense of the vulnerable is under divine indictment.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:1–3

The NET notes the list includes both legitimate leaders (judges, elders) and illegitimate ones (diviners, enchanters). God removes ALL social infrastructure, clean and corrupt alike.

3:15

The NET observes "grinding the face of the poor" uses the Hebrew verb tachan (to grind grain). The poor are treated as raw material to be processed for others’ consumption.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
3:1–15

Oswalt reads the leadership vacuum as the consequence of ch.2’s pride: when leaders exalt themselves instead of serving, God removes them. The vacuum IS the judgment.

3:14–15

Oswalt notes the courtroom imagery: God stands to prosecute the very leaders who should have administered justice. The judge judges the judges.

Childs Notes

childs
3:1–15

Childs sees the passage as the first of Isaiah’s "woe" sequences: leadership failure leads to social collapse leads to judgment. The pattern recurs throughout First Isaiah.

3:12

Childs reads "your guides lead you astray" as the canonical explanation for the exile: bad leadership is the proximate cause of national disaster.

Verses 16–26 — “The Daughters of Zion Are Haughty”
1617181920212223242526

Hebrew Word Study

תִפְאָרָה tifʾārâ — beauty / glory: "The LORD says: The women of Zion are haughty, walking along with outstretched necks, flirting with their eyes, strutting along with swaying hips" (v.16). The inventory of luxury items (vv.18–23) — bangles, headbands, crescent necklaces, earrings, bracelets, veils, headdresses, ankle chains, sashes, perfume, rings, fine robes, cloaks, purses, mirrors, linen garments, tiaras, shawls. Instead of fragrance, stench; instead of a sash, a rope; instead of beauty, branding (v.24). Zion’s gates will lament; destitute, she will sit on the ground (v.26).

Context

The luxury catalogue (21 items) is the most detailed description of women’s fashion in the Bible. It reflects the prosperity of 8th-century Judah under Uzziah and Jotham. The indictment is not of beauty per se but of luxury that coexists with social oppression (cf. 3:14–15). The women’s finery was acquired through the same system that ground the faces of the poor. The reversal (v.24) is systematic: each luxury replaced by its opposite — fragrance → stench, fine hair → baldness, rich robes → sackcloth, beauty → branding.

Cross-Reference

  • Amos 4:1–3Hear this, you cows of Bashan — Amos’s parallel critique of wealthy women who oppress the poor.
  • 1 Pet 3:3–4Your beauty should not come from outward adornment but from the inner self — the NT’s response.
  • Prov 31:30Charm is deceptive, beauty is fleeting; a woman who fears the LORD is to be praised.
  • Rev 18:11–16The merchants weep because no one buys their luxury goods anymore — Babylon’s luxury stripped, like Zion’s in Isa 3.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:16–17

Haughty walking, flirting eyes, swaying hips. The external display of internal pride.

3:18–23

Twenty-one luxury items listed. The most detailed fashion inventory in the Bible. Each item represents wealth extracted from the oppressed.

3:24

Fragrance to stench, sash to rope, styled hair to baldness, fine clothes to sackcloth, beauty to branding. Systematic reversal.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:16–24

Calvin reads the luxury catalogue not as misogyny but as economic critique: these items represent wealth that should have been used for justice. The sin is not wearing jewellery but wearing it while the poor starve.

3:24

Calvin notes the five reversals mirror the five senses: smell (stench), touch (rope), sight (baldness), covering (sackcloth), appearance (branding). Judgment addresses the whole sensory experience of luxury.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:18–23

The NET provides archaeological identification for many items: crescent necklaces (Ishtar-moon symbols found at multiple sites), ankle chains (attested in Mesopotamian art). The list is historically grounded, not imaginative.

3:24

The NET notes "branding" (ki) may refer to the branding of slaves or prisoners of war. The free women of Zion become branded captives.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
3:16–24

Oswalt connects the women’s critique back to the leadership critique (vv.1–15): the women are not separate from the system but part of it. Their luxury is funded by the same injustice that crushes the poor.

3:24

Oswalt reads the five reversals as the five stages of siege and exile: loss of luxury, loss of freedom, loss of beauty, loss of identity, loss of dignity.

Childs Notes

childs
3:16–26

Childs notes the canonical function: the luxury-to-destitution reversal foreshadows the exile. What Jerusalem wore voluntarily as decoration, she will wear involuntarily as captivity.

3:26

Childs reads "she will sit on the ground" as anticipating Lam 1:1 ("How deserted lies the city") and Lam 2:10 (elders sitting in the dust). Isaiah’s prophecy becomes Lamentations’ reality.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
