Isaiah 20

Isaiah’s Prophetic Sign-Act: Three Years Barefoot

Verses 1–3 — “Go, Walk Naked and Barefoot”
123

Hebrew Word Study

עָרוֹם ʿārōm — naked / stripped: "Go and loose the sackcloth from your waist and take your sandals off your feet. And he did so, walking naked (ʿārōm) and barefoot" (v.2). In the year Sargon II’s commander captured Ashdod (711 BC), God commands Isaiah to walk stripped and barefoot as a prophetic sign-act. Three years of public humiliation (v.3). The prophet’s body becomes the message: this is how the prisoners of Egypt and Cush will be led away by Assyria — naked, barefoot, buttocks bared (v.4).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
20:1–2

"The LORD said: Go and loose the sackcloth. Isaiah walked naked and barefoot." The most extreme sign-act in the prophets. Isaiah’s body becomes a billboard for exile.

20:3

"Just as my servant Isaiah has gone stripped and barefoot for three years as a sign and portent against Egypt and Cush." THREE YEARS. Not a one-day demonstration but years of embodied prophecy.

Verses 4–6 — “Where Then Is Our Hope?”
456

Hebrew Word Study

תִּקְוָה tiqwâ — hope / expectation: "Then those who lived in this coastland will say, ‘See what has happened to those we relied on, those we fled to for help and deliverance from the king of Assyria! How then can we escape?’" (v.6). The message: don’t trust Egypt and Cush for protection against Assyria. Those allies will be led away as captives themselves. If you trust human alliances instead of God, your hope (tiqwâ) will be stripped naked like your prophet. Six verses. The shortest oracle in Isaiah. The most personally costly.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
20:4–5

"The king of Assyria will lead away stripped and barefoot the Egyptians and Cushites. Those who trusted in Cush and boasted in Egypt will be dismayed." The allies fail. The nations Judah hoped would save them are themselves captured.

20:6

"How then can we escape?" The rhetorical question that ends the oracle. If Egypt and Cush can’t survive Assyria, what chance does Judah have — unless God himself is the refuge?
