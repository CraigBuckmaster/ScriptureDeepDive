Isaiah 20

Isaiah Walks Naked as a Sign

Verses 1–3 — “Go, Strip Off the Sackcloth and Take Off Your Sandals”
123

Hebrew Word Study

עָרוֹם ʿārōm — naked / stripped: "Go, strip off the sackcloth from your body and take off your sandals." And he did so, going around stripped (ʿārōm) and barefoot. Then the LORD said: "Just as my servant Isaiah has gone stripped and barefoot for three years, as a sign and portent against Egypt and Cush" (vv.2–3). In the year the Assyrian army commander came to Ashdod and captured it (711 BC), God commands Isaiah to walk naked for three years as a living sign that Egypt and Cush will be led away as captives.

Context

The date marker (the fall of Ashdod, 711 BC) is historically confirmed by Sargon II’s own records. The three-year naked prophecy is the most extreme prophetic sign-act in Isaiah. The nakedness represents the humiliation of prisoners of war, who were stripped and marched barefoot. Isaiah embodies the fate of those who trust in Egypt: they will be stripped, exposed, ashamed. The sign is directed against Judah’s temptation to ally with Egypt, not against Egypt itself.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Sam 6:14David danced before the LORD wearing a linen ephod — another example of prophetic nakedness/minimal clothing.
  • Mic 1:8Therefore I will weep and wail; I will go about barefoot and naked — Micah’s parallel sign-act.
  • Jer 13:1–11The linen belt buried at the Euphrates — another extreme prophetic sign-act.
  • Ezek 4:1–17Ezekiel’s 430 days lying on his side — the most extreme sign-act in the OT, paralleling Isaiah’s 3 years.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:2–3

Calvin reads the nakedness as prophetic theatre: God uses Isaiah’s body to communicate what words alone cannot convey. The embarrassment is the point — it matches the embarrassment Egypt’s allies will feel.

20:3

Calvin notes the THREE YEARS: this is not a moment of ecstasy but a sustained commitment. Isaiah lived the message every day for 1,095 days.

Verses 4–6 — “How Then Can We Escape?”
456

Hebrew Word Study

מִפְלָט pālāṭ — escape / refuge: "How then can we escape (pālāṭ)?" (v.6). Egypt and Cush will be led away as prisoners: young and old, naked and barefoot, with buttocks bared — to Egypt’s shame (v.4). Those who trusted in Cush and boasted in Egypt will be dismayed (v.5). The coastland people (Judah?) will say: "See what has happened to those we relied on! How then can we escape?" (v.6).

Context

The rhetorical question "How can we escape?" (v.6) is the oracle’s payoff: if Egypt (the superpower you trusted) cannot save itself, how will it save you? The answer is: it cannot. The only escape is trust in God, not in military alliances. The "buttocks bared" detail (v.4) is not gratuitous but historically accurate: Assyrian reliefs depict prisoners marched naked as humiliation.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 30:1–5Woe to those who go down to Egypt for help and do not look to the Holy One of Israel — the same anti-alliance theology.
  • Isa 31:1Woe to those who go down to Egypt for help, who rely on horses — the repeated warning.
  • Heb 2:3How shall we escape if we ignore so great a salvation? — the NT version of the escape-question.
  • Ps 20:7Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the LORD our God.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:4–6

Calvin applies the lesson broadly: every human alliance that replaces trust in God will fail. The question "how can we escape?" is answered not by finding a better ally but by returning to the LORD.

20:6

Calvin: the rhetorical question is both devastating and hopeful. Devastating because Egypt cannot save; hopeful because the question opens the door to the only answer: God.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:4

The NET notes Assyrian reliefs from Nineveh and Nimrud depict exactly the scene described: prisoners of war stripped naked and marched in procession. The description is historically documented.

20:6

The NET observes "coastland" (iy) typically refers to Mediterranean coastal settlements. The people asking "how can we escape?" are Judah’s allies and neighbours who also relied on Egypt’s protection.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
20:4–6

Oswalt reads the rhetorical question as the chapter’s theological centre: the collapse of human security-systems creates the space for divine trust. Every failed alliance is an invitation to faith.

20:6

Oswalt: "how then can we escape?" implies its own answer: you cannot escape by looking horizontally (to Egypt). You can only escape by looking vertically (to God).

Childs Notes

childs
20:4–6

Childs reads the oracle as a canonical parable on misplaced trust: the nations-oracles section repeatedly warns against alliances that bypass God. The pattern is consistent from ch.7 (Ahaz trusting Assyria) through ch.20 (Judah trusting Egypt).

20:6

Childs notes the question "how can we escape?" remains open in Isaiah — it is answered fully only in Second Isaiah’s proclamation of the Servant who provides the escape (ch.53).

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
