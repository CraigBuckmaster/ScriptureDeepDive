Isaiah 27

Leviathan Slain: The Isaiah Apocalypse Concludes

Verses 1–6 — “In That Day the LORD Will Slay Leviathan”
123456

Hebrew Word Study

לִוְיָתָן liwyāṭān — Leviathan / sea-dragon: "In that day, the LORD will punish with his sword — his fierce, great and powerful sword — Leviathan (liwyāṭān) the gliding serpent, Leviathan the coiling serpent; he will slay the monster of the sea" (v.1). The primordial chaos-monster of Job 41 and Psalm 74:14. In the Isaiah Apocalypse’s climax, God kills Leviathan. The serpent of Genesis 3, the chaos-dragon of creation mythology, the power behind the empires — slain. Then: "In that day: Sing about a fruitful vineyard!" (v.2). The vineyard of ch.5 (which produced bad fruit) is restored. "I, the LORD, watch over it; I water it continually. I guard it day and night" (v.3). God is no longer the destroying farmer (5:5–6) but the protecting gardener (27:3).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:1

"The LORD will slay Leviathan the gliding serpent, Leviathan the coiling serpent; he will slay the monster of the sea." The ultimate enemy destroyed. Revelation 12–13’s dragon draws on this: the ancient serpent, the chaos-monster, finally killed.

27:2–3

"Sing about a fruitful vineyard! I, the LORD, watch over it; I water it continually." The vineyard of ch.5 redeemed. What produced wild grapes now bears fruit. God’s gardening succeeds the second time.

27:6

"Israel will bud and blossom and fill all the world with fruit." The vineyard’s global harvest. Israel’s fruit fills the earth. The mission theology of the vineyard: planted locally, bearing fruit globally.

Verses 7–13 — “In That Day a Great Trumpet Will Sound”
78910111213

Hebrew Word Study

שׁוֹפָר shōfār — trumpet / ram’s horn: "In that day a great trumpet (shōfār) will sound. Those who were perishing in Assyria and those who were exiled in Egypt will come and worship the LORD on the holy mountain in Jerusalem" (v.13). God’s discipline of Israel was measured: "By warfare and exile you contend with her — with his fierce blast he drives her out, as on a day the east wind blows" (v.8). The discipline produces atonement: "By this Jacob’s guilt is atoned for" (v.9). The altars will be demolished (v.9). Then the great ingathering: trumpet sounds, the scattered come from Assyria and Egypt, and worship in Jerusalem (v.13). The Isaiah Apocalypse ends with a trumpet and a pilgrimage.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:8–9

"By warfare and exile you contend with her. By this, Jacob’s guilt is atoned for." Exile as atonement. The suffering is not pointless but purgative. The altar-stones are crushed — idolatry finally ended.

27:12–13

"In that day the LORD will thresh from the Euphrates to the Wadi of Egypt. You will be gathered up one by one. A great trumpet will sound." The ingathering: individual, deliberate, trumpet-announced. Jesus echoes this in Matthew 24:31: "He will send his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather his elect."
