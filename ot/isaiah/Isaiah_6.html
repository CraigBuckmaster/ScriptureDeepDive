Isaiah 6

Holy, Holy, Holy — Isaiah’s Call

Verses 1–7 — “I Saw the Lord, High and Exalted”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

קָדוֹשׁ qādōsh — holy: "Holy, holy, holy (qādōsh) is the LORD Almighty; the whole earth is full of his glory" (v.3). In the year that King Uzziah died (740 BC), Isaiah sees the Lord seated on a throne, high and exalted, the train of his robe filling the temple. Seraphim above him, each with six wings: two covering faces, two covering feet, two for flying. The trisagion: "Holy, holy, holy!" The doorposts shake; the temple fills with smoke. Isaiah: "Woe to me! I am ruined! I am a man of unclean lips" (v.5). A seraph touches his lips with a live coal from the altar: "Your guilt is taken away; your sin atoned for" (v.7).

Context

Uzziah’s death (740 BC) ended 52 years of stability and prosperity. The vision occurs at the political transition-point: the human king dies; the divine King is revealed. The trisagion (triple "holy") is the only attribute of God repeated three times in the Bible — not "love, love, love" or "justice, justice, justice" but "holy, holy, holy." The seraphim’s covered faces and feet indicate that even heavenly beings cannot bear the full weight of divine holiness. Isaiah’s "woe to me" (v.5) uses the same word (hoy) as the six woes of ch.5: the prophet pronounces the seventh woe on himself.

Cross-Reference

  • Rev 4:8Day and night they never stop saying: "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord God Almighty" — John sees the same throne room.
  • Ex 3:5–6Take off your sandals; the place where you are standing is holy ground — Moses’ parallel theophany.
  • Luke 5:8Go away from me, Lord; I am a sinful man! — Peter’s response mirrors Isaiah’s.
  • Heb 12:29Our God is a consuming fire — the coal-and-altar imagery of Isa 6 generalised.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1–2

In the year that Uzziah died, Isaiah saw the Lord. The human throne empties; the divine throne is revealed. Seraphim attend: six wings each.

6:3

"Holy, holy, holy." The triple repetition: the only divine attribute stated three times. Holiness is the foundational attribute from which all others derive.

6:5

"Woe to me! I am ruined! I am a man of unclean lips." The seventh woe — the prophet turns the genre on himself. Encounter with holiness produces not confidence but collapse.

6:6–7

The coal from the altar touches his lips: "Your guilt is taken away." Cleansing is painful (burning coal), sacrificial (from the altar), and immediate ("is taken away" — perfect tense).

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1–3

Calvin reads the throne vision as the definitive revelation of God’s character: before Isaiah can prophesy, he must see who God IS. Mission flows from vision. The call comes after the encounter, not before.

6:5–7

Calvin notes the sequence: vision of God → awareness of sin → divine cleansing → commission. This is the pattern for all genuine calling: you cannot serve until you have been undone and remade.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:2

The NET notes that "seraphim" (seraphim) literally means "burning ones." The root saraph means "to burn." These are fire-beings attending the fire-holy God.

6:3

The NET observes the threefold repetition (trisagion) is superlative in Hebrew: not just "holy" but "most supremely holy." The repetition exceeds normal Hebrew grammar, which achieves superlative by doubling. Tripling goes beyond.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
6:1–7

Oswalt reads the throne vision as Isaiah’s commissioning theophany, parallel to Moses’ burning bush and Ezekiel’s chariot. Each major prophet encounters God directly before being sent. The encounter authenticates the mission.

6:5

Oswalt notes Isaiah’s "unclean lips" is specifically relevant: he is about to be commissioned as a SPEAKER for God. The organ that was unclean is the organ that is cleansed and then deployed.

Childs Notes

childs
6:1–7

Childs reads the throne vision as the canonical key to the entire book: Isaiah’s God is supremely holy, and this holiness drives everything — judgment (chs.1–39), comfort (chs.40–55), and renewal (chs.56–66). "The Holy One of Israel" appears 26 times in Isaiah and only 6 times elsewhere in the OT.

6:3

Childs notes that "the whole earth is full of his glory" is the seraphim’s proclamation, not Isaiah’s observation. The earth is ALREADY full of glory; the problem is human blindness to it.

Verses 8–13 — “Here Am I. Send Me!”
8910111213

Hebrew Word Study

שְׁלָחֵנִי shəlāḥēnî — send me!: "Then I heard the voice of the Lord saying, “Whom shall I send? And who will go for us?” And I said, “Here am I. Send me (shəlāḥēnî)!”" (v.8). But the commission is devastating: make the hearts calloused, ears dull, eyes shut — lest they see, hear, understand, and be healed (vv.9–10). "How long, Lord?" Until cities are ruined, houses left empty, the land utterly forsaken (v.11). Yet: "the holy seed is the stump" (v.13). The devastation is not total; a stump survives.

Context

The commission is paradoxical: Isaiah is sent to PREVENT understanding. The purpose clause of v.10 ("otherwise they might see") is the hardest text in Isaiah — Jesus quotes it in Matt 13:14–15 to explain why he teaches in parables, and Paul quotes it in Acts 28:26–27 as the last word of Acts. The "holy seed is the stump" (v.13b) is the remnant theology in its most compressed form: from the stump of judgment, the Branch of 4:2 and 11:1 will grow. The stump is both the end of one era and the beginning of another.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 13:14–15Jesus quotes Isa 6:9–10 to explain why he speaks in parables — some hearts are already calloused.
  • Acts 28:26–27Paul quotes Isa 6:9–10 as the LAST words of Acts — the book ends with Isaiah’s warning.
  • John 12:39–41John quotes Isa 6:10 and says "Isaiah saw Jesus’ glory" — the throne room was a vision of Christ.
  • Rom 11:8God gave them a spirit of stupor — Paul’s gloss on Isa 6:9–10 applied to Israel’s partial hardening.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:8

"Here am I. Send me!" The volunteer: not drafted but offering. The courage is breathtaking given what follows.

6:9–10

The hardening commission: make hearts fat, ears heavy, eyes shut. The preaching will HARDEN, not soften. Jesus quotes this in Matt 13:14–15.

6:11–12

"How long?" Until complete devastation. The timeline is not "until revival" but "until ruin."

6:13

"The holy seed is the stump." The last clause: after the tree falls, the stump remains. From it, everything will regrow. This is the Bible’s most compressed statement of death-and-resurrection hope.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:8

Calvin marvels at the "send me" response: Isaiah has just been told his ministry will harden hearts, and he STILL volunteers. The call is not to success but to faithfulness.

6:9–10

Calvin reads the hardening as judicial: God does not CAUSE unbelief but confirms the unbelief already present. The preaching hardens those who have already chosen to resist. The light blinds those who have already closed their eyes.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:8

The NET notes the plural "us" in "Who will go for US?" — the divine council (cf. Gen 1:26; 1 Kgs 22:19–22). God deliberates in the heavenly court.

6:13

The NET observes "the holy seed is the stump" connects to 4:3 ("called holy") and forward to 11:1 ("a shoot will come up from the stump of Jesse"). The stump-theology is Isaiah’s signature hope-image.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
6:8–13

Oswalt reads the entire commission as the book’s explanation for why Israel did not repent despite 40 years of prophetic ministry: the hardening was part of the divine plan. Isaiah was faithful; the people were hardened; but the stump survived.

6:13

Oswalt: the holy seed in the stump is the most important theological sentence in First Isaiah. It guarantees that judgment is not the end. From apparent death, life will emerge.

Childs Notes

childs
6:8–13

Childs reads the commission as programmatic for the entire 66-chapter book: chs.1–39 are the hardening; chs.40–66 are the restoration. The stump is the hinge between the two halves.

6:13

Childs notes the canonical significance: John 12:41 says Isaiah "saw Jesus’ glory." The NT reads the throne room as a christophany. The holiness Isaiah encountered was the holiness of the pre-incarnate Christ.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
