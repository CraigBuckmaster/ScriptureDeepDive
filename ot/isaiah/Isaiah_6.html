Isaiah 6

Holy, Holy, Holy: Isaiah’s Call

Verses 1–7 — “I Saw the Lord, High and Exalted”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

קָדוֹשׁ qādōsh — holy: "In the year that King Uzziah died, I saw the Lord, high and exalted, seated on a throne; and the train of his robe filled the temple. Above him were seraphim, each with six wings. And they were calling to one another: ‘Holy, holy, holy (qādōsh, qādōsh, qādōsh) is the LORD Almighty; the whole earth is full of his glory’" (vv.1–3). THE throne-room vision. THE call narrative. The doorposts shake; the temple fills with smoke (v.4). Isaiah’s response: "Woe to me! I am ruined! For I am a man of unclean lips, and I live among a people of unclean lips, and my eyes have seen the King, the LORD Almighty" (v.5). A seraph flies to him with a live coal from the altar, touches his lips: "See, this has touched your lips; your guilt is taken away and your sin atoned for" (vv.6–7). Atonement by fire. Purification by contact with the altar’s sacrifice.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1

"In the year that King Uzziah died, I saw the Lord." The earthly king dies; the heavenly King is seen. Uzziah’s death (c.740 BC) creates the vacancy that reveals God’s throne.

6:2–3

"Holy, holy, holy is the LORD Almighty." The trisagion: the only attribute of God repeated three times. Not "love, love, love" or "power, power, power" but "holy, holy, holy." Holiness is the attribute that governs all others. Revelation 4:8 repeats this exact scene.

6:5

"Woe to me! I am ruined! For I am a man of unclean lips." Isaiah pronounces the seventh woe — on himself. After six woes on others (ch.5), the vision reveals that the prophet is also contaminated. The "unclean lips" connect to ch.1’s problem: Israel’s speech is corrupt.

6:6–7

"A seraph flew to me with a live coal from the altar. ‘Your guilt is taken away.’" The coal comes from the altar — the place of sacrifice. Atonement is not abstract but physical: fire from the sacrifice touches the unclean lips. The priest is purified by the sacrifice.

Verses 8–13 — “Here Am I. Send Me!”
8910111213

Hebrew Word Study

הִנְנִי hinnənî — here I am / behold me: "Then I heard the voice of the Lord saying, ‘Whom shall I send? And who will go for us?’ And I said, ‘Here am I (hinnənî). Send me!’" (v.8). The commission that follows is devastating: "Go and tell this people: ‘Be ever hearing, but never understanding; be ever seeing, but never perceiving.’ Make the heart of this people calloused; make their ears dull and close their eyes" (vv.9–10). Jesus quotes this passage more than any other OT text (Matt 13:14–15; Mark 4:12; John 12:40; Acts 28:26–27). Isaiah asks: "How long, Lord?" Answer: "Until the cities lie ruined and the fields are devastated" (vv.11–12). But a stump remains: "the holy seed is the stump" (v.13). The remnant theology: destruction is not total; a seed survives.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:8

"Whom shall I send? And who will go for us?" The "us" echoes Genesis 1:26 — the divine council. Isaiah volunteers: "Here am I. Send me!" The purified prophet offers himself. No negotiation, no conditions, no Moses-style objections.

6:9–10

"Be ever hearing, but never understanding." The hardening commission: Isaiah’s preaching will not convert but calcify. Jesus quotes this to explain why he teaches in parables (Matt 13:14–15). Paul quotes it as his final word to Rome’s Jews (Acts 28:26–27). The most-quoted Isaiah passage in the NT.

6:11–12

"How long, Lord? Until the cities lie ruined." The commission has a terminus: total devastation. Isaiah’s ministry is not for success but for faithfulness.

6:13

"But as the terebinth and oak leave stumps when they are cut down, so the holy seed will be the stump in the land." The remnant: the stump that survives the axe. The holy seed. From this stump, the Branch of ch.4 and ch.11 will grow.
