Isaiah 57

The Contrite and Lowly in Spirit

Verses 1–13 — “The Righteous Perish and No One Cares”
12345678910111213

Hebrew Word Study

צַדִּיק ṣaddîq — righteous: "The righteous (ṣaddîq) perish, and no one takes it to heart; the devout are taken away, and no one understands that the righteous are taken away to be spared from evil" (v.1). The righteous die unnoticed — but their death is protection, not punishment. Then a fierce condemnation of idolatry: child sacrifice in the valleys (v.5), libations poured to smooth stones in the ravines (v.6), adultery on every high hill (v.7). "Whom have you so dreaded and feared that you have not been true to me? Is it not because I have long been silent that you do not fear me?" (v.11). God silence was misread as absence.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
57:1–2

"The righteous perish and no one cares. They are taken away to be spared from evil." The paradox: the righteous death is mercy. God removes the faithful before the worst arrives.

57:5–6

"You sacrifice your children in the ravines. You pour out libations to smooth stones." Child sacrifice and river-stone worship — the darkest idolatry. The smooth stones are chosen by human aesthetics, not divine command.

57:11

"Is it not because I have long been silent that you do not fear me?" God silence during idolatry was not approval but patience. They mistook restraint for indifference.

Verses 14–21 — “I Live in a High and Holy Place, but Also with the Contrite”
1415161718192021

Hebrew Word Study

דַּכְּא dakkāʾ — crushed / contrite: "I live in a high and holy place, but also with the one who is contrite (dakkāʾ) and lowly in spirit, to revive the spirit of the lowly and to revive the heart of the contrite" (v.15). THE verse of divine paradox: the highest God with the lowest people. Transcendence AND immanence in one sentence. "Build up, build up, prepare the road! Remove every obstacle out of the way of my people" (v.14). God creates peace for the far and the near (v.19). "But the wicked are like the tossing sea. There is no peace for the wicked" (vv.20–21) — the second section-divider (cf. 48:22).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
57:15

"I live in a high and holy place, but also with the one who is contrite and lowly in spirit." The most important verse in Third Isaiah. God inhabits two addresses: the highest heaven and the lowest heart. He goes down to go high.

57:19

"Peace, peace, to those far and near." Ephesians 2:17: "He came and preached peace to you who were far away and peace to those who were near." Paul reads this as Christ preaching to Gentiles (far) and Jews (near).

57:20–21

"The wicked are like the tossing sea. There is no peace for the wicked." The second section-ending refrain (cf. 48:22). The sea that cannot rest: restlessness as the defining condition of the wicked.
