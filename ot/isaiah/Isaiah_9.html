Isaiah 9

For to Us a Child Is Born

Verses 1–7 — “The People Walking in Darkness Have Seen a Great Light”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

פֶלֶא peleʾ — wonder / marvel: "He will be called Wonderful Counsellor (peleʾ), Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace" (v.6). The people in darkness see a great light — in Galilee, the first region conquered by Assyria (v.1). Jesus’ ministry begins there (Matt 4:15–16). Yokes broken, boots burned (vv.4–5). "For to us a child is born, to us a son is given, and the government will be on his shoulders" (v.6). Four throne-names: Wonderful Counsellor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace. Of the greatness of his government and peace there will be no end (v.7).

Context

The four throne-names follow the pattern of Egyptian coronation protocol where a new pharaoh received multiple names at enthronement. "Mighty God" (ʾēl gibbōr) is unambiguously divine — used of YHWH in 10:21. The child who bears this name is therefore more than human: he exercises divine authority. The "great light" in Galilee (v.1) specifically names Zebulun and Naphtali — the first territories lost to Assyria (2 Kgs 15:29). The first to suffer will be the first to be restored. Jesus’ ministry in Capernaum (Matt 4:13–16) fulfils this geographically.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 4:15–16Matthew quotes Isa 9:1–2 for Jesus’ Galilean ministry: "the people living in darkness have seen a great light."
  • Luke 1:32–33He will be great and will be called the Son of the Most High; his kingdom will never end — Gabriel echoes Isa 9:7.
  • Luke 2:10–11I bring you good news of great joy: a Saviour has been born — the birth announcement echoes "to us a child is born."
  • Rev 11:15The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord — the fulfilment of the endless government of 9:7.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:1–2

Galilee: first conquered, first restored. The great light shines in the darkest place. Matt 4:15–16 quotes this for Jesus’ ministry.

9:4–5

Yokes broken, boots burned. The instruments of oppression become fuel for celebration.

9:6

"For to us a child is born." THE Christmas text. Four names: Wonderful Counsellor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace. Each name is a throne.

9:7

"Of the greatness of his government and peace there will be no end." On David’s throne. With justice and righteousness. Forever.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:6

Calvin insists "Mighty God" is not hyperbole but literal: the child exercises divine power. Calvin rejects any reading that reduces the four names to human attributes. This is God incarnate, prophesied 700 years before Bethlehem.

9:7

Calvin reads "no end" as both temporal (forever) and spatial (unbounded). The kingdom expands not by military force but by justice and righteousness. The zeal of the LORD accomplishes this.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:6

The NET notes the four names are coronation names, not personal names — titles bestowed at enthronement describing the king’s governing character. "Mighty God" (ʾēl gibbōr) is applied to YHWH in 10:21, confirming its divine weight.

9:1

The NET identifies Zebulun and Naphtali as the territories ceded to Tiglath-Pileser III in 733 BC (2 Kgs 15:29). The prophecy promises their specific restoration.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
9:1–7

Oswalt reads the passage as the expansion of the Immanuel promise: 7:14 gave a name (God with us); 9:6–7 gives a programme (Wonderful Counsellor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace). The Messiah is not just present but governing.

9:6

Oswalt: "Mighty God" is the interpretive crux. If this title is genuinely divine (as 10:21 confirms), then the child of 9:6 IS God — the clearest pre-incarnation prophecy in the OT.

Childs Notes

childs
9:1–7

Childs reads the passage canonically as the climax of the Immanuel section (chs.7–9): darkness (8:22) → light (9:1–2) → the messianic king (9:6–7). The canonical arrangement is a gospel narrative in miniature.

9:6–7

Childs notes the four names collectively describe a king who combines human and divine attributes: counsellor (wisdom), mighty God (power), eternal father (permanence), prince of peace (shalom). No human king fulfils all four.

Verses 8–21 — “The LORD’s Hand Is Still Upraised”
89101112131415161718192021

Hebrew Word Study

אַף ʾaf — anger: "Yet for all this, his anger (ʾaf) is not turned away, his hand is still upraised" (vv.12, 17, 21). The refrain rings four times (9:12, 17, 21; 10:4). Despite everything, judgment continues. The people do not return to the one who struck them (v.13). Leaders mislead; God cuts off head and tail, palm and reed (vv.14–16). Wickedness burns like a fire (v.18). No one spares their brother; they eat the flesh of their own arm (v.20). Ephraim against Manasseh, both against Judah (v.21).

Context

The four-fold refrain "his hand is still upraised" (9:12, 17, 21; 10:4) structures a poem of escalating judgment that many scholars believe was originally a single unit spanning 9:8–10:4. The internal civil war (Ephraim vs Manasseh) reflects the political chaos of the northern kingdom’s final decades (six kings in 20 years, four assassinated). The fire-and-cannibalism imagery (vv.18–20) anticipates the siege conditions of Lamentations.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 15:8–31The rapid succession of assassinated kings in Israel — the political chaos behind Isa 9:8–21.
  • Lev 26:14–39The escalating covenant curses: disobedience → disease → defeat → exile. Isaiah describes the same escalation.
  • Amos 4:6–11"Yet you have not returned to me" (repeated five times) — the same refrain-of-rejection pattern.
  • Heb 10:31It is a dreadful thing to fall into the hands of the living God — the theology of the "upraised hand."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:12–13

The refrain: "His hand is still upraised." Judgment is not over. And: "The people have not returned." The opportunity for repentance remains open.

9:14–16

God cuts off head and tail. The leaders who mislead are judged; but so are those who follow them. Complicity is not innocence.

9:18–20

Wickedness burns like fire; no one spares their brother; they eat their own arm. Internal destruction precedes external conquest.

9:21

Ephraim against Manasseh, both against Judah. The family that should protect each other destroys each other.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:12–13

Calvin reads the repeated refrain as God’s patience in judgment: the hand is "STILL upraised" — not yet fallen. Each iteration is another chance to repent before the final blow.

9:18–20

Calvin: the self-consumption (eating their own arm) is the ultimate image of a society that has turned on itself. When justice disappears, people become predators of each other.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:12

The NET notes the refrain appears four times, creating a four-stanza poem of judgment. Each stanza escalates: pride (v.9) → enemies (v.11) → fire (v.18) → civil war (v.20).

9:20

The NET observes "eat the flesh of their own arm" may be metaphorical (consuming their own resources) or literal (siege cannibalism). Either reading is devastating.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
9:8–21

Oswalt reads the four-refrain poem as the counterweight to 9:1–7: the messianic hope is real, but so is the present judgment. The two coexist in prophetic tension.

9:12

Oswalt: "his hand is still upraised" means the series of judgments is a programme, not random events. Each blow has a purpose: to provoke return. The hand is raised, not closed.

Childs Notes

childs
9:8–21

Childs reads the four-stanza judgment poem as the canonical bridge between the messianic promise (9:1–7) and the Assyrian instrument passage (10:5–19). The hope is real; the judgment is also real; both are necessary.

9:12

Childs notes the refrain’s persistence: four times God announces that judgment continues. The repetition is itself a form of patience — the declaration keeps the door open for response.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
