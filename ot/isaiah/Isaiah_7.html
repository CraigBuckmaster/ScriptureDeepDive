Isaiah 7

The Immanuel Sign

Verses 1–12 — “Ask the LORD Your God for a Sign”
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

אוֹת ʾōṭ — sign: "Ask the LORD your God for a sign (ʾōṭ)" (v.11). The Syro-Ephraimite crisis (735 BC): Syria and northern Israel march on Jerusalem to depose Ahaz and install a puppet king (v.6). Isaiah meets Ahaz at the aqueduct with his son Shear-Jashub ("a remnant will return"). Message: these two smouldering stubs of firewood will fail (v.4). God offers ANY sign — as deep as Sheol, as high as heaven (v.11). Ahaz refuses, cloaking cowardice in piety: "I will not test the LORD" (v.12).

Context

The Syro-Ephraimite crisis (735–732 BC) is the political catalyst for chs.7–12. Rezin of Syria and Pekah of Israel formed an anti-Assyrian coalition and attacked Judah when Ahaz refused to join. Ahaz secretly appealed to Assyria for help (2 Kgs 16:7–9) — the very act Isaiah warns against. Ahaz’s refusal of the sign is not humility but defiance: he has already decided to trust Assyria and does not want God to interfere with his political strategy. The location at "the aqueduct of the Upper Pool" (v.3) is strategically significant: Ahaz is inspecting Jerusalem’s water supply in preparation for siege.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 16:5–9Rezin and Pekah attack Jerusalem; Ahaz appeals to Tiglath-Pileser — the historical narrative behind Isa 7.
  • Deut 6:16Do not put the LORD your God to the test — the verse Ahaz misapplies. Testing God and asking for a OFFERED sign are different things.
  • Matt 1:22–23All this took place to fulfil what the Lord said through Isaiah: "The virgin will conceive" — the NT application.
  • Rom 8:31If God is for us, who can be against us? — the theology Ahaz rejected.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:3–4

Calvin notes Isaiah brings his son to the meeting: the child’s name (Shear-Jashub = a remnant will return) is simultaneously a promise and a warning. The remnant WILL return; but only a remnant.

7:10–12

Calvin reads Ahaz’s refusal as the worst form of unbelief: it dresses rebellion in the clothing of reverence. He QUOTES Scripture ("do not test") to REFUSE God’s offer.

7:11

The NET observes the extraordinary range: "as deep as Sheol, as high as heaven." God offers any sign in the entire cosmos. Ahaz’s refusal rejects infinite divine generosity.

Childs Notes

childs

Childs reads the encounter as the narrative origin of the Immanuel prophecy: the sign is given not because Ahaz asked but because he REFUSED to ask. God’s grace overrides human resistance.

Verses 13–25 — “The Virgin Will Conceive and Give Birth to a Son: Immanuel”
13141516171819202122232425

Hebrew Word Study

עִמָּנוּ אֵל ʿimmānū ʾēl — God with us: "The virgin will conceive and give birth to a son, and will call him Immanuel (ʿimmānū ʾēl — God with us)" (v.14). Because Ahaz refused a sign, God gives one anyway. Before the child knows right from wrong, the two threatening kings will be gone (v.16). But Assyria — the power Ahaz trusted — will flood Judah (vv.17–20). Curds and honey will be the diet of survivors (v.22). Farmland becomes thorns and briers (vv.23–25).

Context

The Immanuel prophecy is the most debated text in Isaiah. The Hebrew ʿalmâ (v.14) means "young woman" (not specifically "virgin"), but the Septuagint translated it parthenos (virgin), which Matthew 1:23 quotes. The immediate referent may be Isaiah’s own wife, or Ahaz’s wife, with the child serving as a timeline: before the child matures, the crisis will pass. The ultimate fulfilment in Jesus’ virgin birth does not cancel the immediate context but transcends it. The name Immanuel ("God with us") is the counter to Ahaz’s faithlessness: even when the king rejects God’s presence, God is WITH his people anyway.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 1:22–23The virgin will conceive and give birth to a son, and they will call him Immanuel — Matthew’s explicit quotation.
  • Isa 8:8Its outspread wings will cover the breadth of your land, Immanuel — the name recurs as both address and identity.
  • Isa 9:6–7For to us a child is born — the Immanuel promise expanded into the four-name messianic oracle.
  • Matt 28:20I am with you always, to the very end of the age — Jesus as the ultimate Immanuel.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:14

Calvin reads the dual fulfilment: the immediate sign (a child born in Ahaz’s time) and the ultimate fulfilment (the virgin birth of Christ). The lesser sign points to the greater.

7:17–20

Calvin notes the irony: Ahaz trusted Assyria to save him from Syria and Israel. Assyria does remove them — then turns on Judah. The helper becomes the destroyer. Trusting the wrong saviour always backfires.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:14

The NET provides the fullest linguistic analysis: ʿalmâ means "young woman of marriageable age." The word does not inherently mean "virgin" (bethulah is more specific), but neither does it exclude virginity. The LXX’s choice of parthenos guided the NT application.

7:17–20

The NET notes "the king of Assyria" (v.17) is named explicitly for the first time. The unnamed "distant nation" of 5:26 is now identified. The threat has a name.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
7:14

Oswalt argues for a dual-fulfilment reading: the sign has immediate relevance (the Syro-Ephraimite crisis will pass quickly) and ultimate significance (the Messiah will literally be God-with-us). Both are real; neither cancels the other.

7:17–25

Oswalt reads the Assyrian flood as the first consequence of Ahaz’s faithlessness: the very power he chose over God becomes God’s instrument of judgment. The irony is structurally central to First Isaiah.

Childs Notes

childs
7:14

Childs insists on the canonical reading: whatever the original historical referent, the book of Isaiah as we have it presents Immanuel as a figure who transcends any single historical moment. The canonical meaning includes the Matthean application.

7:14–25

Childs notes the sign is simultaneously promise AND judgment: Immanuel means God is present (comfort), but God’s presence in an unfaithful nation means judgment. The name cuts both ways.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
