Isaiah 40

Comfort, Comfort My People

Verses 1–11 — “A Voice Calling in the Wilderness”
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

נַחֲמוּ naḥamū — comfort / console: "Comfort, comfort (naḥamū) my people, says your God. Speak tenderly to Jerusalem, and proclaim to her that her hard service has been completed, that her sin has been paid for, that she has received from the LORD’s hand double for all her sins" (vv.1–2). THE hinge of Isaiah. After 39 chapters of judgment, the music changes entirely. A voice cries: "In the wilderness prepare the way for the LORD; make straight in the desert a highway for our God" (v.3). All four Gospels quote this for John the Baptist (Matt 3:3; Mark 1:3; Luke 3:4–6; John 1:23). Every valley raised, every mountain lowered, the rough ground level, the glory of the LORD revealed (vv.4–5). "All people are like grass; their glory like wildflowers. The grass withers, the flower falls, but the word of our God endures forever" (vv.6–8). Peter quotes this in 1 Peter 1:24–25. "He tends his flock like a shepherd. He gathers the lambs in his arms and carries them close to his heart" (v.11).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
40:1–2

"Comfort, comfort my people." The doubled imperative: the comfort is urgent and emphatic. "Her sin has been paid for." The exile is atoning; its completion signals forgiveness.

40:3

"A voice of one calling: In the wilderness prepare the way for the LORD." All four Gospels apply this to John the Baptist. The wilderness highway: the same road-theology as 11:16; 19:23; 35:8.

40:6–8

"All people are like grass. The grass withers and the flowers fall, but the word of our God endures forever." Human transience versus divine permanence. 1 Peter 1:24–25 quotes this as the basis for the gospel’s reliability.

40:11

"He tends his flock like a shepherd; he gathers the lambs in his arms." After the cosmic highway, the intimate image: God carrying lambs. The shepherd who built the highway uses it to carry the vulnerable.

Verses 12–31 — “Those Who Hope in the LORD Will Soar on Wings Like Eagles”
1213141516171819202122232425262728293031

Hebrew Word Study

קָוָה qāwâ — to wait / to hope: "Those who hope (qāwâ) in the LORD will renew their strength. They will soar on wings like eagles; they will run and not grow weary, they will walk and not be faint" (v.31). The incomparability of God: "Who has measured the waters in the hollow of his hand?" (v.12). Nations are a drop in a bucket, dust on the scales (v.15). Lebanon’s forests are not enough fuel for his altar (v.16). "To whom will you compare me?" (v.25). "Lift up your eyes and look to the heavens: who created all these? He who brings out the starry host one by one and calls forth each of them by name" (v.26). "Do you not know? Have you not heard? The LORD is the everlasting God, the Creator of the ends of the earth. He will not grow tired or weary" (v.28). Then the climax: eagles, running, walking. The order descends from soaring to walking — the greatest miracle is sustained ordinary faithfulness.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
40:12–14

"Who has measured the waters in the hollow of his hand? Who has held the dust of the earth in a basket?" Five rhetorical questions establishing God’s incomparability. No advisor, no teacher, no consultant.

40:15–17

"Surely the nations are like a drop in a bucket. Before him all the nations are as nothing." The political superpowers that terrorised Israel for 39 chapters: a drop. Dust. Nothing.

40:26

"Lift up your eyes. He brings out the starry host one by one and calls them each by name." God as astronomer: not just creating the stars but naming each one. Individual attention on a cosmic scale.

40:31

"They will soar on wings like eagles; they will run and not grow weary; they will walk and not be faint." The famous three-stage promise. Note the descent: soaring → running → walking. The hardest miracle is the last: walking without fainting. Ordinary endurance requires supernatural power.
