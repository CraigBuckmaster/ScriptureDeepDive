Isaiah 22

The Valley of Vision: The Key of David

Verses 1–14 — “The Valley of Vision”
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

חִזָיוֹן ḥizzāyōn — vision: "A prophecy against the Valley of Vision (ḥizzāyōn)" (v.1). Jerusalem panics: everyone has fled to the rooftops (v.1). Rulers flee without using their bows; all captured without a fight (v.3). "Let me weep bitterly. Do not try to console me" (v.4). The Lord strips away Judah’s defences (v.8). They store water, count buildings, tear down houses to fortify walls (vv.9–11). "But you did not look to the One who planned it" (v.11). "Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die!" (v.13). "Till your dying day this sin will not be atoned for" (v.14).

Context

The "Valley of Vision" is Jerusalem (ironic: the city of prophetic vision is blind). The crisis may be Sennacherib’s invasion (701 BC). The detailed defence preparations (vv.9–11) describe real military engineering: water storage, building assessment, wall reinforcement. But: "you did not look to the One who made it long ago" (v.11). The preparations are competent but godless. "Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die" (v.13) is the slogan Paul quotes in 1 Corinthians 15:32 for those who deny the resurrection.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Cor 15:32If the dead are not raised, "let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die" — Paul quotes Isa 22:13 to expose the logic of unbelief.
  • 2 Chr 32:2–8Hezekiah’s preparations for Sennacherib’s siege — the historical event behind Isa 22:9–11.
  • Luke 12:19–20Eat, drink, be merry. But God said, "You fool! This very night your life will be demanded" — Jesus’ parable echoes Isa 22:13.
  • Isa 56:12"Let us drink our fill of beer! Tomorrow will be like today" — the same eat-drink-die cynicism repeated in Third Isaiah.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:1–3

The city panics. Rulers flee without fighting. Captured without a bow-shot. Cowardice, not courage.

22:8–11

Military preparations: water, walls, buildings assessed. But: "you did not look to the One who planned it." Competence without faith.

22:13

"Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die!" 1 Cor 15:32 quotes this. Nihilism as the alternative to faith.

22:14

"This sin will not be atoned for." The most severe verdict in First Isaiah. Not "will not be forgiven easily" but "will not be atoned AT ALL."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:11

Calvin reads "you did not look to the One who planned it" as the central indictment: the problem is not incompetent planning but godless planning. They did everything right except trust God.

22:13–14

Calvin: the eat-and-drink slogan is not hedonism but despair. When people stop believing in God’s future, the only option is present pleasure. The sin is not the feasting but the theology behind it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:1

The NET notes "Valley of Vision" is ironic: Jerusalem, the city where prophets see visions, is itself blind to God’s purposes.

22:11

The NET observes "the One who planned it long ago" refers to God as the author of history. Jerusalem’s crisis was not accidental but divinely orchestrated. The city’s failure is not in planning but in failing to recognise the Planner.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
22:1–14

Oswalt reads the Valley of Vision oracle as the most personally devastating in the nations-section: it targets not a foreign enemy but Jerusalem itself. The prophet’s own city is indicted.

22:13

Oswalt: "tomorrow we die" is the honest conclusion of a worldview without God. Isaiah does not dispute the logic; he disputes the premise. If God exists and resurrection is real, then eat-and-drink nihilism is irrational.

Childs Notes

childs
22:1–14

Childs reads the oracle as a canonical warning placed within the nations-section: Jerusalem is among the "nations" under judgment. Israel is not exempt from the standards applied to others.

22:14

Childs notes the severity: "this sin will not be atoned for" stands in tension with the atonement promise of 53:5–6. The canonical reader asks: what changes between ch.22 and ch.53? The answer: the Servant.

Verses 15–25 — “I Will Place on His Shoulder the Key of the House of David”
1516171819202122232425

Hebrew Word Study

מַפְתֵח maftēaḥ — key: "I will place on his shoulder the key (maftēaḥ) of the house of David; what he opens no one can shut, and what he shuts no one can open" (v.22). Shebna the steward is demoted: you who carved a tomb on the height will be hurled away (vv.15–19). Eliakim son of Hilkiah will replace him (v.20). The key of David on his shoulder (v.22). A peg driven into a firm place (v.23). But even the peg will give way (v.25). The key-of-David passage is quoted in Revelation 3:7 for Christ’s authority over the church.

Context

Shebna was a high official under Hezekiah (mentioned in 36:3). His offence appears to be self-aggrandisement: carving himself an elaborate tomb (v.16) while the nation faces crisis. Eliakim replaces him with the "key of David" — the authority to control access to the king. Revelation 3:7 applies this to Christ: "These are the words of him who is holy and true, who holds the key of David." The peg-that-gives-way (v.25) warns that even Eliakim’s authority is temporary; only Christ’s key-authority is permanent.

Cross-Reference

  • Rev 3:7He who holds the key of David. What he opens no one can shut; what he shuts no one can open — Christ claims Eliakim’s authority.
  • Matt 16:19I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven — Jesus gives Peter key-authority, echoing Isa 22:22.
  • Isa 36:3Eliakim son of Hilkiah the palace administrator — the same Eliakim, now in office during Sennacherib’s crisis.
  • Rev 1:18I hold the keys of death and Hades — Christ’s ultimate key-authority.

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase

Shebna: carving a tomb for himself while the nation crumbles. Self-serving leadership judged.

Eliakim: dressed in Shebna’s robe, given Shebna’s sash, entrusted with the key of David. "What he opens no one can shut." Rev 3:7 quotes this for Christ.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:22

Calvin reads the key-of-David as the authority of stewardship: Eliakim holds the key not as owner but as servant. Christ holds the key as both owner and servant. The key represents legitimate authority delegated and exercised.

22:25

Calvin notes the peg-warning: no human leader’s authority is permanent. Eliakim served well but eventually was replaced. Only Christ’s key-holding is "forever and ever" (Rev 3:7).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:22

The NET notes the key "on his shoulder" refers to the large wooden keys of ancient Near Eastern palaces, worn on the shoulder. The image is of authority physically carried — visible and weighty.

22:22

The NET observes the open/shut language echoes Job 12:14: "What he tears down cannot be rebuilt; those he imprisons cannot be released." The key-holder’s authority is absolute within its domain.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
22:15–25

Oswalt reads the Shebna/Eliakim contrast as a parable of leadership: self-serving leaders are removed; servant-leaders are installed. The key-of-David passage shows that God controls political appointments.

22:22

Oswalt: Revelation’s application of the key to Christ (3:7) is theologically sound: the steward’s authority points to the King’s authority. What Eliakim held temporarily, Christ holds eternally.

Childs Notes

childs
22:15–25

Childs reads the key-of-David passage as the canonical link between Isaiah and Revelation: Isaiah creates the metaphor (22:22); Revelation claims it for Christ (3:7). The canonical Bible treats these as a single theological statement.

22:22

Childs: the key-authority is simultaneously political (access to the king), theological (access to God), and eschatological (Christ’s final authority). All three layers are present in the canonical text.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
