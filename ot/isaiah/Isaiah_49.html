Isaiah 49

The Second Servant Song: A Light for the Gentiles

Verses 1–13 — “I Will Also Make You a Light for the Gentiles”
12345678910111213

Hebrew Word Study

אוֹר ʾōr — light: "I will also make you a light (ʾōr) for the Gentiles, that my salvation may reach to the ends of the earth" (v.6). THE SECOND SERVANT SONG (49:1–6). The servant speaks in first person: "Before I was born the LORD called me; from my mother’s womb he has spoken my name" (v.1). "He made my mouth like a sharpened sword" (v.2). The servant’s frustration: "I have laboured in vain; I have spent my strength for nothing" (v.4). God responds: it is too small a thing for you to restore Israel alone — "I will also make you a light for the Gentiles" (v.6). Paul and Barnabas quote v.6 in Acts 13:47 as justification for the Gentile mission. "Kings will see you and stand up, princes will see and bow down" (v.7). Simeon echoes this at Jesus’ presentation (Luke 2:30–32).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
49:1–2

"Before I was born the LORD called me. He made my mouth like a sharpened sword." The servant’s pre-natal calling: known before birth, named in the womb, weaponised with words.

49:4

"I have laboured in vain; I have spent my strength for nothing." The servant’s frustration. Even the Messiah experiences the feeling of futility. But: "my reward is with my God."

49:6

"It is too small a thing for you to restore Israel. I will make you a light for the Gentiles." The expansion: Israel is not enough. The servant’s mission is global. Acts 13:47: Paul quotes this as his apostolic mandate.

49:13

"Shout for joy, you heavens! The LORD comforts his people and will have compassion on his afflicted ones." The cosmic response to the servant’s commission: creation sings.

Verses 14–26 — “Can a Mother Forget Her Nursing Child?”
14151617181920212223242526

Hebrew Word Study

רַחֲמֶיהָ raḥămeyhā — her compassion / her womb-love: "Can a mother forget the baby at her breast and have no compassion (raḥămeyhā) on the child she has borne? Though she may forget, I will not forget you!" (v.15). Zion’s lament: "The LORD has forsaken me, the Lord has forgotten me" (v.14). God’s answer is the most tender metaphor in the OT: maternal love. "See, I have engraved you on the palms of my hands; your walls are ever before me" (v.16). Engraved — not written but carved. Permanent. Painful. "Lift up your eyes and look around; all your children gather and come to you" (v.18). Zion will be too small for her returned children (v.20). "Who bore me these? I was bereaved and barren; who brought these up?" (v.21). The astonished mother who lost everything now has more children than she can hold.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
49:14–15

"The LORD has forsaken me. Can a mother forget her nursing child? Though she may forget, I WILL NOT FORGET YOU." The "though she may forget" is stunning: God’s love exceeds even maternal love. God’s faithfulness surpasses even the strongest human bond.

49:16

"I have engraved you on the palms of my hands." Engraved, not inked. Permanent, not erasable. The Christian reading: the nail-prints in Christ’s palms.

49:19–21

"The children born during your bereavement will say, ‘This place is too small for us.’ Then you will say, ‘Who bore me these?’" The barren mother overwhelmed by children. The impossible reversal.
