Isaiah 5

The Song of the Vineyard and the Six Woes

Verses 1–7 — “The Vineyard of the LORD Is the Nation of Israel”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

כֶּרֶם kerem — vineyard: "My loved one had a vineyard (kerem) on a fertile hillside. He dug it up, cleared its stones, planted the choicest vines, built a watchtower and a winepress. Then he looked for a crop of good grapes, but it yielded only bad fruit" (vv.1–2). The song lures the listener in before the reveal: "The vineyard of the LORD Almighty is the nation of Israel, and the people of Judah are his cherished plant. He looked for justice (mishpat) but saw bloodshed (mispach); for righteousness (tsedaqah) but heard cries of distress (tse’aqah)" (v.7).

Context

The Vineyard Song is one of the finest poems in the OT — it functions as a parable (like Nathan’s to David, 2 Sam 12) that traps the audience. The listener sides with the vineyard-owner’s frustration before discovering they ARE the vineyard. The wordplay in v.7 (mishpat/mispach, tsedaqah/tse’aqah) is untranslatable: justice/bloodshed and righteousness/cries differ by one consonant in Hebrew. Jesus adapts this parable in Matt 21:33–46 (the Parable of the Tenants).

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 21:33–46Jesus’ Parable of the Tenants directly adapts Isa 5’s vineyard, adding the detail of the rejected son.
  • Ps 80:8–16You transplanted a vine from Egypt — the psalm version of the vineyard metaphor.
  • John 15:1–8I am the true vine — Jesus claims to be what Israel failed to be.
  • Mark 12:1A man planted a vineyard, put a wall around it, dug a winepress, built a watchtower — Mark’s Jesus quotes Isa 5 verbatim.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1–2

The parable: investment, care, expectation, disappointment. God did EVERYTHING right; the vineyard still failed.

5:3–4

"What more could have been done?" The question God asks. The answer: nothing. The failure is not in the gardener but in the vine.

5:5–6

The sentence: remove the hedge, break down the wall, make it a wasteland. No pruning, no rain. Abandonment.

5:7

The devastating wordplay: expected JUSTICE, got BLOODSHED; expected RIGHTEOUSNESS, heard CRIES. One letter separates what God wanted from what God got.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1–4

Calvin reads the vineyard as the church in every age: God invests, expects fruit, and is disappointed. The question "what more could have been done?" eliminates every excuse.

5:7

Calvin notes the wordplay cannot be reproduced in any translation but its effect is clear: the distance between God’s expectation and Israel’s reality is the distance of one consonant. So close, so far.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:1–2

The NET identifies the genre as a "juridical parable" (cf. 2 Sam 12:1–7): a story that invites the listener to render judgment, which then falls on the listener themselves.

5:7

The NET provides the Hebrew wordplay: mishpat/mispach and tsedaqah/tse’aqah. The near-homophony is deliberate and devastating: the sound of justice is almost the sound of bloodshed.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
5:1–7

Oswalt calls this the most theologically sophisticated parable in the OT: it establishes God’s investment (vv.1–2), Israel’s failure (v.4), the sentence (vv.5–6), and the interpretation (v.7) — all in seven verses.

5:7

Oswalt: the wordplay is the most famous in the Hebrew Bible. It shows that Israel’s failure is not a COMPLETE reversal but a SLIGHT deviation — one letter off. But one letter is the difference between justice and murder.

Childs Notes

childs
5:1–7

Childs reads the Vineyard Song as the theological centre of chs.1–5: the indictment (chs.1, 3), the hope (chs.2, 4), and now the verdict (ch.5) are all compressed into one parable.

5:7

Childs notes the wordplay functions as a summary of the entire prophetic critique: Israel was not grotesquely evil but subtly off — close enough to sound right, far enough to be catastrophic.

Verses 8–30 — “Woe to Those Who Call Evil Good and Good Evil”
89101112131415161718192021222324252627282930

Hebrew Word Study

הוֹי hōy — woe!: "Woe (hōy) to those who call evil good and good evil, who put darkness for light and light for darkness" (v.20). Six woes: (1) land-grabbing (v.8), (2) drunken revelry that ignores God’s deeds (vv.11–12), (3) mocking God’s plan (vv.18–19), (4) moral inversion (v.20), (5) self-conceit (v.21), (6) corrupt justice for a bribe (vv.22–23). God’s anger burns; he raises a signal for a distant nation — Assyria — whose arrows are sharp, horses’ hooves like flint (vv.25–30).

Context

The six woes catalogue the specific sins that the vineyard song summarised. The "distant nation" (v.26) is Assyria, though unnamed — the approaching military threat that dominates chs.7–10. Woe 4 ("calling evil good and good evil") has become a widely quoted cultural critique. The seventh woe is missing from ch.5; it appears in 10:1 ("woe to those who make unjust laws"), suggesting the sequence was divided by the insertion of chs.6–9. The lion imagery (v.29) recurs for Assyria throughout the book.

Cross-Reference

  • Amos 6:1Woe to you who are complacent in Zion — the same woe-oracle genre targeting the wealthy.
  • Hab 2:6–20Five woes in Habakkuk parallel Isaiah’s six woes — the prophetic woe-series genre.
  • Matt 23:13–32Jesus’ seven woes against the scribes and Pharisees — the NT’s greatest woe-series, modelled on Isaiah’s.
  • Prov 17:15Acquitting the guilty and condemning the innocent — the LORD detests them both — the same justice-corruption Isa 5:23 indicts.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:8

Woe 1: land-grabbing. "You will live all alone in the land." Monopoly leads to isolation.

5:11–12

Woe 2: drunken parties from morning to night while ignoring God’s deeds. Entertainment as spiritual anaesthesia.

5:20

Woe 4: "Calling evil good and good evil." The most quoted woe. Moral inversion: not just doing wrong but RELABELLING it as right.

5:26–30

God raises a signal for a distant nation. Assyria approaches. Arrows sharp, hooves like flint. No stumbling, no sleeping. The war machine is coming.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:20

Calvin calls this the worst of the six sins: all other sins acknowledge their character; this one disguises it. When a society redefines evil as good, the last defence has fallen.

5:26–30

Calvin reads Assyria as God’s instrument: the distant nation comes not because it chose to but because God summoned it. Even imperial military campaigns serve divine purposes.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:20

The NET notes four reversals in this woe: evil/good, good/evil, darkness/light, light/darkness, bitter/sweet, sweet/bitter. The complete inversion of moral categories is described with linguistic precision.

5:26

The NET observes God "whistles" for the distant nation (v.26) — the same verb used for summoning bees (7:18). Assyria comes at God’s signal, like an animal responding to its master’s call.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
5:8–23

Oswalt organises the six woes as a progression from economic sin (land-grabbing) through social sin (drunkenness, mockery) to intellectual sin (moral inversion, self-conceit) to judicial sin (corrupt justice). The corruption deepens through the sequence.

5:26–30

Oswalt reads the Assyrian approach as the seventh day of the creation-week metaphor: six woes of human sin are followed by God’s "rest" — which in this case is the terrifying rest of letting judgment come.

Childs Notes

childs
5:8–30

Childs reads the six woes as the specific charges in the covenant lawsuit that ch.1 opened. The vineyard song (vv.1–7) summarised the verdict; the woes itemise the evidence.

5:26–30

Childs notes the unnamed "distant nation" connects forward to the Immanuel section (chs.7–8) where Assyria is explicitly named. The narrative suspense is deliberate.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
