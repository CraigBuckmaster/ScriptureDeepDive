Isaiah 5

The Song of the Vineyard and the Six Woes

Verses 1–7 — “What More Could Have Been Done for My Vineyard?”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

כֶּרֶם kerem — vineyard: "My loved one had a vineyard (kerem) on a fertile hillside. He dug it up and cleared it of stones and planted the choicest vines. He built a watchtower in it and cut out a winepress as well. Then he looked for a crop of good grapes, but it yielded only bad fruit" (vv.1–2). The vineyard song: God as farmer who did everything right, yet the vineyard produced wild grapes. "What more could have been done for my vineyard than I have done for it?" (v.4). The answer is devastating: nothing. God did everything; Israel failed. Therefore: "I will take away its hedge and it will be destroyed. I will make it a wasteland" (vv.5–6). "The vineyard of the LORD Almighty is the nation of Israel. He looked for justice (mishpat) but saw bloodshed (mispach); for righteousness (tsedaqah) but heard cries of distress (tse’aqah)" (v.7). The Hebrew wordplay is untranslatable: mishpat/mispach, tsedaqah/tse’aqah.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1–2

"He planted the choicest vines. He looked for good grapes, but it yielded only bad fruit." The parable is a love song that becomes a lawsuit. God is the lovesick farmer.

5:4

"What more could have been done for my vineyard?" The rhetorical question expects one answer: nothing. Every resource was provided. The failure is entirely Israel’s.

5:7

"He looked for mishpat but saw mispach; for tsedaqah but heard tse’aqah." The most devastating wordplay in the OT. Justice/bloodshed, righteousness/distress. The words sound alike; the realities are opposite. Jesus’ vineyard parable (Matt 21:33–45) directly develops Isaiah 5.

Verses 8–30 — “Woe to Those Who Call Evil Good”
89101112131415161718192021222324252627282930

Hebrew Word Study

הוֹי hōy — woe! / alas!: Six woes (hōy): (1) Woe to land-grabbers who join house to house and field to field (v.8). (2) Woe to drunkards who rise early for beer and stay up late for wine, ignoring God’s works (vv.11–12). (3) Woe to those who drag sin along with cords of deceit (v.18). (4) Woe to those who call evil good and good evil, who put darkness for light and light for darkness (v.20). (5) Woe to those who are wise in their own eyes (v.21). (6) Woe to those who are heroes at drinking wine and champions at mixing drinks, who acquit the guilty for a bribe (vv.22–23). The consequence: God’s anger blazes, he lifts a banner for distant nations, they come swiftly (vv.25–30). Assyria is the instrument of God’s judgment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:8

"Woe to you who add house to house and join field to field till no space is left." The first woe: real-estate consolidation that dispossesses the poor. Ancient gentrification.

5:20

"Woe to those who call evil good and good evil, who put darkness for light and light for darkness." The fourth woe: moral inversion. The most quoted of the six. When a society reverses moral categories, judgment is near.

5:21

"Woe to those who are wise in their own eyes and clever in their own sight." The fifth woe: intellectual arrogance. Self-certified wisdom that needs no God.

5:26–30

"He lifts up a banner for the distant nations. They come swiftly and speedily. Their arrows are sharp, all their bows are strung. Their horses’ hooves seem like flint." Assyria approaches. The vineyard’s hedge is removed; the invader rushes in.
