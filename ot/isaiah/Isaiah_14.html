Isaiah 14

The Fall of the Morning Star

Verses 1–11 — “The LORD Will Have Compassion on Jacob”
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

רִחַם riḥam — to have compassion: "The LORD will have compassion (riḥam) on Jacob; once again he will choose Israel and will settle them in their own land" (v.1). Before the taunt-song against Babylon’s king, a promise: Israel will be restored, foreigners will join them, and they will take captive their captors (vv.1–2). Then the taunt begins: the oppressor has come to an end (v.4). The LORD has broken the rod of the wicked (v.5). The whole earth is at rest and breaks into singing; even the cedars of Lebanon rejoice (vv.7–8). Sheol stirs to meet the king of Babylon: "You too have become weak as we are!" (v.10). Maggots and worms are his bed and blanket (v.11).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:1–2

"The LORD will have compassion on Jacob." Before Babylon’s fall is mocked, Israel’s restoration is promised. Grace before judgment.

14:4–8

"How the oppressor has come to an end! The whole earth is at rest and breaks into singing." The tyrant’s fall produces global rest. Even the trees celebrate.

14:9–11

"Sheol stirs to meet you. All those who were leaders rise from their thrones: You too have become weak as we are!" The dead kings greet Babylon’s king with mockery. Death is the great equaliser.

Verses 12–32 — “How You Have Fallen from Heaven, Morning Star!”
121314151617181920212223242526272829303132

Hebrew Word Study

הֵילֵל hēlēl — shining one / morning star / Lucifer: "How you have fallen from heaven, morning star (hēlēl), son of the dawn! You have been cast down to the earth, you who once laid low the nations!" (v.12). The Latin Vulgate translated hēlēl as “Lucifer” — from which the name for Satan derives. In context, this is a taunt against Babylon’s king. "You said in your heart, ‘I will ascend to heaven; I will raise my throne above the stars of God; I will make myself like the Most High’" (vv.13–14). Five “I will” statements of self-exaltation. "But you are brought down to the realm of the dead, to the depths of the pit" (v.15). Jesus says "I saw Satan fall like lightning from heaven" (Luke 10:18), and the church has read Isaiah 14:12–15 as describing Satan’s original rebellion behind the human king’s arrogance. The chapter continues with oracles against Assyria (vv.24–27) and Philistia (vv.28–32).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:12

"How you have fallen from heaven, morning star (hēlēl)!" The Vulgate’s “Lucifer” made this verse the primary biblical text for Satan’s fall. The immediate referent is Babylon’s king; the typological reading sees Satan behind him.

14:13–14

"I will ascend to heaven. I will raise my throne above the stars. I will make myself like the Most High." Five “I will” declarations of cosmic rebellion. The grammar of pride: every sentence begins with I.

14:15

"But you are brought down to the realm of the dead, to the depths of the pit." Five “I will ascend” matched by one “you are brought down.” The symmetry: five boasts, one verdict.

14:24–27

"Surely, as I have planned, so it will be. I will crush the Assyrian in my land." God’s plan versus Assyria’s plan. "The LORD Almighty has purposed, and who can thwart him?"
