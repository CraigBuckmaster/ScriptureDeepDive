Isaiah 14

The Fall of the Morning Star

Verses 1–11 — “The LORD Will Have Compassion on Jacob”
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

רָחַם rāḥam — to have compassion: "The LORD will have compassion (rāḥam) on Jacob; once again he will choose Israel and will settle them in their own land" (v.1). After Babylon’s fall (ch.13), Israel’s restoration. Foreigners will join them (v.1). Nations will escort them home (v.2). The oppressor’s fury has ended (v.6). The whole earth is at rest and breaks into singing; the pines and cedars rejoice (vv.7–8). Sheol stirs to greet the fallen king (vv.9–10): "You too have become weak as we are!" (v.10).

Context

The taunt-song against the king of Babylon (vv.3–21) is one of the OT’s great satirical poems. The Sheol-scene (vv.9–11) personifies the underworld: the shades of dead kings rise from their thrones to mock the newcomer. The irony is savage: the king who terrorised the living is mocked by the dead. Trees celebrate his fall (v.8) because he can no longer cut them down for his building projects. The entire earth rests when the tyrant falls.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezek 32:17–32The descent of Pharaoh to Sheol — the same underworld-reception scene.
  • Luke 10:18I saw Satan fall like lightning from heaven — Jesus may allude to Isa 14:12.
  • Rev 12:7–9The great dragon was hurled down — Revelation’s cosmic version of the fall.
  • Phil 2:8–11Christ humbled himself; therefore God exalted him — the inverse of Isa 14’s pattern: the one who exalts himself is brought low.

Calvin’s Commentary

Calvin reads the restoration as the purpose of judgment: Babylon falls SO THAT Israel can return. Judgment on the enemy IS mercy to the people.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
14:1–11

Oswalt reads the taunt-song as the literary climax of the Babylon oracles (chs.13–14): the empire that seemed invincible is reduced to a corpse mocked by other corpses. The deflation is total.

14:9–11

Oswalt: the Sheol-reception is the book’s most developed afterlife scene. The dead retain awareness and even a grim sense of justice: they ENJOY seeing the tyrant brought to their level.

Childs Notes

childs
14:1–11

Childs reads the restoration promise (vv.1–2) as the canonical frame: the taunt-song exists WITHIN a restoration context. Judgment on Babylon serves Israel’s return. The canonical reader sees through judgment to mercy.

14:9–11

Childs notes the Sheol-scene universalises the message: all tyrants end the same way. The passage addresses every era’s oppressor, not just Babylon’s king.

Verses 12–32 — “How You Have Fallen from Heaven, Morning Star!”

Hebrew Word Study

הֵילֵל hēlēl — morning star / shining one: "How you have fallen from heaven, morning star (hēlēl), son of the dawn! You have been cast down to the earth, you who once laid low the nations!" (v.12). The five "I will" statements: ascend to heaven, raise my throne above the stars, sit on the mount of assembly, ascend above the clouds, make myself like the Most High (vv.13–14). But: "You are brought down to the realm of the dead" (v.15). The nations stare: "Is this the man who shook the earth and made kingdoms tremble?" (v.16). The passage continues with oracles against Assyria (vv.24–27) and Philistia (vv.28–32).

Context

Hēlēl ben-shachar ("Shining One, son of Dawn") was translated into Latin as "Lucifer" by Jerome (the Vulgate). This led to the traditional identification with Satan’s fall, though the immediate reference is the king of Babylon. Jesus’ "I saw Satan fall like lightning from heaven" (Luke 10:18) may allude to this passage. The five "I will" statements (vv.13–14) are the supreme expression of human hubris in the OT: the creature claiming equality with the Creator. The Canaanite mythology of El’s mountain of assembly (Zaphon) lies behind the imagery.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 10:18I saw Satan fall like lightning from heaven — Jesus’ possible allusion to Isa 14:12.
  • Ezek 28:12–17You were the seal of perfection, in Eden; you were anointed as a guardian cherub — Ezekiel’s parallel "fall from glory" passage applied to the king of Tyre.
  • 2 Thess 2:4He will oppose and exalt himself over everything that is called God — the man of lawlessness echoes the five "I will" statements.
  • Rev 22:16I am the bright Morning Star — Jesus claims the title the pretender lost.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:12

How you have fallen, morning star! The Lucifer tradition: the king of Babylon as a type of Satan’s fall from pride.

14:13–14

Five "I WILL" statements: ascend, raise, sit, ascend, make myself. The anatomy of pride: the creature claiming the Creator’s throne.

14:15–17

"Brought down to the depths of the pit." The nations stare: "Is THIS the man?" The terrifying one, now merely pathetic.

14:24–27

The oracle pivots to Assyria: "I will crush the Assyrian in my land." God’s sovereignty over the current empire, not just the future one.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:12–14

Calvin reads the five "I will" statements as the template for all rebellion: the creature aspiring to be Creator. Calvin applies this to papal claims, but the principle is universal: self-exaltation is the primal sin.

14:15–17

Calvin notes the anti-climax: the one who claimed heaven ends in the pit. The nations who trembled now gawk. The deflation of pride is always grotesque.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:12

The NET notes hēlēl means "shining one" or "light-bearer." The Latin Vulgate translated it "Lucifer" (light-bringer), which became a name for Satan in Christian tradition. The original referent is the Babylonian king.

14:13–14

The NET identifies "the mount of assembly" as Mount Zaphon, the divine mountain in Canaanite mythology where the gods convened. The king claims a seat in the divine council itself.

Oswalt Notes

oswalt
14:12–15

Oswalt reads the passage as operating on two levels simultaneously: the historical king of Babylon AND the spiritual power of pride that he embodies. The type-antitype relationship between the king and Satan is legitimate even if the original referent is human.

14:24–27

Oswalt notes the abrupt shift to Assyria (vv.24–27) reminds the reader that Babylon is future; Assyria is present. Isaiah addresses both the immediate threat and the ultimate one.

Childs Notes

childs
14:12–15

Childs reads the passage canonically: whatever the original mythological background (Canaanite, Babylonian), the text as received describes the fall of hubris from heaven to hell. The canonical reading permits both the historical and the typological interpretation.

14:24–32

Childs notes the oracles against Assyria and Philistia (vv.24–32) ground the chapter in Isaiah’s own time: the eschatological Babylon falls in vv.3–21; the contemporary powers fall in vv.24–32. Both are under God’s sovereignty.

Ancient Sources

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 BC)
A clay cylinder in which Cyrus of Persia describes his conquest of Babylon and his policy of returning captive peoples to their homelands — the Persian policy that fulfilled Isaiah 44:28–45:1.
The cylinder confirms Isaiah’s prophecy of a Persian king who would liberate captives and authorize temple rebuilding, whether the prophecy is dated early (8th c.) or late (6th c.).
The Sennacherib Prism (c.700 BC)
Sennacherib’s own account of his 701 BC campaign states he shut Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage” in Jerusalem — but conspicuously does NOT claim to have captured the city.
The silence confirms Isaiah 36–37: Sennacherib besieged but never conquered Jerusalem. His own records inadvertently verify Isaiah’s narrative of divine deliverance.
The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A complete copy of Isaiah found at Qumran, dated c.125 BC. The oldest complete biblical manuscript, it is virtually identical to the Masoretic text 1,000 years later.
The scroll demonstrates the remarkable textual stability of Isaiah across a millennium. The text the church reads today is essentially the text the Qumran community read.
