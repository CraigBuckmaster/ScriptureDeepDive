Verses 1–14 — Agur's Confession; Add Nothing to God's Words; Remove Falsehood and Poverty
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half of ch.30 introduces a new voice: Agur son of Jakeh, who opens with an unprecedented confession of intellectual humility (vv.2-3) before asserting the sufficiency of God's word (v.5) and the prayer for neither poverty nor riches (vv.8-9). The numerical sayings begin (vv.11-14) with the four generations who curse or fail to honour.

Cross-Reference

  • Job 38-41God's speech from the whirlwind — Agur's questions about who has gone up to heaven (v.4) echo the divine interrogation of Job. The unfathomability of God is the prerequisite for genuine humility.
  • Prov 30:5-6Every word of God is flawless; do not add to his words — the warning against supplementing God's word with human additions.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
30:1-14

MacArthur: I am weary, God, but I can prevail... Surely I am only a brute, not a man; I do not have human understanding (vv.1-2). Agur's confession of ignorance is the most theologically humble passage in Proverbs. The sage who has earned the right to speak begins by confessing the limits of what speech can reach.

30:5-6

MacArthur: Every word of God is flawless; he is a shield to those who take refuge in him. Do not add to his words, or he will rebuke you and prove you a liar. The confession of scriptural sufficiency is placed immediately after the confession of human epistemological limits: what Agur cannot know by wisdom, God's flawless word provides.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
30:1-4

Agur's confession -- Alter notes the opening questions are unanswerable, designed to produce the admission: I do not know. The confession of ignorance is not mere humility but epistemological accuracy. The rhetorical questions about divine transcendence leave the creature with no response but silence.

30:7-9

The two-petition prayer -- Alter notes Agur's prayer is the simplest and most honest in Proverbs: give me neither poverty nor riches; give me only my daily bread. The prayer asks for the exact provision that neutralises the spiritual dangers of both extremes.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
30:1-4

Agur's confession of ignorance is Proverbs' most theologically humble passage. Waltke notes the opening question -- 'who has gone up to heaven and come down? who has cupped the wind in his hands? what is his name, and the name of his son?' -- is the OT's most concentrated expression of divine transcendence and human limitation. The mystery of the divine name and son anticipates NT Christological questions.

30:5-6

Add nothing to God's words lest he rebuke you -- the canonical warning following Agur's confession. Waltke notes this is placed after an extended statement of human ignorance: precisely because we know so little, we must not supplement what God has revealed with what we have invented.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
30:4

Calvin: Who has gone up to heaven and come down? The questions emphasise the infinite distance between God and humanity. Only the incarnate Son has descended from and ascended to heaven -- the verse contains an implicit Christological trajectory that John 3:13 makes explicit.

30:8-9

Calvin: Give me neither poverty nor riches -- this is the model prayer of contentment. The man who is too rich forgets God; the man who is too poor may steal and dishonour God's name. The prayer for the middle way is the prayer of wisdom.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
30:4

NET: the name-and-son questions discuss the divine name YHWH (whose name is he?) and the son (what is his son's name?) -- the note traces how these questions set up the revelation theology of the OT and have been read Christologically by the NT and church fathers.

30:5-6

NET: 'add nothing to his words' -- the note traces the canonical warning through Deut 4:2 and Rev 22:18-19. The prohibition against addition is the canonical claim to completeness and sufficiency.

Verses 15–33 — The Four Things; The Way of an Eagle; Locusts Without a King
15161718192021222324252627282930313233

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half of ch.30 is dominated by the numerical sayings — the four things, the way of an eagle (vv.18-19), the earth shaking at four things (vv.21-23), the four small creatures who are wise (vv.24-28), and the four stately creatures (vv.29-31). The numerical form is a teaching device: cataloguing wisdom makes it memorable. The chapter closes with the warning against stirring up anger (v.33).

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 6:6-8Go to the ant, you sluggard — the ants as a wisdom example recurs in v.25. Small creatures with great wisdom are a consistent Proverbs motif.
  • Matt 6:25-34Do not worry about food and clothing — Jesus's teaching embodies the middle way between poverty and riches that Agur's prayer (vv.8-9) requests.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
30:15-33

MacArthur: The numerical proverbs (the four things that never say enough; the four things that are too wonderful; the four things the earth cannot bear; the four small-but-wise creatures) are wisdom's aesthetic mode: identifying patterns in creation that reveal the character of the Creator.

30:24-28

MacArthur: The four small-but-wise creatures — the ant, rock badger, locust, and lizard — each demonstrates wisdom that exceeds its size. The ant stores in summer; the badger builds in rock; locusts organize without a king; the lizard reaches the palace. Wisdom is not a function of power or size but of well-adapted intelligence.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
30:15-33

The numerical proverbs -- Alter notes the X/X+1 form is playful and philosophical simultaneously. The categories resist systematic organisation; the observations are keen and specific. The chapter's second half is Proverbs' most empirical section.

30:24-28

The four small-but-wise creatures -- ants, rock badgers, locusts, lizard. Alter notes each is characterised by wisdom that exceeds its size. The ant stores provision; the rock badger builds in rock; the locusts advance in formation; the lizard enters kings' palaces. The lesson: wisdom is not a function of power or size but of well-adapted intelligence.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
30:15-33

The numerical proverbs of vv.15-33 (three things, four things) are Proverbs' most distinctive literary form in ch.30. Waltke notes the X/X+1 pattern creates suspense and emphasis: the climactic item (the fourth thing) is always the surprise. The observations range from the unsatisfied (vv.15-16) to the mysterious (vv.18-19) to the intolerable (v.21-23) to the small-but-wise (vv.24-28).

30:18-19

The way of an eagle, a snake, a ship, a man with a maiden -- the four mysterious things that leave no trace. Waltke notes the series ends with the most mysterious of all: the way of a man with a maiden. The verse has often been read as about the mystery of human attraction, but the context (things that leave no trace) may suggest something about the inscrutability of relationship.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
30:4

Calvin: What is his son's name? -- this anticipates the full revelation of the New Testament: the Son of God revealed in Christ. The verse does not predict Christ explicitly but opens the question that the NT answers.

30:24-28

Calvin: The four small wise creatures teach that wisdom and greatness are not the same. God uses small things to shame the great and weak things to shame the strong (1 Cor 1:27). Observe the ant and become wise.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
30:18-19

NET: the four mysterious things that leave no trace -- the note discusses whether the fourth item (man with a maiden) refers to physical passion, psychological inscrutability, or the absence of a traceable trajectory. The mystery is the point.

30:24-28

NET: the four small-but-wise creatures -- the note discusses each creature's specific wisdom strategy: the ant's provision-storing, the rock badger's fortified dwelling, the locusts' coordinated advance, the lizard's access to power.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions, especially Egyptian.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs — one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.
The editorial activity shows Proverbs as a living, curated collection.

Reception History

Virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics — the practical outworking of the fear of the LORD.
Practical wisdom
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, governance, and social teaching.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

Agur's Confession and the Theology of Divine Transcendence
Genuine theological humility before divine transcendence
Waltke, most commentators
Agur's confession is not false modesty but accurate epistemology: human beings do not have direct access to the divine wisdom that made the cosmos. The questions (who ascended? who cupped the wind?) are genuinely unanswerable from the human side.
A rhetorical setup for the canonical affirmation (vv.5-6)
Some scholars
The confession of ignorance sets up the canonical claim that God's words are flawless and sufficient. The point is not perpetual agnosticism but the recognition that what we know, we know because God has revealed it.
Both readings are correct and complementary: Agur's genuine humility before divine transcendence is the epistemological ground for the canonical claim that follows. Because we know so little, we must trust and not supplement what God has revealed.
