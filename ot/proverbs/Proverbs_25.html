Proverbs 25

God's Hidden Things; Apt Speech; Honey in Moderation; Coals of Fire

Verses 1–14 — God's Hidden Things; King's Searched Heart; Timely Words
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half of ch.25 opens Hezekiah's collection (25:1) with a theology of divine hiddenness and royal investigation (v.2). V.11's timely-word image is among the most beautiful in Proverbs: a word fitly spoken is like apples of gold in settings of silver. The chapter consistently addresses court life and the exercise of authority.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 29:29The secret things belong to the LORD our God, but the things revealed belong to us — the theological parallel to v.2's divine concealment.
  • Prov 16:13Kings take pleasure in honest lips — the royal-court wisdom of v.2's king-who-searches recurs throughout chs.25-29.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
25:1-14

MacArthur: These are more proverbs of Solomon, copied by the men of Hezekiah — the editorial note places this section in Hezekiah's reign (c.700 BC), over two centuries after Solomon. The preservation and transmission of wisdom across generations is itself a covenant act: what one generation receives, it must pass to the next.

25:11

MacArthur: Like apples of gold in settings of silver is a ruling rightly given. The right word at the right moment has a beauty that both components produce together: the word's truth (gold) and its timing and form (silver). Wisdom in speech is an art form whose medium is the moment.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:2

God conceals — kings search: the epistemological contrast structures the entire Proverbs wisdom program. Alter notes the divine concealment that is 'glorious' is not arbitrary hiddenness but the mystery that makes investigation meaningful. If everything were obvious, there would be no royal glory in discovery. The difficulty is part of the design.

25:20

Singing songs to a heavy heart is like vinegar on soda — the sensory mismatch captures pastoral misfiring. Alter notes the image is viscerally precise: vinegar on soda does not merely fail to help; it produces a violent, useless reaction. Cheerful songs sung to genuine grief do not comfort — they alienate and sometimes offend. Wisdom requires reading the emotional register before speaking.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:2

God conceals; kings investigate -- divine hiddenness is glorious; royal search is the king's glory. Wisdom is the patient investigation of what God has not placed on the surface.

25:11

A word aptly spoken is like gold apples in silver settings -- the right word at the right moment has beauty combining content and timing. Wisdom in speech is an art form.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:2

Calvin: It is God's glory to conceal things — not from cruelty but because some things exceed human comprehension and some things are reserved for direct divine disclosure. The king's glory to search them out establishes human investigation as legitimate and honourable. The two glories are complementary: divine mystery calls forth human inquiry.

25:22

Calvin: Heap burning coals on your enemy's head by feeding them when hungry and giving them water when thirsty — Paul quotes this in Rom 12:20. Calvin notes the coals are the discomfort of conscience that undeserved generosity produces. The generous act achieves what retaliatory violence cannot: it breaks through the enemy's defences by refusing to participate in the logic of hostility.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:2

The NET note on 'it is the glory of God to conceal a matter; to search out a matter is the glory of kings' discusses the epistemological claim: God's concealment is itself a form of glory (honour, splendour), and human investigation of what God has embedded in creation participates in the royal calling of governance. The note traces the wisdom-as-search theme through Prov 2:4 and 8:17.

25:22

The NET note on burning coals heaped on the enemy's head discusses the ancient Near Eastern background: either a ritual of shame or the discomfort produced by the recognition that one's hostility has been met with undeserved kindness. Paul's use in Rom 12:20 establishes the primary application: overcoming evil with good, not in the sense of defeating the enemy but of disarming them through love.

Verses 15–28 — Honey in Moderation; Coals of Fire; City Without Walls
1516171819202122232425262728

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half contains Proverbs' most counter-cultural social ethic: feeding and watering your enemy (vv.21-22), paralleled by Paul in Romans 12:20. The city-without-walls image (v.28) closes the section: the person without self-control is as exposed and vulnerable as a city whose walls have been torn down.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 12:20If your enemy is hungry, feed him; if he is thirsty, give him something to drink. In doing this, you will heap burning coals on his head — Paul quotes v.21-22 directly.
  • Prov 16:32Better a patient person than a warrior — the self-control of v.28's city-without-walls principle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
25:15-28

MacArthur: If your enemy is hungry, give him food to eat; if he is thirsty, give him water to drink. In doing this, you will heap burning coals on his head, and the LORD will reward you (vv.21-22). The burning coals interpretation divides commentators — shame or charcoal for warmth. Either way, the action is counter-intuitive generosity that disrupts the logic of enmity.

25:28

MacArthur: Like a city whose walls are broken through is a person who lacks self-control. The military metaphor is precise: a city without walls is defenseless; a person without self-control is equally exposed. Self-control is the inner wall that keeps the chaos of appetite and impulse from overrunning the person.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:16

The honey-moderation proverb forms a thematic pair with the generous enemy treatment (vv.21-22): both concern calibrating response to context. Too much honey; too much hostility — both miss the right measure. Alter notes that calibration (the right amount, the right response) is the chapter's consistent theme.

25:25

Cold water to a weary soul, good news from a far country — the sensory and emotional refreshment are mapped together. Alter notes Proverbs consistently works at both physical and spiritual levels simultaneously: the physical coolness of water to the thirsty body is the exact experiential parallel to the emotional refreshment of good news to the anxious spirit.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:16

Honey in moderation — Waltke notes the limitation applies to a genuine good. Honey is not bad; too much honey is nauseating. This is Proverbs' clearest statement of the moderation principle applied to legitimate pleasure: the good thing becomes destructive when pursued without limits. Wisdom governs enjoyment; it does not prohibit it.

25:28

A man without self-control is like a city with broken-down walls -- the military metaphor for personal discipline. The unwalled city is exposed to any attacker.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:16

Calvin: Have you found honey? Eat only enough — this teaches the discipline of enjoyment. The gift that is abused becomes destructive; moderation preserves the gift while excess destroys both the gift and the person. Every legitimate pleasure is governed by the wisdom principle: enough is enough, and more is too much.

25:28

Calvin: A man without self-control is like a city with broken-down walls — the most vulnerable territory is the unguarded self. External enemies are less dangerous than internal impulses with no governing discipline. The city without walls is exposed to every attacker; the person without self-control is exposed to every temptation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:16

The NET note on the moderation principle applied to honey identifies it as a type for a broader wisdom principle: any genuine good becomes destructive when pursued without limits. The note traces the moderation theme through Proverbs (honey in 25:16, 27; neighbour visits in 25:17; self-praise in 27:2) as a consistent wisdom curriculum.

25:28

The NET note on the broken-walls city metaphor explains that the ancient city without walls was militarily, commercially, and socially exposed. The person without self-control (ein moshel berucho) is equivalently exposed — to every impulse, every temptation, every external pressure that finds no resistance. The metaphor makes vulnerability visceral.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions, especially Egyptian.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs — one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.
The editorial activity shows Proverbs as a living, curated collection.

Reception History

Virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics — the practical outworking of the fear of the LORD.
Practical wisdom
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, governance, and social teaching.
