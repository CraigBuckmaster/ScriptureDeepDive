Proverbs 23

Thirty Sayings: Counsel on Wealth, Parents, Wine; Wisdom's Future Hope

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–18 — Thirty Sayings: Don't Crave; Move Not the Boundary; Discipline Your Heart
123456789101112131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half of ch.23 continues the Thirty Sayings with warnings against craving the ruler's food (v.3), exploiting the poor (v.10), and neglecting discipline (vv.13-14). The boundary-stone warning (v.10) is among the most pointed social ethics in the collection.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 19:14Do not move your neighbour's boundary stone — the legal basis for v.10's boundary-stone warning.
  • Prov 22:28Do not move an ancient boundary stone — the direct parallel, establishing the principle as a repeated warning.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
23:1-18

MacArthur: When you sit to dine with a ruler, note well what is before you, and put a knife to your throat if you are given to gluttony. The first saying's hyperbole — put a knife to your throat — makes a controlled point: the appetite must be disciplined in social contexts, especially in the presence of power. Self-regulation is the test of wisdom in the dining hall.

23:17-18

MacArthur: Do not let your heart envy sinners, but always be zealous for the fear of the LORD. The antidote to envy is not comparative reasoning but reorientation toward the fear of the LORD. There is surely a future hope for you, and your hope will not be cut off. The long view makes envy irrational.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:5

The eagle-wings image — wealth does not merely decrease but sprouts wings and flies. The personification captures the experience of sudden loss more vividly than any abstract statement about impermanence.

23:9

Do not speak to a fool — Alter notes the instruction acknowledges the limits of wisdom's reach. Not every person is reachable; the fool who scorns wisdom cannot be helped by more wisdom.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:4-5

Do not wear yourself out to get rich — the eagle-wings image for vanishing wealth. The counsel is against the exhausting pursuit of wealth as the supreme good, not against diligence itself.

23:10-11

Do not move the ancient boundary stone — the fatherless have a strong Defender (go'el): the LORD himself. Moving the boundary is a legal offence against the most powerful possible advocate.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:4

Calvin: 'Do not wear yourself out to get rich — this is a direct command against avarice. The exhausted pursuit of wealth is both spiritually and physically destructive.'

23:10

Calvin: 'The fatherless have a strong Defender — those who steal from the powerless make God their adversary. The absence of a human advocate does not leave the fatherless unprotected.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:5

The NET note on the eagle-wings image for vanishing wealth traces the bird metaphor through the OT: wealth that sprouts wings and flies away like an eagle toward heaven is an image of sudden, complete loss. The note identifies this as one of Proverbs' most vivid wealth-impermanence images — not gradual decline but sudden, irreversible departure.

23:10

NET: go'el (Defender) — the kinsman-redeemer who advocates for the vulnerable. God as go'el of the fatherless makes encroachment on their land a legal offence against the most powerful possible advocate.

Verses 19–35 — Listen to Parents; Wisdom's Future Hope; The Warning Against Wine
1920212223242526272829303132333435

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half addresses the parental heart's joy when children are wise (vv.19-25), the warning against the adulterous woman (vv.26-28), and the chapter's most extended passage: the warning against wine (vv.29-35). The wine passage is the most vivid description of addiction in the OT.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 31:4-7It is not for kings to drink wine — the royal wine-warning that mirrors ch.23's extended treatment.
  • Eph 5:18Do not get drunk on wine, which leads to debauchery — the NT application of the wine-warning principle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
23:19-35

MacArthur: The extended wine poem (vv.29-35) is the most detailed description of addiction in Scripture. The sequence — attraction, consumption, aftermath, craving for more — describes not merely drunkenness but the cycle of compulsive behavior. Who has redness of eyes? Those who linger over wine.

23:26

MacArthur: My son, give me your heart and let your eyes delight in my ways — the wisdom teacher's ultimate request. Not merely behavioral compliance but the affective center of the person. Wisdom formation is heart formation. The father appeals to the deepest level because he knows that behavior follows the heart.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:23

Buy wisdom and never sell it — Alter notes placing wisdom in the economic register establishes it as the currency against which all other exchanges are measured.

23:35

The drunkard who wakes seeking another drink — Alter notes this is one of the Bible's most precise descriptions of the addiction cycle. The final verse is both comic and devastating.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:23

Buy truth, wisdom, instruction, understanding and do not sell them — Waltke notes wisdom placed in the economic register of irreplaceable assets. Acquire at cost; never trade away.

23:29-35

The extended wine poem — Proverbs' most vivid and psychologically precise anti-alcohol passage, describing the full addiction cycle including the drunkard who wakes seeking another drink.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:23

Calvin: 'Buy truth and do not sell it — once you have genuine wisdom, it must never be traded away for temporal advantage. It is the asset that all other assets depend on.'

23:35

Calvin: 'The nature of all sensual addiction — the pleasure is brief, the pain enduring, and the first desire upon recovery is to return to the source of the pain. Solomon presents this with the irony that makes it recognisable.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:23

The NET note on qanah (buy/acquire) traces the root through Proverbs' consistent use for acquiring wisdom (4:5, 7; 16:16; 23:23) and through Gen 4:1 ('I have acquired a man with the LORD's help'). The note discusses how the commercial metaphor establishes wisdom as costly to acquire and worth any price — once genuinely possessed, it must never be traded away.

23:31

The NET note on 'do not gaze at wine when it is red, when it sparkles in the cup, when it goes down smoothly' explains that the warning is at the moment of temptation, not merely at the point of intoxication. Wisdom addresses the decision before it is made. The sensory appeal of wine — its colour, sparkle, and smooth taste — is precisely what must not be allowed to govern the choice.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions, especially Egyptian.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs — one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.
The editorial activity shows Proverbs as a living, curated collection.

Reception History

Virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics — the practical outworking of the fear of the LORD.
Practical wisdom
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, governance, and social teaching.
