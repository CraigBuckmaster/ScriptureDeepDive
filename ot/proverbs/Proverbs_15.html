Proverbs 15

Gentle Answer; Eyes of the LORD; Better Than; Path of Life Leads Upward

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–17 — Gentle Answer; Eyes of the LORD; Better Than Wealth
1A gentle answer turns away wrath, but a harsh word stirs up anger.2The tongue of the wise adorns knowledge, but the mouth of the fool gushes folly.3The eyes of the LORD are everywhere, keeping watch on the wicked and the good.4The soothing tongue is a tree of life, but a perverse tongue crushes the spirit.5A fool spurns a parent's discipline, but whoever heeds correction shows prudence.6The house of the righteous contains great treasure, but the income of the wicked brings ruin.7The lips of the wise spread knowledge, but the hearts of fools are not upright.8The LORD detests the sacrifice of the wicked, but the prayer of the upright pleases him.9The LORD detests the way of the wicked, but he loves those who pursue righteousness.10Stern discipline awaits anyone who leaves the path; the one who hates correction will die.11Death and Destruction lie open before the LORD; how much more do human hearts!12Mockers resent correction, so they avoid the wise.13A happy heart makes the face cheerful, but heartache crushes the spirit.14The discerning heart seeks knowledge, but the mouth of a fool feeds on folly.15All the days of the oppressed are wretched, but the cheerful heart has a continual feast.16Better a little with the fear of the LORD than great wealth with turmoil.17Better a small serving of vegetables with love than a fattened calf with hatred.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half is dominated by the tongue (vv.1-2,4,7) and God's omniscience (vv.3,8-9,11). The eyes of the LORD everywhere (v.3) and Death open before him (v.11) are the theological environment in which all speech ethics operate. The two better-than sayings (vv.16-17) introduce the theme: quality of heart matters more than quantity of possession.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 12:18The tongue of the wise brings healing -- the soothing tongue as tree of life (15:4) extends this.
  • Jas 1:19-20Quick to listen, slow to speak, slow to anger -- NT application of v.1's conflict resolution.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
15:1-15

MacArthur: A gentle answer turns away wrath, but a harsh word stirs up anger. Among Proverbs' most practically precise observations. The register of the response — not merely its content — determines the direction of the encounter. De-escalation is available to anyone at any moment through the choice of tone.

15:11

MacArthur: Death and Destruction lie open before the LORD — how much more do human hearts! The argument is from greater to lesser: if God sees into the realm of the dead, the hidden depths of the human heart are entirely transparent to him. The wisdom of fearing God is grounded in the impossibility of concealing anything from him.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:1–7

The speech-ethics cluster that opens ch.15 is its most concentrated. Alter notes the opening proverb (gentle answer turns away wrath) is one of the most practically useful observations in the entire collection — it is immediately actionable, universally applicable, and almost certainly true by direct experience. The cluster that follows (tongue of the wise brings knowledge; tongue of fools pours out folly) develops what the first proverb implies: speech shapes the relational environment.

15:11

'Death and Destruction lie open before the LORD — how much more do human hearts!' The argument a fortiori grounds divine omniscience: if Sheol and Abaddon (Death and Destruction) are visible to God, then certainly the human heart — which is less hidden than the realm of the dead — is fully transparent to him. Alter notes the underground/underwater realm of Sheol is the most inaccessible possible place; and even that is visible.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:1–7

The gentle-answer opening is one of Proverbs' most practical observations about social conflict: 'a gentle answer turns away wrath, but a harsh word stirs up anger.' Waltke notes the speech-control proverb group of vv.1–7 establishes that wisdom in conflict is primarily a speech discipline — not avoidance of conflict but management of its escalation through deliberate word-choice.

15:3

'The eyes of the LORD are everywhere, keeping watch on the wicked and the good.' This is the theological centre of ch.15. Waltke notes divine omniscience is here presented not primarily as a threat but as a structural reality that grounds all the other observations of the chapter. Every speech act, every household meal, every route of life — all are in the presence of the God who sees.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:1

Calvin: 'A soft answer turns away wrath — this is one of those practical proverbs that needs no commentary to be understood. Experience teaches everyone that anger is disarmed by gentleness. But to put the principle into practice in the moment of provocation requires exactly the character formation that Proverbs is building.'

15:11

Calvin on divine omniscience: 'If God's eyes penetrate even to the depths of hell, how much more must they see the innermost recesses of our hearts? This thought should both encourage and constrain us: encourage, because no suffering is beyond his sight; constrain, because no concealed sin is beyond his knowledge.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:1

The NET note on 'a gentle answer turns away wrath' (ma'aneh rakh yashuv chemah) discusses ma'aneh (a response, an answer) — the proverb addresses what you say in reply to an angry person, not how you initiate conversation. The gentle response is a social skill that de-escalates; the harsh word (davar etsev, a painful/grievous word) escalates. The note identifies this as one of Proverbs' most-cited practical observations.

15:3

The NET note on 'the eyes of the LORD are everywhere' (bekol-makom einei YHWH) discusses the theological significance of omniscience in the wisdom tradition: God's oversight is not primarily surveillance for punishment but the structural reality that all human behaviour occurs in the presence of the evaluating God. The note connects to Prov 5:21 ('a man's ways are in full view of the LORD') as the same point from a different angle.

Verses 18–33 — Path of Life Leads Upward; LORD Hears the Righteous; Humility Before Honour
18A hot-tempered person stirs up conflict, but the one who is patient calms a quarrel.19The way of the sluggard is blocked with thorns, but the path of the upright is a highway.20A wise son brings joy to his father, but a foolish man despises his mother.21Folly brings joy to one who has no sense, but whoever has understanding keeps a straight course.22Plans fail for lack of counsel, but with many advisers they succeed.23A person finds joy in giving an apt reply-- and how good is a timely word!24The path of life leads upward for the prudent to keep them from going down to the realm of the dead.25The LORD tears down the house of the proud but he maintains the widow's boundary stones.26The LORD detests the thoughts of the wicked, but gracious words are pure in his sight.27The greedy bring ruin to their households, but the one who hates bribes will live.28The heart of the righteous weighs its answers, but the mouth of the wicked gushes evil.29The LORD is far from the wicked, but he hears the prayer of the righteous.30Light in a messenger's eyes brings joy to the heart, and good news gives health to the bones.31Whoever heeds life-giving correction will be at home among the wise.32Those who disregard discipline despise themselves, but the one who heeds correction gains understanding.33Wisdom's instruction is to fear the LORD, and humility comes before honor.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half continues the speech theme (vv.23,26,28) before addressing the path of life (v.24), the LORD's care for the vulnerable widow (v.25), the LORD's proximity to the righteous (v.29), and the chapter's closing sequence: wisdom → fear → humility → honour (v.33).

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 3:34He gives grace to the humble -- the honour-through-humility sequence of v.33, cited by James (4:6) and Peter (1 Pet 5:5).
  • Prov 11:14Many advisers (v.22) -- the repeated counsellor-proverb. Wisdom is consistently communal.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
15:18-33

MacArthur: The fear of the LORD teaches a person wisdom, and humility comes before honor (v.33). The chapter ends where Proverbs always ends: humility, which is the social expression of the fear of the LORD, is the precondition for honor. The person who seeks honor directly will not find it; the person who pursues humility will receive it.

15:16-17

MacArthur: The two better-than proverbs (vv.16-17) establish the priority hierarchy: the fear of the LORD and love produce a quality of life that no quantity of wealth can replicate. The proverbs are not ascetic — they do not condemn wealth — but they insist on its subordination to covenant character.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:15–17

The 'better than' proverbs (vv.16–17) are among the most counter-cultural in Proverbs. Alter notes their structure: each offers a comparison that challenges the default assumption (wealth/abundance = better). 'Better a little with the fear of the LORD than great wealth with turmoil' directly challenges the logic of the prosperity assumption. 'Better a small serving of vegetables with love than a fattened calf with hatred' challenges the assumption that material abundance trumps relational health.

15:28

'The heart of the righteous weighs its answers, but the mouth of the wicked gushes evil.' Alter notes the contrast of deliberation vs. gushing: the righteous person's speech is preceded by inner reflection (weighs); the wicked person's mouth just pours out whatever is there. The physiology of the two speeches — weighing vs. gushing — encodes their moral difference.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:18–33

The second section develops the 'better than' proverbs (vv.16–17) and the path-of-life imagery. Waltke notes the 'better than' form is Proverbs' most explicit value-clarification device: it forces a ranking between two goods or two evils. 'Better a little with the fear of the LORD than great wealth with turmoil' (v.16) is a direct challenge to the prosperity assumption — the fear of the LORD is more valuable than wealth.

15:33

'Wisdom's instruction is to fear the LORD, and humility comes before honor.' The chapter closes with the thesis statement framed as a journey: fear of the LORD → humility → honour. The sequence is the wisdom life's arc: the foundational orientation (fear of the LORD) produces the character quality (humility) that produces the social outcome (honour). The book's thesis appears again at the chapter's end.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:16–17

Calvin on 'better than' proverbs: 'These comparisons teach us to subordinate external prosperity to spiritual good. A poor man who fears God is richer than a Croesus who has forgotten him. This is not merely an abstract theological claim — it is a practical observation about where genuine wellbeing actually resides.'

15:33

Calvin: 'The instruction of wisdom is to fear the LORD — and humility is both the fruit of this fear and the gate through which wisdom enters. The proud cannot be taught because they already think they know; the humble can be taught because they acknowledge they do not. Humility is not self-deprecation but accurate self-knowledge.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:16

The NET note on the 'better than' form (tov...min) identifies this as one of Proverbs' most important rhetorical structures — explicit value-ranking that forces comparative judgment. The notes trace the 'better than' structure through Proverbs (15:16–17; 16:8, 19, 32; 17:1; 19:1; 21:9, 19; 25:7, 24; 27:5, 10; 28:6) as a sustained curriculum in value-clarification for the wisdom student.

15:33

The NET note on 'humility comes before honour' (lifney khavod anavah) discusses the Hebrew word order: 'before honour [is] humility.' The note traces this principle through 18:12 (before destruction the heart is haughty; before honour comes humility) and into the NT (Jas 4:10; 1 Pet 5:6). The principle is consistent across the entire wisdom tradition: genuine honour is the consequence of genuine humility, not its substitute.

Scholarly Debates

The 'Better Than' Proverbs and Prosperity Theology
A direct refutation of material prosperity as the supreme good
Waltke, most evangelical scholars
The 'better than' proverbs (15:16–17; 16:8, 19) explicitly subordinate material wealth to the fear of the LORD, love, and righteousness. They are one of Proverbs' most direct challenges to any theology that identifies material blessing as the supreme indicator of God's favour.
A qualification, not a rejection of prosperity
Some prosperity-theology adjacent readings
The 'better than' proverbs qualify the prosperity principle (if you must choose, choose righteousness over wealth) without rejecting the general principle that wisdom leads to material blessing. They are the exception clauses, not the rule.
A realistic wisdom about competing goods
Fox, Longman
The 'better than' proverbs teach the wisdom student to rank competing goods correctly. They do not reject material prosperity as a genuine good — they insist that spiritual and relational goods are more valuable when they conflict with material ones. The ranking exercise is the wisdom pedagogy itself.
The 'better than' form is one of Proverbs' most sophisticated teaching tools — it creates a value-curriculum by repeatedly forcing comparative judgments. The consistent pattern (fear of the LORD > wealth; love > abundance; righteousness > riches) is not a fluke but a sustained theological argument: the foundational goods are spiritual and relational, and material goods are secondary to them.
