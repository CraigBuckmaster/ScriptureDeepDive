A Good Name; Rich and Poor Made by the LORD; The Thirty Sayings Begin

Verses 1–16 — A Good Name; Rich and Poor Made by the LORD; Train a Child
12345678910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half of ch.22 contains some of the most quotable sayings in Proverbs: the good name (v.1), the rich and poor meeting — both made by the LORD (v.2), training a child (v.6), the borrower as servant to the lender (v.7). The collection consistently attends to the poor (vv.2,9,16,22-23).

Cross-Reference

  • Eccl 7:1A good name is better than fine perfume — the companion saying to v.1.
  • Prov 13:22A good person leaves an inheritance for their children's children — the multigenerational consequence of a good name.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
22:1-16

MacArthur: A good name is more desirable than great riches; to be esteemed is better than silver or gold (v.1). The wisdom hierarchy places character and reputation above material wealth. A good name is not merely social approval but the accumulated testimony of a life lived with integrity — its value is its truthfulness.

22:6

MacArthur: Start children off on the way they should go, and even when they are old they will not turn from it. The proverb is a general principle of formation, not an absolute promise. It recognizes that character formed early tends to persist — the channels cut in youth carry the water of habit long into adulthood.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:1

The good-name comparison — consistent with the sustained 'better than' curriculum throughout chs.15-21. Character and reputation outlast and outvalue any material wealth.

22:2

Rich and poor have this in common: the LORD made them both — the levelling force of creation theology. Whatever economic differences exist, a common Creator stands behind both.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:1

A good name over great riches — shem (name/reputation/identity) encompasses character and legacy. The comparison to silver and gold places it in the wisdom value-hierarchy above material wealth.

22:9

The generous person will be blessed — Waltke notes that tov ayin (good/generous eye) is the Hebrew idiom for the person who sees others' needs and acts on what they see. The eye that sees generously motivates the hand that gives generously. Generosity begins in perception — the way you look at other people determines how you treat them.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:1

A good name is more desirable than great riches — the person who sacrifices reputation for wealth has made the worst possible trade. Calvin notes that shem (name/reputation) is the social expression of character: a good name is what the community testifies about who you actually are. It cannot be purchased; it must be built through consistent covenant faithfulness.

22:6

Calvin: 'Train up a child in the way they should go — the divine appointment of parents as primary educators. The training is comprehensive: the whole orientation of the child's life is to be shaped.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:2

NET: 'the LORD is the Maker of them all' — creation theology levels all economic distinctions. Rich and poor are the same kind of being made by the same God.

22:6

NET: surveys al-pi darko (according to their way/bent) — instruction fitted to the individual child's character vs. the morally correct way. Both readings are defensible.

Verses 17–29 — The Thirty Sayings: Counsel, Justice, and Skilled Work
17181920212223242526272829

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half introduces the Thirty Sayings (vv.17-29), a formal collection with its own preamble (vv.17-21). The collection's motto is the final verse: a person skilled in their work will stand before kings. Excellence earns extraordinary access.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 12:24Diligent hands will rule — the diligence-leads-to-authority principle of v.29 recurs throughout Proverbs.
  • Amos 8:4Hear this, you who trample the needy — the prophetic parallel to v.22's warning against exploiting the poor.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
22:17-29

MacArthur: The Thirty Sayings of the Wise (vv.17-24:22) introduce a more sustained literary form than the individual proverbs. The collection begins with the call to pay attention and apply your heart — the reader's orientation is as important as the content. Wisdom received without application does not become wisdom.

22:22-23

MacArthur: Do not exploit the poor because they are poor and do not crush the needy in court, for the LORD will take up their case. The theological grounding of social justice: the vulnerable have an advocate more powerful than any court. To exploit the poor is to take on the LORD as an adversary.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:17

The Thirty Sayings transition — the longer units allow more sustained argument than the single-verse proverb. The shift from observation to instruction is formal as well as substantive.

22:22-23

Do not exploit the poor because they are poor — Alter notes the social ethics are grounded in divine advocacy. The LORD will take up the case of those who have no human advocate. What appears to be exploitation of the weak is actually an offence against the most powerful advocate possible — the God who identifies with the vulnerable.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:17

The Thirty Sayings of the Wise begin — the transition from the terse antithetical proverb to extended instructional address. This section has structural and verbal parallels with the Egyptian Instruction of Amenemope.

22:22-23

Do not exploit the poor because they are poor — Waltke notes the theological ground: exploiting the poor is not merely unjust but theologically dangerous. The LORD takes up their case (yarivu rivam), functioning as their legal advocate. The covenant God who is the go'el of the vulnerable will prosecute those who exploit the people he has identified himself with.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:22

Calvin: 'The LORD will plead the cause of the poor — those who oppress the poor provoke God to be their adversary. Social ethics are grounded in divine character, not merely humanitarian sentiment.'

22:29

A diligent person will stand before kings — Calvin notes this is the practical observation that skill and diligence attract the attention of those in power. This is not flattery of ambition but honest observation: competence rises to its appropriate level of responsibility. The excellent worker does not need to seek visibility; their work will eventually attract it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:17

The NET note on the Thirty Sayings discusses the structural and verbal parallels with the Egyptian Instruction of Amenemope, which has a thirty-chapter structure and similar content in several places. The note surveys the scholarly debate about the direction of dependence and concludes that both texts draw on the common ancient Near Eastern wisdom tradition.

22:22-23

The NET note on riv (take up the case, contend as advocate) explains the legal background: the LORD functions as the poor person's legal representative who will prosecute those who exploit them. The note discusses the comparable covenant-advocacy language throughout the Psalms where YHWH is the defender of the afflicted.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions, especially Egyptian.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs — one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.
The editorial activity shows Proverbs as a living, curated collection.

Reception History

Virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics — the practical outworking of the fear of the LORD.
Practical wisdom
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, governance, and social teaching.

Proverbs and Amenemope: Dependence or Common Tradition?

Most critical scholars
The parallels are too specific to be coincidental — structural and verbal similarity indicates literary relationship.

Shared tradition across cultures is equally plausible and does not require direct literary dependence.
