Proverbs 7

Guard Wisdom Like Life; The Seduction Scene in Full

Verses 1–5 — Guard Wisdom Like the Apple of Your Eye
12345

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The opening (vv.1–5) is the shortest instruction preamble in chs.1–9 — just five verses before the narrative begins. Its urgency is striking: "guard my teachings as the apple of your eye" (v.2). The "apple of your eye" (bābat ʿên, literally "daughter of the eye" — the pupil) is the most instinctively protected thing a person has. That is the level of vigilance required. Verse 4's instruction to call wisdom "sister" and insight "relative" is the positive alternative to the coming scene: if wisdom is your intimate, the adulteress has no hold.

Cross-Reference

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    7:1-5

    MacArthur: The opening call to guard wisdom as the apple of your eye and bind her as a jewel employs intimate imagery — wisdom is not merely studied but treasured. The personification of wisdom as an intimate companion is the counter-image to the seductress who follows. The question is which intimate relationship will govern the young man's life.

    7:4

    MacArthur: Say to wisdom, you are my sister — the kinship language (sister was an endearment term in the ancient world, as in Song of Songs) reflects the covenant relationship with wisdom. Wisdom is not merely a skill set but a companion whose presence protects and delights.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    7:1–5

    The opening address is tighter and more urgent than ch.5's parallel opening. Alter notes the escalating intimacy: in ch.2 wisdom is sought 'like silver'; in ch.4 it is a 'garland'; in ch.7 it is to be called 'my sister' and 'my intimate friend.' The relationship to wisdom has become personal and familial — a closeness that the adulteress will attempt to counterfeit.

    7:6–23

    The seduction scene of vv.6–23 is one of the Hebrew Bible's most accomplished narrative passages. Alter identifies its literary technique: the narrator observes from a window, describes the setting (evening, dark night), then follows the young man's encounter with extraordinary psychological and temporal precision. The narrative shows rather than tells — the reader witnesses the seduction unfolding in real time.

    Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

    Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
    7:1–4

    The opening command to 'bind wisdom on your fingers; write it on the tablet of your heart' is a wisdom version of Deut 6:8 (bind God's commands on your hands; write them on your doorposts). Waltke notes the deliberate echo: the same total embodiment commanded for the Torah is here commanded for wisdom. Wisdom is not a mental concept but a physically internalised orientation.

    7:5

    The purpose of all the instruction in vv.1–4 is stated in v.5: 'that they may keep you from the adulteress.' The entire wisdom-formation programme of chs.1–7 has this practical purpose. Wisdom is not theoretical philosophy but protective character-formation against the specific dangers that destroy young men (and by extension, all people).

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    7:1–4

    Calvin: 'The father urges his son to guard wisdom as the apple of his eye — meaning, to preserve it with the utmost care, as what is most precious and necessary for life. The instructions of God deserve this supreme care, since without them we perish.'

    7:4

    Calvin on calling wisdom 'my sister': 'This intimate address signifies that we ought to cherish wisdom with the same tenderness and love with which we cherish those who are nearest and dearest to us. Wisdom should not be a formal duty but a personal love.'

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    7:2

    The NET note on 'guard my commands as the apple of your eye' (ishon aynekha) explains ishon (pupil of the eye) — the most carefully protected part of the most important sensory organ. To guard the commands 'as the apple of your eye' is to protect them with instinctive, reflexive care. The same phrase appears in Deut 32:10 for God's care for Israel.

    7:4

    The NET note on 'say to wisdom: you are my sister' discusses the sister-term in ancient Near Eastern love poetry (Song of Songs 4:9–12; 5:1–2): 'sister' (achot) is an intimate address for a beloved woman, not a literal sibling. Calling wisdom 'my sister' places the relationship to wisdom in the register of devoted personal love.

    Verses 6–27 — The Seduction Scene: Ox to the Slaughter
    6789101112131415161717192021222324252627

    Hebrew Word Study

    Context

    Chapter 7 is the literary masterpiece of Proverbs 1–9 — a detailed dramatic narrative rather than instruction or aphorism. The father observes a scene from his window (a narrator's device giving the reader an elevated viewpoint the youth lacks). The scene is structured as a seduction with three elements: the approach (setting and timing, vv.8–9), the method (physical initiation, religious claim, sensory appeal, husband's absence, vv.13–20), and the result (ox to slaughter, bird to snare, vv.21–23). The adulteress's use of religious language (vv.14–15: "I fulfilled my vows, I have fellowship offering food") is particularly chilling — she weaponises piety as a seduction technique.

    Cross-Reference

      MacArthur Study Notes

      MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
      7:6-23

      MacArthur: The extended narrative of the seduction is the longest single description of moral failure in Proverbs. The young man's vulnerability is architectural: he goes toward her house at twilight in the path to her house (v.8). He is in the wrong place at the wrong time by his own feet.

      7:21-23

      MacArthur: All at once he followed her, like an ox going to the slaughter. The similes — ox to slaughter, deer to a snare, bird to a trap — all share the feature of the animal's ignorance of its fate. Folly does not announce its destination. The wages of foolishness are paid at the end, not at the beginning.

      Alter — Literary Reading

      Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
      7:10–21

      The seductress's speech in vv.14–20 is the book's most psychologically acute portrait of manipulation. Alter notes she addresses every potential objection: the guilt of sleeping with a married woman (she has paid her vows, v.14); the availability of her husband (long journey, v.19); the abundance of the occasion (rich food, spices, v.17). She creates the illusion of safety and permission simultaneously.

      7:22–27

      The final movement from seduction to mortality (vv.22–27) is achieved through the animal similes (ox, deer, bird) followed by the direct statistical claim: 'many strong men have been slain by her; her house is a highway to the grave.' Alter notes the narrator abandons psychological observation for moral arithmetic — the individual story is subsumed into a pattern: she has done this many times; it always ends the same way.

      Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

      Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
      7:10–20

      The seductress's speech is the book's most elaborately developed folly discourse. Waltke notes three features: (a) she initiates and speaks first (inverting the proper order of courtship); (b) she provides abundant practical justification ('I have made fellowship offerings; the meat is available'); (c) she offers total availability ('my husband is gone on a long journey'). Each element counters a potential resistance point.

      7:22–23

      The three animal similes for the youth's fate — ox to slaughter, deer to snare, bird to trap — form a descending sequence of agency: the ox goes, the deer springs, the bird hastens. Waltke notes the verbs describe the animal's own motion toward destruction. The youth is not dragged; he goes. The final simile ('arrow through his liver') makes the death precise and clinical — this is not romantic tragedy but predictable death.

      Calvin’s Commentary

      John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
      7:10

      Calvin on 'dressed like a prostitute, with crafty intent': 'The evil woman does not announce her nature — she disguises it. The father warns his son to recognise her by her approach, not her appearance. This is the strategy of all temptation: to present itself in the most attractive possible form.'

      7:26

      Calvin on 'many are the victims she has brought down': 'The father appeals to experience — not just to principle but to the observable pattern of destruction. History is the teacher here: look at what this path has done to others. The wise man learns from others' destruction; the fool must experience his own.'

      NET Bible Notes

      NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
      7:14

      The NET note on 'I have offered peace offerings; today I have paid my vows' explains that fellowship offerings (zevach shelamim) produced meat that had to be consumed within two days (Lev 7:15–18). The woman is using the religious calendar — she has offered a sacrifice and has abundant meat that must be eaten now — to create practical urgency and to give the encounter a quasi-religious legitimacy.

      7:23

      The NET note on 'an arrow pierces his liver' (chatsa bavedoh) explains the liver as the seat of vital life in Hebrew physiology (parallel to the heart). An arrow through the liver is a mortal wound. The clinical specificity of the body part — not just 'he dies' but a precise lethal injury — is characteristic of Proverbs' insistence on concrete consequences over abstract warnings.

      Not named, not evil — just profoundly unobservant. He positions himself for disaster without recognising it.
      The adulteress

      Ancient Sources

      Egyptian Love Poetry
      The sensory details of vv.16–18 (coloured linens from Egypt, myrrh, aloes, cinnamon) parallel Egyptian love poetry. The Song of Songs uses similar imagery positively. Proverbs uses the same sensory richness to describe the seduction — acknowledging the reality of its appeal while exposing its lethal nature.
      The sensory language places the adulteress scene within the ancient love-poetry tradition it parodies.
      Narrative Wisdom
      The shift from instruction to narrative in ch.7 is deliberate. Narrative engages moral imagination in a way that precept cannot. The reader is put in the observer's position (like the father at the window) and asked to see what the youth cannot see. This is wisdom education through narrative.
      The narrative form makes the reader a wiser observer than the protagonist.

      Reception History

      Thomas Aquinas
      Used Prov 7 to illustrate the process of temptation: proximity to occasion of sin → engagement of the senses → capture of the will. The youth's three steps (wrong place, wrong direction, wrong time) became a standard analysis of how temptation operates.
      CS Lewis, The Screwtape Letters
      The senior devil's advice to use legitimate religious language and social contexts to draw humans into sin mirrors the adulteress's use of her fellowship offering as a seduction tool.

      Literary Structure

      The Seductress: Wisdom's Anti-Image7:6-23The woman described in ch.7 is the precise counter-image of Lady Wisdom in ch.8-9: both invite the young man to her house; both promise satisfaction; both make a meal. The contrast is structural: which invitation will he accept?
      The Path to Her House7:25-27Her house is the way to Sheol, going down to the chambers of death. The spatial and directional language (path, turning, her house, going down) traces a descent that begins with casual proximity and ends in death.

      Scholarly Debates

      The Seductress of Proverbs 7: Moral Warning or Misogynist Portrait?
      A genuine moral warning, not anti-woman
      Waltke, Fox, most evangelical commentators
      The text addresses a specific social danger — the adulteress who preys on young men — and describes her methods with psychological precision. The father's purpose is protective, not misogynist. Lady Wisdom (ch.8) is the counter-figure, and she is also a woman. Proverbs does not associate women with danger but associates a specific moral choice (adultery) with danger for both parties.
      A gendered discourse that encodes patriarchal anxiety
      Feminist scholars (Camp, Brenner)
      The consistent portrayal of the threatening 'other' as a woman in chs.1–9 encodes a patriarchal anxiety about female sexuality and agency. The implied reader is male; danger is coded female; wisdom is domesticated as 'sister' or 'wife.' The gendered structure of chs.1–9 reflects the social arrangements of ancient patriarchy more than eternal moral truth.
      Both concerns are legitimate and need holding together
      Longman, Yoder
      The text has a genuine moral concern (adultery destroys) and a genuine gendered structure (the implied student is male, the threat is female). Both can be acknowledged: the moral concern is true regardless of the gendered framing; the gendered framing reflects its historical context without invalidating the moral point. Contemporary application requires both taking the moral concern seriously and reading the gendered framing contextually.
      The 'both' position is most honest. The moral concern — that adultery destroys — is true and cross-culturally applicable. The gendered framing is genuinely patriarchal in its address (son, not daughter) and its imaging (female threat, male vulnerability). The wisest pastoral reading applies the moral principle universally while acknowledging the historical context of the literary form.
