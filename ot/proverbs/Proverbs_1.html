Proverbs 1

The Prologue; Lady Wisdom Speaks — Purpose, Invitation, Warning

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–7 — The Prologue: Purpose and Thesis
1The proverbs of Solomon son of David, king of Israel:2for gaining wisdom and instruction; for understanding words of insight;3for receiving instruction in prudent behavior, doing what is right and just and fair;4for giving prudence to those who are simple, knowledge and discretion to the young—5let the wise listen and add to their learning, and let the discerning get guidance—6for understanding proverbs and parables, the sayings and riddles of the wise.7The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge, but fools despise wisdom and instruction.

Hebrew Word Study

יִרְאַת יְהוָה yirʾat yhwh — fear of the LORD: The governing phrase of the entire book. Yirʾāh is not terror but reverential awe — a recognition of who God is that reshapes all perception of reality. It is not the beginning of wisdom in the sense of a first step after which one moves on; it is the beginning in the sense of the foundational principle without which nothing else is rightly understood.

חָכְמָה ḥokmāh — wisdom: Not abstract philosophical wisdom but practical skill in living — the ability to navigate life in alignment with created order. The same word is used of Bezalel's craftsmanship (Exod 31:3). Wisdom is both intellectual and practical: it sees truly and acts accordingly.

מוּסָר mûsār — instruction/discipline: From the root yāsar (to chastise, correct, discipline). Wisdom is not merely received; it is formed through correction. The willingness to receive mûsār — to accept correction from father, teacher, and ultimately from God — is the distinguishing mark of the wise over the fool.

Context

The prologue (vv.1–7) is the hermeneutical key to the entire book. It announces who wrote it, who it is for (the simple, the young — but also the wise, v.5), and what it aims to produce: not just moral behaviour but a way of perceiving reality. The thesis statement is v.7: the fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge. Everything that follows is an elaboration of this claim.

Cross-Reference

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
    1:2–4

    Six purpose statements in vv.2–4 define the book's aims: wisdom (ḥokmāh), instruction (mûsār), understanding (bînāh), prudent behaviour, righteousness, and discretion. These are not separate virtues but facets of a single integrated vision of human flourishing — the life lived in alignment with the way things actually are.

    1:7

    "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge." This verse is the thesis of Proverbs, Psalms 111, and in a sense the entire wisdom tradition. It is not saying that religion is the starting point of education. It is saying that the right relationship to God — worshipful, submissive, trusting — is the epistemological foundation without which reality cannot be rightly read. The fool of Proverbs is not primarily stupid; he is someone who has attempted to navigate life without this foundation.

    1:1–7

    Alter notes the prologue's rhetorical sophistication: it opens with a chain of purpose clauses (vv.2–6) that build through synonymous parallelism, each pair of terms reinforcing the previous, until the chain reaches its thesis in v.7. The structure is itself an enactment of wisdom's discipline — ordered, cumulative, purposeful.

    1:20–33

    Lady Wisdom's public address in vv.20–33 is the book's first extended poetic speech. Alter notes her placement at the city gate, the crossroads, the public square — she is not a hidden esoteric treasure but a publicly available, urgent voice. The dramatic irony is that the very people who need her most are the ones who ignore her.

    1:1–6

    Waltke identifies the prologue (vv.1–7) as the book's programmatic statement. The fourfold purpose (wisdom, instruction, understanding, prudence) defines what the entire collection aims to produce in the reader. The prologue does not merely introduce the book; it establishes the epistemological framework: wisdom is not a collection of clever observations but a disciplined orientation of the whole person toward reality as God defines it.

    1:7

    'The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom' is the thesis statement of Proverbs and of OT wisdom literature as a whole. Waltke notes that 'beginning' (reshit) means not merely the starting point but the controlling principle — the foundational epistemological commitment from which all true wisdom derives. Without this orientation, the most brilliant human observation remains 'folly' in the biblical sense.

    1:7

    Calvin on the fear of the LORD: 'This is the chief principle of wisdom — not that timid terror which drives us from God, but that filial reverence which makes us submit to him as children to a beloved father.' The fear is not servile dread but covenantal orientation — the posture of the creature rightly related to its Creator.

    1:24–27

    Calvin on wisdom's withdrawal when refused: 'As it is the height of wickedness to reject God when he calls us, so it is just that he should laugh at those who refused him.' The comedic reversal — those who laughed at wisdom will be laughed at in their calamity — is divine justice in its most ironic form.

    1:7

    The NET note on reshit (beginning) explains it means 'first principle' or 'chief part' rather than merely 'starting point.' The note traces the phrase through Ps 111:10 and Job 28:28, establishing it as the motto of the entire wisdom tradition. Fear of the LORD is not a preparatory stage before wisdom; it is wisdom's essential epistemological foundation.

    1:20

    The NET note on Hokhmot (plural of wisdom, 'Lady Wisdom') discusses whether the plural is a plural of majesty or a personification. Waltke reads it as intensive plural — wisdom in its fullest, most complete expression. The personification of wisdom as a woman is a distinctive Proverbs device developed across chs.1–9 and reaching its apex in ch.8.

    Verses 8–19 — The First Address: The Enticement of Violent Men
    8Listen, my son, to your father's instruction and do not forsake your mother's teaching.9They are a garland to grace your head and a chain to adorn your neck.10My son, if sinful men entice you, do not give in to them.11If they say, "Come along with us; let's lie in wait for innocent blood, let's ambush some harmless soul;12let's swallow them alive, like the grave, and whole, like those who go down to the pit;13we will get all sorts of valuable things and fill our houses with plunder;14cast in your lot with us, and we will share a common purse"—15my son, do not go along with them, do not set foot on their paths;16for their feet rush into evil, they are swift to shed blood.17How useless to spread a net where every bird can see it!18These men lie in wait for their own blood; they ambush only themselves!19Such are the paths of all who go after ill-gotten gain; it takes away the life of those who get it.

    Hebrew Word Study

    Context

    The first address (vv.8–19) introduces the book's characteristic form: a father speaking to his son. This is not merely a literary device. Proverbs is rooted in the family as the primary institution of wisdom transmission — both parents are named (v.8). The specific temptation addressed here is the gang of violent men who offer belonging, wealth, and excitement. The counter-argument is not moral but practical: they are setting a trap that will catch themselves (v.18). The fool's error is not just wickedness but stupidity — he cannot see what is obvious.

    Cross-Reference

    • Ps 1:1The first psalm parallels the first address: "Blessed is the one who does not walk in step with the wicked or stand in the way that sinners take." Both texts begin with the fork between the two ways.
    • Rom 3:15–17Paul quotes vv.15–16 in his indictment of humanity in Romans 3 — Proverbs' description of the violent becomes part of the universal diagnosis of sin.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
    1:10

    "If sinful men entice you" — the word for entice (pātāh) is the same used for seduction in Samson's story and elsewhere. The violent gang uses the language of friendship, belonging, and shared adventure. This is the anatomy of peer pressure: it appeals to the legitimate desire for community and replaces it with a counterfeit that destroys. The father's instruction is the true offer of belonging.

    1:17–18

    The ironic reversal: the hunters become the hunted. The men who lie in wait for others are, in reality, lying in wait for themselves. This is Proverbs' consistent logic: wickedness is not merely wrong — it is self-destructive. The universe is built so that the foolish path leads back on its walker.

    1:8

    The parallel 'instruction of your father... teaching of your mother' is the book's foundational social structure. Alter notes the equal weight given to father and mother — wisdom is transmitted through the entire covenantal household, not through a single patriarchal voice. The mother's teaching (torah) carries the same authoritative weight as the father's discipline (musar).

    1:10–19

    The sinners' speech is a literary set-piece: Alter notes its deliberate mimicry of wisdom's rhetoric. The gangsters promise community ('we shall all share'), describe their target with precision, and invite the youth into a fellowship of violence. The speech is attractive — and deadly. The book teaches discernment partly by showing the form that anti-wisdom takes.

    1:8–19

    The first parental address (vv.8–19) develops the book's central pedagogical structure: the wise parent instructing the attentive child. Waltke notes the address pattern ('my son, hear') occurs throughout chs.1–9 and frames the entire collection as a father's transmission of covenant wisdom to the next generation. The sinners' enticement (vv.10–19) is the book's first extended anti-wisdom speech.

    1:10–19

    The gang's recruiting speech in vv.11–14 is presented with deliberate irony: they promise wealth and shared plunder, but Waltke notes v.18's reversal — 'these men lie in wait for their own blood.' The violence they promise to inflict on others rebounds on themselves. The proverb-within-the-address (v.17: the bird sees the net) underlines the fatal self-deception of those who think they can profit from evil without consequence.

    1:8

    Calvin on parental instruction: 'God has specially committed the charge of the young to parents, not only to educate their bodies but to instruct their minds. This is the most excellent of all callings — to form the minds of children in the fear of God.' Parental wisdom-transmission is not merely cultural practice but covenant obligation.

    1:15–16

    Calvin on separating from sinners: 'The first step of departure from evil companions is the most difficult, but nothing less than complete separation is demanded.' The command to 'not walk in the way with them' (v.15) is a total relational reorientation, not a gradual withdrawal.

    1:9

    The NET note on 'a garland for your head and a chain for your neck' explains these are symbols of honour in ancient Near Eastern culture — the rewards of the successful. The point is that wisdom-shaped character produces visible honour, not that wisdom is a technique for social advancement.

    1:17

    The NET note on the bird-and-net proverb explains the interpretive debate: does v.17 mean (a) it is futile to set a trap in plain sight because birds see it and escape (the sinners are as obvious as a visible net), or (b) even a visible trap catches birds (the sinner is so foolish he walks into a trap he can see). Most commentators prefer (a): the simile mocks the gangsters' self-destruction as obvious to everyone but themselves.

    Verses 20–33 — Lady Wisdom Speaks: The Open Offer and Its Rejection
    20Out in the open wisdom calls aloud, she raises her voice in the public square;21on top of the wall she cries out, at the city gate she makes her speech:22"How long will you who are simple love your simple ways? How long will mockers delight in mockery and fools hate knowledge?23Repent at my rebuke! Then I will pour out my thoughts to you, I will make known to you my teachings.24But since you refuse to listen when I call and no one pays attention when I stretch out my hand,25since you disregard all my advice and do not accept my rebuke,26I in turn will laugh when disaster strikes you; I will mock when calamity overtakes you—27when calamity overtakes you like a storm, when disaster sweeps over you like a whirlwind, when distress and trouble overwhelm you.28"Then they will call to me but I will not answer; they will look for me but will not find me,29since they hated knowledge and did not choose to fear the LORD.30Since they would not accept my advice and spurned my rebuke,31they will eat the fruit of their ways and be filled with the fruit of their schemes.32For the waywardness of the simple will kill them, and the complacency of fools will destroy them;33but whoever listens to me will live in safety and be at ease, without fear of harm."

    Hebrew Word Study

    חָכְמוֹת ḥokmôt — Wisdom (personified): The plural form (lit. "wisdoms") used for the personified figure — a grammatical intensification suggesting the fullness or completeness of wisdom. Wisdom here is not an abstraction but a person who speaks, calls, is rejected, and promises consequences. The NT identifies this personified Wisdom with Christ (1 Cor 1:30).

    שׁוּבוּ šûbû — repent / turn back: The standard OT word for repentance — a turning, a change of direction. Wisdom's offer is not passive; she calls for a concrete change of trajectory. The offer is open now; the passage warns it will close.

    Context

    Lady Wisdom's public speech (vv.20–33) is one of the most dramatic passages in Proverbs. She does not speak in the classroom or the temple but in the public square, at the city gate — the busiest, most exposed place in ancient city life. Her message is urgent and eventually terrible: the time for response is now; those who mock and refuse will find that calamity comes, and when it does, she will not answer. The laughter of v.26 is not cruelty but the judicial irony of consequences — the same mockers who laughed at wisdom will find calamity laughing back.

      MacArthur Study Notes

      MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
      1:20–21

      Wisdom in the street. The gospel offer of wisdom is public, accessible, universal — not hidden in esoteric tradition or available only to the elite. She is at the city gate where everyone passes. The tragedy of the fool is not lack of access but lack of attention.

      1:24–28

      The terrible reversal: "Then they will call to me but I will not answer." This is not an arbitrary divine rejection but the inevitable consequence of hardened refusal. There is a point at which persistent rejection of wisdom closes the door. Proverbs is remarkably sober about this — wisdom is not infinitely patient in the face of deliberate, sustained mockery. The NT parallel is the parable of the ten virgins: "I don't know you" (Matt 25:12).

      1:20–21

      The locations of Wisdom's speech — street, squares, head of the noisy streets, entrance of the city gates — map the social geography of the ancient city. Alter notes these are precisely the places where commerce, legal proceedings, and public life converge. Wisdom is not a private spiritual discipline but a public, social reality available at the intersection of all human activity.

      1:28–31

      The reversal — those who refused Wisdom's call now call and find she does not answer — is one of Proverbs' most structurally precise points. Alter notes the verbal echo: Wisdom 'cried out' (v.20) and they did not answer; now they 'call' (v.28) and she does not answer. The literary symmetry enacts the moral principle.

      1:20–23

      Wisdom's public invitation (vv.20–33) is the book's most urgent passage. Waltke notes the shift from third-person address (vv.1–19) to Wisdom's own first-person speech — the personification takes over the discourse. Wisdom's public position (city gate, marketplace, head of the streets) emphasises her total availability: she is not hidden knowledge for the elite but publicly offered, urgently presented wisdom for all.

      1:24–31

      The withdrawal of wisdom in vv.24–31 is Proverbs' most sobering passage. Waltke notes the conditional structure: wisdom's availability (vv.20–23) is met by refusal (v.24–25), followed by the reversal — calamity comes and wisdom laughs (v.26). The laughter is not sadism but the disclosure of the inevitable: those who chose folly now experience what folly produces.

      Calvin: 'Wisdom cries abroad — God is not satisfied with communicating himself inwardly to a few, but wishes to make his saving truth available to all.' The public nature of Wisdom's address reflects Calvin's doctrine of common grace: the knowledge that produces good life is not reserved for the elect but universally offered, universally available, and universally rejected by the foolish.

      1:29–31

      Calvin on the consequences of refusing wisdom: 'They who stop their ears against sound doctrine, and harden themselves against God, are left to the consequences of their own folly — which is no other than what they chose for themselves.' The judgment is not arbitrary external punishment but the natural fruit of the choices made.

      Chapter-Level Scholarship

      People of the Chapter

      Solomon
      Author, king of Israel
      Son of David; the wisest man in the ancient world (1 Kgs 4:31). The prologue grounds the book in his authority as both king and sage.
      The father
      The speaking voice
      Not identified individually — the universal father addressing the universal son. Both parents are named in v.8, establishing the family as the school of wisdom.
      Lady Wisdom
      Personified Wisdom
      First appears here crying in the streets. She will develop into the cosmic figure of ch.8, present at creation.
      The sinful men
      The first tempters
      Violent men who offer belonging and profit. Their appeal is real; their path is self-destructive.

      NoteThe Hebrew rēʾšît ("beginning") can mean "first in a series" or "the foundational principle." Most commentators read it as the latter: fear of the LORD is not step one but the epistemological foundation.

      Ancient Sources

      ANE Instruction Literature
      Proverbs shares the "father-to-son" address form with Egyptian wisdom texts: the Instruction of Amenemope, the Instruction of Ptahhotep, and the Instruction of Any. These texts address proper conduct, speech, and relationships. Proverbs shares the form but radically recentres it on the LORD rather than on pharaoh or social advancement.
      The parallels in form highlight the theological distinction in content.
      Personification of Wisdom
      Lady Wisdom in vv.20–33 has no precise parallel in ANE literature, though Egyptian Maʿat (order/truth) is sometimes personified. The biblical figure is more dramatically personal — she speaks, calls, is rejected, and responds with judicial consequence.
      Lady Wisdom is a distinctively biblical development of the ANE wisdom tradition.

      Reception History

      NT Christology
      Paul identifies Christ as "the wisdom of God" (1 Cor 1:30) and "in whom are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge" (Col 2:3). Lady Wisdom's cry in the streets is heard in Jesus' public teaching in the Temple courts.
      Jewish tradition
      The Talmud (b. Shabb. 30b) records debates about Proverbs' canonical status but ultimately affirms it. Ben Sira (Sirach 24) extensively develops the personified Wisdom motif, identifying Wisdom with Torah.
      Augustine
      Used Prov 1:7 as the foundation of his educational philosophy: true learning begins with the right posture before God. "Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee" (Confessions) echoes the book's thesis.

      Literary Structure

      Proverbs 1's prologue does not merely introduce the book — it establishes the epistemological framework for all that follows. The fear of the LORD is not one virtue among many but the foundational orientation from which all wisdom proceeds. Every proverb in the collection is an application of this principle: what does it look like to see reality as God defines it?
      Wisdom Invites; Folly Imitates9:1-6,13-18Both Lady Wisdom (ch.9) and Folly (9:13-18) issue identical invitations. The structural parallel is deliberate: wisdom and folly are competing invitations in identical forms. The choice between them requires discernment, not just desire.

      Textual Notes

      1:7'The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom'
      MT: yir'at YHWH reshit da'at (the fear of the LORD is the beginning/chief part of knowledge). LXX: archē sophias phobos kyriou (the beginning of wisdom is the fear of the Lord) — LXX uses sophia (wisdom) where MT uses da'at (knowledge). The same statement appears at 9:10 with hokhmah (wisdom) rather than da'at. Prov 15:33 and Ps 111:10 parallel.
      The MT/LXX variation (knowledge vs. wisdom) is theologically minor since da'at and hokhmah are near-synonyms in Proverbs. More significant is 'beginning' (reshit): it means chief/controlling principle, not merely chronological starting point. The fear of the LORD is not a preliminary stage one passes through to reach wisdom — it is wisdom's foundational epistemological condition.

      Scholarly Debates

      The Personification of Wisdom in Proverbs 1–9: Poetic Device or Ontological Reality?
      Poetic personification only
      Most critical scholars (McKane, Murphy)
      Lady Wisdom is a literary device — personified wisdom as the ideal that the parent urges the son to pursue. The figure has no independent ontological status; it is a rhetorical vehicle for the book's ethical teaching. The background is ancient Near Eastern wisdom literature's tendency to personify abstract qualities.
      Pre-existent cosmic Wisdom with ontological status
      Waltke, Loader, Ringgren
      Prov 8:22–31 presents Wisdom as present at creation, 'beside God' as a master craftsman/delight. This is not mere rhetorical flourish but a claim about a real divine attribute that has quasi-personal status. The NT's identification of Christ as the wisdom of God (1 Cor 1:30; Col 1:15–20) develops this as Christological: Wisdom in Prov 8 is the pre-existent Son.
      Both: literary figure pointing to divine reality
      Fox, Clifford
      Lady Wisdom is simultaneously a literary device AND a pointer toward divine reality. The personification is real as personification and significant as theology. The question of ontological status is not the right question — the text uses the figure to make claims about the character of divine wisdom that the literal-or-figurative binary cannot capture.
      The Christological interpretation (Waltke's position) is strongly supported by the NT's development of the theme (Col 1:15–17; John 1:1–3; Heb 1:2–3). The created/uncreated debate about Prov 8:22 (LXX: 'created me' vs. 'possessed me') has been central to Trinitarian controversy since Origen and Arius. The strongest reading treats Wisdom in Prov 8 as a genuine theological portrait of the divine attribute that becomes incarnate in Christ.

      Theological Themes Tracker

      Proverbs 1 establishes the book's entire epistemological and pedagogical framework. The fear of the LORD is the thesis; Lady Wisdom's public address is the first application; the sinners' enticement is the first counter-example. The chapter trains the reader to see two competing voices — Wisdom urgent and public, folly attractive and deadly — and to choose.
