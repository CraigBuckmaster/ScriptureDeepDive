Proverbs 14

Wise Woman Builds; Each Heart's Own Bitterness; Fear of the LORD as Fortress

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–18 — Wise Woman Builds; Each Heart's Bitterness; Way Seeming Right

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half opens with the wise woman as builder (v.1). V.10 is an island of interiority: each heart knows its own bitterness. V.12 is the chapter's most sobering verse: self-perceived rightness is not a reliable guide.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 9:1Wisdom has built her house -- the wise woman of 14:1 enacts what Lady Wisdom does.
  • Matt 7:13-14The way that seems right but leads to death anticipates the Sermon's broad road.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
14:1-15

MacArthur: The fear of the LORD is a fountain of life, turning a person from the snares of death (v.27). The chapter's theological anchor is placed near the midpoint, not the opening. The fear of the LORD generates the practical wisdom illustrated in the surrounding proverbs.

14:12

MacArthur: There is a way that appears to be right, but in the end it leads to death. The proverb is among Proverbs' most theologically important: the subjective sense of rightness is not a reliable guide. The way that feels correct can lead to destruction. Wisdom requires external correction — the fear of the LORD — not merely internal conviction.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:1–13

The first half of ch.14 moves through a series of observations about appearance and reality: things that look one way but are another. The empty barn that has no grain (v.4); the smooth-talking person who is not wise (v.7); the path that appears right but ends in death (v.12). Alter notes the appearance-reality theme is ch.14's structural interest — wisdom is the capacity to see through appearance to reality.

14:10

'Each heart knows its own bitterness, and no one else can share its joy.' This is one of Proverbs' most psychologically intimate proverbs. Alter notes the observation is about the limits of human empathy: there is a dimension of inner experience — both suffering and joy — that is fundamentally private. No external observer, however wise, can fully know another person's inner life.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:1–7

The 'wise woman who builds her house' (v.1) is one of Proverbs' most compact portraits of wisdom in action. Waltke notes the woman as wisdom's embodiment in ch.14 anticipates the Eshet Chayil of ch.31 — both are women whose wisdom is expressed in household management, social care, and economic productivity. The foolish woman who tears her house down with her own hands is the counter-portrait.

14:12

'There is a way that appears to be right, but in the end it leads to death.' This proverb (repeated verbatim in 16:25) is Proverbs' most sobering epistemological warning. Waltke notes the danger is not the obviously wrong path but the path that appears right — that feels like wisdom, is internally justified, is socially approved, but ends in death. The fear of the LORD is the corrective because only divine wisdom can distinguish appearance from reality in the deepest matters.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:4

Calvin: 'Where there are no oxen, the manger is clean — but much revenue comes by the strength of the ox. This proverb warns against the tidiness of non-effort: the barn that has no grain also has no mess. True productivity requires the acceptance of disorder, difficulty, and labour. The person who keeps their life neat by avoiding responsibility is keeping an empty barn.'

14:34

Calvin: 'Righteousness exalts a nation, but sin is a reproach to any people. This is the most important principle of political theology in Proverbs: the measure of a nation's greatness is not its military power, economic output, or cultural achievement, but its righteousness — its fidelity to the moral order that God has established.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:4

The NET note on 'where there are no oxen the manger is empty, but from the strength of an ox come abundant harvests' discusses the agricultural economics: oxen were the primary agricultural power source for plowing. No oxen = no harvest, but also no messing the barn. The proverb teaches that productivity requires accepting the costs (mess, expense) of the tools needed to produce it.

14:12

The NET note on 'a way that seems right to a man' (derekh yashar lifney ish) discusses the subjective character of human moral reasoning — what seems right to the individual knower may be fatally wrong. The note traces the appearance-reality gap through Proverbs: the simple are led astray by appearances (9:17); the mocker mistakes his folly for wisdom (14:6). Only the fear of the LORD provides an external standard.

Verses 19–35 — Fear of the LORD: Fortress and Fountain; Kindness to the Poor Honours God
1920212223242526272829303132333435

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half addresses poverty (vv.20-21,31), the fear of the LORD as fortress and fountain (vv.26-27), peace-heart versus envying-bones (v.30), and righteousness as national exaltation (v.34). The chapter's theological centre: how you treat the poor reveals your relationship to their Maker.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 13:14Fountain of life -- 14:27 applies the same image to the fear of the LORD.
  • Matt 25:40Whatever you did for the least -- the NT fulfilment of v.31's identification of the poor with their Maker.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
14:19-35

MacArthur: Whoever oppresses the poor shows contempt for their Maker, but whoever is kind to the needy honors God (v.31). The theological grounding of social ethics: how you treat the vulnerable is how you regard their Creator. Covenant ethics toward the poor is not humanitarian sentiment but theological statement about the nature of God.

14:34

MacArthur: Righteousness exalts a nation, but sin condemns any people. The proverb extends wisdom ethics from the individual and household to the national level. Proverbs is not merely personal ethics — its principles, applied at scale, govern the rise and fall of nations.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:20–31

The poverty theology of vv.20–31 is the chapter's most challenging material. Alter notes the sequence: the poor are despised by their neighbours (v.20), which is then characterised as sin (v.21 explicitly), and ultimately as contempt for God (v.31). The progression is damning: social indifference to the poor begins as a fact of life (v.20) and ends as theological blasphemy (v.31). The proverbs force the reader to follow the logic all the way.

14:34

Righteousness exalts a nation, but sin is a reproach to any people' (v.34) is Proverbs' most politically comprehensive proverb. Alter notes the 'nation' (goy) is unusual — most Proverbs concerns individuals; this concerns peoples and nations. The political application of the wisdom principles is significant: the same moral order that governs personal life also governs communal and national life.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:19–35

The second section of ch.14 develops the social and political dimensions of wisdom. Waltke notes the poor-and-rich cluster (vv.20–21, 31) brackets the section: the poor are despised by neighbours (v.20) but to oppress the poor is to insult their Maker (v.31). Between these poles, wisdom ethics about generosity, truth, and national righteousness are developed.

14:31

'Whoever oppresses the poor shows contempt for their Maker, but whoever is kind to the needy honours God.' This is Proverbs' most direct statement of the theological basis of poverty-ethics. Waltke notes the argument is not primarily social (the poor are human too) but theological: God is the Maker of the poor. To treat the poor contemptuously is to express contempt for God's creative act in making them.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:27

Calvin: 'The fear of the LORD is a fountain of life, turning a person from the snares of death — this is the comprehensive benefit of the foundational virtue. All the specific wisdoms of the proverb collections flow from this single source: right orientation toward God produces right orientation toward everything else.'

14:31

Calvin on honouring God through the poor: 'Whatever honour or dishonour is done to the poor is done to God, since they are his creatures and bear his image. The rich who despise the poor are forgetting that God is their common Maker, and that the difference in their conditions is God's appointment, not nature's inevitability.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:27

The NET note on 'the fear of the LORD is a fountain of life, turning a person from the snares of death' connects to 10:11 ('mouth of the righteous is a fountain of life') and 13:14 ('the teaching of the wise is a fountain of life'). The fountain-of-life image is applied to three things: the righteous person's speech, the wise teacher's instruction, and the fear of the LORD. All three are expressions of the same reality.

14:34

The NET note on 'righteousness exalts a nation' discusses the political-theological claim: tsedaqah (righteousness, covenant faithfulness) at the national level produces national exaltation (rum, lifting up). The note discusses whether this is a promise (faithfulness guarantees national prosperity) or an observation (faithful nations generally flourish). The note favours the latter, given Proverbs' general preference for observable principles over mechanical guarantees.

Scholarly Debates

Proverbs 14:34 and Political Theology: Can Nations Be 'Righteous'?
Yes: nations are moral agents with covenant obligations
Calvin, many Reformed theologians
Nations can be and are evaluated by the moral standards of wisdom — not as covenant communities in the OT-Israel sense, but as corporate entities whose policies and cultures reflect righteousness or wickedness. Prov 14:34 applies the personal wisdom ethics to national life directly.
The proverb applies to Israel specifically, not all nations
Some OT scholars
The political theology of this proverb is primarily addressed to Israel as a covenant nation under God's specific governance. Applying it directly to all nations requires the kind of general natural-law extension that is theologically defensible but goes beyond the verse's original context.
Both personal and political application is valid
Most evangelical scholars
Proverbs 14:34's formulation is universal — 'any people' (leumim), not specifically Israel. The general wisdom-principle that moral faithfulness produces flourishing and wickedness produces reproach is a natural-law observation as much as a covenant promise, applicable to all human communities.
The universal application is well-grounded in the proverb's own language ('any people'). The Proverbs tradition consistently makes its observations about the nature of reality universally — what is true for the wise individual is true, in corresponding ways, for wise communities and wise nations. The moral order is not limited to individuals.
