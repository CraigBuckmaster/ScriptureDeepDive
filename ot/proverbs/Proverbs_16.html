Proverbs 16

Divine Sovereignty in Human Planning; Pride Before a Fall; Gracious Words

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–17 — Divine Sovereignty in Human Planning; Better than Gold

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half contains the most concentrated theology of divine sovereignty in Proverbs. Vv.1-9 form a unit: human plans, divine outcomes, committed works, proper ends, weighed motives. The king section (vv.10-15) applies this to political authority.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 37:5Commit your way to the LORD — the same gālaḵ verb as v.3.
  • Prov 19:21Many are the plans in a person's heart, but it is the LORD's purpose that prevails.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
16:1-16

MacArthur: To humans belong the plans of the heart, but from the LORD comes the proper answer of the tongue (v.1). The cluster of sovereignty proverbs in vv.1-9 is one of Proverbs' most theologically concentrated passages. Human planning is real; divine governance is ultimate. Both are affirmed; neither cancels the other.

16:9

MacArthur: In their hearts humans plan their course, but the LORD establishes their steps. The tension between human planning and divine governance is not resolved by eliminating one of the poles. Plan as if the outcome depends on you; trust as if it depends entirely on God. Wisdom holds both.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:1–9

The divine-sovereignty cluster of vv.1–9 is the theological climax of Proverbs' treatment of the relationship between human planning and divine governance. Alter notes the cluster does not resolve the tension but holds it: humans plan (vv.1, 3, 9); the LORD determines (vv.1, 3, 9). The same human activity appears in both halves — planning — and the theological claim is that it occurs within and is governed by divine sovereignty, not that it is replaced by it.

16:9

'A person plans their steps, but the LORD establishes their direction.' Alter notes the precise complementarity of this verse: human planning is not invalidated but directed. The person plans (the activity is real and proper); the LORD establishes (the ultimate determination is divine). This is the Proverbs version of what Paul articulates in Phil 2:12–13: 'work out your salvation... for it is God who works in you.'

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:1–9

Proverbs 16 opens with the most concentrated cluster of divine-sovereignty proverbs in the book. Waltke notes vv.1–9 form a theological unit: all human planning is enclosed within divine sovereignty. V.1 (human heart plans; the tongue's answer is from the LORD), v.3 (commit to the LORD; plans will succeed), v.9 (human heart devises the way; the LORD directs the steps). The sovereignty is not fatalistic but covenantal: the human plans; God determines the outcome.

16:5

'The LORD detests all the proud of heart. Be sure of this: they will not go unpunished.' The divine hatred of pride reappears here as the theological ground of the pride-falls-before-destruction proverb (v.18). Waltke notes that pride (ge'eh lev, proud of heart) is the root form of the zadon (arrogance) that 11:2 identified as wisdom's enemy. The repetition across chapters ensures the reader internalises the connection.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:3

Calvin: 'Commit your work to the LORD — this is not an excuse for idleness but the posture of trust that enables genuine effort. The person who commits their work to God is free to work wholeheartedly, trusting that the outcome is in better hands than their own. The person who trusts only in their own effort is burdened with a governance responsibility they cannot bear.'

16:9

Calvin: 'This is one of Proverbs' most profound theological observations: the same verse teaches both human responsibility (the person plans) and divine sovereignty (the LORD directs). Both are true simultaneously. The mystery of Providence is not that God overrides human choice but that he governs human choice from within, directing what the person genuinely plans.')

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:1

The NET note on 'from the LORD comes the proper answer of the tongue' (umi'YHWH ma'aneh halashon) discusses the interpretive options: (a) God ultimately controls what people say; (b) the wise answer to any question comes from divine wisdom. The note favours (b) in the context of the chapter's divine-sovereignty cluster: the human tongue's wisest responses derive from the wisdom that God provides.

16:9

The NET note on 'a person plans their course, but the LORD establishes their steps' discusses yakhon (establishes, makes firm, directs). The note traces this sovereignty vocabulary through the chapter (v.1: ma'aneh lashon from the LORD; v.3: plans established; v.9: steps directed) to identify the consistent claim: human agency is real and productive within divine sovereignty that encloses and governs it.

Verses 18–33 — Pride Before a Fall; Gracious Words like Honey; Patient Over Warrior

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half contains pride before a fall (v.18) and better patient than a warrior (v.32). The repeated "way seeming right" (v.25 = 14:12) signals its importance. V.33 returns to the opening sovereignty theme: even the lot is the LORD's.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 14:12Way that appears right but leads to death — the exact repetition signals this is among Proverbs' most crucial warnings.
  • Jas 4:6God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble — v.18 is the negative side of this principle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
16:18-33

MacArthur: Pride goes before destruction, a haughty spirit before a fall (v.18). One of Proverbs' most cited verses, and one of its most misquoted. The original does not say pride comes before a fall — it says pride comes before destruction. The sequence is: pride, then the destruction that makes the fall possible.

16:33

MacArthur: The lot is cast into the lap, but its every decision is from the LORD. The chapter closes with the sovereignty proverb that brackets it: even the apparently random outcomes of the lot are governed by God. Proverbs affirms both the reality of chance and the sovereignty that governs what appears to be chance.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:18

'Pride goes before destruction, a haughty spirit before a fall.' Alter notes the grammar: 'goes before' means 'leads to,' not merely 'precedes.' Pride is the condition that makes destruction inevitable — not because God is sending it as punishment, but because the proud person has positioned themselves outside the reality-orientation that wisdom provides. The fall is the natural consequence of the epistemological failure that pride represents.

16:24

'Gracious words are a honeycomb, sweet to the soul and healing to the bones.' The honeycomb image for speech is one of Proverbs' most sensory. Alter notes the dual register: sweet to the soul (psychological) and healing to the bones (physical). Gracious speech heals the whole person — not merely the feeling but the body. The claim is physiological: good words have physical consequences.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
16:18–33

The second section moves from divine sovereignty to pride's consequences and to the social ethics that follow from right orientation. Waltke notes 16:18 ('pride goes before destruction, a haughty spirit before a fall') is the book's most quoted proverb. It is the concentrated summary of what the pride-humility antithesis has been building through the chapters: pride positions you for the fall that reveals your limitations.

16:32

'Better a patient person than a warrior, one with self-control than one who takes a city.' This is Proverbs' most counter-military value-judgment. Waltke notes the comparison deliberately inverts the warrior honour that ancient Near Eastern cultures celebrated: the highest human achievement is not military victory but self-control. Controlling yourself is greater than conquering others.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
16:18

Calvin: 'Pride goes before destruction — this is one of those proverbs that observation and experience confirm so regularly that it has become a byword. The proud man has made himself the measure of all things; and reality, which does not adjust itself to human pride, eventually breaks in and reminds him that he is not the measure of all things.'

16:32

Calvin on better than a warrior: 'This is a direct reversal of the world's honours. The world gives its crowns to those who take cities; God gives his honour to those who govern themselves. The man who is his own master is greater than the man who is master of others — because self-governance is more difficult than military victory and more lasting in its benefit.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
16:18

The NET note on 'pride goes before destruction' (lifney shever gaon) discusses the sequence: gaon (pride, swelling, exaltation) goes lifney (before, in front of) shever (destruction, breaking). The spatial metaphor (pride walking ahead of destruction) implies that pride is the vanguard of its own destruction — it leads the way to the consequence it cannot see coming.

16:24

The NET note on 'gracious words are a honeycomb' (tsuf devash imrei-no'am) discusses the medical claim: 'healing to the bones' (marpe leatsamot). Atsamot (bones) represents the inner structural reality of the body — bones are what you are when everything superficial is stripped away. Gracious speech heals at the deepest structural level, not merely the surface feeling.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

Divine Sovereignty and Human Responsibility in Proverbs 16
Compatible: both fully true simultaneously
Calvin, Reformed evangelical scholars
Proverbs 16:1–9 teaches both full human responsibility (the person genuinely plans, genuinely commits, genuinely devises) and full divine sovereignty (the LORD determines the tongue's answer, establishes the plans, directs the steps). These are not competing claims but complementary truths that hold simultaneously without reducing either.
Sequential: humans plan; God redirects
Some Arminian readings
Human planning is primary and genuine; God then redirects or adjusts the outcome according to his larger purposes. The sovereignty is supervisory and corrective rather than comprehensive. This reading preserves maximal human responsibility but may understate divine sovereignty.
Practical wisdom: plan well, trust God
Some pragmatic readings
The proverbs are not making a systematic theological claim about the relationship of divine sovereignty and human agency — they are making the practical wisdom observation that planning and trust belong together. The wisest person commits their work to God and works diligently. The theological resolution is a practical one, not a philosophical one.
The Reformed compatible-causality position is most consistent with the breadth of the passage. Proverbs 16:1–9 holds together human planning (not just mentioned but commanded — v.3: commit your work) and divine governance (explicitly stated as governing the outcome) without attempting to resolve the philosophical tension. The practical wisdom reading and the theological reading are both correct and complementary.
