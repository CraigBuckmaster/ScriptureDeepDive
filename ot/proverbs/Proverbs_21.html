Proverbs 21

The LORD Weighs the King's Heart; Justice over Sacrifice; Victory Belongs to the LORD

Verses 1–15 — The LORD Weighs the King's Heart; Justice Over Sacrifice
123456789101112131415

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half of ch.21 opens with the divine weighing of the king's heart (v.1) — establishing God's sovereignty over all earthly authority. V.3 is the chapter's moral centre: doing what is right and just is more acceptable to the LORD than sacrifice. The contrast between ritual compliance and genuine justice is the Proverbs echo of the great prophets (Isa 1:11–17; Amos 5:21–24).

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 1:11–17What to me is the multitude of your sacrifices? — the prophetic parallel to v.3. Justice is what the LORD requires; ritual cannot substitute for it.
  • Prov 16:9The LORD establishes steps — the same sovereignty theme as v.1's king-as-channel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
21:1-15

MacArthur: The king's heart is a stream of water in the LORD's hand; he channels it wherever he pleases (v.1). The most powerful human institution — the monarchy — is held by God as simply as a farmer channels an irrigation stream. The proverb does not eliminate royal responsibility; it relativizes royal power.

21:3

MacArthur: To do what is right and just is more acceptable to the LORD than sacrifice. The consistent witness of Proverbs, the Psalms, and the prophets: the substance that sacrifice expresses is more important than the sacrifice itself. Covenant ethics cannot be separated from covenant worship — when they conflict, the ethical substance takes priority.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:1

The watercourse precision — the king who thinks he acts from sovereign will is being channelled by a higher sovereignty. Alter notes the image is agricultural, not militaristic.

21:9

Better on a corner of the roof than in a house shared with a quarrelsome wife — the extremity of the contrast is the rhetorical point. Alter notes Proverbs uses this roof-corner image twice (cf. 25:24), emphasising through repetition that relational peace is worth more than comfortable domestic arrangements. Physical discomfort is preferable to psychological corrosion.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:1

The king's heart in the LORD's hand as a watercourse — divine sovereignty over political power is comprehensive. Even the most powerful human will is directional, like an irrigation channel turned wherever its controller chooses.

21:3

To do right and just is more acceptable than sacrifice — Proverbs rarely engages the cultic system; when it does, it asserts the priority of ethics over ritual.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:1

Calvin: The king's heart is in the LORD's hand — comfort and warning simultaneously. No earthly power operates outside divine governance; those in positions of authority are responsible to the God who directs them, whether they acknowledge him or not. This prevents both despair at unjust rulers and uncritical deference to any human authority.

21:3

Calvin: 'To do what is right is more acceptable than sacrifice — ritual without ethics is an offence, not an offering. The whole prophetic tradition agrees.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:1

The NET note traces the divine-governance-of-kings theme through Isa 10:5-15 (God using Assyria as an instrument), Dan 2:21 (God sets up and deposes kings), and Prov 16:9 (humans plan, God directs). The consistent OT theology: political power is real, human-exercised, and enclosed within comprehensive divine governance.

21:3

The NET note connects to 1 Sam 15:22 ('to obey is better than sacrifice'), Ps 40:6-8, and the prophetic critique throughout Amos, Hosea, Isaiah, and Micah. The consistent OT witness: ethics is the substance that cultic practice expresses. Without the substance, the practice is not merely insufficient but offensive.

Verses 16–31 — Diligence; Pride Leads to Conflict; Wisdom Overcomes Strength
16171819202122232425262728293031

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half addresses diligence versus laziness (vv.25–26), pride as the source of conflict (v.24), and the chapter's climactic verse: no wisdom, insight, or plan can succeed against the LORD (v.30). The divine sovereignty that opened the chapter (v.1) closes it (vv.30–31): human preparation matters, but victory belongs to the LORD.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 33:17A horse is a vain hope for deliverance — the exact parallel to v.31. The Psalmist and the sage both locate victory with the LORD, not with military preparation.
  • Prov 16:18Pride goes before destruction — the same pride-conflict connection as v.24.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
21:16-31

MacArthur: The horse is made ready for the day of battle, but victory rests with the LORD (v.31). The final proverb of the chapter is the culminating sovereignty statement. Military preparation is required — you make the horse ready — but the outcome belongs to God. This is not passivity but the wisdom that holds preparation and trust in proper proportion.

21:21

MacArthur: Whoever pursues righteousness and love finds life, prosperity and honor. The triad life, prosperity, honor corresponds to the three dimensions of covenant blessing. Righteousness pursued directly, not sought as a means to blessing, produces all three. The pursuit of character is the mechanism by which God's covenant blessings flow.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:17

The love-of-pleasure cluster grounds financial ruin in character. Alter notes the accumulation of proverbs on this theme builds a portrait of the person whose desires drive their destruction.

21:31

The horse-and-victory proverb — even in warfare, where human preparation matters most, the outcome belongs to the LORD. The sovereignty principle holds at the extreme.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:17

The love-of-pleasure-to-poverty chain — Waltke notes financial ruin in Proverbs traces to character before circumstance. The love of immediate pleasure is the root; poverty is the fruit.

21:31

The horse prepared for battle, but victory belongs to the LORD — the sovereignty principle applied to the domain where human preparation and power are most concentrated.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:26

Calvin: 'The righteous gives and does not hold back — this is the natural overflow of the character formed by wisdom. Generosity is not calculated; it is what the wise person is.'

21:31

Calvin: 'Safety is of the LORD — use all lawful means (prepare the horse) and commit the outcome entirely to God. To use no means is presumption; to trust the means is idolatry.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:17

NET: the love-of-pleasure proverb connects immediate sensory desire with the poverty it produces. The note traces the pleasure-diligence antithesis through the proverbs collection.

21:31

NET: even in the extreme case of warfare, the outcome belongs to the LORD — the human preparation is not invalidated but enclosed within divine determination.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs.
The editorial note is one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.

Reception History

Wisdom and virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics.
Practical application
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, economics, and social teaching.

Literary Structure

The LORD Weighs the Heart21:2A person may think their own ways are right, but the LORD weighs the heart. The LORD is the evaluator of what was actually intended, not just what was externally done. The divine judgment is interior, not merely behavioral.
Ethics over Ritual21:3To do what is right and just is more acceptable to the LORD than sacrifice. The consistent OT witness: the substance that sacrifice expresses is more important than the sacrifice itself. When the two conflict, the ethical substance takes priority.
