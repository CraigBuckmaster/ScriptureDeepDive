Proverbs 19

Integrity Over Wealth; Patience; Kind to the Poor Lends to God

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–14 — Integrity Over Wealth; Wisdom Yields Patience; Prudent Wife from the LORD
1Better the poor whose walk is blameless than a fool whose lips are perverse.2Desire without knowledge is not good-- how much more will hasty feet miss the way!3A person's own folly leads to their ruin, yet their heart rages against the LORD.4Wealth attracts many friends, but even the closest friend of the poor person deserts them.5A false witness will not go unpunished, and whoever pours out lies will not go free.6Many curry favor with a ruler, and everyone is the friend of one who gives gifts.7The poor are shunned by all their relatives-- how much more do their friends avoid them! Though the poor pursue them with pleading, they are nowhere to be found.8The one who gets wisdom loves life; the one who cherishes understanding will soon prosper.9A false witness will not go unpunished, and whoever pours out lies will perish.10It is not fitting for a fool to live in luxury-- how much more for a slave to rule over princes!11A person's wisdom yields patience; it is to one's glory to overlook an offense.12A king's rage is like the roar of a lion, but his favor is like dew on the grass.13A foolish child is a father's ruin, and a quarrelsome wife is like the constant dripping of a leaky roof.14Houses and wealth are inherited from parents, but a prudent wife is from the LORD.

Hebrew Word Study

אֵשֶׁת מַשְׂכִּלֶת eshet maskelet — prudent wife: V.14: the prudent wife (ʾēšet maśkêlet — wife of understanding) is from the LORD (mēYHWH). Houses and wealth can be inherited through ordinary human means. But the prudent wife is a divine gift. The wise wife is not simply found; she is given.

שֶׂכֶל sekel — wisdom / prudence / understanding: V.11: wisdom (śekel) yields patience (ʾereḵ ʾappô — long of nose, slow to anger). The wise person's patience is not temperamental but theologically grounded: they understand enough to overlook offenses. Patience is the fruit of understanding.

Context

The first half of ch.19 addresses integrity over wealth (v.1), the isolation of poverty (vv.4,6-7), the false witness (vv.5,9), and wisdom-producing patience (v.11). The prudent wife (v.14) is the section's theological climax: she is a divine gift, not an inherited asset.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 18:22He who finds a wife finds what is good and receives favour from the LORD — the direct parallel to v.14's prudent wife as a divine gift.
  • Prov 3:13Blessed is the one who finds wisdom — the structure of "finding" that links wisdom, wife, and divine favour throughout Proverbs.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
19:3

A person's own folly leads to their ruin, yet their heart rages against the LORD. Self-caused disaster produces misdirected blame. The fool's path leads to ruin; when the ruin comes, they blame God.

19:11

A person's wisdom yields patience; it is to one's glory to overlook an offense. The long nose (slow-to-anger) is the product of understanding. Overlooking is often the wiser and more courageous response than retaliating. It is called glory because it requires strength, not weakness.

19:14

Houses and wealth are inherited from parents, but a prudent wife is from the LORD. The contrast establishes a hierarchy: property through ordinary human means; a wise spouse through divine appointment.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
19:6

Many curry favour with a ruler — Alter notes this sardonic observation is a dry, realistic account of how social relationships work around power and wealth. Wisdom requires seeing social reality clearly, including its unflattering dimensions.

19:9

The false witness and liar who perish — the repetition of the false-witness consequence (cf. 19:5) forms a doublet that emphasises the point. Alter notes doublets in the proverb collection are a deliberate emphasis device.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
19:1

The poor-with-integrity vs. rich-with-perverse-lips proverb is Proverbs' most direct challenge to equating wealth with virtue. Waltke notes the economic and moral hierarchies are distinct and can be inverted: the repeated 'better than' proverbs on integrity and poverty form a sustained argument.

19:11

A person's wisdom yields patience; it is glorious to overlook an offense. Waltke notes tiferet (splendour/glory) for the one who can overlook — the dignity of a person secure enough not to demand restitution for every slight. Patience is not weakness but the nobility of one who does not need to prosecute every wrong.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
19:3

Calvin: 'When a man's folly ruins his life, his heart rages against the LORD — this is the nature of human self-deception. The person whose choices produced their misery blames God rather than themselves.'

19:11

Calvin on patience: 'The slow to anger who passes over an offense is the greater man. It is not weakness but magnanimity — the confidence of one who does not need every wrong acknowledged.'

Verses 15–29 — Kind to the Poor Lends to the LORD; LORD's Purpose Prevails; Fear Leads to Life
15Laziness brings on deep sleep, and the shiftless go hungry.16Whoever keeps commandments keeps their life, but whoever shows contempt for their ways will die.17Whoever is kind to the poor lends to the LORD, and he will reward them for what they have done.18Discipline your children, for in that there is hope; do not be a willing party to their death.19A hot-tempered person must pay the penalty; rescue them, and you will have to do it again.20Listen to advice and accept discipline, and at the end you will be counted among the wise.21Many are the plans in a person's heart, but it is the LORD's purpose that prevails.22What a person desires is unfailing love; better to be poor than a liar.23The fear of the LORD leads to life; then one rests content, untouched by trouble.24A sluggard buries his hand in the dish; he will not even bring it back to his mouth!25Flog a mocker, and the simple will learn prudence; rebuke the discerning, and they gain knowledge.26Whoever robs their father and drives out their mother is a child who brings shame and disgrace.27Stop listening to instruction, my son, and you will stray from the words of knowledge.28A corrupt witness mocks at justice, and the mouth of the wicked gulps down evil.29Penalties are prepared for mockers, and beatings for the backs of fools.

Hebrew Word Study

מַלְוֵה יְהוָה malveh YHWH — lends to the LORD: V.17: the one who is kind to the poor (ḥônēn-dāl) is a lender to the LORD (malweh YHWH). The transaction is commercial in form but theological in content: kindness to the poor creates a debt that the LORD himself will repay. You are not giving to the poor; you are lending to God.

עֲצַת יְהוָה atsat YHWH — purpose of the LORD: V.21: the LORD's purpose (ʿǎṣâ) is what prevails (tāqûm — stands, is established). The same sovereignty theme as 16:9. Human planning is real and multiplied; divine purpose is singular and final.

Context

The second half contains three of the chapter's most theologically significant verses: kindness to the poor as lending to the LORD (v.17), the LORD's purpose prevailing (v.21), and the fear of the LORD leading to life and contentment (v.23). These form the theological backbone: social ethics, sovereignty, and the life that flows from reverence.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 25:40Whatever you did for the least of these you did for me — the NT fulfilment of v.17's lending-to-the-LORD principle.
  • Prov 16:9The LORD establishes steps — the same divine sovereignty principle as v.21's prevailing purpose.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
19:17

Whoever is kind to the poor lends to the LORD. The boldest statement of giving theology in Proverbs. The gift to the poor is framed not as charity but as a loan to God. The LORD repays. You are not losing; you are investing in the most reliable lender available.

19:21

Many are the plans in a person's heart, but it is the LORD's purpose that prevails. The companion to 16:9. Human planning is not futile but finite. The plans multiply; the purpose is one.

19:23

The fear of the LORD leads to life; then one rests content. The satisfaction of the person whose ultimate reference point is the LORD rather than circumstances.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
19:17

The loan-to-the-LORD metaphor — Alter notes the audacity of the image is the point. Giving to the poor is presented with the reliability of a loan to the most creditworthy possible borrower. The commercial frame makes the theological claim vivid.

19:20–21

The hear-counsel / many-plans cluster — Alter notes human receptivity (v.20) paired with divine sovereignty (v.21) makes them complementary, not competing. Listen for wisdom; trust God with outcomes.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
19:17

Whoever is kind to the poor lends to the LORD — Waltke notes the commercial metaphor is startling: giving to the poor is a covenant transaction with God as borrower. The return is guaranteed by the most creditworthy possible debtor. This is one of Proverbs' most theologically radical statements.

19:21

Many plans in a person's heart, but the LORD's purpose prevails — Waltke notes this is the chapter's divine-sovereignty statement, parallel to 16:9. Human planning is real and proper; divine governance is comprehensive and prevailing.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
19:17

Calvin: 'When we relieve the poor, we lend to God. God has identified himself with the poor as the recipient of generosity. The return is guaranteed by the most reliable possible guarantor.'

19:21

Calvin: 'Man proposes; God disposes. The many schemes of the human heart are not forbidden — planning is wisdom. But all human planning is enclosed within the divine purpose which ultimately prevails.'

Textual Notes

19:22'What is desired in a person is kindness'
MT: ta'avat adam chasdo (what is desired in a person is his hesed/loyalty). LXX reads quite differently. The MT places hesed as the supreme human desideratum.
The MT's hesed-reading connects to Proverbs' broader theme: the supreme human virtue is covenant kindness. The verse continues: 'better to be poor than a liar' — placing poverty with hesed above wealth with deception.

Scholarly Debates

Proverbs 19:17 and the Theology of Poverty Care
God identifies with the poor: a genuine theological claim
Most evangelical scholars
The loan-to-the-LORD metaphor makes an extraordinary claim: giving to the poor is a transaction with God. Matt 25:40 develops this: whatever you did for the least you did for Christ. The theology is genuine, not merely motivational.
A practical motivational device, not strict theological identity
Some critical readers
The commercial metaphor is primarily persuasive — using the reliability of God's repayment to encourage generosity. The theological claim about divine identity with the poor may read more into the metaphor than it intends.
The 'both' position is most coherent. Proverbs' consistent use of commercial language for theological realities establishes that this metaphor carries genuine theological weight. Matt 25:40 confirms the NT reads it as genuine divine identification, not merely a motivational device.

Theological Themes Tracker

Proverbs 19 applies the wisdom value-hierarchy to economic and social life: integrity consistently outranks wealth; generosity to the poor is a covenant transaction with God; patience in overlooking offense is gloriously noble; the LORD's purpose prevails over all human planning.
