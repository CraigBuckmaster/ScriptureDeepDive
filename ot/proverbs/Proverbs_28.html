Proverbs 28

Wicked Flee; Confess and Find Mercy; Faithful vs. Avaricious; Give to the Poor

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–14 — Wicked Flee; Confess and Find Mercy; Happy Is the Reverent
1The wicked flee though no one pursues, but the righteous are as bold as a lion.2When a country is rebellious, it has many rulers, but a ruler with discernment and knowledge maintains order.3A ruler who oppresses the poor is like a driving rain that leaves no crops.4Those who forsake instruction praise the wicked, but those who heed it resist them.5Evildoers do not understand what is right, but those who seek the LORD understand it fully.6Better the poor whose walk is blameless than the rich whose ways are perverse.7A discerning son heeds instruction, but a companion of gluttons disgraces his father.8Whoever increases wealth by taking interest or profit from the poor amasses it for another, who will be kind to the poor.9If anyone turns a deaf ear to my instruction, even their prayers are detestable.10Whoever leads the upright along an evil path will fall into their own trap, but the blameless will receive a good inheritance.11The rich are wise in their own eyes, but a discerning poor person sees through them.12When the righteous triumph, there is great elation; but when the wicked rise to power, people go into hiding.13Whoever conceals their sins does not prosper, but the one who confesses and renounces them finds mercy.14Blessed is the one who always trembles before God, but whoever hardens their heart falls into trouble.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half of ch.28 contains two of Proverbs' most psychologically precise observations: the wicked who flee though no one pursues (v.1) — the internal corruption that produces perpetual fear — and the mercy that follows genuine confession of sin (v.13). V.14's closing beatitude: blessed is the one who always trembles before God.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 53:5They were overwhelmed with dread where there was nothing to dread — the same flight-without-pursuer psychology as v.1.
  • 1 John 1:9If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us — the NT fulfilment of v.13's mercy that follows genuine confession.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
28:1-14

MacArthur: The wicked flee though no one pursues, but the righteous are as bold as a lion (v.1). Guilt produces a fearfulness with no external cause. The wicked carry their own prosecutor; they are never at rest because they cannot escape themselves. The righteous need no external protection because they have no internal accusation.

28:13

MacArthur: Whoever conceals their sins does not prosper, but the one who confesses and renounces them finds mercy. The two-part movement — confess and renounce — is the covenant pattern of genuine repentance. Confession without renunciation is incomplete; renunciation without confession bypasses the relational repair. Both are required.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
28:1

The wicked flee when no one pursues — Alter notes the psychological insight is one of Proverbs' sharpest: the guilty carry their own prosecution. The righteous are 'as bold as a lion' because they have nothing to flee from. The contrast is not between the powerful and the weak but between the clean-conscienced and the guilty.

28:13

Confess and find mercy — the two-part movement (confess AND renounce) is deliberate. Alter notes that confession without renunciation is incomplete acknowledgment; renunciation without confession may be self-sufficient pride. The combination is the covenant pattern of genuine repentance: honest acknowledgment of what was done paired with genuine turning from it.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
28:1

The wicked flee though no one pursues — Waltke notes the self-generated flight of the guilty conscience is one of Proverbs' most psychologically acute observations. Guilt produces fearfulness without external cause. The person who violates covenant norms carries the awareness of that violation as a persistent source of anxiety that no external security can address.

28:13

Whoever conceals their sins does not prosper; whoever confesses and renounces finds mercy -- the covenant pattern of genuine repentance: honest acknowledgment plus genuine turning.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
28:1

Calvin: The torment of a guilty conscience is its own prosecutor and its own punishment. The person who has offended God carries their condemnation within them. No external security, wealth, or power can silence the inner prosecution. This is simultaneously a warning (sin extracts its own punishment) and a gospel pointer (only forgiveness can silence the prosecution).

28:13

Calvin: Whoever confesses and renounces their sins will find mercy — one of Proverbs' most directly evangelical statements. Mercy is available but it requires two things: honest acknowledgment of failure (confession) and genuine turning from it (renunciation). Either without the other is insufficient.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
28:1

The NET note on the self-generated flight of the guilty discusses the psychological mechanism: guilt (awareness of covenant violation) produces fearfulness without any external threat. The note traces the contrast — wicked flee / righteous bold as a lion — as a character antithesis grounded in the presence or absence of moral integrity. The righteous have nothing to flee from; the wicked carry their prosecution within them.

28:13

The NET note on confession (modeh) and renunciation (azav, forsaking) together constituting genuine repentance traces the two-part structure through 1 Jn 1:9 (if we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive) as the NT parallel. The structure is consistent: honest acknowledgment plus genuine turning equals the covenant pattern of restoration.

Verses 15–28 — The Poor Over the Liar; Giving to the Poor Lacks Nothing; Who Trusts the LORD
15Like a roaring lion or a charging bear is a wicked ruler over a helpless people.16A tyrannical ruler practices extortion, but one who hates ill-gotten gain will enjoy a long reign.17Anyone tormented by the guilt of murder will seek refuge in the grave; let no one hold them back.18The one whose walk is blameless is kept safe, but the one whose ways are perverse will fall into the pit.19Those who work their land will have abundant food, but those who chase fantasies will have their fill of poverty.20A faithful person will be richly blessed, but one eager to get rich will not go unpunished.21To show partiality is not good— yet a person will do wrong for a piece of bread.22The stingy are eager to get rich and are unaware that poverty awaits them.23Whoever rebukes a person will in the end gain favor rather than one who has a flattering tongue.24Whoever robs their father or mother and says, "It's not wrong," is partner to one who destroys.25The greedy stir up conflict, but those who trust in the LORD will prosper.26Those who trust in themselves are fools, but those who walk in wisdom are kept safe.27Those who give to the poor will lack nothing, but those who close their eyes to them receive many curses.28When the wicked rise to power, people go into hiding; but when the wicked perish, the righteous thrive.

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half addresses the poor and their oppressors (vv.15,27), the person who hastens to get rich (v.22), and the section's central warning: the one who trusts in themselves is a fool, but the one who walks in wisdom is kept safe (v.26). The chapter closes with the giving-to-the-poor promise: those who give will lack nothing.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 11:24-25The generous paradox — those who give freely gain more. The same principle as v.27's giving-to-the-poor lacks nothing.
  • Luke 18:14Everyone who exalts himself will be humbled — the NT application of v.26's trust-in-self as folly.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
28:15-28

MacArthur: Whoever gives to the poor will lack nothing, but the one who closes their eyes to them receives many curses (v.27). The covenant economy of generosity: the giving person receives; the withholding person loses. The mechanism is not mechanical but relational — God blesses those who image his own generosity toward the vulnerable.

28:26

MacArthur: Those who trust in themselves are fools, but those who walk in wisdom are kept safe. The contrast is between self-derived confidence and wisdom-formed confidence. Self-trust is not wrong because confidence is wrong but because the self is not a reliable ground for trust. Wisdom-formed trust is grounded in tested reality.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
28:20

Faithful versus eager to get rich — Alter notes both appear to be pursuing wealth; the character of the pursuit makes all the difference. The faithful person builds through patient diligence; the avaricious person seeks shortcuts that compromise the very foundation of what they build. The contrast is in the character of the striving, not in the goal.

28:27

Those who give to the poor will lack nothing; those who close their eyes to them receive many curses — Alter notes the closing-eyes image is an active choice. The person who closes their eyes has decided not to see what they could not help but notice. This active avoidance is what attracts the many curses — not ignorance but deliberate refusal.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
28:20

A faithful person will be richly blessed; one eager to get rich will not go unpunished — Waltke notes the antithesis is between covenant-faithful diligence and the avarice that cuts corners. Both appear to be pursuing wealth; the character of the pursuit determines the result. Faithful diligence builds sustainably; avarice sows the seeds of its own collapse.

28:27

Those who give to the poor will lack nothing; those who close their eyes to them receive many curses -- the closing-eyes image is active avoidance, not ignorance.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
28:20

Calvin: A faithful man shall abound with blessings — faithfulness in small things produces the accumulation that hasty greed cannot achieve. The covenant-faithful person may prosper slowly; they prosper genuinely. The avaricious person appears to prosper quickly and loses quickly. The foundation determines the sustainability.

28:27

Calvin: Those who give to the poor will lack nothing; those who close their eyes to them receive many curses — there is no neutral position in Proverbs' poverty ethics. To close your eyes to the poor when you could help is not neutrality but a covenant violation that attracts the same curse as active exploitation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
28:20

The NET note on aman (faithful, reliable, trustworthy) — covenant-faithful reliability in all things, including economic life — explains why faithfulness produces blessing. The person whose character is reliable attracts the trust and cooperation of the community, which produces the social capital that translates into material prosperity.

28:27

The NET note on the closing-eyes image explains that ra'ah (to see) in the negative form (not seeing/closing eyes) means active avoidance of awareness. The note discusses the covenantal implications: to see and not act is to take a moral position. Proverbs does not recognise neutrality in the face of the poor's need.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions, especially Egyptian.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs — one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.
The editorial activity shows Proverbs as a living, curated collection.

Reception History

Virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics — the practical outworking of the fear of the LORD.
Practical wisdom
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, governance, and social teaching.
