Proverbs 24

Do Not Envy Evil; Rescue the Staggering; The Sluggard's Field

Author

Primarily Solomon son of David (chs.1-29), with additional contributions from Agur son of Jakeh (ch.30) and King Lemuel's mother (ch.31).

When written

Core Solomonic material c.970-930 BC; final compilation under Hezekiah c.715-686 BC.

What prompted it

To transmit the accumulated wisdom of Israel's sages to the next generation -- teaching the fear of the LORD as the foundation of all genuine wisdom.

Verses 1–18 — Do Not Envy Evil; Rescue the Staggering; Wisdom Builds the House
123456789101112131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The first half of ch.24 contains the Thirty Sayings' wisdom-builds-the-house formula (v.3) and the obligation to rescue those staggering toward death (v.11). The honey-and-wisdom comparison (v.13-14) applies the sweetness metaphor: just as honey is good for the body, wisdom is good for the soul — with the same future-hope promise as 23:18.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 9:1Wisdom has built her house — the personified Wisdom of ch.9 builds; wisdom in v.3 is the principle by which all houses are properly built.
  • Prov 14:4Where there are no oxen the manger is clean, but abundant harvests come through the strength of an ox — the same diligence-requires-mess principle implicit in v.27.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
24:1-18

MacArthur: By wisdom a house is built, and through understanding it is established; through knowledge its rooms are filled with rare and beautiful treasures (vv.3-4). The building metaphor recapitulates Proverbs' core claim: wisdom is the organizing principle of the good life, the equivalent of what architectural knowledge is to construction.

24:11-12

MacArthur: Rescue those being led away to death; hold back those staggering toward slaughter. If you say, 'But we knew nothing about this' — does not he who weighs the heart perceive it? The positive obligation to act for others in mortal danger cannot be evaded by selective ignorance. God weighs the quality of the not-knowing.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:11-12

Rescue those led to death — the rhetorical question ('does not he who weighs the heart perceive it?') makes divine moral epistemology explicit: God evaluates what was known and withheld, not just what was done.

24:1

Do not envy evil men or desire to be with them — the chapter's answer to theodicy-envy is consistently temporal: the wicked have no future (v.20). Alter notes this is not denial of the present reality (the wicked do sometimes prosper) but insistence on the long view: the covenant framework evaluates outcomes over the full arc of a life, not at its peak of apparent success.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:11-12

Rescue those led to death; do not use ignorance as excuse — the God who weighs the heart knows what was known. The obligation to act for others in mortal danger cannot be discharged by selective ignorance.

24:1

Do not envy evil men — temporal reframe: the wicked have no future (v.20). Envy is stopped by seeing long-term reality, not by denying present appearances.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:11

Calvin: 'Those who are able to rescue the innocent and do not, offering the excuse of ignorance, are not acquitted — God who weighs the heart knows what they knew.'

24:14

If you find wisdom, there is a future for you and your hope will not be cut off — wisdom is distinguished from all other earthly goods by the permanence of its benefit. Calvin notes that material prosperity can vanish overnight, reputation can be destroyed, health can fail. But wisdom, once genuinely acquired, cannot be taken away by any external circumstance.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
24:11

NET: the rescue-instruction imposes a positive obligation to act, not merely permission. The disclaimer-rejection makes God the evaluator of what was known and decided.

24:5

The NET note on 'the wise have great power, and those with knowledge increase their strength' discusses the counter-intuitive claim: wisdom produces a kind of strength greater than physical power. The note traces the theme through Qohelet (Eccl 9:13-16: a poor wise man saved his city) and identifies wisdom as force-multiplier, not force-replacement.

Verses 19–34 — Do Not Fret Over Evildoers; The Field of the Sluggard; Second Collection Begins
19202122232425262728293031323334

Hebrew Word Study

Context

The second half includes more sayings from the supplementary collection (vv.23-29) and the extended parable of the sluggard's field (vv.30-34) — one of the most vivid portraits of lazy neglect in Proverbs. The field that produced only weeds because its owner loved sleep is the book's sharpest illustration of what happens when diligence is absent.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 6:10-11A little sleep, a little slumber — the sluggard passage of ch.6 is echoed and extended in vv.30-34.
  • 2 Thess 3:10If anyone is not willing to work, let them not eat — the NT application of Proverbs' consistent diligence ethic.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
24:19-34

MacArthur: Do not fret because of evildoers or be envious of the wicked, for the evildoer has no future hope, and the lamp of the wicked will be snuffed out (vv.19-20). The theodicy proverb: the long view resolves the apparent injustice of the wicked's prosperity. The future belongs to the righteous; the wicked's lamp will go out.

24:30-34

MacArthur: The sluggard's field is the chapter's empirical illustration. The teacher observes, reflects, and draws the conclusion: poverty will come on you like a thief; scarcity will come like an armed man. The lesson is drawn from direct observation — wisdom is not merely theoretical but the distillation of experience.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:13-14

The honey-wisdom comparison — Alter notes the comparison is exact: present pleasure (taste) plus future benefit. Honey is genuinely pleasant; so is wisdom. Neither is merely obligatory.

24:30-34

The sluggard's field — unique in the proverb collections for its extended first-person observation. Wisdom as narrated personal experience, not just received tradition.

Waltke — NICOT Proverbs

Bruce K. Waltke, NICOT Commentary on Proverbs (2 vols.) — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:13-14

Honey and wisdom — as honey is sweet to the taste, wisdom is sweet to the soul and has a future/hope (v.14). Wisdom's sweetness is both present and future; folly's sweetness is only present.

24:30-34

The sluggard's field — one of Proverbs' most sustained observational wisdom passages. The teacher observes, reflects, and draws the conclusion. The method is empirical: wisdom can be read from the observable world.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:16

Calvin: 'Though the righteous fall seven times, they rise again — the falling is not the mark of defeat; the rising is the mark of covenant faithfulness. The righteous person rises not because they never fail but because they have a foundation to rise from.'

24:33

Calvin: 'A little sleep, a little slumber — the moderation of the laziness makes the warning more pointed. It is not gross idleness but comfortable small indulgences that destroy.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
24:14

NET: wisdom's future/hope (yesh acharit) — the distinctive temporal promise. Unlike all material goods, wisdom's benefits extend into the future without diminishment.

24:33

NET: verbatim repetition of 6:10-11 — the same excuse used repeatedly produces the same result. The literary echo signals that the sluggard's self-deception is chronic, not occasional.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern wisdom
These proverbs reflect Israel's engagement with international wisdom traditions, especially Egyptian.
Wisdom was a shared human enterprise across cultures.
Hezekian editorial work
Proverbs 25:1 notes that men of Hezekiah copied these proverbs — one of the most historically specific attributions in Proverbs.
The editorial activity shows Proverbs as a living, curated collection.

Reception History

Virtue ethics
Proverbs 21-30 has been foundational to Christian virtue ethics — the practical outworking of the fear of the LORD.
Practical wisdom
These chapters have been widely cited in ethics, governance, and social teaching.

Scholarly Debates

Proverbs' Empirical Method: Observation as a Source of Wisdom
Valid source alongside revelation
Waltke, most OT wisdom scholars
The sluggard's-field observation exemplifies the empirical method: observe the world carefully and draw theological conclusions. Observation-based wisdom is a valid route to knowledge.
Creation observation as general revelation
Calvin, Reformed tradition
Wisdom read from creation expresses natural law embedded within it. God's wisdom is in the structure of things.
Both converge: the empirical method reads the divine wisdom embedded in creation. Proverbs' observations are theological claims about moral reality, not merely pragmatic advice.
