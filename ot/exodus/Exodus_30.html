Exodus 30

The Incense Altar; the Census Tax; the Bronze Basin; Anointing Oil and Incense

Verses 1–10 — The Altar of Incense
12345678910

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
30:1

MacArthur: The incense altar is positioned before the veil -- the closest object in the Holy Place to the divine presence. Its daily use embodies the principle of constant prayer. The incense rising toward the divine presence is the OT's most persistent image of prayer (Ps 141:2; Rev 5:8).

30:9

MacArthur: Do not offer on this altar any other incense. The incense altar is set apart for one exclusive use: the incense offering before YHWH. The exclusivity of worship mirrors the exclusivity of the covenant.

Hebrew Word Study

קְטֹרֶת qĕṭōret — incense: V.1-9: The altar of incense stood before the veil, in front of the Ark. Incense (qĕṭōret) burned before God morning and evening — its ascending smoke the visible image of prayer rising. Ps 141:2: "May my prayer be set before you like incense." Luke 1:10: "the whole assembly was praying outside at the time of incense offering." Rev 5:8 and 8:3-4: the incense of the golden bowls is "the prayers of God's people." Prayer is Israel's incense, rising perpetually before the throne.

כֹּפֶר kōper — ransom / atonement money: V.12-15: "Each one who crosses over to those already counted is to give a half shekel... as an offering to the LORD to make atonement (lĕkhapper) for your lives." The census money is kōper — ransom price. Every Israelite counted owed a life; the half-shekel was the symbolic acknowledgment that life is not self-owned but redeemed. The equalizing principle (v.15: "the rich are not to give more than a half shekel and the poor are not to give less") reflects the equal value of every human life before God.

Historical Context

None

Context

The incense altar's position (v.6: "in front of the curtain that shields the ark") placed it at the threshold of the Most Holy Place. The twice-daily incense corresponded to the twice-daily burnt offering, so the whole day was bracketed by both blood and fragrance: atonement and prayer together.

Cross-Reference

  • Rev 5:8The twenty-four elders hold golden bowls of incense, "which are the prayers of God's people." The incense altar = the prayers of the saints before God's throne.
  • Rev 8:3–4An angel with a golden censer — incense added to the prayers of all God's people, rising before God.
  • Luke 1:10The people praying outside while Zechariah burned incense — the incense hour was the prayer hour.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
30:1

The altar of incense is described separately from the other Holy Place furnishings (ch.25) — its position in ch.30 has generated source-critical discussion about whether it was a later addition to the tabernacle tradition. Sarna responds: the chapter's placement may reflect a logical rather than chronological ordering — the incense altar belongs with the anointing oil and incense formulas that follow (vv.22–38), making ch.30 a chapter about the olfactory dimension of tabernacle worship.

30:10

The annual atonement on the horns of the incense altar — with the blood of the Yom Kippur sin offering — connects the incense altar to the atonement system. Even the prayer altar requires annual purification: the prayers of God's people, however sincere, carry the contamination of the sinful worshippers who offer them. The incense altar's annual blood-atonement covers the inadequacy of Israel's prayer.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
30:7–10

The incense altar is the Holy Place's most intimate object — positioned directly before the veil, it is the priest's daily point of closest approach to the divine presence. Alter notes the sensory dimensions of the tabernacle have been carefully distributed: the menorah provides light, the table provides food (bread), the incense altar provides fragrance. The human senses of sight, taste, and smell all have their tabernacle correlates — the whole person is engaged in the approach to God.

30:8-10

The annual atonement on the incense altar's horns is the only maintenance requirement for the incense altar. Alter notes the annual rhythm — once a year, a special atonement, on the same day as the great atonement of the tabernacle complex — marks the incense altar as participating in the complete sanctuary system, not as an auxiliary piece.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
30:7–8

Calvin on the incense: 'The incense burning on the altar signified the prayers of the people, which ascend to God as a sweet-smelling savour.' The fragrance is prayer made visible — or rather, prayer given a physical form that rises toward heaven as smoke rises. Calvin's NT application: 'Under the Gospel, when we have obtained access to the Father through Christ, incense is no longer required; but prayer itself, seasoned by the merits of Christ, is our spiritual incense.' The physical incense is replaced by the prayer it represented.

30:6-9

Calvin: The incense altar stood between the lampstand and the table, directly before the veil — this positioning makes it the Holy Place's central object. Its daily use (morning and evening) embodies the principle of constant prayer: the covenant community approaches God at the beginning and end of every day.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
30:10

The NET note on the annual atonement on the incense altar horns specifies the Yom Kippur blood application (Lev 16:18). The note discusses the theological significance: the incense altar, though in the Holy Place rather than the Holy of Holies, also required annual atonement. This suggests that even the instruments of prayer are contaminated by the sinful community that uses them — the entire worship system requires the annual comprehensive atonement of Yom Kippur.

30:9-10

The NET note on 'strange incense' (qetoret zarah) — incense other than the prescribed formula — that must not be offered on the altar discusses the Nadab and Abihu incident (Lev 10:1-3) as the narrative illustration of this prohibition. Deviation from the divine prescription in worship has fatal consequences.

Verses 11–21 — The Census Tax; the Bronze Basin

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
30:15

MacArthur: Rich and poor alike must give the same half shekel. Covenant belonging is not calibrated by wealth. Before God in the census, every person counted equally.

30:18-20

MacArthur: The bronze basin between the altar and the tent serves as the priests' washing station. They shall wash their hands and feet so that they will not die. The holiness barrier between sinful humanity and the holy God is maintained through ritual cleanliness.

Hebrew Word Study

שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה shemen hammishḥāh — anointing oil: V.22-33: The sacred anointing oil — myrrh, cinnamon, calamus, cassia in olive oil — was a unique formula prohibited for ordinary use. It was used only for the most holy objects and persons: Tabernacle, Ark, altars, lampstand, laver, and priests. The fragrance of the oil was the smell of holiness, of divine appointment. The restriction underscored the singularity of what God had set apart: the anointed ones were not a category among many but uniquely designated for divine service.

רוּחַ חָכְמָה rûaḥ ḥokmāh — spirit of wisdom: V.31-35 (context: Bezalel, ch.31): The craftsman Bezalel is said to be filled with rûaḥ ḥokmāh (spirit of wisdom/skill). The same Spirit who hovered over creation (Gen 1:2) and would empower Israel's deliverers now enables the artisans. Skill in craftsmanship — the ability to create beautiful things — is explicitly attributed to the Spirit of God. Artistic excellence and sacred vocation are not separate categories.

Historical Context

None

Context

The half-shekel tax was equal for all — rich and poor alike (v.15). This radical equality is the census's theological statement: all lives are equally redeemed, equally ransomed. No one can purchase more atonement with wealth; no one can be exempted by poverty. The basin's position (between altar and tent) placed cleansing on the path of approach: after atonement (altar) but before entry (tent).

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 17:24–27Jesus and the temple tax — paying the half-shekel. Jesus pays the ransom for himself and Peter, even though as the Son he is technically exempt.
  • John 13:8Jesus washing the disciples' feet: "Unless I wash you, you have no part with me" — the laver's cleansing fulfilled in the upper room.
  • 1 John 1:9Ongoing cleansing in the Christian life — the laver's continued application for those already in covenant with God.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
30:12

The half-shekel as 'ransom for his life' (kofer nafsho) employs the kofer (ransom/redemption price) language that runs through the atonement system. Sarna notes that a man's life — forfeit to God by being numbered (a presumptuous act) — is redeemed by the fixed payment. The census tax is a ritual acknowledgment of God's ownership of each person: you belong to God; the half-shekel acknowledges the debt.

30:18

The laver's placement — between the altar and the tent — creates a three-station approach sequence: enter the court → pass the altar (atonement) → pass the laver (purification) → enter the tent (presence). The sequence is the covenant's logic of approach: sacrifice first, purification second, presence third. No stage can be skipped; no shortcut to the divine presence bypasses either the altar or the laver.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
30:11–16

The census tax episode introduces a theological problem into what might seem a purely administrative act: counting people is dangerous (cf. 2 Sam 24 — David's census and the plague that follows). Alter notes the ransom payment is the solution to the spiritual hazard of the census: the half-shekel acknowledges that each life is God's, and the payment ransoms each person counted from the dangerous presumption of numbering God's people.

30:15-16

The census tax's equalising provision — no more, no less, from rich and poor alike — is the covenant community's most explicit statement of equal value before God. Alter notes the atonement money becomes the tabernacle's ransom price: everyone pays the same because everyone is equally subject to the covenant's requirements and equally valued within it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
30:15

Calvin on the equal half-shekel: 'God would not have poor people excluded from redemption, and therefore he would not have the rich consider themselves more acceptable to him because of their wealth.' The equal ransom is the covenant's anti-plutocracy statement: economic standing has no bearing on standing before God. The wealthy cannot purchase greater covenant status; the poor are not excluded by their poverty. The half-shekel is the covenant's egalitarian statement.

30:20–21

Calvin on the laver: 'The washing was an external symbol of the inward purity required of those who approach God... not as if water could wash away sin, but because it was a figure of that spiritual cleansing which God's Spirit produces in the hearts of his own.' The physical washing symbolises what God does spiritually; the symbol is real and required without being independent of its referent.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
30:13

The NET note on the half-shekel identifies its weight: 10 gerahs = 1/2 shekel = approximately 5.7 grams of silver. The note discusses the 'shekel of the sanctuary' (shekel haqodesh) — a standard weight used in sacred transactions that may have differed slightly from commercial weights. The atonement money collected was used specifically for the tabernacle's maintenance (38:25–28), connecting the census tax directly to the tabernacle's silver foundation (the silver sockets of ch.26).

30:15-16

The NET note on the ransom (kofer) for each person's life discusses the atonement vocabulary: kofer elsewhere means a ransom payment that deflects divine judgment. The note traces the theology: the census payment is not tribute to a national treasury but covenant acknowledgment that each life belongs to God and is sustained by divine mercy.

Verses 22–38 — The Sacred Anointing Oil and Incense Formulas
2223242526272829303132333435363738

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
30:23-25

MacArthur: The anointing oil's four spice components -- myrrh, cinnamon, cane, cassia -- all appear in the Song of Songs as the fragrance of the beloved. The covenant relationship between YHWH and Israel has the character of marital intimacy.

30:22-38

MacArthur: The anointing oil and incense formulas are given with explicit prohibition against replication for personal use (vv.32-33, 37-38). The sacred is not simply material but designated — the same substances used for common purposes are profane; used as God specifies, they are holy. Holiness is a category of designation, not of chemistry.

Hebrew Word Study

כִּיּוֹר kiyyôr — bronze basin / laver: V.17-21: The bronze laver (kiyyôr) stood between the altar and the Tent of Meeting. Priests washed hands and feet here before serving — failure to do so meant death (v.20). The washing before approaching was not ritual formality but a theological statement: approaching the holy requires cleansing. The NT's baptismal theology draws on this: "Let us draw near to God with a sincere heart and with the full assurance that faith brings, having our hearts sprinkled to cleanse us from a guilty conscience and having our bodies washed with pure water" (Heb 10:22).

סַם sam — spice / ingredient: V.34-38: The incense formula — stacte, onycha, galbanum, and frankincense in equal parts — was a unique, sacred blend. Like the anointing oil, its use for common purposes was prohibited. The sacredness of specific blends points to the particularity of covenant worship: not any approach will do, not any formula substitutes. True worship is defined by God, not invented by the worshipper.

Historical Context

None

Context

The anointing oil and incense formulas both carry the same restriction: do not replicate them for personal use. The sacred must remain sacred — distinct, not blended into ordinary life. The penalty (being "cut off") is severe precisely because the offence is a category violation: treating the holy as common. This is what Nadab and Abihu's "unauthorised fire" (Lev 10) would later violate — approaching the Holy on one's own terms.

Cross-Reference

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    30:23

    The anointing oil's four spice ingredients are all attested in ancient Egyptian and Mesopotamian texts as luxury aromatic substances. Sarna notes the combination is not derived from existing religious practice but was uniquely formulated for the tabernacle — an exclusive formula that no other culture shared. The covenant community's sacred scent is its own, not borrowed from surrounding religions.

    30:34

    The sacred incense formula also uses rare ingredients. Sarna notes that frankincense (levonah) was imported from southern Arabia and the Horn of Africa — an expensive luxury used in royal and divine contexts throughout the ancient Near East. Its inclusion in the tabernacle incense connects Israelite worship to the broader ancient world's understanding of sacred fragrance while maintaining the unique combination that marks the tabernacle's incense as exclusively divine.

    Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    Moses↗ People
    Prophet, lawgiver, and covenant mediator
    Moses stands at the centre of the Pentateuch’s narrative — the one man who spoke with God face to face (Exod 33:11; Num 12:8) and mediated the Sinai covenant to Israel. In Leviticus he receives the priestly legislation; in Numbers he leads, intercedes, and ultimately fails at Meribah (Num 20). The writer of Hebrews calls him "faithful in all God’s house" (Heb 3:5) while distinguishing him from Christ, the Son over the house.
    Aaron↗ People
    First high priest of Israel
    Aaron, Moses’s brother, is consecrated as Israel’s first high priest in Lev 8–9. He bears the names of the twelve tribes on his breastplate before God and enters the Most Holy Place once a year on Yom Kippur (Lev 16). His failures — the golden calf (Exod 32), challenging Moses’s authority (Num 12), complicity at Meribah (Num 20) — make him the paradigm of the imperfect priest who prefigures a better high priest (Heb 7:26–28).

    Miriam↗ People
    Prophetess and worship leader; Moses’s sister
    Miriam leads Israel’s women in worship after the Red Sea crossing (Exod 15:20) and is called a prophetess. In Num 12 she challenges Moses’s unique prophetic authority alongside Aaron and is struck with ṣāraʿat — only to be healed after Moses’s seven-word intercession. She dies at Kadesh (Num 20:1), her passing noted in two terse Hebrew words before the water crisis.
    Joshua↗ People
    Moses’s military commander and designated successor
    Joshua son of Nun serves as Moses’s lieutenant throughout the wilderness period — commanding at Rephidim (Exod 17), ascending Sinai with Moses (Exod 24), guarding the tent of meeting. In Num 13–14 he stands with Caleb as one of two faithful spies. He is formally commissioned in Num 27 through Moses’s hand-laying and inherits the leadership role that Moses cannot fulfil past the Jordan.

    Ancient Sources

    Ancient Incense
    Incense burning was a universal feature of ancient worship. Archaeological excavations have found incense altars at numerous Israelite and Canaanite sites.
    The incense altar is archaeologically well-grounded in the ancient Levantine worship context.
    Spice Trade
    The spices listed — myrrh, cinnamon, calamus, cassia — were luxury trade goods. Their use in the anointing oil signals that Israel's worship offered its most precious resources to God.
    The economics of the anointing oil: maximum value, given to God.

    Scholarly Debates

    The Incense Altar's Late Placement in the Text
    Source-critical explanation: later addition
    Wellhausen, some critical scholars
    The incense altar is absent from the ch.25 list of tabernacle furnishings and appears separately in ch.30. This suggests it was added to the tabernacle tradition at a later stage — possibly during the monarchy period, when daily incense burning became standard temple practice.
    Deliberate topical arrangement
    Sarna, Childs, evangelical scholars
    The placement of the incense altar in ch.30 alongside the anointing oil and incense formulas reflects a deliberate thematic arrangement: all the olfactory elements of the tabernacle are grouped together. This is not evidence of late addition but of literary organisation. The incense altar belongs with the incense formula that follows it.
    The source-critical argument rests on argumentum ex silentio (absence from ch.25 = later addition) which is inherently weak. The topical arrangement explanation is more consistent with the evident care taken in the organisation of chs.25–31. The altar's presence in the tabernacle construction (39:38; 40:5, 26–27) confirms it as an original element of the design.
