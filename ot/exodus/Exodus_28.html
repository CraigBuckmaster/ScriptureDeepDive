Exodus 28

Sacred Garments for Dignity and Honour; Ephod; Breastpiece; Robe and Gold Plate

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–5 — The Call of Aaron; Sacred Garments for Dignity and Honor
12345

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
28:1

MacArthur: Have Aaron your brother brought to you from among the Israelites. The priestly calling is both chosen (Aaron and his sons were specifically designated) and gracious (they were from among the Israelites, not inherently holy).

28:3

MacArthur: Tell all the skilled workers to whom I have given wisdom that they are to make garments for Aaron. The priestly garments are made by Spirit-gifted craftsmen following divine specification.

Hebrew Word Study

בִּגְדֵי קֹדֶשׁ bigdê qōdesh — holy garments: V.2-4: The priestly garments are made lĕkābôd ûlĕtipʾāret — "for dignity and beauty." The High Priest's garments were not functionally minimal but lavishly beautiful: blue, purple, scarlet, gold, fine linen, precious stones. The beauty of priestly vestments reflects the truth that approaching God engages the whole person — including the aesthetic. The priest dressed for the Presence as one dresses for a king.

חֹשֶׁן ḥōshen — breastplate of judgment: V.15-30: The ḥōshen mishpāṭ (breastplate of judgment) bore the twelve tribes on twelve stones over the High Priest's heart as he entered the Presence. The priest carried Israel before God literally — the people were physically borne on the chest of their mediator. Heb 4:14-16 reads Christ as the ultimate High Priest who carries his people "at the right hand of the Majesty in heaven." We are carried before the Father in the priestly ministry of the Son.

Historical Context

None

Context

The six garments listed (breastpiece, ephod, robe, woven tunic, turban, sash) constitute the full high priestly regalia. Each has theological significance: the ephod with its onyx shoulder stones bore Israel's names on the priest's shoulders ("bearing their names before the Lord on his two shoulders as a memorial," v.12). He carried Israel into God's presence literally — their names over his heart and on his shoulders.

Cross-Reference

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    28:2

    The phrase kavod vetiferet (dignity/glory and beauty/splendour) for the priestly garments is unique in the OT's use of these terms for clothing. Sarna notes that kavod (the word for divine glory) is here applied to human priestly dress — the priest's garments are a mediated form of the divine glory appropriate to his representative role. The priest does not share God's glory intrinsically; he wears its representation in the performance of his office.

    28:9–12

    The two onyx stones on the ephod's shoulder straps, each engraved with six names of the twelve tribes, are a representative act: Aaron carries the names of all twelve tribes into the divine presence. When the high priest enters the sanctuary, he brings the entire covenant community with him. The engraved names are the covenant community's membership list carried before God.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    28:6–14

    The ephod is the most ornate of the priestly garments — gold, blue, purple, scarlet, and fine twisted linen, with onyx stones bearing the tribal names. Alter notes the description combines the most precious materials with the most significant theological function: the ephod is the garment that holds the breastpiece (and therefore the Urim and Thummim) and carries the tribal names before God. It is simultaneously the most beautiful and the most theologically loaded garment in Israelite religion.

    28:3-5

    The artisans who make the priestly garments are specifically qualified as those 'whom I have given wisdom' — the Spirit-gift precedes the craft. Alter notes the divine qualification of craftsmen establishes a theology of Spirit-enabled work that ch.31 will develop further with Bezalel. Sacred work requires both human skill and divine enabling.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    28:2

    Calvin on priestly dignity and honour: 'Not only did God wish Aaron to be adorned with beautiful garments, that he might perform his holy functions with greater dignity, but that the Israelites might learn to approach God with reverence and not with levity.' The garments are not for Aaron's personal honour but for the community's theological education. The visual splendour of the priesthood teaches the people the gravity of the divine encounter. What the garments say about the priest, they say about the God he serves.

    28:12

    On Aaron bearing the names before the LORD: 'In this way he represented the whole people before God — a function pre-eminently fulfilled in Christ, who, entering into the heavenly sanctuary, bears all his own with him.' Calvin's typological reading: Aaron carrying the twelve names is the OT anticipation of Christ's high-priestly intercession — he brings his people with him into the divine presence.

    28:3

    The NET note on 'wisdom' (chokmah) as the craftsmen's qualification discusses the Hebrew concept of chokmah in the context of skill: in the OT, chokmah encompasses both intellectual wisdom and practical skill/craftsmanship. The 'wise-hearted' craftsmen (chakhmei-lev, literally 'wise of heart') are those whose practical skills are divinely endowed — there is no separation between spiritual gift and technical ability in the OT concept of craftsmanship.

The note observes that the listing before the details prepares the reader to understand each garment's place in the integrated system of priestly dress.

    Verses 6–30 — The Ephod, Breastpiece, and Urim and Thummim

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    28:21

    MacArthur: There are to be twelve stones, one for each of the names of the sons of Israel. The twelve stones on the breastpiece carry the covenant community into the divine presence. Aaron bears the names of all twelve tribes on his heart when he enters.

    28:36

    MacArthur: Holy to the LORD -- the inscription is Israel's vocation statement concentrated into three words. What the gold plate announces over Aaron's forehead is what the whole covenant community is called to embody.

    Hebrew Word Study

    אוּרִים וְתֻמִּים ûrîm wĕtummîm — Lights and Perfections: V.30: The Urim and Thummim — placed in the breastplate — were the priestly method of discerning God's will on significant questions. Their exact nature is unknown (possibly sacred lots, possibly inscribed stones). What matters is their function: Israel's High Priest had a means of accessing divine guidance in difficult decisions. The Spirit's guidance for which Christians pray (John 14:26; Jas 1:5) is the new covenant analog of this Urim and Thummim function.

    צִיץ tsîts — golden plate / diadem: V.36-38: The tsîts — a gold plate engraved "HOLY TO THE LORD" (qōdesh laYHWH) — was attached to the High Priest's turban and worn on his forehead. Its function: "Aaron is to bear the guilt involved in the sacred gifts the Israelites consecrate." The High Priest absorbs the ritual imperfection of the offerings on Israel's behalf, making them acceptable. Christ fulfills this: "God made him who had no sin to be sin for us" (2 Cor 5:21).

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    The shoulder stones and breastpiece together encapsulate the priest's mediatorial role: he carried Israel on his shoulders (responsibility) and over his heart (love). When he entered the Most Holy Place, he brought the whole nation with him — their names engraved in stone, carried before God. The high priest was not a private worshipper; he was the representative of an entire people.

    Cross-Reference

    • Heb 7:25Christ "always lives to intercede" for those who come to God through him — the eternal high priest carrying his people before God.
    • John 17:9Jesus' high priestly prayer: "I pray for them" — bearing the names of his people over his heart before the Father.
    • Isa 49:16"I have engraved you on the palms of my hands." God bearing Israel before him — the inverse of the priest bearing Israel before God.

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    28:29

    The repetition of 'over his heart' in v.29 (tribal names) and v.30 (Urim and Thummim) creates a deliberate theological emphasis: the priest's heart-position is the channel of covenant representation. Sarna notes that 'heart' (lev) in Hebrew is the seat of will, understanding, and decision — not merely emotion. The priest who bears Israel over his heart is the one who is most fully invested in the community's welfare before God.

    28:38

    The gold plate's function — bearing 'the guilt arising from the holy gifts the Israelites consecrate' — addresses a theological problem: even properly performed sacred acts can be insufficiently holy (cf. Eccl 5:1–2). The plate provides continuous atonement for the inevitable shortfall of human worship. Sarna notes this is a remarkably honest acknowledgment within the cult itself: the worship system carries provision for the system's own inadequacy.

    28:30-35

    The bells and pomegranates on the robe's hem announce the high priest's entrance into the Holy Place. Sarna notes the auditory dimension of holiness: the priest's approach to the divine presence is signalled by sound. The bells are not decorative but functional — they announce an arrival that would otherwise be silent and therefore potentially fatal.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    28:36–38

    The gold plate inscription — 'Holy to the LORD' — is the tabernacle's mission statement in three words. Alter notes its placement on the turban, above the face, makes it the first thing visible when the high priest is seen. Before you see his face, you see his calling: 'Holy to the LORD.' The inscription is the priestly identity — what the priest is, visibly declared. Every priestly act is performed under this banner.

    28:29-30

    Aaron's bearing of the twelve tribal names on his breastpiece 'as a continuing memorial before the LORD' establishes the high priest as the covenant community's representative in the divine presence. Alter notes the priestly function: he carries the people before God; he brings the divine response back to the people. The breastpiece encodes this mediation.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    28:30

    Calvin on the Urim and Thummim: 'God wished to consult himself, as it were, and to be consulted by his people, through the medium of the high priest.' The oracle's function is the institutionalisation of divine consultation. The covenant community has an authorised channel of divine communication — not improvised prayer alone but a structured priestly process. Calvin emphasises that this function is fulfilled by Christ: all divine consultation now passes through the great high priest.

    28:38

    Calvin on the gold plate bearing Israel's guilt: 'Aaron was a type of Christ, who, by coming between us and God, takes upon himself all our pollution... covering our unworthiness with his righteousness.' The high priest's gold plate covering the inadequacy of Israel's worship is the OT image of Christ's righteousness covering the inadequacy of the church's worship. The worship system's self-provision for its own shortfall is the typological architecture of the doctrine of imputed righteousness.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    28:30

    The NET note on Urim and Thummim discusses their use in the OT: consulted at military decisions (Num 27:21; 1 Sam 28:6), tribal land allocation (Josh 18–19), and judicial enquiry (1 Sam 14:41–42). They disappear from the narrative after David's era and are not available in the post-exilic period (Ezra 2:63; Neh 7:65). The note suggests their physical nature may have been intentionally obscured to prevent misuse — the text describes their function but not their form.

    28:36-38

    The NET note on qodesh laYHWH (Holy to the LORD) on the gold plate discusses the placement on the turban above Aaron's forehead: the inscription is positioned at the intersection of the priest's identity and his representational function. What is written over his face is what he embodies.

    Verses 31–43 — The Robe, the Gold Plate, and the Remaining Garments

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    28:42-43

    MacArthur: The linen undergarments cover the priests' nakedness. The requirement for covering recalls the provision of garments for Adam and Eve after the Fall (Gen 3:21). The approach to God requires that sin be covered.

    28:31-43

    MacArthur: The blue robe, the fringe of pomegranates and bells, the linen tunic — the high priest's garments are covenant theology in textile. The bells that announce his movement in the Most Holy Place are the audible signal that the representative of Israel is approaching and has survived the encounter with divine holiness.

    Hebrew Word Study

    אֵפוֹד ʾēpôd — ephod: V.6-14: The ʾēpôd (ephod) was the distinctive priestly garment — a woven apron of gold, blue, purple, and scarlet with two onyx shoulder pieces bearing the twelve tribal names. The High Priest literally carried all Israel on his shoulders (v.12: "Aaron is to bear the names on his shoulders"). The image of bearing a community on one's shoulders — one who carries them before God — is the incarnational model of pastoral ministry.

    רִמּוֹן rimmôn — pomegranate: V.33-34: The hem of the High Priest's robe alternated golden bells and pomegranates (rimmōnîm) — crimson, blue, and purple fabric fruit. The bells rang as the priest moved in the holy place: "the sound will be heard when he enters the Holy Place before the LORD and when he comes out, so that he will not die." The audibility of priestly presence in the holy place established that the community could track their mediator's movement on their behalf.

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    The gold plate "Holy to the Lord" (v.36–38) was the high priest's most theologically significant garment. He bore on his forehead the guilt of Israel's sacred offerings — making them acceptable to God. Even Israel's best worship was tainted by human imperfection; the priest bore that guilt so it would be covered. Christ bears the guilt of all our imperfect worship and makes it acceptable before the Father.

      The auditory dimension of the priestly robe is unique among the tabernacle's furnishings — everything else is visual; the robe is also sonic. Alter notes the covenant logic: to approach the holy God improperly is not merely a ritual violation but an existential danger. The garments are not costume but covenant protection.

      Ancient Sources

      ANE Priestly Vestments
      Egyptian, Mesopotamian, and Hittite priestly garments are well documented. Egyptian high priests wore leopard skins and special headdresses. The Tabernacle garments are distinct: no animal imagery, no magical inscriptions — only Israel's names and "Holy to the Lord."
      The priestly garments are culturally intelligible but theologically distinctive.
      Urim and Thummim
      Divination lots are attested across the ANE — the Mesopotamian bārû-priests used various methods of determining divine will. Israel's Urim and Thummim are distinct: not magic or astrology but a covenantal instrument for seeking the God who speaks.
      The means of divine consultation is fitted into Israel's covenant framework.

      Textual Notes

      Scholarly Debates

      The Urim and Thummim: Oracle, Lots, or Something Else?
      Sacred lots (binary yes/no oracle)
      Most mainstream scholars
      The Urim and Thummim (v.30) were likely a pair of objects drawn by lot to give yes/no answers to specific questions addressed to God. 1 Sam 14:41–42 (LXX fuller text) describes their use for binary selection. They were 'taken out' (1 Sam 14:37–38) and gave definitive answers, consistent with a lot-oracle mechanism.
      Flash of divine light (illumination oracle)
      Some traditional Jewish interpreters
      The root ur (Urim = lights) suggests visual illumination — specific letters on the high priest's breastpiece would light up to spell out a divine answer. This interpretation appears in rabbinic sources (Talmud Yoma 73b) and in some patristic writers. The name 'Urim' (lights) favours this reading.
      Objects of unknown function
      Many critical scholars
      The exact nature of the Urim and Thummim is irretrievably lost. They appear in early Israelite texts (1 Sam, Num) and then disappear after the Babylonian exile. Post-exilic texts (Ezra 2:63) acknowledge that their function cannot be performed without a priest who knows how to use them.
      The lot-oracle interpretation has the most straightforward textual support. The illumination-oracle is more dramatic but relies primarily on the etymology of 'Urim.' Both are speculative; the text does not explain the mechanism. The theological point is unambiguous regardless of mechanism: the high priest had an authorised means of seeking divine guidance that has no post-exilic equivalent.
