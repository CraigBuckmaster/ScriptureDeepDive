Exodus 40

God Commands Erection; Moses Obeys; Glory Fills the Tabernacle

See on Timeline — Tabernacle
Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
40:1-2

MacArthur: Set up the tabernacle, the tent of meeting, on the first day of the first month. The tabernacle's erection is dated exactly one year after the Exodus began. The covenant community's first anniversary is marked by the installation of God's dwelling among them.

40:16

MacArthur: Moses did everything just as the LORD commanded him. The single sentence summarising the entire tabernacle construction and erection. Faithfulness to divine instruction is the standard; Moses achieved it.

Hebrew Word Study

קִים qîm — set up / raised up: V.17-19: "On the first day of the first month of the second year, the tabernacle was set up (hûqam)." The verb qûm (to rise, to set up, to establish) marks the moment of Tabernacle inauguration. The same verb describes the establishment of covenants (Gen 17:7) and the resurrection of the dead. The Tabernacle being "raised up" on the first day of the first month (New Year's Day) was a new year, a new covenant, a new beginning — ordered life recommencing around the divine Presence.

עָנָן ʿānān — cloud: V.34-38: "Then the cloud covered the tent of meeting, and the glory of the LORD filled the tabernacle. Moses could not enter the tent of meeting because the cloud had settled on it, and the glory of the LORD filled the tabernacle." The cloud (ʿānān) that had guided Israel from Egypt (13:21-22) now settled as permanent occupant of the Tabernacle. The divine Presence that had been mobile and external became resident and interior — a movement Jesus fulfills when the Spirit comes to dwell within believers (John 14:17).

Historical Context

None

Context

The command to erect the Tabernacle on the first of the first month creates a precise one-year symmetry: Israel left Egypt on the fifteenth of Nisan; the Tabernacle stands on the first of Nisan, twelve months later. The year of wilderness formation — plagues, Passover, Red Sea, Sinai, the calf, the covenant renewal — culminates in God moving in. The entire year's journey was preparation for this moment.

  • Rev 21:3"God's dwelling place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them." The ultimate fulfilment of Exod 40.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
40:34

MacArthur: The glory of the LORD filled the tabernacle. The glory cloud that filled the tabernacle (1 Kgs 8:11 at the Temple's dedication; John 1:14 at the Incarnation) is the covenant goal achieved.

40:36-37

MacArthur: Whenever the cloud lifted from above the tabernacle, the Israelites would set out; but if the cloud did not lift, they did not set out. The ending of Exodus is not arrival but departure -- the portable God leads the travelling people.

Hebrew Word Study

כָּבוֹד יְהוָה kābôd YHWH — glory of the LORD: V.34-38: The kābôd (glory) of the LORD that filled the completed Tabernacle is the climax of the entire book of Exodus. The same glory had appeared at the Red Sea (14:4), on Sinai (24:16), and now settled permanently in the midst of the people. This inaugurating glory-filling will be repeated at Solomon's Temple (1 Kgs 8:10-11) and its absence will mark the Exile (Ezek 10-11). Its return in fullness is the eschatological promise: "the earth will be filled with the knowledge of the glory of the LORD as the waters cover the sea" (Hab 2:14).

עַל-פִּי יְהוָה ʿal-pî YHWH — at the LORD's command: V.36-38: "In all the travels of the Israelites, whenever the cloud lifted from above the tabernacle, they would set out; but if the cloud did not lift, they did not set out — until the day it lifted." The entire movement of the wilderness community was governed by the cloud. ʿal-pî YHWH ("at the mouth/command of the LORD") was the governing principle of Israel's travel: not military calculation, seasonal timing, or human decision, but simple responsiveness to divine direction. This is the model of the Spirit-led life.

Historical Context

None

Context

The erection narrative (vv.17–33) repeats "as the Lord commanded him" with each element — six more times in addition to v.16. The total across Exod 39–40 reaches the theological number. Moses is meticulous: every furnishing placed, every lamp lit, every piece of bread set out, every offering made. The book's final verse of human action is "so Moses finished the work" — the same phrase used of God at creation (Gen 2:2).

    Hebrew Word Study

    מָלֵא māleʾ — filled / full: V.34-35: The tabernacle was "filled" (māleʾ) with the glory of the LORD — so completely that Moses could not enter. The same verb describes: the Spirit filling Bezalel (31:3); the earth filled with God's glory (Num 14:21); the Temple filled at dedication (1 Kgs 8:10). The eschatological vision is the whole earth filled with divine glory (Isa 6:3; Hab 2:14). The Tabernacle is the first down-payment on that promise: God fills what is consecrated and offered.

    לֵאמֹר lēʾmōr — saying / speaking: V.1: "The LORD said to Moses..." The book of Exodus opens with God's people in silence under oppression and closes with God speaking continuously, directing every movement of the liberated community. The lēʾmōr ("saying") that drives 40+ divine speeches throughout Exodus charts the transformation: from a people who cried out and were heard, to a people who move in response to God's own continuous word.

    Context

    The glory filling the Tabernacle (vv.34–35) is the climax of the entire book of Exodus — indeed the climax of the entire Pentateuch's narrative of covenant relationship. God descends into the dwelling his people built for him. The cloud that led them out of Egypt now leads from within. The fire that burned at Sinai now burns at the centre of the camp. The covenant promise — "I will dwell among them" (29:45) — is fulfilled.

    Ancient Sources

    Temple Dedication Parallels
    Ancient Near Eastern temple dedication texts describe the deity's arrival into the completed temple — the god "entering" the new house. The Tabernacle's glory-filling fits this pattern but with a radical difference: the God of Israel arrives uninvited, spontaneously, in overwhelming glory that even Moses cannot withstand.
    God's arrival in the Tabernacle is sovereign and unmanageable — not the controlled "entrance" of a deity into a managed ritual.
    Cloud and Fire
    The pillar of cloud and fire appeared at the Exodus (13:21) and now closes the book at the Tabernacle (40:38). The literary inclusio frames the entire Exodus narrative: cloud and fire from departure to dwelling.
    The cloud and fire form a literary bracket around the entire book.

    Reception History

    1 Kgs 8:10–11
    Solomon's Temple dedication: the same cloud fills the temple; the priests cannot minister. The Tabernacle's glory-filling is the template for all subsequent divine indwelling in the OT.

    Textual Notes

    Scholarly Debates

    Why Does Exodus End with the Glory and Not with Entry into Canaan?
    The Exodus's goal is divine dwelling, not territorial possession
    Childs, Fretheim
    The book ends where its purpose statement said it would (29:45: 'I will dwell among the Israelites'). The land is the next chapter; the covenant goal — divine presence among the covenant people — is achieved here.
    Editorial decision to separate Exodus from Numbers
    Source critics
    The Pentateuch's division into five books is a later editorial decision. The natural narrative continuation is the journey to Canaan. Exodus 40 is an artificial stopping point.
    The theological reading is more satisfying: the book that began with Israel unable to cry out ends with God so fully present that Moses cannot enter the tent. The reversal is complete. The Exodus narrative has achieved its end.
