Exodus 9

Plagues Five through Seven — Livestock, Boils, Hail

Verses 1—12 — Plagues Five and Six: Livestock and Boils
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

דֶּבֶר deber — plague / pestilence: V.3: The fifth plague — deber, a severe pestilence — kills Egypt's livestock while Israel's are unharmed. In later prophetic literature deber is one of the triad of divine judgment: sword, famine, and plague (Jer 14:12; Ezek 6:11). The progression of plagues follows an intensifying pattern: from nuisances (frogs, gnats) to economic devastation (livestock, hail, locusts) to existential threat (death of firstborn).

שְׁחִין shĕḥîn — boils / festering sores: V.9-11: The sixth plague — shĕḥîn, festering boils — strikes even the magicians who "could not stand before Moses because of the boils." Their earlier ability to replicate plagues collapses entirely. They are now themselves plague-afflicted, unable to maintain ritual purity for priestly function. The irony deepens: those who served Egypt's gods are marked by the same God those gods cannot resist.

Historical Context

None

Context

Plague five (livestock) is verified by Pharaoh's own inspection (v.7) — he sends to check, finds not one Israelite animal dead, and still hardens his heart. The evidence is direct, personal, and conclusive. The boils (plague six) are the first plague to come without warning and the first to physically afflict the Egyptians themselves (not just their environment). Verse 12 marks the first explicit statement that YHWH hardened Pharaoh's heart — previously it was Pharaoh hardening himself.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 9:17Paul cites Exod 9:16 — "for this very purpose I have raised you up, that I might show my power in you, and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth." The hardening is purposeful and global.
  • Rev 16:2The first bowl judgment: "harmful and painful sores came upon the people" — the boils of plague six as eschatological template.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:1–3

The fifth plague targets Egyptian livestock — horses, donkeys, camels, cattle, sheep and goats. Each of these had divine associations in Egypt: horses were sacred to Set; cattle to Hathor and Apis; rams to Khnum. The comprehensive livestock plague is a systematic judgment against the animal deities of Egypt. Sarna notes the theological precision: the God of Israel demonstrates sovereignty over the creatures Egypt worships.

9:7

Pharaoh's investigation — he sends to find out if Israelite livestock has died — is a significant narrative detail. Sarna notes it shows Pharaoh taking the discrimination claim seriously enough to verify. The investigation confirms: not one Israelite animal died. The evidence is as clear as it can be. His subsequent hardening is therefore not uninformed; it is defiance of verified evidence.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:1–7

The fifth plague is the most concisely narrated of the ten: announcement, execution, discrimination, verification, hardening — five beats in seven verses. Alter notes the compression accelerates the narrative pace. The plagues are building toward something; the economy of narration mirrors the increasing inevitability of the outcome. Pharaoh's investigation and subsequent hardening is the chapter's emotional peak — evidence examined and defied.

9:13–16

The announcement of the seventh plague contains the book's most explicit statement of the purpose of the hardening: 'For this purpose I have raised you up, to show you my power, so that my name may be proclaimed in all the earth.' Alter notes the scope has expanded: the Exodus is not merely about Israel's liberation or Egypt's judgment. It is a global declaration of divine identity. Pharaoh is the instrument through which the name of God becomes known throughout the earth.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:7

Calvin on Pharaoh's investigation: 'Pharaoh was forced against his will to make inquiry, as if he had hoped to have found some alleviation of his condition; but he is again convicted.' The investigation is Calvin's evidence of Pharaoh's bad faith — he looks for a loophole, finds none, and hardens anyway. Evidence cannot convert a heart committed to unbelief. This is Calvin's anthropology: the natural mind is not neutral toward evidence; it is actively hostile to what it does not want to see.

9:16

Calvin on being raised up for God's purpose: 'Although Pharaoh does not yield voluntary obedience to God, yet he is compelled to serve him involuntarily.' The sovereignty of God extends even to those who resist it — they serve the divine purpose through their resistance. The name of God is proclaimed through Pharaoh's defiance precisely because the defiance makes the divine response necessary and spectacular.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:3

The NET note on dever (pestilence/plague on livestock) discusses whether this is a specific disease or a general divine judgment. The note mentions anthrax as one proposed identification, which would fit the historical period. The note cautions against over-specific identification — the text presents the death as miraculously discriminating and precisely timed, which resists purely naturalistic explanation.

9:6-7

The NET note on Pharaoh investigating Israel's livestock and finding none dead discusses the theological meaning: the plague is not natural disease but targeted divine action. The investigation that confirms the separation between Israel and Egypt is itself an act that should produce belief. That it does not — 'yet his heart was unyielding' — is the definition of hardness.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
9:4

MacArthur: The LORD will make a distinction between the livestock of Israel and that of Egypt. The covenant community is protected within the same territory being judged -- a picture of the gospel's application to those under the blood.

9:12

MacArthur: For the first time the text says the LORD hardened Pharaoh's heart rather than Pharaoh hardened his heart. Six times Pharaoh hardens his own heart; then God's judicial hardening confirms what Pharaoh's own choices established.

Verses 13—35 — Plague Seven: Hail; Pharaoh’s Hardened Heart

Hebrew Word Study

בָּרָד bārād — hail: V.18-23: The seventh plague — bārād, hail — is announced with a warning: those who fear the word of the LORD (yārēʾ ʾet-devar YHWH) shelter their servants and livestock. Some Egyptian officials respond to the warning — the first Egyptians who "fear the word of the LORD." This small turning is theologically significant: the plagues are not just judgment but invitation. YHWH's self-revelation is an offer of knowledge that anyone can accept.

הִכְבַּד hiḵbad — hardened / made heavy: V.34: Pharaoh "hardened his heart" (wayyakbēd) — using the verb kābed (heavy, to make heavy). The word kābed also means "glory" and "liver" (the seat of emotion in Hebrew thought). Pharaoh's heart becomes heavy with refusal; by contrast Moses will speak of Israel's God being "glorified" (kābed) through the same events (14:4). Pharaoh's heaviness of heart and God's weight of glory are set against each other.

Historical Context

None

Context

The hail plague is the pivot of the plague sequence — the most explicitly theological, with the global purpose statement (v.16: "that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth"). For the first time, some of Pharaoh's servants "feared the word of the LORD" (v.20) and protected themselves — a mixed multitude is beginning to form even within Egypt. Pharaoh's confession (v.27) and immediate retraction (v.34) confirm the diagnosis: he can acknowledge YHWH's righteousness but cannot submit to it. The truth is received cognitively; it changes nothing volitionally.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 9:17Paul cites v.16 as the definitive statement of God's sovereign use of Pharaoh: "that I might show my power in you, and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth." The hail plague is the verse Paul builds his doctrine of election on.
  • Josh 2:10–11Rahab: "we have heard how the LORD dried up the water of the Red Sea... our hearts melted." The proclamation of YHWH's name in all the earth — promised in Exod 9:16 — has reached Jericho by Josh 2.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:14

The purpose statement — 'I will send all my plagues on your heart' — is unusual. Sarna notes plagues are typically sent on the body or the land; sending them 'on your heart' targets the inner person, the seat of decision-making. The phrase suggests that the entire plague sequence is directed at Pharaoh's will, not merely his resources. The goal is not destruction but revelation — forcing Pharaoh to encounter the reality of who God is.

9:24

The hail with fire running through it is uniquely lethal — the combination of ice and lightning was unknown in Egypt, which almost never experiences hail. Sarna notes that this climatic impossibility was itself a theological sign: natural forces are being combined in ways that transcend natural possibility. The Egyptians who feared God's word and took shelter (vv.20–21) had learned to read the signs; those who did not were destroyed.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:27

Pharaoh's confession of sin after the hail is his most elaborate verbal acknowledgement of divine justice. Alter notes the speech is grammatically more complex than his earlier responses — the syntax itself signals greater pressure. But the trajectory of the plague narrative makes clear that verbal complexity does not equal genuine change. Pharaoh's elaborate confessions are inversely proportional to his actual repentance. The more elaborate the words, the more complete the subsequent betrayal.

9:34–35

The chapter ends with a double hardening: 'Pharaoh sinned again and hardened his heart, he and his servants.' The servants are now explicitly complicit. The plague narrative has moved from Pharaoh's individual resistance to a collective hardening of the Egyptian ruling class. The Exodus will eventually harden every dimension of Egyptian power before breaking it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:20

Calvin on the Egyptians who feared God's word: 'Some, taught by so many punishments, began to bow before the power of God; others remained contumacious... and so it will always be, for God's judgments never find all men equally obedient.' The hail plague produces differentiation within Egypt — a remnant of God-fearers emerges. Calvin reads this as evidence that divine judgment always produces a remnant who respond rightly, even within the nation being judged.

9:27

On Pharaoh's confession: 'Although he acknowledges the justice of God and the sin of himself and his people, yet this is not true repentance but only that forced confession which extorted by pain... the ungodly often confess, under chastisement, what they immediately deny as soon as it is removed.' The distinction between pain-produced confession and genuine repentance is Calvin's practical theology of the hardened heart.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:18

The NET note on 'such as has never been in Egypt from the day it was founded until now' explains this is a standard superlative formula in the ancient world (cf. Joel 2:2; Dan 12:1) — it does not require that this specific weather event was literally unprecedented in all Egyptian history but that it was uniquely severe and divinely directed. The formula establishes the plague's extraordinary character.

9:26

The NET note on Goshen specifically spared from the hail notes this is the fifth time the narrative has marked Goshen's exemption (plagues 4–7). The accumulation of explicit exemptions serves a theological argument: the precision of divine protection is evidence of divine knowledge and covenantal commitment. The plagues are not scatter-shot; they are targeted.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
9:16

MacArthur: I have raised you up for this very purpose, that I might show you my power and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth. God declares his purpose directly to Pharaoh. Paul cites this in Romans 9:17.

9:27

MacArthur: Pharaoh's confession -- This time I have sinned -- is the closest thing to repentance in the plague narrative. But v.35 shows he hardened his heart again when the plague ended. Circumstantial confession without repentance produces no durable change.

Ancient Sources

Hail in Egypt
Hail is rare in Egypt (the Delta receives ~150mm rain annually) but documented. A major hail event would be catastrophic precisely because the infrastructure — crops, animals, people — is not protected against it.
The hail plague is plausible as a rare but hypernatural meteorological event.
Rom 9:17
Paul's explicit citation of Exod 9:16 in his argument about divine sovereignty — making this plague the exegetical anchor of one of the NT's most important doctrinal passages.
The hail plague is the most directly theologically significant plague for NT doctrine.

Reception History

Romans 9:17
Paul makes Exod 9:16 the foundation of his discussion of divine sovereignty and hardening — the plague's purpose-statement becomes the OT anchor of NT election theology.
Augustine, City of God
Pharaoh's false repentance (v.27,34) is for Augustine the paradigm of worldly sorrow vs. godly sorrow (2 Cor 7:10): acknowledging consequences without embracing truth.

Scholarly Debates

The Purpose of Pharaoh: Romans 9 and Divine Election
Pharaoh as vessel of wrath: individual election/reprobation
Augustine, Calvin, Reformed theology
Paul's use of Exod 9:16 in Rom 9:17 ('For this very purpose I raised you up') and Rom 9:18 ('he hardens whom he wills') establishes Pharaoh as the paradigm case of God's sovereign election to judgment. God prepared Pharaoh as a vessel of wrath (Rom 9:22) to demonstrate his power and proclaim his name. This is the basis for individual unconditional reprobation in Reformed theology.
Pharaoh as corporate/historical example
Arminian, New Perspective scholars
Paul uses Pharaoh to make a corporate/national point about God's freedom in election history (Israel vs. Egypt, Jacob vs. Esau), not to establish individual predestination to damnation. The hardening is judicial — response to persistent rebellion — not unconditional. God raised Pharaoh to his position of power precisely so the Exodus could produce global proclamation of the divine name.
Middle position: compatible agency
Fretheim, Wright
Both divine sovereignty and human freedom are real. God raised Pharaoh to his position and uses his choices; Pharaoh genuinely chooses to harden; these are compatible rather than competing realities. The text preserves both without resolution. Paul uses the story to establish divine freedom in election, not to settle the philosophical problem of determinism.
This is one of the most contested passages in Pauline theology. The Exodus text itself presents Pharaoh's hardening as both self-chosen and divinely used without philosophical resolution. The theological dispute is ultimately about the compatibility of divine sovereignty and human agency — a question the plague narrative raises acutely but does not resolve abstractly.
