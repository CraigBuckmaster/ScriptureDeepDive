Exodus 10

Plagues Eight and Nine — Locusts; Darkness

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–20 — Plague Eight: Locusts
1Then the Lord said to Moses, "Go to Pharaoh, for I have hardened his heart and the hearts of his officials so that I may perform these signs of mine among them2that you may tell your children and grandchildren how I dealt harshly with the Egyptians and how I performed my signs among them, and that you may know that I am the Lord."3So Moses and Aaron went to Pharaoh and said to him, "This is what the Lord, the God of the Hebrews, says: 'How long will you refuse to humble yourself before me? Let my people go, so that they may worship me.4If you refuse to let them go, I will bring locusts into your country tomorrow.5They will cover the face of the ground so that it cannot be seen. They will devour what little you have left after the hail, including every tree that is growing in your fields.6They will fill your houses and those of all your officials and all the Egyptians—something neither your parents nor your ancestors have ever seen from the day they settled in this land till now.'" Then Moses turned and left Pharaoh.7Pharaoh's officials said to him, "How long will this man be a snare to us? Let the people go, so that they may worship the Lord their God. Do you not yet realize that Egypt is ruined?"8Then Moses and Aaron were brought back to Pharaoh. "Go, worship the Lord your God," he said. "But tell me who will be going."9Moses answered, "We will go with our young and our old, with our sons and our daughters, and with our flocks and herds, because we are to celebrate a festival to the Lord."10Pharaoh said, "The Lord be with you—if I let you go, along with your women and children! Clearly you are bent on evil.11No! Have only the men go and worship the Lord, since that's what you have been asking for." Then Moses and Aaron were driven out of Pharaoh's presence.12And the Lord said to Moses, "Stretch out your hand over Egypt so that locusts swarm over the land and devour everything growing in the fields, everything left by the hail."13So Moses stretched out his staff over Egypt, and the Lord made an east wind blow across the land all that day and all that night. By morning the wind had brought the locusts;14they invaded all Egypt and settled down in every area of the country in great numbers. Never before had there been such a plague of locusts, nor will there ever be again.15They covered all the ground until it was black. They devoured all that was left after the hail—everything growing in the fields and the fruit on the trees. Nothing green remained on tree or plant in all the land of Egypt.16Pharaoh quickly summoned Moses and Aaron and said, "I have sinned against the Lord your God and against you.17Now forgive my sin once more and pray to the Lord your God to take this deadly plague away from me."18Moses then left Pharaoh and prayed to the Lord.19And the Lord changed the wind to a very strong west wind, which caught up the locusts and carried them into the Red Sea. Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

Locust swarms (Schistocerca gregaria) are documented in Egyptian administrative texts — one papyrus describes crops consumed, starvation following. The eastern wind (v.13: "the LORD brought an east wind upon the land") is the sirocco (khamsin) — a hot wind from the Arabian desert that periodically devastates Egyptian agriculture. The western wind that removed the locusts (v.19) is equally natural — and equally under YHWH's command.

Context

Two new elements appear in plague eight: (1) the explicit memorial purpose — "that you may tell your son and grandson" (v.2) — establishes the Exodus as a story to be retold in every generation; (2) Pharaoh's officials break ranks and urge capitulation (v.7: "Egypt is ruined"). Pharaoh's court has reached the same conclusion as the magicians in plague three — but Pharaoh still hardens his heart. He is now isolated even from his own advisors.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 12:26–27The "tell your children" command of 10:2 is fulfilled in the Passover ritual: "What does this ceremony mean to you?" — the institutionalised retelling of the Exodus.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:1–2

The intergenerational purpose announced in v.2 is foundational to Israelite worship. Sarna identifies this as the first explicit commandment to tell children about the Exodus — a command that becomes the structural principle of the Passover Seder (Exod 13:8–9; Deut 6:20–25). The plagues are designed not only as historical events but as memorable, retellable demonstrations that will shape Israelite identity across all future generations.

10:13

The east wind that brings the locusts is the qadim — the hot, dry desert wind from the Arabian Peninsula. Sarna notes that while locust plagues were historically known in Egypt, they were extremely rare; the region's climate typically produced western winds. An east wind strong enough to carry a locust swarm of this density from the east is climatologically extraordinary — another sign that natural forces are under divine direction.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:7

The servants' intervention — 'Do you not yet understand that Egypt is ruined?' — is a dramatic shift in the narrative. For the first time, voices within Pharaoh's own court plead for capitulation. Alter notes the political realism: the servants are not theologians; they are pragmatists who have witnessed enough to calculate the outcome. Their plea makes Pharaoh's subsequent hardening even more wilful — he is now defying not only God but his own advisors.

10:24–26

Pharaoh's final compromise — take everyone but leave the livestock — is met by Moses' most absolute refusal: 'not a hoof shall be left behind.' The negotiating positions have reached their limits. Both parties know it. Pharaoh's expulsion of Moses in v.28 is the breaking point; the next chapter will be the final act.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1–2

Calvin on the intergenerational purpose: 'God wished his mighty power to be celebrated throughout all ages, lest the memory of so great a benefit should perish.' The plagues are designed for retelling — they create the content of Israelite worship, the stories that will be rehearsed at Passover tables for millennia. The didactic purpose of the Exodus is built into the plague narrative from the beginning.

10:16–17

Pharaoh's confession after the locusts — 'I have sinned against the LORD your God and against you' — is his most elaborate verbal capitulation. Calvin notes the specificity: 'against the LORD your God and against you' names both dimensions of the sin. But the pattern holds: confession under pressure, betrayal under relief. Calvin reads the elaborate confession as evidence of how deeply the conscience can be moved without the will being changed.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:4

The NET note on locusts (arbeh) explains the term derives from the root rabah ('to be many') — locusts are named for their multitude. The note discusses the historical ecology: locust swarms of biblical proportions (covering the land so it was 'darkened') are documented in North Africa and the Middle East. A swarm can cover hundreds of square miles and consume vegetation completely. The plague's scale, timing, and direction (east wind, then west wind removing them) are the signs of divine direction.

10:22

The NET note on the darkness plague notes that complete darkness — 'darkness that could be felt' — for three days is not naturally occurring in Egypt. The note discusses the khamsin (a dusty windstorm that can reduce visibility) as a possible natural mechanism intensified divinely, but notes that three days of total darkness affecting Egypt while Goshen had light is not reducible to any natural phenomenon.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The darkness plague is no-warning (like the boils) and reaches the deepest theological point of the plague sequence: Ra, the sun-god, the supreme deity of Egypt, whose image Pharaoh bears as divine son — YHWH extinguishes him for three days. The darkness is "to be felt" — not merely visual absence but a presence of darkness. Three days echoes creation (light on day one) and anticipates the three days of Jonah and the three days of Christ in the tomb. The final verse — Pharaoh's threat and Moses's acceptance of it — sets up the last and greatest plague.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 1:3"Let there be light" — YHWH is the creator of light. The darkness plague reverses creation in Egypt while preserving it in Goshen. YHWH does with Egypt's sun what he did with the world's light: he controls it absolutely.
  • Exod 14:20The pillar of cloud: light to Israel, darkness to Egypt. The same polarity at the Red Sea.
  • Matt 27:45Three hours of darkness at the crucifixion — the ninth plague's three days of darkness as typological background.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:21–23

The darkness plague is directed against Ra, the sun god, Egypt's most important deity. Sarna notes that the Pharaoh himself was considered the son of Ra — the supreme identification between divine and royal in Egyptian religion. Darkness for three days was not merely an inconvenience; it was the annihilation of the divine power that defined Egyptian civilisation. The light in Goshen is the counter-sign: YHWH's people live in light while the sun-god's people sit in darkness.

10:24-29

Pharaoh's final offer — 'go, worship the LORD, only leave your flocks and herds' — and Moses' refusal constitute the last negotiation before the final plague. Sarna notes Moses' answer is theologically precise: 'We do not know what we will use to worship the LORD until we arrive there.' Worship requires full freedom; no pre-commitment to what God will require.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:21–23

The darkness plague is narrated with unusual physicality: 'darkness that could be felt' (v.21), 'a thick darkness' (v.22), no one rose from his place for three days. Alter notes the immobility — people literally unable to move — is the plague's most powerful expression. The society that built monuments with forced labour is now paralysed. The contrast with Israel ('had light in their dwellings') is the sharpest discrimination in the narrative.

10:24-29

The final negotiation breaks down irreparably: Pharaoh's offer leaves the livestock; Moses refuses; Pharaoh says 'if you appear before me again you will die.' Alter notes the dramatic irony: this is the last time Moses will see Pharaoh. The death threat seals the relationship's end before the final plague.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:22–23

Calvin reads the palpable darkness as a judgment on Egyptian unbelief: 'Let us learn that nothing is more miserable than to be left by God in the darkness of our own mind.' The physical darkness mirrors the spiritual darkness that Pharaoh has inhabited throughout the plague narrative. Egypt's sun-god cannot produce light; the God of Israel provides it specifically for his people. The physical and spiritual dimensions of the sign correspond precisely.

10:22-23

Calvin on the three days of darkness: 'The light of the sun was extinguished for three days — not that the sun ceased its course, but God intercepted the sun's light from reaching Egypt. This miracle was specifically directed against the Egyptian sun worship.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:21

The NET note on 'darkness that can be felt' (choshek afelah) explains afelah is an intensified form of darkness — absolute, impenetrable blackness. The note discusses whether this could refer to a severe khamsin (dust storm) lasting three days, which would explain the physicality. But the discrimination (light in Goshen) exceeds any natural explanation. The note notes that three-day complete darkness is without known natural parallel for the Nile Delta region.

10:24-27

The NET note on 'not a hoof is to be left behind' discusses the legal and theological point: worship requires bringing everything that might be needed. Moses cannot pre-commit what sacrifices the LORD will require before arriving at the place of worship. The demand for complete freedom is not stubbornness but covenant principle.

Ancient Sources

Ra and Pharaonic Theology
Pharaoh was considered the divine son of Ra (the sun-god), the embodiment of his rule on earth. Ra's daily journey across the sky was the theological cornerstone of Egyptian religion.
The darkness plague targets Egyptian theology at its pinnacle — the supreme deity's primary domain.
Khamsin / Sirocco
The hot desert wind (khamsin) can bring sandstorms that blot out the sun for days — known as "the darkness that can be felt." Whether the plague was a hypernatural khamsin or something entirely different, the description matches an Egyptian meteorological phenomenon taken to an extreme.
The "felt darkness" description is culturally grounded.

Reception History

Matt 27:45
Three hours of darkness at the crucifixion during Jesus's death — early Christian interpreters read this as the darkness plague's typological fulfilment: as YHWH darkened Egypt's sun-god in judgment, so the sun is darkened at the death of the Son of God.
Rev 16:10
The fifth bowl judgment darkens the throne of the beast — the darkness plague as eschatological template, targeting the center of anti-God power.

Scholarly Debates

Pharaoh's Servants' Role in the Hardening Narrative
Servants as morally culpable accomplices
MacArthur, most conservative scholars
Exodus 9:34 explicitly states 'he and his servants hardened their hearts.' The servants are morally responsible participants in the corporate resistance to God. Their later plea (10:7) does not exonerate them — it merely shows their pragmatic calculation has changed while Pharaoh's has not.
Servants as narrative device for escalation
Alter, literary critics
The servants' plea in 10:7 functions narratively to isolate Pharaoh — he is now resisting not only God and Moses but his own court. The servants are less developed characters than Pharaoh; their shift marks the political isolation that precedes Pharaoh's total defeat in ch.14.
Both readings are compatible. The servants are morally responsible accomplices (9:34) who reach a pragmatic tipping point before Pharaoh does (10:7). Their shift does not earn them exemption from the final plague (12:29 kills the firstborn of 'all the servants'), but it does mark the narrative's final stage before the climactic tenth plague.
