Sabbath Restated; the Offering; Bezalel and Oholiab Announced

Verses 1–19 — Sabbath Restated; the Offering and Skills Announced

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
35:2

MacArthur: The seventh day shall be your holy day. The Sabbath command is given again in the context of tabernacle construction -- even the building of the sanctuary must not override the covenant sign.

35:10

MacArthur: All who are skilled among you are to come and make everything the LORD has commanded. The tabernacle's construction requires every skilled person to contribute -- the universal mobilisation of gifts for God's house.

Hebrew Word Study

נְדִיבֵי לֵב nĕdîbê leb — willing hearts / generous hearts: V.5,21-22: "Everyone who is willing (nĕdîb leb) is to bring to the LORD an offering." The Tabernacle is built entirely by freewill offering — no taxation, no coercion. The word nādîb (generous, willing, noble) is used of those whose hearts were moved (nĕśāʾô libbô — lit. "their hearts lifted them"). The outpouring of generosity described in vv.21-29 — everyone brought more than was needed (36:6-7) — models the principle of 2 Cor 9:7.

כָּל חֲכַם לֵב kol ḥakam leb — all who are skilled / wise-hearted: V.10,25: "All who are skilled among you are to come and make everything the LORD has commanded." The phrase ḥakam leb ("wise/skilled of heart") appears repeatedly in the Tabernacle construction narrative. In Hebrew thought, skill resides in the heart, not the hands: craftsmanship is a matter of inner formation, not merely technical training. The wise heart is the Spirit-filled heart (31:3-4) that translates vision into realized form.

Historical Context

None

Context

The Sabbath command (vv.1–3) comes before the work begins — a deliberate counterweight to the urgency of the project. Even the most sacred building project must stop on the seventh day. The specific prohibition against lighting fire on the Sabbath (v.3) may address the smelting and metalwork required for Tabernacle construction — even holy work respects God's rest pattern.

    Verses 20–29 — The Willing-Heart Offering
    20212223242526272829

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    35:21

    MacArthur: Everyone who was willing and whose heart moved them came and brought an offering to the LORD. The tabernacle is financed by voluntary generosity, not mandatory taxation. When grace is truly understood, generosity overflows.

    35:20-29

    MacArthur: The voluntary freewill offering is the economic foundation of the tabernacle. Every material used in the sanctuary came from the same community that had just sinned catastrophically with the golden calf. The offering is simultaneously repentance, restoration, and worship — the community redirects its gold from the calf to the covenant dwelling.

    Hebrew Word Study

    תְּרוּמָה tĕrûmāh — offering raised up: V.21-29: The tĕrûmāh (offering "raised up" before God) came from the entire community. Men and women alike contributed: jewelry, linens, skills, time. The list of materials is specific — gold, silver, bronze, blue/purple/scarlet yarns, fine linen, goat hair, rams' skins, acacia wood, spices, olive oil, onyx, gems. The Tabernacle embodied the entire community's sacrifice: when they worshipped within it, they worshipped within their own offered lives.

    שַׁבָּת שַׁבָּתוֹן shabbāt shabbātôn — Sabbath of complete rest: V.2-3: The Tabernacle construction instructions open with a Sabbath reminder — even building God's house must not violate God's rest. The phrase shabbāt shabbātôn (a Sabbath of complete Sabbath-ness) is the most emphatic form: total rest. The proximity of Tabernacle-building and Sabbath command makes a profound point: the rest that the Tabernacle's God embodies is not overridden by the urgency of sacred construction. The work of God must be done in the rhythm of God.

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    The giving described in vv.21–29 is one of the most beautiful passages in Exodus. Men and women bring gold jewelry — earrings, brooches, rings. Skilled women spin yarn. Leaders bring gems. Everyone gives what they have, and they give it willingly. The contrast with ch.32 is stark: the same gold earrings that went into the golden calf are now being given for the Tabernacle. The repentance of ch.32–34 has produced transformed generosity.

    Cross-Reference

    • 2 Cor 9:7"Each of you should give what you have decided in your heart to give, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver." Paul's principle mirrors the Tabernacle giving precisely.
    • Exod 36:6–7The people gave too much and had to be restrained — the only offering drive in Scripture where the problem was excess generosity.

    Verses 30–35 — Bezalel and Oholiab Announced; Spirit-Gifted Teachers
    303132333435

    Hebrew Word Study

    אֹהֶל ʾōhel — tent fabrics / weaving: V.26: "All the women who were willing and had the skill spun the goat hair." The Tabernacle was a community product — artisans, craftswomen, metalworkers, embroiderers, donors. No single figure (except Bezalel as master-craftsman) is elevated. The corporate nature of sacred construction is itself a theological statement: the house of God belongs to the people of God, built by all of them together.

    נְשִׂיאִם nĕśîʾîm — leaders / chiefs: V.27-28: The leaders (nĕśîʾîm) brought the onyx and other gems for the priestly garments and the spices and oils for anointing and incense. Their special contribution — the most precious materials — reflects a tithe of leadership: those who lead give most. The pattern recurs throughout Scripture: the greatest gift comes from the most prominent position (cf. Luke 21:1-4 where the widow's offering exceeds all, and John 19:39 where Nicodemus brings 75 pounds of spices).

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    The announcement of Bezalel and Oholiab (vv.30–35) answers the question the offering raises: the materials are now here; who has the skill to use them? God has already provided the answer — Spirit-filled craftsmen, equipped before the project began. This is the pattern of divine provision: the resources and the skills arrive together, both given by God, both freely offered by the community.

      Ancient Sources

      Voluntary Temple Offerings
      Ancient Near Eastern temple building was typically funded by royal decree and corvée labor. Israel's freewill offering model is exceptional in the ancient world — a whole community voluntarily funding a sacred project.
      The willing-heart model is theologically and historically distinctive.
      Women's Participation
      The spinning women of vv.25–26 represent significant female participation in the Tabernacle project. Their skilled work (spinning yarn) is explicitly honored as a contribution.
      The Tabernacle project is notable for the explicit inclusion of women's contributions.

      Reception History

      2 Cor 9:6–8
      Paul's theology of giving draws directly on the Tabernacle model: "whoever sows generously will also reap generously... God loves a cheerful giver."
      1 Chr 29:14
      David's prayer at the Temple offering: "Who am I, and who are my people, that we should be able to give as generously as this? Everything comes from you, and we have given you only what comes from your hand." The Tabernacle giving posture continued in Temple building.

      Scholarly Debates

      The Willing Heart: Covenant Response or Natural Impulse?
      Covenant response to grace
      Calvin, Reformed tradition
      The willing heart is the fruit of the Spirit's work in response to covenant grace already received. Israel gives freely because God gave first — the Exodus, Sinai, forgiveness after the golden calf.
      Natural religious community dynamics
      Sociological reading
      The outpouring of gifts reflects the social and religious dynamics of a community with a shared project. Large voluntary communal gifts are a feature of intense religious community life.
      Both dynamics are present. The covenant-response reading is theologically primary — the text frames the giving as response to the forgiveness and renewed covenant of chs.33–34.
