Exodus 6

God Renews His Promise; The Covenant Name LORD; Genealogy

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1—13 — God Renews His Covenant Promise: I Am the Lord
12345678910111213

Hebrew Word Study

שְׁמִי יְהוָה shemî YHWH — my name is the LORD: V.3: "I appeared to Abraham, Isaac and Jacob as God Almighty (El Shaddai), but by my name the LORD (YHWH) I did not make myself fully known to them." This is not a contradiction of earlier texts where YHWH appears — it is a statement about depth of revelation. The patriarchs knew the name; Exodus is the generation that will know the name through covenant fulfillment in action. YHWH is the covenant name, the name of redemption.

בְּרִית berît — covenant: V.4-5: The word berît (covenant) anchors the entire Exodus commission: God hears Israel's groaning and "remembers" his covenant. In biblical Hebrew, "remembering" a covenant (zākar berît) means actively fulfilling its terms, not merely recalling a past agreement. The four-fold promise of v.6-8 ("I will bring out / I will free / I will redeem / I will take") is the covenant being remembered into action.

Historical Context

None

Context

The seven "I will" statements (vv.6–8) are God's covenant oath in response to Moses's complaint. They structure the entire Exodus narrative: each "I will" is a divine commitment that the plagues, the crossing, and the covenant at Sinai will fulfil. The number seven signals completeness — this is the totality of God's redemptive intention for Israel. Jewish tradition reads these seven statements as the basis for four cups of wine at Passover (corresponding to vv.6–7).

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:2–3

The statement 'by my name the LORD I did not make myself known to them' (Abraham, Isaac, Jacob) is the crux of the documentary source division: J uses YHWH throughout Genesis; E and P do not. Source critics take 6:3 as the P source's introduction of the divine name. Conservative scholars read 6:3 as a claim about experiential knowledge — the patriarchs knew the name but not its full covenant significance as the God who redeems an entire nation from superpower oppression.

6:6

The verb 'redeem' (ga'al) appears here for the first time in Exodus. It is the language of the kinsman-redeemer (go'el) who buys back family members from slavery. By using this term, God presents himself as Israel's divine kinsman — the one with both the obligation and the power to purchase his people's freedom. The Exodus is framed as a family redemption, not merely a national liberation.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:2–8

The renewed commission in ch.6 is notably more formal and elaborated than the burning bush encounter. Alter notes the shift in register: this passage has the structure of a covenant formulary — self-identification, historical recital, promise enumeration, concluding formula. The seven-fold 'I will' of vv.6–8 has the character of a legal covenant document. God is not merely encouraging Moses; he is formally binding himself to a programme of action.

6:9

'But they did not listen to Moses, because of their broken spirit and harsh slavery.' Alter notes the phrase qotzer ruach (shortness of spirit, crushed spirit) is unique in the OT. The slaves cannot receive the liberation because oppression has destroyed their capacity to imagine it. This is a realistic observation about the psychology of the enslaved — freedom must be enacted before it can be believed, not the reverse.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:3

Calvin on the disclosure of the divine name: 'God had made himself known to the patriarchs under a different form — he had not yet displayed that power by which he was about to free his people. Now, therefore, he promises that he will be known by a new and unusual proof of his power.' The name YHWH is not new information but new experience — the patriarchs knew the name; what they did not know was the full saving power the name encodes. The Exodus is the name's experiential definition.

6:6–7

Calvin on the covenant promises: 'In these words, as in a mirror, we behold the grace of God towards his Church from the beginning. He binds himself to his people by his word; he makes them his peculiar possession; he promises to be their God.' The seven 'I wills' are Calvin's proof that the initiative in the covenant is entirely divine. God commits himself; Israel does not earn the commitment.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:3

The NET note on El Shaddai ('God Almighty') explains the etymology is debated: possibly from Hebrew shadday ('sufficient') or Akkadian shadu ('mountain'). Most likely it emphasises divine power and provision. The note distinguishes the two forms of divine self-disclosure: El Shaddai = God as protector and provider of the patriarchal promises; YHWH = God as covenant-keeper and active redeemer. Both are true of the same God; they emphasise different aspects of the divine character.

6:6

The NET note on ga'al ('redeem') discusses the go'el institution: the nearest male relative who had both the obligation and right to redeem family property or members from slavery. By presenting himself as Israel's go'el, God is claiming kinship responsibility. The Exodus is not an act of power alone but an act of covenantal family obligation — God redeems his people because they are his people.

Verses 14—30 — The Genealogy of Moses and Aaron
1415161718192021222324252627282930

Hebrew Word Study

גָּאַל gāʾal — redeem / kinsman-redeemer: V.6: "I will redeem you with an outstretched arm." The verb gāʾal — the kinsman-redeemer root (cf. Boaz/Ruth) — is used here for God's own redemptive action. As the gōʾēl pays the price to free a relative from slavery or debt, God acts as Israel's divine kinsman to buy them out of Egypt. This becomes the paradigm for all subsequent redemption theology including Paul's in Gal 3-4.

מוֹעֵד môʿēd — appointed time / meeting: The genealogy in Exod 6:14-25 interrupts the narrative to anchor Moses and Aaron in tribal history. Their Levitical credentials — descendants of Levi, of Kohath, of Amram — are essential. The one who will mediate the covenant must be traceable. The genealogy is not bureaucratic padding but theological preparation: the covenant people are a named, rooted community, not a crowd.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:14–25

The genealogy reaches its climax in the naming of Aaron's grandson Phinehas (v.25), whose priestly lineage Sarna identifies as the point of the entire genealogical digression. Phinehas will appear in Num 25:7–13 as the zealous priest who averts divine wrath and is rewarded with a covenant of perpetual priesthood. The genealogy traces the Aaronic line to its most celebrated heir — validating the entire priestly tradition that Exodus inaugurates.

6:26-27

The genealogy closes by explicitly connecting its two central figures to the narrative frame: 'It was this Aaron and Moses to whom the LORD said... it was they who spoke to Pharaoh.' Sarna notes the repetition is the genealogy's theological conclusion: the cosmic mission of the Exodus is being executed by historically specific, genealogically verifiable men.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:14–27

The genealogy's placement — in the middle of Moses' renewed commission — is awkward by modern narrative standards but conventional in ancient composition. Alter notes the Wiederaufnahme (resumptive repetition) of vv.28–30 — the text essentially repeats the commissioning statement of v.10–12 after the genealogy, picking up the thread. This is a standard ancient editorial technique for handling embedded material. The genealogy validates what follows; the narrative frame resumes without apology.

6:26-30

The genealogy closes with a resumptive narrative device — repeating the frame that introduced it. Alter notes the effect: the genealogy is bracketed by the larger narrative, and when we return to Moses and Aaron they carry the genealogical weight of the entire Israelite lineage. They speak to Pharaoh as representatives of the whole covenant people.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:14–25

Calvin on the genealogy: 'Moses inserts the genealogy, that he may show his own origin, and also that of his brother Aaron. For their authority would have had less weight if their tribe and family had not been distinguished.' The genealogy is not pedantry but credentialing — it establishes Moses and Aaron as legitimate representatives of the covenant people, not self-appointed leaders of uncertain origin.

6:14-27

Calvin on the genealogical placement: Moses wanted to establish the authentic lineage of the Exodus leaders so no one could doubt their authority. The genealogy is legal certification as well as historical record — it establishes that Aaron and Moses stand in the direct line of the Levitical covenant.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:20

The NET note on Amram marrying his aunt Jochebed notes that Lev 18:12 later prohibits marriage between nephew and aunt. The note explains that the Sinai law was not yet in force — pre-Sinai patriarchal marriage customs were more permissive (cf. Abraham marrying his half-sister, Gen 20:12). The genealogical note is not inconsistency but evidence of historical distance between the patriarchal period and the Sinai legislation.

6:26-27

The NET note on the resumptive formula ('it was this Aaron and Moses') discusses its function as a discourse marker — signalling the end of the genealogical digression and the return to the main narrative. The note traces similar resumptive devices elsewhere in the Pentateuch.

Ancient Sources

Kinsman-Redeemer (gōʾēl) Law
Lev 25:47–55 formalises what God does here in ch.6 — the nearest kin's obligation to buy back an enslaved family member. God acts as Israel's divine gōʾēl.
The redemption theology of Exodus 6 is grounded in Israelite kinship law.
Hittite Suzerainty Treaties (c.1400–1200 BC)
These treaties begin with a historical prologue ("I am the one who...") followed by covenant stipulations — the structure of Exod 6:2–8 matches this form closely.
The seven covenant pledges follow the ANE treaty-formula pattern.

Scholarly Debates

Exodus 6:3 and the Documentary Hypothesis
Evidence for separate sources (J and P)
Wellhausen, Noth, von Rad
Genesis consistently uses YHWH (attributed to J), while P uses Elohim. Exodus 6:3 (attributed to P) introduces YHWH as a new name because P's author did not know of J's earlier usage. The contradiction is resolved by recognising two originally independent documents combined by later editors.
Single authorship — qualitative disclosure
Kitchen, Sarna, MacArthur
The patriarchs used the name YHWH (Gen 15:7; 28:13) but did not experience the redemptive acts the name encodes. Exodus 6:3 claims that the full covenantal significance of YHWH — as the God who redeems with mighty acts from national slavery — was not previously demonstrated. The 'not knowing' is experiential, not nominal.
Single authorship — rhetorical question reading
Buber, Benno Jacob
The Hebrew can be read as a rhetorical question: 'And by my name YHWH, was I not known to them?' This reading removes the apparent contradiction entirely and is grammatically possible, though requiring an interrogative sense without the usual particle.
The debate is foundational to Pentateuchal source criticism. Conservative scholars find the qualitative-disclosure reading exegetically coherent and historically plausible. The documentary hypothesis provides an alternative that resolves the surface tension by literary means. Both approaches have internal consistency; the choice involves prior commitments about biblical authorship and composition.
