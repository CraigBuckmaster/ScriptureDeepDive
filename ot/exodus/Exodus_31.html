Exodus 31

Bezalel and Oholiab; the Sabbath as Covenant Sign; the Stone Tablets

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–11 — Bezalel and Oholiab: Spirit-Filled Craftsmen
1234567891011

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
31:3

MacArthur: I have filled him with the Spirit of God -- Bezalel is the first person in Scripture explicitly identified as filled with the Spirit of God for craftsmanship. The Holy Spirit's enabling extends to all legitimate human activity done for God's purposes.

31:6

MacArthur: I have given ability to all the skilled workers. The gifts of craftsmanship are distributed throughout the community. The tabernacle is a community project requiring the gifts of many people all working under the same divine specification.

Hebrew Word Study

רוּחַ אֱלֹהִים rûaḥ ʾĕlōhîm — Spirit of God: V.3: "I have filled him with the Spirit of God (rûaḥ ʾĕlōhîm), with wisdom, with understanding, with knowledge and with all kinds of skills." Bezalel is the first person in Scripture described as filled with the Spirit of God — and it is for artistic work, not prophetic utterance. The Spirit enables creativity: the same divine Breath that brought order out of chaos at creation equips Bezalel to make beautiful things out of raw materials. All creativity, properly received, is Spirit-given.

מְלָאכָה mĕlāʾkāh — work / skilled craft: V.3-5: Bezalel's skills include ḥārashet (engraving/carving), works in gold, silver, and bronze, cutting and setting stones, woodworking "and every kind of skilled work (kol-mĕlāʾkāh)." The word mĕlāʾkāh is the same used for God's creative work (Gen 2:2-3) — Bezalel's craft participates in the divine creative activity. The Tabernacle is not merely constructed but created, making Bezalel the first recorded artist-theologian.

Historical Context

None

Context

Bezalel and Oholiab represent two tribes (Judah and Dan) — a priestly-royal combination covering the whole nation. Their Spirit-filling for craftsmanship is a deliberate theological statement: the work of building God's dwelling requires divine enablement, not just human skill. The most mundane-seeming task (cutting stone, weaving fabric) is Spirit-work when it serves God's purposes.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 11:2The sevenfold Spirit resting on the Messiah — wisdom, understanding, counsel, might, knowledge, fear of the Lord. The Bezalel endowment anticipates the messianic Spirit-filling.
  • Acts 6:3The seven deacons chosen for practical service must be "full of the Spirit and wisdom" — the Bezalel model applied to the church's practical needs.
  • Exod 35:30–36:1The fulfilment: Moses announces Bezalel's calling; the people bring offerings; the craftsmen begin work.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:3

Bezalel's Spirit-filling is the first explicit statement of this kind in the OT. Sarna notes the three gifts (wisdom, understanding, knowledge) establish artistic craftsmanship as a divine endowment. The tabernacle is not built by self-taught skill but by divinely granted capacity — reinforcing that the sanctuary belongs to God from its inception, not merely in its dedication.

31:6

The pairing of Bezalel (Judah) and Oholiab (Dan) — first and last tribes in the camp — represents the full community contributing to the sanctuary. Sarna notes the collaborative model anticipates Nehemiah's wall-building (every family rebuilding the section before their house) and the NT church's multiple-gifted body.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:1–11

Seven verses appoint and equip the two master craftsmen for the most elaborate construction project in Israel's history. The brevity after the architectural detail of chs.25–30 is deliberate — the human builders are instruments, not originators. The Spirit who fills Bezalel is the real builder; Bezalel's hands are the means.

31:3-6

Bezalel's Spirit-filling and Oholiab's appointment establish a theology of Spirit-enabled craftsmanship. Alter notes the same Spirit that empowered prophets and judges here equips craftsmen. The tabernacle's construction is not merely a building project but a Spirit-directed creation — the Spirit who moved over the waters at creation now equips the builders of the portable sanctuary.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
31:3

Calvin: 'The Holy Spirit is not only the author of wisdom, faith, and righteousness, but also the fountain of all gifts and faculties that conduce to human advantage... so that every excellence we behold in men must be regarded as proceeding from God.' Artistic and technical gifts are Spirit-gifts equally with spiritual gifts.

31:6

Calvin on Oholiab: 'This teaches us that the gifts of the Spirit are not confined to spiritual things but extend to the arts and professions of civil life.' No domain of human skill is outside the Spirit's purview.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
31:3

The NET note explains that chokmah in Exodus contexts denotes both intellectual wisdom and practical craft skill — the 'wise-hearted' craftsmen are those whose technical abilities are divinely endowed. Spirit-filling for craftsmanship establishes that sacred work and skilled work are not separate categories.

31:3-6

The NET note on 'I have filled him with the Spirit of God' (vaemaleh oto ruach Elohim) discusses this as one of the few Spirit-filling formulas in the Pentateuch. The note traces the Spirit's role in craftsmanship through the broader OT wisdom tradition — chokmah (wisdom) is both a divine attribute and a Spirit-given human capacity.

Verses 12–18 — The Sabbath as Covenant Sign; the Stone Tablets
12131415161718

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
31:13

MacArthur: You must observe my Sabbaths. This will be a sign between me and you for the generations to come. Even the building of God's house must stop on God's day. The Sabbath is the covenant sign that identifies Israel as the covenant community.

31:18

MacArthur: The two tablets of the covenant law, the tablets of stone inscribed by the finger of God. The divine authorship of the covenant law underscores its authority.

Hebrew Word Study

אוֹת ʾôt — sign / symbol: V.13: "You must observe my Sabbaths. This will be a sign (ʾôt) between me and you for the generations to come, so you may know that I am the LORD who makes you holy." The Sabbath is an ʾôt — a sign of the covenant relationship between YHWH and Israel. The same word is used for circumcision (Gen 17:11) and the rainbow (Gen 9:12-13). Signs are not explanations but embodied declarations of covenantal reality, visible reminders of an invisible commitment.

חֵרֶף / חָלַל ḥillēl — profane / desecrate: V.14: "Anyone who desecrates (mĕḥalĕlehā) it is to be put to death." The verb ḥālal (to desecrate, to make common what is holy) is the opposite of qādash. The severity of the Sabbath law reflects the severity of what is being defaced: the Sabbath is God's own rest imprinted on human time. Profaning it erodes the image of God in human society. Heb 4:1-11 re-reads Sabbath as the "rest" that remains for God's people in Christ — still sacred, now spiritual.

Historical Context

None

Context

The Sabbath command here comes between the Tabernacle instructions (chs.25–31) and the golden calf catastrophe (ch.32). Its placement is deliberate: even while building God's dwelling, Israel must stop on the seventh day. The Tabernacle construction does not justify Sabbath violation. This ruling would later ground the prohibition on Temple construction on the Sabbath — even the holiest work stops. The principle: God's rest pattern is not suspended by human urgency.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 2:2–3The Sabbath rooted in creation — "God rested on the seventh day." The Sinai covenant institutionalises what creation established.
  • Heb 4:9–11The Sabbath rest as the type of the believer's spiritual rest in Christ — "there remains, then, a Sabbath rest for the people of God."
  • Rev 1:10John on the Lord's Day — the first-day gathering that fulfils and transforms the Sabbath pattern.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:13

The Sabbath as sign (ot) marks sacred time rather than a location or body. Sarna notes it is unique among covenant signs — identified by what the covenant community stops doing one day in seven, not by a physical mark or location.

31:17

Sarna notes that 'vayinnafash' (refreshed/caught breath) is a remarkably anthropomorphic statement about divine rest. It implies the Sabbath is genuinely restorative for God, not merely a pedagogical model — making the Sabbath a divine experience before it is a human obligation.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
31:12–17

The Sabbath instruction at the end of chs.25–31 is placed strategically: the covenant sign is restated at the moment the covenant is about to be catastrophically violated. The community that should be observing the Sabbath (Israel at the mountain's base) is instead making an idol. The repetition of the sign sets up the golden calf's irony.

31:14-17

The Sabbath instruction at the end of the building commission is the central paradox of the tabernacle narrative: even the construction of God's dwelling must stop on the Sabbath. Alter notes the structural placement is significant: the Sabbath command brackets the tabernacle instructions (also mentioned in 20:8-11), ensuring that even the most sacred work is governed by the cosmic rhythm God established at creation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
31:13

Calvin: 'As the covenant of God required an outward ceremony by which it might be attested, God chose the Sabbath to separate the Israelites from other nations.' The day of rest is simultaneously worship and covenant identity — the sign that marks the covenant community as distinct.

31:14-16

Calvin: Even the building of the sanctuary was to observe the Sabbath — showing that no work, however sacred, supersedes the covenant ordinances. The Sabbath is not suspended for religious activity; it governs religious activity. The craftsmen who build God's house must also rest on God's day.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
31:18

The NET note on 'two tablets of the Testimony' discusses whether they were two copies (one for each covenant party, as in suzerainty treaties) or two tables covering the two aspects of the Decalogue. Both interpretations have support; the two-party-copy model fits ancient Near Eastern treaty practice best.

31:14-18

The NET note on the two tablets 'written with the finger of God' (ketuvim betsia Elohim) discusses the theological claim: the Decalogue's physical inscription by divine action distinguishes it from all other Mosaic legislation. The note traces the 'finger of God' language through Exod 8:19 (the magicians' recognition) and Deut 9:10.

Ancient Sources

Craftsman Guilds
Ancient Near Eastern craftsmen who worked on temples and royal monuments were organised into professional guilds. The Tabernacle craftsmen are different: their skill is explicitly attributed to divine gifting, not professional training.
The theological point is not that skill doesn't matter but that it comes from God.
Sabbath in the ANE
The Babylonian šapattu was a monthly unlucky day; the Israelite Sabbath is a weekly day of rest with no unlucky connotation — it is positive, holy, and restorative. The distinction is total.
The Israelite Sabbath is without parallel in the ancient world.

Scholarly Debates

Sabbath: Universal Creation Ordinance or Specific Israelite Covenant Sign?
Universal creation ordinance
Calvin, Reformed tradition
The Sabbath is grounded in creation (Gen 2:2–3) and is therefore binding on all humanity. Sinai formalises what creation already established. Sunday worship maintains the creation-rest principle with the day changed to celebrate resurrection.
Specific Israelite covenant sign
Dispensationalists
The 'sign between me and the Israelites forever' (v.17) makes it specifically Israelite. The new covenant does not require Sabbath observance; Col 2:16 and Rom 14:5 support Christian freedom regarding days.
Both: creation rest particularised in the Sinai covenant
Westminster/Reformed consensus
The creation Sabbath is universally binding; Sinai specifies the day and the penalty. The new covenant fulfils the Sabbath in Christ (Heb 4) while maintaining the one-in-seven principle.
The 'sign between me and the Israelites forever' supports the covenant-specific reading; 'in six days the LORD made the heavens and the earth' supports the universal reading. The majority Christian tradition holds both — Sabbath-rest is a creation principle whose specific observance is shaped by covenant context.
