The Song of Moses and Miriam; Marah — Bitter Water Made Sweet

Verses 1—21 — The Song of Moses and Miriam: The Lord Is My Strength
123456789101112131415161718192021

Hebrew Word Study

שִׁירָה shîrāh — song / poem: V.1: "Then Moses and the Israelites sang this song (shîr) to the LORD." The Song of the Sea (Exod 15:1-21) is one of the oldest surviving Hebrew poems and the first recorded corporate act of worship in Scripture. The verb shîr (to sing) here marks a transformation: slaves become a singing people. Worship as the immediate response to salvation is established at the sea — and replicated in Rev 15:3 where the redeemed sing "the song of Moses and the song of the Lamb."

עָזִּי וְזִמְרָת ʿāzzî wĕzimrāt — my strength and my song: V.2: "The LORD is my strength and my defence (zimrāt)." The word zimrāt is rare and disputed — possibly "song," possibly "power" or "protection." If "song," the LORD himself is the song of his people. This verse is quoted three times in the OT (Ps 118:14; Isa 12:2) and becomes a template for thanksgiving. Isaiah 12:2 makes it the song of the eschatological gathering: the day when "with joy you will draw water from the wells of salvation."

Historical Context

The Song of the Sea is widely regarded by scholars as among the oldest texts in the Hebrew Bible, based on archaic grammatical forms, poetic structure, and the presence of Ugaritic cognates. Its antiquity suggests it was composed close to the event. Ancient Near Eastern victory hymns (e.g., Egyptian Hymn to the Nile, Ugaritic Baal Cycle) celebrate divine warriors — the Song of the Sea appropriates and transforms the genre to celebrate a God who needs no weapons.

Context

The Song is not a summary of ch.14 in poetic form — it is a theological interpretation of it. The crossing is cast as cosmic warfare: God as divine warrior, the sea as the instrument of judgment, Egypt's army as the enemy hurled down. The poem moves from past rescue (vv.1–12) to future conquest (vv.13–17) to eternal reign (v.18) — Israel's salvation at the sea is the first act of the story that culminates in God's dwelling among his people.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 77:19–20The Psalmist meditates on the Red Sea crossing: "Your way was through the sea, your path through the great waters."

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:1–2

The Song of the Sea (Shirat haYam) is widely regarded as one of the oldest texts in the Hebrew Bible, possibly preserving archaic linguistic features from the pre-monarchic period. Sarna notes its antiquity is supported by archaic verbal forms (qal imperfect used as past tense), unusual vocabulary, and poetic structures paralleled in Ugaritic epic poetry from the same period. The song may be approximately contemporaneous with the events it celebrates.

15:11

The incomparability formula — 'who is like you among the gods?' (mi khamokha ba'elim YHWH) — is a standard form in ancient Near Eastern hymnody. Every national deity was proclaimed incomparable. What distinguishes Israel's use is the context: YHWH has just demonstrated his incomparability through ten plagues and the sea crossing. The incomparability claim is not assertion but narrative conclusion drawn from observed events.

15:20–21

Miriam is designated a 'prophetess' (neviah) — one of the earliest uses of this title for a woman in the OT. Sarna notes that her role in leading the women's response (tambourines, dancing, antiphonal song) is a distinct liturgical function, not merely imitation of Moses. The women's worship is communal, embodied, and musical — a form of corporate praise that runs from Miriam through the Psalms.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:1–18

The Song of the Sea is a poetic masterpiece and a theological treatise simultaneously. Alter identifies its structure: the immediate past (the sea crossing, vv.1–12), the approaching future (the conquest, vv.13–17), and the eternal present (YHWH reigns, v.18). The poem moves from historical event to eschatological anticipation to doxological conclusion. It is not merely celebration of the past but a programme for the future.

15:20–21

Miriam's song is the refrain that began Moses' song (v.1b = 21b). Alter notes the call-and-response structure: Moses and the men sing the full song; Miriam and the women repeat the opening verse as refrain. The structure is antiphonal — the communal worship divides into responsive groups. This is the first recorded instance of antiphonal singing in Israelite worship, establishing a pattern that will structure the Psalms and temple liturgy.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:2

Calvin on 'he has become my salvation': 'This is a remarkable passage, which teaches us that the mercy which God shows us in adversity is such as should engross all our thoughts, and constrain us to celebrate his praises.' The Exodus produces a praise that is existential, not merely liturgical. The song is not a religious obligation performed after the crisis; it is the natural overflow of a people who have seen their own salvation.

15:11

Calvin on divine incomparability: 'If we consider how great is the distance between God and the angels, and how far above all creatures he is exalted, the language used here will not seem too strong.' The rhetorical question drives toward adoration: there is no comparable being. Calvin uses the verse to establish divine aseity and incomparability as the foundation of all worship.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:3

The NET note on 'the LORD is a warrior' (YHWH ish milchamah) discusses the divine warrior motif in the ancient Near East. Every major deity had a warrior aspect; YHWH's warrior title is established by the sea crossing. The note traces this motif through the Psalms (Ps 24:8) and the Prophets (Isa 42:13) and into the NT (Rev 19:11–16, where Christ appears as divine warrior). The title is not metaphorical — it describes the God who actually destroys armies.

15:20

The NET note on Miriam's tambourine (tof) explains it was a small frame drum, typically played by women in celebratory contexts (1 Sam 18:6; Ps 68:25). The note also discusses Miriam's designation as 'prophetess' — the same title given to Deborah (Judg 4:4) and Huldah (2 Kgs 22:14). The prophetic title may indicate her role as a worship leader who speaks under divine inspiration, not merely as Moses' sister.

Northwestern Sinai
After the sea crossing, Israel enters the Wilderness of Shur — three days into the desert with no water. Marah ("bitter") is the first test after the great salvation. The geography moves immediately from the miraculous to the ordinary: Red Sea to bitter water in three days.
Marah and Elim
Sinai Peninsula
Marah (bitter water sweetened) and Elim (twelve springs, seventy palms) are the desert's two faces: bitter and sweet. Elim's abundance follows directly on Marah's bitterness. The pattern is the wilderness journey in miniature: hardship followed by superabundant provision.
Verses 22—27 — Bitter Water Made Sweet; Marah and Elim
222324252627

Hebrew Word Study

מָרָה mārah — bitter: V.23: The waters of Marah (mārāh — bitter) are the first test after the triumph at the sea. The journey from the Red Sea to Marah takes three days — three days without water. The bitterness of Marah is not a divine cruelty but a diagnostic: can Israel trust after the sea? God's provision (a thrown piece of wood makes the water sweet) introduces the testing principle: "If you listen carefully to the LORD your God... I will not bring on you any of the diseases I brought on Egypt, for I am the LORD who heals you (YHWH Ropheka)." This is the first of the compound divine names.

רָפָא rāpāʾ — healed / doctor: V.26: "I am the LORD who heals you" — YHWH Ropheka, the first compound divine name in Exodus. The verb rāpāʾ (to heal, mend) will reappear in the Servant Song of Isa 53:5 ("by his wounds we are healed") and become the primary medical metaphor in the NT. The healing at Marah introduces a God who is not only powerful over nations but personally attentive to the physical and spiritual health of his people.

Historical Context

None

Context

The Marah test (v.25: "there he tested them") comes three days after the greatest miracle Israel has witnessed. The speed of the transition from singing to complaining is not incidental — it is the nature of faith under wilderness conditions. God tests at Marah not to destroy but to form: "if you will diligently listen." The healing of the water is the first sign that the God who judged Egypt heals Israel — the same power applied differently.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 2:19–22Elisha heals bitter water by throwing salt into it — the Marah pattern repeated in the prophetic period.
  • John 4:14The water Jesus gives becomes "a spring of water welling up to eternal life" — the Marah pattern fulfilled: the bitter water of human thirst becomes living water.
  • Rev 22:2The leaves of the tree of life are for the healing of nations — the log thrown into Marah prefigures the cross-shaped wood that heals the creation.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:22

The three-day journey without water is the first of the wilderness provision stories. Sarna notes the pattern: need → complaint → divine provision → statutes/test. This pattern recurs in chs.16–17 (manna, water from rock) and throughout the wilderness narratives in Numbers. The wilderness is structurally a series of divine provisioning episodes designed to teach Israel dependence on YHWH.

15:26

The self-designation YHWH rophekha ('the LORD your healer') is the fourth divine name of provision in the Pentateuch (after El Roi, El Shaddai, and YHWH Yireh). Sarna notes it appears only here in this exact form, though the concept of divine healing pervades the Psalms and Prophets. The name connects physical provision to covenant relationship — healing is a covenant benefit, not a general divine attribute available to all.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:22–25

The Marah episode is narrated with deliberate compression after the extended poetry of the Song. Three days, no water, bitter water, complaint, divine provision — five elements in four verses. Alter notes the compression mirrors the brevity of the trial: God provides immediately. The complaint is real; the provision is swift. The wilderness narrative will later develop longer complaint-provision cycles; here the sequence is sketched in miniature.

15:25-27

The Marah healing episode establishes the pattern of provision-through-testing that will structure the entire wilderness narrative. Alter notes the brevity is instructive: no extended complaint, no elaborate divine response. The sweetening of the water is accomplished in three words. The narrative economy signals that this is the first in a series of provision episodes, not the primary one.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:24

On the people's grumbling: 'Although this ingratitude is inexcusable, especially so soon after so remarkable an instance of divine grace, yet we are all more or less guilty of the same fault.' Calvin refuses to make Israel exceptional sinners — their complaint is the natural human response to hardship following deliverance. The lesson is universal: the memory of past grace is the antidote to present grumbling, but memory is fragile.

15:26

Calvin on 'I am the LORD who heals you': 'He promises to be their physician, and to defend them against all diseases, on the condition that they submit themselves to his authority.' The covenant structure of the promise is essential for Calvin: healing is not unconditional; it is the covenant benefit of obedient relationship. The wilderness will test whether Israel will fulfil the condition — and Numbers records the recurring failure.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:25

The NET note on 'he cried to the LORD' and the tree discusses whether the sweetening was miraculous or natural (some plants have astringent properties that can affect water taste). The note cites the divine specification ('the LORD showed him a tree') as indicating that the act was divinely directed regardless of any natural properties the tree may have had. The miracle is in the appointment and direction, not necessarily in the tree's properties.

15:27

The NET note on Elim (twelve springs, seventy palm trees) notes the symbolic resonance: twelve springs for the twelve tribes of Israel; seventy palms possibly recalling the seventy who descended to Egypt (Gen 46). The stopping place is an oasis that provides abundant refreshment — a deliberate contrast with the bitter water of Marah and a sign that YHWH's provision encompasses both the trial and the rest.

Ancient Sources

Ugaritic Victory Hymns
The genre of divine warrior victory hymns is well attested in Ugaritic literature (Baal Cycle). The Song of the Sea appropriates the form to celebrate YHWH's superiority over all divine warriors — including Baal.
The Song of the Sea is a polemical text as well as a celebration — it declares the LORD supreme over all other claimants.
Archaic Hebrew
Linguistic analysis of the Song of the Sea identifies archaic forms (the use of yiqtol for past events, the prefix conjugation as historic present) consistent with early Israelite poetry, supporting antiquity.
The Song's language is among the strongest evidence for its early composition.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

The Song of the Sea: Date and Historical Authenticity
Archaic, near-contemporary composition
Cross and Freedman (1955), Kitchen, Sarna
The archaic linguistic features date the song to approximately the 13th–12th century BC, consistent with Mosaic authorship or composition within a generation of the Exodus. The song is one of the earliest extended Hebrew texts, preserving features that were archaic by the time of standard Biblical Hebrew prose.
Later composition attributed to Moses
Most critical scholars (von Rad, Noth)
The song is a later literary composition (10th–8th century BC) celebrating the Exodus traditions. The archaic features are literary archaisms — deliberate stylistic choices imitating early poetry rather than evidence of actual antiquity. The song was attributed to Moses as part of the literary convention of placing speeches and songs in the mouths of central characters.
The debate hinges on the status of the archaic linguistic features. Cross and Freedman's analysis of those features as genuine archaisms (not literary imitations) has been widely influential. The song's early dating does not require Mosaic authorship but is consistent with it. The near-contemporary composition hypothesis is linguistically defensible and increasingly favoured by archaeological parallels.
