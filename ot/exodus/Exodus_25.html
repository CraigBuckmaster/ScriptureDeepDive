Exodus 25

The Offering; the Ark; the Table; the Lampstand

Verses 1–9 — The Offering; the Command to Build
123456789

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
25:8

MacArthur: Have them make a sanctuary for me, so that I may dwell among them. The purpose of the tabernacle is expressed simply: divine dwelling in the midst of the covenant community. The tabernacle is the portable Sinai.

25:9

MacArthur: Make this tabernacle exactly like the pattern I will show you. The tabernacle is not a human artistic achievement but a replica of the heavenly sanctuary (Heb 8:5).

Hebrew Word Study

מִשְׁכָּן mishkān — tabernacle / dwelling: V.9: "Make this tabernacle (mishkān) and all its furnishings exactly like the pattern (tabnît) I will show you." The mishkān is from shākan (to dwell) — a portable dwelling for the divine Presence. Hebrews 8:5 identifies it as a "copy and shadow of what is in heaven." John 1:14 uses the same root: the Word "tabernacled (eskēnōsen) among us" — the divine dwelling became flesh. The Tabernacle is not merely a worship structure but a Christological preview.

תְּרוּמָה terûmāh — offering / contribution: V.2: "Tell the Israelites to bring me an offering (terûmāh)." The first instruction for building the Tabernacle is not a tax but an invitation: "from everyone whose heart prompts them to give." The freewill offering principle establishes that the house of God is built by willing generosity, not coerced tribute. Paul echoes this in 2 Cor 9:7: "Each of you should give what you have decided in your heart to give, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver."

Historical Context

Ancient Near Eastern temples were understood as dwelling places for deities — the god's house on earth, where heaven and earth met. Egyptian, Mesopotamian, and Ugaritic temples all reflect this cosmic geography. Israel's Tabernacle is both continuous with and radically different from this tradition: the God of Israel genuinely dwells among his people, but he cannot be confined, imaged, or manipulated.

Context

The sequence of Exodus is theologically precise: redemption (chs.1–15) → covenant offer (chs.19–24) → Tabernacle instructions (chs.25–31). God rescues Israel, ratifies the covenant, then immediately specifies how he will live with them. The Tabernacle is the answer to the Sinai holiness problem: how can the Holy God dwell with sinful people? Through a precisely ordered system of approach, sacrifice, and priestly mediation.

Cross-Reference

  • John 1:14"The Word became flesh and dwelt (tabernacled) among us." The Incarnation is the fulfilment of the Tabernacle's promise.
  • Rev 21:3"God's dwelling place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them." The eschatological Tabernacle fulfils Exod 25:8.
  • Heb 8:5The earthly Tabernacle is "a copy and shadow of what is in heaven" — the heavenly pattern of v.9.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:8

The tabernacle as divine dwelling (mishkan from shakan, 'to dwell/reside') represents a radical theological claim: the God who cannot be contained in the heavens (1 Kgs 8:27) chooses to localise his presence among his people in a portable tent. Sarna notes the tension: divine transcendence (God fills all) and divine immanence (God dwells specifically) are both maintained. The tabernacle is not God's house in the sense of confining him; it is the appointed place of his accessible presence.

25:2–7

The materials list — gold, silver, bronze, yarn, linen, goat hair, ram skins, acacia wood, oil, spices, gemstones — is an inventory of the finest materials available in the ancient Near East. Sarna notes that the spoiling of Egypt (12:35–36) provided exactly these materials: the wealth Israel received as they left Egypt funds the construction of God's dwelling. Egypt's tribute involuntarily builds YHWH's sanctuary.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:8–9

The double command — 'make me a sanctuary that I may dwell among them' and 'make it exactly according to the pattern I show you' — establishes the tabernacle as simultaneously a practical object and a theological copy. Alter notes the tension: the tabernacle is real, physical, constructable by human craftsmen; and it replicates a heavenly pattern beyond human invention. The earthly is the vehicle of the heavenly without being reduced to it.

25:3-9

The list of materials for the tabernacle — gold, silver, bronze, blue and purple and scarlet yarn, fine linen, goat hair, ram skins, sea cow hides, acacia wood, olive oil, spices, onyx — is a catalogue of the finest available in the ancient Near East. Alter notes the comprehensiveness signals that the best of everything goes to the divine dwelling.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:8

Calvin on the tabernacle: 'God did not choose to dwell in a tabernacle as if he were shut up within its narrow limits, but that he might exercise the Israelites, by the external ceremony, in the desire for his presence.' The tabernacle is accommodation — God uses a physical structure to cultivate Israel's theological orientation toward his presence. The point is not the building but the habit of seeking God that the building trains.

25:9

Calvin on the heavenly pattern: 'The pattern which God showed to Moses was not cut in stone, or engraved in any material, but was spiritual and celestial.' Moses saw a vision, not a physical blueprint. The earthly construction is an approximation of the spiritual reality shown to Moses on the mountain. Hebrews 8–9 will develop this: the tabernacle is a 'shadow' of the heavenly sanctuary, whose reality is Christ's priestly ministry.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:9

The NET note on 'pattern' (tabnit) discusses whether this was a vision, a model, or a set of architectural instructions. The note cites Heb 8:5 ('who serve a copy and shadow of the heavenly things, just as Moses was warned when he was about to complete the tabernacle') as the NT's interpretation of the 'pattern.' The heavenly-earthly correspondence is both architecturally specific and theologically significant: the tabernacle's design is determined by what it represents, not merely by what is practically convenient.

25:8-9

The NET note on the 'pattern' (tabnit) shown to Moses discusses whether this was a vision, a material model, or a written plan. The note surveys the options and notes that 'all their forms' (kekol asher ani mareh otkha) implies a comprehensive visual communication. The tabernacle was not designed by human creativity but by divine specification.

Verses 10–22 — The Ark and the Mercy Seat

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
25:16

MacArthur: Then put in the ark the tablets of the covenant law. The Ark's primary function is to contain the law. The mercy seat above it is the atonement point where the law's demands and God's grace meet.

25:22

MacArthur: There I will meet with you and give you all my commands for the Israelites. The mercy seat is not merely a lid but a throne -- the place from which God governs Israel.

Hebrew Word Study

כַּפֹּרֶת kappōret — mercy seat / atonement cover: V.17-22: The kappōret — the gold cover of the Ark — is the place where God meets Moses "from between the two cherubim." The word comes from kāpar (to atone/cover). The LXX translates it hilastērion — the same word Paul uses in Rom 3:25 for Christ: "God presented Christ as a sacrifice of atonement (hilastērion)." The mercy seat is the precise OT type fulfilled in the cross: the place where blood was sprinkled and where God and humanity met.

מְנוֹרָה mĕnôrāh — lampstand / seven-branched lamp: V.31-40: The seven-branched golden menorah, hammered from one piece of pure gold, gave light before God continually. In Revelation 1-2, the seven lampstands are the seven churches — the lampstand as the image of the witnessing community. Jesus declares himself the "light of the world" (John 8:12) — the fulfillment of the Tabernacle's perpetual light. Zechariah 4's vision of the menorah flanked by two olive trees (interpreted as Spirit-supply) becomes the background of Rev 11:4.

Historical Context

None

Context

The ark is the most sacred object in Israel's worship — it is the footstool of God's invisible throne (1 Chr 28:2). The mercy seat (kappōret) is the specific site of atonement on Yom Kippur: the high priest sprinkles blood on it to atone for the nation's sins. God's meeting-place with Moses (v.22: "I will meet with you") is not the burning bush or Sinai but here — above the blood-covered law. This is the logic of the entire sacrificial system in one piece of furniture.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 3:25Christ presented as a "sacrifice of atonement" (hilastērion — the same word as LXX kappōret). Jesus is the true mercy seat.
  • Heb 9:5The cherubim of glory overshadowing the mercy seat — the author of Hebrews explicitly references the Tabernacle furniture.
  • 1 Sam 4:4The ark called "the ark of the covenant of the Lord Almighty, who is enthroned between the cherubim."

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:17

The kapporet (mercy seat/atonement cover) derives from the root kapar (to cover, atone). Sarna notes the term is related to Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) — the annual covering of sin. The mercy seat is literally the 'atonement place' — where the blood of the sin offering is applied and the divine-human relationship is reconciled. Its position over the law tablets encodes the theological sequence: atonement covers the law's judgment.

25:18–20

The cherubim on the mercy seat are a distinctive feature that has puzzled interpreters. Sarna notes that cherubim (keruvim) in the ancient Near East were composite divine beings — often lion-bodied with eagle wings and human faces — who guarded sacred spaces. They appear as guardians of Eden (Gen 3:24), throne-bearers of God (Ps 18:10; Ezek 1:5–24), and curtain decorations in the tabernacle (26:1). Their overshadowing wings create the divine throne-space above the mercy seat.

25:10-22

The Ark dimensions (2.5 x 1.5 x 1.5 cubits) are unique in the ancient Near East — no direct parallel has been found. Sarna notes the specificity is itself theologically significant: God does not merely ordain a type of object but specifies exact dimensions. The covenant God is not satisfied with approximations.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:10–22

The Ark description moves from dimensions (vv.10–15) to function (v.16: tablets inside) to the mercy seat (vv.17–22) in an order of ascending holiness. Alter notes the precision of the dimensions — 2.5 × 1.5 × 1.5 cubits — and the symbolic quality of every measurement. The Ark is both a physical object and a theological statement: the covenant law housed in the atonement throne, accessible only through mediation and blood.

25:17-22

The mercy seat (kapporet) is the tabernacle's theological centre — the gold cover from which God speaks, the locus where atonement is made. Alter notes the two cherubim facing each other with wings spread above form a throne for the invisible God. The mercy seat is the OT's most concrete image of the divine dwelling-place in creation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:22

Calvin on meeting with God at the mercy seat: 'Under the law, God was propitiated by the blood of beasts, since he looked to Christ, in whom the full truth of the figure was finally exhibited.' The mercy seat's function is Christological for Calvin: the annual blood-sprinkling on the kapporet was a forward-pointing enactment of what Christ would accomplish in his own blood. The OT priest entered the Holy of Holies; Christ entered heaven itself (Heb 9:24).

25:10-22

Calvin on the Ark of the Covenant: the ark is not a material depository of divinity but the pledge of God's presence — the mercy seat is where he condescends to hear the prayers of the people. The entire construction is covenant pedagogy: God teaching through visible forms what cannot yet be grasped spiritually.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:17

The NET note on kapporet discusses the translation 'atonement cover' (NET) vs. 'mercy seat' (KJV) vs. 'propitiatory' (some Catholic versions). The note explains that LXX renders it hilastērion (propitiation/mercy seat), which Paul uses in Rom 3:25 for Christ: 'whom God put forward as a propitiation.' 25:17-21

Calvin on the cherubim: God commanded his face to be represented between the cherubim to signify that he would be propitious to his people. The cherubim are not worshipped as divine but are the attendants of the divine presence -- the living creatures who surround the throne.

25:20-22

The NET note on the cherubim with wings spread upward traces the ancient Near Eastern throne-guardian parallels: the cherubim serve as the throne-base for the invisible God who sits enthroned between them. The mercy seat is the throne footstool on which the invisible King rests.

Verses 23–40 — The Table and the Lampstand

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
25:31-32

MacArthur: The lampstand is made of pure gold, hammered from a single piece -- base, shaft, and branches all one. The unity of the lampstand prefigures Christ as the one light (John 8:12) and the church as lampstands (Rev 1:20) that receive and reflect his single light.

25:40

MacArthur: See that you make them according to the pattern shown you on the mountain. The worship God accepts must conform to what God has specified, not to what humans find aesthetically pleasing.

Hebrew Word Study

שֻׁלְחַן shulḥān — table / the Table of Presence: V.23-30: The table of pure gold held the "bread of the Presence" (leḥem happānîm — lit. "face bread") permanently before God. Twelve loaves, one for each tribe, renewed weekly. The table symbolized Israel's constant access to the divine table — covenant communion as an ongoing reality, not a one-time event. Jesus's feeding miracles, Last Supper, and the Marriage Supper of the Lamb (Rev 19:9) all draw on this image of perpetual covenant fellowship at table.

אֲרוֹן ʾārôn — ark / chest: V.10-16: The Ark of the Covenant (ʾărôn habbĕrît) — acacia wood overlaid with gold inside and out — was Israel's most sacred object. Its function was threefold: to contain the tablets of the Law, to be the footstool/throne of the divine Presence (1 Sam 4:4; Ps 80:1), and to be the meeting point between God and Israel (v.22). Paul's "grace throne" (Heb 4:16 — "throne of grace") reflects the Ark's function as mercy seat above the law.

Historical Context

None

Context

The three furnishings of the Holy Place — ark (atonement), table (bread/communion), and lampstand (light) — form a complete theology: atonement enables communion; communion is sustained by light/revelation. The lampstand lit the table; the table held the bread; the bread signified Israel's covenant relationship with the God enthroned above the ark. Each piece interprets the others.

Cross-Reference

  • John 6:35"I am the bread of life" — Jesus as the true bread of the Presence.
  • John 8:12"I am the light of the world" — Jesus as the true lampstand.
  • Rev 1:12–13The seven golden lampstands are the seven churches; Christ walks among them — the true light of the Presence.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:23–30

The table of the Presence (shulchan) is placed in the Holy Place opposite the lampstand. Sarna notes the table is one of the three furnishings of the Holy Place (table, lampstand, incense altar) and corresponds to the three furnishings of the Holy of Holies (Ark). The Holy Place is the priests' regular service space; the Holy of Holies is the high priest's annual access point. The graduated sanctity of the spaces corresponds to graduated human access.

25:31–40

The menorah (lampstand) is the most artistically described object in the tabernacle — flowers, buds, blossoms, cups — all beaten from a single piece of pure gold. Sarna notes the floral imagery: the lampstand is an almond tree in bloom (cf. Num 17:23, where Aaron's staff buds with almonds). The lamp that lights the sanctuary is a golden flowering tree — creation's light-producing imagery translated into sacred art.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
25:31–40

The lampstand description is the chapter's most elaborate — nine verses for an object described with botanical precision: cups shaped like almond flowers with buds and petals, branches with the same features, the central shaft identical to the branches. Alter notes the repetitive precision serves both practical (exact construction specifications) and literary purposes — the elaborate description enacts the object's beauty. The lampstand is the tabernacle's poem in metal.

25:37-40

The seven lamps of the lampstand are to give light 'in front of it' — illuminating the Table of the Presence across the Holy Place. Alter notes the lampstand's function is practical (light) and symbolic (perpetual fire in the presence of God) simultaneously. The technical description and the theological function are inseparable.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
25:30

Calvin on the bread of the Presence: 'This bread was placed before God, as it were, in his sight and presence, to testify that the people were sustained and nourished by his grace and fatherly care.' The twelve loaves are the twelve tribes' acknowledgment that their sustenance comes from God — a weekly eucharistic act before the covenant sanctuary. The bread before God is the covenant people's acknowledgment of dependence.

25:37

Calvin on the lampstand: 'The lamp was lit in God's tabernacle, to symbolise the spiritual light which God grants his Church.' The light of the menorah is the light of God's word and presence — not natural light but the specific illumination of the divine presence. Calvin's typological reading: Christ is the light of the world (John 8:12), the fulfilment of the menorah's function. The tabernacle's lamp is replaced by the incarnate Word.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
25:31

The NET note on the menorah (menorat zahav) explains it is made from a single hammered piece (mikshat) of pure gold — an extraordinary feat of metalwork. The note discusses the menorah's post-biblical significance: it became one of Judaism's most enduring symbols, appearing in the Arch of Titus relief (depicting the spoils taken from the Jerusalem temple in 70 AD) and in the modern State of Israel's national emblem.

The NET note on the seven lamps of the menorah discusses the symbolic significance of seven — the number of completeness — and the requirement that the lamps illuminate 'the space in front of it.' The note also discusses the physical gold-from-a-single-piece construction requirement as emphasising the lampstand's unity.

Ancient Sources

ANE Temple Parallels
Mesopotamian and Egyptian temples were conceived as divine dwelling places where the god's statue resided. Building instructions from the gods are attested in texts like the Gudea Cylinders (c.2100 BC), where the king receives temple plans in a dream.
The Tabernacle fits within the ANE temple-building tradition while radically transforming it: God cannot be imaged; his presence is real but not localised in a statue.
Ark Parallels
Egyptian sacred chests (carrying shrines for statues) were overlaid with gold and carried on poles. The ark uses the same form but contains the law, not an image — the covenant document, not a representation of God.
Form is borrowed; theology is transformed.

Reception History

Hebrews 8–10
The sustained NT typological reading of the Tabernacle: every element points to Christ. The mercy seat is his atonement; the bread of Presence is his body; the lampstand is his light. The earthly copy gives way to the heavenly reality.
John's Gospel
The Prologue's "tabernacled among us" (1:14) sets the entire Gospel as the new Tabernacle narrative — Jesus is where God dwells, where the glory is seen, where the bread and light are found.

Literary Structure

The Tabernacle: God's Desire to Dwell25:8Make a sanctuary for me that I may dwell among them. The goal is not the building but the dwelling -- the portable divine presence in the midst of the covenant community. Every specification serves this single theological purpose.
The Mercy Seat: Where God Speaks25:22From above the mercy seat, between the two cherubim, I will meet with you and speak with you. The tabernacle's theological centre is not the Ark but the mercy seat -- the place of meeting, atonement, and divine speech.

Scholarly Debates

Does the Tabernacle Describe a Historical Structure or a Literary-Theological Construct?
Historical structure: real tent of meeting
Kitchen, evangelical scholars
The tabernacle materials, dimensions, and construction techniques are consistent with late Bronze Age Egyptian and Near Eastern portable sanctuaries. Kitchen documents parallels with Egyptian tent-shrine structures from the period. The tabernacle was a real physical structure built in the wilderness, consistent with its described materials and size.
Ideal/literary construct: theological statement about God's presence
Some critical scholars (Noth, Cross)
The tabernacle descriptions are too elaborate and sophisticated for a wilderness context. They reflect the ideology of the Jerusalem temple projected backward onto the wilderness period. The tabernacle narrative is a theological idealisation, not a historical record.
Kitchen's documentary evidence for Bronze Age parallels significantly strengthens the historical case. The critical position relies on an assumed incompatibility between wilderness conditions and the described craftsmanship that archaeological evidence has challenged. The acacia wood (available in Sinai), the portable design, and the modest scale (compared to Solomon's temple) are all consistent with a genuine wilderness construction.
