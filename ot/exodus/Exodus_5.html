Exodus 5

Moses Before Pharaoh; Bricks Without Straw; Israel's Complaint

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–9 — Moses and Aaron Before Pharaoh; Bricks Without Straw

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
5:1-2

MacArthur: Who is the LORD, that I should obey him? Pharaoh's response is the theological premise of the plagues. The ten plagues answer this question systematically: each plague reveals a dimension of who YHWH is.

5:9

MacArthur: Pharaoh calls Moses's message lies. The escalated oppression tests whether the Israelites will hold to the promise of deliverance. Genuine faith is tested by whether it survives when circumstances worsen before they improve.

Hebrew Word Study

שַׁלַּח shalaḥ — let go / send forth: V.1: "Let my people go" (shalaḥ ʿammî) — the Exodus refrain in concentrated form. The hiphil imperative of shālaḥ (to send away, release) is used 10× in the plague narrative. Each refusal by Pharaoh intensifies the paradox: Israel's God claims sovereignty over Egypt's most prized resource. The verb itself becomes the heartbeat of the liberation story.

תֶּבֶן teben — straw: V.7: Pharaoh's withholding of straw — forcing the Israelites to gather their own while maintaining the same quota — is a bureaucratic cruelty designed to demoralize. Teben (straw/chaff) is the material that binds mud bricks. Its absence breaks production. The episode establishes that liberation will not be achieved through negotiation but through divine power that overpowers Pharaoh's system.

Historical Context

Egyptian administrative papyri document the brick-quota system in detail — the Anastasi Papyrus records daily brick quotas for workers. Straw was necessary to bind the clay; withholding straw while maintaining quotas is a documented form of administrative punishment in Egyptian labour management. The specific detail of Pithom and Rameses (1:11) and the brick-making process are archaeologically grounded.

Context

Pharaoh's response — "Who is the LORD?" — is not ignorance but contempt. He knows there is a god called YHWH; he is dismissing his authority. His counter-move is brilliant: increase the work, create resentment between Israelites and their own foremen, make Moses's message look like the cause of their suffering. The strategy almost works — the foremen blame Moses (v.21), and Moses blames God (v.22). The chapter is a study in how God's purposes can appear to make things worse before they get better.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:1

The formula 'Thus says the LORD' (ko amar YHWH) is the standard prophetic messenger formula, used throughout the prophetic books. Its use here marks Moses as functioning in the prophetic role — he is the divine messenger delivering the divine king's decree to a vassal. Sarna notes that this formula implies a claim of divine royal authority directly competing with Pharaoh's claim. It is not a polite request; it is a royal command.

5:3

The specific request — a three-day journey to sacrifice — is sometimes read as a diplomatic minimalism (asking for less than the full Exodus to begin negotiations). Sarna notes it more likely reflects the actual religious practice: periodic pilgrimages to desert shrines were common in the ancient Near East. The request is not deceptive but preliminary — a first step in the process God has announced will require full divine power to complete.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:1–2

The first confrontation between Moses and Pharaoh is a dialogue of theological assertion. Moses speaks in the prophetic messenger formula; Pharaoh responds with a counter-assertion of ignorance — 'I do not know the LORD.' Alter notes the exchange sets up the entire plague narrative as an extended lesson: ten demonstrations of divine power will be required to educate the man who begins with 'Who is the LORD?'

5:10–14

The foremen scene — Egyptian taskmasters beating Israelite foremen for failing to meet the brick quota — is narrated with specific detail. The particularity (the daily quota unchanged, the straw withheld, the foremen beaten) grounds the oppression in observable, verifiable bureaucratic cruelty. This is not rhetorical abstraction; it is a precise description of how systemic oppression operates through layers of complicit management.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:2

Calvin on Pharaoh's 'I know not the LORD': 'His pride and contempt for God are here painted in their true colours. He not only rejects the divine command, but treats it with scorn.' The boast of not knowing God is, for Calvin, the paradigmatic form of human pride. Pharaoh represents every human power that sets itself against divine authority. His ignorance is not innocent; it is aggressive and contemptuous.

5:22–23

Moses' complaint to God — 'You have not rescued your people at all' — is Calvin's proof that even the greatest servants of God struggle with apparent divine inaction. Calvin reads it not as faithlessness but as honest prayer: 'He does not charge God with treachery, but complains of his own condition.' The complaint brings Moses before God rather than driving him away. Honest lament is a form of faith.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:1

The NET note on 'a feast to me in the wilderness' (chag li bamidbar) explains that chag refers to a pilgrimage festival — one of the three annual feasts of later Israelite practice (Passover, Weeks, Tabernacles). The request for a wilderness festival is theologically coherent: Sinai will become the site of the first great Israelite festival (ch.19). The request anticipates what the Exodus will actually produce.

5:16

The NET note on 'your servants are beaten' explains the Hebrew chamim (sinning, at fault) — the foremen are told they are the ones at fault for the quota shortfall. The bureaucratic injustice is precise: the workers are blamed for the conditions imposed on them. This pattern of blaming the oppressed for the consequences of oppression is a recurring feature of systemic cruelty throughout history.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
5:22-23

MacArthur: Moses complains to God: Why have you brought trouble on this people? The lament is the authentic voice of faith under pressure -- not rejection of God but complaint based on his stated promises. God's answer (6:1-8) is the full disclosure of the covenant name and plan.

5:10-23

MacArthur: The removal of straw while maintaining the quota is a classic oppressor's tactic: make the demand impossible, then blame the failure on the workers. Moses's complaint to God (v.22) demonstrates the acceptable form of lament: honest accusation directed to God rather than rebellion against his command.

Hebrew Word Study

רָעָה rāʿā — evil / harm: V.22-23: Moses's complaint to God — "You have not rescued your people at all" — uses the verb hēraʿ (to do evil/harm). Moses accuses God of allowing harm to worsen. This is not irreverence but honest prophetic intercession. God's response in ch.6 is the full covenant self-disclosure of the four-fold promise — the answer is not an explanation but a fuller revelation of who God is.

נֹגְשִׂים nōgesîm — slave drivers / overseers: The oppressive taskmasters are nōgesîm — those who drive or press. The same root describes oppression in Isa 53:7 ("he was oppressed") and in the prophets' condemnations of unjust rulers. Pharaoh's system of graduated oppression — Egyptian overseers → Israelite foremen → workers — mirrors how totalitarian control distributes coercive power to create internal division.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:20–21

The foremen's accusation — 'you have made us a stench to Pharaoh' — uses the Hebrew be'ashtem (you have made us stink). The idiom of being a 'stench' before a superior is a strong diplomatic term for having become politically offensive, having destroyed one's standing. Sarna notes the foremen's complaint is politically accurate: Moses' intervention has made Israel's situation worse in the short term. Their complaint is comprehensible even if it misreads the larger divine plan.

5:22-23

Moses' complaint at the end of ch.5 is the first time in Exodus that the covenant mediator questions God's faithfulness. Sarna notes this is not the last such complaint — the pattern of complaint-and-divine-response becomes a structural feature of the wilderness narrative. God's response in ch.6 will reaffirm the covenant name and renew the commission.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:22–6:1

Moses' complaint and God's response are structurally significant: the chapter ends with Moses' doubt and the next chapter opens with God's renewed commission. Alter notes this pattern — crisis of faith → divine recommitment — is the structure of the entire Exodus narrative. The question 'why have you not rescued your people?' is the question the book of Exodus exists to answer, and it is asked here, near the beginning, by the protagonist himself.

5:22-23

Moses' complaint at the chapter's end sets up ch.6's great divine response: the full disclosure of the covenant name YHWH and its significance. Alter notes the literary function of the complaint: it is the darkness before the dawn of revelation. The deeper the crisis articulated, the more complete the divine self-disclosure that answers it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:22

Calvin on Moses' complaint: 'Although Moses, in the vehemence of his grief, expresses himself somewhat inconsiderately, yet God pardons his infirmity, and gently corrects it.' The grace of God toward the complaining servant is itself instructive. God does not rebuke Moses for the complaint; he answers it. The relationship between God and Moses is robust enough to absorb honest protest. This is the model of the Psalms of lament.

5:22-23

Calvin: Moses complains with more passion than wisdom — 'why have you sent me?' But God does not rebuke the complaint. He answers it. This teaches that God bears with the weakness of his servants even when their faith temporarily fails. He does not withdraw the commission because the commissioned person doubts.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:23

The NET note on 'you have not rescued your people at all' (hatsil lo hitsalta) notes the use of the infinitive absolute for emphasis — the most emphatic possible way of saying 'you have absolutely not rescued.' Moses is not being diplomatic. The emotional force of the complaint is grammatically encoded. God does not rebuke the grammar; he answers the substance.

5:22-23

The NET note on Moses' double complaint — 'you have not rescued your people at all' — traces the lament form throughout the Psalms. The note identifies this as covenant lament: not rejection of God but challenge to God based on his own stated promises. The lament form is itself an act of covenant engagement.

The Exodus labour context is well-grounded in Egyptian administrative practice.

Reception History

Calvin, Commentary on Exodus
The increased oppression after Moses's first confrontation is for Calvin the normal pattern of God's redemptive action: things often appear to get worse before the deliverance. Faith is tested precisely at the point where the promise seems most contradicted by circumstances.
Origen, Homilies
Moses's complaint to God (vv.22–23) is read by Origen as the soul's honest prayer in spiritual darkness — God welcomes the honest complaint far more than performance of contentment.

Scholarly Debates

The 'Three-Day Journey': Diplomatic Minimalism or Genuine Request?
Diplomatic opening gambit
Cassuto, Fretheim
Moses asks for less than God intends in order to begin negotiation. This is a standard ancient diplomatic approach — start with a modest, reasonable request; escalate if refused. The three-day journey is not the full Exodus but a first step deliberately designed to seem achievable.
Genuine religious request
Sarna, Kitchen
Wilderness sacrifices were a genuine practice. The request is real, not tactical. The full extent of the Exodus was not revealed to Pharaoh immediately because the revelation was progressive — each refusal revealed more of God's plan. The initial request was genuine; the subsequent plagues were the escalating divine response to Pharaoh's refusal.
Both readings are theologically defensible. The narrative supports progressive revelation of divine intention — God did not present the full Exodus programme in the first encounter. Whether this constitutes tactical minimalism or genuine incremental request, the theological outcome is identical: Pharaoh's refusal of even the minimal request justifies all that follows.
