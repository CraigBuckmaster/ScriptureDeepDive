Exodus 27

The Bronze Altar; the Courtyard; the Perpetual Lamp

Verses 1–8 — The Bronze Altar
12345678

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
27:1-2

MacArthur: The bronze altar is the first object encountered when entering the tabernacle court. The approach to God begins with sacrifice. No one passes the altar without passing the atonement point. Access to the divine presence requires the prior address of sin.

27:6

MacArthur: The altar's bronze poles and rings enable it to be carried -- the portable altar for a portable sanctuary. The worship of the God who accompanies his people cannot be fixed to a location.

Hebrew Word Study

מִזְבֵּחַ mizbēaḥ — altar: V.1-8: The bronze altar of burnt offering — the largest piece of Tabernacle furniture — stood at the entrance to the courtyard, the first thing encountered by anyone approaching God. Everything else in the Tabernacle existed in the shadow of this altar. The word mizbēaḥ comes from zābaḥ (to slaughter for sacrifice). The altar is where the substitutionary principle operates: life given for life, blood for blood, animal for human. The cross is the ultimate altar.

עֹלָה ʿōlāh — burnt offering / that which goes up: V.1 (context): The ʿōlāh (burnt offering) was the primary regular sacrifice — wholly consumed by fire, the entire animal "going up" to God. The root ʿālāh (to go up, ascend) describes both the smoke ascending and the worshipper's offering rising to God. Heb 10:5-7 applies Ps 40:6-8 to Christ: "burnt offerings and sin offerings you did not desire... then I said, Here I am — I have come to do your will." Christ is the perfect ʿōlāh — the wholly given sacrifice.

Historical Context

None

Context

The bronze altar stood in the outer courtyard — the first furnishing encountered by anyone entering the Tabernacle complex. Its placement is theological: there is no access to God except through the altar. The journey from the gate to the Most Holy Place required passing the altar first. The NT equivalent: "No one comes to the Father except through me" (John 14:6) — the cross is the altar you must pass.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:1

The altar of burnt offering is constructed from acacia wood overlaid with bronze — not gold, like the inner furnishings. Sarna notes the material progression: the closer to the divine presence, the more precious the material (bronze → silver → gold). The outer altar, farthest from the Holy of Holies, is bronze. The Ark, closest to the divine presence, is pure gold. The material gradient encodes spatial holiness.

27:8

The altar is described as hollow, made of boards — a design that allowed it to be filled with earth or stone at each encampment (cf. 20:24: 'make an altar of earth'). Sarna notes this reconciles the bronze-overlaid altar with the earlier earth-altar command: the frame is metal; the fill at each location is local earth. The design is portable (hollow boards) and locally grounded (earth-filled) simultaneously.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:1–8

The altar description shares the precision of the inner-furnishing descriptions but differs in material register — bronze instead of gold, functional utensils instead of artistic lampstand branches. Alter notes the contrast is theological: the altar is where the animal sacrifice happens, where blood is poured, where flesh is burned. Its material is appropriate to its function — durable, fire-resistant, serviceable. The altar is not meant to be beautiful; it is meant to be effective.

27:3-8

The bronze altar's accessories — pots, shovels, basins, forks, fire-pans — are listed in precise detail. Alter notes that the functional tools of slaughter and blood-management are specified with the same care as the most sacred objects. Covenant worship requires both the sublime (the Ark, the mercy seat) and the practical (the pots and shovels).

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
27:1–2

Calvin on the altar of burnt offering: 'The altar was placed in the outer court, before the tabernacle, that the people might understand that they could only approach God through the medium of sacrifice.' The altar's position — first in the approach sequence — is pedagogically essential. Before the worshipper can enter the presence of God, the sacrifice must be made. Calvin reads this as the structural expression of the gospel: no access to God without atonement.

27:3

Calvin on the bronze utensils: 'God would have his worship conducted decently and in order, and therefore prescribed not only the general form of the altar, but also the instruments for its service.' The precision extends to the tools of the sacrifice — the pots, shovels, basins, forks, firepans. Even the implements of slaughter and burning are divinely specified. Worship ordered by God must be orderly in every detail.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
27:2

The NET note on the altar's four horns (qarnot) explains they were projections at the four upper corners of the altar. The note discusses their function: blood was applied to the horns in certain sacrifices (29:12; Lev 4:7). They were also places of asylum — a person fleeing justice could grasp the altar horns (1 Kgs 1:50; 2:28). The horns thus served both liturgical (blood application) and legal (asylum) functions.

27:3-7

The NET note on the bronze grating (mikhbar nechoshet) inside the altar discusses its function: a bronze shelf halfway up the altar sides on which the wood burned, allowing ash to fall below for collection. The note traces the altar's structural engineering — the grating made the fire manageable and the altar cleanable.

Verses 9–21 — The Courtyard; the Perpetual Lamp

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
27:20-21

MacArthur: Keep the lamps burning before the LORD from evening till morning. The continuous light is the covenant community's maintenance of the divine presence's visible sign.

27:9-21

MacArthur: The courtyard is the boundary zone of graduated holiness. The linen hangings create a visible boundary that teaches the covenant community to approach the divine presence intentionally, not casually. The gate on the east side means Israel approaches with the sunrise — covenant worship structures the movement toward God.

Hebrew Word Study

שֶׁמֶן זַיִת shemen zayit — pure olive oil: V.20: "Command the Israelites to bring you clear oil of pressed olives for the light so that the lamps may be kept burning." The menorah burning on pure olive oil was to burn tamîd — continually, perpetually. The verb dāʿak (to go out/be extinguished) is deliberately avoided: the light must never go out. In John 8:12 Jesus declares "I am the light of the world" — the fulfillment of the perpetual Tabernacle light.

תָּמִיד tāmîd — continual / perpetual: V.20: The word tāmîd (continual, perpetual) governs the Tabernacle lamp, the showbread (25:30), the incense (30:8), and the daily burnt offering (29:38). The constancy of worship is not mechanical repetition but embodied acknowledgment that God's Presence is constant. The repetition of covenant acts embeds the reality of covenantal relationship into the rhythm of every day.

Historical Context

None

Context

The courtyard (100 x 50 cubits) enclosed the entire Tabernacle complex and provided a defined sacred space distinct from the common camp. Its linen curtains were five cubits high — tall enough that no one could peer over into the sacred space but not so tall as to be overwhelming. The entrance on the east side (always the sunrise side in the ancient world) was the only way in. The perpetual lamp (vv.20–21) is the lampstand's continuous function: God's house is never dark.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 27:4"To gaze on the beauty of the Lord and to seek him in his temple." The courtyard was the space where Israelites encountered the Tabernacle — the outer precinct of divine dwelling.
  • John 10:9"I am the gate; whoever enters through me will be saved." The single gate of the courtyard → Christ as the single way of access.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:9–19

The courtyard measurements (100 × 50 cubits) give the tabernacle complex specific proportions. Sarna notes that the sanctuary (the tent proper) occupies exactly half the length of the courtyard, situated at the western end — making the altar and laver the forward half of the sacred precinct. The spatial arrangement emphasises the altar's centrality: sacrifice dominates the visible court; the sanctuary is behind and beyond it.

27:20

Pure beaten olive oil (shemen zayit zakh katit) is specified for the lamp — the finest possible quality. Sarna notes that the process of beating olives (rather than pressing them) produced a clearer, less acid oil that burned with a purer flame and less smoke. The quality of the oil is the quality of the offering: only the best serves in the sacred space. The lamp before the LORD requires the most refined material available.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:20–21

The chapter closes with the command for the perpetual lamp — a brief paragraph that anchors the elaborate architectural descriptions in daily practice. Alter notes the contrast: seven chapters of precise construction details, and then the simplest possible instruction: keep oil in the lamp, morning to evening, forever. The grand structure exists to house the ongoing practice. Architecture serves liturgy.

27:20-21

The perpetual lamp command — olive oil of pressed olives, kept burning from evening to morning before the LORD — closes the tabernacle instructions with an image of constant covenant presence. Alter notes the lamp's perpetuity symbolises what the entire structure is designed to achieve: God's continuous, uninterrupted presence among his people.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
27:20

Calvin on the perpetual lamp: 'The lamp burning continually before God typified the light of the divine word, which should never be extinguished in the Church.' The perpetual flame is the covenant's ongoing declaration: the word of God, the presence of God, the worship of God must not lapse. Calvin's NT application: the church maintains the perpetual lamp through the faithful preaching of the gospel and the celebration of the sacraments.

27:21

On Aaron and his sons maintaining the lamp: 'It was the special duty of the priests... that the covenant might be preserved; and thus we are admonished that it is the peculiar task of pastors to cherish the light of heavenly doctrine in the Church.' The priestly responsibility for the lamp is the OT form of the pastor's responsibility for the ongoing proclamation of the word. The lamp kept burning = the word faithfully preached.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
27:20

The NET note on 'olive oil of pressed olives, clear' discusses the production method: cold-pressing the first extraction of ripe olives produced the clearest, highest-quality oil. The note also explains the phrase 'before the LORD' (liphnei YHWH): the lamps burned in the Holy Place, which is 'before' the veil of the Holy of Holies — in the divine presence without being in the most holy space itself.

27:20-21

The NET note on 'a lasting ordinance' (chuqat olam) for the lamp discusses this phrase as a marker for permanent covenant requirements in the Pentateuch. The note traces its occurrences and identifies the perpetual lamp as one of the core ongoing covenant-maintenance obligations — not seasonal but constant.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Moses↗ People
Prophet, lawgiver, and covenant mediator
Moses stands at the centre of the Pentateuch’s narrative — the one man who spoke with God face to face (Exod 33:11; Num 12:8) and mediated the Sinai covenant to Israel. In Leviticus he receives the priestly legislation; in Numbers he leads, intercedes, and ultimately fails at Meribah (Num 20). The writer of Hebrews calls him "faithful in all God’s house" (Heb 3:5) while distinguishing him from Christ, the Son over the house.
Aaron↗ People
First high priest of Israel
Aaron, Moses’s brother, is consecrated as Israel’s first high priest in Lev 8–9. He bears the names of the twelve tribes on his breastplate before God and enters the Most Holy Place once a year on Yom Kippur (Lev 16). His failures — the golden calf (Exod 32), challenging Moses’s authority (Num 12), complicity at Meribah (Num 20) — make him the paradigm of the imperfect priest who prefigures a better high priest (Heb 7:26–28).

Miriam↗ People
Prophetess and worship leader; Moses’s sister
Miriam leads Israel’s women in worship after the Red Sea crossing (Exod 15:20) and is called a prophetess. In Num 12 she challenges Moses’s unique prophetic authority alongside Aaron and is struck with ṣāraʿat — only to be healed after Moses’s seven-word intercession. She dies at Kadesh (Num 20:1), her passing noted in two terse Hebrew words before the water crisis.
Joshua↗ People
Moses’s military commander and designated successor
Joshua son of Nun serves as Moses’s lieutenant throughout the wilderness period — commanding at Rephidim (Exod 17), ascending Sinai with Moses (Exod 24), guarding the tent of meeting. In Num 13–14 he stands with Caleb as one of two faithful spies. He is formally commissioned in Num 27 through Moses’s hand-laying and inherits the leadership role that Moses cannot fulfil past the Jordan.

Ancient Sources

ANE Courtyard Temples
Ancient Near Eastern temples regularly featured an outer courtyard separating the sacred interior from the profane world. Egyptian temples like Karnak and Luxor have successive courtyards of increasing holiness leading to the innermost sanctuary.
The Tabernacle courtyard fits within the known ANE temple typology.
Perpetual Fire
Sacred fires that were never extinguished are attested in ancient religions from Rome (the Vestal flame) to Persia. Israel's perpetual lamp is distinct: it is not a magical fire but a priestly ordinance serving the practical function of illuminating God's house while signifying his continuous presence.
The perpetual light is functionally intelligible within its cultural context while carrying unique covenant significance.

Reception History

John 10:9
The single gate of the courtyard as the type of Christ as the one gate: "I am the gate; whoever enters through me will be saved." Early church commentators universally made this connection.
Ps 27:4
Dwelling in the house of the Lord — the courtyard is as far as the people could come. Yet the Psalmist longs to dwell there. The outer court of the earthly tabernacle was the fullest access available to most of Israel.

Scholarly Debates

The Altar's Horns: Liturgical, Cosmic, or Legal?
Liturgical: sites of blood application
Most scholars
The primary function of the altar horns is the application of sacrificial blood in specific offering types (Lev 4:7, 18, 25, 30, 34; 8:15; 9:9). The horns make the altar a site of atonement in its most concentrated form — blood on the horns is the most direct application of the sacrificial act to the altar itself.
Cosmic: symbols of divine power and protection
Some scholars (Albright)
Horns in the ancient Near East were symbols of divine power — the horn of the bull was the emblem of El and other deities. The altar's four horns may represent the four directions of divine power extending from the central sanctuary. The asylum function (grasping the horns) would then invoke that divine protective power.
Both functions are textually attested: blood application (sacrifice texts) and asylum (1 Kgs 1:50; 2:28). The two functions may be related: the altar is the site of divine presence and power; therefore it is both where atonement is made and where protection is found. The cosmic-power interpretation adds a third dimension without displacing the liturgical one.
