Exodus 33

God Threatens to Withdraw; Moses Refuses Without God's Presence; Moses Asks to See God's Glory

Verses 1–6 — God Threatens to Withdraw His Presence
123456

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
33:3

MacArthur: I will not go with you, because you are a stiff-necked people. The withdrawal of the divine presence is the most severe consequence of the golden calf sin. For Moses, life without God's presence is unacceptable.

33:5

MacArthur: If I were to go with you even for a moment, I might destroy you. The tabernacle's elaborate atonement provisions exist to solve this problem: how can the holy God dwell in the midst of a sinful people?

Hebrew Word Study

שְׁכִינָה shekhinah (shākan) — the divine Presence / dwelling: V.14: "My Presence (pānāy — lit. my face) will go with you, and I will give you rest." The divine Presence is expressed as pānîm — face. God's "face" is the aspect of his being that can be engaged, that turns toward people. When God threatens to send only an angel instead of himself (v.3), Moses refuses to move: "If your Presence does not go with us, do not send us up from here." The Presence of God — not his provision or power — is the essential requirement.

הֲסִירוּ עֶדְיְכֶם hāsîrû ʿedyĕkem — strip off your ornaments: V.5-6: God tells Israel to take off their ornaments — the very gold jewelry from which the calf had been made. The ornament (ʿedî — from the root meaning "to adorn/testify") had been misused as raw material for idolatry. Its removal is an act of mourning and self-emptying. The willingness to divest what adorns and distinguish us is the posture that makes renewed encounter with God possible.

Historical Context

None

Context

God's offer is still generous by ordinary standards: an angel to lead, the land to possess, the enemies driven out. Everything promised, everything delivered — except the one thing Moses and the people most need: "you, personally, with us." Israel's mourning (v.4) is appropriate: they understand what they have jeopardised. The removal of ornaments is an act of repentance — stripping the beauty that accompanied the golden calf's gold.

Cross-Reference

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    33:2–3

    The withdrawal threatened is the withdrawal of the direct, personal divine presence — the intimate accompaniment that distinguishes Israel from all other nations. Sarna notes that military success without divine presence is not the covenant's goal. The angel can deliver the land; only God's presence makes Israel what they are called to be.

    33:5

    The 'stiff-necked people' (am qesheh oref) is a recurring divine characterisation of Israel throughout the wilderness narrative. Sarna notes the image is of a work animal that refuses to submit to the yoke — structurally resistant to divine direction. The term explains why God's direct presence is dangerous: the holy God alongside a perpetually resistant people risks consuming them.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    33:3

    God admits that his holiness is dangerous: 'I might destroy you on the way.' Alter notes the unusual honesty — the withdrawal of presence is presented as mercy, protection from the consuming holiness that accompanies the divine. The threat is not punishment but the honest acknowledgment of what it means to be near the holy God without adequate mediation.

    33:11

    'The LORD spoke to Moses face to face, as a man speaks to his friend' — and yet v.20 says no one can see God's face and live. Alter notes the paradox is held deliberately: the intimacy is real ('face to face, as a friend') and the transcendence is equally real ('no one can see my face and live'). Moses stands at the intersection of divine intimacy and divine inaccessibility.

    Calvin: 'What God threatens here was more terrible than the destruction of the whole people by the sword — for God declares that he will be in some measure absent from them.'     Calvin on 'face to face': 'This familiar manner of speaking does not imply that Moses saw the essence of God, but that God manifested himself to him more clearly than to other men.'     Verses 7–17 — The Tent of Meeting; Moses Refuses to Go Without God's Presence
    7891011121314151617

    Places

    Tent of Meeting — outside the camp
    On the edge of the camp, separate
    After the golden calf, the Tent of Meeting is pitched outside the camp. The divine presence will not dwell in the midst of a rebellious people. The tent's geography is the geography of covenant rupture: God is available, but at a distance, outside the community.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    33:11

    MacArthur: The LORD would speak to Moses face to face, as one speaks to a friend. The intimacy of Moses's relationship with God is the OT's highest designation of prophetic communion. Moses's unique access (Num 12:6-8) is the type that Christ fulfils perfectly.

    33:14

    MacArthur: My Presence will go with you, and I will give you rest. God agrees to go personally. The God Moses meets face to face will accompany Israel face-foreward -- present and attentive.

    Hebrew Word Study

    כָּבוֹד kābôd — glory: V.18-23: Moses asks to see God's glory (kābôd). God says his face (pānāy) he cannot show — "no one may see me and live" — but he will show his goodness (ṭûbî) and proclaim his name. The word kābôd (literally "weight, heaviness") describes the overwhelming reality of God's being. Moses is hidden in the rock as the glory passes; he sees God's "back" (ʾāḥōr — the aftermath, the trailing edge of the divine Presence). The encounter is real but asymmetrical: God can be known but not fully grasped.

    חַנּוֹתִי ḥannōtî — I will be gracious / I will show favor: V.19: "I will have mercy on whom I will have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I will have compassion." This divine declaration — cited by Paul in Rom 9:15 as the foundation of sovereign grace — is God's definition of his own freedom: grace is not earned or predictable, it is given on God's own terms. Moses's intercession did not obligate God; it moved within the sovereignty of God's own gracious character.

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    Moses' argument (vv.15–16) is one of the most theologically precise in Scripture: the distinguishing mark of Israel among the nations is not their morality or their religious system — it is the presence of God with them. Without God's presence, Israel is no different from any other people. The presence of God is Israel's only distinctive. This is Paul's point in 1 Cor 1:18–25: the distinguishing mark of the church is not wisdom or power but the presence of Christ crucified.

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    33:14

    God's promise 'my Presence will go' uses the word panim (face/presence) — the same word at stake in v.20's prohibition. The covenant is restored to the point of God's face-presence accompanying Israel. Sarna notes this is the chapter's turning point: from the threatened withdrawal to the renewed accompaniment promise. The presence that was threatened in vv.1–3 is restored in v.14.

    33:18

    Moses' request to see God's glory (kavod) is the most audacious prayer in the Exodus narrative. Sarna notes the kavod is the full, unmediated divine self-manifestation — what no human can survive (v.20). God's response is to show his goodness (tuv) instead: the divine character proclaimed verbally rather than the divine being perceived visually. The glory is revealed through the name, not through unmediated vision.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    33:14–15

    Moses' condition — 'do not send us up from here' — is the chapter's most decisive moment. He will not move without God. Alter notes the intimacy of the negotiation: Moses treats the covenant as a relationship in which his preferences have standing. He is not demanding; he is declaring his position. The God who initiated the relationship is not offended by Moses' refusal to go without him — he has already offered to go.

    33:18–23

    God's response to the glory-request is the most theologically dense passage in Exodus. Alter notes the movement from visual request (show me your glory) to verbal response (I will proclaim my name) — the divine self-disclosure shifts from image to word. The glory cannot be seen; the name can be heard. The revelation of the divine character is verbal, not visual.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    33:15

    Calvin on 'do not send us up from here': 'Moses shows that the value of the land consisted in this alone — that God would dwell in it with them. He refuses, therefore, a land flowing with milk and honey without God's presence.' The material blessing without the divine presence is worthless. Calvin applies this to the church: the richest, most successful institution without God's presence is an empty shell.

    33:18

    Calvin on the glory request: 'Moses was not so absorbed by the familiar intercourse as to forget the distance between God and man. He felt that there was still something higher to know, something more excellent to behold.' The more intimate the relationship with God, the more intense the desire to know him more fully. Moses' request is the prayer of the person most thoroughly in the divine presence.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    33:19

    The NET note on 'I will cause all my goodness to pass before you' (aavir kol tuvi al panekha) explains that tuv (goodness/beauty) is not a visual but a characterological disclosure — the divine moral character, especially as expressed in grace and compassion. The note connects this to the name-proclamation of ch.34:6–7 (the thirteen divine attributes): the glory Moses sees is the divine character verbally declared.

    33:17-23

    The NET note on 'I will have mercy on whom I have mercy' discusses Paul's use in Rom 9:15-16 as the foundation of his doctrine of election. The note traces the theological claim: God's mercy is not earned, demanded, or calculable — it is the free expression of divine character. Moses cannot request it; he can only receive it.

    Verses 18–23 — Moses Asks to See God's Glory
    181920212223

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    33:19

    MacArthur: I will cause all my goodness to pass in front of you, and I will proclaim my name, the LORD, in your presence. The response to Moses's request to see God's glory is the proclamation of the divine character. The name is the glory; the character is the revelation.

    33:18-23

    MacArthur: Moses asks to see the divine glory; God responds with the proclamation of the divine name and the passing of his goodness. The answer redefines the question: the glory of God is his character, not a visible form. Moses sees the back — the aftermath of divine passage — because the full display of divine character would be fatal to mortal sight.

    Hebrew Word Study

    מוֹדִיעַ môdîaʿ — make known / reveal: V.12-13: "Moses said to the LORD, 'You have been telling me to lead these people, but you have not let me know (hôdaʿtanî) whom you will send with me... Teach me your ways (dĕrākekā) so I may know you."' Moses prays for revealed knowledge of God's character. The response — the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy (34:6-7) — is the most complete divine self-disclosure in the OT. Knowing God's ways (dĕrākîm) enables alignment with divine purposes, not just compliance with divine commands.

    אֹהֶל ʾōhel — tent: V.7-11: The "tent of meeting" pitched outside the camp (ʾōhel môʿēd) after the golden calf was a provisional arrangement while the Tabernacle awaited construction. The cloud descended on this tent when Moses entered; the people stood and worshipped at their own tent entrances. The scene depicts a community worshipping at a distance — they had disqualified themselves from proximity — while Moses alone entered into the divine cloud on their behalf.

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    God's answer to "show me your glory" is the proclamation of his name (34:6–7 will supply the content). The most important moment in Sinai theology is not the thunder and lightning of ch.19 but this: God proclaiming his own character to Moses in the cleft of the rock. Thirteen divine attributes (mercy, grace, slow to anger, abounding in love and faithfulness, etc.) are the glory Moses receives. The glory of God is his character.

    Cross-Reference

    • John 1:18"No one has ever seen God, but the one and only Son... has made him known." The limitation of 33:20 resolved in the Incarnation.
    • 2 Cor 3:18"We all, who with unveiled faces contemplate the Lord's glory, are being transformed into his image." The cleft-in-the-rock vision transformed into the permanent contemplation of believers.
    • John 17:24"Father, I want those you have given me to be with me where I am, and to see my glory." Jesus promises what Moses longed for.

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    33:20

    The 'face of God' (panai) that cannot be seen is the unveiled divine essence — the full, unmediated divine self-manifestation. Sarna notes that throughout the OT, people who encounter the divine directly fear they will die (Gideon, Judg 6:22; Isaiah, Isa 6:5; Ezekiel's prostration, Ezek 1:28). The fear is not irrational; it reflects genuine asymmetry between divine holiness and human mortality.

    33:20-23

    The vision of God's back rather than his face is the OT's most concentrated statement of divine transcendence within covenant relationship. Sarna notes the paradox: the God who speaks with Moses 'face to face, as a man speaks with his friend' (v.11) simultaneously declares that no one can see his face and live. The closeness and the distance coexist.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    33:21–23

    The cleft in the rock, God's hand covering Moses, the passing of the glory, the removal of the hand — this is the most physically specific description of the divine-human encounter in the OT. Alter notes the paradox is enacted spatially: Moses is in the rock (protected), covered by the divine hand (shielded), then sees the 'back' (achar) of God — what has passed, not what is fully present. The encounter is real and its limits are equally real.

    33:21-23

    The cleft in the rock where God hides Moses while the divine glory passes — the hand covering, the removal of the hand, the glimpse of the divine back — is the Bible's most intimate description of the limit of human encounter with the divine. Alter notes the spatial and tactile precision: God's hand, the rock cleft, the covering and uncovering, are described as physical events.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    33:19

    Calvin on 'I will have mercy on whom I will have mercy': 'This passage is quoted by Paul to prove the freedom of election — that God is not bound by any necessity, but confers grace on whom he will.' The divine freedom to show mercy is the foundation of election. Mercy is not owed; it is given. The covenant is not Israel's achievement; it is God's sovereign choice.

    33:23

    Calvin on seeing God's back: 'Moses was not permitted to see the full glory of God, but only such a display as human sense could bear. God accommodated himself to the measure of Moses' capacity.' The back-not-face distinction is the principle of divine accommodation: God reveals himself as much as the human recipient can receive without being destroyed.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    33:19

    The NET note on 'I will have mercy on whom I have mercy' quotes Paul's use in Rom 9:15 and discusses the tension between divine sovereignty and human responsibility. The note observes that the statement is made in the context of covenant renewal after Israel's apostasy — the mercy is sovereign precisely because Israel has forfeited all claim to it. The freedom of divine mercy is asserted in the moment when human merit is most clearly absent.

    33:19-23

    The NET note on 'I will proclaim my name YHWH before you' discusses what Moses actually receives in response to his request to see God's glory. He receives not a visual theophany but a verbal one — the proclamation of the divine name in ch.34. The response redefines what 'seeing God's glory' means: the glory is the character, and the character is proclaimed in words.

    Ancient Sources

    Divine Face and Presence
    In ANE religions, "the face of the god" referred to the divine statue — the visible representation of the deity. For Moses to seek God's face without an image is to seek the invisible God directly, without the mediation of representation. The "face" language is the same but the theological content is radically different.
    Moses' request is the inverse of the golden calf: seeking the invisible God rather than creating a visible substitute.
    Thirteen Attributes
    The rabbinic tradition counts thirteen divine attributes in the proclamation of Exod 34:6–7 (the answer to Moses' request in 33:18). These thirteen attributes became the foundation of Jewish liturgy and theology.
    The character of God disclosed to Moses in 33:19/34:6–7 became the most formative theological statement in Judaism.

    Reception History

    2 Cor 3:7–18
    Paul's extended meditation on Moses' unveiled face and the greater glory of the new covenant. The veil Moses placed over his face (34:33) becomes the veil over Israel's hearts — removed only in Christ.
    John 1:14,18

    Textual Notes

    Scholarly Debates

    The Tent of Meeting: Same as the Tabernacle or a Prior Structure?
    A separate, earlier structure
    Most critical scholars, many evangelical
    The tent of meeting in Exod 33 is placed outside the camp, before the tabernacle is built (chs.35–40). It is an improvised structure that served as the pre-tabernacle meeting place. Once the tabernacle is complete, it takes over the function of the tent of meeting and is placed in the centre of the camp.
    Proleptic reference to the tabernacle
    Some traditional commentators
    The tent of meeting is the tabernacle, described proleptically before its construction is narrated. The chronological narrative sometimes anticipates the completed structure.
    The outside-the-camp placement strongly supports a distinct prior structure. The tabernacle by design is inside the camp (Num 2). The contrast between the outside tent (ch.33) and the inside tabernacle (ch.40) reflects the golden calf's effect on the covenant relationship — God meets Moses at the margins; eventually he returns to the centre.
