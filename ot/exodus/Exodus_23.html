Exodus 23

Justice, Truth, Sabbath Rest; Three Festivals; Conquest Promise

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1–13 — Justice, Truth, and Sabbath Rest
1"Do not spread false reports. Do not help a guilty person by being a malicious witness.2"Do not follow the crowd in doing wrong. When you give testimony in a lawsuit, do not pervert justice by siding with the crowd,3and do not show favoritism to a poor person in a lawsuit.4"If you come across your enemy's ox or donkey wandering off, be sure to return it.5If you see the donkey of someone who hates you fallen down under its load, do not leave it there; be sure you help them with it.6"Do not deny justice to your poor people in their lawsuits.7Have nothing to do with a false charge and do not put an innocent or honest person to death, for I will not acquit the guilty.8"Do not accept a bribe, for a bribe blinds those who see and twists the words of the innocent.9"Do not oppress a foreigner; you yourselves know how it feels to be foreigners, because you were foreigners in Egypt.10"For six years you are to sow your fields and harvest the crops,11but during the seventh year let the land lie unplowed and unused. Then the poor among your people may get food from it, and the wild animals may eat what is left. Do the same with your vineyard and your olive grove.12"Six days do your work, but on the seventh day do not work, so that your ox and your donkey may rest, and so that the slave born in your household and the foreigner living among you may be refreshed.13"Be careful to do everything I have said to you. Do not invoke the names of other gods; do not let them be heard on your lips."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
23:2

MacArthur: Do not follow the crowd in doing wrong. The covenant community is called to resist majority pressure in pursuit of justice. Truth is not determined by consensus; the covenant standard is independent of popular opinion.

23:11

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The enemy's ox law (v.4) is the most radical social command in the Book of the Covenant — help your enemy recover his property now, before any reconciliation. The sabbatical year (vv.10–11) extends the Sabbath principle to the land itself: six years cultivate, seventh year rest and let the poor eat freely. The land belongs to God; Israel is steward, not owner.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 5:44Jesus' "love your enemies" is the intensification of Exod 23:4–5 — helping the enemy's animal is the OT seed.
  • Lev 25:1–7The sabbatical year expanded: land rests, debts released, slaves freed.
  • Acts 2:1Pentecost falls on the Festival of Weeks — the Spirit given on the firstfruits feast.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:4–5

The command to return an enemy's ox and help an enemy's donkey is one of the OT's clearest anticipations of the ethic Jesus will articulate in Matt 5:43–48. Sarna notes this law requires active kindness toward those one is in legal or personal conflict with — not merely tolerating the enemy but actively working for their welfare (or at least their property's welfare). The covenant law does not permit private feelings to override social obligation.

23:10–12

The sabbatical year (shemitah) applies the Sabbath principle to the land itself. Sarna notes the agricultural wisdom: regular fallow years restore soil fertility. But the law's stated purpose is social and theological: the poor shall eat (v.11) and your animals and slaves shall be refreshed (v.12). The Sabbath principle is not merely religious observance but a comprehensive system of ecological sustainability and social equity.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:1–9

The legal section of ch.23 is a meditation on the integrity of the justice system itself — false testimony, mob pressure, bribery, enemy's property, the poor in their lawsuits. Alter notes that the law is not only concerned with individual transactions but with the systemic conditions that make justice possible. A bribed judge, a false witness, mob pressure — these are the structural corruptions that destroy justice regardless of how good the individual laws are.

23:4-9

The juxtaposition of enemy-ox assistance (vv.4-5) and covenant-community legal faithfulness (vv.6-9) in the same cluster is the Book of the Covenant's most radical ethical moment. Alter notes that helping your enemy precedes the justice instructions — the law's ethical demands are not limited by social boundaries.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:4–5

Calvin on helping the enemy's animal: 'It would be altogether monstrous if, for the sake of satisfying our hatred against men, we should cruelly neglect dumb animals.' The obligation to the animal is independent of feelings about the owner. Calvin uses this to establish the principle that cruelty to animals (even as a way of punishing their hated owner) is morally forbidden — the animal's welfare is a legitimate moral concern in its own right.

23:10–11

Calvin on the sabbatical year: 'God not only provided for the poor in their necessity, but accustomed his people to liberality by a formal observance.' The compulsory fallow year is covenant-structured generosity: the owner does not choose whether to leave the field for the poor; the law requires it. Calvin reads mandatory generosity as God's way of disciplining the natural tendency toward hoarding.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:6

The NET note on 'do not deny justice to your poor people in their lawsuits' (lo tateh mishpat evyonkha berivav) discusses the legal context: the poor were structurally disadvantaged in ancient courts — unable to pay advocates, lacking social connections with judges, vulnerable to the influence of the wealthy. The specific command addresses this systemic disadvantage: the poor person's legal claim is equal before the covenant court regardless of economic standing.

23:8-9

The NET note on the foreigner-protection command — 'you know how it feels to be a foreigner' (yedatem et nefesh hager) — explains this as the covenant's foundational empathy principle. Israel's own experience of oppression is the ethical ground for their treatment of the vulnerable among them. Experience of injustice obligates justice.

Verses 14–33 — The Three Annual Festivals; the Conquest Promise
14"Three times a year you are to celebrate a festival to me.15"Celebrate the Festival of Unleavened Bread; for seven days eat bread made without yeast, as I commanded you. Do this at the appointed time in the month of Aviv, for in that month you came out of Egypt. No one is to appear before me empty-handed.16"Celebrate the Festival of Harvest with the firstfruits of the crops you sow in your field. You must demolish them and break their sacred stones to pieces.25Worship the Lord your God, and his blessing will be on your food and water. I will take away sickness from among you,26and none will miscarry or be barren in your land. I will give you a full life span.27"I will send my terror ahead of you and throw into confusion every nation you encounter. I will make all your enemies turn their backs and run.28I will send the hornet ahead of you to drive the Hivites, Canaanites and Hittites out of your way.29But I will not drive them out in a single year, because the land would become desolate and the wild animals too numerous for you.30Little by little I will drive them out before you, until you have increased enough to take possession of the land.31"I will establish your borders from the Red Sea to the Mediterranean Sea, and from the desert to the Euphrates River. I will give into your hands the people who live in the land, and you will drive them out before you.32Do not make a covenant with them or with their gods.33Do not let them live in your land or they will cause you to sin against me, because the worship of their gods will certainly be a snare to you."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
23:14-16

MacArthur: The three annual pilgrimage festivals structure Israel's year around covenant celebration: Unleavened Bread (deliverance from Egypt), Harvest/Firstfruits (agricultural provision), Ingathering/Tabernacles (wilderness provision).

23:20-22

MacArthur: I am sending an angel ahead of you to guard you along the way. The Angel of the LORD who guides Israel is identified as one whose name is in God's name -- a description of divine identity.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The three pilgrimage festivals map onto Israel's redemptive story: Passover (redemption from Egypt), Weeks/Shavuot (fifty days after Passover — the law at Sinai), and Booths/Sukkot (the wilderness). Every year Israel re-enters these events. The conquest promise closes the chapter: God sends his angel before them, but warns that the nations' gods remain the primary threat.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Cor 5:7Christ our Passover — the first festival fulfilled in his death.
  • Acts 2:1–4Pentecost (Festival of Weeks) — Spirit given fifty days after resurrection-Passover. The Torah written on stone replaced by Torah written on hearts.
  • John 7:37–39Jesus at the Feast of Booths: "If anyone thirsts, let him come to me" — the water-pouring ceremony of Sukkot fulfilled.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:20

The 'angel' (malach) of v.20 is identified in v.21 as carrying 'my Name' — making him the divine presence in representational form. Sarna notes this is the same angel-of-the-LORD who appeared to Moses (3:2) and who will later be identified with the pillar of cloud and fire (14:19). The Name-bearer is the covenant God in theophanic representation — distinguishable from God yet identified with him.

23:29–30

The graduated conquest serves a practical ecological purpose: the land requires its own population to maintain agricultural productivity and prevent wild animal multiplication. Sarna notes this reflects a realistic understanding of Bronze Age agricultural ecology — an abruptly depopulated land would quickly revert to wilderness. The divine plan includes ecological stewardship even in the process of judgment.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:20–33

The conquest promise concludes the Book of the Covenant by shifting from legal code to divine pledge. Alter notes the rhetorical shift: the conditional 'if you obey' has governed the legal sections; now unconditional divine commitment ('I will') dominates. The covenant code ends with promise, not merely demand. The law is embedded in grace; the final word of the code is the divine pledge to accomplish what the law describes.

23:25-33

The conquest promise's conditions — 'worship the LORD and he will bless' — frame the military campaign as covenant fidelity rather than ethnic cleansing. Alter notes the theological framing is deliberate: the dispossession of Canaanites is not racial policy but covenant consequence. The land grant is for those who maintain the covenant relationship with the land's owner.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:20

Calvin on the angel: 'God is said to send his angel, who should guide and protect them through the wilderness: and this angel, I have no doubt, was the Son of God, who had previously appeared to the patriarchs.' Calvin's Christological reading of the angel-of-the-LORD passages applies here: the divine presence that accompanies Israel through the wilderness is the second person of the Trinity in pre-incarnate form. The conquest is led by Christ.

23:29–30

Calvin on the gradual conquest: 'The reason why he did not exterminate them all at once was the same as before — lest the land should become waste; for men are necessary for its cultivation.' Providence works with natural means toward supernatural ends. The timing of the conquest is not divine slowness but divine wisdom: the land's ecological integrity is a proper concern of the covenant God who gave it as a gift.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:28

The NET note on 'the hornet' (hatsirah) discusses whether this is literal (an insect swarm) or metaphorical (divine terror/panic). Deut 7:20 and Josh 24:12 use the same term. The note notes that some scholars see a connection with Egyptian military operations that preceded the conquest — the Egyptian campaigns against Canaan in the 14th–13th centuries BC may have weakened the Canaanite city-states before Israel's arrival, functioning as God's 'hornet.'

23:29-30

The NET note on 'I will not drive them out in a single year' — gradual dispossession rather than immediate — discusses the theological explanation given: immediate removal would leave the land desolate and the wild animals would multiply. The note identifies this as a covenant accommodation: God's method serves Israel's practical sustainability.

Ancient Sources

Festival Calendar
Israel's three festivals map onto the agricultural calendar. The theological reinterpretation as redemptive memorials is distinctive — nature festivals become history festivals.
The agricultural rhythm is redeemed by being made to carry the covenant story.
Sabbatical Year
No precise ANE parallel exists for voluntarily fallowing all land for an entire year. Unique to Israel's covenant legislation.
The sabbatical year's uniqueness underlines that the land belongs to God, not the cultivator.

Literary Structure

Enemy's Ox: Ethics Beyond Social Boundaries23:4-5If you see your enemy's ox or donkey wandering off, bring it back. The command to assist an enemy's animals reaches beyond social covenant to the neighbour-love that Leviticus will name. Covenant ethics crosses the boundary of enmity.
Three Pilgrimage Festivals: The Covenant Year23:14-17Three times a year all males are to appear before the LORD. The three festivals -- Unleavened Bread, Harvest, Ingathering -- structure the covenant community's annual rhythm around divine appearance, not agricultural necessity.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

The Feast Legislation: Historical Development or Mosaic Antiquity?
Mosaic antiquity
Kitchen, evangelical scholars
The three annual feasts — Unleavened Bread, Harvest/Weeks, and Ingathering/Booths — are ancient, fitting a pre-settlement semi-nomadic context transitioning to agricultural life. The agricultural references (firstfruits, harvest) reflect anticipation of Canaan, not Canaanite borrowing. The Book of the Covenant is Mosaic in origin.
Post-settlement development
Many critical scholars
The agricultural festivals (Firstfruits, Harvest, Ingathering) presuppose a settled agricultural life and may have been adopted from Canaanite agricultural religion and theologised into Israelite covenant terms. The law codes developed over centuries; the Book of the Covenant represents an early stratum (perhaps 10th–9th century BC) of this development.
The festivals' agricultural character is consistent with both anticipation of Canaan (Mosaic) and adoption/adaptation of existing Canaanite festivals (post-settlement). The theological reinterpretation is what is distinctively Israelite in either case: the harvest festivals are connected to the Exodus (Unleavened Bread = the night of departure) and the wilderness (Booths = the wilderness shelters). The historicisation of nature festivals is the distinctively Israelite contribution.
