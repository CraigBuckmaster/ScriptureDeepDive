Exodus 29

The Ordination Ceremony; the Daily Offering; I Will Dwell Among You

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
29:1

MacArthur: The ordination procedure takes seven days -- a complete cycle, a new creation. Washing (cleansing), clothing (covering), anointing (divine enabling), sacrifice (atonement maintained).

29:7

MacArthur: Take the anointing oil and anoint him by pouring it on his head. The anointing of the priest is the origin of the title Messiah. The high priest anointed for his mediating role prefigures the ultimate High Priest anointed with the Spirit without measure (John 3:34).

Hebrew Word Study

מִלֻּאִים millûʾîm — ordination / filling: V.29: The ordination ceremony for priests is called millûʾîm — literally "fillings." To ordain is to fill: the priest is "filled" for the role, equipped and commissioned. The image of divine filling (mālēʾ) runs through Scripture: the Spirit fills the craftsmen (35:31), Joshua (Deut 34:9), and at Pentecost the disciples (Acts 2:4) — all ordinations, all enablments for a role in God's service.

מָשַׁח māshaḥ — anoint: V.7,21: The anointing of Aaron with the sacred oil (shemen hammishḥāh) is the central act of priestly ordination. Māshaḥ (to smear, anoint) gives us "Messiah" (māshîaḥ) and "Christ" (christos — "anointed one"). The king (1 Sam 16:13), the High Priest, and occasionally the prophet were all anointed. Jesus received the Spirit without measure (John 3:34) — the ultimate anointing that fulfilled and surpassed all priestly, prophetic, and royal anointings.

Historical Context

None

Context

The seven-day ordination ceremony mirrors creation's seven days — the priesthood is a new creation, a new beginning of ordered relationship with God. The three sacrifices (sin offering/bull, burnt offering/ram, ordination offering/ram) move from atonement to dedication to celebration: the priests are first cleansed of guilt, then wholly given to God, then established in the fellowship of the sacred meal.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:4

The washing (rachatz) that begins the ordination is a purification ritual that removes ritual contamination. Sarna notes that priestly purification rites are attested throughout the ancient Near East — the unique feature of Israel's version is its connection to covenant appointment rather than merely cultic cleansing. Aaron and his sons are washed into a new status, not merely cleaned from an old one.

29:7

The anointing oil (shemen hamishchah) poured over Aaron's head is the act that makes him 'the anointed priest' (hakohen hamashiach, Lev 4:3). Sarna notes that anointing was used in the ancient Near East for the consecration of kings, priests, and sacred objects. The oil poured on Aaron's head makes him a mashiach (anointed one) — the same word applied to kings and prophets, and ultimately to the Messiah.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:4–7

The ordination sequence is a physical transformation narrative: the ordinary man is washed, clothed in sacred garments, and anointed with oil. Alter notes the sequence moves from the body (washing, dressing) to the person's status (anointing). The ceremony is not merely preparation for service; it is a constitutive act that creates the priest. Without the ceremony, there is no priest; after it, there is.

29:12–14

The sin offering's disposal — blood on the horns, fat on the altar, flesh outside the camp — is the most complex of the ordination sacrifices. Alter notes the spatial movement: the most sacred elements (blood, fat) go to the altar; the remainder goes outside the camp. The spatial contrast between inside (holy) and outside (expelled) will develop throughout Leviticus. Christ's death 'outside the gate' (Heb 13:12) reverses the spatial logic: the holy One goes outside to bear the expelled.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
29:4–9

Calvin on the ordination ceremony: 'These were not empty ceremonies without any spiritual meaning; for God had one end in view — to direct his people to Christ.' The three acts — washing, clothing, anointing — are for Calvin the OT form of the Christian's reception of the Holy Spirit: 'The priests were washed that they might represent that purity and holiness of life which is required; clothed, that they might sustain the honour of the office; anointed, that they might be endued with spiritual gifts for its discharge.'

29:12

Calvin on blood at the altar horns: 'As the blood was the symbol of expiation, this was a confirmation that the altar was purified by blood; and that God was only to be approached through an expiatory sacrifice.' The ordination ceremony is itself a sacrificial act — the priests are consecrated through the same blood-altar structure that governs all subsequent priestly service. The system's beginning is the system's pattern.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
29:7

The NET note on anointing the priest discusses the anointing oil formula (30:22–25): myrrh, cinnamon, cane, and cassia in olive oil. The note explains that the anointing of the high priest is described as oil 'poured' on the head (Ps 133:2), while the anointing of ordinary priests may have been a sprinkling on the garments (Lev 8:30). The high priest's anointing is the most abundant and the most visible — the oil flowing over the garments as the ceremony proceeds.

29:10-14

The NET note on the sin offering bull discusses the laying of hands on the bull's head before slaughter — the identification gesture. The note traces the theological mechanics: the priest identifies himself with the sacrifice; the sacrifice bears what the priest brings to it. This is the foundational gesture of substitutionary atonement.

Hebrew Word Study

חַטָּאת ḥaṭṭāʾt — sin offering / purification offering: V.14,36: The ḥaṭṭāʾt (sin/purification offering) was specifically designed to deal with inadvertent or ritual uncleanness. The word comes from the root ḥāṭāʾ (to miss the mark, to sin). The offering purifies the sanctuary as well as the offerer — a concept crucial to understanding why Christ's death was necessary not just for individual sinners but for the cosmic restoration of the defiled creation (cf. Heb 9:23; Rom 8:20-21).

זֶבַח שְׁלָמִים zebaḥ shĕlāmîm — peace offering / fellowship offering: V.28: The shĕlāmîm (peace/fellowship offering) was unique in that the worshipper ate part of the sacrifice. It was a shared meal between God, the priests, and the offerer. The root shālôm (peace, wholeness, completeness) names both the offering and its goal: restored relationship, shared table, communal harmony. The Lord's Supper is the new covenant shĕlāmîm — the shared meal that enacts and celebrates peace between God and his people.

Historical Context

None

Context

The blood on right ear, thumb, and big toe (v.20) is the ordination's most distinctive element. It does not appear in any other sacrificial context — it is unique to priestly consecration. The same rite is used in the cleansing of lepers (Lev 14:14), signifying complete restoration. The priest is entirely consecrated: everything he hears, everything he does, everywhere he goes is claimed by God.

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    29:38–42

    The daily (tamid) offering — two lambs, morning and evening — is the covenant's foundational sacrifice. Sarna notes the tamid ('perpetual/regular') offering structures Israelite time: morning and evening sacrifices bracket the day. The God of Israel is approached twice daily, every day, through the same prescribed sacrifice. The regularity is the theological statement: covenant worship is not occasional but perpetual, not emergency-driven but calendar-structured.

    29:45–46

    The concluding statement of the ordination instructions — 'I will dwell among the Israelites' — is the teleological statement of the entire tabernacle project. Sarna identifies this as the climax of the Exodus narrative: the purpose of liberation was not freedom per se but divine-human co-habitation. The tabernacle section that began with 'let them make me a sanctuary so that I may dwell among them' (25:8) finds its resolution here: the dwelling is coming; it will be accomplished.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    29:45–46

    The chapter's final verses are its theological summit. Alter notes that the long, technical chapters of tabernacle design and priestly ordination are framed by the simplest possible divine commitment: 'I will dwell among the Israelites and be their God.' All the complexity — the cubits, the materials, the priestly garments, the ordination sacrifices — exists to enable the most intimate possible relationship: God with his people, in their midst, as their God.

    29:42-43

    The 'meeting with the Israelites' promised at the tent's entrance — 'there I will meet with the Israelites, and the tabernacle will be sanctified by my glory' — is the architectural climax. Alter notes the tabernacle's purpose is not the building itself but the meetings it enables. God is building a place to meet with his people, not a monument to himself.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    29:43-45

    MacArthur: I will dwell among the Israelites and be their God. The stated goal of the entire tabernacle project: divine dwelling in the midst of the covenant community. This is the covenant's ultimate promise, fulfilled progressively through tabernacle, temple, incarnation, Pentecost, and the new Jerusalem (Rev 21:3).

    29:38-46

    MacArthur: The daily burnt offerings — morning and evening — establish the rhythm that all subsequent covenant worship will follow. The tamid (regular offering) is not occasional but structural: twice daily, without interruption. God's dwelling with Israel is not crisis-response but sustained covenantal presence maintained by uninterrupted sacrifice.

    Hebrew Word Study

    תָּמִיד tāmîd — daily / continual burnt offering: V.38-42: "Offer two lambs a day as the regular burnt offering (ʿōlat tāmîd) — one in the morning, one at twilight." The daily sacrificial rhythm structured Israel's entire temporal existence around the offering of life to God. The priest who served at the morning and evening offering gave each day a sacrificial frame. Heb 7:27 draws the contrast: Christ "does not need to offer sacrifices day after day... he sacrificed for their sins once for all when he offered himself."

    דַּעַת daʿat — knowledge / personal encounter: V.46: "They will know (yādĕʿû) that I am the LORD their God, who brought them out of Egypt so that I might dwell among them." The purpose of the Exodus and the Tabernacle is yĕdîʿāh — knowledge of God. But this is not cognitive knowledge: yādaʿ in Hebrew is covenantal, intimate, experiential knowledge (cf. Gen 4:1 where Adam "knew" Eve). The whole elaborate system of Tabernacle worship is designed to facilitate deep personal encounter with the living God.

    None

    Context

    The daily burnt offering (vv.38–42) frames the Tabernacle's ongoing life: a lamb at dawn, a lamb at dusk. Every day began and ended with sacrifice. The people woke to the smoke of a burnt offering and went to sleep to the same. Their daily rhythm was punctuated by the reminder that approach to God requires the giving of life. Hebrews argues that Christ's single sacrifice replaced this perpetual offering — once for all instead of once every morning.

    29:43

    The sanctuary is 'sanctified by my glory' (veniqdash bikhvodi). Sarna notes the divine passive: the tabernacle's holiness is not inherent in its materials or construction but derived from the divine presence that inhabits it. The same principle appears in 40:34–35: the glory cloud fills the completed tabernacle, making it holy. Holiness always flows from the divine presence to the structure, never the reverse.

    29:44-46

    The triple consecration — of the tent, the altar, Aaron, and his sons — by divine glory rather than human action establishes that the tabernacle's sanctity is God's gift, not a human achievement. Sarna notes the covenant logic: God is the one who makes holy; the human beings are those who receive the holiness extended to them.

    Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    Moses↗ People
    Prophet, lawgiver, and covenant mediator
    Moses stands at the centre of the Pentateuch’s narrative — the one man who spoke with God face to face (Exod 33:11; Num 12:8) and mediated the Sinai covenant to Israel. In Leviticus he receives the priestly legislation; in Numbers he leads, intercedes, and ultimately fails at Meribah (Num 20). The writer of Hebrews calls him "faithful in all God’s house" (Heb 3:5) while distinguishing him from Christ, the Son over the house.
    Aaron↗ People
    First high priest of Israel
    Aaron, Moses’s brother, is consecrated as Israel’s first high priest in Lev 8–9. He bears the names of the twelve tribes on his breastplate before God and enters the Most Holy Place once a year on Yom Kippur (Lev 16). His failures — the golden calf (Exod 32), challenging Moses’s authority (Num 12), complicity at Meribah (Num 20) — make him the paradigm of the imperfect priest who prefigures a better high priest (Heb 7:26–28).

    Miriam↗ People
    Prophetess and worship leader; Moses’s sister
    Miriam leads Israel’s women in worship after the Red Sea crossing (Exod 15:20) and is called a prophetess. In Num 12 she challenges Moses’s unique prophetic authority alongside Aaron and is struck with ṣāraʿat — only to be healed after Moses’s seven-word intercession. She dies at Kadesh (Num 20:1), her passing noted in two terse Hebrew words before the water crisis.
    Joshua↗ People
    Moses’s military commander and designated successor
    Joshua son of Nun serves as Moses’s lieutenant throughout the wilderness period — commanding at Rephidim (Exod 17), ascending Sinai with Moses (Exod 24), guarding the tent of meeting. In Num 13–14 he stands with Caleb as one of two faithful spies. He is formally commissioned in Num 27 through Moses’s hand-laying and inherits the leadership role that Moses cannot fulfil past the Jordan.

    Ancient Sources

    ANE Ordination Rites
    Mesopotamian priestly initiation rites involved ritual washing, investiture, and animal sacrifice. The Aaronic ordination uses similar forms but unique content: the blood on ear, thumb, and toe is without parallel in known ANE sources.
    The uniqueness underlines its distinctive covenant theology.
    Daily Sacrifice
    The tāmîd was the backbone of Temple worship. Its cessation in 70 AD — when the Romans destroyed the Temple — was the defining catastrophe of ancient Judaism.
    The tāmîd's importance across Israel's history reflects the theological centrality of Exod 29:38–42.

    Scholarly Debates

    Ordination: Does the Ceremony Make the Priest or Confirm Divine Appointment?
    The ceremony constitutes the priest
    Catholic, Orthodox, high-church tradition
    The ordination ceremony is a sacramental act that effects what it signifies: the person is not a priest before the ceremony and is a priest after it. The washing, clothing, and anointing are not merely symbols of an appointment already made; they are the means by which the appointment is executed. Holy orders is a sacrament that confers priestly character.
    The ceremony confirms and publicly marks a prior divine appointment
    Protestant/Reformed tradition
    God's appointment precedes the ceremony; the ceremony publicly marks and confirms what God has already determined. Calvin reads the priestly garments and ordination as divine accommodation to human weakness — Israel needed visible, enacted signs of what God was doing spiritually. The ceremony is meaningful and necessary but not independently efficacious.
    The text supports the view that the ceremony is constitutive: Aaron was not the high priest before it (he was Moses' spokesman) and is after it (he performs priestly functions). Whether the ceremony is the means of the appointment or the public enactment of it, the ceremony is required and transformative. The Reformed/Catholic distinction matters for NT ecclesiology; for the Exodus context, both readings affirm the ceremony's necessity.
