Exodus 36

Work Begins; Too Much Generosity; Curtains and Frames Constructed

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1—19 — The Skilled Workers Begin; The Tabernacle Curtains

Hebrew Word Study

הוֹתֵר hôtēr — more than enough / surplus: V.5-7: "The people are bringing more than enough for doing the work the LORD commanded." Moses had to restrain the people from giving — they had to be told to stop. This is the only instance in the OT (or the NT) of an offering needing to be halted due to excess. The surplus was itself a spiritual indicator: Israel had moved from the golden calf (stealing from God's service for idolatry) to the Tabernacle (giving beyond the need). The direction of generosity reveals the direction of the heart.

בְּצַלְאֵל bĕtsalʾēl — Bezalel / "in the shadow of God": V.1-2: Bezalel (bĕ-tsel-ʾēl — "in the shadow/protection of God") and Oholiab (ʾĂhōlîʾāb — "the Father is my tent") together supervised all the construction. Their names are theological: the craftsman lives "in God's shadow" and "under the Father's tent." The work of sacred artistry is done under divine protection and patronage. The integration of meaning in a craftsman's very name is a form of vocational consecration.

Historical Context

None

Context

The restraint of the giving (vv.4–7) is the only moment in Scripture where an offering drive was stopped because the people gave too much. The craftsmen had to tell Moses: we cannot use what is being brought. Moses had to issue a public order to stop giving. This is the overflow of a repentant, Spirit-moved community — the golden calf's guilt resolved in extravagant generosity.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Chr 29:9David's Temple offering: "The people rejoiced at the willing response of their leaders, for they had given freely and wholeheartedly to the Lord." The same overflow of joy.
  • 2 Cor 8:3The Macedonian churches: "they gave as much as they were able, and even beyond their ability." The Tabernacle offering is the OT precedent for this NT phenomenon.

36:6–7

Sarna notes this is the only restraint-of-giving command in the OT. The community's overflowing generosity is an act of covenant renewal — the same people who donated gold for the calf (32:2–3) now give more than the tabernacle can use. The excess giving is penitential as well as generous.

36:4–7

Alter notes the embarrassment of overabundant giving is uniquely human. The craftsmen come to Moses not with a problem but with the best possible news: there is too much. The restraint-of-giving command is the book's most unexpected moment of institutional modesty.

36:6

Calvin: 'It is rare for men to be so liberal that they must be restrained from giving. But this people, stirred by zeal of repentance, poured forth their offerings with such abundance that Moses had to stop them.' The excessive giving is covenant renewal in material form.

Hebrew Word Study

עֶשֶׂר יְרִיעוֹת ʿeser yĕrîʿôt — ten curtains: V.8-13: The ten inner curtains were joined in two sets of five with loops and gold clasps — a unified whole assembled from coordinated parts. The number ten (ʿeser) in Tabernacle construction is systematic: ten curtains, ten sockets (for the altar), ten cubits of height. The architectural precision of the Tabernacle design reflects the theological precision of the covenant it housed: God's dwelling is not approximate but exact.

אֶדֶן ʾeden — socket / foundation: V.24-26: The silver sockets (ʾădānîm) into which the wall frames fitted were made from the census ransom money (30:11-16) — each Israelite's half-shekel formed the literal foundation of the Tabernacle. The house of God rested on the ransom of the people. Every Israelite, rich or poor, contributed equally; the foundation embodied the equal worth of every redeemed life before God.

Historical Context

None

Context

The detailed repetition of the Tabernacle construction (mirroring Exod 26) is intentional and theologically significant. The text could simply say "they did as commanded." Instead it repeats every measurement and material. The repetition is the message: Israel did exactly what God said, nothing more and nothing less. After the golden calf — where they did exactly what God did not say — the precise obedience of execution is its own theological statement.

    Moses stands at the centre of the Pentateuch’s narrative — the one man who spoke with God face to face (Exod 33:11; Num 12:8) and mediated the Sinai covenant to Israel.     Ancient Sources

    Narrative Repetition
    The repetition of the Tabernacle instructions in Exodus 25–31 (command) and 35–40 (execution) is a deliberate literary structure — sometimes called the "command-execution" pattern. Found in Egyptian building texts and Mesopotamian temple accounts, it confirms that the work was done exactly as ordered.
    The literary repetition is not poor editing but deliberate theological emphasis: exact obedience.
    Silver bases from census
    The forty silver bases under the north and south frames (vv.24,26) were cast from the census ransom silver (38:25–27) — every Israelite's half-shekel literally supporting the Tabernacle structure.
    The Tabernacle rested on the ransom price of the whole community.

    Reception History

    2 Cor 8:3
    The Macedonian overflow of giving → Paul uses it as the model for the Jerusalem collection. The Tabernacle giving is the OT paradigm for NT community generosity.
    Obedience theme
    The command-execution pattern in Exod 35–40 became a model for understanding the relationship between divine revelation and human response throughout the OT: what God commands, his people execute faithfully.

    Scholarly Debates

    Why Repeat the Tabernacle Instructions in Full?
    Covenant verification
    Childs, Sarna
    The execution chapters confirm every instruction was followed. The reader witnesses total obedience — which is the theological point after the golden calf's total disobedience. The length is the message.
    Two independent sources combined
    Some critical scholars
    The instruction (P source) and execution accounts were originally independent documents combined by a later editor. The repetition reflects source combination rather than intentional literary structure.
    The covenant-verification reading is more satisfying literarily and theologically. The repetition is not accidental but deliberate — it creates the narrative experience of witnessing complete obedience after complete disobedience.
