Exodus 21

Hebrew Servants; Personal Injury Laws; Laws on Oxen

Verses 1–11 — Hebrew Servants
1234567891011

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
21:2

MacArthur: The first covenant law after the Ten Commandments addresses the treatment of Hebrew servants -- those most vulnerable to exploitation. The Book of the Covenant opens at the bottom of the social hierarchy. The covenant community's justice is measured from the bottom up.

21:5-6

MacArthur: The servant who freely chooses to remain in service is marked with an awl through his ear at the door -- a voluntary, permanent covenant bond. This man chose service over freedom because he loved his master. Paul uses this pattern (Phil 1:1) as the image of willing service to Christ.

Hebrew Word Study

מִשְׁפָּטִים mishpāṭîm — judgments / case laws: V.1: The "mishpatim" (judgments/ordinances) that follow the Decalogue are casuistic law — "if/when... then" case-law that applies covenant principles to specific situations. The section covers servants (vv.1-11), violence (12-36), and property (22:1-17). Their arrangement after the Decalogue shows that the Ten Words are principles; the ordinances are their applications. Moses's role as judge (from Exod 18) gives these laws their context: they emerged from real disputes.

עֶבֶד עִבְרִי ʿebed ʿibrî — Hebrew servant: V.2: The law limiting Hebrew servitude to six years — with freedom in the seventh — applies Sabbath logic to persons: as the seventh day brings rest, the seventh year brings release. This is the foundation of the Jubilee legislation (Lev 25) and the Sabbatical year. Jesus reads this liberation principle into his own mission (Luke 4:18-19 quoting Isa 61): the acceptable year of the LORD is the ultimate Jubilee, the permanent release from all servitude.

Historical Context

The Book of the Covenant contains the closest parallels in the Pentateuch to ancient Near Eastern law codes: Code of Hammurabi (c.1750 BC), Laws of Eshnunna, and Hittite Laws. The parallels demonstrate shared legal culture while the differences are theologically significant — Israelite law uniquely protects slaves' rights (vv.2–11), limits punishments more strictly, and grounds all law in the character of the covenant God.

Context

Hebrew slavery in these laws is not chattel slavery — it is debt servitude with a six-year maximum and a jubilee mechanism. The slave who chooses to remain (vv.5–6) does so out of love, not compulsion. The ear-boring ceremony expresses voluntary permanent commitment. The laws for female slaves (vv.7–11) provide protections far exceeding ANE norms — they cannot be sold to foreigners and must be freed if their rights are violated.

Cross-Reference

  • Lev 25:39–55The jubilee expansion of the slavery laws — freedom every fifty years, grounded in the Exodus: "they are my servants whom I brought out of Egypt."
  • Matt 5:38–39Jesus on the lex talionis: "You have heard... an eye for an eye... but I say to you, do not resist the one who is evil." He addresses its misapplication in personal vengeance.
  • Ps 40:6–8The ear-boring imagery: "You have dug ears for me" — the Psalmist uses the voluntary-slave metaphor for joyful covenant obedience. "I delight to do your will, O my God."

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:2

The laws governing Hebrew servants open the Book of the Covenant. Sarna notes the opening with slave laws is not accidental — Israel has just been freed from slavery; the first legislation ensures their own experience shapes their treatment of those in bondage.

21:7-11

The laws governing female Hebrew servants are more protective than those for males — if the master fails to provide food, clothing, or conjugal rights, she goes free without payment. Sarna notes the asymmetry reflects the greater vulnerability of female servants: the law compensates for the power imbalance with stronger protections.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:2–6

The most striking feature is the option for voluntary permanent slavery — the slave who loves his master chooses bondage. Alter notes the law takes the slave's subjective experience seriously. The voluntary slave is not less dignified than the freed one; the dignity is in the choice.

21:7-11

The female servant's protections — if the three conditions are not met, she goes free — establish minimum standards for the servant-wife relationship. Alter notes the law's anthropological realism: it acknowledges that this form of servitude existed and legislates its minimum conditions rather than eliminating it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:2

Calvin: 'It was not God's intention to approve of slavery, but since the condition had already prevailed in the world, he contented himself with correcting its abuses.' The Mosaic law regulates existing social practice to protect those within it.

21:7-11

Calvin on the female servant laws: God did not institute these arrangements but found them already existing and chose to regulate them rather than abolish them immediately. The regulation is mercy applied within an imperfect system: even within unjust structures, the covenant God insists on minimum protections for the vulnerable.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:6

The NET note on 'bring him to the door or the doorpost' discusses whether this refers to the household door or the sanctuary. The note favours the household door — the ceremony is a domestic transaction. The awl-pierced ear was a permanent mark that could not be hidden or removed.

21:10-11

The NET note on the three conditions (food, clothing, conjugal rights) discusses their legal status in the ancient Near East. The note identifies parallels in Mesopotamian law codes and concludes that the Israelite legislation is distinctive in specifying these as legal obligations enforceable by the servant's freedom if violated.

Verses 12–27 — Personal Injuries
12131415161718192021222324252627

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
21:24

MacArthur: Eye for eye, tooth for tooth -- the lex talionis principle establishes proportionality in punishment. The principle is a limit on retaliation, not a licence for it. Jesus's discussion (Matt 5:38-39) addresses the personal application, not the judicial principle.

21:26-27

MacArthur: If a man hits a male or female slave in the eye and destroys it, he must let the slave go free to compensate for the eye. Even the vulnerable have legal protection under covenant law.

Hebrew Word Study

נֶפֶשׁ תַּחַת נֶפֶשׁ nepesh taḥat nepesh — life for life: V.23-25: "Life (nepesh) for life, eye for eye, tooth for tooth." The lex talionis is not a license for revenge but a limit on disproportionate vengeance. It enforces proportionality: the punishment may not exceed the injury. In a legal context (as here), it means courts must calibrate penalties to offenses. Jesus does not abolish it but reframes it: "You have heard that it was said... but I tell you..." — moving from legal minimum to ethical maximum (Matt 5:38-42).

אָוֶן ʾāwen / ʾāmam — harm / injury: V.22-23: The case of injury to a pregnant woman introduces the protection of unborn life in Israelite law. A miscarriage (yātsāʾ yĕlādehā) resulting from a fight incurs a financial penalty; death of the woman incurs life-for-life. Both cases have legal standing. The passage establishes that prenatal life is within the scope of legal protection, making Israelite law more protective of the unborn than most ancient Near Eastern legal codes.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:23–25

The lex talionis formula appears in Hammurabi's Code and Assyrian law. Sarna notes the crucial distinction: ancient Near Eastern parallels apply the principle differently by social class — Israel's version applies the same standard regardless of class. The equality before the law is the covenant's distinctive contribution.

21:23-27

The lex talionis principle — identical punishment for identical injury — is a legal limitation as much as a legal prescription. Sarna notes it prevents escalation: the response must not exceed the injury. The principle of proportionality is the covenant law's contribution to legal ethics across cultures.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:12–17

The death penalty cases establish the moral weight the Book of the Covenant attaches to family relationships and human freedom. Alter notes kidnapping (v.16) is treated as a capital offence equivalent to murder — the forcible removal of freedom is, in the law's moral arithmetic, equivalent to killing.

21:18-22

The bodily injury laws distinguish between immediate death (murder, vv.12-17) and delayed consequences (v.18-19, v.20). Alter notes the distinctions are legally sophisticated: intent, timing, and degree of injury are all relevant factors. The covenant law is not mechanical but attentive to moral complexity.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:24

Calvin on lex talionis: 'The law does not permit private revenge, but orders that judges should take cognizance of wrongs done, and make the punishment equal to the offence.' Jesus in Matt 5:38–39 addresses the misuse of the principle as justification for personal revenge — he does not abolish the judicial principle but corrects its application.

21:24-25

Calvin: The lex talionis does not require literal application in every case — the courts applied monetary equivalents in practice. The principle is proportionality, not literalism. Jesus in the Sermon on the Mount (Matt 5:38-39) corrects the misapplication of this principle as a licence for personal revenge, not the judicial principle itself.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:23

The NET note clarifies that in ancient Israelite legal practice, the lex talionis formula was almost certainly applied as monetary compensation (cf. vv.18–19, 30–32 where monetary payments are the remedy). The formula establishes proportionality as the principle; monetary equivalence is the practical application.

21:24-27

The NET note on the practical application of lex talionis traces the evidence from later Israelite practice that monetary compensation was the standard application. The principle of proportionality (no more than injury for injury) remained; the literal eye-for-eye was reserved for the most extreme cases of deliberate mutilation.

Verses 28–36 — Laws About Oxen; Property and Negligence
282930313233343536

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
21:28

MacArthur: The ox that kills a person must be stoned. Human life has unique dignity because every victim bears the image of God (Gen 9:6). The death of any image-bearer requires accountability.

21:28-36

MacArthur: The ox-goring laws extend covenant ethics to property management and animal stewardship. The owner's prior knowledge of the animal's dangerous behavior introduces the principle of culpable negligence: what you know creates what you owe. Liability follows from awareness.

Hebrew Word Study

גֹּאֵל הַדָּם gōʾēl haddām — avenger of blood / kinsman redeemer of blood: The cities of refuge legislation (anticipating Num 35) arises from the concept of the gōʾēl haddām — the "blood redeemer" whose obligation was to avenge a killed relative. This duty of the nearest kinsman to act on behalf of the dead is the same root as Boaz's role as Ruth's gōʾēl. In both cases, the kinsman-redeemer acts when the nearest relative is unable to act for themselves. Paul reads Christ as the ultimate gōʾēl: the kinsman who acts to redeem what the debtor-sinner could not reclaim.

רֹצֵחַ rōtsēaḥ — murderer / one who slays: V.12-14: The distinction between intentional and unintentional killing (murder vs. manslaughter) is the basis of the cities of refuge institution. The law distinguishes between rātsaḥ (intentional murder) and cases where God "lets it happen" (ʾimmēn hāʾĕlōhîm) — accidental death. This distinction between intention and outcome, and the provision of shelter for those who acted without malice, represents a remarkable sophistication in ancient legal thought.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:35–36

The goring-ox property law provides the first example of comparative negligence liability. Sarna notes the text distinguishes strict liability (known risk) from contributory fault (unknown risk shared equally). This is remarkably sophisticated negligence jurisprudence anticipating modern tort law.

21:33-36

The negligence liability principles in the goring ox and pit-falling laws are the Book of the Covenant's most jurisprudentially sophisticated material. Sarna notes the distinction between known risk (strict liability) and shared negligence (half-and-half) anticipates modern tort law's comparative negligence doctrine by three millennia.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:28–36

The goring ox laws reveal the Book of the Covenant's interest in intention and knowledge as moral categories. Alter notes the law is not mechanically punitive — it reads character and knowledge into its judgments. This is covenant ethics: the inner life matters to the law.

21:33-36

The property damage laws reveal the covenant law's concern for accountability without over-punishment. Alter notes the goring ox laws in particular: the known-dangerous animal incurs full liability; the unknown-dangerous animal incurs shared loss. The law reads moral knowledge into its judgments.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:33–34

Calvin: 'Negligence is here condemned, not design. Whoever through carelessness does harm to another is not exempt from guilt.' The law extends moral responsibility beyond intentional acts to foreseeable consequences of carelessness.

21:33-36

Calvin: The pit and ox laws teach that negligence is morally culpable even without malice. The person who creates foreseeable danger and fails to prevent foreseeable harm bears moral responsibility for the consequence. The covenant law extends moral accountability to failures of care, not only to deliberate harms.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:29

The NET note on 'in the habit of goring' (vayuad) explains legal 'warning' in Israelite law required formal testimony before witnesses — the owner must have been officially informed, not merely aware. This procedural specificity shows the Book of the Covenant's concern for due process.

21:35-36

The NET note on the half-and-half ox loss when the animal's dangerous character was unknown discusses the comparative negligence principle. The note identifies this as one of the most analytically sophisticated legal principles in ancient Near Eastern law — shared loss for shared negligence.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Moses↗ People
Prophet, lawgiver, and covenant mediator
Moses stands at the centre of the Pentateuch’s narrative — the one man who spoke with God face to face (Exod 33:11; Num 12:8) and mediated the Sinai covenant to Israel. In Leviticus he receives the priestly legislation; in Numbers he leads, intercedes, and ultimately fails at Meribah (Num 20). The writer of Hebrews calls him "faithful in all God’s house" (Heb 3:5) while distinguishing him from Christ, the Son over the house.
Aaron↗ People
First high priest of Israel
Aaron, Moses’s brother, is consecrated as Israel’s first high priest in Lev 8–9. He bears the names of the twelve tribes on his breastplate before God and enters the Most Holy Place once a year on Yom Kippur (Lev 16). His failures — the golden calf (Exod 32), challenging Moses’s authority (Num 12), complicity at Meribah (Num 20) — make him the paradigm of the imperfect priest who prefigures a better high priest (Heb 7:26–28).

Miriam↗ People
Prophetess and worship leader; Moses’s sister
Miriam leads Israel’s women in worship after the Red Sea crossing (Exod 15:20) and is called a prophetess. In Num 12 she challenges Moses’s unique prophetic authority alongside Aaron and is struck with ṣāraʿat — only to be healed after Moses’s seven-word intercession. She dies at Kadesh (Num 20:1), her passing noted in two terse Hebrew words before the water crisis.
Joshua↗ People
Moses’s military commander and designated successor
Joshua son of Nun serves as Moses’s lieutenant throughout the wilderness period — commanding at Rephidim (Exod 17), ascending Sinai with Moses (Exod 24), guarding the tent of meeting. In Num 13–14 he stands with Caleb as one of two faithful spies. He is formally commissioned in Num 27 through Moses’s hand-laying and inherits the leadership role that Moses cannot fulfil past the Jordan.

Ancient Sources

Code of Hammurabi
The Babylonian law code (c.1750 BC) contains numerous parallels to Exod 21 — similar case-law format and many of the same situations (goring oxen, injury compensation, slave regulations). The key differences: Hammurabi's code is class-stratified; Exodus equalises punishment regardless of social rank.
The parallels demonstrate shared legal tradition; the differences reveal the covenant's distinctive values.
Hittite Laws
Hittite legal texts address similar cases (homicide, bodily harm, slavery) with striking structural parallels to Exod 21.
The Book of the Covenant participates in the broader ANE legal tradition while transforming it through covenant theology.

Reception History

Luther
The civil law of Israel as the expression of natural law — the lex talionis as the universal principle of proportional justice underlying all just legal systems.
John Calvin
The Book of the Covenant as applied covenant theology — the Decalogue provides the principles; the mišpāṭîm provide the cases. The law is not arbitrary but the expression of God's character in community.

Scholarly Debates

Does the Mosaic Law Approve of Slavery?
Regulation without endorsement
Calvin, most evangelical scholars
The Mosaic law regulates existing social institutions without divine endorsement. Israel's law introduced significant protections (six-year limit, freedom for injury) substantially more humane than surrounding cultures.
Progressive revelation toward abolition
Most contemporary evangelical scholars
The trajectory moves from regulation toward liberation: Jubilee (Lev 25), the prophets, the NT's 'neither slave nor free' (Gal 3:28), and Philemon all point toward abolition as the covenant's ultimate direction.
The Mosaic law's servant regulations were significantly more protective than surrounding ANE codes. The NT's vision extends this trajectory to its logical conclusion.
