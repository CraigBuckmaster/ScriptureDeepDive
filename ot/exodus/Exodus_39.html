Exodus 39

Ephod and Breastpiece Made; Remaining Garments; Inspection and Blessing

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Hebrew Word Study

כְּאֲשֶׁר צִוָּה יְהוָה kĕʾăsher tsiwwāh YHWH — as the LORD commanded: V.1,5,7,21,26,29,31,43: The phrase "as the LORD commanded Moses" (kĕʾăsher tsiwwāh ʾet-mōsheh) appears seven times in Exodus 39 as the Tabernacle garments are completed and inspected. The sevenfold repetition echoes the seven days of creation (Gen 1) — the completion of the Tabernacle is a new creation, ordered and blessed just as the original. When Moses inspected the work and saw that "they had done it just as the LORD commanded" (39:43), he "blessed them" — as God had blessed the creation.

שְׁנֵי תוֹלָעַת shĕnî tôlāʿat — scarlet (crimson worm): V.1,3,5,29: The scarlet thread (shĕnî tôlāʿat — literally "crimson of the worm") woven into the priestly garments is the dye extracted from the cochineal insect (kermes or coccus ilicis). It appears alongside blue and purple in all the sacred textiles. Scarlet appears also in the Passover Rahab narrative (Josh 2:18), in David's lament (2 Sam 1:24), and in Prov 31:21. Isaiah 1:18: "Though your sins are like scarlet (kĕshānî), they shall be white as snow."

Historical Context

None

Context

The ephod and breastpiece (vv.2–21) are described in the same detail as their instructions (Exod 28:6–30). The twelve stones on the breastpiece — one for each tribe, each engraved with a name — represent the entire community carried over the high priest's heart. The shoulder stones carry the names as a "memorial" (v.7) — a reminder before God. Every tribe, every person, is carried into God's presence by the one who enters on their behalf.

Cross-Reference

  • John 17:9,20Jesus' high-priestly prayer: "I pray for them... I pray also for those who will believe." He carries names over his heart before the Father.

Hebrew Word Study

תּוֹלֵדוֹת tôlĕdôt — the account / generations: V.43 (context for completion): "Moses inspected the work and saw that they had done it just as the LORD commanded. So Moses blessed them." The word bārak (to bless) connects Tabernacle-completion to creation-completion: Gen 1:28 (God blessed the created humans), Gen 2:3 (God blessed the seventh day). Moses acts as God's agent of blessing over the work his people have completed. Sacred construction, done in obedience, deserves the same divine approbation as the original creation.

חֹשֶׁן מִשְׁפָּט ḥōshen mishpāṭ — breastpiece of judgment: V.8-21: The breastpiece of judgment (ḥōshen mishpāṭ) bore twelve stones — one for each tribe, engraved with their names. The High Priest carried "the names of the sons of Israel over his heart... as a continuing memorial before the LORD." The people's names were physically present before God whenever the High Priest entered the Most Holy Place. No Israelite was forgotten or anonymous before God: each name was carried by the one who stood for them all.

Historical Context

None

Context

The inspection and blessing of v.43 is the theological climax of the construction narrative. The phrase "just as the Lord had commanded Moses" appears seven times in vv.32–43 — one for each day of creation, one for each dimension of the Tabernacle project. The sevenfold confirmation of obedience is the construction narrative's doxology: Israel did everything God said. After the golden calf — where they did what God did not say — this sevenfold confirmation is profound.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 1:31"God saw all that he had made, and it was very good." Moses' inspection (v.43) deliberately echoes God's creative evaluation. The Tabernacle's construction is a new creation event.
  • Matt 25:21"Well done, good and faithful servant!" Moses' blessing is the OT version of this — the master inspects the servants' work and pronounces blessing for faithful execution.

Ancient Sources

Seven-fold Obedience
The phrase "as the Lord commanded Moses" appears seven times in Exod 39:1–43 (vv.1,5,7,21,26,29,31,42,43 — depending on the text). The sevenfold repetition mirrors the seven days of creation, presenting the Tabernacle's completion as a new creation.
The literary structure of Exod 39 deliberately echoes Gen 1.
Gold Plate Inscription
Archaeological finds of inscribed gold and silver objects from the ancient Near East confirm the practice of engraved dedications. The "Holy to the Lord" inscription on the high priest's forehead may be unique in its use as personal adornment rather than architectural dedication.
The inscription's use is distinctive even within the ANE epigraphic tradition.

'Just as the LORD commanded': How Many Times in Ch.39?
Seven times specifically in ch.39
Sarna, most commentators
The phrase appears seven times in ch.39 (vv.1, 5, 7, 21, 26, 29, 31) — the creation number. The sevenfold completion of the priestly garments mirrors the seven days of creation.
Approximately sixteen times in chs.39–40
Broad count
Counting all instances in the execution section, the phrase appears approximately sixteen times. The cumulative effect confirms total obedience.
