MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
34:6-7

MacArthur: The LORD, the LORD, the compassionate and gracious God, slow to anger, abounding in love and faithfulness... The thirteen attributes are cited or echoed over a dozen times throughout the OT (Num 14:18; Neh 9:17; Ps 86:15; Joel 2:13; Jonah 4:2).

34:9

MacArthur: Moses's prayer acknowledges the sin (stiff-necked), appeals to divine grace (if I have found favour), and grounds the request in covenant identity (take us as your inheritance). A masterpiece of covenant intercession.

Hebrew Word Study

רַחוּם וְחַנּוּן raḥûm wĕḥannûn — compassionate and gracious: V.6-7: "The LORD, the LORD, the compassionate (raḥûm) and gracious (ḥannûn) God, slow to anger, abounding in love and faithfulness, maintaining love to thousands, and forgiving wickedness, rebellion and sin." This divine self-proclamation — the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy in Jewish tradition — is the most cited divine self-description in the OT. It is quoted in Num 14:18; Neh 9:17; Ps 86:15; 103:8; Joel 2:13; Jonah 4:2. It is the theological heart of what YHWH means.

בְּרִית berît — covenant: V.10,27: "I am making a covenant (berît) with you." After the golden calf rupture, God renews the covenant — re-granting what had been forfeited by idolatry. The renewal is not a lesser covenant but the same one, its terms restated and the tablets re-cut by Moses's own hand (v.28). The renewal of covenant after catastrophic betrayal is itself the most powerful statement of divine grace: the relationship survives the worst failure the people can manage.

Context

The thirteen attributes of vv.6–7 are the answer to Moses' request in 33:18 ("show me your glory"). God's glory is not a visual spectacle but a character disclosure. The attributes are cited repeatedly throughout the OT: Num 14:18, Neh 9:17, Ps 86:15, 103:8, 145:8, Joel 2:13, Jon 4:2, Nah 1:3. They became the foundation of Israel's theology and liturgy — the character of God that makes covenant possible despite human faithlessness.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 103:8"The Lord is compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, abounding in love" — a direct quotation of Exod 34:6.
  • John 1:14"Full of grace and truth" — the NT echo of Exod 34:6's "abounding in love (ḥesed) and faithfulness (ʾemet)."
  • Jon 4:2Jonah's complaint about God: "I knew that you are a gracious and compassionate God, slow to anger and abounding in love." He cites Exod 34:6 as the problem — God is too merciful.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
34:27

MacArthur: Write down these words, for in accordance with these words I have made a covenant with you and with Israel. The written covenant is the authoritative document. Both the oral proclamation of the divine character and the written tablets are essential: character and commandment.

34:28

MacArthur: Moses was there with the LORD forty days and forty nights without eating bread or drinking water. Moses's physical dependence on the divine presence during both Sinai periods is the OT's most radical expression of living by divine word, not bread alone (Deut 8:3).

Hebrew Word Study

קָרַן qāran — shining / radiant / horned: V.29-35: "Moses did not know that the skin of his face was shining (qāran)." The verb qāran (to send out rays, to shine) shares a root with qeren (horn). Jerome's Vulgate translated it as "his face had horns" — giving rise to Michelangelo's famous horned Moses. The Hebrew clearly means radiance: Moses's face reflected the divine glory he had encountered. Paul uses this passage in 2 Cor 3:7-18 to contrast the fading glory of the old covenant with the surpassing, unfading glory of the new.

מַסְוֶה masweh — veil: V.33-35: Moses wore a veil over his shining face between the times of speaking with God and with Israel. Paul's allegory in 2 Cor 3:13-18 is careful: Moses veiled his face "so that the Israelites might not see the end of what was passing away." The veil concealed the fading. Christ removes the veil: "whenever anyone turns to the Lord, the veil is taken away." The glory of the new covenant is not hidden but transforming.

Historical Context

None

Context

The renewed covenant (vv.10–28) focuses primarily on the dangers of Canaanite religious influence — the very threat that produced the golden calf. The lessons of ch.32 are immediately applied: no treaties, no intermarriage, no Asherah poles, no other gods. God's jealousy (v.14: "the Lord, whose name is Jealous") is the appropriate response to covenant love that cannot be shared. The three annual festivals (Unleavened Bread, Weeks, Ingathering) frame Israel's calendar with covenant remembrance.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 4:2Jesus fasts forty days and forty nights — echoing Moses' forty days in Exod 34:28.
  • Deut 4:23–24"The Lord your God is a consuming fire, a jealous God." Moses later cites this chapter as the ground for the warning against Canaanite religion.
  • 2 Cor 6:14"Do not be unequally yoked with unbelievers" — the NT application of the treaty prohibition of Exod 34:12.

Verses 29–35 — Moses' Radiant Face; the Veil
29303132333435

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
34:29-30

MacArthur: Moses came down from Mount Sinai unaware that his face was radiant. The involuntary radiance of Moses's face is the physical consequence of sustained divine encounter. Paul (2 Cor 3:7-18) develops this as the contrast between the fading glory of the old covenant and the surpassing, permanent glory of the new.

34:29-35

MacArthur: The radiant face of Moses is unconscious — he does not know his face is shining. The glory is not manufactured display but the overflow of extended divine encounter. The veil is worn not to protect Moses but to protect Israel from the fading of the glory, which Paul (2 Cor 3) interprets as the transience of the Mosaic administration compared to the permanent glory of the new covenant.

Hebrew Word Study

עֲבֹדַת פְּרָזִים ʾabōdat / ḥag — Feast of Weeks / Festival: V.22-24: The three pilgrimage festivals are re-stated after the covenant renewal: Unleavened Bread, Weeks (Shavuot), and Ingathering. Their placement here after the golden calf and covenant renewal underscores that worship — structured, seasonal, embodied encounter — is the purpose of liberation. The Exodus was not an end in itself but the means to a worshipping people. The festivals are the recurring answer to the question "why did God bring you out of Egypt?"

לֹא תִשְׁחַט lōʾ tishḥat — do not slaughter with leaven: V.25: "Do not offer the blood of a sacrifice to me along with anything containing yeast." The prohibition on leaven at sacrifice time (ḥāg hammasôt) is not food law but purity law: leaven represents contamination, puffing up, moral inflation. Paul's metaphor in 1 Cor 5:6-8 draws on this: "A little yeast leavens the whole batch of dough. Get rid of the old yeast... for Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed."

Historical Context

None

Context

The shining face is the physical consequence of sustained encounter with God's glory. Moses did not know his face was radiant (v.29) — the transformation was not self-generated but received. This is the pattern of genuine holiness: it is not performed but reflected. The people who encounter the glory of God are changed by it without knowing it. 2 Cor 3:18 promises all believers this transformation: "we all, who with unveiled faces contemplate the Lord's glory, are being transformed into his image with ever-increasing glory."

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 17:2The Transfiguration: "His face shone like the sun." Jesus on the mountain of transfiguration fulfils what Moses on Sinai foreshadowed.
  • Rev 1:16The glorified Christ: "His face was like the sun shining in all its brilliance." The ultimate radiance beyond Moses' reflected glory.

34:33

The veil (masveh) that Moses puts on after speaking to the people is unique in the OT. Sarna notes it creates a three-mode pattern: bare face (in God's presence), radiant face (descending with the word), veiled face (in regular daily ministry).

34:33-35

The veil's three-state pattern (removed before God, radiating before the people, covered in ordinary life) encodes the covenant's graduated-revelation structure. Sarna notes the veil is not concealment of what Moses lacks but protection of what the people cannot bear. The mediation is pastoral, not hierarchical.

34:33–35

The veil and unveiling pattern enacts the theology of mediated revelation: the people cannot bear the full brilliance of the divine word as Moses receives it; the veil makes the mediated word bearable.

34:33-35

The veil's use creates a visual theology of mediation: the people see Moses' radiant face when he transmits the divine word, then the veil goes on for ordinary interaction. Alter notes the ritual establishes that the divine glory is not permanently visible — it must be re-received in each new encounter and communicated at the moment of transmission.

Ancient Sources

Thirteen Attributes in Judaism
The thirteen divine attributes (raḥûm, ḥannûn, etc.) of Exod 34:6–7 became the most quoted passage in Jewish liturgy. They are recited on Yom Kippur and on every fast day. Every petition for forgiveness in Jewish prayer returns to this passage as its foundation.
The theological centrality of these attributes in Judaism demonstrates their formative power in OT theology.
Paul's Midrash on the Veil
2 Corinthians 3:7–18 is Paul's sustained midrashic interpretation of Exod 34:29–35. His reading is not arbitrary; it follows rabbinic interpretive patterns while applying them to Christ.
2 Corinthians 3 is the most sustained NT engagement with any single Exodus passage.

Reception History

2 Cor 3:7–18
Paul's most extended engagement with a single Exodus passage — the veil of Moses as the type of the veil over Israel's hearts, removed in Christ; the transformation of believers with unveiled faces.
Transfiguration (Matt 17, Mk 9, Lk 9)
Moses appears at the Transfiguration alongside Elijah — the two great Sinai figures. Jesus' face shines like the sun, surpassing the reflected glory of Moses' face. The Transfiguration is the Sinai theophany fulfilled and exceeded.

Scholarly Debates

The Thirteen Attributes: Mercy or Mercy-and-Justice?
Mercy predominates
Most Jewish liturgical use
The list opens with five mercy-attributes and closes with two justice-attributes. Jewish liturgical use emphasises mercy as the foundation of Israel's appeal to God.
Justice limits mercy: both absolute
Calvin, Reformed theology
God's mercy and justice are not in tension — both are fully expressed, but only through the cross where the punishment falls on the substitute rather than the sinner.
The tension is real and productive. The NT's resolution — Christ bears the punishment the forgiven do not receive — is the most satisfying synthesis: both mercy and justice absolute, both fully expressed, meeting in Christ.
