Exodus 14

Pharaoh Pursues; The Sea Divided; Egypt Destroyed

See on Timeline — Exodus See on Map — The Exodus & Plagues
Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1—14 — Pharaoh Pursues; Israel’s Fear; The Lord Will Fight for You

Hebrew Word Study

וְכָבַדְתִּי wĕkābhadtî — I will gain glory: V.4,17-18: "I will gain glory (kābadetî) through Pharaoh." The root kābed (heavy, honoured, glorious) appears repeatedly: God's glory (kābôd) is made heavy and visible at the sea. The same root that described Pharaoh's hardened heart (heḵbed libbô) here becomes the occasion for divine glorification. What Pharaoh made heavy in resistance, God makes heavy in triumph.

יְשׁוּעָה yĕshûʿāh — salvation / deliverance: V.13: "Stand firm and you will see the deliverance (yĕshûʿat) the LORD will bring you today." The word yĕshûʿāh (salvation, rescue) — from the same root as Yeshua/Joshua/Jesus — appears at the Red Sea as the name for what God is about to do. The crossing of the sea is the first great yĕshûʿāh; the NT Gospels present Jesus as the final and universal yĕshûʿāh.

Historical Context

None

Context

Israel's cry (v.10) is fear, not faith — and God acts anyway. The people's complaint (vv.11–12: "Is it because there are no graves in Egypt?") is their first of many wilderness complaints, but it does not delay the rescue. God does not require composure before he saves; he saves the afraid, the complaining, the unworthy. Moses' word — "Fear not, stand firm, see the salvation" — is the first great formula of faith in the face of impossible odds.

Places

Pi Hahiroth / Baal Zephon / Red Sea
Eastern Delta coast
God commands Israel to camp at Pi Hahiroth between Migdol and the sea, opposite Baal Zephon. This specific, apparently trapped position — sea in front, desert on sides, Egyptian army behind — is the divine setup for the greatest redemptive act since creation.
The Sea (Yam Suph)
Red Sea / Reed Sea; possibly northern arm of Red Sea
Yam Suph is traditionally the Red Sea; some scholars identify it with a marshy lake in the Suez region. Whichever identification is correct, the theological point is the same: the sea that blocks escape becomes the instrument of both salvation and judgment.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 43:2"When you pass through the waters, I will be with you." Isaiah draws on the Red Sea crossing as the paradigm for future redemptions.
  • Ps 46:10"Be still, and know that I am God." The imperative silence of Exod 14:14 becomes a Psalm of confidence — stillness as the posture of faith when God fights.
  • 1 Cor 10:1–2Paul identifies the cloud and the sea crossing as baptism — Israel "baptised into Moses" as the type of Christian initiation into Christ.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:5

Pharaoh's change of heart — 'What have we done, letting Israel go?' — is psychologically precise. The loss of 600,000 workers registers only when the workforce is gone. Sarna notes the irony: Pharaoh who has been trying to destroy Israel now grieves its loss as an economic catastrophe. His pursuit is motivated by labour economics as much as military pride. The power that oppressed Israel cannot stop trying to reclaim it even after God has delivered it.

14:13

Moses' 'stand firm' (hityatsvu) is a military term — take your battle position. The command to stand still and watch is itself an act of courage: resisting the instinct to flee requires as much discipline as fighting. Sarna notes the parallel to Isa 30:15 ('in quietness and trust is your strength') — the theological principle that God's deliverance operates through Israel's restrained trust, not through military action.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:11–12

Israel's complaint is structured as a series of rhetorical questions that blame Moses. Alter notes the complaints escalate in specificity: 'not enough graves in Egypt?' then 'what have you done?' then 'is this not what we said in Egypt?' Each question is more accusatory than the last. The people are not asking for information; they are expressing panic through the rhetorical form of accusation. Moses' calm four-part response (vv.13–14) is the counter-rhythm to their panic.

14:19–20

The angel of God moving from before Israel to behind Israel — the pillar of cloud repositioning to stand between Israel and Egypt — is a military manoeuvre in divine form. Alter notes the cloud simultaneously guides Israel (light on their side) and blinds Egypt (darkness on their side). The same divine presence serves opposite functions for the two parties it separates. This is the discrimination motif at its most spatially precise.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:13

Calvin on 'see the salvation of the LORD': 'Moses exhorts them not to rely on their own powers, since God alone was sufficient to protect them.' The command to stand still is the antithesis of self-reliance. Calvin reads it as the paradigmatic posture of faith: not passivity but the active choice to trust rather than panic. The battle belongs to God; Israel's role is to witness, not to fight.

14:21

On the division of the sea: 'God, to destroy all excuse for distrust, displayed his power by an incredible miracle.' Calvin does not attempt to explain the natural mechanism of the event — the miracle is the point. The east wind is mentioned (v.21), but Calvin does not reduce the event to the wind: 'For though the natural means were not wanting, yet the effect far exceeded what nature could have produced.'

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:21

The NET note on the dividing of the sea discusses the natural mechanism: 'God drove the sea back with a strong east wind all that night and turned the sea into dry land.' The wind is the named instrument; the timing, scale, and precision exceed natural explanation. The note discusses the Hort theory (natural seiche wave or wind setdown in a shallow lake) and its strengths and weaknesses. The discrimination — dry land for Israel, muddy ground that stuck Egyptian chariot wheels (v.25) — is the detail that most resists naturalistic explanation.

14:10-14

The NET note on the Israelites' complaint at the Red Sea discusses its function as the first of the wilderness complaints — establishing the murmuring pattern that will recur throughout Numbers. Moses' response (vv.13-14) is the paradigmatic answer to covenant crisis: 'Stand firm and see the salvation the LORD will accomplish.'

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
14:13-14

MacArthur: Do not be afraid. Stand firm and you will see the deliverance the LORD will bring you today. The LORD will fight for you; you need only to be still. The most concentrated statement of faith-in-crisis in the Torah.

14:4

MacArthur: I will gain glory for myself through Pharaoh and all his army, and the Egyptians will know that I am the LORD. The pursuit, the crossing, and the destruction of the Egyptian army are all framed as a single display of divine glory.

Verses 15—31 — The Crossing of the Red Sea; Egypt Destroyed
1516171819202122232425262728293031

Hebrew Word Study

רוּחַ קָדִים rûaḥ qādîm — east wind: V.21: "The LORD drove the sea back with a strong east wind." The rûaḥ (wind/spirit) that divided the waters at creation (Gen 1:2: "the Spirit of God was hovering over the waters") now divides the waters again for the new creation of the nation. Paul reads the crossing as a collective baptism: "all passed through the sea... all were baptized into Moses" (1 Cor 10:1-2) — death and resurrection enacted in water.

אֱמוּנָה ʾĕmûnāh — faith / trust / faithfulness: V.31: "And when the Israelites saw the mighty hand of the LORD displayed against the Egyptians, the people feared the LORD and put their trust in him and in Moses his servant." The verb ʾāman (to believe, trust, be firm) — from which ʾāmēn derives — marks this as the pivotal moment of corporate faith. The same verb will be used of Abraham in Gen 15:6: "He believed the LORD, and he credited it to him as righteousness." At the sea, Israel walks the same path.

Historical Context

None

Context

The closing verse (v.31) is the theological conclusion: Israel saw the great power, feared the LORD, and believed. The sequence matters — seeing produces fear produces faith. This is not blind faith; it is responsive faith, grounded in observable divine action. The Red Sea crossing is the epistemological foundation of Israel's trust in Moses and in God throughout the wilderness — it is what every subsequent complaint forgets and every call to faith remembers.

Places

The Sea — crossing
Sea bed made dry land
The sea divided is the creation motif repeated: God separates waters to make dry ground (Gen 1:9-10; Exod 14:21-22). Israel walks through on dry ground — the same language as creation day three. The Exodus is not merely historical rescue but a new creation act.
Egyptian camp / Sea bed
Israel watching from the other bank
The Egyptians pursue into the sea bed and are destroyed when the waters return. The entire Egyptian chariot force — the military expression of Pharaoh's power — is eliminated in the same sea that Israel crossed safely. Geography of salvation is simultaneously geography of judgment.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 15:1–21The Song of Moses immediately follows — the crossing becomes the first great poem of Israel's worship.
  • Isa 51:10"Was it not you who dried up the sea... that made the depths of the sea a way for the redeemed to pass over?" Isaiah uses the crossing as the paradigm for new-covenant redemption.
  • Rev 15:3The redeemed in heaven sing "the song of Moses and the Lamb" — the Red Sea Song (Exod 15) and the cross are the two great redemption events the song celebrates.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:25

The detail of the chariot wheels 'coming off' (wayyasar) is a significant narrative particularity. Sarna notes that ancient chariot wheels were pinned with a linchpin that could dislodge in soft ground. The Egyptian army, which had the tactical advantage of chariots, is defeated precisely by the advantage of those chariots becoming liabilities in the wet ground that the returning waters produced. The superior weapon becomes the instrument of defeat.

14:31

The statement 'they believed in the LORD and in Moses his servant' is the first time Israel's belief in Moses is explicitly stated. Sarna notes the parallel to Exod 4:31 (they believed when Moses performed the signs in Egypt), but there the subsequent chapters showed that belief to be shallow — it evaporated when the bricks-without-straw hardship came. Whether this belief will prove durable is the question the wilderness narrative answers.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:27–28

The return of the waters is narrated with absolute economy: Moses stretches out his hand, the sea returns, the Egyptians flee, the LORD throws them into the sea, the waters return and cover the chariots and horsemen — all the host of Pharaoh. 'Not even one of them remained.' Alter notes the finality of the negative universal — a narrative technique that closes a chapter of the story permanently. Egypt will not pursue Israel again.

14:31

The chapter's final verse is the pivot between the liberation narrative and the worship narrative (ch.15). 'Feared the LORD and believed in him and in Moses his servant' — three theological responses that constitute the proper outcome of the Exodus. Alter notes that the fear-belief-trust sequence is the same sequence the burning bush was designed to produce in Moses (3:6 — Moses fears; 4:31 — Israel believes). What God began with one man, he has now accomplished with a nation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:28

Calvin on the destruction of the Egyptian army: 'The end of the wicked, who obstinately fight against God, is always miserable... God suffered Pharaoh to proceed in his obstinacy, only that the punishment might correspond to the atrocity of his crimes.' The proportion between sin and judgment is Calvin's theodicy of the sea crossing: Egypt's systematic oppression of Israel is met by the complete destruction of the military instrument of that oppression.

14:31

On Israel's fear and belief: 'They are now taught, by actual experience, what they could not be persuaded to believe by words.' Experience produces what exhortation could not. The Exodus is God's pedagogical method — demonstration creates the faith that proclamation alone cannot. Calvin extends this: the sacraments (baptism and the Lord's Supper) are God's ongoing pedagogy, accommodating the weakness of faith that requires visible signs.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:24

The NET note on 'the morning watch' (ashmoret haboqer) explains this is the last watch of the night (approximately 2–6 AM). The divine intervention through the pillar of fire and cloud occurs in the pre-dawn darkness — the moment of deepest darkness before light. The note connects this to the Passover night timing (midnight) and to the pattern of divine intervention in the darkest moment.

14:26-29

The NET note on 'the water flowed back and covered the chariots and horsemen — the entire army of Pharaoh' discusses the completeness of the military destruction. The note traces the theological significance: the army that had enslaved Israel for generations is entirely destroyed in a single night, making Egypt's military power permanently irrelevant to the covenant people.

Ancient Sources

Egyptian Chariots
New Kingdom Egyptian chariotry was among the most advanced military technology of the ancient world — iron-fitted wheels, trained horses, composite bows. The army pursuing Israel represented the full military might of the ancient superpower.
The destruction of this force in the sea is presented as the definitive demonstration that Egypt's greatest weapon is no match for God.
Poem of Pentaur
Egyptian war poetry (e.g., Battle of Kadesh) celebrated Pharaoh's personal military victories. The silence about the Red Sea catastrophe is the inverse of this tradition — what is not recorded is as significant as what is.
Egyptian archival silence about the Exodus is consistent with how they treated defeats.

Reception History

Origen, Homilies on Exodus
The Red Sea crossing as baptism — Israel passes through water, is born as a new people on the other side; Pharaoh's army (the power of sin and death) is drowned. The typology is developed extensively in patristic literature.
Martin Luther King Jr., "I've Been to the Mountaintop" (1968)
The Red Sea crossing as the paradigm of liberation from oppression — the most influential modern reception of Exodus 14 in the African-American freedom tradition.

Scholarly Debates

The Crossing of the Sea: Historical and Geographical Questions
Traditional: miraculous crossing of deep water
MacArthur, evangelical consensus
The 'walls of water on the right hand and on the left' (14:22, 29) describe deep water divided supernaturally. The Egyptian army drowned in returning waters. The event is miraculous and historically particular. The Song of the Sea (ch.15) celebrates it as an unambiguous act of divine power.
Natural event: wind setdown in shallow lake
Hort, Nof and Paldor (1992)
A strong east wind could produce a wind setdown in a shallow lake (like Lake Manzala in the eastern Delta), exposing a ridge of dry land. Israel crosses; the wind stops; Egyptians drown in the returning water. The event is real but naturalistic. This model fits the yam suph = Reed Sea reading.
Theological narrative: historically grounded but not literally divisible sea
Some critical scholars
The crossing narrative is a theological celebration of a real historical event (perhaps Israel escaped Egypt through a providential circumstance) that has been amplified into cosmic myth in the Song of Moses. The 'walls of water' are poetic, not descriptive.
The 'walls of water' language (14:22, 29) and the Song of Moses' cosmic imagery (15:8: 'the floods stood up in a heap') push against purely naturalistic readings. The event is presented as miraculous throughout the OT tradition (Ps 77:16–20; 114:3–5; Isa 51:9–10). Most evangelical scholars accept a miraculous crossing while acknowledging we cannot identify the exact location.
