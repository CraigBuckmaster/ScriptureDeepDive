Exodus 17

Water from the Rock; The Battle with Amalek

See on Map — Wilderness Wandering
Verses 1–7 — Water from the Rock at Massah and Meribah
1234567

Places

Rephidim
Sinai Peninsula; near Horeb
Rephidim is the last campsite before Sinai (19:1-2 shows the next stop is the Wilderness of Sinai). The water crisis at Massah and Meribah ("testing" and "quarrelling") occurs here. The place names become permanent theological markers: Israel's failure at the water rock is memorialized in the names of the site.
Horeb (rock at Horeb)
Sinai / Mount of God
Moses strikes the rock "at Horeb" — the mountain of God, where the burning bush appeared and where the law will be given. The water from the Horeb rock is water from the covenant mountain. Paul (1 Cor 10:4) identifies the rock as Christ.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
17:6

MacArthur: Strike the rock, and water will come out of it for the people to drink. Paul (1 Cor 10:4) identifies the rock as Christ. The water from the rock is a picture of the living water that Christ gives to those who are spiritually thirsty (John 7:37-38).

17:7

MacArthur: Moses named the place Massah (testing) and Meribah (quarreling). The names are the theological commentary: they tested him by asking Is the LORD among us or not? The question of divine presence is the wilderness community's recurring crisis of faith.

Hebrew Word Study

צָמָא tsāmāʾ — thirst: V.3: Israel's thirst in the wilderness becomes the test of trusting memory: can they trust the God who provided manna to provide water? Their answer — "Why did you bring us up out of Egypt to make us and our children and livestock die of thirst?" — frames the Exodus as rescue into danger rather than rescue into safety. Every desert thirst in Scripture carries this theological weight: thirst is the occasion for either faith or murmuring.

מַסָּה וּמְרִיבָה Massāh ûMĕrîbāh — Testing and Quarreling: V.7: Moses names the place Massah (testing) and Meribah (quarreling) "because the Israelites quarreled and because they tested the LORD, asking, 'Is the LORD among us or not?'" The names become watchwords for faithless provocation in the Psalms: Ps 95:8-9 ("Do not harden your hearts as you did at Meribah... where your ancestors tested me") is quoted in Heb 3:7-11 as the defining warning against apostasy.

Historical Context

None

Context

The question "Is the LORD among us or not?" (v.7) is the sharpest statement of wilderness doubt in Exodus. After ten plagues, the Red Sea, the Song of the Sea, Marah, and manna — Israel still asks whether God is present. This is not one complaint among many; it is the root complaint: Is God actually here? Moses' anger (v.4: "they are almost ready to stone me") shows the crisis has reached its peak. God's answer is to stand on the rock himself — his presence demonstrated in the water that flows from it.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 95:7–8The Psalm uses Meribah as the warning: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts, as at Meribah." Heb 3–4 quotes this extensively.
  • 1 Cor 10:4Paul: "they drank from the spiritual Rock that followed them, and the Rock was Christ" — the typological identification.
  • Num 20:8–12Moses strikes the rock at Meribah a second time (disobedience) instead of speaking to it — the failure that costs him entrance to Canaan.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:2

The word 'quarrel' (rib) in 'Why do you quarrel with me?' is the language of a legal dispute — Israel is bringing a formal charge against Moses (and implicitly against God). Sarna notes this legal register is significant: the wilderness complaints are not merely emotional outbursts but formal challenges to God's covenant faithfulness. The implied accusation is that God brought Israel into the wilderness to kill them — a breach of the covenant obligation to provide.

17:6

The rock at Horeb connects this episode to the burning bush theophany (3:1, 12) — both occur at Horeb. Sarna notes the continuity: the God who revealed himself at Horeb as the I AM now stands at Horeb to provide water for his people. The mountain of revelation is also the mountain of provision. The staff Moses uses to strike the rock is the same staff used in the plagues — the instrument of judgment becomes the instrument of grace.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:2–4

The escalation of the complaint at Massah/Meribah reaches its most threatening form: 'They were almost ready to stone me' (v.4). Moses' prayer is a cry of personal danger. Alter notes this is the first time Moses explicitly fears for his physical safety from his own people. The complaint has crossed from theological challenge to physical threat. The leader's vulnerability is real; the provision must come quickly.

17:6

The command 'strike the rock' with the staff used to strike the Nile (v.5: 'your staff with which you struck the Nile') creates a precise parallel: the staff that turned water to blood now draws water from rock. The instrument of judgment in Egypt becomes the instrument of grace in the wilderness. The reversal is deliberate — what was weapon becomes gift.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:3

Calvin on the water complaint: 'It was not so much thirst as rebellion that oppressed them.' The physical need is real, but Calvin identifies the theological content of the complaint: they are challenging God's intention toward them. The question 'Is the LORD among us or not?' (v.7) is the complaint's true content. Physical need is the occasion; theological doubt is the substance.

17:6

Calvin on the rock: 'God manifests that no obstacle can prevent him from supplying his people with whatever they need.' The rock as source of water is the paradigmatic impossibility overcome by divine provision. Calvin quotes Paul's application (1 Cor 10:4) as the completed typological meaning: 'Christ was the spiritual rock which followed them' — the divine provision in the wilderness was a sacramental form of Christ's sustaining grace.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:6

The NET note on the rock at Horeb discusses Paul's typological use in 1 Cor 10:4. The note also notes the Numbers 20:1–13 parallel, where Moses strikes the rock again at Meribah Kadesh — but this time without divine sanction (God commanded him to speak, not strike). The note discusses the two Meribah accounts (Exod 17 and Num 20) as either two distinct events or variant traditions of the same event, with most conservative scholars accepting two distinct incidents.

17:7

The NET note on Massah and Meribah — 'testing' and 'quarrelling' — traces these place names through Ps 95:8 ('do not harden your hearts as at Meribah, as you did at Massah in the wilderness') and Heb 3:8. The episode becomes the canonical model for the hardness that rejects divine provision and provision-tests the God who provides.

Verses 8–16 — The Battle with Amalek; Moses' Raised Arms
8910111213141516

Places

Rephidim battlefield
Sinai Peninsula
The battle against Amalek is fought at Rephidim while Israel is still in the wilderness, before reaching Sinai. The hilltop where Moses stands with his arms raised overlooks the valley battle — the first military encounter after the Exodus.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
17:12

MacArthur: Aaron and Hur held Moses's hands up -- one on each side. The battle won by sustained intercessory prayer requires community support. No intercessor can sustain the effort alone; the hands that grow weary need to be held up by others.

17:15

MacArthur: Moses built an altar and called it YHWH Nissi -- The LORD is my Banner. The covenant God is both the cause and the guarantor of covenant victory.

Hebrew Word Study

נֵס nēs — banner / standard: V.15: After Israel's victory over Amalek (sustained by Moses's raised hands), Moses builds an altar and calls it "YHWH Nissi" — the LORD is my banner. The nēs (standard/banner) was the rally-point for troops and the symbol of military identity. God as Israel's nēs means he is the source of their victory and the centre around which they gather. Isaiah uses the same word for the Messianic "root of Jesse" who will be a banner for the nations (Isa 11:10).

אֲמָלֵק ʿĂmālēq — Amalek: V.8-16: The Amalekites who attacked Israel at Rephidim became the paradigm of all opposition to God's people: they struck "the stragglers at the rear" (Deut 25:18) when Israel was exhausted. God's declaration that "war against Amalek" would continue "from generation to generation" elevated them to a theological category: the persistent enemy of the covenant people. Saul's failure to fully defeat them (1 Sam 15) is the act that cost him the kingdom.

Historical Context

None

Context

The Amalek battle is the first military engagement of Israel as a nation. Joshua commands in the valley; Moses intercedes on the hill with the staff of God. The correlation between Moses' raised arms and Israel's prevailing is the chapter's theological lesson: the battle is won by intercession, not by military force alone. Aaron and Hur holding up Moses' arms is the first image of corporate intercession — the community sustaining the intercessor so the prayer can continue.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 25:17–18Amalek attacked the stragglers — the weakest — and "did not fear God." This characterisation grounds the permanent enmity.
  • 1 Sam 15Saul's failure to destroy Amalek as commanded — the consequence of the Rephidim decree echoes centuries later.
  • Esth 3:1Haman the Agagite — descendant of Amalek's king Agag — the Amalek conflict persisting to the Persian period.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:9

Joshua appears here for the first time — identified simply as 'Joshua son of Nun.' His selection to lead the fighting force while Moses intercedes on the hill establishes the Moses-Joshua relationship: Moses is the prayer-intercessor and covenant mediator; Joshua is the military executive. The division of roles prefigures the entirety of their working relationship and the succession in which Joshua leads the conquest that Moses initiates.

17:15

The altar named YHWH Nissi ('the LORD is my banner/standard') is the fifth divine name in the Pentateuch. Sarna notes that nissi (my banner) is a military term — the standard around which troops rally. The altar commemorates the victory by naming God as the rally-point of Israel's military identity. Every future battle is fought under this banner — YHWH is Israel's standard, not human military strategy.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:11–12

The raised-and-lowered hands narrative is one of the Bible's strangest battle accounts. Israel's military success is tied not to tactics or strength but to the posture of an old man on a hill. Alter notes the narrative's theological deliberateness: it refuses to explain the military victory in military terms. The battle's outcome is entirely determined by the intercessory connection — an impossible causation that requires faith to accept.

17:14-16

The YHWH Nissi altar and the divine war against Amalek 'from generation to generation' establish a permanent enmity that will surface repeatedly in Israel's history (Saul, 1 Sam 15; Haman the Agagite, Esth 3:1). Alter notes the episode is not merely historical but eschatological — the war against Amalek is part of the long arc of covenant history.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:11

Calvin on Moses' raised hands: 'The stretching forth of the hands of Moses was a kind of prayer.' The raised hands are intercessory prayer rendered physically — a visible act whose effect is spiritual and military simultaneously. Calvin uses this to establish the connection between prayer and providence: human outcomes are determined by divine response to human prayer. The battle of Rephidim is the paradigm case.

17:14

Calvin on the written record: 'Moses was commanded to write this history, in order to show that the enmity between Israel and Amalek was of divine appointment.' The command to write is a command to permanence — the divine verdict on Amalek is not temporary anger but perpetual judgment, recorded and transmitted. Calvin notes this establishes the principle that some divine judgments are not negotiable with time.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:12

The NET note on Aaron and Hur supporting Moses' arms discusses the episode's prayer typology. The note cites Jas 5:16–17 (Elijah's effective prayer) as the NT counterpart. The physical support of the intercessor's arms is a striking image of communal prayer sustaining the individual intercessor. The note also notes Hur's only named appearance here; he appears once more in 24:14 and is associated with the craftsman Bezalel in 31:2.

17:11-16

The NET note on the battle outcome's dependence on Moses' raised hands discusses the intercessory/symbolic dimension. The note surveys interpretations: is the raised staff (17:9) the key, or Moses' hands, or the combined action? The ambiguity may be deliberate — the victory belongs to YHWH; Moses' role is to keep covenant attention directed upward.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Moses↗ People
Prophet, lawgiver, and covenant mediator
Moses stands at the centre of the Pentateuch’s narrative — the one man who spoke with God face to face (Exod 33:11; Num 12:8) and mediated the Sinai covenant to Israel. In Leviticus he receives the priestly legislation; in Numbers he leads, intercedes, and ultimately fails at Meribah (Num 20). The writer of Hebrews calls him "faithful in all God’s house" (Heb 3:5) while distinguishing him from Christ, the Son over the house.
Aaron↗ People
First high priest of Israel
Aaron, Moses’s brother, is consecrated as Israel’s first high priest in Lev 8–9. He bears the names of the twelve tribes on his breastplate before God and enters the Most Holy Place once a year on Yom Kippur (Lev 16). His failures — the golden calf (Exod 32), challenging Moses’s authority (Num 12), complicity at Meribah (Num 20) — make him the paradigm of the imperfect priest who prefigures a better high priest (Heb 7:26–28).

Ancient Sources

Amalekite Identity
The Amalekites are identified in Gen 36:12 as descendants of Esau — Edomites who became nomadic raiders in the Negev and Sinai. Their attack on Israel at Rephidim establishes the long antagonism documented through Judges, Samuel, and Esther.
The Amalek conflict has deep genealogical and geographic roots in the patriarchal narrative.
Intercession and Battle
Ancient Near Eastern texts frequently describe royal or priestly intercession as determining military outcomes. Moses' raised staff is the Israelite equivalent — divine power mediated through the covenant representative.
The battle-by-intercession pattern is culturally intelligible in its ANE context while being theologically distinctive.

Reception History

Origen, On Prayer
Moses' raised arms as the pre-eminent OT type of prayer — specifically of Christ's arms extended on the cross. "The cross is the eternal intercession."
Chrysostom
Aaron and Hur holding Moses' arms as the model of corporate prayer — the church holds up its intercessors; no one bears the weight of intercession alone.

Textual Notes

Scholarly Debates

Exodus 17 and Numbers 20: One Meribah or Two?
Two distinct events
Calvin, MacArthur, most evangelical scholars
Exodus 17 (Rephidim, early in the wilderness) and Numbers 20 (Kadesh, forty years later) are two separate incidents. The similar features (water, rock, complaint, Meribah) reflect recurring patterns rather than duplication. Moses' striking the rock in Num 20 is a new act of disobedience, not the same act as Exod 17.
Duplicate accounts of one event
Many source critics
The two accounts share too many features (location names, staff, complaint, divine provision from rock) to be independent events. They represent two variant traditions (J and P) of the same incident that were preserved separately and appear in different positions in the canonical narrative.
The conservative reading is historically plausible — water crises recur in the wilderness, and the place-name Meribah (quarrelling) could have been applied to more than one location. The critical reading requires a documented source divergence. The theological implications differ: if two events, Moses' sin in Num 20 is a new failure; if one, the two accounts must be harmonised differently.
