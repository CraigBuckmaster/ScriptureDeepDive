Exodus 7

Aaron's Staff; Plague One — Water to Blood

Verses 1–9 — Aaron as Moses’ Prophet; Their Roles Before Pharaoh
123456789

Hebrew Word Study

נָבִיא nābîʾ — prophet: V.1: "I have made you like God to Pharaoh, and your brother Aaron will be your prophet (nābîʾ)." This is the earliest definition of prophetic function in the OT: the prophet speaks what the one with authority has given him to speak. Aaron's role anticipates all prophetic vocation — the prophet is the mouthpiece of a higher word, not the originator of his own message.

חָזַק ḥāzaq — hardened / strengthened: V.3: "I will harden Pharaoh's heart (ḥizzaqtî ʾet-leb parʿōh)." The verb ḥāzaq (to be strong, firm, hard) is used differently in different plague texts — sometimes God hardens (chs 7-10), sometimes Pharaoh hardens himself (chs 8-9). The theological point: Pharaoh's own resistance reveals his character while simultaneously serving God's purposes for multiplying signs and witnesses (Exod 9:16; Rom 9:17).

Historical Context

Egyptian magicians' ability to manipulate serpents is documented — a text from the First Intermediate Period describes a magician handling serpents. The uraeus cobra was the symbol of pharaonic power and divine protection. The staff-to-serpent sign directly confronts Egypt's most potent religious symbol: YHWH's serpent swallows Pharaoh's serpent.

Context

The framework speech (vv.1–7) is crucial for reading all ten plagues. Three elements: (1) purpose — "the Egyptians shall know that I am the LORD"; (2) mechanism — the hardening; (3) scope — "great acts of judgment." The plagues are not primarily punishments but revelations. Each plague targets a domain Egypt claims is controlled by one of its gods — YHWH systematically dismantles the Egyptian theological worldview domain by domain.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 9:17–18Paul uses the hardening of Pharaoh's heart to discuss divine sovereignty and human responsibility — both are real; neither cancels the other.
  • Rev 16The seven bowl judgments echo the ten plagues — blood (bowl 2), darkness (bowl 5), etc. Exodus's plague sequence is the typological template for eschatological judgment.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:1

Sarna notes the designation of Moses as 'elohim to Pharaoh' is unique in the OT. Elohim is used here in its sense of 'divine being' or 'one with divine authority' — Moses is the representative of the divine in the confrontation with Pharaoh. The phrasing inverts Pharaoh's claim to divine status: the one Pharaoh claims to be (god-king), Moses actually is — not inherently, but by divine appointment for this encounter.

7:11

The Egyptian magicians who replicate Moses' signs are called chakhamim (wise men) and mekhashfim (sorcerers/enchanters). Sarna notes that Egyptian religious texts describe elaborate ritual magic involving serpent transformation. The Torah does not dismiss Egyptian magic as illusion; it acknowledges its reality while demonstrating its subordination to the power of YHWH. The theological claim is not that Egyptian magic is fake but that it is outmatched.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:3–5

The purpose statement frames the plague narrative as a demonstration project. Alter notes the three-part audience: Pharaoh (who will not listen), Egypt (who will know), and implicitly Israel (who must witness). The plagues are simultaneously a judgment on Egypt, a liberation of Israel, and a revelation of divine character to all. The multiple purposes explain why the process is prolonged rather than immediate — the demonstration must be complete.

7:12

Aaron's rod swallowing the Egyptian rods is a small but pointed victory — a preview of what the full plague sequence will accomplish. The narrative presents it without commentary; the swallowing speaks for itself. The image is structurally precise: just as the staff consumes the staffs, the Exodus will consume Egypt's power over Israel. The sign is proleptic.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1

Calvin on Moses as 'god to Pharaoh': 'Moses is called the god of Pharaoh because he is the interpreter and messenger of the Most High God, and is armed with his power.' The role is functional, not ontological — Moses represents God's authority in this specific confrontation. Calvin extends the principle: the minister of God's word always brings divine authority to bear, not personal authority.

7:11–12

Calvin on the magicians: 'I have no doubt that Satan aided them to perform these things.' He takes the magicians' power seriously — it is real, but demonic. The theological point is that demonic power is genuine but limited; it can imitate but cannot surpass divine creation. When Aaron's rod swallows the magicians' rods, the limit of counterfeit power is publicly demonstrated.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:3

The NET note on 'harden' (qasheh, 'make hard/stiff') here is a third hardening verb (the others being chazaq and kabed). The note explains all three convey related ideas of resolute refusal — the variation in vocabulary reflects the richness of Hebrew idiom rather than theological difference. The accumulation of hardening language across the plague narrative underscores the total, comprehensive nature of Pharaoh's resistance.

7:11

The NET note on the Egyptian magicians references the tradition preserved in 2 Tim 3:8 that names them Jannes and Jambres. These names appear in post-biblical Jewish sources (Dead Sea Scrolls, Targum Jonathan) and in Paul's letter. The note discusses whether the names were known from lost sources or represent later legendary development.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
7:3-5

MacArthur: I will harden Pharaoh's heart, and the Egyptians will know that I am the LORD. The purpose of the plagues is dual: Israel's deliverance and Egypt's instruction. The multiplied signs proclaim YHWH's identity to the watching world.

7:12

MacArthur: Aaron's staff swallowed the magicians' staffs. Yet Pharaoh's heart was hardened. The sign was given and rejected. Hardening is not the absence of evidence but the refusal of evidence.

Verses 10—25 — The First Plague: Water Turned to Blood

Hebrew Word Study

תַּנִּין tannîn — serpent / sea monster: V.9-12: Aaron's staff becomes a tannîn — a serpent or sea-creature (the same word used for sea monsters in Gen 1:21; Ps 74:13; Isa 27:1). When Aaron's serpent swallows the Egyptian magicians' serpents, it signals YHWH's sovereignty over chaos and the forces Egypt's magic tried to manipulate. Egypt's power is not matched but consumed.

חַרְטֻמִּים ḥarṭummîm — magicians / sacred scribes: The Egyptian magicians who replicate Moses and Aaron's signs are ḥarṭummîm — likely lector-priests with access to magical texts. They succeed with the first two signs (staff to serpent, blood, frogs) but fail at the gnats (v.18). Their failure at the third plague marks the tipping point: "This is the finger of God." Egyptian sacred power is exposed as finite before YHWH's infinite authority.

Historical Context

None

Context

The first plague targets the Nile — Egypt's most sacred resource and the source of its agricultural power. The Egyptian gods of the Nile (Hapi, Osiris) cannot protect their domain. Fish die; water is undrinkable for seven days (v.25). The magicians' ability to replicate the plague (v.22) does not diminish YHWH — they cannot reverse it, only add to the problem. Pharaoh's response (v.23: "he did not take even this to heart") sets the pattern for the entire plague sequence: each judgment is met with hardened refusal.

Cross-Reference

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    7:17

    The first plague's theological target is unmistakable. Sarna notes that Hapi, the god of the Nile inundation, was one of the most important deities in the Egyptian pantheon. The annual Nile flood was worshipped as the source of agricultural life. Turning the Nile to blood was not merely a natural catastrophe but a theological statement: the god of Egypt's life-source cannot protect its own domain.

    7:24

    The Egyptians digging around the Nile for water demonstrates two things: the plague's severity (the river itself was undrinkable) and human ingenuity under divine judgment (they found groundwater). Sarna notes this small detail grounds the narrative in realistic detail — the plague did not make all water unavailable, just the Nile itself. The suffering was severe but not immediately lethal.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    7:20–21

    The plague's execution is narrated with deliberate economy: Moses does as commanded, the Nile turns to blood, the fish die, the water stinks, the Egyptians cannot drink. Five consequences in rapid succession. Alter notes the pace — no pause, no Pharaoh's reaction, no dialogue — until the single sentence about the magicians (v.22). The brevity of the execution matches the definitiveness of the act.

    7:22-25

    The magicians' ability to replicate the first plague — turning water to blood — is the narrative's first indication that they can duplicate but not reverse divine signs. Alter notes the Pharaoh-hardens pattern: each plague that the magicians replicate allows Pharaoh to dismiss the sign as non-unique. The replication is a mercy that becomes a hardness.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    7:20

    Calvin on the first plague: 'God chose the Nile rather than any other water because it was held in the greatest veneration by the Egyptians. He signified thereby that the most holy things of Egypt were the objects of his wrath.' The theological targeting is precise: the sacred is judged first. The Nile's sanctity made its defilement the most powerful possible statement of divine superiority over Egyptian religion.

    7:20-22

    Calvin: The magicians replicated the miracle not to vindicate Moses but to harden Pharaoh's heart. God permitted this satanic replication to demonstrate that he would vindicate his servant through a power that no human imitation could match. The greater the opposition, the greater the vindication.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    7:19

    The NET note on 'all their pools of water' (mikvehem) discusses the geography of Egyptian water storage: the Nile, its canals, the pools (reservoirs), and vessels of wood and stone. The scope of the plague is comprehensive — not just the main river but all water storage. The note also discusses the magicians' replication: if all water was already blood, what water did the magicians use? The text may imply they found water not yet affected, or the replication was symbolic rather than large-scale.

    7:20-25

    The NET note on the seven-day duration after the plague discusses the theological significance: the Nile was sacred to Egypt. Converting it to blood for seven full days was not a single act but a sustained declaration that YHWH's power over the sacred river was complete and prolonged.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    7:20-21

    MacArthur: The Nile turned to blood -- the river Egypt worshipped as the god Hapi was turned into death. The first plague targets the source of Egypt's life and the object of its worship simultaneously. Every plague has a target in the Egyptian pantheon.

    7:14-25

    MacArthur: The blood of the Nile strikes Egypt's most sacred geographic feature. The Nile was worshipped as Hapy, the god of flood and fertility. Turning it to blood was a theological confrontation before it was an environmental judgment. YHWH's first blow lands on what Egypt considered most divine.

    Ancient Sources

    Ipuwer Papyrus
    Egyptian lamentation text describing national catastrophe including a river of blood, widespread death, and social collapse. Disputed dating (c.1850 or 1650 BC).
    Whether or not directly related to the Exodus plagues, it demonstrates Egypt's literary capacity to describe such catastrophe.
    Egyptian Serpent Cult
    The uraeus cobra adorned Pharaoh's crown as the divine protector. Snake-handling by Egyptian priests is documented in magical papyri.
    Aaron's serpent swallowing Pharaoh's is a deliberate theological challenge to Egyptian royal theology.

    Scholarly Debates

    Natural vs. Supernatural: The Nature of the Plagues
    Purely supernatural
    MacArthur, traditional reading
    Each plague is a direct divine act with no natural mechanism. The timing, specificity, and discrimination (Goshen spared in plagues 4–10) require direct divine causation. The plagues are signs precisely because they transcend natural explanation.
    Naturalistic cascade
    Greta Hort (1957)
    Hort proposed that each plague follows naturally from the previous: unusual Nile algae (blood-red) → frog exodus → dying frogs → flies and disease → etc. The sequence is climatologically plausible for Egypt. Divine causation operates through natural processes rather than suspending them.
    Theological signs with natural elements
    Sarna, Fretheim, Enns
    The plagues may involve natural phenomena divinely intensified and directed. The theological significance is in the timing, the discrimination between Egypt and Goshen, and the interpretation provided by Moses. God works through nature without being limited to its normal patterns.
    The discrimination between Egypt and Goshen in plagues 4–10 is the strongest argument against a purely naturalistic reading — natural processes do not respect political boundaries. The supernatural timing and the specific interpretive framework (these are acts of YHWH) are equally resistant to naturalistic reduction. Most conservative scholars accept supernatural causation while acknowledging the plagues may have had natural expressions.
