Exodus 8

Plagues Two through Four — Frogs, Gnats, Flies

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
8:10

MacArthur: Pharaoh set the time for the removal of the frogs -- and Moses agreed, demonstrating confidence that the miracle would occur exactly as promised. The willingness to let Pharaoh set the terms heightens the sign's evidential force.

8:18-19

MacArthur: The magicians tried to produce gnats by their secret arts, but they could not. This is the finger of God. The magicians' limit is reached; they can replicate but cannot originate miracles.

Hebrew Word Study

צְפַרְדֵּעַ tsepardēaʿ — frogs: The second plague fills Egypt with frogs — an ironic assault on Heqet, the Egyptian goddess of fertility depicted as a frog-headed woman. The plague weaponizes a symbol of Egyptian divine power. When Pharaoh asks Moses to pray away the frogs, Moses asks "when?" — Pharaoh says "tomorrow." A puzzling choice: why not immediately? Possibly because to say "today" would be to concede divine power; "tomorrow" is face-saving.

עֲרֹב ʿārob — swarms / flies: V.20-24: The fourth plague — ʿārob, usually translated "flies" or "swarms" — marks a new phase: God distinguishes (hiplā) between Goshen and Egypt. For the first time, Israel is protected. The Hebrew ʿārob may denote a mixture of insects (LXX: "dog fly"). More significant: the distinction between Egypt and Goshen makes visible God's covenantal separation of his people, anticipating the Passover distinction.

Historical Context

Frogs (Rana mascareniensis) emerge from the Nile in large numbers after the annual flood — the plague is a hypernatural version of a known natural phenomenon. The frog goddess Heqet was worshipped especially in the Delta region. Gnats/mosquitoes (kinnim) are endemic to the Nile Valley. Flies (ʿārōb, possibly the dog-fly or horsefly) are a significant summer pest. The cascade of plagues follows a natural sequence: blood → frogs → insects — suggesting a chain of related ecological consequences.

Context

The magicians' failure at plague three is the turning point of the contest. They could replicate blood (7:22) and frogs (8:7) — adding to the problem — but they cannot produce gnats. Their "finger of God" testimony is the first crack in Egypt's theological confidence. Pharaoh's response (v.19: he does not listen) shows that even credible testimony from his own advisors does not move him. Plague four introduces the distinction of Goshen — Israel begins to be visibly separated from Egypt's judgment.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 11:20Jesus: "if it is by the finger of God that I cast out demons, then the kingdom of God has come upon you." The Egyptian magicians' phrase applied to the kingdom.
  • Exod 8:23"I will put a division between my people and your people" — the first explicit separation of Israel from Egypt within the plagues.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:2

The frog (tsefardea) plague targeted Heket, the frog-headed goddess of fertility and childbirth, one of the most revered deities in the Egyptian pantheon. Sarna notes the theological irony: the creature most associated with divine blessing becomes the instrument of divine judgment. Sacred animals cannot protect their worshippers when the One who created them deploys them against Egypt.

8:16

The gnat/lice plague (kinnim) is the first plague executed without warning to Pharaoh and without the requirement of a raised staff — Aaron strikes the dust and gnats appear. Sarna notes the escalating immediacy: earlier plagues had elaborate announcement rituals; this one simply happens. The progression from negotiated warning to sudden judgment mirrors the trajectory of divine patience.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:8–15

The frog negotiation is the chapter's most psychologically precise episode. Pharaoh asks for relief; Moses grants him the initiative ('set the time'); the frogs die at the exact moment; 'but when Pharaoh saw there was respite, he hardened his heart.' Alter notes the sequence enacts a principle: those who harden their hearts do so in moments of relief, not in moments of pressure. The space between plagues is where Pharaoh's character is fully expressed.

8:19

'This is the finger of God' — the magicians' testimony is a remarkable narrative moment. Alter notes they are the prosecution's own witnesses turning for the defence. People hired to discredit Moses' power are forced to acknowledge its divine source. Pharaoh's refusal to accept even their testimony is the measure of his hardening — he has moved beyond evidence to pure wilfulness.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:10

On Moses giving Pharaoh the choice of timing: Calvin reads this as divine condescension to remove every possible excuse. 'Moses sought to take away all pretext for excuse from Pharaoh, lest he should pretend that the removal of the frogs happened by chance.' The delay until tomorrow is not negligence but precision — the plague's removal is calibrated to demonstrate divine control, not coincidence.

8:15

Calvin on Pharaoh hardening his own heart: 'Here we learn that wicked men, in their prosperity, are more and more hardened. Adversity constrains them to pretend repentance; but as soon as God relaxes his hand, they return to their wonted obduracy.' The structure is psychologically universal: external pressure produces apparent compliance; removal of pressure reveals the underlying character. Pharaoh is not unusual; he is the extreme case of a common pattern.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:2

The NET note on 'frogs' (tsefardea) notes it is a collective noun — the plague is a swarm, not individual frogs. The note discusses the plague's geographical plausibility: following a disrupted Nile (plague 1), frogs would naturally multiply then leave the water as it became unliveable, swarming onto land. The naturalistic observation does not reduce the divine causation but notes that God can work through ecological cascades he has initiated.

8:16

The NET note on kinnim ('gnats' or 'lice') discusses the translation options: the Hebrew term appears only in the plague narrative and in Ps 105:31. LXX renders skniphes ('gnats' or 'midges'); Vulgate cinipes. Most modern versions choose 'gnats' or 'lice.' The ambiguity does not affect the theological point. The note also explains why this plague lacks warning: the escalation from warned judgments to sudden judgments reflects increasing divine displeasure at Pharaoh's repeated refusals.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
8:22-23

MacArthur: I will deal differently with the land of Goshen, where my people live; no swarms of flies will be there. The distinction between Israel and Egypt within the plagues demonstrates that YHWH's judgment is discriminating, not indiscriminate.

8:20-32

MacArthur: Pharaoh's counter-offer — sacrifice in Egypt rather than in the wilderness — is the first of three compromise attempts (8:25, 8:28, 10:24). Each compromise is a strategic concession that stops short of full obedience. Moses refuses each because partial obedience is not obedience.

Hebrew Word Study

כִּנִּים kinnîm — gnats / lice: V.16-19: The third plague — gnats (kinnîm) from dust — is the first the Egyptian magicians cannot replicate. "This is the finger of God (ʾetsbaʿ ʾĕlōhîm)." The phrase "finger of God" appears again when Moses receives the stone tablets at Sinai (Exod 31:18), and Jesus uses it in Luke 11:20 to describe his exorcisms — connecting creation, law, and kingdom authority under the same divine agency.

הֶפְלָה heplāh — distinction / separation: V.23: "I will make a distinction (sim pedût / hiplāh) between my people and your people." The verb pālāh (to distinguish, set apart) marks the crucial theological shift in the plagues: the same divine power that judges Egypt protects Israel. God's wrath and God's grace are not separate attributes but two aspects of the one covenantal action.

Historical Context

None

Context

The Goshen distinction in plague four is the visible sign that the Exodus is not merely a power contest but a redemptive act. YHWH knows who his people are and protects them specifically. This "putting a division" between Israel and Egypt will reach its climax at the Red Sea — where the cloud that gives light to Israel gives darkness to Egypt (14:20). The same divine presence serves as protection for the redeemed and judgment for their pursuers.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 9:4,26; 10:23The Goshen distinction continues through the livestock plague, the darkness, and the death of the firstborn — each time Israel is explicitly protected.
  • Exod 14:20The cloud: light to Israel, darkness to Egypt. The climactic expression of the Goshen-distinction principle.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:22

The discrimination between Egypt and Goshen beginning with plague four is theologically significant. Sarna notes it demonstrates that the plagues are not natural catastrophes affecting all in the region equally but precisely directed acts of judgment. The theological point: YHWH knows his people, distinguishes them from their oppressors, and can deliver targeted judgments that spare the innocent while judging the guilty.

8:26

Moses' explanation for why Israel cannot sacrifice in Egypt — 'we will sacrifice what Egyptians consider abominable' — refers to the fact that cattle (especially cows, associated with Hathor) and sheep (associated with various Egyptian deities) were sacred in Egypt. Sacrificing them publicly would provoke violence. Sarna notes this is a real diplomatic objection, not a pretext; it reflects genuine cultural tension between Israelite and Egyptian religious practice.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:28–32

The flies negotiation reveals Pharaoh at his most sophisticated. He agrees, modifies (not far), Moses accepts, the flies go, Pharaoh reneges. Alter notes the exchange has the rhythm of a chess game — each move is precise, each counter-move calculated. But the game is rigged: Pharaoh will always defect when the pressure lifts. Moses knows this; he warns explicitly ('do not deal deceptively again'). Pharaoh does it anyway. The pattern has moved from surprise to inevitability.

8:29-32

Pharaoh's pattern of negotiation, promise, and withdrawal is established most clearly here. Alter notes that Pharaoh's 'request' (pray for me, v.28) and Moses' condition (do not deceive again, v.29) and Pharaoh's compliance-then-reversal make this episode the clearest model of the cycle that will repeat through the remaining plagues.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:25

Pharaoh's first compromise — 'sacrifice here in Egypt' — is Calvin's model of worldly accommodation: 'Satan never ceases to invent subtleties, that he may not be compelled to let go the prey which he has seized.' The offer is superficially reasonable; its effect would be to prevent the Exodus from beginning. Calvin reads every compromise offer in the plague narrative as a spiritual trap — the enemy gives ground on peripheral points to hold the essential ground.

8:25-28

Calvin: Pharaoh's compromise offer — 'sacrifice here in Egypt' — is the model of all false religion's response to the true God: it invites worship but on its own terms, in its own territory, under its own conditions. Moses refuses. The covenant God is not worshipped by those who dictate the terms of worship.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:21

The NET note on 'flies' (arov) explains the Hebrew term's meaning is uncertain — it appears only here and in Ps 78:45 and 105:31. LXX translates kynomuia ('dog-fly,' a biting fly). Other translations include 'swarms of insects,' 'wild animals,' or 'beetles.' The scarab beetle was sacred in Egypt (associated with Khepri, the sun god), making a beetle plague another targeted theological statement if that reading is correct.

8:22-24

The NET note on the fly swarms affecting only Egyptian territory but not Goshen discusses the separation (pala, to be distinct/wonderful) between Israel and Egypt. This is the first plague that explicitly distinguishes between the peoples — establishing the theological principle that divine judgment targets covenant opposition while sparing the covenant community.

Ancient Sources

Heqet — Frog Goddess
Egyptian goddess of fertility, depicted with a frog head. Sacred in the Delta region. The frog plague desecrates one of Egypt's most venerated symbols.
The plague targets Egyptian theology directly.
Plague Cascade Theory
Some scholars (e.g., Ziony Zevit, Colin Humphreys) argue the plagues form an ecological cascade: Nile-blood → frog death → insects from dead frogs → livestock disease → human disease → hail → locusts → darkness. The natural sequence does not diminish the miracle — it shows YHWH working through and intensifying natural processes.
The ecological coherence of the plague sequence supports its historical plausibility.

Reception History

Luke 11:20
The "finger of God" phrase used by Egyptian magicians is taken up by Jesus for his own exorcisms — the same power that overcame Pharaoh's court overcomes the spiritual powers in Jesus's ministry.

Scholarly Debates

Pharaoh's Hardening: Divine Causation or Human Responsibility?
Divine determinism
Some Calvinist readings
God hardens Pharaoh's heart from the beginning (4:21; 7:3). Pharaoh's resistance is foreordained. Paul's use of this narrative in Rom 9:17–18 ('God hardens whom he wills') supports a strong predestinarian reading. Pharaoh is the vessel of wrath prepared for destruction to demonstrate divine glory.
Human responsibility
Arminian and many evangelical readings
In chs.8–9, Pharaoh hardens his own heart (8:15, 8:32, 9:34). The divine hardening in chs.4–7 is either proleptic (describing what will happen) or responds to Pharaoh's prior pattern of resistance. God hardens what Pharaoh has already chosen to be. Divine hardening is judicial confirmation of human choice.
Compatible causality
Calvin, Reformed evangelical consensus
Both are true simultaneously. God sovereignly works through Pharaoh's free choices without overriding them. Pharaoh genuinely chooses to harden; God sovereignly uses that choice to multiply demonstrations of his power. The mystery is not resolved by choosing one side; it is held in tension as compatible realities.
The text itself presents both realities without resolving the tension: 'Pharaoh hardened his heart' (8:15) and 'the LORD hardened Pharaoh's heart' (9:12) describe the same event from different perspectives. The narrative refuses to choose between divine sovereignty and human agency, presenting both as simultaneously operative.
