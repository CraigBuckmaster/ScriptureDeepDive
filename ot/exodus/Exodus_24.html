Exodus 24

The Covenant Ratified with Blood; The Elders See God; Moses Enters the Cloud

Verses 1–8 — The Covenant Ratified with Blood
12345678

Places

Foot of Mount Sinai
Base of Sinai
The covenant ratification happens at the foot of the mountain — Moses builds an altar there and sets up twelve pillars. The twelve pillars represent the twelve tribes; the altar represents YHWH. The blood is sprinkled between them: spatial covenant enactment at the mountain's base.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
24:7

MacArthur: Moses took the Book of the Covenant and read it to the people. They responded, We will do everything the LORD has said; we will obey. The formal covenant ratification includes the reading of covenant terms and the explicit consent of the parties. Covenant is not imposed but entered.

24:8

MacArthur: This is the blood of the covenant. The covenant is sealed with blood sprinkled on both altar and people. Jesus's words at the Last Supper (this is my blood of the covenant) deliberately echo this scene.

Hebrew Word Study

דַּם הַבְּרִית dam habbĕrît — blood of the covenant: V.8: "Moses took the blood, sprinkled it on the people and said, This is the blood of the covenant that the LORD has made with you." The Sinai covenant is sealed with blood. This phrase — dam habbĕrît — is almost certainly in Jesus's mind when he takes the cup at the Last Supper: "This is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many" (Matt 26:28). The old covenant blood of bulls is replaced by the new covenant blood of the Son.

נַעֲשֶׂה וְנִשְׁמָע naʿăseh wĕnishmāʿ — we will do and we will obey: V.7: "We will do everything the LORD has said; we will obey (naʿăseh wĕnishmāʿ)." The Hebrew order — "do" before "hear/obey" — was celebrated in rabbinic tradition as Israel's supreme commitment: acting before full understanding, trusting before comprehension. Whether this represents praiseworthy faith or presumptuous overconfidence is debated — the golden calf only weeks later suggests the fragility of this national commitment.

Historical Context

Covenant-sealing with blood is attested across the ANE. The Sinai ceremony is distinctive: the entire people, not just the leadership, are covenant partners. The Book of the Covenant is read aloud and responded to — covenant is a community event, not a priestly transaction.

Context

The double agreement (vv.3 and 7) and the blood thrown on the people create the most solemn covenant-sealing in the Pentateuch. When Jesus at the Last Supper says "this is the blood of the covenant," he deliberately echoes Moses' words here — the new covenant sealed in his blood, not the blood of bulls.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:7

The 'Book of the Covenant' read to the people — and their response 'we will do and we will hear/obey' (na'aseh venishma) — is the formal acceptance of the covenant document. Sarna notes that in the ancient Near Eastern treaty tradition, the covenant document was read to the vassal, who formally accepted its terms. Israel's response is the treaty acceptance formula. The unusual word order ('do' before 'hear') is theologically significant: total commitment precedes full comprehension.

24:10

'Under his feet was something like a pavement made of sapphire, clear as the sky itself.' The throne-pavement vision is one of the OT's most striking divine throne descriptions, closely paralleled in Ezek 1:26 (the sapphire/lapis lazuli throne). Sarna notes that lapis lazuli was the most precious blue stone in the ancient world, associated with the sky and divine royalty. The vision does not describe God himself but the platform of his presence — the transcendent cannot be seen; only the approach can be described.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:6–8

The blood ceremony is the OT's most formally structured covenant-sealing ritual. Alter notes the precise symmetry: half the blood on the altar (God's side), half on the people (Israel's side). The blood connects the two parties physically — the same life-substance touches both. Moses' recitation — 'this is the blood of the covenant that the LORD has made with you' — is the covenant formula, the words that make the action a legal act.

24:9–11

The meal on the mountain is the chapter's most theologically astonishing moment — quietly narrated after the elaborate ritual. 'And they beheld God, and ate and drank.' Alter notes the simplicity of the statement is its power: the most sacred act in Israelite experience (standing in the divine presence) is described in the most mundane terms (eating and drinking). The extraordinary is normalised; the transcendent permits intimacy.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:8

Calvin on the blood of the covenant: 'The sprinkling of blood was a confirmation of the covenant, for the people were bound by it as by a solemn tie to God. And the covenant itself was consecrated with blood, to teach that men can only come to God by means of a sacrifice.' The Levitical sacrificial system is implicit in the Sinai blood ceremony: access to God requires atonement; the covenant is sealed with death. Hebrews 9:18–22 draws the explicit connection to Christ's blood.

24:10–11

Calvin on the vision of God: 'Though they saw God with their eyes, they did not perceive his essence, but only symbols which were accommodated to their capacity.' The vision is real but accommodated — what the elders saw was not the divine essence (which is invisible) but a divine self-accommodation to human perception. Calvin's doctrine of divine accommodation explains all theophanic visions: God presents himself in forms human perception can receive without perishing.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
24:8

The NET note on 'blood of the covenant' (dam habrit) notes that Heb 9:18–22 explicitly cites this passage and draws the connection to Christ's covenant blood. The note explains that in ancient Near Eastern treaties, blood oaths were the most solemn form of covenant commitment — often involving the parties passing between the cut halves of an animal (cf. Gen 15:17). The Sinai blood ceremony is the covenant's most solemn moment, exceeded only by the blood of the new covenant.

24:10

The NET note on the sapphire/lapis lazuli pavement discusses the parallel with Ezek 1:26 and the broader ancient Near Eastern tradition of divine throne descriptions using precious stones. The note also discusses the paradox of the passage: 'they saw the God of Israel' (v.10) but Moses is warned that no one can see God and live (33:20). The resolution: what is seen is the throne-platform, not the divine face — theophanic presence without full divine disclosure.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
24:10-11

MacArthur: They saw the God of Israel... They saw God, and they ate and drank. The elders saw God and ate a covenant meal in his presence. The meal in the divine presence anticipates the Marriage Supper of the Lamb (Rev 19:9).

24:18

MacArthur: Moses stayed on the mountain forty days and forty nights. The number forty is associated with periods of testing, preparation, and divine encounter throughout Scripture -- Moses at Sinai twice, Elijah at Horeb, Jesus in the wilderness.

Hebrew Word Study

כְּבוֹד יְהוָה kĕbôd YHWH — glory of the LORD: V.16-17: "The glory of the LORD settled on Mount Sinai. To the Israelites the glory of the LORD looked like a consuming fire on top of the mountain." The kābôd (glory/weight/honor) of YHWH is his visible self-revelation — here as fire. This glory will later fill the Tabernacle (Exod 40:34-35), fill Solomon's Temple (1 Kgs 8:10-11), depart in Ezekiel's vision (Ezek 10-11), and return in the person of Jesus: "the Word became flesh and we have seen his glory (doxa)" (John 1:14).

פַּנֵּי יְהוָה pĕnê YHWH — the face of the LORD: V.9-11: Moses, Aaron, Nadab, Abihu, and the seventy elders "saw the God of Israel" and ate and drank — the covenant meal ratifying the Sinai agreement. The text notes they "saw God" yet "God did not raise his hand against the nobles of Israel." Face-to-face encounter with God is normally lethal (cf. Exod 33:20); here it is communion. The mediation of the covenant allows what would otherwise be impossible: the creature and Creator share a table.

Historical Context

None

Context

The theophany of the elders is the closest the OT comes to unmediated corporate communion with God. Yet even here Moses alone enters the cloud — the elders see from a distance. The contrast is instructive: the covenant creates access; only the mediator has full access. Hebrews resolves this tension: Christ, the ultimate mediator, gives the entire community the access Moses alone had.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:12

The stone tablets are 'written by God' — the only object in the Pentateuch explicitly said to be of divine authorship. Sarna notes the theological weight: human writing transmits human words; these tablets transmit divine words in the most direct form available. When Moses later shatters the tablets (32:19), he is physically destroying the written covenant — an act of enormous theological significance that requires the covenant's rewriting in ch.34.

24:16–17

The glory (kavod) of the LORD settling on Sinai — appearing as a consuming fire — is the chapter's final theophanic image. Sarna notes the connection to the tabernacle: the same kavod that rests on Sinai will rest on the completed tabernacle (40:34–35). The tabernacle is a portable Sinai — the mountain's glory transferred to a structure Israel can carry. The forty days of tabernacle instruction (chs.25–31) will design the vessel for the glory.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:12–18

The ascent narrative frames the forty days of instruction as a moment outside normal time. Alter notes the deliberate cosmic framing: six days of cloud, seventh-day summons, forty days and nights in the cloud. The numbers are covenantally loaded (seven = completion, forty = formation). Moses in the cloud is removed from the narrative into the divine presence; the reader must wait through chs.25–31 for his return.

24:15-18

The forty days in the cloud is the first of two extended periods Moses spends on the mountain. Alter notes the number's structural function: forty days on the mountain receiving the instructions, forty years in the wilderness fulfilling them. The number encodes the relationship between received divine guidance and enacted human response.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:12

Calvin on the written tablets: 'When God himself prescribes and writes the rule of a good and holy life, men are deprived of all excuse... the Law is no product of human reason or human will, but is the very voice of God.' The divine authorship of the tablets is Calvin's ground for the law's absolute authority. No human can edit, supplement, or revise what God has written. The permanence of the stone form is the permanence of the divine will.

24:18

On Moses' forty days in the cloud: 'God wished to exercise the faith and patience of his people by the long absence of their leader; and thus their fall into idolatry is rendered inexcusable.' The forty-day absence is a test of the covenant commitment they have just made. Israel will fail it within the forty days (ch.32: the golden calf). The covenant ratification of ch.24 and the covenant violation of ch.32 are the same distance apart as Moses' ascent and descent.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
24:17

The NET note on 'consuming fire' (esh okhlah) explains that the same phrase is used for the divine presence at the burning bush and for the divine glory in the tabernacle. The 'consuming' character of divine holiness is not destructive in these contexts but transformative — the mountain is not consumed; the tabernacle is not burned. The consuming fire imagery signals the absolute holiness of the divine presence without implying destruction of what it inhabits.

24:15-18

The NET note on the 'consuming fire' visible to all Israel from below while Moses is inside the cloud discusses the theological message to the watching community: the divine glory that would destroy the unholy can be entered by the covenant mediator. Moses' entry into the fire is the visual demonstration of his priestly function.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Moses↗ People
Prophet, lawgiver, and covenant mediator
Moses stands at the centre of the Pentateuch’s narrative — the one man who spoke with God face to face (Exod 33:11; Num 12:8) and mediated the Sinai covenant to Israel. In Leviticus he receives the priestly legislation; in Numbers he leads, intercedes, and ultimately fails at Meribah (Num 20). The writer of Hebrews calls him "faithful in all God’s house" (Heb 3:5) while distinguishing him from Christ, the Son over the house.

Ancient Sources

Covenant Meals
ANE treaties were ratified with shared meals between covenant parties — shared eating signified peace established and covenant in force. Sinai gives unprecedented content to this known form: the suzerain is the God of the universe.
The covenant meal participates in known ANE practice while transforming it completely.
Sapphire Pavement
The sapphire-like pavement (v.10) echoes Ezekiel's throne vision (Ezek 1:26) and Revelation's sea of glass (Rev 4:6) — consistent theophanic imagery across the canon.
The vision uses familiar imagery while remaining deliberately restrained about the divine form.

Scholarly Debates

The Sinai Covenant and Ancient Near Eastern Suzerainty Treaties
Close structural parallel: covenant follows treaty form
Mendenhall (1954), Kline, Kitchen
Second-millennium Hittite suzerainty treaties have the same structural elements as the Sinai covenant: preamble (self-identification of the great king), historical prologue (the king's acts for the vassal), treaty stipulations (obligations), document clause (write and read), divine witnesses, blessings and curses. The structural parallel supports a second-millennium date for the Sinai covenant, consistent with Mosaic authorship.
First-millennium parallels are also relevant
Some critical scholars
Assyrian and Neo-Babylonian treaty forms from the first millennium BC also contain similar elements. The parallel with second-millennium forms is not unique; treaty forms evolved across the ancient Near East. The structural argument for a second-millennium date requires that the specific combination of elements is uniquely second-millennium, which some scholars dispute.
Mendenhall's and Kline's analysis of the Hittite treaty parallels has been widely influential in supporting the second-millennium dating of the Sinai covenant. The structural argument is not conclusive by itself but is consistent with and supportive of Mosaic authorship. Kitchen's work on the treaties in his larger study of ancient Near Eastern historiography remains the most comprehensive treatment.
