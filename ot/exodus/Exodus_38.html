Exodus 38

Bronze Altar and Basin; the Courtyard; Materials Census

Author

Moses, according to Jewish and Christian tradition.

When written

c.1445-1405 BC during the wilderness period.

What prompted it

To record God's redemption of Israel from Egypt and the establishment of the covenant at Sinai.

Verses 1—20 — The Altar of Burnt Offering and the Bronze Basin
1234567891011121314151617181920

Hebrew Word Study

נְחֹשֶׁת nĕḥōshet — bronze / copper: V.1-8: The outer courtyard's altar and laver were bronze (nĕḥōshet) — not gold or silver. The progression of materials from courtyard to Holy Place to Most Holy Place (bronze → silver → gold) maps the gradient of holiness. Bronze, associated with judgment and strength (see bronze serpent, Num 21), appropriately marks the place of sacrifice and washing — the first encounters with God require dealing with sin and impurity before entry becomes possible.

מַחֲלָפוֹת maḥalāpôt — bronze mirrors: V.8: The bronze laver was made from the mirrors of the women who served at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting. Their mirrors — objects used for self-examination and beautification — were melted into the vessel used for purification. The transformation is symbolic: self-contemplation given over becomes the vessel of cleansing for others. The women's act of offering their mirrors prefigures Paul's instruction that we all "reflect the Lord's glory" (2 Cor 3:18) until our lives are transformed into his likeness.

Historical Context

None

Context

The bronze basin from the women's mirrors (v.8) is one of the most unexpected details in the Tabernacle construction. These women gave their personal beauty objects — the mirrors that showed them their own faces — to make the basin where priests would wash before entering God's presence. Their sacrifice is both literal and symbolic: what serves self-reflection is given for holy service.

Verses 21—31 — The Inventory of Materials Used
2122232425262728293031

Hebrew Word Study

כֶּסֶף הַפְּקֻדִּים kesep happĕqûdîm — silver of the census / counted ones: V.25-28: The silver from the census — 301,775 half-shekels — was cast into the 100 silver sockets (ʾădānîm) of the Tabernacle framework. Every socket of the Tabernacle's foundation was made of half a shekel of silver from a counted Israelite. The word pĕqûdîm (counted/mustered) means both "census" and "care/appointment" — to be counted by God is to be under his oversight. The foundation of the dwelling place was the counted, redeemed people.

בִּקּוּרִים biqqûrîm / pikudê — accounting / inventory: V.21: "These are the amounts of the materials used for the tabernacle, the tabernacle of the Testimony, which were recorded (meforshim) at Moses's command." The careful accounting of all materials used — down to the last talent of bronze — reflects the accountability principle of public trust. Moses gave full transparency: the community's offering was faithfully used. The stewardship of sacred gifts is subject to open accounting.

Historical Context

None

Context

The materials census (vv.21–31) is an act of covenantal accountability — everything given is accounted for, verified, and reported. The gold, silver, and bronze are all tallied. The 100 silver talents cast into 100 bases (v.27: one talent per base, one base per person counted) is the Tabernacle's most explicit connection between the ransom silver and the community's redemption. The Tabernacle stood on 603,550 people's atonement payments.

    Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    Moses↗ People
    Prophet, lawgiver, and covenant mediator
    Moses stands at the centre of the Pentateuch’s narrative — the one man who spoke with God face to face (Exod 33:11; Num 12:8) and mediated the Sinai covenant to Israel. In Leviticus he receives the priestly legislation; in Numbers he leads, intercedes, and ultimately fails at Meribah (Num 20). The writer of Hebrews calls him "faithful in all God’s house" (Heb 3:5) while distinguishing him from Christ, the Son over the house.
    Aaron↗ People
    First high priest of Israel
    Aaron, Moses’s brother, is consecrated as Israel’s first high priest in Lev 8–9. He bears the names of the twelve tribes on his breastplate before God and enters the Most Holy Place once a year on Yom Kippur (Lev 16). His failures — the golden calf (Exod 32), challenging Moses’s authority (Num 12), complicity at Meribah (Num 20) — make him the paradigm of the imperfect priest who prefigures a better high priest (Heb 7:26–28).

    Miriam↗ People
    Prophetess and worship leader; Moses’s sister
    Miriam leads Israel’s women in worship after the Red Sea crossing (Exod 15:20) and is called a prophetess. In Num 12 she challenges Moses’s unique prophetic authority alongside Aaron and is struck with ṣāraʿat — only to be healed after Moses’s seven-word intercession. She dies at Kadesh (Num 20:1), her passing noted in two terse Hebrew words before the water crisis.
    Joshua↗ People
    Moses’s military commander and designated successor
    Joshua son of Nun serves as Moses’s lieutenant throughout the wilderness period — commanding at Rephidim (Exod 17), ascending Sinai with Moses (Exod 24), guarding the tent of meeting. In Num 13–14 he stands with Caleb as one of two faithful spies. He is formally commissioned in Num 27 through Moses’s hand-laying and inherits the leadership role that Moses cannot fulfil past the Jordan.

    Ancient Sources

    603,550 Men
    The census number here matches Numbers 1:46. The consistency across Exodus 38 and Numbers 1 is significant. Various interpretations exist for the large number: literal; ʾelep as "unit" rather than "thousand" (giving a much smaller total); symbolic for a great multitude.
    The number is consistent across texts; its interpretation remains debated.
    Materials Accounting
    The detailed materials census has parallels in ancient building accounts — Egyptian and Mesopotamian building inscriptions often include detailed accounts of materials used. The Tabernacle census fits within this ancient tradition of temple accounting.
    Accountability for sacred materials was a universal ancient Near Eastern practice.

    Reception History

    Numbers 1:46
    The census of 603,550 men reappears in Numbers 1 — confirming the connection between the Tabernacle's silver bases and the whole community's counted, ransomed lives.
    Stewardship tradition
    The materials census of Exod 38 became the model for covenantal financial accountability — the principle that God's resources must be carefully accounted for, as exemplified in Nehemiah's rebuilding (Neh 7:70–73) and the NT church's collection for Jerusalem (2 Cor 8:20–21).

    Scholarly Debates

    The Materials Census: Historical Record or Theological Testimony?
    Historical record
    Kitchen, evangelical archaeologists
    The census is a genuine financial record consistent with ancient Near Eastern temple building records. Egyptian and Mesopotamian records routinely documented materials used.
    Theological idealization
    Some critical scholars
    The numbers are too precise to be genuine records — a theological statement about perfect obedience, not an accountant's ledger.
    The numbers' internal consistency (603,550 men × half-shekel = 100 talents + 1,775 shekels) supports historical accuracy. A theological fabrication would not necessarily maintain this precision. The record is both historically accurate and theologically significant.
