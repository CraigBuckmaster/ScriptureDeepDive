Ruth 4

The Gate; Legal Redemption; Marriage; Obed; The Genealogy to David

Author

Unknown; Jewish tradition attributes authorship to Samuel.

When written

c.1000 BC, possibly early monarchy period.

What prompted it

To record God's providential care for a Moabite widow and her mother-in-law, and to trace the lineage of King David.

Verses 1–12 — The Gate: Legal Proceedings; Redemption; Witnesses
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

šaʿar SHA-ar — gate: The town gate (v.1) is the civic centre of Israelite legal life — where elders gathered, legal transactions were witnessed, and binding decisions made (Deut 21:19; 22:15; 25:7). Boaz's deliberate move to the gate transforms what could have been a private arrangement into a public, legally binding covenant transaction. The gate is where Israel's communal covenant life is administered, and Boaz insists the redemption occur there — under the full witness of the community.

gāʾal / gĕʾullāh ga-AL / ge-u-LAH — redeem / redemption: The legal exchange in vv.1-12 turns on the gāʾal root — to redeem. The nearer kinsman is first willing to redeem the land but declines when he learns it includes Ruth, the Moabite widow. The exchange of the sandal (v.7-8) formalises his renunciation. Boaz then publicly declares his intention to "acquire" (qānāh) all of Elimelech's property and "raise up the name of the dead" (v.10). The entire transaction is the legal mechanism by which ḥesed becomes covenant action.

bānāh ba-NAH — to build / to build up a family: The witnesses' blessing (vv.11-12) uses the root bānāh — "may the LORD make the woman... build up the house of Israel." This is the covenant language of family-building as national-history building. Rachel and Leah are invoked: the two women who "built the house of Israel." Ruth the Moabite is placed in the lineage of the matriarchs — her union with Boaz is not merely personal but national, genealogically linking her to the line that will produce David and, through David, Messiah.

Historical Context

None

Context

The gate scene moves with the precision of a legal document. Boaz's strategy is transparent and fair: he gives the nearer redeemer first right of refusal (vv.3–4), then reveals the full obligation when he accepts (v.5). The man's refusal is not villainous — it is economically rational. His problem is that he thinks only about his own estate. Boaz thinks about the dead and about Ruth.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 38Tamar in the blessing (v.12). Like Ruth, she secured the covenant line through unconventional means when the male actors failed their obligation. Both women appear in Matthew's genealogy of Jesus.
  • Gen 29–30Rachel and Leah built up the house of Israel — the community blesses Ruth using the founding mothers of the twelve tribes. A Moabite woman is placed in the company of Israel's matriarchs.

Places

City gate of Bethlehem
Legal and civic centre
The city gate was the ancient town's courtroom and marketplace. Boaz goes to the gate and sits down — the legal posture of one waiting to conduct public business. Ten elders as witnesses and the sandal transaction are all gate proceedings. The book's climax is entirely public geography.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:1–6

The anonymous kinsman is one of the Bible's most interesting minor characters: he is doing nothing wrong, he is not a villain, and his decline of the fuller redemption is economically rational. Alter notes the narrator deliberately keeps him nameless — 'so-and-so' (peloni almoni) — while Boaz is named. The contrast is between the man who will not pay the full price and the man who will; the one without a name and the one whose name will be remembered.

4:5

Boaz's disclosure — 'you must also acquire Ruth the Moabite, the dead man's widow' — is the scene's pivot. The kinsman had been willing to buy the land. The addition of Ruth changes the calculation: her son would inherit the redeemed land, not the kinsman's existing sons. The land would leave his estate in the next generation. Alter notes this is not greed but legitimate estate planning; yet it is precisely the cost that redemption requires.

Hubbard — NICOT Ruth

Robert L. Hubbard Jr., NICOT Commentary on Ruth (1988) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:1–2

The gate (sha'ar) in ancient Israelite cities was the formal legal space — contracts were witnessed, legal proceedings held, elders convened. Hubbard notes the gate scene in Ruth 4 follows precise legal protocol: Boaz assembles ten elders (the minimum required for a valid legal transaction), presents the case formally, offers first right of refusal to the nearer kinsman, and records the transfer of obligation when the kinsman declines.

4:7–8

The sandal-removal ceremony (vv.7–8) is one of the OT's most precisely described legal customs. Hubbard notes it is similar to but distinct from the levirate ceremony in Deut 25:9 (where the widow removes the sandal in public shame). Here the kinsman himself removes his sandal and gives it to Boaz — a voluntary transfer of right rather than a public shaming. The narrator explains the custom for a presumably later audience, suggesting the practice had become archaic.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1

Calvin on the gate scene: 'The city gate was the place where all affairs of the community were publicly transacted — it was as it were the court of law. By repairing thither, Boaz showed that he wished to conduct the whole business openly and honestly, without any concealment.'

4:6

Calvin on the kinsman's refusal: 'He was not to be blamed for declining what he had a right to decline. But Boaz undertook the obligation not from compulsion but from free choice — and this free election of a harder duty is the mark of a true go'el, who redeems not because he must but because he chooses to.' The voluntary nature of Boaz's redemption makes it theologically significant.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:1

The NET note on 'friend/so-and-so' (peloni almoni) explains the phrase is a Hebrew idiom for a deliberately unnamed person (cf. 1 Sam 21:2). The note suggests the anonymity is not accident but literary choice — the man who refuses to complete the redemption does not deserve to have his name preserved, while Boaz's name will endure through the Davidic line.

4:7

The NET note on the sandal custom explains the narrator's editorial comment ('this was the custom in former times in Israel') is a significant datum: by the time Ruth was composed, the sandal-transfer was already archaic, suggesting the events (or the legal institution) belong to an earlier period. This supports the early dating of the book's setting even if not its composition.

Verses 13–17 — Marriage, Birth, and Obed: Naomi's Emptiness Filled
1314151617

Hebrew Word Study

yālad ya-LAD — to bear / to give birth: The birth announcement "Ruth bore a son" (v.13) reverses the deaths that opened the book (1:3-5). The verb yālad in covenant narrative is never merely biological — it marks moments when God's purposes advance through human generation (Gen 21:2; 29:32; 1 Sam 1:20). The LORD's direct agency is specified: "the LORD enabled her to conceive" (v.13). The child is simultaneously the answer to Ruth's loyalty, Naomi's lament, and Boaz's covenant action.

gōʾēl go-EL — kinsman-redeemer: The women's blessing identifies Obed as Naomi's gōʾēl (v.14) — a striking use of the term. The redeemer in this context is the child himself, who will "renew your life and sustain you in your old age." The theology is complete: Boaz redeemed the land and the widow; Obed redeems Naomi from emptiness. The chain of redemption runs from God (ultimate gōʾēl) through human covenant actors to the child who embodies the restored future.

ʾômenet o-ME-net — nurse / foster-mother: Naomi "took the child in her arms and cared for him" (v.16) using the term ʾômenet — the role of a trusted nurse or foster-mother (Num 11:12; 2 Sam 4:4). The women's declaration that "Naomi has a son" (v.17) completes the reversal: she who returned "empty" (1:21) is now called mother. The covenantal ḥesed that Ruth enacted (1:8; 2:20; 3:10) has worked through legal redemption to restore not just property but personhood, family, and future.

Historical Context

None

Context

The birth scene accelerates rapidly — three verses (vv.13–15) compress what took years. The LORD enables conception (the only direct divine act in the book). The women of Bethlehem praise the LORD and describe the child as Naomi's guardian-redeemer. The one who came back empty (1:21) now holds a child on her lap. The reversal is complete.

  • 1 Sam 16:1Jesse the father of David — the child born in this verse is the father of Jesse, the grandfather of the greatest king of Israel. The book of Ruth is the backstory of the Davidic monarchy.

4:13-17

MacArthur: The LORD enabled her to conceive — the narrator attributes Obed's birth directly to divine action. Ruth had been barren throughout her marriage to Mahlon (10 years). The child is the covenant answer to the book's opening desolation: the family line that was cut off is restored, and more than restored.

4:14-15

MacArthur: The women's blessing is addressed to Naomi, not Ruth: your daughter-in-law who loves you, who is better to you than seven sons. Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:13–17

The birth announcement and the women's response form the book's emotional climax. Alter notes that the women say 'Naomi has a son' rather than 'Ruth has a son' — the restoration is attributed to Naomi because it was her loss that the book set out to reverse. Ruth, who has been the active agent throughout, recedes as Naomi is named as the beneficiary. The narrative ending belongs to the character whose emptying it was designed to fill.

4:15

'Better to you than seven sons' — the superlative praise applied to Ruth by the community women. Alter notes that seven sons was the biblical ideal of male blessing. The comparison makes Ruth's hesed greater than the maximum possible blessing from within Israel's patrilineal system. A foreign daughter-in-law is worth more than the ideal male blessing.

Hubbard — NICOT Ruth

Robert L. Hubbard Jr., NICOT Commentary on Ruth (1988) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:13

The LORD's enabling of Ruth's conception is the book's only explicit divine action. Hubbard notes the significance of the silence before this moment — through three chapters, God has worked through human agents without being directly mentioned as acting. The single explicit divine action at the story's biological climax makes clear that the entire chain of events was under divine governance. Obed is a providential gift, not merely a natural result.

4:14–17

The women of Bethlehem function as a chorus in vv.14–17 — they bless, name, and interpret the significance of Obed's birth for Naomi. Hubbard notes this communal role mirrors the communal witnesses at the gate (vv.9–11). The community that witnessed the legal transaction now witnesses and interprets the theological completion. The individual story is embedded in covenant community.

Verses 18–22 — The Genealogy: From Perez to David
1819202122

Hebrew Word Study

tôledôt to-le-DOT — genealogy / generations / account of origins: The closing genealogy (vv.18-22) uses the same structural word that organises Genesis — "these are the generations of Perez" (cf. Gen 2:4; 5:1; 6:9; etc.). The tôledôt formula signals that what Ruth and Boaz have enacted is not a private story but a link in the chain of covenant history. The ten names from Perez to David place the story of two widows and a faithful landowner within the arc of Israel's entire narrative from Judah to the throne.

Dāwid da-VEED — David / beloved: The genealogy's final name — "and Jesse the father of David" (v.22) — is the book's theological destination. David's name means "beloved," and the entire book of Ruth is the story of covenant love (ḥesed) generating the beloved king. That the greatest king of Israel traces his line through a Moabite woman who chose Israel's God is a radical statement: covenant faithfulness, not ethnic purity, is what constitutes membership in Israel's redemptive story.

Perets PEH-rets — Perez / breakthrough: The genealogy begins with Perez (v.18) — the son of Judah and Tamar (Gen 38:29), whose name means "breaking through." Tamar, like Ruth, was a foreign woman who secured her place in Israel's covenant line through a bold act of covenant loyalty. The book of Ruth places itself in a deliberate interpretive conversation with Genesis 38: both stories are about foreign women, sexual and legal unconventionality, and the perpetuation of the covenant line against all expectation. The genealogy completes the conversation.

Historical Context

None

Context

The ten-generation genealogy from Perez to David is not an appendix — it is the destination. Everything in the book has been moving toward this revelation. The famine that drove Elimelek's family to Moab, the deaths, the Moabite widow's loyalty, the gleaning, the midnight proposal, the gate proceedings — all of it was the hidden mechanism producing the line that would produce David, and through David, the Messiah. The genealogy detonates the full significance of everything that preceded it.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 1:5–6Salmon the father of Boaz by Rahab, Boaz the father of Obed by Ruth. Matthew's genealogy of Jesus explicitly names both Rahab and Ruth — two foreign women whose ḥesed was woven into the Messianic line.
  • Gen 38:29Perez, who opens the genealogy, was born to Tamar — another woman who secured the covenant line through unconventional means. The genealogy's first name signals the pattern: faithful women outside the expected network, used by God to preserve the line.

Scholarly Debates

Ruth as Typology of Christ's Redemption: How Direct is the Connection?
Direct Christological typology
MacArthur, most evangelical commentators
Boaz is a type of Christ in the go'el institution: the kinsman-redeemer who voluntarily accepts the full cost of redemption that the law requires, rescuing the widow (the helpless) at personal cost, restoring the inheritance, preserving the family name. The parallels are structural and precise: Christ redeems not from compulsion but from voluntary love; he accepts the cost that others decline; his redemption is complete.
Canonical resonance without strict typology
Some scholars
The go'el institution anticipates redemption theology without Boaz being a type of Christ in the technical typological sense. The connections are thematic: redemption through a kinsman, voluntary cost, inclusion of the Gentile. But the book's primary theological concern is hesed as a human and divine virtue, not Christological anticipation.
The Christological reading is well-grounded structurally: the voluntary go'el who accepts the fuller cost that the nearer kinsman declines, redeems the helpless widow, and brings the Gentile into the covenant — these are precisely the contours of Christ's redemption. The anonymous kinsman's declining of the full obligation clarifies the type: the law cannot complete what grace undertakes.
