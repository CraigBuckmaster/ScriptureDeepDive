Ruth 1

Naomi's Loss; the Road; Ruth's Loyalty; Return to Bethlehem

See on Timeline — Ruth
Author

Unknown; Jewish tradition attributes authorship to Samuel.

When written

c.1000 BC, possibly early monarchy period.

What prompted it

To record God's providential care for a Moabite widow and her mother-in-law, and to trace the lineage of King David.

Verses 1–7 — A Family in Moab: Famine, Migration, and Loss
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

raʿab ra-AV — famine / hunger: The famine that drives Elimelech's family to Moab (v.1) is the same Hebrew word used of the famines in Abraham's and Jacob's stories (Gen 12:10; 41:27). Famine in the OT is never merely agricultural — it functions as a covenant signal, prompting movement, testing faith, and ultimately serving providence. The narrator places Ruth's story within this theological pattern from its first sentence.

ger GAYR — sojourner / resident alien: Elimelech's family go to "sojourn" (la-gur) in Moab (v.1) — the same verb used of Abraham's sojourns. The irony the narrator will exploit is that Ruth, herself a foreigner, will eventually sojourn in Israel. The ger holds a liminal status in Israelite law — neither citizen nor slave — yet Torah repeatedly commands Israel to love the ger, "for you were sojourners in Egypt" (Lev 19:34).

ʾalmānāh al-ma-NAH — widow: Naomi's status as widow (v.3, 5) places her among the most legally and economically vulnerable members of ancient society. The Torah's "widow and orphan" legislation (Exod 22:22; Deut 27:19) reflects how completely a woman's security depended on male kinship structures. Ruth's story is in part a story of how covenant loyalty — first Ruth's, then Boaz's — restores what widowhood destroys.

Historical Context

None

Context

The opening seven verses establish maximum loss in minimum space: homeland, husband, sons. The setting in "the days when the judges ruled" (Israel's most faithless era) makes the ḥesed that follows all the more striking. The LORD's provision of food in v.6 is the first divine action in the book — and it is what sets the whole redemption plot in motion.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    1:1-7

    MacArthur: The famine that drives Elimelech to Moab sets the narrative's moral geography: Israel's covenant land is experiencing covenant curse (Deut 28), while Moab represents the world outside the covenant. The family's departure is not rebellion but desperation — and it will cost them.

    1:3-5

    MacArthur: Three deaths in Moab — Elimelech, Mahlon, Chilion — strip Naomi of every male protector. The ancient world measured a woman's security by the men attached to her. Naomi is left with nothing: no husband, no sons, no grandchildren, no prospects. The book opens in absolute desolation to make its ending's restoration all the more stunning.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    1:1–5

    Alter notes the opening five verses constitute one of the most concentrated sequences of death in biblical narrative — four deaths (famine, Elimelech, Mahlon, Chilion) in five verses. The compression is rhetorical: the narrative establishes absolute destitution before it shows how that destitution will be reversed. The darker the opening, the more complete the eventual restoration.

    1:3–5

    The rapid succession of deaths — Elimelech, then the two sons — strips the narrative of every possible male protector before a word of dialogue is spoken. Alter notes this is the narrative's way of zeroing out all human resources before the story of hesed begins. What follows will be entirely grace, not recovery of former assets.

    Hubbard — NICOT Ruth

    Robert L. Hubbard Jr., NICOT Commentary on Ruth (1988) — Scholarly Paraphrase
    1:1

    Hubbard notes the Book of Ruth's careful historical positioning. The 'days of the judges' invokes an era of covenant unfaithfulness (cf. Judg 21:25: 'everyone did what was right in his own eyes'). Ruth is a story of covenant faithfulness — hesed — set against the backdrop of its absence. The contrast is programmatic: what Israel's leaders failed to provide, a Moabite woman embodies.

    1:4

    The marriage of Mahlon and Chilion to Moabite women is narrated without moral comment. Hubbard notes this is deliberate — the narrator does not condemn the marriages (as Ezra-Nehemiah's post-exilic context might). The marriages are simply facts that enable the story. Ruth's Moabite identity becomes the vehicle of hesed, not an obstacle to it.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    1:1

    Calvin on the famine: 'God often chastises whole nations for the sins of a few, or for the purpose of exercising the faith and patience of his people.' The famine is not random natural disaster but providential pressure. Elimelech's response — flight to Moab — is Calvin's negative example: he abandoned the covenant land rather than enduring the discipline within it.

    1:3

    Calvin on Elimelech's death in Moab: 'God punished his unfaithfulness in leaving the inheritance of his fathers and taking refuge among idolaters.' The punishment is not certain — the narrator does not say so — but Calvin reads the sequence as moral pattern: departure from the covenant community followed by loss. The return will require Naomi to make the same journey back that Elimelech refused.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    1:1

    The NET note on 'when the judges ruled' (bishfot hashoftim) discusses the period's historical range (c.1380–1050 BC) and the narrative's deliberate vagueness about which specific judge's era is meant. The vagueness universalises the story — Ruth is not tied to a single historical moment but belongs to the entire period of pre-monarchic Israel.

    1:4

    The NET note on 'Moabite women' discusses the later prohibition of Moabites from the assembly (Deut 23:3) and how Ruth's integration contradicts it. The note explains the rabbinic solution: Deut 23:3 says 'Moabite,' meaning male Moabites only, not females. Ruth's integration is not a violation but a demonstration that the spirit of the law is covenant faithfulness, not ethnic exclusion.

    Verses 8–18 — The Road: Orpah Departs; Ruth Clings
    89101112131415161718

    Hebrew Word Study

    ḥesed KHEH-sed — loyal love / covenant faithfulness: Naomi's prayer that the LORD show the daughters-in-law ḥesed (v.8) is the first use of the book's theological keyword. Ḥesed is not mere kindness but faithful love that goes beyond legal obligation — the love of covenant. The word appears at every pivotal moment in Ruth (1:8; 2:20; 3:10), binding Ruth's loyalty to Boaz's generosity to the LORD's character.

    dābaq da-VAK — cling / hold fast: Ruth "clung" to Naomi (v.14) with the same verb used of a husband cleaving to his wife (Gen 2:24) and of Israel's covenant fidelity to the LORD (Deut 10:20; 11:22). The word marks Ruth's decision as a covenantal act, not merely a family bond. She is choosing Israel — its people, its God, its future — with the intensity of a marriage vow.

    ʾelohim / YHWH e-lo-HEEM / ya-HEH — God / the LORD: Ruth's oath (v.17) invokes both titles in their full covenantal weight: "May the LORD do so to me, and more also." She uses Israel's covenant name (YHWH) — the name revealed to Moses at Sinai — committing herself not to a generic deity but to the God of Israel's redemption history. This is the theological climax of the conversion that vv.14-17 narrate.

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    The road scene is the dramatic heart of chapter 1. Naomi's speech invoking the levirate impossibility (vv.11–13) is legally precise and emotionally honest. Orpah's departure is not condemned — she makes the sensible choice. Ruth's clinging is extraordinary precisely because it is not sensible. The declaration of vv.16–17 is structured as three intensifying couplets, closing with a self-imprecatory oath — the strongest form of promise available in the ancient world.

    Cross-Reference

    • Gen 2:24A man shall leave his father and mother and cling (dābaq) to his wife. Ruth performs this act toward Naomi — the covenant-clinging of marriage applied to loyalty between women.
    • Deut 10:20Hold fast (dābaq) to the LORD your God. Ruth's clinging to Naomi is simultaneously clinging to Naomi's God — which is exactly what v.16 makes explicit.

    Places

    Road from Moab to Judah
    Transjordan to Jordan ford to Judean hills
    The road from Moab to Bethlehem crosses the Jordan and ascends the Judean hill country. Ruth's commitment is made on this literal road between two worlds. Orpah turns back on the same road. The physical journey is the spiritual decision.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    1:8-17

    MacArthur: Orpah's return is described without condemnation — she does what is culturally expected and what Naomi urged. Ruth's clinging goes beyond all social expectation. The word for clung (dabaq) is the same used in Gen 2:24 for the husband cleaving to his wife. Ruth's covenant loyalty is described in the language of marriage.

    1:16-17

    MacArthur: Where you go I will go — the most complete expression of hesed loyalty in the OT. Ruth commits to people, place, community, and God in one speech. Notably, she commits to Naomi's God (your God shall be my God) before she has any prospect of material benefit. The commitment is to the covenant, not to the covenant's rewards.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    1:8–13

    Naomi's three-fold attempt to send her daughters-in-law back is a masterpiece of narrative structure. Alter notes Naomi uses legal, emotional, and theological arguments — the women can remarry (legal), Naomi is too old to provide husbands (personal), the LORD's hand is against her (theological). She is releasing them with every legitimate argument available. Their resistance to being released is the measure of their loyalty.

    1:14

    Orpah's departure and Ruth's clinging are narrated in a single sentence — 'Orpah kissed her mother-in-law goodbye, but Ruth clung to her.' Alter notes the contrast is achieved without moral judgment of Orpah. She does the reasonable thing; Ruth does the extraordinary thing. The narrative holds both choices without condemning the first, which makes the second even more remarkable.

    Hubbard — NICOT Ruth

    Robert L. Hubbard Jr., NICOT Commentary on Ruth (1988) — Scholarly Paraphrase
    1:8

    The word hesed (loyal love, lovingkindness) appears three times in Ruth (1:8; 2:20; 3:10) and is the book's theological keyword. Hubbard notes that Naomi prays YHWH will show hesed to her daughters-in-law because she herself can no longer do so. The hesed she cannot provide, she prays God will supply. The book traces that prayer's answer through Boaz.

    1:16–17

    Ruth's declaration (vv.16–17) is the Book of Ruth's theological summit. Hubbard notes the declaration is structured as a tricolon of commitment (go/lodge/die) with a theological capstone ('your God my God') and a death-oath ('the LORD deal with me'). Ruth is not merely expressing affection; she is making a legally binding covenant declaration — the same structure as covenant oaths throughout the OT.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    1:16

    Calvin on Ruth's declaration: 'Ruth, by following her mother-in-law, gave proof of remarkable piety toward God, since she preferred the worship of God and fellowship with his people to her native country, her kindred, and all earthly advantages.' The declaration is fundamentally theological — the family loyalty is the form that covenant commitment takes.

    1:17

    Calvin on the death-oath: 'Death alone shall separate us — and not even death, for I wish to be buried in the same place.' Ruth's commitment extends beyond death. Calvin reads this as a figure of the believer's union with the covenant people — a commitment that no circumstance, however extreme, can dissolve.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    1:16

    The NET note on 'your God will be my God' explains that Ruth's declaration constitutes a formal conversion — she is adopting YHWH as her deity and Israel as her people. The formula parallels covenant-making language throughout the OT (Gen 17:7–8; Exod 6:7; Jer 31:33). Ruth is not making a social choice but a theological one.

    1:14

    The NET note on 'Ruth clung to her' (davqah bo) uses the same verb (davaq) as Gen 2:24 ('a man shall... cling to his wife') and Deut 10:20 ('hold fast to him'). The note identifies the word as covenant-loyalty language — Ruth's clinging to Naomi echoes Israel's commanded clinging to YHWH. The family loyalty and the theological commitment are inseparable.

    Verses 19–22 — Arrival in Bethlehem: Naomi's Lament; Harvest Begins
    19202122

    Hebrew Word Study

    māraʾ ma-RAH — bitter / defiant: Naomi's request to be called Māraʾ instead of Naomi ("pleasant") is a wordplay of theological precision (v.20). The root mārāh appears in Israel's complaint at the bitter waters of Marah (Exod 15:23) and describes Israel's rebellion against God. Naomi is not merely sad — she is naming her condition as a kind of spiritual desolation, a felt contradiction between God's name and her experience.

    Shaddai sha-DAI — the Almighty / God of the mountains: Naomi uses ʾEl Shaddai (v.20-21) — the patriarchal name for God (Gen 17:1; 28:3; 35:11), associated with covenant promise and fruitfulness. The irony is devastating: Shaddai, the God who promised Abraham fruitfulness, has returned Naomi empty (rēqam). She is measuring the distance between God's promises and her present reality using the name that most embodies those promises.

    qāṣîr śĕʿōrîm kah-TZEER seh-o-REEM — barley harvest: The narrator's closing note that "they arrived at the beginning of the barley harvest" (v.22) is an agricultural calendar marker with theological significance. Harvest in the OT is the time of abundance that God provides (Deut 16:9-12) and of the gleaning laws that protect the poor (Lev 19:9-10). The timing signal tells the reader: Providence is moving. The emptiness of v.21 will be filled in what follows.

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    The arrival scene is brief but theologically precise. Naomi's lament (vv.20–21) is not apostasy — it is honest prayer, the language of the lament psalms. She names God as the agent of her affliction using his most sovereign title (Shaddai) — which is also faith. The chapter's final verse holds the whole reversal in two details: Ruth the Moabite (her ethnic identity, which makes everything that follows remarkable) and the barley harvest beginning (the physical mechanism of God's provision about to begin).

    Cross-Reference

    • Exod 15:23Marah — the bitter waters. Naomi's self-naming draws on exodus vocabulary of wilderness suffering. As Marah was transformed (Exod 15:25), so will Naomi's bitterness be.
    • Ruth 3:17Don't go back to your mother-in-law empty-handed. The direct reversal of rēqām — Boaz's gift is the beginning of the answer to Naomi's lament.

    The whole town is stirred by their arrival. Naomi is returning to a community that remembers her — the town's reaction confirms this is a homecoming, not an arrival. The barley harvest timing places them in spring: the narrator signals that providence has positioned them correctly.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    1:20-21

    MacArthur: Call me Mara — Naomi's name change is theological confession: she attributes her suffering directly to the LORD. This is not despair but robust covenant theology. She believes God governs her circumstances; she simply cannot yet see his purpose. Her theology is sound; her circumstances will validate it.

    1:22

    MacArthur: They arrived at the beginning of the barley harvest — the narrator's temporal marker is also providential signal. The harvest season was the context of gleaning rights (Lev 19:9-10), which will drive the next chapter's encounter. The timing that seems coincidental is the covenant God positioning his provision.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    1:20–21

    Naomi's lament is structured as a double contrast: 'I went out full, but the LORD has brought me back empty.' The Hebrew is exact — male'ah (full) and reqam (empty). Alter notes this is the book's central antithesis, which ch.4 will reverse: 'Naomi has a son' (4:17). The woman who returned empty will be filled — but by a Moabite daughter-in-law, not by a direct divine miracle.

    1:20-21

    Naomi's lament is compressed into one perfectly balanced sentence: 'I went out full — the LORD has brought me back empty.' Alter notes the antithesis of male'ah (full) and reqam (empty) is the book's structural pivot. The rest of the book is the narrative of how the empty woman is filled.

    Hubbard — NICOT Ruth

    Robert L. Hubbard Jr., NICOT Commentary on Ruth (1988) — Scholarly Paraphrase
    1:20

    Naomi's renaming from 'Pleasant' to 'Bitter' (Mara) is a formal act of self-identification with her condition. Hubbard notes the name change echoes Jacob's wrestling renaming (Gen 32) and Abraham's renaming (Gen 17) — names in the OT encode identity and destiny. Naomi claims a new identity that the rest of the book will then reverse: the woman who calls herself Bitter will be filled.

    1:22

    The harvest notice that closes ch.1 is carefully placed. Hubbard notes that the agricultural calendar creates the story's timeline: barley harvest begins the provision that will fill Naomi. The emptiness of v.21 and the full fields of v.22 are deliberately juxtaposed. The reader sees what Naomi cannot yet see: the answer to her complaint is already in the ground.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    1:20

    Calvin on Naomi's complaint: 'She did not complain against God with murmuring and impatience, but acknowledged that he was the author of her afflictions. Such acknowledgment, however bitter the feeling, is the beginning of true faith's response to suffering.' Naomi's theology is intact even while her circumstance is broken. She accuses God accurately — and that accuracy is itself a form of trust.

    1:20-21

    Calvin: Naomi does not attribute her suffering to chance or to enemy malice but directly to the LORD. Her theology is intact even while her circumstances are broken. This honest acknowledgment of divine agency — however painful — is the beginning of genuine faith's response to suffering.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    1:20

    The NET note on Shaddai (El Shaddai, 'God Almighty') discusses the divine name used twice in Naomi's lament (vv.20–21). The name emphasises divine power and sufficiency. Naomi uses it deliberately — she is not complaining against a weak god who failed her but against the Almighty who chose to afflict her. The complaint is theologically precise.

    1:20-22

    The NET note on Naomi's arrival in Bethlehem 'as the barley harvest was beginning' explains the agricultural calendar's theological function: Naomi returns in the season of potential provision. The harvest beginning at the chapter's close is the narrative's quiet announcement that the emptying is not the end.

    Scholarly Debates

    The Date of Ruth: Pre-Exilic or Post-Exilic Composition?
    Pre-exilic, possibly Solomonic era
    Most evangelical scholars (Block, Bush, Hubbard)
    The archaic legal practices described (the sandal-removal ceremony, the kinsman-redeemer institution) fit the pre-monarchic or early monarchic period. The book's positive portrayal of Moabites would be unusual in the post-exilic period (cf. Ezra-Nehemiah's harsh stance). The genealogy ending with David (4:17–22) suggests composition shortly after David's rise.
    Post-exilic, composed as a counter-narrative to Ezra-Nehemiah
    Many critical scholars (Würthwein, Sasson)
    Ruth's positive portrayal of a Moabite woman and her integration into Israel counters the Ezra-Nehemiah programme of expelling foreign wives. The book is a literary argument for inclusion, composed after Ezra's reforms to challenge their rigidity.
    Persian period, but not as Ezra-Nehemiah polemic
    Some scholars (Hubbard partial)
    The language shows some late features but the legal customs appear archaic. The book may have been composed or heavily edited in the Persian period without being directly polemical.
    The question remains open. The archaic legal customs, the early genealogy reference, and the book's positive OT theological themes are consistent with pre-exilic composition. The post-exilic polemic reading is possible but requires reading the book as primarily argumentative rather than primarily narrative. Most evangelical scholars prefer a pre-exilic date.
