Ruth 3

Naomi's Plan; the Threshing Floor; Ruth's Proposal; Boaz's Oath

Author

Unknown; Jewish tradition attributes authorship to Samuel.

When written

c.1000 BC, possibly early monarchy period.

What prompted it

To record God's providential care for a Moabite widow and her mother-in-law, and to trace the lineage of King David.

Verses 1–6 — Naomi's Plan: The Threshing Floor
1One day Ruth's mother-in-law Naomi said to her, "My daughter, I must find a home for you, where you will be well provided for.2Now Boaz, with whose women you have been working, is a relative of ours. Tonight he will be winnowing barley on the threshing floor.3Wash, put on perfume, and get dressed in your best clothes. Then go down to the threshing floor, but don't let him know you are there until he has finished eating and drinking.4When he lies down, note the place where he is lying. Then go and uncover his feet and lie down. He will tell you what to do."5"I will do whatever you say," Ruth answered.6So she went down to the threshing floor and did everything her mother-in-law told her to do.

Hebrew Word Study

mānôaḥ ma-NO-akh — rest / settled security: Naomi's desire to find "a home" (mānôaḥ) for Ruth (v.1) uses the same word she used of the LORD's gift to the daughters-in-law in 1:9 — "may the LORD grant you rest (mānôaḥ)." The word signifies more than shelter: it is covenant security, the settled condition of one whose future is secured. Naomi is now actively working to bring about what she had previously only prayed for — she has moved from lament to agency.

gōren GO-ren — threshing floor: The threshing floor (v.2-3) is a liminal space in the OT — a place of agricultural abundance (Hos 9:1-2), of legal transaction (2 Sam 24:18-25), and in this narrative, of covenant negotiation. At night, after the celebratory harvest meal, the workers sleep on the threshing floor to guard the grain. Naomi's plan places Ruth in a space where legal-covenant action can occur, under cover of both darkness and the legitimate social custom of workers sleeping near the harvest.

yādaʿ ya-DAH — to know: Naomi's instruction that Ruth should "uncover his feet and lie down, and he will tell you what to do" (v.4) uses yādaʿ — the rich Hebrew verb that encompasses intellectual knowledge, experiential knowledge, and intimate relationship (Gen 4:1). The ambiguity is intentional: Ruth will make herself known to Boaz as a covenant claimant, and Boaz — a man of integrity who already "knows" Ruth's character (2:11) — will respond with equal covenant faithfulness.

Historical Context

None

Context

Naomi's plan is bold and legally precise. She is not passive — she is engineering the redemption using the tools available to her. The instructions (wash, dress, approach at night, uncover feet, lie down) are unconventional but not improper. The threshing floor setting was public enough to be non-scandalous, private enough for a confidential proposal. Ruth's response ("I will do whatever you say") echoes the covenant commitment of 1:16.

    The threshing floor is elevated and wind-exposed for chaff separation — geography serving agriculture. Its between-time quality (working by day, workers sleeping there at night during harvest) makes it the site for Ruth's bold legal petition.

    The threshing floor was both work site and social space during harvest season. Naomi's plan exploits its geography: isolated enough for private conversation, close enough to the community that Boaz's honour requires him to act properly.

    Verses 7–13 — The Proposal: Kānāp; ʾĒšet Ḥayil; Boaz's Oath
    7When Boaz had finished eating and drinking and was in good spirits, he went to lie down at the far end of the grain pile. Ruth approached quietly, uncovered his feet and lay down.8In the middle of the night something startled the man; he turned—and there was a woman lying at his feet!9"Who are you?" he asked. "I am your servant Ruth," she said. "Spread the corner of your garment over me, since you are a guardian-redeemer of our family."10"The LORD bless you, my daughter," he replied. "This kindness is greater than that which you showed earlier: You have not run after the younger men, whether rich or poor.11And now, my daughter, don't be afraid. I will do for you all you ask. All the people of my town know that you are a woman of noble character.12Although it is true that I am a guardian-redeemer of our family, there is another who is more closely related than I.13Stay here for the night, and in the morning if he wants to do his duty as your guardian-redeemer, good; let him redeem you. But if he is not willing, as surely as the LORD lives, I will do it. Lie down until morning."

    Hebrew Word Study

    kānāp ka-NAF — wing / skirt / corner of garment: Ruth's request that Boaz "spread the corner of his garment over her" (v.9) uses the same word Boaz used in his blessing (2:12) — "may you be repaid by the LORD God of Israel, under whose wings you have come to take refuge." Ruth is asking Boaz to become the human instrument of his own prayer. The kānāp is simultaneously the symbol of marriage (Ezek 16:8) and of divine protection — Boaz spreading it over Ruth is both a marriage proposal acceptance and a covenant-protective act.

    ʾēšet ḥayil AY-shet kha-YIL — woman of noble character / valiant woman: Boaz's declaration that "all the people of my town know that you are a woman of noble character" (v.11) uses the precise phrase of Prov 31:10 — the book's concluding portrait of ideal womanhood. The term ḥayil, used of Boaz himself in 2:1, is now attributed to Ruth. The narrator is completing a theological portrait: these two — man and woman of ḥayil — are matched not by accident but by character, and the town's corporate witness confirms it.

    gāʾal ga-AL — to redeem / act as kinsman-redeemer: Boaz's acknowledgment that there is "a kinsman-redeemer closer than I" (v.12) introduces the legal complication that structures chapter 4. The verb gāʾal carries full legal weight: redemption of property, redemption of the widow, perpetuation of the dead man's name and line. Boaz does not sidestep the legal precedence of the nearer kinsman — his integrity requires that the law be followed even when it might cost him Ruth. This is ḥesed enacted in legal form.

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    The threshing floor scene is the book's most delicate. Ruth's proposal is legally precise (invoking the gōʾēl obligation) and personally courageous (choosing an older man of standing when she could have sought younger suitors). Nothing improper occurs. Boaz responds with legal precision — he accepts, praises her ḥesed, acknowledges the complication (a nearer redeemer exists), and makes a binding oath. The scene is governed entirely by honour.

    Cross-Reference

    • Prov 31:10ʾēšet ḥayil — "who can find a woman of noble character?" Boaz applies the Proverbs 31 title to Ruth here, before it appears in Proverbs. Ruth is the living embodiment of the poem.

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    3:9

    Ruth's request — 'spread your kanaf over me' — directly echoes Boaz's prayer in 2:12 ('under whose wings you have taken refuge'). Alter notes the verbal echo is the book's most elegant piece of narrative craft: Ruth is asking Boaz to become the answer to his own prayer. The kanaf that YHWH was asked to provide in ch.2 is now the kanaf that Boaz is asked to provide in ch.3. Human hesed is the medium of divine hesed.

    3:10

    Boaz's blessing of Ruth is structured as a comparison: her second hesed (pursuing Boaz rather than a younger man) is greater than her first (accompanying Naomi). Alter notes the comparison elevates Ruth: the woman who displayed remarkable loyalty in ch.1 has now displayed even more remarkable fidelity. Boaz recognises a pattern of character, not merely a single generous act.

    Hubbard — NICOT Ruth

    Robert L. Hubbard Jr., NICOT Commentary on Ruth (1988) — Scholarly Paraphrase
    3:9

    Ruth's request — 'spread your kanaf over me' — is the book's most theologically precise moment. Hubbard notes that spreading one's garment over a woman was a recognised gesture of betrothal in the ancient Near East (Ezek 16:8 uses the same imagery for God's covenant with Israel: 'I spread the corner of my garment over you and covered your nakedness'). Ruth is formally proposing within the covenant-marriage framework.

    3:10–11

    Boaz's designation of Ruth as eshet chayil ('woman of valor/character') echoes his own description as ish gibbor chayil (2:1). Hubbard notes the deliberate verbal parallel — the narrator has been preparing this moment since ch.2's introduction of Boaz. The two principal characters are moral equals, both characterised by chayil (strength, valor, excellence of character).

    Verses 14–18 — Six Measures; Naomi's Confidence
    14So she lay at his feet until morning, but got up before anyone could be recognized; and he said, "No one must know that a woman came to the threshing floor."15He also said, "Bring me the shawl you are wearing and hold it out." When she did so, he poured into it six measures of barley and placed it on her. Then he went back to town.16When Ruth came to her mother-in-law, Naomi asked, "How did it go, my daughter?" Then she told her everything Boaz had done for her17and added, "He gave me these six measures of barley, saying, 'Don't go back to your mother-in-law empty-handed.'"18Then Naomi said, "Wait, my daughter, until you find out what happens. For the man will not rest until the matter is settled today."

    Hebrew Word Study

    šēš śĕʿōrîm SHAYSH seh-o-REEM — six measures of barley: Boaz's gift of six measures of barley (v.15-17) is a symbolic quantity. Six is one short of seven (completeness/rest/Sabbath) — but generous beyond ordinary gleaning. The gift signals that a covenant process has begun that is not yet complete. Naomi interprets it correctly: "he will not rest (šāqaṭ) until the matter is settled today" (v.18). The six measures are a promise-in-kind: the seventh measure of completeness (the marriage, the son, the restored family) is coming.

    šāqaṭ sha-KAT — to be quiet / to rest / to settle: Naomi's confident prediction that Boaz "will not rest" (v.18) until the matter is resolved uses a word that denotes the settled peace of resolution. The same root describes the land's rest from war in Joshua (Josh 11:23; 14:15). Naomi has moved from the bitter woman who asked to be called Māraʾ to a woman of remarkable theological confidence, reading the signs of Boaz's character and predicting, rightly, that his integrity will drive the narrative to its covenant conclusion.

    ʾēm AYM — mother: The narrative's repeated "mother-in-law" language (ḥāmôt) throughout chapters 1-3 builds toward chapter 4's resolution, where the women of Bethlehem declare that Ruth "is better to you than seven sons" (4:15) and Naomi becomes ʾômenet (foster-mother) to the child. Ruth the Moabite, who had no place in Israel's covenant structure, becomes the conduit through which Naomi's "mother" status is restored — a remarkable reversal of the emptiness of 1:20-21.

    The six measures of barley are the earnest — the legal pledge that the transaction will be completed.     Garment-spreading as proposal
    Ancient Near Eastern custom — a groom spreading his cloak over the bride as a covenant act. Ezek 16:8 uses it for God's covenant with Israel.
    The gesture has clear legal and covenantal significance beyond romance.
    Six measures
    The amount is not specified — barley is measured in seahs or ephahs. Whatever the exact quantity, it was enough that Naomi immediately asked "how did it go?" and Ruth needed to explain.
    The generosity signals earnest intent.

    Textual Notes

    Scholarly Debates

    The Morality of the Threshing Floor Scene: Naomi's Plan and Ruth's Action
    Entirely proper: a legal marriage proposal
    Bush, Block, most evangelical commentators
    The scene is structured as a formal go'el marriage petition. The uncovering of feet, the lying at his feet, the request to spread his kanaf — all are elements of a recognised legal proposal, not a sexual encounter. The narrator's silence about any impropriety is evidence of propriety. Boaz's immediate concern for Ruth's reputation (3:14) confirms the scene was understood as legally, not sexually, charged.
    Ambiguous: the text deliberately leaves the night ambiguous
    Some feminist and literary scholars
    The scene deliberately uses vocabulary with sexual connotations (feet can be a euphemism, the darkness, the secrecy). The ambiguity is the point — the narrator leaves open what happened in order to focus on Ruth and Boaz's character: whatever happened, both acted honourably.
    The narrative's evidence points strongly toward a proper legal transaction: Boaz's blessing (3:10), his concern for reputation (3:14), the immediate move to formal legal process in ch.4. The book's moral theology throughout emphasises hesed — covenant faithfulness — not sexual opportunism. The threshing floor scene is hesed in its most daring form.
