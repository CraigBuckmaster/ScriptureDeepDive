Ruth 2

Ruth Gleans; Boaz's Generosity; The Gōʾēl Revealed

See on Timeline — Ruth
Author

Unknown; Jewish tradition attributes authorship to Samuel.

When written

c.1000 BC, possibly early monarchy period.

What prompted it

To record God's providential care for a Moabite widow and her mother-in-law, and to trace the lineage of King David.

Verses 1–7 — Boaz Introduced; Ruth Gleans; The Overseer's Report
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

gibbôr ḥayil gi-BOR kha-YIL — man of standing / mighty man of valour: The phrase used of Boaz (v.1) is the OT's highest social commendation — used of Gideon (Judg 6:12), Jephthah (Judg 11:1), and Israel's warriors. It combines social prominence with moral character. The same root (ḥayil) will later describe Ruth herself (3:11: ʾēšet ḥayil — "woman of noble character"), creating a deliberate parallel between the two characters the narrative is drawing together.

lāqaṭ la-KAT — to glean: Ruth's request to glean (v.2) invokes the Torah's specific poverty-relief legislation (Lev 19:9-10; 23:22; Deut 24:19-21). The gleaning laws required landowners to leave the edges of their fields unharvested for the poor, widow, orphan, and alien — all four of which describe Ruth. Her use of the legal category signals she knows Israeli law and is operating within it, even as a Moabite foreigner.

miqreh mik-REH — chance / as it happened: The narrator's phrase "as it happened, she came to the portion of the field belonging to Boaz" (v.3) uses the Hebrew word for "chance" or "coincidence" — the only use of this word in the book. The narrator is being deliberately ironic: the theological point of the whole book is that there is no such thing as mere coincidence in a world governed by divine providence. The "chance" leads directly to the kinsman-redeemer.

Historical Context

None

Context

The opening of ch.2 works on two levels. On the surface: a Moabite widow exercises her legal gleaning rights in the fields outside Bethlehem. Beneath the surface: the gōʾēl (kinsman-redeemer) has just been named in v.1, and Ruth has "chanced" into his field. The narrative's use of coincidence language highlights that the coincidence is providential.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    2:1-7

    MacArthur: Boaz is introduced as a man of standing (gibor chayil — the same phrase used of military valor). His character is displayed immediately: he greets his workers with a blessing (the LORD be with you), he allows Ruth to glean, he instructs his men to leave extra grain. Covenant generosity is the natural expression of a man who fears God.

    2:3

    MacArthur: She happened to come to the portion of the field belonging to Boaz — the narrator's understatement is the book's theological signature. The providence that positions Ruth in Boaz's field is not announced; it simply happens. God's governance of the ordinary is the book of Ruth's dominant theme: the extraordinary is produced by a series of apparently coincidental ordinary events.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    2:1–3

    Alter notes the opening of ch.2 introduces Boaz in the third person before moving to Ruth in the first — a technique that allows the reader to carry privileged information (Boaz's identity, his kinsman-relationship) into the scene that Ruth enters without that knowledge. The dramatic irony is structural: we know more than Ruth does, which makes Boaz's kindness even more significant.

    2:3

    The phrase 'as it turned out' (wayyiqer miqreha) is one of the Hebrew Bible's most deliberate uses of apparent coincidence to signal providence. Alter notes the narrator carefully avoids saying 'God led her' — the theological point is made through the language of contingency, which is more sophisticated than direct assertion. The story shows providence without announcing it.

    Hubbard — NICOT Ruth

    Robert L. Hubbard Jr., NICOT Commentary on Ruth (1988) — Scholarly Paraphrase
    2:1

    Hubbard notes the introduction of Boaz as 'a kinsman of Naomi's husband' (moda' le'ishaah) before he appears in the story is a standard biblical technique of narrative foreshadowing. The reader is given the information needed to understand the significance of what is about to happen: this man is not just a field owner but a potential redeemer.

    2:3

    The Hebrew miqreh (chance, accident, what happens to one) is used with deliberate irony. Hubbard notes the word appears in Ecclesiastes for the random 'chance' that befalls all (e.g., Eccl 2:14–15). Here the narrator uses the word of human randomness for what is in fact divine guidance. The 'chance' of Ruth's field selection is the narrative form of unseen providence.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    2:1

    Calvin on Boaz's introduction: 'God thus provided for the necessities of his handmaiden Ruth, conducting her feet by a secret influence to the field of Boaz, though she was ignorant whither she went.' The providence is real even though Ruth does not know it. Calvin reads the narrative's apparent coincidences as the form that divine guidance takes in ordinary life.

    2:3

    Calvin on 'as it turned out': 'The Holy Spirit here commends the providence of God, which directed the wandering steps of Ruth so that she came, without knowing it, to the very field of the near kinsman, who was to be her husband.' The 'chance' is the language of human perception; the reality is divine direction.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    2:1

    The NET note on gibbor chayil ('man of standing/valor') explains the phrase is used for military heroes (Judg 11:1; 1 Sam 9:1), wealthy men (1 Kgs 11:28), and men of social standing and moral character. Applied to Boaz it encompasses all three dimensions: he has wealth (a large field), social standing (respected at the gate), and moral character (his treatment of Ruth demonstrates).

    2:3

    The NET note on miqreha (her chance/fortune happened) notes the word's ironic use. The same root appears in 1 Sam 6:9 for the Philistines testing whether the ark's return was 'chance' or divine act. The narrator uses secular vocabulary to describe what the story reveals as divine guidance — a sophisticated narrative theology that respects human contingency while asserting divine sovereignty.

    Verses 8–17 — Boaz's Generosity: Protection, Meal, and an Ephah
    891011121314151617

    Hebrew Word Study

    kānāp ka-NAF — wing / hem / corner: Boaz prays that the LORD repay Ruth under whose "wings" she has taken refuge (v.12) — the same word that will describe Boaz's own robe in 3:9. The image of refuge under divine wings appears in the Psalms as the ultimate picture of divine protection (Ps 17:8; 36:7; 57:1; 91:4). Boaz's blessing over Ruth is the theological frame that his own subsequent actions will fulfil: he becomes the human answer to his own prayer.

    gāmar ga-MAR — to complete / fully reward: Boaz's prayer that "the LORD... fully reward you" (v.12) uses a word meaning to bring to completion or fulfilment. The same root underlies the prayer in Psalm 57:2 — "God who fulfils his purpose for me." The book of Ruth is structured around the movement toward completeness: from emptiness (1:21) to fullness, from broken family to restored genealogy, from Naomi's bitter complaint to her neighbours' blessing.

    ḥāzaq kha-ZAK — to strengthen / encourage: Boaz's practical provision — parched grain, water, the right to glean in his field — is described in terms that go beyond generosity to active strengthening. When Ruth returns to Naomi with an ephah of barley (v.17), it is approximately 22 litres — roughly ten times what a gleaner could normally expect. Boaz's provision is exorbitant, deliberately so, signalling that something more than charity is at work.

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    Boaz's behaviour in vv.8–16 consistently exceeds the legal minimum. The law required leaving field edges; Boaz permits gleaning among the sheaves and instructs his men to pull out extra stalks. The law required access to water; Boaz provides it proactively. The meal inclusion (v.14) is entirely above and beyond — Boaz serves Ruth himself and she eats until satisfied with food left over. He is practising ḥesed before anyone has asked him to.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    2:8-16

    MacArthur: Boaz's sevenfold care for Ruth — permission to glean, instruction to stay, protection from the men, invitation to water, shared meal, deliberate extra grain — exceeds all gleaning-law requirements. He goes beyond what the law demands because hesed is the overflow of character, not the calculation of obligation.

    2:11-12

    MacArthur: Boaz has heard Ruth's entire story before he meets her. His blessing (May the LORD repay you) is prophetic — he will become the instrument of that repayment. The man who invokes the LORD's reward is the man God will use to deliver it. Boaz prays what he will perform.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    2:8–13

    The dialogue between Boaz and Ruth is one of the Bible's most carefully observed social exchanges. Alter notes the delicacy of their respective speech acts: Boaz commands and provides without condescending; Ruth responds with profound humility without self-abasement. The conversation is a dance of social rank and moral equality — Boaz recognises in Ruth a quality that transcends her legal status as a foreigner.

    2:11–12

    Boaz's speech to Ruth in vv.11–12 demonstrates that he has done research: he knows everything Ruth has done for Naomi. The detail — leaving father, mother, homeland, coming to a people you did not know — echoes Abraham's call (Gen 12:1). Alter notes Boaz implicitly compares Ruth to Abraham: both left everything to follow the covenant God to an unknown land. The comparison elevates Ruth to the company of the patriarchs.

    Hubbard — NICOT Ruth

    Robert L. Hubbard Jr., NICOT Commentary on Ruth (1988) — Scholarly Paraphrase
    2:8–9

    Boaz's immediate provision for Ruth exceeds the requirements of the gleaning law (Lev 19:9–10; 23:22). Hubbard notes that the law required only that corners and dropped grain be left — it did not require an invitation to stay, water provision, or worker protection. Boaz goes far beyond legal obligation: he is exercising hesed, not merely fulfilling law.

    2:12

    The 'wings' of YHWH under which Ruth has taken refuge (kenafav YHWH Elohei Yisrael) is a rich metaphor. Hubbard notes the same image appears in Ps 17:8; 36:7; 57:1; 91:4. In Ruth 3:9, Ruth asks Boaz to spread his 'wing' (kanaf) over her — the same word. Boaz's prayer in 2:12 is answered in 3:9 by his own action: he becomes the instrument of the divine wing-shelter he invoked.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    2:12

    Calvin on the wing-shelter metaphor: 'Under the wings of God means under his protection — as a hen gathers her chickens under her wings (Matt 23:37). Ruth, forsaking her country and her gods, had sought the protection of the true God; Boaz recognises this as a remarkable act of faith and prays it may be richly rewarded.' The prayer is simultaneously recognition and intercession.

    2:8

    Calvin on Boaz's protection of Ruth: 'Boaz commands that she should not be molested by any of his servants — he exercises a paternal care over a stranger, not because of any natural affection but from a pure desire to show kindness to the destitute.' Boaz's generosity is theologically motivated, not sentimentally motivated.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    2:12

    The NET note on 'under whose wings you have taken refuge' (chased lachasot tachat kenafav) discusses the bird-wing metaphor in the Psalms (17:8; 36:7; 57:1; 61:4; 63:7; 91:4). The note identifies this as a legal asylum metaphor — the wings provide protection from threat. Ruth's taking refuge under YHWH's wings is formally equivalent to conversion and covenant membership.

    2:14-16

    The NET note on 'dip your bread in the wine vinegar' and 'take some roasted grain' explains these are the standard fieldworkers' meal — bread, wine, and grain. Boaz invites Ruth to share the workers' meal, not a formal banquet. The generosity is in the welcome, not the food: she eats with the harvesters as an equal.

    Verses 18–23 — Return to Naomi: The Gōʾēl Revealed
    181920212223

    Hebrew Word Study

    gōʾēl go-EL — kinsman-redeemer: Naomi's exclamation that Boaz is "one of our kinsman-redeemers" (v.20) introduces the book's central legal institution. The gōʾēl was a near male relative with specific obligations: to redeem land sold under poverty (Lev 25:25), to marry a childless widow (levirate marriage, Deut 25:5-10), and to avenge a kinsman's blood (Num 35:19). The three obligations converge in Boaz. The word becomes one of the OT's richest theological metaphors for God's own redemptive character (Isa 41:14; 43:14; 44:6).

    ḥesed KHEH-sed — loyal love / covenant faithfulness: Naomi's blessing — "May the LORD... show kindness (ḥesed) to the living and the dead" (v.20) — uses the book's keynote word for the second time. The phrase "the living and the dead" is remarkable: Naomi sees Boaz's kindness to Ruth as simultaneously honouring her dead husband and sons. In Hebrew covenant thinking, faithfulness to the dead — keeping memory, fulfilling obligation, carrying name forward — is as binding as faithfulness to the living.

    šāmar sha-MAR — keep / watch over / guard: Boaz's instruction that Ruth "stay close" to his workers uses the verb šāmar (v.21-22), which carries the sense of careful watching and protective keeping. The same verb describes God keeping Israel (Num 6:24; Ps 121:3-8). Ruth gleaning "close to the women of Boaz's household" while under his protective oversight is a picture of the covenant community's protection of the vulnerable — enacted here by one man of integrity.

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    The return scene pivots the entire story. Naomi's theology shifts in v.20 — from "the LORD has brought misfortune upon me" (1:21) to "The LORD bless him!" She uses the divine name for the first time since her lament, now in blessing rather than accusation. The gōʾēl recognition is the moment Naomi sees the hand of God in the coincidence of v.3. He has not stopped showing ḥesed to the living and the dead.

      MacArthur: Naomi's recognition of the LORD's kindness has not abandoned the living and the dead is the theological climax of ch.2.       2:20

      Alter — Literary Reading

      Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
      2:19–23

      Naomi's questioning of Ruth and Ruth's revelation of Boaz's name produces the book's second major recognition scene. Alter notes the pacing: Naomi's questions (where, where), Ruth's answer (a man named Boaz), Naomi's blessing and recognition (he is our go'el) — each beat leads to the next revelation. The scene is perfectly structured to deliver maximum theological impact at minimum narrative length.

      2:20-23

      Naomi's exclamation connects the immediate (Boaz's generosity) to the structural (his go'el status). Alter notes the narrative economy: one chapter of gleaning discloses the entire legal framework that will govern the book's resolution. What appeared to be a chance gleaning session has opened a covenant legal pathway to restoration.

      Hubbard — NICOT Ruth

      Robert L. Hubbard Jr., NICOT Commentary on Ruth (1988) — Scholarly Paraphrase
      2:20

      Naomi's exclamation at Boaz's name introduces the go'el concept — the kinsman-redeemer whose covenant obligation to the family includes redeeming property and potentially marrying the widow. Hubbard notes the institution is rooted in Leviticus 25's redemption laws and Deuteronomy 25's levirate laws. Naomi immediately perceives the legal possibility that Ruth's gleaning has opened.

      2:23

      The closing notice — Ruth gleaned with Boaz's workers until the end of the barley and wheat harvests — spans approximately seven weeks (Passover to Pentecost). Hubbard notes this extended provision is itself an expression of hesed: Boaz's generosity sustains Naomi and Ruth through the entire harvest season, not just one encounter. The provision is systematic.

      Calvin’s Commentary

      John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
      2:20

      Calvin: 'By these words, Naomi first gives God the praise that is due to him, and acknowledges that his goodness had been shown both to the dead and to the living; and then she adds the reason why they might hope for something still greater from Boaz.' The recognition of hesed moves from past (the dead husband and sons) to present (Ruth and Naomi) to future (the redemption possibility Boaz represents).

      2:20-23

      Calvin: Naomi recognises that God has been at work in what appeared to be Ruth's individual initiative. The hesed she had prayed for in 1:8 is being answered through Ruth's gleaning and Boaz's generosity. God answers prayers through human acts without reducing those acts to mere instruments.

      2:20

Naomi's recognition that Boaz is their go'el opens all four possibilities simultaneously.

      2:19-23

      The NET note on Naomi's question 'where did you glean today?' discusses the function of her inquiry: she is doing more than asking out of curiosity. Her recognition of Boaz as their go'el is the theological insight the chapter has been building toward.

      Scholarly Debates

      The Go'el Institution: Levirate Marriage or Property Redemption?
      Property redemption primarily
      Most critical scholars
      The go'el in the Pentateuchal law is primarily a property-redemption institution (Lev 25:25–55). The levirate marriage law (Deut 25:5–10) is a separate institution applying specifically to a dead brother's widow. Ruth conflates or adapts both institutions in ways that don't exactly match either.
      A unified institution of family rescue
      Bush, Hubbard, most evangelical scholars
      Ruth presents a coherent institution in which property redemption and family preservation (including widow-care) belong together. The go'el who redeems property without marrying the widow leaves the family's future unprotected. Boaz understands the institution holistically — not as two separate legal obligations but as one act of family redemption.
      The legal background of Ruth 4 suggests Boaz understood his redemptive obligation to include both property and family continuity. Whether this represents a unified legal institution or a narrative adaptation of two separate laws, the theological point is the same: complete redemption requires addressing all dimensions of the family's need, not merely the most convenient one.
