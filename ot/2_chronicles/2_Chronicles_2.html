2 Chronicles 2

Preparations with Hiram of Tyre

Verses 1–10 — Solomon’s Message to Hiram; Temple Specifications
12345678910

Context

Solomon conscripts 153,600 labourers and sends to Huram (Hiram) of Tyre for materials and a master craftsman. His message to Huram contains a theological statement absent from Kings: “The temple I am going to build will be great, because our God is greater than all other gods.” Solomon acknowledges the temple’s inadequacy: “The heavens, even the highest heavens, cannot contain him. Who then am I to build a temple for him?” Grandeur and humility coexist.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 5:1–12The Kings parallel. The Chronicler expands Solomon’s theological reasoning and adds specifications.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:6

“Who am I to build a temple for him?” teaches that awareness of God’s transcendence should accompany every act of worship. We build for God knowing our buildings cannot contain him.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:6

The rhetorical question echoes Solomon’s later prayer (6:18) and David’s (1 Chr 17:16). The “who am I?” of inadequacy is a persistent Davidic-Solomonic theme.

Verses 11–18 — Huram’s Response; the Labour Force
1112131415161718

Context

Huram replies with a remarkable confession: “Because the LORD loves his people, he has made you their king.” A pagan king attributes Solomon’s kingship to YHWH’s love. He sends Huram-Abi, a mixed-heritage craftsman (Danite mother, Tyrian father). Solomon organises the alien labour force: 70,000 carriers, 80,000 stonecutters, 3,600 foremen. The numbers match the Exodus workforce — the temple is built by the same scale of organised labour as the tabernacle.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 36:1–7Bezalel and Oholiab, the tabernacle craftsmen. Huram-Abi is the temple’s equivalent — a divinely skilled artisan for sacred construction.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:11

A pagan king confessing YHWH teaches that God’s sovereignty is recognised even by those outside the covenant. Divine love for Israel is visible to the watching world.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:14

The mixed parentage of Huram-Abi differs from 1 Kgs 7:14 (which says his mother was from Naphtali). The discrepancy may reflect different traditions about tribal affiliation after intermarriage.
