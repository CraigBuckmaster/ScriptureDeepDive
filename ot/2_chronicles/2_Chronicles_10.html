2 Chronicles 10

The Kingdom Divides

Verses 1‐11 — Rehoboam at Shechem; the People’s Demand
1234567891011

Context

All Israel gathers at Shechem to make Rehoboam king. Jeroboam returns from Egypt. The people ask for lighter burdens. The elders counsel moderation; the young men counsel harshness. Rehoboam chooses the young men’s advice. The Chronicler follows 1 Kings 12 closely — this disaster is too important to modify.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 12:1–15The Kings parallel, nearly identical.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:8

Rehoboam’s folly teaches that leadership requires humility to receive counsel from those wiser than yourself. The most dangerous moment for any leader is the moment they stop listening.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:11

“Scorpions” (ʿaqrabbîm) likely refers to whips tipped with metal barbs. The metaphor communicates maximum cruelty.

Verses 12‐19 — Israel Rebels; the Kingdom Splits
1213141516171819

Context

The ten northern tribes reject Rehoboam: “What share do we have in David?” Adoram (the forced-labour overseer) is stoned to death. Rehoboam flees to Jerusalem. “So Israel has been in rebellion against the house of David to this day.” The Chronicler preserves the formula but his real concern is what happens next: the northern kingdom essentially disappears from his narrative. From here on, “Israel” in Chronicles means Judah.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 12:16–19The parallel. The Chronicler adds no unique material to the revolt itself.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:15

That the division was “from God” teaches that God accomplishes his purposes even through human folly. Providence is not thwarted by sin; it operates through it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:18

Adoram (also Adoniram in 1 Kgs 4:6) was the minister of forced labour (mas). His assassination was a targeted political act.
