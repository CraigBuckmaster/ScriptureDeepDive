Daniel 9

The Seventy Weeks

Verses 1–19 — “We Have Sinned and Done Wrong”
12345678910111213141516171819

Hebrew Word Study

תְחִנָּה təḥinnâ — plea for mercy / supplication: "I turned to the Lord God and pleaded with him in prayer and petition (təḥinnâ), in fasting, and in sackcloth and ashes" (v.3). Daniel reads Jeremiah’s prophecy that Jerusalem’s desolation will last seventy years (v.2; cf. Jer 25:11–12; 29:10). He responds not with curiosity but with confession: one of the greatest prayers in the OT (vv.4–19). "We have sinned and done wrong. We have been wicked and have rebelled" (v.5). Daniel includes himself in the national guilt: "we." The prayer confesses sin (vv.5–11), acknowledges God’s justice (vv.7, 14), and appeals to God’s mercy and reputation: "We do not make requests because of our righteousness, but because of your great mercy" (v.18). "For your sake, Lord, look with favour on your desolate sanctuary" (v.17).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:2–3

Daniel reads Jeremiah. Scripture interprets Scripture: the prophet studies the prophet. Daniel’s response to reading God’s word is prayer, not speculation. The seventy-year exile is ending; Daniel prays it into reality.

9:4–6

"Lord, the great and awesome God who keeps his covenant of love. We have sinned and done wrong. We have not listened to your servants the prophets." Covenant confession: God is faithful; we are unfaithful. The "we" includes Daniel himself.

9:17–19

"For your sake, Lord, look with favour on your desolate sanctuary. We do not make requests because of our righteousness, but because of your great mercy." The appeal is not to Israel’s merit but to God’s character and reputation. Mercy, not justice, is the basis of the prayer.

Verses 20–27 — “Seventy ‘Sevens’ Are Decreed”
2021222324252627

Hebrew Word Study

שָׁבֻעִים shābuʿîm — weeks / sevens / periods of seven: "Seventy ‘sevens’ (shābuʿîm) are decreed for your people and your holy city" (v.24). Gabriel arrives while Daniel is still praying (v.21). The prophecy: 70 sevens (490 years) are decreed to finish transgression, put an end to sin, atone for wickedness, bring in everlasting righteousness, seal up vision and prophecy, and anoint the Most Holy Place (v.24). The timeline: 7 sevens + 62 sevens from the decree to restore Jerusalem until "the Anointed One" (v.25). After the 62 sevens, the Anointed One will be "cut off and will have nothing" (v.26). A ruler will come and destroy the city and the sanctuary (v.26). He will confirm a covenant for one seven, and in the middle of that seven he will put an end to sacrifice (v.27). The most debated prophecy in the Bible: interpreted as pointing to Christ’s death, the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70, or Antiochus’s desecration — or all three in telescoping fulfilment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:24

"Seventy sevens are decreed to finish transgression, put an end to sin, atone for wickedness, bring in everlasting righteousness." Six purposes for the 490 years. The first three are negative (end sin); the last three are positive (establish righteousness). The cross accomplishes all six.

9:25

"From the issuing of the decree to restore and rebuild Jerusalem until the Anointed One, the ruler, comes, there will be seven sevens and sixty-two sevens." 7 + 62 = 69 sevens (483 years). From Artaxerxes’ decree (445 BC, Neh 2:1–8) to Christ’s triumphal entry: ~483 years. The calculation is debated but the Christological reading is ancient.

9:26

"After the sixty-two sevens, the Anointed One will be cut off and will have nothing." Cut off: killed. Have nothing: no kingdom, no visible vindication. The Messiah dies without receiving his kingdom — the cross.

9:27

"He will confirm a covenant with many for one seven. In the middle of the seven he will put an end to sacrifice and offering." The final seven: the most debated half-week in prophetic literature. Fulfilled at the cross (sacrifice ended), or still future (the tribulation), or both.
