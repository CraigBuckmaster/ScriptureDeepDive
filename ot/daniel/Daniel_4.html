Daniel 4

Nebuchadnezzar’s Madness and Restoration

Verses 1’18 — “The Tree That Reached to the Sky”
123456789101112131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

אִילָן ʾilān — tree: "I saw a tree (ʾilān) in the middle of the land. Its height was enormous. The tree grew large and strong; its top touched the sky" (vv.10–11). This chapter is Nebuchadnezzar’s own testimony — written in first person. He dreams of an enormous tree that shelters all creatures, then a heavenly messenger commands: "Cut down the tree! But let the stump remain, bound with iron and bronze. Let him be drenched with dew and live with the animals for seven periods of time, till he knows that the Most High is sovereign over all kingdoms" (vv.14–17). Daniel is terrified by the interpretation (v.19) but speaks it: the tree is Nebuchadnezzar (v.22).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1–3

Nebuchadnezzar writes in first person: "It is my pleasure to tell you about the miraculous signs the Most High God has performed for me." The king becomes a witness. This is his testimony.

4:10–12

The tree: enormous, beautiful, sheltering everything. The description echoes Ezekiel 31 — a great tree representing a great empire. The metaphor is fitting: empires shelter and provide, but they also claim the sky.

4:15–17

"Let the stump remain. Let him live with the animals for seven times, till he knows that the Most High is sovereign." The purpose of the madness is stated before it happens: recognition of divine sovereignty. The punishment is pedagogical, not punitive.

4:17

"The Most High is sovereign over all kingdoms on earth and gives them to anyone he wishes." The thesis restated. This is Daniel’s central claim about political power: God assigns it.

Verses 19–37 — “Now I, Nebuchadnezzar, Praise the King of Heaven”
19202122232425262728293031323334353637

Hebrew Word Study

עִלָּיְאָה ʿillāyʾâ — Most High: "Now I, Nebuchadnezzar, praise and exalt and glorify the King of heaven (ʿillāyʾâ), because everything he does is right and all his ways are just. And those who walk in pride he is able to humble" (v.37). Daniel interprets: you are the tree (v.22). The dream is fulfilled twelve months later when Nebuchadnezzar boasts from his palace roof: "Is not this the great Babylon I have built?" (v.30). Immediately a voice from heaven declares: your kingdom is departed (v.31). He is driven from people, eats grass like an ox, his hair grows like eagle feathers, his nails like bird claws (v.33). After "seven times," he looks up to heaven, his sanity returns, and he praises the Most High (v.34). The most powerful man on earth is reduced to an animal until he looks UP.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:27

"Renounce your sins by doing what is right, and your wickedness by being kind to the oppressed." Daniel’s counsel to the king: repentance + justice. The prescription for national leaders across all time.

4:30–31

"Is not this the great Babylon I have built by my mighty power and for the glory of my majesty?" The words are still on his lips when the voice speaks: "Your royal authority has been taken from you." The boast is the trigger. Pride literally precedes the fall.

4:33–34

Driven to the fields. Grass. Eagle-feather hair. Bird-claw nails. Then: "I raised my eyes toward heaven, and my sanity was restored." The direction of the gaze determines everything. Down = madness. Up = restoration. The chapter’s moral in two directions.

4:37

"Those who walk in pride he is able to humble." The last sentence of the king’s testimony. The most powerful pagan ruler in the OT ends his story by praising the God of Israel. The conversion is complete.
