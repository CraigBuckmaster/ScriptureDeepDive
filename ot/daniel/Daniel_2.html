Daniel 2

Nebuchadnezzar’s Dream: The Statue of Four Kingdoms

Verses 1–23 — “The Mystery Was Revealed to Daniel in a Vision of the Night”
1234567891011121314151617181920212223

Hebrew Word Study

רָז rāz — mystery / secret: "The mystery (rāz) was revealed to Daniel in a vision of the night" (v.19). Nebuchadnezzar demands his wise men tell him both the dream AND its meaning (vv.1–11). They protest: no king has ever asked this (v.10). Daniel asks for time, gathers friends to pray, and God reveals the mystery in a night vision (vv.14–19). Daniel’s praise-prayer declares God "changes times and seasons, removes kings and sets up kings" (v.21) — the book’s thesis in miniature.

Context

The second year of Nebuchadnezzar (c.603 BC). The demand to reveal both dream and interpretation is an impossible test designed to distinguish genuine divine access from clever guessing. Babylonian dream-interpretation manuals (omen texts) required the dreamer to recount the dream first. Nebuchadnezzar reverses the procedure. The chapter shifts from Hebrew to Aramaic at 2:4b and remains Aramaic through ch.7 — the lingua franca section addressing the nations directly.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 41:15–16Joseph interprets Pharaoh’s dream: "I cannot do it, but God will give the answer" — the same theology as Daniel 2:27–28.
  • Matt 21:44Jesus: "The stone will crush them" — echoing the unhewn stone of Daniel 2:34–35.
  • Rev 17:12–14Ten kings who receive authority for one hour — developing the ten-toe imagery of Daniel 2:41–43.
  • 1 Cor 1:20Where is the wise person? God made foolish the wisdom of the world — the pattern Daniel 2 establishes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1–6

The impossible demand: tell me the dream AND what it means. The king tests whether his advisors have genuine access to divine knowledge.

2:10–11

"No one can reveal it except the gods, and they do not live among humans." The wise men inadvertently state the theological truth — only God reveals mysteries, and he DOES dwell among his people through Daniel.

2:19–23

Daniel’s praise-prayer: "He changes times and seasons; he deposes kings and raises up kings." The theology of the entire book compressed into four verses.

2:20–21

"He gives wisdom to the wise and knowledge to the discerning. He reveals deep and hidden things." God as the source of all wisdom.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1–6

Calvin notes that God permitted the dream to torment Nebuchadnezzar so that the king’s anxiety would create the occasion for Daniel’s elevation. Providence works through psychological distress.

2:19–23

Calvin praises Daniel’s prayer as a model: first worship, then thanksgiving, then petition. The order matters — Daniel does not rush to the answer but pauses to praise the God who gives it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:4

The NET notes the language shift from Hebrew to Aramaic at 2:4b. The Aramaic section (2:4b–7:28) addresses themes relevant to all nations, not just Israel. The bilingual structure is deliberate.

2:21

The NET observes that "changes times and seasons" uses the Aramaic cognate of Hebrew ʿet (appointed time), asserting God’s sovereignty over the political calendar of empires.

Longman Notes

longman
2:19–23

Longman highlights the hymnic structure of Daniel’s prayer: it moves from God’s eternal nature (v.20) to his sovereign acts (v.21) to his revelation of mysteries (v.22) to personal thanksgiving (v.23). A four-stanza doxology.

2:21

Longman: "He deposes kings and raises up kings" is the thesis statement that chs.2–7 progressively demonstrate — through Nebuchadnezzar, Belshazzar, and Darius.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
2:4–11

Goldingay reads the wise men’s protest as an honest theological statement: "the gods do not live among humans." Daniel’s answer is that Israel’s God does — or at least, his wisdom does, through his servant. The contest is not Daniel vs magicians but YHWH vs "the gods."

2:19

Goldingay notes that the rāz (mystery) vocabulary here becomes central to Qumran’s self-understanding and to Paul’s concept of mystērion (Eph 3:3–6; Col 1:26–27).

Collins Notes

collins
2:1–11

Collins identifies the court-contest genre: a wise exile solves what the native experts cannot. The pattern appears in the Joseph cycle, Ahiqar, and later in the Contest of Darius’s Bodyguards (1 Esdras 3–4).

2:14–23

Collins notes that Daniel’s approach is diplomatic, not confrontational. He asks for time (v.16), organises prayer (v.17–18), and gives credit to God (v.28). The wise courtier navigates the system without compromising theology.

Verses 24–49 — “The God of Heaven Has Set Up a Kingdom That Will Never Be Destroyed”

Hebrew Word Study

אֶבֶן ʾeben — stone: "A rock (ʾeben) was cut out, but not by human hands. It struck the statue on its feet and smashed them" (v.34). Daniel reveals the dream: gold head (Babylon), silver chest (Medo-Persia), bronze belly (Greece), iron legs (Rome), iron-clay feet (divided kingdoms). A stone "cut out but not by human hands" strikes the feet and the entire statue collapses. The stone becomes a mountain that fills the earth (vv.34–35). "The God of heaven will set up a kingdom that will never be destroyed" (v.44).

Context

The four-kingdom schema was known in the ancient world (Hesiod’s ages of gold, silver, bronze, iron), but Daniel’s version is uniquely theological: the kingdoms decline not randomly but under divine sovereignty, and they are replaced not by a fifth human empire but by God’s own kingdom. The "stone cut without hands" is the decisive anti-imperial image: God’s kingdom comes from outside the political system entirely. Nebuchadnezzar’s response (v.46–47) is the first of his progressive confessions, reaching full monotheistic praise in ch.4.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 2:9You will break them with a rod of iron — the messianic king shattering nations, parallel to the stone shattering the statue.
  • Luke 20:18Everyone who falls on that stone will be broken — Jesus identifies himself as the stone of Daniel 2.
  • Rev 11:15The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Messiah — the fulfilment of Daniel 2:44.
  • 1 Cor 15:24Then the end will come, when he hands over the kingdom to God the Father after he has destroyed all dominion — the Danielic programme completed.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:31–35

The statue: gold, silver, bronze, iron, iron-clay. Each metal is less valuable but harder — kingdoms decline in glory but increase in military force.

2:34–35

"A rock was cut out, not by human hands." The unhewn stone: no human agency. God’s kingdom is not built by political or military means.

2:44

"The God of heaven will set up a kingdom that will never be destroyed." The most important verse in Daniel: God’s kingdom outlasts all empires.

2:47

"Surely your God is the God of gods." Nebuchadnezzar’s first confession. He calls Daniel’s God supreme.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:34–35

Calvin reads the stone as Christ and the mountain as the church: small and despised at first, then filling the earth. The stone’s growth is organic, not military — the gospel spreads by Word and Spirit, not by sword.

2:44

Calvin insists that the "kingdom that will never be destroyed" is not an earthly political kingdom but Christ’s spiritual reign, inaugurated at the first advent and consummated at the second.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:34

The NET notes that "cut out, but not by human hands" is a divine passive: God is the unnamed agent. The phrase recurs in 8:25 ("destroyed, but not by human power"), establishing a pattern of divine agency without human instrumentality.

2:43

The NET observes the iron-clay mixture as an image of attempted but failed integration — strong and weak elements that cannot cohere. The marriages mentioned may refer to Hellenistic political alliances.

Longman Notes

longman
2:34–35

Longman emphasises the "not by human hands" as the key theological qualifier: every human kingdom is built by human effort; God’s kingdom arrives by divine initiative alone. This is the gospel’s logic: salvation is not a human achievement.

2:46–47

Longman notes the irony: Nebuchadnezzar "fell prostrate" and ordered offerings — treating Daniel almost as a deity. His confession is real but confused. The full understanding comes only in ch.4.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
2:36–45

Goldingay reads the four-kingdom schema as a narrative of political entropy: power becomes more diffuse, more violent, less stable. The trajectory of empire is toward fragmentation. Only divine intervention reverses the trend.

2:44

Goldingay: "A kingdom that will never be destroyed" is the one genuinely hopeful political statement in the book. All human politics is temporary; only God’s politics is permanent.

Collins Notes

collins
2:31–45

Collins identifies four main scholarly identifications of the kingdoms: (1) Babylon, Media, Persia, Greece (critical consensus); (2) Babylon, Medo-Persia, Greece, Rome (traditional); (3) Assyria, Babylon, Persia, Greece; (4) variations with Rome. The traditional identification became standard in Christian interpretation through Jerome.

2:44–45

Collins notes that the stone-kingdom is described in political, not purely spiritual, terms: it "crushes" and "brings to an end." The language is eschatological, pointing to a future divine intervention, not a gradual spiritual transformation.

Ancient Sources

The Prayer of Nabonidus (4Q242, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A fragmentary Qumran text describes a Babylonian king struck with illness for seven years who is healed by a Jewish exorcist — strikingly parallel to Nebuchadnezzar’s madness in Daniel 4.
The Prayer of Nabonidus may preserve an older version of the tradition behind Daniel 4, or reflect a common Near Eastern motif of royal humbling. Either way, it confirms the antiquity of the Daniel traditions.
1 Maccabees 1–2 (2nd c. BC)
Describes the historical desecration of the Jerusalem temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC — the event Daniel 8:9–14 and 11:31 prophesy as the “abomination of desolation.”
The fulfilment is documented in non-biblical Jewish sources, confirming Daniel’s prophetic precision whether the prophecy is dated to the 6th or 2nd century BC.
Josephus, Antiquities 10.11 (1st c. AD)
Josephus records that when Alexander the Great approached Jerusalem (332 BC), the high priest showed him Daniel’s prophecy about a Greek king conquering Persia, and Alexander was so impressed he offered sacrifices at the temple.
Whether historically accurate or legendary, Josephus’s account shows that Daniel’s prophecies were understood as referring to Greek conquest well before the Christian era.
