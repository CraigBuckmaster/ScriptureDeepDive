Daniel 10

The Heavenly Messenger

Verses 1–9 — “A Man Dressed in Linen”
123456789

Hebrew Word Study

אִישׁ ʾish — man: "I looked up and there before me was a man (ʾish) dressed in linen, with a belt of fine gold. His body like topaz, face like lightning, eyes like flaming torches, arms and legs like burnished bronze, voice like a multitude" (vv.5–6). Daniel mourns and fasts three weeks. Then this overwhelming theophany. The description parallels Rev 1:13–16. Daniel alone sees the vision; companions flee in terror.

Context

The third year of Cyrus (v.1), approximately 536 BC. The Persian period has begun; the decree to return has been issued (Ezra 1). Daniel’s three-week fast (vv.2–3) coincides with Passover/Unleavened Bread (Nisan 1–21). The figure’s description exceeds any ordinary angel: topaz body, lightning face, torch eyes, bronze limbs, multitude-voice. Whether this is Christ, Gabriel, or another being is debated; the description surpasses Gabriel’s appearances elsewhere.

Cross-Reference

  • Rev 1:13–16One like a son of man: white hair, blazing eyes, bronze feet, thunderous voice — John sees the same figure Daniel sees.
  • Ezek 1:26–28The glory of the LORD: a figure of fire with a rainbow — the throne-vision that parallels Daniel’s.
  • Matt 17:2Jesus was transfigured: face like the sun, clothes white as light — the Transfiguration echoes the heavenly figure.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:2–3

Three weeks of mourning: no choice food, no meat, no wine. Extended fasting precedes the most detailed angelic revelation in the OT.

10:5–6

The man in linen: topaz body, lightning face, torch eyes, bronze limbs, multitude-voice. The description exceeds any ordinary angel.

10:7–9

Daniel’s companions flee. Daniel loses all strength, falls face down. The consistent pattern: encountering the divine produces collapse.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:5–6

Calvin reads the man in linen as a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ, noting that the description exceeds any created being. The details — lightning, fire, bronze, multitude — are divine attributes, not angelic characteristics.

10:7–9

Calvin notes that even those who cannot see the vision experience its effects: Daniel’s companions flee in terror from what they cannot perceive. God’s presence radiates beyond the immediate recipient.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:5–6

The NET provides the closest parallels for each element: topaz (Ezek 1:16), lightning face (Ezek 1:13–14), flaming eyes (Dan 7:9), bronze limbs (Ezek 1:7), voice of a multitude (Ezek 1:24). The composite figure draws from Israel’s entire theophanic tradition.

10:9

The NET notes Daniel’s "deep sleep" (nirdam) with face to the ground echoes Abraham (Gen 15:12) and Ezekiel (Ezek 1:28). Theophanic collapse is the standard prophetic response.

Longman Notes

longman
10:5–6

Longman leaves the identification open: the description exceeds Gabriel but the angel’s need for help (v.13) complicates a christological identification. The ambiguity may be deliberate: the figure straddles categories.

10:7–9

Longman: "I had no strength left, my face turned deathly pale, and I was helpless." The prophet’s vocabulary of weakness: no strength, pale, helpless. Revelation depletes rather than empowers.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
10:5–6

Goldingay reads the composite description as deliberately overwhelming: the figure cannot be processed in a single category. He is priestly (linen), royal (gold belt), cosmic (topaz, lightning, fire, bronze). The description forces the reader to hold multiple associations simultaneously.

10:7–9

Goldingay: the companions’ flight while seeing nothing is the most psychologically realistic detail in the chapter. The supernatural leaks into the natural world as inexplicable terror.

Collins Notes

collins
10:5–6

Collins identifies the figure as an angel rather than a divine appearance, noting that the angel needs Michael’s help (v.13) — a limitation inconsistent with deity. However, Collins acknowledges the description pushes against this identification.

10:7–9

Collins compares the terror-and-collapse sequence to Paul’s Damascus road experience (Acts 9:3–9): blinding light, falling down, companions affected but unable to see. The pattern suggests a genre convention of divine encounter.

Verses 10–21 — “The Prince of Persia Resisted Me Twenty-One Days”
101112131415161718192021

Hebrew Word Study

שַׂר sar — prince / ruler: "The prince (sar) of the Persian kingdom resisted me twenty-one days. Then Michael, one of the chief princes, came to help me" (v.13). The veil is pulled back on heavenly warfare. Daniel’s prayer was heard on day one (v.12), but the answer was delayed by spiritual battle. Michael intervenes. Every earthly empire has a spiritual dimension; prayer engages heavenly warfare; delays are not denials.

Context

This passage is the OT’s most developed teaching on the spiritual dimension of political power. The "prince of Persia" is not the human king but a demonic power behind the empire. Michael is identified as Israel’s guardian angel (v.21; 12:1). The 21-day delay matches Daniel’s three-week fast (vv.2–3): his fasting covered the exact period of heavenly combat. The passage implies a structured spiritual hierarchy: national-level demonic powers, archangels, and the messengers between them.

Cross-Reference

  • Eph 6:12Our struggle is not against flesh and blood but against the rulers, authorities, powers of this dark world — the NT’s fullest statement of the worldview Daniel 10 reveals.
  • Jude 9Michael the archangel disputed with the devil about the body of Moses — Michael’s role as contender against demonic powers.
  • Rev 12:7–9Michael and his angels fought against the dragon — the final battle that Daniel 10 anticipates.
  • 2 Kgs 6:17Elisha’s prayer: open his eyes so he may see the heavenly army — the same two-tier reality Daniel 10 describes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:12–13

"Since the first day you prayed, your words were heard. But the prince of Persia resisted me twenty-one days." The answer left heaven on day 1. It arrived on day 21. The gap is spiritual warfare.

10:13

"Michael, one of the chief princes, came to help me." Michael’s first appearance. Israel’s guardian angel. The angelic hierarchy: chief princes (archangels), territorial princes, messengers.

10:20–21

"Soon I will return to fight the prince of Persia, and when I go, the prince of Greece will come." As empires rotate, so do demonic princes. History is two-layered.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:12–13

Calvin reads the 21-day delay as evidence that God’s answers to prayer are not instantaneous but pass through a contested spiritual realm. Calvin counsels perseverance: keep praying through the silence.

10:20–21

Calvin notes the succession of demonic princes (Persia then Greece) mirrors the succession of human empires (ch.2, 7, 8). The political and spiritual dimensions are correlated but distinct.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:13

The NET notes that "prince" (sar) is used for both the demonic prince of Persia and for Michael ("one of the chief princes"). The same title applies to both sides of the spiritual conflict, suggesting comparable rank.

10:20–21

The NET observes that the angel’s statement implies ongoing, sequential spiritual warfare: one demonic power is replaced by another as empires change. The spiritual battle is continuous, not episodic.

Longman Notes

longman
10:12–13

Longman: the 21-day delay is the most pastorally significant revelation in the chapter. God heard on day 1; the answer arrived on day 21. The gap is not divine indifference but spiritual combat. Every unanswered prayer may have the same explanation.

10:20–21

Longman: the prince-of-Greece announcement is prospective: the Greek empire does not yet exist, but its spiritual patron is already active. Spiritual powers precede political manifestation.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
10:12–13

Goldingay: the concept of prayer entering a contested spiritual realm is Daniel’s most distinctive contribution to OT theology. Elsewhere, prayer is heard or unheard; here, it is heard but the answer is physically intercepted en route.

10:20–21

Goldingay reads the angel’s candour as remarkable: "I must return to fight." The heavenly messenger admits vulnerability and the need for reinforcement. The spiritual battle is real, costly, and ongoing.

Collins Notes

collins
10:12–13

Collins identifies the "princes" as patron angels of nations, a concept attested in Deuteronomy 32:8 (LXX: "according to the number of the sons of God") and in Ben Sira 17:17. Each nation has a heavenly patron; Israel’s is Michael.

10:20–21

Collins reads the angel’s return to battle as a narrative frame: the revelation of chs.11–12 occurs during a brief pause in the heavenly war. The information is delivered between battles.

Ancient Sources

The Prayer of Nabonidus (4Q242, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A fragmentary Qumran text describes a Babylonian king struck with illness for seven years who is healed by a Jewish exorcist — strikingly parallel to Nebuchadnezzar’s madness in Daniel 4.
The Prayer of Nabonidus may preserve an older version of the tradition behind Daniel 4, or reflect a common Near Eastern motif of royal humbling. Either way, it confirms the antiquity of the Daniel traditions.
1 Maccabees 1–2 (2nd c. BC)
Describes the historical desecration of the Jerusalem temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC — the event Daniel 8:9–14 and 11:31 prophesy as the “abomination of desolation.”
The fulfilment is documented in non-biblical Jewish sources, confirming Daniel’s prophetic precision whether the prophecy is dated to the 6th or 2nd century BC.
Josephus, Antiquities 10.11 (1st c. AD)
Josephus records that when Alexander the Great approached Jerusalem (332 BC), the high priest showed him Daniel’s prophecy about a Greek king conquering Persia, and Alexander was so impressed he offered sacrifices at the temple.
Whether historically accurate or legendary, Josephus’s account shows that Daniel’s prophecies were understood as referring to Greek conquest well before the Christian era.
