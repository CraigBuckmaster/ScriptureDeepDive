Daniel 1

Daniel’s Resolve in Babylon

Verses 1–10 — “Daniel Resolved Not to Defile Himself”
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

בְלֵב bəlēb — in his heart / he resolved: "Daniel resolved (bəlēb) in his heart not to defile himself with the royal food and wine" (v.8). Nebuchadnezzar besieges Jerusalem and carries off temple vessels and young Israelite nobles (vv.1–2). Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah are selected for Babylonian re-education: new language, new literature, new names (vv.3–7). Babylon’s programme is total identity replacement. Daniel’s refusal of the king’s food is not dietary pickiness but covenant faithfulness — the first act of resistance in the book.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1–2

Nebuchadnezzar besieges Jerusalem in the third year of Jehoiakim (605 BC). Temple vessels are carried to the treasure house of Babylon’s gods. God’s own vessels in a pagan temple — the theological crisis that frames the entire book.

1:3–7

The re-education programme: three years of Babylonian language, literature, and court cuisine. The name changes are theological: Daniel (“God is my judge”) becomes Belteshazzar (a Babylonian deity name). Identity erasure.

1:8

"Daniel resolved in his heart not to defile himself." The key verb: resolved. Not a spontaneous reaction but a settled decision. Covenant identity survives Babylonian renaming because of internal resolve.

1:9–10

God grants Daniel favour with the official. The official’s fear of the king is reasonable; Daniel’s proposal addresses it directly.

Verses 11–21 — “God Gave Them Knowledge and Understanding”
1112131415161718192021

Hebrew Word Study

מַדָּע maddāʿ — knowledge / understanding: "God gave them knowledge (maddāʿ) and understanding of all kinds of literature and learning" (v.17). Daniel proposes a ten-day vegetable test (vv.11–14). After ten days they look healthier than those eating royal food (v.15). The guard removes the royal food permanently (v.16). God gives the four young men exceptional learning; Daniel receives the additional gift of understanding visions and dreams (v.17). At the examination, Nebuchadnezzar finds them ten times better than all the magicians and enchanters in his realm (v.20). Daniel remains until the first year of Cyrus (v.21) — he outlasts the empire.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:12–15

The ten-day test: vegetables and water only. "At the end of ten days they looked healthier and better nourished." God vindicates the faithfulness. The test is small; the theology is enormous.

1:17

"God gave them knowledge." Four times in Daniel 1, God is the subject of the verb: God gave the vessels (v.2), God gave favour (v.9), God gave knowledge (v.17), God gave Daniel vision-understanding (v.17). Babylon takes; God gives.

1:20

"Ten times better than all the magicians." The ratio is not incidental — it establishes the pattern for the entire book: God’s wisdom surpasses Babylon’s in every contest.

1:21

"Daniel remained until the first year of King Cyrus." He outlasts Nebuchadnezzar, Belshazzar, and Babylon itself. The exile has a terminus.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Daniel
Judean exile; prophet and court sage
One of the young nobles carried to Babylon in 605 BC. Given the name Belteshazzar. His resolve not to defile himself with royal food is the book’s first act. He will interpret dreams, survive the lion’s den, and receive apocalyptic visions spanning centuries.
Nebuchadnezzar
King of Babylon (605–562 BC)
The most powerful ruler in the ancient world. Conquered Jerusalem, destroyed the temple (586 BC), and deported Judah. In Daniel, he is both antagonist and eventual worshipper — his journey from arrogance to humility is one of the OT’s most dramatic conversion narratives.
