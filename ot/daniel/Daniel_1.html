Daniel 1

Daniel’s Resolve in Babylon

Verses 1–10 — “Daniel Resolved Not to Defile Himself”
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

בְלֵב bəlēb — in his heart / he resolved: "Daniel resolved (bəlēb) in his heart not to defile himself with the royal food and wine" (v.8). Nebuchadnezzar besieges Jerusalem and carries off temple vessels and young Israelite nobles (vv.1–2). Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah are selected for Babylonian re-education: new language, new literature, new names (vv.3–7). Babylon’s programme is total identity replacement. Daniel’s refusal of the king’s food is not dietary pickiness but covenant faithfulness — the first act of resistance in the book.

Context

The year is 605 BC — the first deportation under Nebuchadnezzar. Babylon’s strategy was cultural assimilation: rename captives after Babylonian gods (Belteshazzar = Bel protect his life), retrain them in Babylonian literature and language, and feed them from the king’s table to create loyalty. Daniel’s refusal of the food is the first test of whether covenant identity can survive total cultural immersion. The setting is the third year of Jehoiakim (v.1), synchronised with Babylonian records of Nebuchadnezzar’s first western campaign.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 24:1–2Nebuchadnezzar’s siege of Jerusalem under Jehoiakim — the historical event behind Daniel 1:1–2.
  • Deut 32:38Moses warns against eating food offered to foreign gods — the Torah basis for Daniel’s refusal.
  • Phil 3:7–8Paul counts all things loss for Christ — the NT parallel to Daniel’s costly refusal of royal privilege.
  • 1 Cor 10:31Whatever you eat or drink, do it for the glory of God — the principle Daniel embodies.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1–2

Nebuchadnezzar besieges Jerusalem in the third year of Jehoiakim (605 BC). Temple vessels carried to Babylon. God’s own vessels in a pagan temple — the theological crisis framing the book.

1:3–7

The re-education programme: three years of Babylonian language, literature, and court food. Name changes are theological: Daniel (“God is my judge”) becomes Belteshazzar.

1:8

Daniel resolved in his heart not to defile himself. The key verb: resolved. Not spontaneous but a settled decision. Covenant identity survives renaming because of internal resolve.

1:9–10

God grants Daniel favour with the official. The official’s fear of the king is reasonable; Daniel’s proposal addresses it directly.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1–2

Calvin notes that God permitted Nebuchadnezzar’s victory to discipline Judah — the Babylonian triumph is not evidence of Marduk’s power but of YHWH’s sovereign use of pagan instruments.

1:8

The resolve not to defile himself shows that true religion survives even when outward observance is impossible. Daniel could not sacrifice or attend the temple, but he could maintain dietary faithfulness. Calvin sees this as a paradigm for believers under hostile governments.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:2

The NET notes the phrase “The Lord gave Jehoiakim into his hand” — the theological subject is God, not Nebuchadnezzar. The deportation is divinely orchestrated.

1:8

The NET observes that “resolved” (sam al-leb) literally means “placed upon his heart.” The decision is deliberate and internal — a volitional act, not an emotional reaction.

Longman Notes

longman
1:8

Longman emphasises that Daniel’s food refusal is not asceticism but covenant loyalty. The royal food likely involved meat offered to Babylonian gods and wine libated to them — consuming it would constitute participation in pagan worship.

1:17

God gave them knowledge: Longman notes the fourfold divine giving in ch.1 (vv.2, 9, 17a, 17b) — God is the true agent throughout. Babylon takes; God gives.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
1:1–2

Goldingay reads the temple-vessel transfer as the book’s central symbol: God’s holy objects in Marduk’s house. The question the book answers: whose god is actually sovereign? The vessels will return (Ezra 1:7–11).

1:8–16

The ten-day test functions as a “prophetic sign-act in miniature”: Daniel’s dietary faithfulness produces visible, measurable blessing. God vindicates what seems like an absurd risk.

Collins Notes

collins
1:1–2

Collins notes the chronological tension between Daniel’s “third year of Jehoiakim” and Jeremiah’s “fourth year” (Jer 25:1), likely reflecting different calendar systems (Babylonian accession-year vs Judean non-accession-year reckoning).

1:3–7

The court-tale genre is well attested in the ancient Near East: a wise exile rises in a foreign court. Collins compares the Joseph narrative (Gen 39–41) and Ahiqar. Daniel’s distinctiveness is the theological framework: success comes from God, not cleverness.

Verses 11–21 — “God Gave Them Knowledge and Understanding”
1112131415161718192021

Hebrew Word Study

מַדָּע maddāʿ — knowledge / understanding: "God gave them knowledge (maddāʿ) and understanding of all kinds of literature and learning" (v.17). Daniel proposes a ten-day vegetable test (vv.11–14). After ten days they look healthier (v.15). God gives the four young men exceptional learning; Daniel receives additional gift of understanding visions and dreams (v.17). Nebuchadnezzar finds them ten times better than all magicians in his realm (v.20). Daniel remains until the first year of Cyrus (v.21) — he outlasts the empire.

Context

The ten-day test is a miniature of the book’s macro-theme: faithfulness tested, God vindicates. The number ten recurs in Daniel as a testing number (cf. the ten horns, the ten days of Rev 2:10). Daniel’s gift of vision-interpretation (v.17b) distinguishes him from his three friends and sets up the dream-narratives of chs.2, 4, 7–12. The note that Daniel “remained until the first year of Cyrus” (v.21) spans the entire Babylonian empire — 605 to 539 BC, roughly 66 years of faithful service.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 41:38–39Pharaoh recognises the Spirit of God in Joseph — the pattern Daniel follows: foreign ruler acknowledges divine wisdom in an Israelite exile.
  • Prov 1:7The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge — the theological foundation for Daniel’s intellectual superiority.
  • Acts 7:22Moses was educated in all the wisdom of Egypt — the same pattern: God’s servant masters pagan learning while maintaining covenant identity.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:12–15

The ten-day test: vegetables and water only. God vindicates faithfulness. The test is small; the theology is enormous.

1:17

God gave them knowledge. Babylon takes; God gives. The verb is emphatic and repeated.

1:20

Ten times better than all the magicians. The ratio establishes the pattern for the entire book: God’s wisdom surpasses Babylon’s in every contest.

1:21

Daniel remained until the first year of Cyrus. He outlasts Nebuchadnezzar, Belshazzar, and Babylon itself.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:17

Calvin observes that God gives two distinct gifts: general learning (shared by all four) and vision-interpretation (unique to Daniel). The general gift sanctifies secular learning; the special gift establishes prophetic authority.

1:20–21

The tenfold superiority is God’s public vindication. Calvin notes that God often waits until the test is complete before revealing the result — patience in trial precedes visible blessing.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:17

The NET notes that “literature and learning” (sefer and chokmah) encompass Babylonian omen texts, mathematical tables, astronomical records, and diplomatic correspondence. Daniel masters the entire curriculum.

1:21

The NET observes the structural purpose of v.21: it brackets the entire Babylonian period. Daniel 10:1 updates this to “the third year of Cyrus,” extending his career into the Persian period.

Longman Notes

longman
1:17–20

Longman emphasises the four-fold divine agency: God delivered (v.2), God granted favour (v.9), God gave knowledge (v.17a), God gave Daniel vision-understanding (v.17b). Human effort is real but secondary to divine causation.

1:20

The comparison with “magicians and enchanters” introduces the contest-motif that dominates chs.2–5: Babylonian wisdom versus divine revelation.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
1:17

Goldingay notes that Daniel’s additional gift of “understanding visions and dreams” is narratively prospective — it sets up his role as interpreter in chs.2, 4, 5 and as visionary in chs.7–12. The gift is not self-generated but granted.

1:21

The mention of Cyrus connects Daniel’s story to Isaiah’s prophecy (Isa 44:28–45:1). The same Cyrus whom Isaiah called “my shepherd” and “my anointed” is the terminus of Daniel’s exile-career.

Collins Notes

collins
1:17–20

Collins notes parallels with the Wisdom of Solomon tradition: the wise person surpasses pagans not through magic but through divinely given insight. Daniel’s “ten times better” echoes Joseph’s superiority to Pharaoh’s magicians.

1:21

Collins reads v.21 as a theological statement: the exile has a terminus. Daniel’s career spans the punishment and reaches the restoration. The first verse of the book is defeat; the last verse of ch.1 is survival.

People of the Chapter

Daniel
Judean exile; prophet and court sage
One of the young nobles carried to Babylon in 605 BC. Given the name Belteshazzar. His resolve not to defile himself with royal food is the book’s first act.
Nebuchadnezzar
King of Babylon (605–562 BC)
The most powerful ruler in the ancient world. Conquered Jerusalem, destroyed the temple (586 BC), and deported Judah.

Ancient Sources

The Prayer of Nabonidus (4Q242, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A fragmentary Qumran text describes a Babylonian king struck with illness for seven years who is healed by a Jewish exorcist — strikingly parallel to Nebuchadnezzar’s madness in Daniel 4.
The Prayer of Nabonidus may preserve an older version of the tradition behind Daniel 4, or reflect a common Near Eastern motif of royal humbling. Either way, it confirms the antiquity of the Daniel traditions.
1 Maccabees 1–2 (2nd c. BC)
Describes the historical desecration of the Jerusalem temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC — the event Daniel 8:9–14 and 11:31 prophesy as the “abomination of desolation.”
The fulfilment is documented in non-biblical Jewish sources, confirming Daniel’s prophetic precision whether the prophecy is dated to the 6th or 2nd century BC.
Josephus, Antiquities 10.11 (1st c. AD)
Josephus records that when Alexander the Great approached Jerusalem (332 BC), the high priest showed him Daniel’s prophecy about a Greek king conquering Persia, and Alexander was so impressed he offered sacrifices at the temple.
Whether historically accurate or legendary, Josephus’s account shows that Daniel’s prophecies were understood as referring to Greek conquest well before the Christian era.
