Daniel 8

The Ram and the Goat

Verses 1–14 — “The Shaggy Goat Is the King of Greece”
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

חָזוֹן ḥāzōn — vision: "In the vision (ḥāzōn) I saw a ram standing beside the canal" (v.3). Daniel’s second vision. A two-horned ram (Medo-Persia, v.20) charges west, north, and south — no one can stand against it (v.4). Then a goat with a prominent horn (Greece/Alexander, v.21) crosses the earth without touching the ground (v.5) and shatters the ram (vv.6–7). At the height of his power, the goat’s horn breaks off and four horns replace it (v.8) — Alexander’s death and the four successor kingdoms. From one of them comes a small horn that grows toward the south, east, and the Beautiful Land (Israel) (v.9). It sets itself up against the Prince of the host, takes away the daily sacrifice, and desecrates the sanctuary for 2,300 evenings and mornings (vv.10–14).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:3–4

The ram with two horns: Medo-Persia. Two horns, one longer — Persia emerged later but dominated. No animal could stand against it. The ram charges in three directions: the exact pattern of Persian conquest.

8:5–7

The goat from the west: Greece. Crossing the earth "without touching the ground" — the speed of Alexander’s conquest (334–323 BC). The single prominent horn: Alexander himself. The ram is shattered and cannot recover.

8:8

"At the height of its power, the large horn was broken off, and in its place four prominent horns grew." Alexander’s death at 32 and the four Diadochi: Ptolemy (Egypt), Seleucus (Syria/East), Cassander (Greece), Lysimachus (Thrace/Asia Minor).

8:9–12

The small horn from one of the four: Antiochus IV Epiphanes (175–164 BC). He desecrated the Jerusalem temple, prohibited Jewish worship, and erected an altar to Zeus — the "abomination of desolation" (cf. 11:31; Matt 24:15). The 2,300 evenings and mornings approximate the period of desecration.

Verses 15–27 — “The Vision Concerns the Time of the End”
15161718192021222324252627

Hebrew Word Study

קֵץ qēṣ — end: "The vision concerns the time of the end (qēṣ)" (v.17). Gabriel appears — the first time an angel is named in the Bible (v.16). Gabriel explains: the ram is Medo-Persia (v.20), the goat is Greece (v.21), the four horns are four kingdoms from that nation (v.22). A fierce king will arise who destroys the mighty and the holy people (v.24), takes his stand against the Prince of princes, but will be destroyed — "not by human power" (v.25). The vision is sealed for the distant future (v.26). Daniel is exhausted and ill for days; "I was appalled by the vision; it was beyond understanding" (v.27). The prophet is physically devastated by what he has seen.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:15–16

"Gabriel, tell this man the meaning of the vision." Gabriel’s first appearance in Scripture. The same angel who will later announce John the Baptist’s birth (Luke 1:19) and Jesus’s conception (Luke 1:26).

8:20–22

"The two-horned ram represents the kings of Media and Persia. The shaggy goat is the king of Greece. The large horn is the first king." The most explicit kingdom-identification in Daniel. No ambiguity: ram = Medo-Persia, goat = Greece, horn = Alexander.

8:25

"He will cause astounding devastation. He will destroy those who are mighty, the holy people. He will take his stand against the Prince of princes. Yet he will be destroyed, but not by human power." Antiochus’s end: not by military defeat but by madness and disease (1 Macc 6:8–16). Divine judgment, not human victory.

8:27

"I, Daniel, was worn out. I lay exhausted for several days. I was appalled by the vision; it was beyond understanding." The prophet’s response to apocalyptic revelation: not triumph but trauma. Seeing the future costs the seer physically.
