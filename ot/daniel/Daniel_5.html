Daniel 5

The Writing on the Wall

Verses 1–16 — “The Fingers of a Human Hand Wrote on the Plaster”
12345678910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

כְתַב kəṭab — writing / inscription: "Suddenly the fingers of a human hand appeared and wrote (kəṭab) on the plaster of the wall" (v.5). Belshazzar’s feast: a thousand nobles, drinking from the sacred vessels taken from Jerusalem’s temple. A hand writes. The king’s face turns pale, his knees knock. No wise man can read it. The queen remembers Daniel.

Context

The chapter moves from Nebuchadnezzar (ch.4) to Belshazzar with no transition — the intervening rulers (Evil-Merodach, Neriglissar, Labashi-Marduk) are skipped. Belshazzar was co-regent with his father Nabonidus, who was absent from Babylon. The Nabonidus Chronicle confirms Belshazzar’s role as acting king. The desecration of the temple vessels is deliberate: Nebuchadnezzar stored but never used them; Belshazzar desecrates them at a drunken feast.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Chr 36:22–23Cyrus’s decree fulfilling Jeremiah’s prophecy — the Persian conquest that Daniel 5 narrates from the Babylonian side.
  • Rev 17:1–6Babylon the great, drunk with the blood of the saints — Revelation’s Babylon draws on the feast imagery of Daniel 5.
  • Ps 75:8In the hand of the LORD is a cup full of foaming wine — the cup of judgment Belshazzar drinks from literally.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1–4

Calvin reads the vessel-desecration as the sin that tips the scales: Nebuchadnezzar’s sins were against people; Belshazzar’s sin is directly against God’s sacred objects. The escalation provokes immediate judgment.

5:5–6

Calvin notes that God uses the simplest means to terrify the mightiest: a hand and some words on a wall. No army, no earthquake — just handwriting.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:1

The NET notes that archaeological evidence confirms Belshazzar as Nabonidus’s son who acted as co-regent. The "third highest ruler" offer (v.16) makes sense: Nabonidus is first, Belshazzar second, the reward is third.

5:5–6

The NET observes the detail of writing on "the plaster of the wall" — Babylonian palace walls were covered in white gypsum plaster, providing a visible writing surface in lamplight.

Longman Notes

longman
5:1–4

Longman: the contrast with Nebuchadnezzar is the chapter’s structural key. Nebuchadnezzar was humbled and restored; Belshazzar knew the story (v.22) but repeated the arrogance without the repentance.

5:10–12

Longman notes the queen mother’s role as living memory: she bridges the reigns, remembering Daniel when the current administration has forgotten him.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
5:1–6

Goldingay: the feast with the temple vessels is Babylon’s last supper — a celebration that becomes a funeral. The empire drinks from God’s cups and dies the same night.

5:5

Goldingay reads the disembodied hand as the most economical judgment-scene in the Bible: no angel, no voice from heaven, no natural disaster. Just fingers and plaster. God’s minimalism is terrifying.

Collins Notes

collins
5:1–4

Collins identifies the feast as a deliberate act of hubris: the temple vessels are trophies of conquest, and drinking from them celebrates Babylon’s victory over Jerusalem’s God. The sacrilege is political and theological.

5:5–6

Collins notes the literary artistry: the hand writes while the king watches in real-time. The slow revelation — writing, then failed interpretations, then Daniel’s summons — builds maximum dramatic tension.

Verses 17–31 — “MENE, MENE, TEKEL, PARSIN”
171819202122232425262728293031

Hebrew Word Study

מְנֵא תְקֵל פַרְסִין mənēʾ təqēl parsîn — numbered, weighed, divided: "MENE, MENE, TEKEL, PARSIN" (v.25). Daniel recounts Nebuchadnezzar’s humbling then rebukes Belshazzar: "You knew all this, yet you have not humbled yourself" (vv.22–23). MENE — numbered. TEKEL — weighed and found wanting. PARSIN — divided and given to Medes and Persians. That very night Belshazzar is slain (v.30). Darius the Mede takes over (v.31).

Context

The three Aramaic words are also units of weight/currency: mina, shekel, half-mina. The pun works on two levels: (1) monetary terms (your kingdom has been measured like currency) and (2) verbal roots (numbered, weighed, divided). The wordplay on PARSIN/Persia (prs) adds a third level: your kingdom goes to Persia. The Babylonian Chronicle records Cyrus’s conquest of Babylon on October 12, 539 BC — the city fell without a battle, consistent with the feast-night surprise.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 25:11–12Jeremiah’s prophecy of 70 years of Babylonian dominion — fulfilled at the moment Daniel 5 describes.
  • Isa 21:9"Fallen, fallen is Babylon!" — the double-fallen formula that Daniel 5 narrates.
  • Rev 18:10In one hour your judgment has come — Revelation’s Babylon falls as suddenly as Daniel’s.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:22–23

Calvin emphasises the aggravating factor: Belshazzar’s sin is worse than Nebuchadnezzar’s because he had the precedent and ignored it. Privilege increases accountability.

5:30

Calvin notes the simultaneity: feast and fall on the same night. The cup of celebration becomes the cup of judgment.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:25–28

The NET provides the fullest analysis of the wordplay: mənēʾ (mina/numbered), təqēl (shekel/weighed), parsîn (half-minas/divided + Persia). The words function as both economic metaphor and prophetic verdict.

5:31

The NET notes the historical difficulty of "Darius the Mede" — no figure by this exact name appears in extra-biblical records. Proposed identifications include Gubaru (governor of Babylon) or Cyrus himself under a throne-name.

Longman Notes

longman
5:22–23

Longman: the charge against Belshazzar is not ignorance but defiance. He KNEW Nebuchadnezzar’s story and chose arrogance anyway. Known truth that is rejected produces harder judgment.

5:30

Longman contrasts the two kings: Nebuchadnezzar was given twelve months to repent (4:29); Belshazzar is given one evening. The window of grace narrows for those who have more precedent to learn from.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
5:25–28

Goldingay: the three words function as a death sentence in commercial language — the empire’s accounts have been audited and found deficient. God as divine accountant.

5:30–31

Goldingay reads the overnight regime change as the book’s most compressed narrative: feast (v.1), writing (v.5), interpretation (v.25), death (v.30), new empire (v.31). Five scenes in thirty-one verses.

Collins Notes

collins
5:25–28

Collins notes the wordplay is unique in ancient literature: a cryptic inscription using commercial terms that double as prophetic verdicts. The writing functions like an oracle but is delivered in accounting language.

5:30–31

Collins observes that the shift from Babylonian to Medo-Persian rule in one night confirms the statue’s prediction (ch.2): the gold head gives way to the silver chest. The prophecy self-verifies within the narrative.

Ancient Sources

The Prayer of Nabonidus (4Q242, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A fragmentary Qumran text describes a Babylonian king struck with illness for seven years who is healed by a Jewish exorcist — strikingly parallel to Nebuchadnezzar’s madness in Daniel 4.
The Prayer of Nabonidus may preserve an older version of the tradition behind Daniel 4, or reflect a common Near Eastern motif of royal humbling. Either way, it confirms the antiquity of the Daniel traditions.
1 Maccabees 1–2 (2nd c. BC)
Describes the historical desecration of the Jerusalem temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC — the event Daniel 8:9–14 and 11:31 prophesy as the “abomination of desolation.”
The fulfilment is documented in non-biblical Jewish sources, confirming Daniel’s prophetic precision whether the prophecy is dated to the 6th or 2nd century BC.
Josephus, Antiquities 10.11 (1st c. AD)
Josephus records that when Alexander the Great approached Jerusalem (332 BC), the high priest showed him Daniel’s prophecy about a Greek king conquering Persia, and Alexander was so impressed he offered sacrifices at the temple.
Whether historically accurate or legendary, Josephus’s account shows that Daniel’s prophecies were understood as referring to Greek conquest well before the Christian era.
