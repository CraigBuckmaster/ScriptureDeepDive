Daniel 5

The Writing on the Wall

Verses 1–16 — “The Fingers of a Human Hand Wrote on the Plaster”
12345678910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

כְתַב kəṭab — writing / inscription: "Suddenly the fingers of a human hand appeared and wrote (kəṭab) on the plaster of the wall" (v.5). Belshazzar’s feast: a thousand nobles, drinking from the sacred vessels taken from Jerusalem’s temple (vv.1–4). The vessels that Nebuchadnezzar stored but never used — Belshazzar desecrates them. A hand appears and writes on the wall. The king’s face turns pale, his knees knock, his legs give way (v.6). No wise man can read the writing (v.8). The queen remembers Daniel: "There is a man in your kingdom who has the spirit of the holy gods in him" (v.11). Daniel is summoned.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1–4

Belshazzar’s feast. A thousand nobles. Drinking wine from Jerusalem’s temple vessels while praising gods of gold, silver, bronze, iron, wood, and stone. The sacrilege is deliberate and public.

5:5–6

"The fingers of a human hand appeared and wrote on the plaster of the wall." The king watched the hand as it wrote. His face turned pale; his thoughts terrified him; his knees knocked together. Terror enters Babylon’s throne room through handwriting.

5:10–12

The queen mother remembers Daniel. "He has insight, intelligence, wisdom like the gods." Daniel has been forgotten by the new regime but not by the queen mother. Institutional memory saves the day.

5:13–16

Daniel is brought in. Belshazzar offers him purple robes and the third-highest position. Daniel refuses the gifts but agrees to read the writing.

Verses 17–31 — “MENE, MENE, TEKEL, PARSIN”
171819202122232425262728293031

Hebrew Word Study

מְנֵא תְקֵל פַרְסִין mənēʾ təqēl parsîn — numbered, weighed, divided: "MENE, MENE, TEKEL, PARSIN" (v.25). Daniel first recounts Nebuchadnezzar’s humbling (vv.18–21) then rebukes Belshazzar: "You knew all this, yet you have not humbled yourself. You set yourself up against the Lord of heaven" (vv.22–23). The interpretation: MENE — God has numbered your kingdom and brought it to an end. TEKEL — you have been weighed on the scales and found wanting. PARSIN — your kingdom is divided and given to the Medes and Persians (vv.26–28). That very night Belshazzar is slain (v.30). Darius the Mede takes over (v.31). The Babylonian empire ends mid-feast.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:22–23

"But you, Belshazzar, have not humbled yourself, though you knew all this. You set yourself up against the Lord of heaven." The charge: you had Nebuchadnezzar’s example and ignored it. Knowledge without humility is condemnation.

5:25–28

"MENE: God has numbered your kingdom. TEKEL: you have been weighed and found wanting. PARSIN: your kingdom is divided." Three words, three verdicts. Numbered (its days are counted), weighed (and failed), divided (between Medes and Persians). The wordplay on PARSIN/Persia is deliberate.

5:30

"That very night Belshazzar, king of the Babylonians, was slain." The swiftest judgment in the OT. The writing appears; the interpretation is given; the king dies. No delay, no reprieve, no second chance. Contrast with Nebuchadnezzar, who was given twelve months (4:29).

5:31

"Darius the Mede took over the kingdom." The empire changes hands overnight. The gold head of ch.2’s statue gives way to the silver chest. Babylon falls as Daniel prophesied.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Belshazzar
Last king of Babylon
Co-regent with Nabonidus. His feast desecrating the temple vessels provokes the writing on the wall. Slain the same night. The contrast with Nebuchadnezzar is the point: Nebuchadnezzar was humbled and restored; Belshazzar knew the story but repeated the arrogance without the repentance.
