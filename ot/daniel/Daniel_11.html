Daniel 11

The Kings of the North and South

Verses 1–20 — “From Persia to the Seleucids”
1234567891011121314151617181920

Hebrew Word Study

מֶלֶךְ melek̅ — king: The most detailed predictive prophecy in the Bible. The angel traces the succession of kings from Persia (v.2: three more kings, then a fourth of great wealth — Xerxes), through Alexander (v.3: a mighty king whose kingdom is divided to the four winds), to the Ptolemies (kings of the south) and Seleucids (kings of the north) warring over Israel for two centuries (vv.5–20). Every verse corresponds to documented history: the marriage alliance of v.6 (Berenice and Antiochus II), the retaliatory invasion of v.7–8 (Ptolemy III), the campaigns of Antiochus III (vv.10–19), and his tax collector Heliodorus (v.20).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:2–4

Three more Persian kings, then a mighty king (Alexander) whose empire splits to the four winds. The same vision as ch.8 but now with historical detail.

11:5–15

The Ptolemaic-Seleucid wars. Verse-by-verse history of 3rd–2nd century BC politics in the Levant. The "beautiful land" (v.16) = Israel, caught between northern and southern empires.

11:20

"His successor will send out a tax collector. In a few years he will be destroyed, yet not in anger or in battle." Seleucus IV and Heliodorus. The precision is remarkable.

Verses 21–45 — “A Contemptible Person: Antiochus Epiphanes”

Hebrew Word Study

שִׁיקוּץ shiqquts — abomination: "His armed forces will rise up to desecrate the temple fortress and will abolish the daily sacrifice. Then they will set up the abomination (shiqquts) that causes desolation" (v.31). Antiochus IV Epiphanes: the "contemptible person" (v.21) who seizes power through intrigue, not right. He invades Egypt (vv.25–28), is humiliated by Rome (v.30, the "ships of Kittim"), and takes out his rage on Jerusalem. He abolishes the daily sacrifice and sets up the abomination — an altar to Zeus in the temple (December 167 BC). Those who know their God will firmly resist (v.32). The wise will instruct many, though some will fall by sword, flame, captivity, and plunder (vv.33–35). Verses 36–45 shift: many scholars see the description exceeding Antiochus and pointing to a future antichrist — the king who "exalts himself above every god" (v.36).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:31

"They will set up the abomination that causes desolation." Jesus quotes this in Matthew 24:15 as a sign of the end. The phrase bridges Antiochus (historical), Rome’s destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70, and the eschatological tribulation.

11:32–33

"The people who know their God will firmly resist him. Those who are wise will instruct many." The Maccabean resistance: faithful Jews who refused to compromise. Knowing God produces resistance; wisdom produces teaching even under persecution.

11:35

"Some of the wise will stumble, so that they may be refined, purified and made spotless until the time of the end." Suffering as purification. The stumbling is not failure but pedagogy. The refining has a terminus: "the time of the end."

11:36

"The king will do as he pleases. He will exalt and magnify himself above every god." At this point, many interpreters see the text shifting from Antiochus to a future figure. The self-exaltation "above every god" exceeds Antiochus and echoes 2 Thessalonians 2:4.
