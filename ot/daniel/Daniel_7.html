Daniel 7

The Four Beasts and the Son of Man

Verses 1–14 — “One Like a Son of Man Coming with the Clouds of Heaven”
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

בַר אֱנָשׁ bar ʾĕnāsh — son of man: "One like a son of man (bar ʾĕnāsh), coming with the clouds of heaven. He was given authority, glory and sovereign power; all nations worshipped him. His dominion is everlasting" (vv.13–14). Four beasts from the sea: lion (Babylon), bear (Medo-Persia), leopard (Greece), terrifying fourth beast with iron teeth and ten horns (Rome). Ancient of Days on his fire-throne, books opened. The son of man receives eternal dominion. Jesus uses "Son of Man" over 80 times in the Gospels — all trace to this verse.

Context

Chapter 7 is the structural pivot of the book: the last chapter in Aramaic, the first apocalyptic vision, and the mirror of ch.2’s statue (same four kingdoms, different imagery). The "sea" from which the beasts emerge is the primordial chaos-ocean of ancient Near Eastern mythology (cf. Gen 1:2). The "Ancient of Days" is the oldest representation of God as an aged, white-haired figure in Jewish literature. The "son of man" figure is the most debated identity in the OT: individual Messiah, angelic being, or corporate symbol for the faithful remnant.

Cross-Reference

  • Mark 14:62Jesus before the Sanhedrin: "You will see the Son of Man sitting at the right hand of the Mighty One and coming on the clouds of heaven" — the moment they accuse him of blasphemy. He claims to be Daniel’s figure.
  • Rev 1:7Look, he is coming with the clouds, and every eye will see him — Revelation’s opening vision quotes Daniel 7:13.
  • Rev 4:2–11The throne room of heaven — directly developed from Daniel 7:9–10.
  • Matt 28:18All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me — Jesus claims the authority Dan 7:14 assigns to the son of man.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1–4

Four beasts from the sea. The lion with eagle wings: Babylon. Wings torn off, stands like a man — possibly Nebuchadnezzar’s humbling and restoration.

7:7–8

The fourth beast: terrifying, iron teeth, ten horns. A little horn with human eyes and a boastful mouth — the primary OT source for NT antichrist theology.

7:9–10

The Ancient of Days: clothing white as snow, hair like pure wool, throne of fire. Thousands upon thousands attend him. The heavenly courtroom.

7:13–14

The son of man: divine authority, eternal kingdom, universal worship. Jesus claims this at his trial (Mark 14:62). This is the title he uses most.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:9–10

Calvin reads the Ancient of Days as a vision of God’s eternal kingship revealed in time. The white clothing symbolises purity; the fire symbolises both judgment and purification. The opened books are the records of all human deeds.

7:13–14

Calvin identifies the son of man as Christ without hesitation: no merely human figure could receive "all nations worshipping him" and "an everlasting dominion." The cloud-riding is a divine prerogative (Ps 104:3).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:9

The NET notes that "Ancient of Days" (ʿattiq yōmin) is unique to Daniel. The white hair denotes wisdom and venerability, not weakness. The fire-throne parallels Ezekiel 1’s merkabah vision.

7:13–14

The NET observes that "one like a son of man" uses the Aramaic preposition kə ("like"), suggesting the figure resembles a human but may not be merely human. The cloud-riding is elsewhere exclusively a divine activity (Ps 68:4; Isa 19:1).

Longman Notes

longman
7:9–10

Longman: the Ancient of Days scene is the most developed vision of God as judge in the OT. The fire is both purifying and consuming. The books are the permanent record. Nothing is hidden from this court.

7:13–14

Longman: whether the son of man is an individual or collective figure in the original context, Jesus’ appropriation of the title settles the question for Christian readers. He claims it; the NT develops it; the church confesses it.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
7:9–14

Goldingay reads the scene as a two-phase transfer of power: first, the beast’s authority is removed (vv.11–12); second, the son of man’s authority is granted (vv.13–14). The gap between the two phases is the space in which the reader lives.

7:13–14

Goldingay: the cloud-riding is the decisive clue. In every other OT occurrence, cloud-riding is God’s prerogative. The figure "like a son of man" approaches God’s throne and receives God’s authority. The text pushes the reader toward a more-than-human identification.

Collins Notes

collins
7:1–14

Collins provides the standard critical identification: lion = Babylon, bear = Media, leopard = Persia, fourth beast = Greece (with the little horn = Antiochus IV). The traditional identification (fourth = Rome) became dominant through Christian interpretation but requires combining Media and Persia into one kingdom.

7:13–14

Collins argues the "son of man" originally represented the angelic figure Michael (cf. 10:13, 21; 12:1) or a collective symbol for "the saints of the Most High" (v.27). The individual-messianic reading developed in the first century and is reflected in the Similitudes of Enoch and the Gospels.

Verses 15–28 — “The Saints Will Receive the Kingdom Forever”
1516171819202122232425262728

Hebrew Word Study

קַדִּישֵׁי qaddishē — holy ones / saints: "The saints (qaddishē) of the Most High will receive the kingdom and will possess it forever" (v.18). Daniel asks for the interpretation. The four beasts are four kingdoms (v.17). The little horn wages war against the saints and defeats them — until the Ancient of Days pronounces judgment in their favour (vv.21–22). The horn will oppress the saints for "a time, times and half a time" (v.25) — 3.5 years, the template for Revelation’s tribulation period.

Context

The "time, times and half a time" (3.5 years = 42 months = 1,260 days) becomes the most important time-measurement in apocalyptic literature. Revelation uses it in 11:2, 11:3, 12:6, 12:14, 13:5. The period likely originates from the approximate duration of Antiochus IV’s temple desecration (167–164 BC). The saints’ kingdom (v.27) is described in the same terms as the son of man’s (v.14), suggesting identification between the two. The chapter ends with Daniel deeply troubled (v.28) — the apocalyptic visionary is not triumphant but devastated.

Cross-Reference

  • Rev 11:2–3The holy city trampled for 42 months; two witnesses prophesy for 1,260 days — developing Daniel’s 3.5-year period.
  • Rev 13:5–7The beast given authority for 42 months, to wage war against God’s holy people — directly from Daniel 7:21, 25.
  • 1 Cor 6:2–3The saints will judge the world; they will judge angels — the Pauline version of Daniel 7:22, 27.
  • Matt 25:34Come, take your inheritance, the kingdom prepared for you — the saints receiving the kingdom.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:17–18

"Four beasts = four kingdoms. But the saints will receive the kingdom." Beasts temporary; saints’ kingdom forever.

7:21–22

The horn defeats the saints UNTIL God intervenes. Faithfulness does not guarantee immediate victory; it guarantees ultimate vindication.

7:25

"A time, times and half a time." 3.5 years: the template for Revelation’s tribulation. A limit is set on suffering.

7:27

Sovereignty and power handed to the holy people. The end: not destruction but transfer of power. The saints inherit.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:21–22

Calvin: the saints lose UNTIL God acts. This is the hardest aspect of Daniel’s vision — the righteous are genuinely defeated before they are vindicated. Calvin counsels patience: the "until" has an expiration date.

7:27

Calvin reads the kingdom-transfer as the progressive triumph of the gospel: not a single political event but the gradual extension of Christ’s reign through the church, completed at the second advent.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:25

The NET notes that "time, times and half a time" = 1 + 2 + 0.5 = 3.5 years. The expression became a fixed apocalyptic formula for the period of eschatological tribulation, adopted directly by Revelation.

7:27

The NET observes the parallel between v.14 (the son of man’s kingdom) and v.27 (the saints’ kingdom). The saints receive what the son of man receives, suggesting either identification or representation.

Longman Notes

longman
7:21–22

Longman: the phrase "defeating them — until" is the most pastoral word in Daniel 7. Persecution is real; defeat is genuine; but the word "until" limits it. The suffering has a terminus.

7:27

Longman: the vision ends with the saints possessing the kingdom "forever." The last word of the Aramaic section (chs.2–7) is eternal sovereignty for God’s people. Everything between now and then is penultimate.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
7:25

Goldingay: the 3.5 years is half of seven — the completeness-number cut in half. The tribulation is deliberately incomplete, a broken period that signals it will end before reaching its natural conclusion.

7:27–28

Goldingay notes that Daniel is "deeply troubled" at the end. The visionary who receives the most exalted prophecy is the most personally disturbed. Apocalyptic knowledge does not produce serenity; it produces holy dread.

Collins Notes

collins
7:15–28

Collins identifies the "saints of the Most High" (qaddishē ʿelyōnîn) as potentially angelic beings (as "holy ones" often means in the OT, e.g., Deut 33:2–3; Ps 89:5) or as the faithful human community, or both. The ambiguity may be deliberate: heavenly and earthly faithful share the same kingdom.

7:25

Collins reads the 3.5-year period as alluding to the desecration of the temple under Antiochus IV (167–164 BC), which lasted approximately three and a half years according to 1 Maccabees.

Ancient Sources

The Prayer of Nabonidus (4Q242, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A fragmentary Qumran text describes a Babylonian king struck with illness for seven years who is healed by a Jewish exorcist — strikingly parallel to Nebuchadnezzar’s madness in Daniel 4.
The Prayer of Nabonidus may preserve an older version of the tradition behind Daniel 4, or reflect a common Near Eastern motif of royal humbling. Either way, it confirms the antiquity of the Daniel traditions.
1 Maccabees 1–2 (2nd c. BC)
Describes the historical desecration of the Jerusalem temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC — the event Daniel 8:9–14 and 11:31 prophesy as the “abomination of desolation.”
The fulfilment is documented in non-biblical Jewish sources, confirming Daniel’s prophetic precision whether the prophecy is dated to the 6th or 2nd century BC.
Josephus, Antiquities 10.11 (1st c. AD)
Josephus records that when Alexander the Great approached Jerusalem (332 BC), the high priest showed him Daniel’s prophecy about a Greek king conquering Persia, and Alexander was so impressed he offered sacrifices at the temple.
Whether historically accurate or legendary, Josephus’s account shows that Daniel’s prophecies were understood as referring to Greek conquest well before the Christian era.
