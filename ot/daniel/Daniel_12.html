Daniel 12

The Resurrection and the Time of the End

Verses 1–4 — “Multitudes Who Sleep in the Dust Will Awake”
1234

Hebrew Word Study

הָקִיצוּ hāqîṣū — they will awake: "Multitudes who sleep in the dust of the earth will awake (hāqîṣū): some to everlasting life, others to shame and everlasting contempt" (v.2). THE clearest resurrection text in the OT. Michael rises to protect Israel during unprecedented distress (v.1). Everyone written in the book will be delivered. Then: the dead awake to two destinations. "Those who are wise will shine like the brightness of the heavens, and those who lead many to righteousness, like the stars" (v.3).

Context

Daniel 12:2 is, alongside Isaiah 26:19, the OT’s strongest statement of bodily resurrection. The concept develops through the OT: Job 19:25–27 (I shall see God), Psalm 16:10 (you will not abandon me to the grave), Isaiah 25:8 (he will swallow up death), Isaiah 26:19 (your dead will live). Daniel 12:2 is the culmination: explicit, dual-destination, bodily resurrection. Jesus quotes it in John 5:28–29. The wise shining "like stars" (v.3) is echoed in Matthew 13:43 ("the righteous will shine like the sun").

Cross-Reference

  • John 5:28–29A time is coming when all who are in their graves will hear his voice and come out — Jesus directly quotes Daniel 12:2.
  • Matt 13:43The righteous will shine like the sun in the kingdom of their Father — echoing Daniel 12:3.
  • Isa 25:8He will swallow up death forever — the Isaiah parallel to Daniel’s resurrection promise.
  • 1 Cor 15:42–43Sown in dishonour, raised in glory; sown in weakness, raised in power — Paul’s resurrection theology builds on Daniel 12.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:1

Calvin reads the "time of distress such as has not happened" as both the Maccabean persecution and a type of the final tribulation. The historical and the eschatological are linked, not separated.

12:2–3

Calvin identifies Daniel 12:2 as the definitive OT proof of bodily resurrection. He notes the dual destination (life vs contempt) establishes the doctrine of final judgment that the NT fully develops.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:1

The NET notes that Michael is the only angel called "great prince" in the OT. His rising to act on Israel’s behalf signals an eschatological crisis beyond normal history.

12:2

The NET observes that "sleep in the dust" uses the standard OT euphemism for death (cf. Job 7:21; Ps 22:29). "Awake" reverses the metaphor: the sleepers rise. The two-destination teaching is unprecedented in its clarity.

Longman Notes

longman
12:2

Longman: the phrase "everlasting life" (ḥayye ʿōlām) appears here for the first time in the Bible. The concept that individual existence continues forever — in two possible directions — is Daniel’s most consequential contribution to theology.

12:3

Longman: "those who lead many to righteousness" introduces the concept of post-mortem reward for earthly ministry. The wise are not just saved; they are glorified in proportion to their faithfulness.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
12:2

Goldingay: the dual-destination resurrection is the text’s most revolutionary teaching. Death is not the end, and the afterlife is not neutral. What you did in history determines what you experience in eternity.

12:3

Goldingay reads the star-imagery as commissional: the wise SHINE. Their glory is not passive but radiating. The reward is visibility: those who were faithful in obscurity become cosmically luminous.

Collins Notes

collins
12:1–3

Collins notes that Dan 12:2 is the first unambiguous OT text affirming individual resurrection. Earlier texts (Isa 26:19; Ezek 37) may refer to national restoration rather than individual bodily resurrection. Daniel 12:2 removes the ambiguity: individuals awake from the dust to individual destinies.

12:3

Collins reads the wise "shining like stars" against the background of astral immortality in Hellenistic thought. The righteous become luminous beings — a concept that bridges Jewish and Hellenistic eschatology.

Verses 5–13 — “Go Your Way, Daniel; You Will Rise”
5678910111213

Hebrew Word Study

קֵץ qēṣ — end: "How long will it be before these astonishing things are fulfilled?" (v.6). Two angels stand on either side of the river. The man in linen raises both hands and swears: "a time, times and half a time" (v.7). Daniel does not understand and asks: "What will the outcome be?" (v.8). The answer: "Go your way, Daniel. The words are sealed until the time of the end" (v.9). Two additional numbers: 1,290 and 1,335 days. "Blessed is the one who waits for the 1,335 days" (v.12). The book’s final word: "You will rest, and then at the end of the days you will rise to receive your allotted inheritance" (v.13).

Context

The book ends with three time-periods: 3.5 years (v.7), 1,290 days (v.11), and 1,335 days (v.12). The differences (30 days and 45 days) have generated countless interpretive schemes. The simplest reading: 1,260 days = the tribulation proper (Rev 11:3; 12:6); 1,290 days = 30 additional days for purification; 1,335 days = 45 more days for full restoration. But no consensus exists. The final verse (v.13) is Daniel’s personal resurrection promise: rest, then rise, then inherit.

Cross-Reference

  • Rev 22:10"Do not seal up the words of this prophecy" — Revelation UNSEALS what Daniel SEALED. The contrast is deliberate.
  • Rev 14:13"Blessed are the dead who die in the Lord from now on. They will rest from their labour" — the NT echo of Daniel’s "you will rest and then rise."
  • Matt 24:21–22Great distress, unequalled from the beginning of the world — Jesus directly echoes Dan 12:1.
  • Heb 11:39–40These were all commended for their faith, yet none received what had been promised — Daniel waits, like all the faithful, for the resurrection.

"How long?" The 3.5 years from 7:25 confirmed with an oath. Both hands raised, sworn by the Eternal One.

12:9–10

"The words are sealed until the end. Many will be purified, but the wicked will continue." The wise understand; the wicked don’t. Purification ongoing.

12:12

"Blessed is the one who waits for the 1,335 days." The beatitude of patience: endurance through the numbered days.

12:13

"Go your way. You will rest, and then you will rise to receive your allotted inheritance." Daniel’s personal commission: go, rest, RISE.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:9–10

Calvin reads the sealing as God’s deliberate limitation on prophetic knowledge: not everything is revealed at once. The sealed book will be opened "at the time of the end" — Revelation 5 narrates this unsealing.

12:13

Calvin calls this the most comforting verse in the OT for any believer facing death: "you will rest, and then you will rise." Rest is not permanent; rising follows. The inheritance awaits beyond the grave.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:7

The NET notes the double-handed oath is the most solemn form of oath in the ancient world. The man in linen swears by "him who lives forever" — the oath-formula binds heaven itself to the timetable.

12:13

The NET observes three sequential promises: (1) go your way (live your remaining life faithfully), (2) you will rest (die in peace), (3) you will rise to receive your inheritance (bodily resurrection). The Christian doctrine of death and resurrection is compressed into one verse.

Longman Notes

longman
12:9

Longman: the sealed scroll connects Daniel to Revelation. What Daniel sealed, Christ unseals (Rev 5). The literary connection between the books is not accidental but canonical.

12:13

Longman calls this the most personal verse in the book: after eleven chapters of empires, visions, and angels, God speaks directly to one man: "You will rest, and then you will rise." The cosmic becomes intimate.

Goldingay Notes

goldingay
12:12

Goldingay: "Blessed is the one who waits" is Daniel’s beatitude. The spiritual achievement is not understanding the numbers but surviving them. Endurance, not calculation, is the virtue.

12:13

Goldingay: the book’s last word is not "kingdom" or "sovereignty" or "prophecy" but "inheritance." The personal promise: what God has prepared for Daniel — and for the reader — is waiting at the end of the days.

Collins Notes

collins
12:5–13

Collins notes the three time-periods reflect the apocalyptic community’s ongoing reinterpretation of Daniel’s numbers as the expected end failed to arrive precisely on schedule. The additions (1,290 and 1,335) may represent successive recalculations.

12:13

Collins reads Daniel’s personal promise as the book’s interpretive key: the individual believer’s hope is not in the accuracy of the timetable but in the certainty of the resurrection. The numbers may be debated; the rising is sure.

Ancient Sources

The Prayer of Nabonidus (4Q242, Dead Sea Scrolls)
A fragmentary Qumran text describes a Babylonian king struck with illness for seven years who is healed by a Jewish exorcist — strikingly parallel to Nebuchadnezzar’s madness in Daniel 4.
The Prayer of Nabonidus may preserve an older version of the tradition behind Daniel 4, or reflect a common Near Eastern motif of royal humbling. Either way, it confirms the antiquity of the Daniel traditions.
1 Maccabees 1–2 (2nd c. BC)
Describes the historical desecration of the Jerusalem temple by Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC — the event Daniel 8:9–14 and 11:31 prophesy as the “abomination of desolation.”
The fulfilment is documented in non-biblical Jewish sources, confirming Daniel’s prophetic precision whether the prophecy is dated to the 6th or 2nd century BC.
Josephus, Antiquities 10.11 (1st c. AD)
Josephus records that when Alexander the Great approached Jerusalem (332 BC), the high priest showed him Daniel’s prophecy about a Greek king conquering Persia, and Alexander was so impressed he offered sacrifices at the temple.
Whether historically accurate or legendary, Josephus’s account shows that Daniel’s prophecies were understood as referring to Greek conquest well before the Christian era.
