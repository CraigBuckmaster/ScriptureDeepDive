2 Kings 2

The Chariot of Fire — Elijah Taken, Elisha Succeeds

Verses 1–12 — Gilgal to Bethel to Jericho to Jordan; the Chariot of Fire
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

pî-shĕnayim pi-shnayim — double portion: Elisha asks for a “double portion” of Elijah’s spirit (v.9). This is not double power but the firstborn’s inheritance share (Deut 21:17). Elisha claims the right of the spiritual firstborn — primary heir to the prophetic ministry.

rekheb ʾish rechev esh — chariot of fire: The chariots and horses of fire (v.11) are YHWH’s heavenly army. Elisha will later pray his servant’s eyes be opened to see the same fiery hosts at Dothan (6:17).

sĕʿarāh searah — whirlwind: Elijah is taken up in a sĕʿarāh — a storm-wind (v.1, 11). The same word describes God’s appearance to Job (38:1; 40:6). The whirlwind is a theophanic vehicle.

Context

The journey from Gilgal to Bethel to Jericho to the Jordan is a reverse conquest route — Elijah retraces Joshua’s steps backward through the land before departing it. At each stop the sons of the prophets confirm: “The LORD is going to take your master today.” Elisha’s response — “I know; keep silent” — reveals both awareness and grief. The Jordan parts under Elijah’s mantle, and then the chariot of fire separates them. Elijah ascends. Elisha tears his garments, picks up the fallen mantle, and begins.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 21:17The firstborn receives a double portion. Elisha’s request uses this exact legal formula.
  • Josh 3:14–17Joshua led Israel across the Jordan on dry ground; Elijah now parts the Jordan with his mantle.
  • Mal 4:5“I will send the prophet Elijah before that great and dreadful day of the LORD.” Elijah’s departure without death opens the door for his return.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:9

Elisha’s request teaches us to desire above all things the gifts of the Spirit. The request is not presumptuous but faithful: he asks for what the ministry requires.

2:11

Elijah’s translation without death is a visible sermon on resurrection. God demonstrated that death is not the final word.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

2:3

The “sons of the prophets” (bĕnê hannebîʾîm) represent prophetic guilds — organised communities associated with particular sanctuaries. Similar religious communities are attested in Mari texts.

2:8

The Jordan crossing with a rolled mantle parallels Moses’ Red Sea crossing and Joshua’s Jordan crossing. The ANE wonder-working garment motif is well attested.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

2:9

Elijah’s conditional response — “If you see me taken, it will be yours” — introduces divine testing. The double portion requires spiritual perception.

2:12

Elisha tears his clothes (grief) but picks up the mantle (obedience). The prophetic calling continues through personal loss.

Verses 13–25 — Elisha Parts the Jordan; Jericho’s Water Healed; the Bears at Bethel
13141516171819202122232425

Hebrew Word Study

qĕlallîm kelallim — youths/young men: The “youths” (nĕʿārîm qĕtannîm, v.23) who mock Elisha are not small children but young men — the same word (naʿar) describes military aides throughout Kings. Their taunt “Go up, baldy!” is a theological challenge: “Go up like Elijah — disappear! We don’t need your ministry here.”

Context

Elisha parts the Jordan with Elijah’s mantle, confirming the prophetic succession. The sons of the prophets recognise it immediately: “The spirit of Elijah rests on Elisha.” They search three days for Elijah’s body and find nothing. Elisha then performs two acts that establish the twin poles of his ministry: healing Jericho’s water (creative, life-giving) and confronting mockery at Bethel (judgment on those who reject the prophetic office). The bears at Bethel — a centre of Jeroboam’s calf worship — are instruments of the covenant curses (Lev 26:22).

Cross-Reference

  • Josh 3:16–17The Jordan parted for Joshua’s ark; now it parts for Elisha’s mantle. The prophetic mantle carries the same authority as the ark of the covenant.
  • Exod 15:25Moses made bitter water sweet; Elisha makes bad water wholesome. Both prophets transform the environment.
  • Lev 26:22“I will send wild animals against you.” The covenant curses include animal attacks on communities that reject YHWH.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:14

God’s power belongs to the office, not the individual. The church should learn that the loss of a great leader does not mean the loss of God’s presence.

2:19

God can reverse any curse. What was barren becomes fruitful — a parable of regeneration.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:14

ʾadderet ʾēliyyāhû — Elijah’s mantle. The ʾadderet is a wide outer garment associated with prophetic office (1 Kgs 19:13, 19).

2:23

nĕʿārîm qĕtannîm — literally “small youths.” In context these are adolescents or young men old enough to form a mob and issue theological taunts.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:20

Salt was used in ANE purification rituals. Hittite and Babylonian texts describe salt ceremonies for consecrating new buildings and purifying water sources.

2:14

The striking of the Jordan with the ʾadderet is a symbolic act — the prophetic equivalent of Moses’ staff. In ANE thought, the implement carried the authority of its owner.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:15

The declaration “The spirit of Elijah rests on Elisha” is the book’s answer to the succession crisis. Israel’s real strength was never in its kings but in its prophets.

2:23

The key is location: Bethel was the centre of Jeroboam’s counterfeit worship. The young men represent a community that has systematically rejected YHWH’s prophets.
