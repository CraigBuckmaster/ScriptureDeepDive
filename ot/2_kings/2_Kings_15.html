2 Kings 15

The Parade of Kings — Rapid Decline

Verses 1–22 — Azariah/Uzziah; Five Kings of Israel in Rapid Succession
12345678910111213141516171819202122

Context

Azariah (Uzziah) of Judah reigns 52 years and does right — but is struck with leprosy and lives in quarantine while his son Jotham governs. Meanwhile in Israel, political chaos erupts: Zechariah (6 months, assassinated), Shallum (1 month, assassinated), Menahem (10 years, extracted brutal tribute to pay Assyria), Pekahiah (2 years, assassinated). Five kings in one chapter — the narrator accelerates through them because the northern kingdom is spiralling toward destruction. The Assyrian shadow falls: Tiglath-Pileser III (Pul) extracts a thousand talents of silver from Menahem.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Chr 26:16–21Chronicles explains Uzziah’s leprosy: he entered the temple to burn incense, usurping the priests’ role. Pride at the height of success brought judgment.
  • Isa 6:1“In the year that King Uzziah died, I saw the Lord.” Isaiah’s call vision occurs at the transition this chapter describes.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:19

Menahem’s tribute to Assyria teaches that those who abandon God for political alliances always pay a higher price than faithfulness would have cost. Foreign protection is never free.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:19

Pûl is an alternate name for Tiglath-Pileser III, used in Babylonian chronicles. The identification is confirmed by the Assyrian king list.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:19

Tiglath-Pileser III (biblical “Pul”) is well attested in Assyrian records. His annals mention Menahem of Samaria (Me-ni-hi-im-me Sa-me-ri-na-a-a) paying tribute. This is one of the clearest Assyrian confirmations of a biblical king.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:10

The rapid succession of assassinated kings demonstrates that the northern monarchy has lost all legitimacy. When power can only be seized by murder, the institution itself is bankrupt. The system Jeroboam I created is consuming itself.

Verses 23–38 — Pekah, Jotham; Assyria’s Shadow Falls
23242526272829303132333435363738

Context

Pekah seizes Israel’s throne through yet another assassination and reigns 20 years. During his reign Tiglath-Pileser conquers the northern territories — Gilead, Galilee, Naphtali — and deports the population. The northern kingdom is being dismembered piece by piece. Hoshea assassinates Pekah and takes the throne. In Judah, Jotham reigns faithfully but cannot stop the rot: “the high places were not removed.” The chapter ends with the Aramean-Israelite coalition forming against Judah — the Syro-Ephraimite crisis that will trigger Isaiah’s Immanuel prophecy.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 7:1–14The Aramean-Israelite threat against Judah produces Isaiah’s most famous prophecy: “The virgin will conceive and give birth to a son, and will call him Immanuel.”
  • 1 Chr 5:26Chronicles confirms the Assyrian deportation of the Transjordanian tribes: Reuben, Gad, and the half-tribe of Manasseh.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
15:37

The coalition against Judah tests whether the Davidic king will trust God’s promise or seek human alliances. This is the same test every generation faces: faith or self-reliance.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
15:29

The cities listed — Ijon, Abel Beth Maakah, Janoah, Kedesh, Hazor — trace a path through upper Galilee. The deportation policy was standard Assyrian practice: removing local populations and replacing them with deportees from elsewhere.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:29

Tiglath-Pileser III’s campaign against Israel is extensively documented in his own annals. He records the conquest of cities in Galilee and the deportation of populations to Assyria — exactly as described here. Archaeological evidence of destruction layers at Hazor, Dan, and Megiddo confirms the military campaign.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
15:29

The Assyrian deportations introduce the theme of exile that will dominate the rest of 2 Kings. The removal of populations is not merely military strategy but theological judgment: the people are removed from the land God gave them.
