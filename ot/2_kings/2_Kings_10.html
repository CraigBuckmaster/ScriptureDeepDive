2 Kings 10

The Purge — Jehu Destroys Baal Worship

Verses 1–17 — Seventy Sons of Ahab Killed; the House of Ahab Destroyed
1234567891011121314151617

Context

Jehu sends letters to Samaria’s leaders demanding the heads of Ahab’s seventy sons. The elders comply, sending the heads in baskets. Jehu stacks them at the gate of Jezreel — then uses them to make a theological argument: “I conspired against my master and killed him, but who killed all these? Know that not a word the LORD has spoken against the house of Ahab will fail.” He kills everyone connected to Ahab’s house, then encounters forty-two relatives of Ahaziah of Judah and kills them too. The purge is comprehensive and brutal.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 21:21“I will cut off from Ahab every last male.” Jehu executes this sentence literally.
  • Hos 1:4Hosea will later condemn Jehu’s “massacre at Jezreel” — suggesting that Jehu exceeded his commission.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:10

Jehu’s use of prophecy to justify bloodshed warns us that Scripture can be weaponised. The word of God must be handled with reverence, not wielded as a political instrument.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:1

“Seventy sons” may include grandsons and other male descendants. The number seventy is conventional for a large royal family (cf. Judg 8:30; 9:5).

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:1

The demand for seventy heads sent in baskets is attested in ANE military practice. Assyrian reliefs from Nineveh show piles of enemy heads at city gates. Jehu’s tactic combines ANE military convention with prophetic justification.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:16

Jehu’s “zeal for the LORD” is genuine but also self-serving. The narrator presents it without endorsement, allowing the reader to see both the legitimacy of the prophetic commission and the excess of its execution.

Verses 18–36 — Baal Worshippers Trapped and Killed; Verdict on Jehu
18192021222324252627282930313233343536

Context

Jehu announces: “Ahab served Baal a little; Jehu will serve him much.” It’s a trap. He summons every Baal priest and worshipper to a great sacrifice, then seals the temple and sends soldiers to kill them all. The Baal temple is demolished and turned into a latrine. Yet the Deuteronomistic verdict is devastating: “Jehu was not careful to keep the law of the LORD with all his heart. He did not turn away from the sins of Jeroboam.” He destroyed Baal but kept the golden calves. Zeal for reform that stops short of full obedience is still failure.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 18:40Elijah killed 450 prophets of Baal at Carmel. Jehu finishes what Elijah started — but with institutional destruction rather than prophetic contest.
  • Deut 13:12–18The law commanding total destruction of cities that worship other gods. Jehu executes this against Baal worship but ignores the golden calves.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:31

Jehu’s failure teaches that dramatic acts of reform are worthless without sustained personal holiness. It is easier to destroy an enemy’s temple than to purify one’s own heart. External revolution without internal transformation is theatre.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:27

The temple of Baal was converted into a lātrîn (latrine/refuse dump) — the ultimate desecration. The deliberate defilement ensured the site could never be reconsecrated for worship.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:27

The Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III shows Jehu (or his ambassador) prostrating before the Assyrian king and paying tribute. This extra-biblical evidence confirms Jehu’s historicity and reveals that his anti-Baal revolution did not protect him from Assyrian domination.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
10:29

The Jeroboam clause is the narrator’s permanent standard: every Israelite king is measured against Jeroboam’s foundational sin. No amount of Baal-destroying zeal compensates for maintaining the alternative worship system.
