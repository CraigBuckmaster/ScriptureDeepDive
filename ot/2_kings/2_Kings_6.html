2 Kings 6

Invisible Armies — Elisha and the Arameans

Verses 1–7 — The Floating Axe Head
1234567

Context

The sons of the prophets need more space and go to the Jordan to cut timber. A borrowed iron axe head falls into the water. Elisha throws a stick in and the iron floats. This small miracle — easily overlooked between dramatic narratives — reveals the prophetic ministry’s scope: God cares about a poor man’s borrowed tool. Iron was expensive; losing a borrowed axe head could mean debt slavery (cf. ch 4:1). The God who parts seas also floats axe heads.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 4:1–7The widow’s oil miracle — another intervention preventing economic catastrophe for the prophetic community.
  • Matt 10:29–31“Are not two sparrows sold for a penny? Yet not one of them will fall to the ground outside your Father’s care.” God’s attention to small things.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:6

God humbles himself to care about a lost axe head. This teaches that no concern of God’s people is too small for his attention. He who sustains the universe does not disdain to help a poor prophet retrieve a borrowed tool.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:5

The Hebrew shāʾal (“borrowed”) implies legal obligation to return the item. Loss of a borrowed tool could create a debt obligation.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:5

Iron tools were valuable in this period. While iron technology was widespread by the 9th century BC, quality iron implements were still expensive — roughly equivalent to several months’ wages for a labourer. The detail that the axe was borrowed underscores the prophetic community’s poverty.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:6

The floating axe head is the smallest miracle in Kings. Its inclusion alongside the parting of the Jordan and raising the dead makes a theological point: God’s power is not reserved for cosmic events but extends to the mundane losses of ordinary people.

Verses 8–23 — “Those Who Are with Us Are More”
891011121314151617181920212223

Hebrew Word Study

rekeb-ʾesh wĕsûsî ʾesh rechev-esh — chariots and horses of fire: Elisha prays that his servant’s eyes be opened, and the servant sees the hills full of horses and chariots of fire (v.17). The same heavenly army that escorted Elijah (2:11) now surrounds Elisha. The fire-army is always present; only spiritual sight reveals it.

Context

The king of Aram is enraged: every ambush he plans against Israel is foiled because Elisha reveals his war council’s secrets to Israel’s king. Aram sends an army to capture one prophet. Elisha’s servant wakes to find Dothan surrounded by horses and chariots. His terror produces one of Scripture’s greatest declarations: “Don’t be afraid. Those who are with us are more than those who are with them.” Elisha prays; the servant sees the mountain filled with fiery horses and chariots. Then Elisha prays for the Aramean army to be blinded, leads them into Samaria, and when their sight is restored they find themselves surrounded by Israel’s army. Instead of killing them, Elisha feeds them a feast and sends them home. The raids stop.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 2:11–12The same fiery chariots that took Elijah now protect Elisha. Elisha’s earlier cry — “The chariots and horses of Israel!” — named what he can now see surrounding him.
  • Rom 8:31“If God is for us, who can be against us?” The NT articulation of the principle Elisha demonstrates at Dothan.
  • Matt 5:44“Love your enemies.” Elisha feeds the Aramean army instead of destroying them — centuries before Jesus commands it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:16

Elisha’s confidence is not in his own strength but in God’s army. Every believer surrounded by trouble should remember that the invisible resources of God always exceed the visible threats of the world.

6:22

The feast for enemies teaches that God conquers not by force but by grace. The Arameans went home having tasted Israelite hospitality, and the raids ceased. Kindness was more effective than killing.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:17

The verb pāqah (“to open”) is used specifically for opening eyes to see spiritual reality (cf. Gen 21:19; Num 22:31). The fire imagery connects to the theophanic tradition of Exod 3, 13:21, and 19:18.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:13

Dothan (modern Tell Dothan) lies approximately 20 km north of Samaria in a strategic valley on the north-south trade route. Archaeological excavations have confirmed occupation in the Iron Age II period, consistent with this narrative.

6:17

The concept of a divine warrior with a heavenly army of fire-chariots has parallels in Canaanite mythology (Baal as chariot-rider) but the biblical version is distinctive: YHWH’s army serves the prophet, not the king.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:17

The Dothan episode is a parable of two ways of seeing. The servant sees with natural eyes and panics. Elisha sees with prophetic eyes and is calm. The entire book of Kings is an exercise in the second kind of seeing — reading political events through theological lenses.

6:22

Elisha’s command to feed the enemy is the most radical ethical statement in the Elisha cycle. It anticipates Jesus’ teaching on enemy-love by eight centuries.

Verses 24–33 — Ben-Hadad Besieges Samaria; Famine Horror
24252627282930313233

Context

Ben-Hadad besieges Samaria until famine reaches apocalyptic levels: a donkey’s head sells for eighty shekels of silver; a quarter cab of dove’s dung for five shekels. The king walks the wall and a woman cries out: she and another woman agreed to eat their children — she boiled her son yesterday, but today the other woman hid hers. The king tears his robes, revealing sackcloth underneath. He vows to kill Elisha, blaming the prophet for the siege. The chapter ends mid-crisis — judgment deferred to chapter 7.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 28:53–57Moses warned that siege conditions would drive Israel to cannibalism: “You will eat the fruit of the womb, the flesh of the sons and daughters the LORD your God has given you.” This is covenant curse language fulfilled literally.
  • Lam 4:10“With their own hands compassionate women have cooked their own children.” The same horror at Jerusalem’s fall in 586 BC.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:33

The king’s despair teaches that those who do not know God rightly will either blame him for their suffering or abandon him altogether. True faith holds to God precisely when circumstances seem to contradict his goodness.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:25

Eighty shekels of silver for a donkey’s head reflects hyperinflation under siege conditions. A normal donkey might sell for two to five shekels. The factor of 16–40x increase indicates complete economic collapse.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:25

The commodities mentioned — donkey head and dove’s dung — represent desperate extremes. “Dove’s dung” may be a colloquial name for a cheap wild plant (Ornithogalum umbellatum, or Star of Bethlehem) or may be literal, used as fuel or food substitute. Siege conditions this extreme are well documented in Assyrian and Babylonian records.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:28

The narrator places this horror without theological commentary because no commentary is adequate. The Deuteronomistic framework provides the interpretation: this is Deut 28:53 being enacted. The reader who knows Torah will recognise the covenant curse being fulfilled.
