2 Kings 21

Manasseh — The Point of No Return

Verses 1–18 — 55 Years of Apostasy; Prophets Declare Irreversible Judgment
123456789101112131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

heʿĕvir heevir — passed through: Manasseh “passed his son through the fire” (v.6), practised sorcery and divination, and consulted mediums and spiritists. The catalogue of abominations is comprehensive — every category of prohibited practice in Deuteronomy 18:10–12 is represented.

Context

Manasseh reigns 55 years — the longest reign of any king — and undoes everything Hezekiah built. He rebuilds the high places, erects Baal altars and an Asherah pole in the temple, worships the starry hosts, sacrifices his son, practises sorcery, and fills Jerusalem with innocent blood “from end to end.” The prophets declare: “I am going to bring such disaster on Jerusalem and Judah that the ears of everyone who hears of it will tingle.” God will “wipe Jerusalem as one wipes a dish.” Manasseh’s 55 years determine Judah’s fate: even Josiah’s reform cannot undo this damage (23:26–27).

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 23:26–27“Still the LORD did not turn away from the heat of his fierce anger... because of all that Manasseh had done.” Even Josiah’s reform cannot reverse Manasseh’s damage.
  • Jer 15:4“I will make them abhorrent to all the kingdoms of the earth because of what Manasseh son of Hezekiah king of Judah did in Jerusalem.” Jeremiah cites Manasseh by name as the cause.
  • 2 Chr 33:10–13Chronicles adds that Manasseh repented in Assyrian captivity. The Deuteronomistic historian omits this — his narrative requires Manasseh to be the irredeemable cause of exile.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:6

Manasseh’s sins demonstrate that evil has no bottom. Those who abandon God descend progressively into darker practices. The trajectory of apostasy is always downward, and there is always something worse below.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:13

The “measuring line of Samaria and the plumb line of Ahab” uses construction metaphors for destruction: God will demolish Jerusalem with the same precision he used to judge Samaria.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:1

Manasseh is mentioned in Assyrian records as a vassal of both Esarhaddon and Ashurbanipal. The Prism of Esarhaddon lists “Manasi king of Judah” (Ma-na-si-i shar Ia-u-di) among tribute-paying kings. His long reign reflects willing submission to Assyrian suzerainty.

21:3

The “starry hosts” (tseba hashsahmayim) refers to astral worship imported from Mesopotamia. Assyrian suzerainty brought Assyrian religion; Manasseh’s astral worship was likely a political-religious concession to his Assyrian overlords.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:13

The “measuring line” and “plumb line” are instruments of both construction and destruction. God built Jerusalem with these tools; now he will demolish it with the same. The Creator becomes the Destroyer when creation rejects its Creator.

Verses 19–26 — Amon: Assassinated After Two Years
1920212223242526

Context

Amon succeeds Manasseh and continues his practices for two years before his own officials assassinate him. The “people of the land” (landowners, traditional power base) kill the conspirators and enthrone eight-year-old Josiah. The transition is rapid and violent — two verses for a king who made no difference.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 12:20–21Joash was also assassinated by his officials. The pattern recurs: unfaithful kings die by internal conspiracy.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
21:24

The enthronement of a child-king teaches that God’s purposes do not depend on human maturity or readiness. He places eight-year-old Josiah on the throne because age is no barrier to God’s calling.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
21:24

ʿam hāʾārets (“people of the land”) — a socio-political term for the landed nobility or free citizenry who wielded influence in state affairs, distinct from both the court aristocracy and the urban populace.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:24

The “people of the land” (ʿam haʾārets) represent the landed gentry of Judah — a politically conservative class that appears at critical moments in the monarchy’s history to ensure dynastic continuity.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
21:24

The “people of the land” function as guardians of the Davidic succession. Their intervention at moments of crisis (here and in 11:14, 18–20) reveals that the dynasty survives not by its own strength but through the loyalty of its traditional base.
