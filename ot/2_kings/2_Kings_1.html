2 Kings 1

Fire from Heaven — Elijah vs Ahaziah

Verses 1–8 — Ahaziah’s Injury; Baal-Zebub; the Messengers Intercepted
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

baʿal zĕbûb baal-zevuv — lord of the flies: Ahaziah sends to consult Baʿal Zĕbûb, the god of Ekron (v.2). The name means “lord of flies” — almost certainly a deliberate Hebrew mockery of the Philistine title Baʿal Zĕbûl (“lord prince”). By the NT period this becomes Beelzebul, a name for Satan (Matt 12:24).

hălōʾ mibbĕlî halo-mibli — is it because there is no?: Elijah’s devastating question (v.3, repeated vv.6, 16): “Is it because there is no God in Israel that you are going to consult Baal-Zebub?” The rhetorical force is crushing — the king’s action implies YHWH either doesn’t exist or can’t answer.

Context

The opening of 2 Kings continues the theological crisis of 1 Kings: the house of Ahab is still on the throne, and Ahab’s son Ahaziah falls through a lattice in his upper room. Injured, he sends messengers not to YHWH’s prophet but to Baal-Zebub, the Philistine deity at Ekron — a journey of roughly 60 kilometres, bypassing every sanctuary of the living God. Elijah intercepts the messengers with the question that frames the entire chapter: “Is it because there is no God in Israel?” The king’s injury is minor; his theological defection is fatal.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 18:21“How long will you waver between two opinions?” Elijah’s challenge on Carmel confronted the nation; now the same prophet confronts the king directly with the same issue — who is God?
  • Matt 12:24The Pharisees accuse Jesus of casting out demons by “Beelzebul, the prince of demons” — the name derived from this Philistine deity.
  • Deut 18:10–12The prohibition against consulting pagan oracles. Ahaziah violates the covenant’s most fundamental boundary.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:3

When Elijah asks “Is it because there is no God in Israel?” he exposes the absurdity underlying all idolatry. Every act of false worship is practical atheism.

1:4

The sentence of death is precisely fitting. The shepherd who leads the flock to poisoned water bears the greater guilt.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:2

Baʿal Zĕbûb — literally “lord of the flies.” Most scholars view this as a deliberate pejorative distortion of Baʿal Zĕbûl (“Baal the Prince”).

1:8

ʾish baʿal sēʿār can mean either “a hairy man” or “a man wearing a hairy garment.” Most translations favour the latter.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:2

Ekron (modern Khirbet el-Muqannaʿ) was a major Philistine city with documented cultic activity. The Baal-Zebub oracle may have been associated with divination by observing insect behaviour — a practice attested in Mesopotamian and Hittite texts.

1:8

The hairy mantle and leather belt match depictions of prophetic figures in ANE art. The Assyrian Black Obelisk shows similar rough-clad religious figures.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:2

The narrator doesn’t explain why Ahaziah consults Baal-Zebub — the action speaks for itself. In the Deuteronomistic framework, consulting foreign gods is the defining sin of apostate kings.

1:3

Elijah positions himself between the king and the false god, forcing a confrontation with the real God. The prophet as roadblock is a recurring motif in Kings.

Verses 9–18 — Fire from Heaven; the Third Captain’s Plea; Ahaziah Dies
9101112131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

ʾish esh — fire: Fire (ʾish) descends from heaven twice in this chapter (vv.10, 12), echoing the fire at Carmel (1 Kgs 18:38). Fire is YHWH’s characteristic manifestation — consuming, purifying, unanswerable.

ʾish hāʾĕlōhîm ish ha-elohim — man of God: The captains address Elijah as “man of God” (v.9) — the standard respectful title for a prophet. But the captain’s command “come down!” turns the title into mockery. He uses God’s designation while ordering the prophet like a servant.

Context

Ahaziah sends three companies of fifty soldiers to arrest Elijah. The first two captains issue peremptory commands: “Man of God, the king says come down.” Fire from heaven consumes each company. The third captain falls on his knees and pleads: “Let my life be precious in your sight.” Humility is the only survival strategy when confronting God’s representative. After descending, Elijah delivers the same death sentence in person. “So he died, according to the word of the LORD” — the Deuteronomistic formula: the prophetic word is the engine of history. Joram succeeds the childless Ahaziah.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 18:38Fire from heaven at Carmel consumed the sacrifice. The same God who answered by fire to prove his existence now answers by fire to defend his prophet.
  • Luke 9:54James and John want to call fire “as Elijah did” — Jesus rebukes them. The new covenant operates with different weapons.
  • Num 16:35Fire from the LORD consumed Korah’s 250 rebels who challenged Mosaic authority. Challenging God’s spokesman has consistent consequences.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:10

God defends his servants not out of partiality but to vindicate his own honour. Irreverence toward God’s ministers is ultimately irreverence toward God.

1:13

The flames that consumed two companies did not make the third captain presumptuous; they made him wise. True fear of God is the beginning of safety.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:10

ʾish ʾĕlōhîm (“fire of God”) — in the context of the Elijah narratives, a supernatural divine action is clearly intended.

1:17

The chronological note creates a well-known synchronism difficulty. Most scholars attribute this to co-regencies common in both kingdoms.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:9

The military companies of fifty (hammishshim) reflect standard Israelite army organisation. A company of fifty with a captain was a basic tactical unit, attested also in Assyrian army texts.

1:17

The synchronistic dating reflects standard ANE practice of cross-referencing regnal years between kingdoms. Similar synchronisms appear in Assyrian and Babylonian chronicles.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:10

The escalating violence raises difficult questions, but the narrator presents it without apology: those who treat God’s prophet as disposable discover that God takes his prophets seriously.

1:13

The third captain demonstrates that the pattern is not mechanistic. Humility breaks the cycle — the same theological point as Nineveh in Jonah.
