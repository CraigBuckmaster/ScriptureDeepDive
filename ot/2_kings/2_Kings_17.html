2 Kings 17

The End of the Northern Kingdom

Verses 1–6 — Hoshea’s Reign; Assyrian Siege; Samaria Falls
123456

Context

Hoshea, Israel’s last king, does evil “but not like the kings of Israel who preceded him” — a faint praise that changes nothing. He becomes Shalmaneser V’s vassal, then conspires with Egypt’s So (Osorkon IV or Tefnakht). Shalmaneser besieges Samaria for three years. In 722 BC the city falls and its population is deported to Halah, Gozan, and the towns of the Medes. The northern kingdom of Israel ceases to exist after roughly 200 years. Ten tribes scattered across the Assyrian Empire — never to return as a political entity.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 28:36“The LORD will drive you and the king you set over you to a nation unknown to you or your ancestors.” The exile was prophesied by Moses before Israel entered the land.
  • Hos 13:16“Samaria must bear her guilt, because she has rebelled against her God.” Hosea, prophesying during this period, saw exactly what was coming.
  • Amos 5:27“I will send you into exile beyond Damascus.” Amos, a generation earlier, specified the direction of exile.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:6

The fall of Samaria teaches every nation that covenant unfaithfulness has consequences no military alliance can prevent. When God determines judgment, the strongest walls and most powerful allies are insufficient.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:4

“So king of Egypt” — the identification remains debated. Candidates include Osorkon IV of Tanis, Tefnakht of Sais, or an Egyptian military commander. The name may be a shortened form of an Egyptian royal name.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:3

Shalmaneser V (727–722 BC) is attested in both Assyrian king lists and the Babylonian Chronicle. His successor Sargon II claimed credit for Samaria’s fall in his own inscriptions, creating a historical debate about which king completed the conquest. The biblical text attributes the siege to Shalmaneser.

17:6

Halah, Gozan (Tell Halaf), and the Habor River are well-identified sites in upper Mesopotamia. Assyrian administrative texts from Gozan actually mention West Semitic personal names consistent with Israelite deportees.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:6

The narrator records the deportation with devastating brevity: one verse to end a kingdom. The compression is itself a theological statement — 200 years of history collapses into a single sentence of exile.

Verses 7–23 — The Deuteronomistic Verdict: Why Israel Fell
7891011121314151617181920212223

Hebrew Word Study

hevel hevel — worthless/vapour: The Israelites “followed worthless idols and themselves became worthless” (v.15) — the verb hāvāl means to become vapour, to become nothing. You become what you worship. Those who worship emptiness become empty. This is the book’s most devastating anthropological statement.

wayāsûrû vayasuru — they removed/turned aside: “They rejected his decrees and the covenant” (v.15) — the verb sûr means to turn aside, remove, depart. The same verb used for removing high places is now used for removing oneself from God. Israel’s departure from YHWH is described with the same vocabulary as the reforms they refused to make.

Context

This is the theological centre of 2 Kings — and arguably of the entire Deuteronomistic History. The narrator steps back from the chronicle of events and delivers a comprehensive indictment: Israel fell because they sinned against YHWH who brought them out of Egypt. The charges are systematic: they worshipped other gods, followed the practices of the nations God drove out, built high places, set up sacred stones and Asherah poles, burned incense, served idols, sacrificed their sons and daughters in the fire, practised divination and sought omens. God warned them through every prophet, but “they would not listen and were as stiff-necked as their ancestors.” The verdict: “They followed worthless idols and themselves became worthless.”

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 4:25–28Moses predicted exactly this outcome: “If you then become corrupt and make any kind of idol... you will quickly perish from the land... The LORD will scatter you among the peoples.”
  • Deut 28:15–68The covenant curses: invasion, siege, famine, exile. Every element of chapter 17 was foretold in Deuteronomy.
  • Jer 2:5“What fault did your ancestors find in me, that they strayed so far from me? They followed worthless idols and became worthless themselves.” Jeremiah echoes this passage exactly.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:13

God’s warning through “every prophet” demonstrates that judgment never falls without prior warning. God is not arbitrary; he is patient. But patience exhausted becomes judgment executed.

17:15

The transformation of the worshipper into the image of the idol is the deepest curse of false worship. We are shaped by what we adore. Those who worship the living God are made alive; those who worship emptiness are made empty.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:15

wayyehbālû — “they became worthless/vaporous.” From the root hābal, the same root as Ecclesiastes’ hebel (“vanity”). The wordplay connects Israel’s fate to the universal human tendency toward meaninglessness apart from God.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:9

The phrase “they secretly did things against the LORD their God that were not right” uses the rare verb hāpāh (“to cover, do secretly”). The implication is that Israel’s apostasy began as private practice before becoming public policy. The rot was internal before it was institutional.

17:16

The “two golden calves” are Jeroboam I’s foundational sin (1 Kgs 12:28–30). The historian traces the line from the first act of apostasy to the final destruction — 200 years of consequences from one decision.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:7

This passage is the narrator’s sermon — the only extended theological commentary in the entire book. He has been showing events; now he explains them. The placement is deliberate: at the moment of maximum catastrophe, the narrator finally speaks in his own voice.

17:15

The phrase “they became worthless” is the narrator’s most important anthropological claim: idolatry is not merely a religious error but a process of dehumanisation. To worship what is less than human is to become less than human.

Verses 24–41 — Foreign Peoples Settle in Samaria; Syncretistic Worship
242526272829303132333435363738394041

Context

The Assyrian king resettles foreigners from Babylon, Cuthah, Avva, Hamath, and Sepharvaim in the cities of Samaria. Lions kill some of them, so the king sends an Israelite priest back to teach the worship of “the god of that land.” The result is syncretism: “They worshipped the LORD, but they also served their own gods.” Each group makes its own idols while theoretically acknowledging YHWH. The narrator’s verdict: “To this day they persist in their former practices.” This passage explains the origin of the Samaritans and the deep hostility between Jews and Samaritans that persists into the NT.

Cross-Reference

  • John 4:19–22The Samaritan woman tells Jesus, “Our ancestors worshipped on this mountain.” Jesus responds: “You Samaritans worship what you do not know.” The theological critique of this passage echoes through the NT.
  • Ezra 4:1–3The returned exiles refuse Samaritan help in rebuilding the temple, citing their mixed worship. The roots of this conflict are in 2 Kings 17.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
17:33

Adding God to a collection of idols is not worship but insult. God demands exclusive devotion not because he is jealous in a petty sense but because divided worship is no worship at all. A God who shares the altar with Nergal and Ashima is not God.

17:41

The persistence of syncretism “to this day” warns every generation: false worship, once established, becomes cultural habit that outlasts the theology that produced it. Breaking with inherited idolatry requires radical, deliberate choice.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
17:33

The phrase “worshipping the LORD” (yērĕʾîm ʾeth-YHWH) alongside serving their own gods (wĕʾeth-ʾlōhēyhem hāyû ʿōbĕdîm) precisely describes the theological impossibility of syncretism: YHWH and the other gods occupy the same grammatical and cultic space.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:24

The cities listed — Babylon, Cuthah (Tell Ibrahim), Avva, Hamath, Sepharvaim — are all identifiable Mesopotamian and Syrian locations. Assyrian administrative texts confirm the policy of population exchange. The “Cutheans” (from Cuthah) became a later Jewish designation for Samaritans.

17:30

The deities mentioned — Succoth Benoth, Nergal, Ashima, Nibhaz, Tartak, Adrammelek, Anammelek — are a catalogue of Mesopotamian and Syrian divine names. Their identification with specific cities confirms the multinational origin of the resettled population.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
17:33

The syncretistic pattern — “worshipped the LORD but served their own gods” — is the narrator’s definition of the permanent religious problem. It applies not only to the Samaritans but, by implication, to every Israelite and Judahite who maintained high places alongside the temple.

17:41

The concluding “to this day” transforms the chapter from history into ongoing reality. The narrator writes for an audience that can verify the claim by visiting Samaria. The past is not past; its consequences are present.
