2 Kings 13

Elisha’s Death — The Arrow of Victory

Verses 1–9 — Jehoahaz; Israel Oppressed by Aram
123456789

Context

Jehoahaz (Jehu’s son) reigns 17 years and follows Jeroboam’s sins. Hazael and Ben-Hadad of Aram crush Israel until Jehoahaz’s army is reduced to 50 horsemen, 10 chariots, and 10,000 foot soldiers — a force barely sufficient for border defence. In desperation Jehoahaz “sought the LORD’s favour, and the LORD listened” — a rare moment of repentance in the northern kingdom. God sends a deliverer (unnamed), but the people still don’t remove the Asherah pole in Samaria.

Cross-Reference

  • Judg 3:9, 15The “deliverer” pattern echoes Judges: Israel sins, is oppressed, cries out, God raises a deliverer. The cycle repeats even in the monarchy.
  • 2 Kgs 10:32Hazael began reducing Israel during Jehu’s reign. The Aramean oppression spans two generations.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:4

God’s willingness to hear even a partially repentant king teaches that he does not wait for perfect contrition. He responds to the first movement of the heart toward him, however halting.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:5

The “deliverer” (mōshīaʿ) uses the same term as the judges-era saviours. The narrative deliberately links the monarchy’s crisis to the pre-monarchic pattern.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:7

The army figures — 50 horsemen, 10 chariots, 10,000 infantry — represent a dramatic reduction from Solomon’s era (1 Kgs 10:26: 1,400 chariots, 12,000 horsemen). Assyrian records from Shalmaneser III’s era show Ahab fielding 2,000 chariots at the Battle of Qarqar. The decline is verifiable.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:4

The narrator’s note that God “listened” to a king who continued in Jeroboam’s sins is theologically remarkable. It suggests that God’s compassion operates even when repentance is incomplete — a theme that challenges strict retribution theology.

Verses 10–25 — Jehoash; Elisha’s Deathbed Prophecy; Elisha’s Bones
10111213141516171819202122232425

Hebrew Word Study

chēts tĕshûʿat YHWH chets-teshuat-YHWH — arrow of the LORD’s victory: Elisha tells Jehoash to shoot an arrow eastward (v.17) and declares it “the arrow of the LORD’s victory, the arrow of victory over Aram.” The arrow is both a military weapon and a prophetic symbol — the direction (east, toward Aram) and the prophet’s declaration transform a physical act into a covenant promise.

Context

Jehoash (Joash) of Israel visits the dying Elisha and weeps: “My father! The chariots and horses of Israel!” — the same cry Elisha made for Elijah (2:12). Elisha tells him to shoot an arrow and then strike the ground with the remaining arrows. Jehoash strikes three times and stops. Elisha is angry: “You should have struck five or six times; then you would have destroyed Aram completely.” Partial obedience yields partial victory. Then Elisha dies and is buried. Later, a dead man thrown into Elisha’s tomb touches the prophet’s bones and revives — even in death, prophetic power persists.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 2:12Elisha’s cry for Elijah — “My father! The chariots and horses of Israel!” — is now echoed by Jehoash for Elisha. The title transfers across prophetic generations.
  • Matt 27:52–53At Jesus’ death, tombs opened and bodies were raised. Elisha’s bone-miracle anticipates the theme of death unable to contain God’s power.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
13:21

The miracle at Elisha’s grave teaches that God’s power is not extinguished by death. The same God who raised the Shunammite’s son through the living prophet now raises a stranger through the dead prophet’s bones. Death cannot limit omnipotence.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
13:17

The “arrow of victory” (ḥēts tĕshûʿah) combines military and theological language. The shooting of the arrow is a prophetic enacted sign — the physical action initiates the spiritual reality.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:20

Moabite raiding bands entering Israel in the spring were a regular feature of the period. The Moabite Stone confirms ongoing hostility between the two kingdoms throughout the 9th century.

13:21

The resurrection at the prophet’s tomb has no ANE parallel. Other cultures attributed power to the graves of holy men, but spontaneous resurrection through contact with bones is unique to this text.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
13:19

The arrow episode teaches that prophetic promises are not self-executing; they require human participation at full intensity. Half-hearted response to God’s invitation yields half-hearted results.
