Elisha’s Miracles — The Prophet Among the People

Verses 1–7 — The Widow’s Oil
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

ʾasûkh shemen asukh-shemen — jar of oil: The widow has only an ʾasûkh shemen — a small flask of oil (v.2). The same word appears in 1 Kgs 17:12 for the widow of Zarephath’s jar. Elijah and Elisha both multiply the last reserves of destitute widows — the prophetic ministry consistently begins with the marginalised.

Context

A prophet’s widow faces a creditor who will take her two sons as debt slaves. She has nothing but a flask of oil. Elisha tells her to borrow empty vessels, then pour. The oil flows until every vessel is full. She sells the oil, pays the debt, and lives on the rest. The miracle addresses real-world need: debt slavery was a concrete social crisis. God’s provision works through human action (she must borrow), is proportional to faith (the oil stops when vessels run out), and is comprehensive (debt paid, sons freed, livelihood secured).

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 17:8–16The widow of Zarephath’s oil never ran out. Elijah sustained one widow; Elisha multiplies oil to save another. The pattern escalates.
  • Mark 12:41–44The widow’s mite — another destitute widow whose tiny resource God honours disproportionately.
  • Deut 15:1–3Sabbatical year debt release was designed to prevent this situation. The creditor’s cruelty violates covenant social legislation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:2

God asks what we have, not what we lack. He begins with our resources and multiplies them. The principle governs all ministry.

4:7

Miracles restore us to responsible living, not exemption from it. Divine provision enables us to meet our obligations.

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:2

ʾasûkh shāmen — a narrow-mouthed jug, probably holding a few ounces. The diminutive size emphasises the contrast with the resulting abundance.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:1

Debt slavery was regulated by both Israelite law (Exod 21:2–4; Lev 25:39–42) and Mesopotamian law codes (Hammurabi §§117–119). The six-year limit was often ignored during economic stress.

4:2

Olive oil was the primary liquid commodity — used for cooking, lighting, medicine, cosmetics, and ritual. A supply filling many vessels would represent substantial wealth.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:1

The widow addresses Elisha as the successor responsible for the prophetic community. The master is responsible for deceased prophets’ dependents.

4:6

The cessation of oil when vessels run out introduces a theology of human participation in miracle. God works within the space human faith creates.

Verses 8–37 — The Shunammite Woman; Her Son Raised from Death
8910111213141516171819202122232425262728293031323334353637

Hebrew Word Study

ʿaliyyat qîr aliyat-kir — upper room on the wall: The Shunammite builds Elisha an ʿaliyyat qîr (v.10) — a permanent roofed room with bed, table, chair, and lamp. She creates institutional infrastructure for the prophetic ministry.

hāvĕq chavak — to embrace: The verb hāvĕq frames the miracle: the child she was promised (“you will embrace a son,” v.16), then held in death (v.20), she now holds alive (v.37).

Context

The longest narrative in the Elisha cycle. A wealthy Shunammite builds Elisha a room, and he promises her a son. The child is born but dies years later of apparent heatstroke. She rides to Carmel: “Did I ask you for a son? Didn’t I say, Don’t raise my hopes?” Gehazi’s staff fails; Elisha comes himself, lies upon the child, and the boy revives. The miracle parallels 1 Kings 17:17–24 but with deeper emotional texture — particularly the woman’s resistance to hope and her raw grief when hope fails.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 17:17–24Elijah raised the widow of Zarephath’s son by stretching over the child three times. Elisha uses the same method (v.34).
  • Luke 7:11–17Jesus raises the widow of Nain’s son. The crowd declares, “A great prophet has appeared.” The Elijah-Elisha echo is deliberate.
  • Gen 18:10–14Sarah laughed at the promise of a son; the Shunammite resists for the same reason. Both receive against all expectation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:16

God’s promises often exceed our capacity to trust them. God does not blame our weakness but fulfils his word despite it.

4:34

Elisha’s body-to-body contact prefigures the Word made flesh — God enters our condition so his life might flow into our death.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:10

ʿaliyyat qîr qĕtannāh — “a small upper room on the wall.” A permanent structure with bed, table, chair, lamp — a fully equipped guest room.

4:34

wayyasem pîw ʿal-pîw — “he placed his mouth on his mouth.” The parallel with 1 Kgs 17:21 is exact.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:8

Shunem (modern Solem) was on the Hill of Moreh in the Jezreel Valley, about 10 km south of the Sea of Galilee, on the north-south route — a natural stopping point for travellers.

4:34

The “stretching upon the child” parallels 1 Kings 17:21. The biblical narrative frames it as prophetic intercession, not sympathetic magic.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:28

“Did I ask you for a son?” is the book’s most piercing critique of prophetic promise. Her grief challenges Elisha to demonstrate that God’s prophets are agents of resurrection, not dispensers of empty words.

4:34

Gehazi’s staff fails; Elisha’s presence succeeds. Prophetic power is not transferable through objects. There are no shortcuts in the ministry of resurrection.

Verses 38–44 — Death in the Pot; Feeding a Hundred
38394041424344

Context

Two brief miracles close the chapter, both during famine. Poisonous gourds contaminate the prophets’ stew; Elisha adds flour and it becomes safe. Then a man brings twenty barley loaves and Elisha feeds a hundred prophets with leftovers. Both foreshadow Jesus: the healing of contaminated food recalls his authority over clean and unclean (Mark 7:18–19); the feeding of a hundred with twenty loaves anticipates the feeding of the five thousand (John 6). The ratio is 1:5; Jesus’ miracle is 1:1,000. Elisha establishes the category; Jesus shatters the scale.

Cross-Reference

  • John 6:1–14Jesus feeds 5,000 with five loaves and two fish — leftovers fill twelve baskets. Elisha feeds 100 with twenty loaves. The pattern is identical; the scale transforms.
  • Matt 14:20“They all ate and were satisfied.” Jesus’ miracle exceeds Elisha’s by a factor of fifty.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:43

The miracle begins precisely where human capacity ends. God asks only that we obey and then watch him work.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:42

lehem bikkûrîm — “bread of firstfruits.” Normally brought to the sanctuary (Deut 26:1–11). Bringing them to Elisha indicates the prophet was functioning as an alternative religious authority.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:39

The “wild gourds” (paqqûʿot) are likely Citrullus colocynthis, the colocynth or “bitter apple,” common in the Jordan Valley — highly purgative and potentially lethal.

4:42

Baal-Shalishah (modern Kefr Thilth) was about 25 km north of Lydda. The man’s gift of firstfruits to Elisha rather than a sanctuary suggests the prophetic community was a recognised recipient of sacral offerings.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:42

The servant sees twenty loaves and a hundred mouths. Elisha sees the word of the LORD. Obedience to the prophetic word, not mathematics, determines the outcome.
