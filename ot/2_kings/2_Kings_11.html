2 Kings 11

Athaliah and Joash — The Boy King Saved

Verses 1–3 — Athaliah Seizes the Throne; Joash Hidden for Six Years
123

Context

When Athaliah sees her son Ahaziah dead, she destroys the entire royal family and seizes the throne — the only non-Davidic ruler to sit on Judah’s throne. But Jehosheba (Joash’s aunt and the high priest Jehoiada’s wife) rescues the infant Joash and hides him in the temple for six years. The Davidic line — the promise of 2 Samuel 7 — hangs by a single thread: one baby, hidden in the house of God.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Sam 7:16“Your house and your kingdom will endure forever.” Athaliah’s purge comes within one child of extinguishing the very promise God swore would be eternal.
  • Matt 2:13–18Herod’s slaughter of Bethlehem’s infants echoes Athaliah’s purge. In both cases, God preserves the Davidic heir through the intervention of a faithful individual.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:2

The preservation of Joash demonstrates that God’s promises are not defeated by human wickedness. When the enemy has destroyed everything, God preserves a remnant — even if that remnant is a single child.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:1

“She proceeded to destroy the entire royal family” — zeraʿ hammamlākāh, literally “seed of the kingdom.” The term “seed” connects to the Davidic covenant promise (2 Sam 7:12).

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:1

Athaliah is the only woman to rule either Israelite kingdom. Her six-year reign (c.841–835 BC) is unique in the entire history of the Davidic dynasty.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:2

The survival of one child against a systematic royal purge is the narrative’s theological wager: God’s promise to David cannot be defeated by human violence. The smallest unit of survival — one hidden infant — is sufficient for God’s purposes.

Verses 4–21 — Jehoiada’s Coup; Athaliah Overthrown; Covenant Renewed
456789101112131415161718192021

Context

In the seventh year, Jehoiada the priest executes a carefully planned coup. He brings the temple guards and military commanders into the conspiracy, shows them the boy king, and positions armed guards throughout the temple complex. Joash is brought out, crowned, and anointed. The people shout “Long live the king!” Athaliah tears her robes and screams “Treason!” — but she is the usurper. She is executed outside the temple. Jehoiada then leads a covenant renewal: between the LORD, the king, and the people. The Baal temple is destroyed, its priest killed, and the boy king sits on the throne. The city is calm.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 17:18–20The law of the king: he must write a copy of the Torah and read it daily. The covenant renewal puts the relationship between king and law back at the centre.
  • 2 Chr 23:1–21The parallel account adds that Jehosheba was Jehoiada’s wife, giving the priest both personal and institutional motivation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
11:17

The renewal of covenant after Athaliah teaches that every generation must recommit to God’s covenant afresh. Faith is not inherited automatically; it must be chosen, confessed, and enacted by each new community.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
11:12

nēzer (crown) and ʿēdût (testimony/covenant document) together symbolise the dual basis of Davidic kingship: divine election and covenantal obligation.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:12

The “testimony” (tēdût) given to the king at coronation likely refers to a covenant document — either the Deuteronomic law or a formal statement of the king’s obligations. Similar coronation documents are attested in Hittite and Assyrian sources.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
11:17

The covenant renewal is not merely a ceremony but a constitutional reset. Athaliah’s reign represented a break in both the Davidic dynasty and the Sinaitic covenant. Jehoiada restores both simultaneously.
