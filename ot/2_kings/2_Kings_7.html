2 Kings 7

The Siege Lifted — Lepers at the Gate

Verses 1–2 — Elisha’s Prophecy: Abundance Tomorrow
12

Context

At the depth of the famine, Elisha announces the impossible: “About this time tomorrow, a seah of flour for a shekel and two seahs of barley for a shekel at the gate of Samaria.” An officer scoffs: “Even if the LORD opened the floodgates of heaven, could this happen?” Elisha’s reply is lethal: “You will see it with your own eyes, but you will not eat any of it.” The officer’s doubt is not intellectual uncertainty but active mockery of God’s power. His death sentence is proportional: he will witness the fulfilment he denied was possible.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 18:14“Is anything too hard for the LORD?” Sarah’s doubt and this officer’s scorn both challenge divine capacity.
  • Isa 55:8–9“My thoughts are not your thoughts.” The officer’s calculation of what’s possible is limited by his horizon.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1

God delights in reversing impossible situations overnight. The worse the crisis, the more dramatic the deliverance — so that the glory belongs entirely to him. Those who lose hope at midnight miss the dawn.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:2

The phrase “windows of heaven” (ʾarubbōt hashshāmayim) echoes Genesis 7:11 (the flood) and Malachi 3:10 (blessing). The officer’s sarcasm inverts a biblical promise.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:1

A seah was approximately 7.3 litres. A shekel of flour at the gate price means the market has normalised. The “gate” is the commercial centre where transactions were conducted and disputes adjudicated — the return to normal commerce signals the complete reversal of siege conditions.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:2

The officer’s doubt is framed as a question about divine mechanics: “How could this happen?” Elisha’s response ignores the mechanics entirely — he addresses not the “how” but the “whether.” The prophetic word doesn’t explain; it declares.

Verses 3–20 — Four Lepers Discover the Empty Camp; the Officer Trampled
34567891011121314151617181920

Context

Four lepers at the city gate reason: “If we sit here, we die. If we enter the city, we die. Let’s go to the Aramean camp.” They find the camp deserted — the LORD had caused the Arameans to hear the sound of a massive army, and they fled in panic. The lepers eat, drink, plunder — then conscience strikes: “This is a day of good news and we are keeping it to ourselves.” They report to the city. The king suspects a trap; scouts confirm the rout. The people plunder the camp and prices collapse exactly as Elisha prophesied. The doubting officer is appointed to manage the gate traffic — and is trampled to death by the crowd. He sees the abundance but never eats it.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 17:12–19Ten lepers approach Jesus — only one returns to give thanks. The Elisha narrative establishes lepers as agents of divine disclosure: the most marginalised carry the best news.
  • Isa 52:7“How beautiful are the feet of those who bring good news!” The lepers become the first heralds of salvation — the least likely messengers.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:9

The lepers recognise that grace imposes responsibility. Those who have received freely must give freely. To hoard the gospel is to deny its nature — good news is by definition meant for sharing.

7:17

The officer’s death is a warning to all who scoff at God’s promises. He stands as the eternal example of the person who stands close to salvation but refuses to enter. Proximity without faith is not enough.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:6

The verb hashmîyaʿ (“caused to hear”) is hiphil — a causative form. God actively produced the sound; this was not the Arameans’ imagination but a divinely generated auditory phenomenon.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:6

The Hittites and Egyptians mentioned as the army the Arameans feared hearing represent the two great military powers of the region. Ben-Hadad would have known that an alliance of Israel with either power would be devastating. The “sound” God produced triggered a realistic military panic.

7:16

The exact price fulfilment — a seah of flour for a shekel — confirms the prophetic word’s precision. The narrative carefully records the commercial data to demonstrate that God’s promises have measurable, verifiable outcomes.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:9

The lepers’ conscience — “we are not doing right” — transforms them from scavengers to evangelists. The shift happens spontaneously: encountering grace creates the obligation to share it. This is the Elisha narrative’s version of the Great Commission.

7:17

The officer’s death is narrated twice (vv.17, 20) — the repetition is deliberate. The Deuteronomistic historian wants no reader to miss the point: the prophetic word, once spoken, is inescapable. Belief or unbelief determines your relationship to its fulfilment, not the fulfilment itself.
