2 Kings 18

Hezekiah — Judah’s Great Reformer

Verses 1–16 — Hezekiah Destroys the High Places; Pays Tribute to Sennacherib
12345678910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

nĕhushtan nechushtan — bronze thing: Hezekiah smashes Moses’ bronze serpent (v.4), calling it Nehushtan — a dismissive pun meaning “just a bronze thing.” The object God used to save Israel (Num 21:8–9) had become an idol. Even legitimate sacred objects can become barriers to worship when they replace the God they were meant to represent.

Context

Hezekiah receives the highest verdict of any Judahite king: “There was no one like him among all the kings of Judah, either before him or after him.” He removes the high places, smashes the sacred stones, cuts down the Asherah poles, and destroys Moses’ bronze serpent. He trusts the LORD and does not cease to follow him. Yet when Sennacherib invades and captures Judah’s fortified cities, Hezekiah strips the temple doors and doorframes of their gold to pay tribute. Even the best king has moments of compromise.

Cross-Reference

  • Num 21:8–9God told Moses to make a bronze serpent; anyone bitten by a snake who looked at it would live. Hezekiah destroys it because Israel worships the object instead of its Creator.
  • John 3:14–15Jesus compares himself to Moses’ serpent: “Just as Moses lifted up the snake in the wilderness, so the Son of Man must be lifted up.”

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:4

Hezekiah’s destruction of the bronze serpent teaches that no material object, however ancient or sacred its origin, may become a substitute for God. The church must be vigilant against venerating relics, images, or traditions to the point of idolatry.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
18:4

nĕhushtan — a wordplay on nĕhōshet (“bronze”) and nāhāsh (“serpent”). Hezekiah’s dismissive renaming reduces the object from sacred symbol to mere material.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:13

Sennacherib’s 701 BC campaign against Judah is one of the best-documented events in ancient history. The Taylor Prism (British Museum) records Sennacherib’s own account: “As for Hezekiah the Judahite, who did not submit to my yoke, forty-six of his strong walled cities I besieged and conquered.”

18:14

Hezekiah’s tribute — 300 talents of silver and 30 talents of gold — closely matches Sennacherib’s own figure of 800 talents of silver and 30 talents of gold. The discrepancy in silver may reflect different weight standards or inclusion of other valuables.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:4

The Nehushtan episode demonstrates that religious reform sometimes requires destroying objects of genuine sacred history. The bronze serpent was God’s instrument; it became Israel’s idol. The reformer must distinguish between the gift and the Giver.

Verses 17–37 — The Rabshakeh’s Speech: Psychological Warfare at Jerusalem’s Walls
171819202122232425262728293031323334353637

Hebrew Word Study

rab-shaqēh rav-shakeh — chief cupbearer/field commander: The Rabshakeh (v.17) is not a name but a title — the chief Assyrian envoy. His speech in Hebrew (Yehudit, v.26) is a deliberate provocation: he speaks the people’s language to undermine their king’s authority directly.

Context

Sennacherib sends his Rabshakeh (field commander) with a large army to Jerusalem’s walls. The Rabshakeh delivers a masterclass in psychological warfare in Hebrew: Don’t trust Hezekiah. Don’t trust Egypt. Don’t trust YHWH — “Has the god of any nation ever delivered his land from the king of Assyria?” When Judah’s officials beg him to speak Aramaic (the diplomatic language) so the people on the wall can’t understand, he refuses: he wants the common people to hear and despair. The people remain silent, following the king’s command.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 36:1–22The parallel account in Isaiah provides the same speech with minor variations, confirming this as a fixed tradition.
  • Ps 46:1–3“God is our refuge and strength. Therefore we will not fear, though the earth give way.” A psalm possibly composed during the Sennacherib crisis.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
18:30

The Rabshakeh’s speech contains truths mixed with lies — the most dangerous form of deception. Yes, no other god has defeated Assyria. But YHWH is not “other gods.” The categorical error is fatal to the Rabshakeh’s argument.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
18:26

yĕhûdît (“Judean”/Hebrew) vs. ʾaramît (Aramaic) — one of the earliest references to Hebrew as a distinct language. The bilingual situation reflects the multilingual reality of 8th-century Judah.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:17

The Tartan, Rabsaris, and Rabshakeh are Assyrian military-administrative titles, not personal names. Their identification as titles (not names) was confirmed by the decipherment of Assyrian cuneiform in the 19th century.

18:26

The request to speak Aramaic (ʾaramît) rather than Hebrew (yĕhûdît) reflects the linguistic reality: Aramaic was the diplomatic lingua franca of the Assyrian Empire, while Hebrew was the vernacular. The Rabshakeh’s insistence on Hebrew is psychological warfare.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
18:25

The Rabshakeh’s claim to divine authorisation is the most sophisticated form of blasphemy: using God’s own language against God’s people. The claim is false, but the reader only knows this because the narrator has already told us Hezekiah “trusted in the LORD.”
