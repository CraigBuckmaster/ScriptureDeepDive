2 Kings 5

Naaman the Leper — Faith from an Unexpected Source

Verses 1–14 — Naaman Comes to Elisha; “Wash in the Jordan Seven Times”
1234567891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

mĕtsōraʿ metsora — leprous/skin-diseased: Naaman is a mĕtsōraʿ (v.1) — afflicted with a serious skin disease. The term covers a range of dermatological conditions beyond modern leprosy (Hansen’s disease). In Israel, a mĕtsōraʿ was ritually unclean and excluded from the community (Lev 13–14). That a foreign general bears this condition while commanding armies creates a stark irony.

naʿarāh qĕtannāh naarah-ketannah — young girl: The catalyst for Naaman’s healing is a captured Israelite slave girl (v.2) — a naʿarāh qĕtannāh. She has been torn from her homeland by the very army Naaman commands, yet she directs her master to the prophet of the God of Israel. Faith in the powerless changes the powerful.

Context

Naaman is commander of the Aramean army — a great warrior, honoured by his king, but afflicted with a skin disease. A captured Israelite girl tells his wife about Elisha. Naaman arrives with horses, chariots, and ten talents of silver, expecting a dramatic healing ceremony. Instead, Elisha doesn’t even come to the door; he sends a messenger: “Wash in the Jordan seven times.” Naaman is furious — the rivers of Damascus are better than the Jordan! His servants persuade him to obey, and his flesh is restored “like that of a young boy.” The miracle demolishes every human expectation: the wrong person (a Gentile), the wrong method (no ceremony), the wrong river (the muddy Jordan).

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 4:27Jesus cites this episode: “There were many lepers in Israel in Elisha’s time, but only Naaman the Syrian was cleansed.” The Nazareth synagogue erupts — Jesus is saying God’s grace crosses ethnic boundaries.
  • Lev 13–14The elaborate ritual purity laws for skin diseases. Naaman’s healing bypasses the entire system — a Gentile is cleansed by a prophet’s word, not by priestly examination.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:10

God deliberately uses humble means to humble the proud. The Jordan is muddy and insignificant; precisely for that reason it is chosen. God’s instruments are always beneath human dignity so that glory belongs to him alone.

5:14

The sevenfold washing in the Jordan is a sacramental act — the outward sign of inward transformation. Naaman’s obedience is the channel through which grace flows. The water does not heal; God heals through the water.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:1

sar-tsaba — “commander of the army.” This is the highest military rank below the king. The note that “through him the LORD had given victory to Aram” is theologically striking: YHWH works through pagan armies.

5:12

“Are not Abana and Pharpar, the rivers of Damascus, better than all the waters of Israel?” The Barada (Abana) and A’waj (Pharpar) were indeed cleaner and more impressive than the Jordan.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:1

Aram (Syria) with its capital Damascus was Israel’s most significant regional rival. Naaman’s position as army commander (sar-tsaba) made him second only to the king. Assyrian records confirm Aramean military strength in this period.

5:5

Ten talents of silver (approximately 340 kg), six thousand shekels of gold, and ten sets of clothing represent enormous wealth — a payment appropriate for a royal treaty, not a medical consultation. Naaman treats the healing as a diplomatic transaction.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:11

Naaman’s expectation — “I thought he would surely come out, stand and call on the name of the LORD” — reveals his assumption that healing requires theatrical performance. Elisha’s simple instruction deconstructs the pagan model of wonder-working.

5:13

The servants’ logic is devastating: “If the prophet had told you to do some great thing, would you not have done it?” The objection to simplicity is pride, not reason.

Verses 15–27 — Naaman’s Confession; Gehazi’s Greed; Leprosy Transferred
15161718192021222324252627

Hebrew Word Study

gōʾēl goel — no God in all the earth: Naaman’s confession (v.15): “Now I know that there is no God in all the earth except in Israel.” This is a monotheistic declaration from a pagan general — the theological climax of the chapter. He moves from consulting his king to confessing Israel’s God.

Context

Naaman returns to Elisha and makes the confession that eluded every king of Israel: “There is no God in all the earth except in Israel.” He offers gifts; Elisha refuses — grace is not for sale. Naaman asks for two mule-loads of Israelite earth (to worship YHWH on holy ground) and forgiveness for bowing in Rimmon’s temple with his king. Elisha’s response — “Go in peace” — is breathtakingly generous. Then Gehazi, Elisha’s servant, runs after Naaman and lies to extract the gifts. Elisha confronts him: “Naaman’s leprosy will cling to you.” The contrast is total: the Gentile gains faith; the Israelite servant gains leprosy.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 10:8“Freely you have received; freely give.” Elisha’s refusal of payment establishes the principle Jesus commands his disciples to follow.
  • Acts 8:18–20Simon Magus offers money for the Spirit’s power; Peter rebukes him. The Gehazi pattern recurs in the early church.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:15

Naaman’s confession teaches that God’s grace breaks through every barrier — ethnic, military, religious. The gospel was already reaching Gentiles in Elisha’s day.

5:20

Gehazi’s sin is not merely theft but blasphemy — he monetises what God gave freely, turning grace into commerce. This is the foundational corruption that Jesus will drive from the temple.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:17

The request for “two mule-loads of earth” reflects the common ANE concept of deity-land association. Naaman wants to worship YHWH on YHWH’s own soil.

5:18

Rimmon — an alternate name for the Aramean storm-god Hadad. The name means “Thunderer” and is related to Akkadian Rammānu.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:17

Naaman’s request for Israelite earth reflects the ANE belief that gods were territorial — each deity could only be properly worshipped on their own soil. Naaman’s theological understanding is incomplete but genuine.

5:18

Rimmon (Hadad-Rimmon) was the chief deity of Damascus, equivalent to the storm-god Baal. The temple of Rimmon in Damascus has been identified with the site of the later Roman Temple of Jupiter and the current Umayyad Mosque.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:18

Elisha’s “Go in peace” to Naaman’s request about bowing in Rimmon’s temple is one of the most theologically generous moments in the OT. The prophet accepts imperfect obedience from a genuine convert. Naaman’s situation — a new believer in an pagan court — requires pastoral wisdom, not rigid demands.

5:27

The Gehazi episode inverts the Naaman miracle: faith heals, greed infects. The servant who should have learned from the master’s example instead provides the counter-example.
