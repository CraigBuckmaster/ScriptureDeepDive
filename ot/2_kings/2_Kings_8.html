2 Kings 8

Hazael, Joram, Ahaziah — The Net Tightens

Verses 1–15 — The Shunammite’s Land Restored; Elisha Weeps Over Hazael
123456789101112131415

Hebrew Word Study

bakah bakah — to weep: Elisha weeps (v.11) — not for himself but for the future suffering Hazael will inflict on Israel. The prophet sees what the diplomat will become: “You will set fire to their fortified places, kill their young men, dash their children to the ground, and rip open their pregnant women.” Prophetic sight brings prophetic grief.

Context

Two scenes intercut: the Shunammite woman from chapter 4 returns from seven years in Philistia and has her land restored — at the exact moment Gehazi is telling the king about Elisha’s miracle of raising her son. Providence arranges the timing. Then Elisha visits Damascus, where Ben-Hadad is ill. Hazael is sent to ask whether the king will recover. Elisha’s answer is deliberately ambiguous: “Go, say to him, ‘You will certainly recover’ — but the LORD has revealed to me that he will in fact die.” Elisha stares at Hazael and weeps: he sees the atrocities Hazael will commit against Israel. The next day Hazael smothers Ben-Hadad with a wet cloth and seizes the throne.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 19:15At Horeb, God told Elijah to anoint Hazael king of Aram. Elisha now fulfils this commission — the prophetic word from Sinai reaches Damascus.
  • Amos 1:3–4“For three sins of Damascus, even for four, I will not relent. Because she threshed Gilead with sledges having iron teeth.” Amos condemns the very atrocities Elisha foresees here.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:11

Elisha’s tears show that true prophets are not cold dispensers of divine decrees but broken-hearted witnesses to human suffering. The prophet who sees most clearly weeps most deeply.

8:15

Hazael’s rise to power through murder warns us that God’s instruments are not always God’s saints. He uses the wicked to accomplish his purposes, then holds them accountable for their wickedness.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:10

The Hebrew is ambiguous: lō-yimût can be read as “he will certainly not die” (from the disease) or “he will certainly die” (by other means). The ketiv-qere variation here suggests the ambiguity is original and deliberate.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:7

An Assyrian inscription of Shalmaneser III refers to Hazael as “son of nobody” — i.e., a usurper without royal lineage. This confirms the biblical account of Hazael’s seizure of power by violence rather than legitimate succession.

8:12

The atrocities Elisha describes — killing young men, dashing children, ripping open pregnant women — are stock language for the horrors of ANE warfare, attested in Assyrian royal inscriptions. The Assyrians boasted of such acts; Israelite prophets wept over them.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:1

The intercutting of the Shunammite’s restoration with the Hazael scene creates a structural contrast: God’s care for individuals (the woman’s land) versus God’s use of nations as instruments of judgment (Hazael’s rise). Both are acts of sovereignty.

8:15

The narrator reports the regicide without moral commentary. The Deuteronomistic historian trusts the reader to supply the judgment: this is what happens when God’s instruments are themselves morally corrupt.

Verses 16–29 — Joram and Ahaziah of Judah; the House of Ahab Corrupts Judah
1617181920212223242526272829

Context

The focus shifts to Judah. Joram son of Jehoshaphat becomes king — and “walked in the ways of the kings of Israel, as the house of Ahab had done, for he married a daughter of Ahab.” The Omride corruption has crossed the border through marriage alliance. Edom revolts; Judah loses control. Then Ahaziah succeeds and continues the same pattern. The stage is set for Jehu’s revolution: both kingdoms are now ruled by kings connected to the house of Ahab, and both are ripe for judgment.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 16:31Ahab’s marriage to Jezebel introduced Baal worship. Now the pattern repeats: intermarriage with Ahab’s house imports Ahab’s sins into Judah.
  • 2 Chr 21:6The parallel in Chronicles adds that Joram murdered his brothers upon taking the throne — further evidence of the Omride corruption spreading through the marriage alliance.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:19

God preserves Judah not for the sake of its current king but for the sake of his promise to David. This teaches that God’s faithfulness to his covenant outlasts the unfaithfulness of his people.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:19

The “lamp” (nīr) metaphor for the Davidic dynasty appears also in 1 Kgs 11:36 and 15:4. It signifies ongoing dynastic presence — the flame of David’s house has not been extinguished.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:20

Edom’s revolt from Judahite control is confirmed by archaeological evidence from the 9th century BC. The copper mines at Timna and the Red Sea port at Ezion-Geber were significant economic assets; their loss would have been felt severely.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:19

The lamplight metaphor — “to maintain a lamp for David” — means that the Davidic line continues to burn even when individual kings are faithless. The promise to David is unconditional in its persistence, even though individual kings face consequences.
