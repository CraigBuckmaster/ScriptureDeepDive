2 Kings 14

Amaziah and Jeroboam II

Verses 1–22 — Amaziah’s Reign; War with Israel; Jerusalem Breached
12345678910111213141516171819202122

Context

Amaziah of Judah does right “but not as David had done” — the qualified verdict. He defeats Edom, then unwisely challenges Israel’s Jehoash. Jehoash replies with a parable: “A thistle in Lebanon sent a message to a cedar: ‘Give your daughter to my son in marriage.’ Then a wild beast trampled the thistle.” Amaziah ignores the warning. Israel defeats Judah, breaks down 180 metres of Jerusalem’s wall, and plunders the temple. A Judahite king’s overconfidence produces the first breach of Jerusalem since David captured it.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Sam 5:6–9David captured Jerusalem and made it his capital. The breach of the wall under Amaziah reverses David’s achievement.
  • Prov 16:18“Pride goes before destruction, and a haughty spirit before a fall.” Amaziah’s challenge to Israel is pride incarnate.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:9

The thistle parable teaches that self-knowledge is essential to wisdom. Those who overestimate their own strength court destruction. Humility is not weakness but accurate self-assessment.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:7

Sela (literally “rock”) may be Petra or another Edomite fortress. The renaming to Joktheel is a standard ANE practice of asserting sovereignty through toponymic change.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:7

The Valley of Salt (Wadi el-Milh, south of the Dead Sea) was a recurring battlefield between Judah and Edom. David fought there (2 Sam 8:13); Amaziah’s victory echoes the earlier campaign.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:10

Amaziah’s defeat by Israel demonstrates that military victory over one enemy (Edom) does not guarantee invincibility. Success in one arena produces dangerous overconfidence in another.

Verses 23–29 — Jeroboam II: Prosperity Without Righteousness
23242526272829

Context

Jeroboam II reigns 41 years and restores Israel’s borders from Lebo Hamath to the Dead Sea — the greatest territorial extent since Solomon. The narrator mentions Jonah son of Amittai (v.25) as the prophet who predicted this expansion. But the treatment is shockingly brief: the most prosperous king since Solomon gets seven verses. The narrator has no interest in political success that isn’t accompanied by covenant faithfulness. Amos and Hosea prophesy during this reign, condemning the wealth and injustice that Jeroboam’s prosperity enables.

Cross-Reference

  • Amos 6:1–7Amos condemns the luxury of Jeroboam II’s elite: ivory beds, choice meats, idle music — while the poor are trampled. Prosperity without justice is judgment in disguise.
  • Jonah 1:1Jonah son of Amittai — the same prophet mentioned here — is sent to Nineveh. His Israelite nationalism (he wants Nineveh destroyed) fits the context of Jeroboam’s expansionist reign.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
14:26

God’s compassion for Israel during Jeroboam II’s reign demonstrates that divine mercy is not conditioned on human merit. God sends relief to a wicked nation not because they deserve it but because he is gracious.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
14:25

Lebo Hamath (modern Labweh at the watershed between the Orontes and Litani rivers) marks the northern boundary of Israel’s ideal territory. Its recovery under Jeroboam II is confirmed by the general geopolitical situation: Assyria’s temporary weakness allowed regional powers to expand.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:25

The borders “from Lebo Hamath to the Dead Sea” match the ideal boundaries described in Num 34:7–12. Jeroboam II’s achievement represents the closest any northern king comes to the Davidic-Solomonic ideal — but without its theological foundation.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
14:23

Seven verses for a 41-year reign is the narrator’s most eloquent commentary: political success without covenant faithfulness is theologically irrelevant. The Deuteronomistic historian is not writing political history but theological history.
