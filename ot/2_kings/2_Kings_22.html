2 Kings 22

Josiah Finds the Book of the Law

Verses 1–13 — Temple Repair; Hilkiah Finds the Book; Josiah Tears His Robes
12345678910111213

Hebrew Word Study

sēpher hattorah sefer-hatorah — Book of the Law: Hilkiah discovers the sēpher hattorah — the “Book of the Law” (v.8). Most scholars identify this with Deuteronomy or its core (chs 12–26). The book’s content produces immediate and extreme grief in Josiah, suggesting it contains specific covenant curses that match Judah’s current condition.

Context

Josiah, now 26, orders temple repairs (echoing Joash in chapter 12). During the work, Hilkiah the high priest discovers the “Book of the Law” — apparently lost or hidden during Manasseh’s long reign. When the book is read to Josiah, he tears his robes: “Great is the LORD’s anger that burns against us because those who have gone before us have not obeyed the words of this book.” The discovery of Torah produces crisis, not comfort. The king realises how far Judah has fallen from God’s requirements.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 31:24–26Moses commanded that the Book of the Law be placed beside the ark as a witness. Josiah discovers it in the temple — exactly where Moses put it.
  • Deut 28:15–68The covenant curses that would have terrified Josiah: siege, famine, exile, disease. Reading Deuteronomy 28 while living in Manasseh’s aftermath would be horrifying.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:11

Josiah’s grief upon hearing God’s word teaches that Scripture is not meant to comfort the comfortable but to disturb the disturbed. The word that tears robes is doing its proper work.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:8

sēpher hattōrah (“the book of the law/instruction”) is the same designation used for the document Moses wrote and placed beside the ark (Deut 31:26). The definite article suggests a specific, known document.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:3

The 18th year of Josiah (622 BC) is one of the most securely dated events in OT chronology. It coincides with Assyria’s dramatic weakening — Ashurbanipal died around 627 BC, and the empire was collapsing. Josiah’s reforms were politically possible because Assyrian control had evaporated.

22:8

The discovery of a “lost” law book in a temple during renovation has Near Eastern parallels. Ashurbanipal claims to have found ancient texts during temple repairs at Nineveh. The pattern — renovation reveals forgotten sacred texts — is a documented phenomenon.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:8

The book’s loss and rediscovery raises profound questions: how does a nation lose its founding document? The answer is Manasseh: 55 years of systematic apostasy can bury Scripture so deeply that its rediscovery feels like a new revelation.

Verses 14–20 — Huldah the Prophetess: Judgment Delayed for Josiah
14151617181920

Context

Josiah sends the high priest and his officials to inquire of the LORD. They go not to Jeremiah or Zephaniah (both active prophets) but to Huldah, a prophetess living in Jerusalem’s Second District. She delivers a two-part oracle: (1) “I am going to bring disaster on this place” — the judgment announced in chapter 21 stands; (2) “Because your heart was responsive and you humbled yourself before the LORD... you will be gathered to your ancestors in peace” — Josiah will die before the catastrophe. A woman validates the Book of the Law and pronounces its verdict. The text sees nothing remarkable in this — prophetic authority belongs to whomever God calls.

Cross-Reference

  • Judg 4:4–5Deborah judged Israel and prophesied. Huldah stands in the same tradition: women as authoritative voices of God at critical moments.
  • 2 Chr 34:22–28The parallel preserves the same account. Huldah’s oracle is transmitted identically in both traditions.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:19

God’s responsiveness to Josiah’s humble heart demonstrates that personal piety is never wasted, even when it cannot change the nation’s trajectory. The faithful individual receives God’s personal attention even within corporate judgment.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:14

hannĕbîʾāh (“the prophetess”) with the definite article suggests a recognised, established prophetic figure. Other named prophetesses include Miriam (Exod 15:20), Deborah (Judg 4:4), and Isaiah’s wife (Isa 8:3).

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:14

The “Second District” (mishneh) of Jerusalem is the area northwest of the Temple Mount, expanded during Hezekiah’s reign to accommodate refugees from the fallen northern kingdom. Archaeological excavations in the Jewish Quarter confirm 8th–7th century residential expansion in this area.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:14

Huldah’s prophetic authority is presented without comment or qualification. She is simply “the prophetess” — the definite article suggests she is well known. The text treats female prophetic authority as unremarkable, a fact often overlooked.
