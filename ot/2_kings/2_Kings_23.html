2 Kings 23

The Great Reform — and Its Limits

Verses 1–20 — Covenant Renewed; Pagan Shrines Destroyed; Bethel Altar Demolished
1234567891011121314151617181920

Context

Josiah launches the most comprehensive reform in Israel’s history. He reads the Book of the Law publicly, renews the covenant, then systematically destroys: the Baal and Asherah vessels in the temple, the idolatrous priests, the high places from Geba to Beersheba, the Topheth in the Valley of Hinnom (the child-sacrifice site), the horses dedicated to the sun god, the altars Manasseh built in the temple courts, and the high places Solomon built for foreign gods on the Mount of Olives. Then he crosses into former Israelite territory and destroys Jeroboam’s altar at Bethel — fulfilling the prophecy of the unnamed man of God from 1 Kings 13:2, spoken 300 years earlier.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 13:1–2The man of God prophesied against Jeroboam’s altar: “A son named Josiah will be born to the house of David. On you he will sacrifice the priests of the high places.” Fulfilled here, by name, three centuries later.
  • Deut 12:2–3“Destroy completely all the places where the nations you are dispossessing worship their gods.” Josiah finally executes what Moses commanded.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:3

Josiah’s reform teaches that no nation is beyond the reach of repentance, even if the consequences of past sin remain. The reform is genuine, the obedience is real, and God honours it — even though it cannot reverse the national judgment.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:10

topheth — the sacrificial site in the Valley of Hinnom south of Jerusalem. The name may derive from tōph (“drum”) — drums were beaten to drown out children’s cries during sacrifice. The defiled site later became Jerusalem’s rubbish dump, providing the imagery for Gehenna.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:4

The “vessels made for Baal, Asherah, and the starry hosts” inside the temple confirm that Manasseh’s paganisation extended into the sanctuary itself. The reforms required physical purging of the temple — literally carrying out contaminated objects.

23:11

Horses dedicated to the sun god reflect Mesopotamian influence. Sun-worship was prominent in Assyrian religion, and Manasseh’s introduction of it was likely linked to his vassal status. Archaeological evidence from Tell Taanach includes cult stands with sun-disc imagery.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:15

The fulfilment of the 1 Kings 13 prophecy is the Deuteronomistic historian’s most dramatic demonstration that the prophetic word controls history. Three hundred years pass, a king is born with the right name, and the word is executed. History is the unfolding of what God has spoken.

Verses 21–37 — Passover Restored; “Still the LORD Did Not Turn”; Josiah Killed; Final Kings
2122232425262728293031323334353637

Context

Josiah celebrates a Passover greater than any since the judges — the narrator calls it unprecedented. The verdict seems to confirm his greatness: “Neither before nor after Josiah was there a king like him, who turned to the LORD with all his heart and soul and strength.” But then the devastating qualifier: “Nevertheless, the LORD did not turn away from the heat of his fierce anger... because of all that Manasseh had done.” Josiah’s faithfulness is real but the damage is irreversible. Then Pharaoh Necho marches north to support Assyria against Babylon; Josiah intercepts him at Megiddo and is killed. The “peace” promised by Huldah (22:20) is relative — he dies before the exile, not in it. Jehoahaz succeeds briefly before Egypt deports him and installs Jehoiakim.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 6:5“Love the LORD your God with all your heart, soul, and strength.” Josiah is the only king who fulfils the Shema completely — and it is still not enough.
  • 2 Chr 35:20–27Chronicles adds that Necho warned Josiah not to interfere, claiming divine commission. Josiah ignored the warning and was fatally wounded by archers.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
23:26

That the greatest reform cannot prevent judgment teaches the most important lesson in Kings: human obedience, however perfect, cannot atone for accumulated sin. Atonement requires something the law cannot provide. The OT narrative reaches its limit and points beyond itself.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
23:25

The triple formula “heart, soul, strength” (lēbāb, nephesh, mĕʾōd) directly quotes Deut 6:5, the Shema. Josiah is the only king to receive this commendation. The irony is crushing: the one king who perfectly embodies the covenant’s demand cannot save the nation from the covenant’s curse.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:29

Pharaoh Necho II (610–595 BC) was marching to support Assyria against the rising Babylonian-Median coalition at Carchemish. Josiah’s interception at Megiddo was likely an attempt to block Egyptian reinforcement of Assyria, aligning Judah with Babylon. The Battle of Megiddo (609 BC) is confirmed by Egyptian and Babylonian records.

23:33

Necho’s imposition of tribute (100 talents of silver, one talent of gold) and installation of Jehoiakim follows standard Egyptian imperial practice. Judah has traded Assyrian vassalage for Egyptian vassalage.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
23:26

The “nevertheless” after the superlative verdict on Josiah is the theological hinge of the entire Deuteronomistic History. It declares that the covenant framework — obey and be blessed — has been so comprehensively violated that no amount of subsequent obedience can restore the balance. The system itself requires something beyond itself.
