2 Kings 12

Joash Repairs the Temple

Verses 1–16 — Joash’s Reign; the Temple Repair Project
12345678910111213141516

Context

Joash does right “all the years Jehoiada the priest instructed him” — a qualification the narrator flags early: Joash’s faithfulness depends on his mentor’s presence. When the priests fail to use temple revenue for repairs, Joash establishes a new system: a chest at the temple gate where offerings are collected directly, with royal secretaries and the high priest counting the money together. Craftsmen are hired and the work proceeds with integrity — “they did not require an accounting from those to whom they gave the money, because they acted with complete honesty.”

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Kgs 22:3–7Josiah will later initiate an almost identical temple repair project. The pattern — temple decline under bad kings, restoration under reformers — repeats throughout Kings.
  • 2 Chr 24:17–22Chronicles adds devastating detail: after Jehoiada’s death, Joash abandoned the LORD and even had Jehoiada’s son Zechariah stoned — the priest’s grandson killing the priest’s son.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:4

The temple’s disrepair during periods of apostasy teaches that neglecting worship leads to the physical decay of worship’s infrastructure. Communities that abandon God eventually abandon his house.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:9

The “chest” (ʾārōn) literally means “box” or “ark” — a different word from the ark of the covenant (ʾārōn habbĕrît). The wordplay may be intentional: a new “ark” facilitates the restoration of the temple that houses the original ark.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:9

The collection chest drilled with a hole is one of the earliest attested examples of systematic charitable giving infrastructure. Similar temple collection mechanisms are described in Mesopotamian texts from the Neo-Babylonian period, though the Joash system predates them by several centuries.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:2

Joash’s dependent faith raises a crucial question: is faith that relies entirely on a mentor’s guidance genuine faith? The narrative suggests it is real but insufficient. Personal conviction must eventually replace external instruction.

Verses 17–21 — Hazael Threatens; Joash Assassinated
1718192021

Context

Hazael’s Aramean army advances toward Jerusalem. Joash buys him off with the temple’s sacred objects and the treasury gold — the very treasures he worked to restore. The irony is bitter: the temple repairer becomes the temple raider when threatened. His officials conspire and assassinate him — Chronicles (2 Chr 24:25) says this was vengeance for the blood of Jehoiada’s son Zechariah, whom Joash had murdered. The king who was saved as an infant in the temple dies at the hands of his own servants.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Chr 24:20–22Zechariah son of Jehoiada prophesies against Joash; Joash has him stoned in the temple court. Zechariah’s dying words: “May the LORD see this and call you to account.” He does.
  • Matt 23:35Jesus references “Zechariah son of Berekiah, whom you murdered between the temple and the altar.” Some scholars identify this with Jehoiada’s son.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
12:18

Joash’s capitulation teaches that faith untested by a mentor’s death is faith untested. When Jehoiada was alive, Joash served God; when Jehoiada died, Joash served himself. The mentor’s death revealed what was always missing: personal conviction.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
12:18

The phrase “all the sacred objects... and all the gold” uses the comprehensive formula that signals total depletion. Nothing is held back; the treasury is emptied.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:17

Hazael’s advance as far as Gath (a Philistine city west of Jerusalem) represents a dramatic Aramean expansion. Archaeological evidence from Tell es-Safi (Gath) confirms a major destruction layer from this period.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
12:18

The temple-raiding pattern — kings stripping the temple to pay foreign tribute — will recur throughout 2 Kings (16:8; 18:15; 24:13). Each episode represents a step toward the final catastrophe of chapter 25.
