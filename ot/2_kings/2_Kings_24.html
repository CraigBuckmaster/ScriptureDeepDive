2 Kings 24

Babylon Rises — Judah’s Final Kings

Verses 1–7 — Jehoiakim Rebels; Nebuchadnezzar Invades
1234567

Context

Jehoiakim serves Nebuchadnezzar for three years, then rebels. God sends raiding bands from Babylon, Aram, Moab, and Ammon — “in accordance with the word of the LORD proclaimed by his servants the prophets.” The narrator is explicit: “Surely these things happened to Judah according to the LORD’s command, in order to remove them from his presence because of the sins of Manasseh.” Manasseh is named one last time as the ultimate cause. The king of Egypt “did not march out from his own country again” — Egypt’s power is broken at Carchemish (605 BC). Judah has no ally left.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 25:1–11Jeremiah prophesied 70 years of exile beginning with Nebuchadnezzar’s first year. The timeline matches exactly.
  • Jer 36:1–32Jehoiakim burns Jeremiah’s scroll. The king who destroys the prophetic word is the king under whom the kingdom begins to fall.
  • Dan 1:1–6Daniel and his companions are taken to Babylon during Jehoiakim’s reign — the first deportees of Judah’s exile.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:4

The statement that “the LORD was not willing to forgive” is terrifying in its finality. It teaches that God’s patience, though vast, has limits. Those who presume on grace indefinitely discover that grace has a threshold beyond which judgment becomes irreversible.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
24:1

Nebuchadnezzar (Nĕbûkadreʾtstsar in Hebrew, Nabu-kudurri-usur in Akkadian) means “Nabu, protect the boundary stone.” His name appears over 90 times in the OT, reflecting his central role in Judah’s final decades.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:1

Nebuchadnezzar II (605–562 BC) became king of Babylon after defeating Egypt at the Battle of Carchemish in 605 BC. The Babylonian Chronicles confirm his campaign into Syria-Palestine in his first year, during which Jehoiakim submitted.

24:7

The Babylonian Chronicles record that in 601 BC Nebuchadnezzar fought Egypt to a draw, after which he returned to Babylon to refit his army. Jehoiakim’s rebellion may have been triggered by this perceived Babylonian weakness.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:3

The narrator’s insistence on Manasseh as the cause — even after Josiah’s reform — reveals the Deuteronomistic theology of accumulated guilt. Sin builds up like a national debt; occasional good kings make payments but cannot clear the balance.

Verses 8–20 — Jehoiachin Deposed; First Deportation; Zedekiah Installed
891011121314151617181920

Context

Jehoiachin reigns three months before Nebuchadnezzar besieges Jerusalem. Jehoiachin surrenders personally, and Nebuchadnezzar deports the king, the queen mother, the officials, the fighting men (10,000), and all the craftsmen and artisans. The temple treasures are cut up and taken. Only the poorest are left. Nebuchadnezzar installs Mattaniah (renamed Zedekiah) as puppet king. Among the deportees is the prophet Ezekiel (Ezek 1:1–3). The first deportation (597 BC) strips Judah of its leadership, its wealth, its military capacity, and its prophetic voice.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezek 1:1–3Ezekiel receives his call vision “among the exiles by the Kebar River.” He is among the 597 BC deportees — his entire prophetic ministry occurs in exile.
  • Jer 22:24–30Jeremiah prophesied that Jehoiachin (“Coniah”) would be cast off: “None of his offspring will prosper, none will sit on the throne of David.” Yet the book ends with Jehoiachin’s release (25:27–30).
  • Matt 1:11–12Matthew’s genealogy traces Jesus through Jehoiachin (“Jeconiah”). The deported king becomes an ancestor of the Messiah. Exile is not the end of the Davidic promise.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
24:13

The temple’s treasures carried to Babylon fulfil Isaiah’s prophecy from a century earlier. This teaches that God’s announced consequences, however delayed, are never cancelled. Time does not diminish the word’s authority.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
24:14

The number 10,000 deportees is the total; v.16 breaks this down as 7,000 “men of valour” plus 1,000 craftsmen and smiths. The discrepancy is likely due to different counting methods (family heads vs. military-age males).

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:12

The Babylonian Chronicles confirm the siege and capture of Jerusalem in 597 BC with remarkable precision: “In the seventh year [of Nebuchadnezzar], in the month of Kislev, the king of Akkad mustered his troops, and marched to the Hatti-land. He encamped against the city of Judah and on the second day of Adar he seized the city and captured the king.”

24:15

Cuneiform tablets discovered in Babylon (the “Jehoiachin’s Rations Tablets”) record oil and food rations allocated to “Yaukin, king of the land of Yahud” (Jehoiachin) and his five sons. This is extraordinary extra-biblical confirmation of the exiled king’s continued existence in Babylon.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
24:14

The removal of “all but the poorest” creates the social dynamic that will define post-exilic Judaism: the educated elite in Babylon preserving literary and theological traditions, the impoverished remnant in the land struggling to survive. The exile creates two Judaisms.
