2 Kings 9

Jehu’s Revolution — Blood at Jezreel

Verses 1–13 — Jehu Anointed King by a Prophetic Messenger
12345678910111213

Hebrew Word Study

mashach mashach — to anoint: Jehu is anointed (v.6) in a private, hurried ceremony — a young prophet pours oil, delivers the divine commission, and flees. Unlike Solomon’s public anointing at Gihon, this is a covert revolutionary act. The oil is the same; the context is war.

Context

Elisha sends a young prophet to Ramoth Gilead to anoint Jehu son of Nimshi as king of Israel with a specific commission: “You are to destroy the house of Ahab.” The anointing is furtive — the prophet bursts in, pours oil, delivers the message, and runs. Jehu’s fellow officers ask “What did that madman want?” When Jehu reveals the anointing, they instantly pledge loyalty, blowing trumpets on the bare steps: “Jehu is king!” The speed of the coup reveals how fragile the Omride dynasty has become.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 19:16At Horeb, God told Elijah to anoint Jehu. The commission has waited through Elijah’s entire ministry and Elisha’s — now it is finally executed.
  • 1 Kgs 21:21–24Elijah’s prophecy against Ahab’s house: “I will cut off every last male... Dogs will devour Jezebel.” Jehu is the instrument of fulfilment.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:7

God raises up Jehu as an instrument of judgment, not as a model of righteousness. The church must distinguish between God’s use of a person and God’s approval of that person.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:11

meshuggaʿ (“madman”) was sometimes applied to ecstatic prophets (cf. Jer 29:26; Hos 9:7). The term reflects both the intensity of prophetic behaviour and the cultural distance between prophets and military officers.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:1

Ramoth Gilead (Tell er-Rumeith or Tell Ramith in modern Jordan) was a strategic fortress on the Transjordanian frontier. Its contested status between Israel and Aram made it a natural staging ground for a military coup — far from the Omride capital at Samaria.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:11

The officers’ rapid acceptance of Jehu’s claim suggests deep dissatisfaction with the Omride dynasty. A regime change that requires no persuasion is a regime that has already lost legitimacy.

Verses 14–37 — Joram and Ahaziah Killed; Jezebel’s Death
141516171819202122232425262728293031323334353637

Hebrew Word Study

shalōm shalom — peace: Joram’s repeated question — “Is it peace, Jehu?” (vv.17, 19, 22) — receives the devastating answer: “What peace can there be, as long as the idolatry and witchcraft of your mother Jezebel abound?” Shalōm is impossible while the Omride religious program continues.

Context

Jehu drives his chariot furiously to Jezreel. King Joram sends two messengers; neither returns. Joram rides out himself with Ahaziah of Judah and meets Jehu at Naboth’s vineyard — the exact site of Ahab’s judicial murder (1 Kgs 21). The geographical precision is the narrator’s theological signature: judgment comes at the crime scene. Jehu kills Joram, and his body is thrown onto Naboth’s plot. Ahaziah flees but is fatally wounded. Then Jezebel — painted and defiant at the window — is thrown down by her own eunuchs. Dogs consume her body, fulfilling Elijah’s word exactly.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 21:19“In the place where dogs licked up Naboth’s blood, dogs will lick up your blood.” Fulfilled at Naboth’s vineyard.
  • 1 Kgs 21:23“Dogs will devour Jezebel by the wall of Jezreel.” Fulfilled in vv.35–36.
  • Hos 1:4Hosea declares that God will “punish the house of Jehu for the massacre at Jezreel.” Even divinely commissioned judgment can become excessive.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:30

Jezebel’s painted defiance teaches that impenitence can wear the mask of courage. She faces death without flinching but also without repenting. Hardness of heart can look like strength; it is in fact the ultimate weakness.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:20

“The driving is like that of Jehu son of Nimshi — he drives like a madman!” The phrase hammanhig kĕshiggaʿōn suggests reckless speed. Jehu’s chariot driving became proverbial.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:27

Ahaziah of Judah’s death at Megiddo (or Ibleam, the texts vary) draws the Judahite monarchy into the consequences of its Omride alliance. The marriage between the two royal houses means that judgment on one kingdom inevitably touches the other.

9:30

Jezebel’s cosmetic preparation before death is attested in ANE royal protocol — a queen presents herself in full regalia as a final assertion of status. Mesopotamian texts describe similar behaviour by royal women facing defeat.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:22

Jehu’s answer to Joram’s “Is it peace?” is the book’s thesis in one sentence: there is no peace while idolatry continues. Political stability requires theological fidelity — this is the Deuteronomistic conviction in its most concentrated form.

9:36

The narrator’s appeal to Elijah’s prophecy is the interpretive key: Jehu’s violence is not random but the execution of a prophetic sentence pronounced decades earlier. History is the unfolding of the prophetic word.
