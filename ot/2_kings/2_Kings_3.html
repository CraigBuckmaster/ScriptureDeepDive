2 Kings 3

The Campaign Against Moab

Verses 1–12 — Joram of Israel; the Three Kings March; Water Crisis
123456789101112

Hebrew Word Study

matztzevet habbaʿal matzevet-habaal — sacred stone of Baal: Joram removes Ahab’s matztzevet habbaʿal — the standing stone of Baal (v.2). But the Deuteronomistic historian immediately adds: “yet he clung to the sins of Jeroboam.” Removing Baal is not enough; the golden calves remain. Half-measures are no measures.

Context

After Ahab’s death, Moab rebels. Joram enlists Jehoshaphat and Edom for a joint campaign through the wilderness south of the Dead Sea. The army runs out of water after seven days. Joram blames YHWH; Jehoshaphat asks for a prophet. The contrast is sharp: Joram sees God as the problem, Jehoshaphat sees God as the solution — echoing the same dynamic in 1 Kings 22.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 22:7Jehoshaphat asks “Is there no longer a prophet of the LORD?” — the identical question he asks here. His faithfulness is consistent.
  • 2 Sam 8:2David conquered Moab. Moab’s rebellion shows how far Israel has fallen from Davidic power.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:10

When their own foolishness creates a crisis, the impenitent blame Providence. Those who refuse to seek God before the crisis have no right to accuse him during it.

3:11

Elisha’s willingness to help for Jehoshaphat’s sake demonstrates corporate solidarity — the faithful benefit the unfaithful by their presence.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:4

The Mesha Stele (Moabite Stone), discovered 1868 at Dhiban, confirms this chapter. King Mesha describes the same rebellion from Moab’s perspective: “Omri king of Israel oppressed Moab for many days.” One of the most important extra-biblical inscriptions.

3:9

The circuitous march around the Dead Sea — approximately 100 miles through the wilderness of Edom — was a standard strategy to avoid Moab’s heavily fortified northern border.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:2

Joram’s partial reform illustrates the northern kingdom’s persistent problem: even its best kings cannot break with the foundational sin of the divided monarchy.

3:11

Elisha’s response — “I would not even look at you” to Joram but “I have respect for Jehoshaphat” — shows that legitimate kingship matters.

Verses 13–27 — Elisha’s Prophecy; Water in the Valley; Mesha’s Child Sacrifice
131415161718192021222324252627

Hebrew Word Study

qetzef gadōl ketzef-gadol — great wrath/fury: After Mesha sacrifices his firstborn son on the wall, “great wrath” (qetzef gadōl) came against Israel (v.27). Whose wrath? The narrator leaves it deliberately ambiguous — the horror of child sacrifice produces a response so overwhelming that Israel withdraws despite military advantage.

Context

Elisha calls for a harpist; as the music plays, the hand of the LORD comes upon him and he prophesies: dig ditches and the valley will fill with water without rain. The prophecy is fulfilled at dawn. The Moabites, seeing sun reflected red in the water, mistake it for blood and charge into an ambush. Israel devastates Moab. But at the final siege, King Mesha sacrifices his firstborn son on the wall. “There came great wrath against Israel,” and they withdraw. The chapter ends with a theological shudder: the narrator does not explain the mechanism; he lets the horror speak for itself.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 17:6Moses struck the rock and water came forth — God provides water in the wilderness. Elisha’s miracle operates on the same pattern.
  • Lev 18:21“Do not give any of your children to be sacrificed to Molek.” Child sacrifice is the ultimate covenant violation.
  • 1 Sam 10:5Saul encountered prophets with harps, and the Spirit came upon him. Music and prophecy are associated throughout the OT.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:16

God commands digging before sending water — he works through means. Faith and effort are not opposed. We pray for rain and dig the channels.

3:27

The sacrifice reveals the demonic logic of idolatry at its extreme. The progression from superstition to human sacrifice is not a departure from idolatry but its natural conclusion.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:15

kĕnaggen hamĕnaggen — “as the musician played.” The “hand of the LORD” (yad-YHWH) is a standard expression for prophetic inspiration (cf. 1 Kgs 18:46; Ezek 1:3).

3:27

qetzef gadōl — “great wrath.” The subject is unstated, creating deliberate ambiguity. Proposals include divine anger, Chemosh’s anger (reported without endorsement), or Israel’s own revulsion.

Wiseman — TOTC 1–2 Kings

Donald J. Wiseman, 1 and 2 Kings, Tyndale OT Commentary (1993) — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:15

Music accompanying prophetic activity is well attested in the ANE. Egyptian texts describe temple musicians who induce ecstatic states. The biblical text distinguishes Israel’s prophecy by grounding it in the “hand of the LORD” — a personal divine action.

3:24

The Mesha Stele confirms the military details from Moab’s perspective. Mesha records: “Israel has perished forever” — a premature obituary. The stele also confirms Chemosh worship and herem warfare.

Provan — NIBCOT 1–2 Kings

Iain W. Provan, 1 and 2 Kings, New International Biblical Commentary (1995) — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:18

The phrase “this is an easy thing” redefines the narrative scale. The prophetic perspective constantly reframes human events in divine terms.

3:27

The narrator’s refusal to explain the “great wrath” is itself a theological statement. The withdrawal is not military defeat but moral collapse before unspeakable evil.
