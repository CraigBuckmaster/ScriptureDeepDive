1 Chronicles 29

Offerings for the Temple; David’s Prayer; Solomon Enthroned

Verses 1–9 — David and the Leaders Give Generously
123456789

Context

David gives from his personal treasury: 3,000 talents of gold and 7,000 talents of refined silver. Then he asks: “Who is willing to consecrate themselves to the LORD today?” The leaders respond with 5,000 talents of gold, 10,000 darics, 10,000 talents of silver, 18,000 talents of bronze, and 100,000 talents of iron. “The people rejoiced at the willing response of their leaders, for they had given freely and wholeheartedly to the LORD. David the king also rejoiced greatly.” The offering is voluntary, joyful, and extravagant.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 35:20–29The free-will offering for the tabernacle. The pattern repeats: God’s people give freely for the construction of God’s dwelling.
  • 2 Cor 9:7“God loves a cheerful giver.” The NT principle of joyful giving has its roots in this passage.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
29:9

Joy in giving teaches that true generosity is not sacrifice but delight. When giving feels like loss, something is wrong in the heart. When it feels like worship, the heart is right.

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
29:7

darkĕmōnîm — Persian darics, first minted under Darius I (c.515 BC). Their appearance in a Davidic-era narrative is the most commonly cited anachronism in Chronicles, confirming post-exilic composition.

Verses 10–30 — David’s Doxology; Solomon Enthroned; David Dies
101112131415161718192021222324252627282930

Hebrew Word Study

lĕkā YHWH haggĕdullāh lekha-YHWH-haggedullah — Yours, O LORD, is the greatness: David’s doxology (v.11): “Yours, O LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory and the majesty and the splendour.” Five divine attributes ascribed in a single sentence. This becomes one of the most quoted prayers in Jewish and Christian liturgy — Jesus’ Lord’s Prayer doxology (“for yours is the kingdom and the power and the glory”) echoes it directly.

Context

David’s prayer before the whole assembly is the theological climax of 1 Chronicles: “Yours, O LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory and the majesty and the splendour, for everything in heaven and earth is yours.” He acknowledges that the offerings came from God first: “Everything comes from you, and we have given you only what comes from your hand.” Then Solomon is enthroned “a second time” (the first was 23:1), acknowledged by all leaders, and prospers. David dies “at a good old age, having enjoyed long life, wealth and honour.” The book ends not with exile but with worship, generosity, and orderly succession.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 6:13The Lord’s Prayer doxology: “For yours is the kingdom and the power and the glory forever.” A direct echo of David’s prayer.
  • 1 Kgs 2:10–12David’s death in Kings is brief and political. The Chronicler’s version is devotional and liturgical.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
29:11

“Yours is the greatness” teaches that all praise is recognition, not creation. We do not make God great by praising him; we acknowledge the greatness that already is. Worship is the perception of reality.

29:14

“We have given you only what comes from your hand” is the death of all religious pride. If everything we offer to God was first given by God, then our generosity is simply God’s generosity returning to its source. We are channels, not origins.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
29:11

This verse is the source of the traditional doxology appended to the Lord’s Prayer in Matt 6:13 (in many manuscripts): “for yours is the kingdom and the power and the glory.” The verbal parallels are unmistakable.

29:22

“A second time” (shēnît) refers to a second anointing ceremony — the first was the private/emergency anointing in 23:1; this is the public, full ceremony.
