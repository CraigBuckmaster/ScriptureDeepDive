1 Chronicles 20

More Wars; Philistine Giants

Verses 1–3 — Capture of Rabbah; the Great Omission
123

Context

“In the spring, at the time when kings go off to war, Joab led out the armed forces.” In Samuel, this verse (2 Sam 11:1) launches the Bathsheba-Uriah narrative. The Chronicler uses the same opening but skips to the fall of Rabbah. 2 Samuel 11–12 — adultery, murder, Nathan’s confrontation, the dead child — is completely absent. David takes Rabbah’s crown and puts the Ammonites to forced labour. The Chronicler’s David never sins with Bathsheba, never murders Uriah, never hears “You are the man!” This is the most significant omission in the entire book.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Sam 11:1–12:25The Bathsheba affair — entirely absent from Chronicles. The Chronicler’s David is the David of worship and temple, not the David of sin and repentance.
  • 2 Sam 12:26–31The capture of Rabbah — which the Chronicler preserves. Only the military conclusion is retained; the moral crisis is erased.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:1

The Chronicler’s silence on Bathsheba reminds us that the same event can serve different theological purposes. Samuel uses David’s sin to teach about repentance; Chronicles uses David’s worship to teach about proper liturgy. Both are true; both are selective.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:1

The transition from “Joab led out the armed forces” directly to “Joab attacked Rabbah” compresses an entire year of narrative (2 Sam 11:1–12:26) into a single verse break.

Verses 4–8 — Three Philistine Giants Killed
45678

Context

Three Philistine warriors of extraordinary size are killed by David’s men: Sibbecai kills Sippai at Gezer; Elhanan kills Lahmi brother of Goliath (in Samuel: Elhanan kills Goliath himself — a famous textual problem); Jonathan son of Shimea kills a six-fingered giant at Gath. The Chronicler closes: “These were descendants of the giants in Gath, and they fell at the hands of David and his men.” Brief, martial, and uncomplicated.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Sam 21:18–22The parallel. The Elhanan/Goliath discrepancy is one of the most discussed textual problems in the OT.
  • 1 Sam 17:4–51David kills Goliath. The Chronicler omits this famous story entirely but preserves these lesser-known giant-killings.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:8

That giants fell “at the hands of David and his men” teaches that God uses ordinary soldiers to accomplish extraordinary victories. Not every giant requires a David; sometimes a Sibbecai will do.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:5

The Hebrew of “Lahmi brother of Goliath” (ʾet-lahmî ʾahî golyat) may be a secondary reading derived from misreading bet-hallāhmî (“the Bethlehemite”) in the Samuel parallel.
