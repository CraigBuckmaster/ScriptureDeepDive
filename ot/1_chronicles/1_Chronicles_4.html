1 Chronicles 4

Judah and Simeon — Jabez’s Prayer

Verses 1–23 — More Judahite Clans; Jabez’s Prayer
1234567891011121314151617181920212223

Hebrew Word Study

yaʿbēts yaabets — he causes pain: Jabez’s name means “pain” — his mother named him for her difficult labour (v.9). His prayer asks God to reverse the meaning of his name: expand my territory, be with me, keep me from harm so I cause no pain. God grants it. Buried in a genealogy, this two-verse prayer became one of the most famous passages in Chronicles.

Context

The Judahite genealogies continue with additional clans, including the famous Jabez prayer (vv.9–10). In the middle of a list of names, the narrator pauses for two verses: Jabez prays, and God grants his request. Then the genealogy resumes as if nothing happened. The effect is striking — a flash of personal devotion in an institutional document. The Chronicler includes it because it illustrates his central theme: those who seek God find him. The genealogy then moves to Simeon’s tribe, with territorial notes about expansion into Edomite territory.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 35:18Rachel named her dying son Ben-Oni (“son of my trouble”); Jacob renamed him Benjamin (“son of my right hand”). Jabez’s prayer similarly seeks to overcome a name of pain.
  • Matt 7:7“Ask and it will be given to you.” Jabez asks; God gives. The pattern is simple and universal.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:10

Jabez’s prayer teaches that God is accessible to anyone who calls upon him. No special status, no priestly mediation — a man whose very name means “pain” receives God’s full blessing through simple, direct prayer.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:9

The name yaʿbēts is related to ʿōtseb/ʿetseb (“pain, sorrow”). The wordplay: “she named him Jabez (Pain), saying, ‘I gave birth to him in pain.’” His prayer asks God to overturn the destiny encoded in his name.

Verses 24–43 — Simeon’s Descendants and Territorial Expansion

Context

Simeon’s genealogy is brief compared to Judah’s, reflecting the tribe’s small size and eventual absorption into Judah. The Chronicler notes that Simeonites expanded into Gedor and Mount Seir during Hezekiah’s reign, dispossessing the Hamites and Amalekites. This territorial note connects the genealogy to lived history: names map onto land, and land claims persist across generations.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 49:5–7Jacob’s prophecy: Simeon and Levi will be scattered in Israel. Simeon’s absorption into Judah fulfils this prediction.
  • Josh 19:1–9Simeon’s allotment was within Judah’s territory — the smallest tribal inheritance.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:43

“To this day” links the past to the present. The Chronicler writes not as an antiquarian but as a witness: these genealogies describe people who are still living in these places. History is not over.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:41

The “Hamites” (hammĕʿûnîm or hammāʿōnîm) living in Gedor are otherwise unknown. The text may be corrupt; some LXX manuscripts read “Gerar” instead of “Gedor,” which would place the expansion in the Negev.
