Lamentations 5

Remember, LORD, What Has Happened to Us

Verses 1–10 — “Our Inheritance Has Been Turned Over to Strangers”
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

זְכֹר zək̅ōr — remember: "Remember (zək̅ōr), LORD, what has happened to us; look, and see our disgrace" (v.1). The ONLY chapter that is NOT an acrostic — though it has 22 verses (matching the Hebrew alphabet count). The form is deliberately broken: when grief is total, even poetic structure fails. The prayer is communal: our inheritance given to aliens, our houses to foreigners (v.2). We are orphans; our mothers are widows (v.3). We buy our own water; our own wood costs us (v.4). We are weary and find no rest (v.5). We submitted to Egypt and Assyria to get enough bread (v.6). Our ancestors sinned; we bear their punishment (v.7). Slaves rule over us (v.8). We get our bread at risk of our lives (v.9). Our skin is hot as an oven from the burning heat of famine (v.10).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1

"Remember, LORD, what has happened to us." The first word is the prayer’s heart: remember. Not "fix" or "avenge" but "see." The community asks to be noticed.

5:2–3

"Our inheritance has been turned over to strangers. We have become fatherless, our mothers are widows." Every social structure dismantled: property, family, identity.

5:5–7

"Those who pursue us are at our heels; we are weary and find no rest. Our ancestors sinned and are no more, and we bear their punishment." The intergenerational burden: they sinned; we suffer. The question of theodicy across generations.

5:10

"Our skin is hot as an oven, from the burning heat of famine." The body as furnace. Famine produces internal fire. The physical detail is medical: protein starvation causes skin inflammation.

Verses 11–22 — “Restore Us, and We Will Return”
111213141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

חַדֵּשׁ ḥaddēsh — renew / restore: "Restore us (ḥaddēsh) to yourself, LORD, and we will return; renew our days as of old" (v.21). Women are violated in Zion; princes hung by their hands (vv.11–12). Young men grind at the mill; boys stagger under loads of wood (v.13). The elders are gone from the city gate; the young men have stopped their music (v.14). Joy is gone; dancing turned to mourning (v.15). "The crown has fallen from our head. Woe to us, for we have sinned!" (v.16). "Mount Zion lies desolate, with jackals prowling over it" (v.18). Then the final prayer: "You, LORD, reign forever; your throne endures from generation to generation" (v.19). "Why do you always forget us? Why do you forsake us so long?" (v.20). "Restore us to yourself, LORD, and we will return; renew our days as of old" (v.21). And the book’s devastating final line: "Unless you have utterly rejected us and are angry with us beyond measure" (v.22). The book ends with an unanswered question. No resolution. No comfort. Jewish liturgical tradition repeats v.21 after v.22 so the book doesn’t end on despair. But the text itself ends in darkness.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:15–16

"Joy is gone from our hearts; our dancing has turned to mourning. The crown has fallen from our head. Woe to us, for we have sinned!" Joy → mourning. Crown → fallen. The reversal is total. And then the confession: "we have sinned."

5:19–20

"You, LORD, reign forever; your throne endures from generation to generation. Why do you always forget us?" God’s permanence alongside human experience of abandonment. You reign forever — so why do you forget?

5:21

"Restore us to yourself, LORD, and we will return; renew our days as of old." The prayer of return. We cannot return on our own; you must restore us TO yourself. The initiative must be divine.

5:22

"Unless you have utterly rejected us and are angry with us beyond measure." The final word. No answer. The book hangs in the air. The rabbis added the custom of repeating v.21 so the reading doesn’t end in despair. But the text allows the silence to stand.
