Lamentations 3

His Compassions Never Fail

Verses 1–33 — “Great Is Your Faithfulness”

Hebrew Word Study

חֲדָשִׁים ḥădāshîm — new (pl.) / renewed: "His compassions never fail. They are new (ḥădāshîm) every morning; great is your faithfulness" (vv.22–23). THE centre of Lamentations and its only moment of sustained hope. The chapter is a TRIPLE acrostic (3 verses per Hebrew letter = 66 verses). It begins in darkness: "I am the man who has seen affliction by the rod of the LORD’s wrath" (v.1). Driven into darkness (v.2), flesh wasted (v.4), walled in (v.7), shut out from prayer (v.8). Then v.21: "Yet this I call to mind, and therefore I have hope." The turn is volitional — he CALLS to mind. Hope is a decision, not a feeling. vv.22–23 follow: compassions new every morning. Then the declaration: "The LORD is my portion; therefore I will wait for him" (v.24).

Context

The triple acrostic (each Hebrew letter governs three verses) makes this the most formally elaborate poem in the OT. The first 20 verses are unrelieved suffering; vv.21–33 are the hope-centre; the remainder returns to lament. The hope does NOT replace the grief but exists INSIDE it. Thomas Obadiah Chisholm wrote the hymn "Great Is Thy Faithfulness" (1923) from vv.22–23, making this the most musically influential passage in Lamentations. The "I am the man" (v.1) voice is individual, not corporate — one sufferer speaking for all.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 30:5Weeping may stay for the night, but rejoicing comes in the morning — the same morning-renewal theology as Lam 3:23.
  • Rom 8:28In all things God works for the good of those who love him — the NT version of Lam 3:25–26 (waiting for God’s salvation).
  • Heb 10:23He who promised is faithful — the same faithfulness (ʾĕmūnâ) that Lam 3:23 celebrates.
  • Ps 73:26God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever — the psalm equivalent of Lam 3:24.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1–6

"I am the man who has seen affliction." The individual voice: one man’s suffering representing all. Driven into darkness, flesh wasted, walled in.

3:7–9

"He has walled me in; he has weighed me down with chains. Even when I call or cry, he shuts out my prayer." God blocking prayer is the deepest darkness: when the escape-route itself is closed.

3:21–24

"Yet this I call to mind, and therefore I have hope." The TURN. Volitional: he DECIDES to remember. "His compassions never fail. New every morning. Great is your faithfulness. The LORD is my portion."

3:25–33

"The LORD is good to those whose hope is in him. It is good to wait quietly for the salvation of the LORD." Waiting as active faith. Bearing the yoke in youth. Sitting alone in silence when God imposes it.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:21–24

Calvin calls these the most important verses in the OT for sufferers: the decision to hope is made INSIDE the darkness, not after it lifts. Hope is not the absence of grief but its companion.

3:25–33

Calvin reads the "waiting quietly" as the hardest spiritual discipline: not passive resignation but active trust when every circumstance contradicts the promise. To wait for God when God seems to have abandoned you is the ultimate act of faith.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:22–23

The NET notes that chesed (covenant love) and rachamim (compassions/womb-mercies) are paired here: God’s committed loyalty and his tender mercy together constitute the ground of hope. Neither alone would suffice.

3:24

The NET observes "the LORD is my portion" echoes the Levitical inheritance tradition (Num 18:20; Ps 16:5): the Levites received no land because God himself was their inheritance. The sufferer claims a priestly identity.

Berlin Notes

berlin
3:1–20

Berlin notes the first 20 verses systematically demolish every possible ground of hope: health, freedom, prayer, memory, peace. The demolition is deliberate — it creates a void that only vv.22–23 can fill.

3:22–23

Berlin reads "new every morning" as a counter to the night-weeping of 1:2. If grief belongs to the night, mercy belongs to the morning. The daily renewal matches the daily destruction: grace is as persistent as suffering.

Oconnor Notes

oconnor
3:21–24

O’Connor reads the hope-passage as a survival strategy, not a theological conclusion: the sufferer chooses to remember mercy because the alternative is annihilation. Hope here is not optimism but the refusal to die.

3:25–33

O’Connor notes the ‘waiting quietly’ is not passivity but the patience of someone who has no other option. The postures — sitting alone, putting mouth in the dust, offering the cheek to the striker — are embodied submission. Jesus enacts these in the Passion (cf. Isa 50:6).

Verses 34–66 — “Let Us Examine Our Ways and Return to the LORD”

Hebrew Word Study

נַחְפְשָה naḥpəsâ — let us examine: "Let us examine (naḥpəsâ) our ways and test them, and let us return to the LORD. Let us lift up our hearts and our hands to God in heaven" (vv.40–41). The individual voice becomes communal: "let US." But confession meets silence: "We have sinned and rebelled, and you have not forgiven" (v.42). Fear and pit and snare (v.47). "Streams of tears flow from my eyes because my people are destroyed" (v.48). God hunted them like a bird (v.52). From the depths of the pit: "You came near when I called you; you said, Do not fear" (v.57). The chapter ends petitioning God to repay the enemies (vv.64–66).

Context

The second half of chapter 3 moves through three movements: communal confession (vv.40–42), resumed individual lament (vv.43–54), and recalled deliverance (vv.55–58) followed by a plea for justice (vv.59–66). The admission "you have not forgiven" (v.42) is extraordinary — it comes AFTER the hope of vv.22–23. Hope does not guarantee immediate resolution. The closing imprecation (vv.64–66) may trouble modern readers, but it channels rage toward God rather than toward vigilante action. The prayer for vengeance IS the restraint: it trusts God to act rather than acting oneself.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Chr 7:14If my people humble themselves, pray, seek my face, and turn from their wicked ways — the pattern Lam 3:40–41 follows.
  • Ps 130:1–2Out of the depths I cry to you, LORD — the same "pit" prayer as Lam 3:55.
  • Rom 12:19Do not take revenge; leave room for God’s wrath — the NT version of Lam 3:64–66’s imprecation: let GOD repay.
  • 2 Cor 4:8–9Hard pressed but not crushed; persecuted but not abandoned — the Pauline echo of Lam 3’s survival-through-suffering.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:40–42

"Let us examine our ways and return to the LORD." The communal call. But: "We have sinned and you have NOT FORGIVEN." The unforgiveness is stated without bitterness, as fact.

3:48–51

"Streams of tears flow. My eyes will flow unceasingly, without relief, until the LORD looks down from heaven and sees." The tears are presented as evidence: look at these, God.

3:55–58

"I called your name from the depths of the pit. You came near. You said, Do not fear." A past rescue remembered. Even in the pit, God once spoke. The memory sustains.

3:64–66

"Pay them back for what they have done. Put a veil over their hearts." The imprecation: hand the enemies to God. Trust his justice rather than enacting your own.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:40–42

Calvin notes the honest juxtaposition: "let us return" (v.40) then "you have not forgiven" (v.42). Repentance does not guarantee immediate relief. Calvin calls this the mature faith that persists beyond felt forgiveness.

3:55–58

Calvin reads the remembered deliverance as the bridge between past grace and future hope: God spoke "Do not fear" once; he can speak it again. Memory of past faithfulness is fuel for present endurance.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:42

The NET notes the shocking frankness: "you have not forgiven." The Hebrew lo salahta is a direct statement of God’s ongoing wrath. The poet does not sugarcoat the situation even after the hope of vv.22–23.

3:55–58

The NET observes a shift to past tense: "you came near," "you said." This is remembered deliverance, not current experience. The gap between past rescue and present suffering is the space of faith.

Berlin Notes

berlin
3:40–42

Berlin reads the communal shift ("let US") as the poem’s attempt to move from individual to collective processing. Grief shared is grief survived.

3:55–58

Berlin notes the "Do not fear" is the only reported divine speech in all five poems. God speaks exactly once — and what he says is: do not be afraid. The rarity makes the word precious.

Oconnor Notes

oconnor
3:42

O’Connor reads ‘you have not forgiven’ as the most courageous sentence in the book: after confessing sin, after hoping in mercy, the poet reports that forgiveness has not arrived. The honesty is brutal and essential.

3:55–66

O’Connor notes the chapter ends with imprecation, not resolution. The sufferer hands the enemy to God’s judgment. This is not vengeful but realistic: someone must answer for what happened. Entrusting that answer to God is an act of trust, not hate.

Ancient Sources

Sumerian City Laments (c.2000 BC)
The Lamentation over the Destruction of Ur and similar Sumerian texts follow a comparable pattern: the deity abandons the city, enemies destroy it, the population mourns, and a tentative plea for restoration concludes.
Lamentations participates in an ancient Near Eastern genre of city-lament that predates it by over a millennium. The genre validates public grief as a literary and theological act.
The Babylonian Chronicles
Cuneiform records document Nebuchadnezzar’s siege and capture of Jerusalem in 597 and 586 BC, confirming the historical catastrophe behind Lamentations.
The archaeological evidence corroborates the destruction described in the poems, including evidence of massive burning in the City of David excavations.
