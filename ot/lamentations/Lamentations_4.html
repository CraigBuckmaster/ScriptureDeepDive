Lamentations 4

The Gold Has Lost Its Lustre

Verses 1–11 — “The Sacred Gems Are Scattered”
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

זָהָב zāhāb — gold: "How the gold (zāhāb) has lost its lustre, the fine gold become dull! The sacred gems are scattered at every street corner" (v.1). The siege’s physical horrors. Precious children, once valued as gold, are now treated like cheap clay pots (v.2). Even jackals nurse their young, but "my people have become heartless like ostriches in the desert" (v.3). Infants’ tongues stick to the roof of their mouths from thirst (v.4). Those who ate delicacies are destitute; those raised in purple now lie on ash heaps (v.5). The punishment is worse than Sodom’s, which was destroyed in a moment without hands (v.6). The princes’ bodies once whiter than milk are now blacker than soot (vv.7–8). "Those killed by the sword are better off than those who die of famine" (v.9).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1–2

"How the gold has lost its lustre! The precious children of Zion, once worth their weight in gold, are now considered as pots of clay." Human beings as devalued currency. The metaphor is economic: people’s worth has collapsed.

4:4–5

"The infant’s tongue sticks to the roof of its mouth from thirst. Those who once ate delicacies are destitute in the streets." Specific physical details: thirst, hunger, reversal of fortune. The siege strips every comfort.

4:6

"The punishment of my people is greater than that of Sodom, which was overthrown in a moment without a hand turned to help her." Sodom died instantly; Jerusalem dies slowly. Quick destruction is mercy compared to prolonged siege.

4:9–10

"Those killed by the sword are better off than those who die of famine. Compassionate women have cooked their own children." Famine worse than battle. Mothers as cannibals. The extremity of siege warfare documented without flinching.

Verses 12–22 — “The LORD’s Anger Will Not Last Forever”
1213141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

תַּם tam — complete / finished / spent: "Your punishment will end (tam), Daughter Zion; he will not prolong your exile" (v.22). The world didn’t believe Jerusalem could fall — neither kings nor enemies thought its gates could be breached (v.12). The cause: the sins of prophets and priests, who shed innocent blood (v.13). They wander blind in the streets (v.14). The anointed one (the king) is captured (v.20) — "the LORD’s anointed, caught in their traps, he of whom we said, ‘Under his shadow we will live among the nations.’" The chapter closes with a promise: Zion’s punishment will end (v.22). Edom’s will begin (v.22). There IS a terminus to the suffering.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:12–13

"The kings of the earth did not believe that enemies could enter the gates of Jerusalem. But it happened because of the sins of her prophets, who shed the blood of the righteous." The world thought Jerusalem was invincible. The prophets’ sins proved otherwise.

4:17

"Our eyes failed, looking in vain for help; from our towers we watched for a nation that could not save us." Waiting for Egypt’s help that never came. The failure of political alliances when covenant faithfulness is broken.

4:20

"The LORD’s anointed, our very life breath, was caught in their traps." The king as God’s anointed, captured. The hope of the nation, trapped. Messianic overtones: the anointed one suffers.

4:22

"Your punishment will end, Daughter Zion; he will not prolong your exile." The chapter of unrelenting horror ends with a one-verse promise: there is a limit. The exile has an end date.
