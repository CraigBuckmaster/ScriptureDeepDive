Lamentations 2

The Lord Has Swallowed Up Israel

Verses 1–10 — “The Lord Is Like an Enemy”
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

כְּאוֹיֵב kəʾōyēb — like an enemy: "The Lord is like an enemy (kəʾōyēb); he has swallowed up Israel" (v.5). The most theologically shocking chapter. God is not the defender but the attacker. He has hurled down Israel’s splendour (v.1), destroyed without pity (v.2), burned like a flaming fire (v.3), bent his bow like a foe (v.4), swallowed up all her palaces (v.5), laid waste his dwelling (v.6), rejected his altar (v.7), planned to destroy the wall of Daughter Zion (v.8). Her gates have sunk into the ground; her king and princes are in exile among the nations; the law is no more; her prophets find no visions from the LORD (v.9). The elders sit on the ground in silence; young women bow their heads (v.10).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1–3

"How the Lord has covered Daughter Zion with the cloud of his anger! He has hurled down the splendour of Israel. He has burned in Jacob like a flaming fire." God’s anger is the subject of nearly every verse. This is not pagan-god caprice; it is covenant-wrath against covenant-breach.

2:5–6

"The Lord is like an enemy. He has destroyed her strongholds. He has laid waste his dwelling like a garden." God destroys his OWN house. The temple he commanded Solomon to build, he demolishes. The garden metaphor: a garden torn up by its own gardener.

2:9

"Her gates have sunk into the ground. The law is no more, and her prophets no longer find visions from the LORD." Triple loss: infrastructure (gates), instruction (Torah), revelation (prophecy). The city is not just damaged but disconnected from God.

2:10

"The elders of Daughter Zion sit on the ground in silence; they have sprinkled dust on their heads. The young women bow their heads to the ground." Silence. Dust. Bowed heads. The body language of total defeat.

Verses 11–22 — “Pour Out Your Heart Like Water”
111213141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

שִׁפְכִי shifkî — pour out: "Pour out (shifkî) your heart like water in the presence of the Lord. Lift up your hands to him for the lives of your children, who faint from hunger at every street corner" (v.19). The narrator’s own grief: "My eyes fail from weeping, I am in torment within; my heart is poured out on the ground because my people are destroyed" (v.11). Children and infants faint in the streets (v.11). Mothers eat their own children (v.20). "Should women eat their offspring, the children they have cared for?" (v.20). The prophets gave false visions (v.14). The passers-by clap their hands and hiss (v.15). The chapter ends addressing God directly: "Look, LORD, and consider: whom have you ever treated like this?" (v.20).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:11–12

"My eyes fail from weeping. Children and infants faint in the streets of the city." The narrator enters. His tears match the city’s. Children dying in public — the most visceral image.

2:14

"The visions of your prophets were false and worthless; they did not expose your sin to ward off your captivity." False prophecy’s cost: the prophets who said "peace" when there was no peace are now exposed.

2:19

"Pour out your heart like water in the presence of the Lord. Lift up your hands to him for the lives of your children." The only instruction in the chapter: pour out your heart. The metaphor is liquid: grief as water, unstoppable, formless, released.

2:20

"Should women eat their offspring, the children they have cared for?" The ultimate horror: cannibalism from starvation. The question is addressed to God. Not rhetorical — accusatory. Should this happen under your watch?
