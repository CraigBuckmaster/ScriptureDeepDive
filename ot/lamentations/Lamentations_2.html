Lamentations 2

The LORD Is Like an Enemy

Verses 1–10 — “He Has Swallowed Up Israel”
12345678910

Hebrew Word Study

יָעִיב āʿib — enemy: "The Lord is like an enemy (āʿib); he has swallowed up Israel" (v.5). The most theologically shocking statement in the book. God himself has destroyed: his dwelling (v.2), his altar (v.7), his temple (v.7), his festival and Sabbath (v.6). The king and priest are rejected (v.6). The wall weeps (v.8). The gates sink into the ground (v.9). The elders sit on the ground in silence, dust on their heads; the young women bow down (v.10).

Context

Chapter 2 escalates from chapter 1: there, God was the acknowledged cause; here, God is described AS the enemy. The poet uses language normally reserved for foreign invaders (swallowed, destroyed, demolished) but applies it to YHWH. The destruction of the temple (v.7) is the theological catastrophe — the place where God dwelt is the place God destroyed. The silence of v.10 (elders sit wordless, dust-covered) is the appropriate human response: when God acts as enemy, words fail.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 21:5–6I myself will fight against you with an outstretched hand — Jeremiah’s warning that God would fight against Jerusalem, which Lamentations 2 describes as fulfilled.
  • Ps 74:4–8Your foes roared in the place where you met with us. They burned your sanctuary to the ground — the same temple-destruction grief.
  • Isa 63:10They rebelled and grieved his Holy Spirit, so he turned and became their enemy — Isaiah’s version of Lam 2’s theology.
  • Matt 27:51The temple curtain was torn in two — the NT’s temple-destruction, this time enacted by God for redemption, not judgment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1–3

"How the Lord has covered Daughter Zion with the cloud of his anger!" Three verses of relentless divine destruction. God is the subject of every verb.

2:5–6

"The Lord is like an enemy." God has destroyed his own dwelling, his own altar, his own Sabbaths. This is not pagan attack but self-demolition.

2:7–8

"The LORD has rejected his altar and abandoned his sanctuary. He has given the walls of her palaces into the hands of the enemy." God gave the walls away. The enemy received what God handed over.

2:10

Silence. Elders sit on the ground. Dust on their heads. Young women bow their heads to the ground. When the shock is total, the body speaks what the mouth cannot.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:5–6

Calvin calls this the hardest theology in the OT: God as enemy of his own people, destroyer of his own house. Calvin insists this must be held without softening: if God can destroy the temple, no human institution is safe from his judgment.

2:10

Calvin reads the silence as worship — not the worship of songs but the worship of submission. When God acts beyond comprehension, the appropriate response is to sit in the dust and wait.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:5

The NET notes that "The Lord is LIKE an enemy" uses the comparison particle kə — God is not literally the enemy but acts AS IF he were. The distinction is theologically crucial: the enemy-behaviour is temporary; the covenant identity remains.

2:7

The NET observes that God "rejected his altar and abandoned his sanctuary" uses the same verbs elsewhere applied to divorced wives (cf. Isa 54:6). The temple’s destruction is a divorce metaphor.

Berlin Notes

berlin
2:1–8

Berlin counts the divine actions: in 8 verses, God is the subject of over 40 destructive verbs. The relentlessness is the point — the poet refuses to let the reader escape the full weight of divine agency in the disaster.

2:5

Berlin notes that "like an enemy" is the poem’s most dangerous sentence. To compare God to Israel’s enemies is to risk blasphemy — but the poet considers the risk worth the honesty.

Oconnor Notes

oconnor
2:1–8

O’Connor reads this as trauma theology: the TRUSTED protector has become the attacker. This is the structure of betrayal-trauma, and the poem gives it full voice without resolution.

2:10

O’Connor calls the silence of v.10 the poem’s most powerful verse: dust, ground, bowed heads. The body processes what language cannot articulate. Silence here is not emptiness but overwhelmed fullness.

Verses 11–22 — “Pour Out Your Heart Like Water”
111213141516171819202122

Hebrew Word Study

שִׁפְכִי shifkî — pour out: "Pour out (shifkî) your heart like water in the presence of the Lord" (v.19). The poet breaks down: "My eyes fail from weeping, I am in torment within" (v.11). Children faint in the streets, asking for food (v.12). The question: "What can I compare you to, Daughter Jerusalem? Who can heal you? Your wound is as deep as the sea" (v.13). The prophets gave false visions (v.14). Then the command: lift your hands to him for the lives of your children who faint with hunger at every street corner (v.19). The chapter ends with accusation: "On the day of the LORD’s anger no one escaped or survived" (v.22).

Context

Verse 13 declares the wound "deep as the sea" — infinite, immeasurable. The false prophets of v.14 are blamed for the catastrophe: they gave "worthless and misleading" oracles instead of exposing sin. Verse 19 contains the book’s only instruction: POUR OUT YOUR HEART. This is the single imperative in five chapters of grief. It does not say "understand" or "accept" or "move on." It says POUR. The image is liquid — grief as water, released before God. The chapter ends not with hope but with accusation (v.22): God invited enemies to a feast-day of slaughter.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 14:14The prophets are prophesying lies in my name — the false prophecy that Lam 2:14 blames for the disaster.
  • Ps 62:8Trust in him at all times; pour out your hearts to him — the psalm version of Lam 2:19’s command.
  • Joel 2:12–13Return to me with all your heart, with fasting and weeping — the prophetic call that matches Lam 2:19’s imperative.
  • Matt 26:38–39My soul is overwhelmed with sorrow. He fell with his face to the ground and prayed — Jesus in Gethsemane enacts Lam 2:19.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:11–12

"My eyes fail from weeping. Children faint in the streets." The physical detail: the poet’s body breaking; children dying publicly. Grief is not abstract here.

2:13

"Your wound is as deep as the sea. Who can heal you?" The wound’s depth is oceanic. The question expects no human answer. Only God can heal what God has wounded.

2:14

"The visions of your prophets were false and worthless. They did not expose your sin." The prophets are indicted: they told you what you wanted to hear. The false comfort is worse than no comfort.

2:19

"Pour out your heart like water before the Lord." The book’s only imperative. Not: understand. Not: accept. POUR. The instruction is to empty yourself before God.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:13

Calvin notes the sea-wound metaphor: the sea has no bottom that human plumbing can reach. Only God, who made the sea, can fathom and heal a sea-deep wound.

2:19

Calvin calls this the most important verse in Lamentations: when everything else has failed, the one remaining act is to pour out the heart before God. Prayer is not the last resort; it is the only resort that was never exhausted.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:14

The NET observes that the prophets’ failure was specific: they "did not expose your sin to ward off your captivity." True prophecy confronts; false prophecy comforts. The distinction is the difference between exile and survival.

2:19

The NET notes the three-part instruction: (1) arise, (2) cry out in the night, (3) pour out your heart like water. The sequence moves from physical posture to vocal expression to emotional release.

Berlin Notes

berlin
2:13

Berlin reads "who can heal you?" as a question the poet genuinely cannot answer. The book will not answer it until 3:22–23. The gap between the question and the answer spans an entire chapter.

2:19

Berlin emphasises that "like water" means irreversibly — water poured cannot be gathered back. The grief released before God is not recoverable. The act of pouring is total self-emptying.

Oconnor Notes

oconnor
2:11–12

O’Connor reads the children’s suffering as the poem’s moral crisis point: the innocent die. Whatever theological framework explains the adults’ suffering cannot explain the children’s. The poem does not resolve this.

2:19

O’Connor calls ‘pour out your heart like water’ the poem’s invitation to radical honesty: not polite prayer but the complete discharge of grief, anger, and accusation before God. This is liturgical permission to be devastated.

Ancient Sources

Sumerian City Laments (c.2000 BC)
The Lamentation over the Destruction of Ur and similar Sumerian texts follow a comparable pattern: the deity abandons the city, enemies destroy it, the population mourns, and a tentative plea for restoration concludes.
Lamentations participates in an ancient Near Eastern genre of city-lament that predates it by over a millennium. The genre validates public grief as a literary and theological act.
The Babylonian Chronicles
Cuneiform records document Nebuchadnezzar’s siege and capture of Jerusalem in 597 and 586 BC, confirming the historical catastrophe behind Lamentations.
The archaeological evidence corroborates the destruction described in the poems, including evidence of massive burning in the City of David excavations.
