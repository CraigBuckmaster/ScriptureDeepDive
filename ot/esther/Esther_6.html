Esther 6

The King’s Sleepless Night; Haman’s Humiliation

Verses 1–5 — Insomnia, Chronicles, Discovery
12345

Hebrew Word Study

נָדְדָה nāḏəḏâ — fled / departed: Sleep "fled" the king. Not "could not sleep" but "sleep fled." In a book of hidden agency, even insomnia has a subject — who caused sleep to flee?

Historical Context

Reading royal chronicles as a sleep remedy was Persian court practice. That the king chose this night to read this section is the book’s master coincidence.

Context

This is the pivot. The king’s insomnia → chronicles read → Mordecai’s unrewarded service discovered → Haman arrives to request execution → collision. The timing is impossible as coincidence and perfect as providence.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 41:1Pharaoh’s dream — divine intervention through a king’s disturbed night.
  • Dan 2:1Nebuchadnezzar’s sleep disturbed — same pattern.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:1

That night the king could not sleep. Seven words that change everything. No angel, no vision. Just insomnia.

6:2

The chronicles reveal Mordecai’s unrewarded service from chapter 2. Years-old information, now activated.

6:3

Nothing has been done for him. The king is appalled.

6:4–5

Haman enters to request Mordecai’s execution at the exact moment the king wants to honour Mordecai. The collision is the most dramatic irony in the Hebrew Bible.

Verses 6–11 — "What Should Be Done for the Man the King Delights to Honour?"
67891011

Hebrew Word Study

יְקָר yəqār — honour / glory: Haman assumes the honour is for himself. His pride designs his own humiliation. Every detail he specifies, he must perform for Mordecai.

Context

Pure comedy and pure theology. Haman designs an elaborate honour ceremony for himself, then executes it for his enemy. His imagination creates his own punishment.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 26:27Whoever digs a pit will fall into it.
  • Ps 37:12–13The Lord laughs at the wicked.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:6

Whom would the king delight to honour more than me? Pride is the mechanism of his own humiliation.

6:7–9

Royal robe, royal horse, crown, public herald. Maximum visibility — for the wrong recipient.

6:10

Do just as you have suggested for Mordecai the Jew. Do not neglect anything. Every detail.

6:11

Haman leads Mordecai through the streets shouting his own defeat.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase

Calvin: pride blinded Haman so completely that he could not imagine the honour was for anyone else.

6:11

Calvin: the reversal is so complete that only God’s providence accounts for it.

6:10

The king says "Mordecai the Jew" — linking the honour to the condemned people.

Verses 12–14 — Haman Rushes Home; Zeresh Predicts His Fall
121314

Hebrew Word Study

נָפוֹל תִּפּוֹל nāpōl tippōl — you will surely fall: The doubled verb expresses certainty. Even Haman’s wife sees the pattern: opposing Jews leads to destruction.

Context

Reverses 5:10–14 exactly. There: boasting, gallows advised. Here: mourning, doom predicted. Same cast, opposite advice.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 12:3Whoever curses you I will curse — the promise Zeresh unknowingly invokes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:12

Mordecai returns to his post unmoved. Haman rushes home in grief. The contrast is total.

6:13

If Mordecai is of Jewish origin, you cannot stand against him. His own advisors invoke covenant theology from outside the covenant.

6:14

While still talking, eunuchs arrive to hurry Haman to the banquet. No time to process. The pace accelerates.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:13

Calvin: even pagans recognise that God protects his people.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:13

The NET notes "of Jewish origin" uses zeraʿ (seed), the covenant term.

Levenson Notes

levenson
6:12–14

Levenson: the reversal of 5:10–14 is mathematically precise. The structure argues for governing intelligence.

Jobes Notes

jobes
6:13

Jobes: Zeresh’s words are the closest thing to a theological statement in the book. She invokes covenant theology from outside the covenant.
