Esther 5

Esther’s First Banquet; Haman’s Gallows

Verses 1–8 — The Golden Sceptre; Invitation to Two Banquets
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

שַׁרְבִיט sharbīṭ — sceptre: The king extends the golden sceptre — Esther lives. The sceptre represents absolute authority and grants physical access. Esther touches its tip — the distance between death and life is one arm’s length.

Historical Context

Esther approaches on the third day of her fast — physically weakened but spiritually resolved. She dresses in royal robes — outward display masking three days of secret fasting.

Context

The king’s offer — "up to half the kingdom" — is a standard Persian diplomatic formula (cf. Mark 6:23). Esther’s restraint in not immediately revealing her request draws the king in gradually.

Cross-Reference

  • Mark 6:23Herod’s identical offer to Salome — same formula, opposite outcome.
  • Prov 15:23A word aptly spoken — Esther’s timing is her art.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1–2

Three days of fasting culminate in one second of royal favour. The sceptre’s extension is the book’s first explicit reversal.

5:3–4

Esther does not take the offer. She invites to a banquet instead. Strategy, not impulse.

5:6–8

At the first banquet, the king repeats his offer. Esther delays again: come tomorrow. The delay creates the space for the sleepless night of chapter 6.

5:8

Between tonight and tomorrow, the king will not sleep, will read the chronicles, will discover Mordecai. Esther’s delay creates the window for providence.

5:8

Calvin: she delayed not from weakness but from wisdom. God guided her to create the interval he needed.

Verses 9–14 — Haman’s Joy and Rage; The 75-Foot Gallows
91011121314

Hebrew Word Study

עֵץ ʿēṣ — tree / gallows / pole: Zeresh advises a gallows fifty cubits (75 feet) high. The excess mirrors the king’s excess in chapter 1. Overreach always precedes downfall.

Historical Context

Impalement poles in Persian practice were used for public display. The 50-cubit height is dramatic exaggeration — the victim displayed above the skyline.

Context

Haman leaves the banquet elated, then sees Mordecai. One man’s refusal collapses all joy. His emotional volatility — elation to rage in one verse — is his defining weakness.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 16:18Pride goes before destruction — the gallows Haman builds becomes his own.
  • Ps 7:15–16He who digs a pit falls into it.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:9

Happy and in high spirits. Then: filled with rage. Two emotions in one verse.

5:10–12

Haman boasts to wife and friends: wealth, sons, promotion, royal invitation. Enumeration of blessings before the fall.

5:13

All this gives me no satisfaction as long as I see Mordecai. One man’s existence poisons all achievements.

5:14

Zeresh: build gallows, ask the king in the morning. The plan is perfect — except that tonight the king will not sleep.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:13

Calvin: such is the nature of envy — it turns blessings into curses. Haman, amid honours, was more miserable than the man he despised.

5:14

Calvin: the gallows was the monument of his pride. God often permits the wicked to prepare the instrument of their own destruction.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:14

The NET notes fifty cubits is exceptional and possibly symbolic. Impalement poles were typically much shorter.

Levenson Notes

levenson
5:9–14

Levenson: Haman builds what will kill him, boasts of what he will lose, plans a morning that will destroy him. The reader knows what Haman does not.

Jobes Notes

jobes
5:13

Jobes: Haman has everything and it means nothing because one man will not bow. The psychology of hatred makes all blessings worthless.

5:14

The 75-foot gallows: narrative hyperbole signalling overreach.

People of the Chapter

Zeresh
Haman’s wife; advises the gallows
First advises building gallows (5:14), then predicts doom (6:13). She sees the truth before Haman does.
