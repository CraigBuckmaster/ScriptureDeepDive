Esther 3

Haman’s Plot to Destroy the Jews

Verses 1–6 — Mordecai Refuses to Bow; Haman’s Rage
123456

Hebrew Word Study

אֲגָגִי ʾagāgî — Agagite: Haman is called "the Agagite" — descended from Agag, king of the Amalekites (1 Sam 15). The ancient enmity between Israel and Amalek (Exod 17:8–16; Deut 25:17–19) resurfaces. This is not a personal conflict but an ancestral war. Saul’s failure to destroy Agag now bears fruit in Haman.

כָרַע kāraʿ — to bow down / kneel: Mordecai "would not kneel or bow down" (lōʾ yik̅raʿ wəlōʾ yištaḥăweh). No explicit reason is given. The refusal may be theological (bowing to a descendant of Amalek), personal, or ethnic. The ambiguity is deliberate — the narrator foregrounds the act, not the motivation.

Historical Context

Haman’s elevation "above all the nobles" (v.1) parallels Joseph’s elevation in Egypt — but inverted. Joseph saved; Haman destroys. The Amalekite-Israelite enmity was ancient: God declared "war against Amalek from generation to generation" (Exod 17:16). Haman is the latest incarnation.

Context

Haman’s response to one man’s refusal is genocide. The escalation from personal insult to ethnic annihilation reveals the nature of the enemy: this is not rational grievance but disproportionate, irrational hatred. "He scorned the idea of killing only Mordecai" (v.6) — the singular slight produces a universal sentence.

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 17:8–16Amalek attacks Israel in the wilderness — the origin of the enmity.
  • 1 Sam 15:7–33Saul spares Agag — the failure that echoes into Esther.
  • Deut 25:17–19"Remember what Amalek did... blot out their name" — the command Mordecai embodies.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1

Haman son of Hammedatha "the Agagite" — the ethnic identifier is the narrator’s signal. This is not a new villain but an ancient one. Amalek lives.

3:2

Mordecai’s refusal is absolute and repeated: "day after day." This is not a momentary lapse but sustained civil disobedience. The servants report him; he does not relent.

3:4

"He had told them he was a Jew." Mordecai’s identity is now public — the concealment that defined Esther is reversed in Mordecai. He declares what she hides.

3:5–6

Haman is "enraged" and decides to destroy "all Mordecai’s people, the Jews, throughout the whole kingdom." One man’s refusal → genocide of a nation. The escalation is the essence of anti-Semitism: the individual stands for the collective, and the collective must be destroyed.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:2

Calvin: "Mordecai’s refusal to bow was not pride but principle. Whether it was religious or ethnic, the refusal reflected a conviction that some honours belong to God alone."

3:6

Calvin: "Haman’s rage shows what lurks in the heart of the ungodly: when they cannot destroy one servant of God, they would destroy all."

Levenson Notes

levenson
3:1

Levenson reads the Agagite label as establishing Haman as the antitype of Israel: Amalek versus Israel, Agag versus Saul, Haman versus Mordecai. The book replays the ancient conflict with the outcome reversed.

3:5–6

Levenson connects Haman’s plan to Pharaoh’s (Exod 1:9–10): both see a minority population as a threat and both respond with attempted extermination. Esther is an Exodus retold.

Jobes Notes

jobes
3:1–6

Jobes: "The Agagite label is a flashing red light. The reader who knows 1 Samuel 15 understands immediately: this is the Saul-Agag conflict replayed. What Saul left undone, Haman will try to complete — in reverse."

3:6

"He scorned the idea of killing only Mordecai." Jobes: "The logic of genocide is always the same: the individual offence is made to represent the group, and the group must pay. Haman’s reasoning is as old as Pharaoh’s."

Verses 7–15 — The Lot Cast; The Decree Issued
789101112131415

Hebrew Word Study

פּוּר pûr — lot: The pur ("lot," from Akkadian pūru) is cast to determine the date of destruction. The lot falls on the 13th of Adar — eleven months away. The delay gives time for the counter-plot. What Haman intends as random fate becomes providential timing. The festival of Purim takes its name from this lot.

נָבוֹכָה nāḇōkâ — bewildered / confused / perplexed: "The city of Susa was bewildered" (nāḇōkâ). The empire’s capital is disturbed by the decree. The non-Jewish population is not enthusiastic but confused. The genocide is the king and Haman’s project, not the people’s.

Historical Context

The pur (lot) is an Akkadian loanword for divination by casting. Haman uses the lot to determine the "lucky" date — pagan divination choosing the day for Jewish genocide. The 13th of Adar (February–March 473 BC) gives eleven months of suspense — the longest dramatic delay in the book.

Context

Haman offers the king 10,000 talents of silver (approximately 375 tons) to fund the genocide. Whether the king accepts or refuses the money is ambiguous (v.11: "Keep the money" could mean "I decline" or "It’s yours to use"). The financial transaction gives the genocide an economic dimension: anti-Semitism as revenue source.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 16:33"The lot is cast into the lap, but its every decision is from the LORD" — the theological comment on the pur.
  • Exod 1:9–10Pharaoh’s rationale for oppression — the structural parallel to Haman’s.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:7

The pur is cast in the first month (Nisan) for execution in the twelfth month (Adar). Eleven months. Haman believes the lot chose the date; the reader knows God chose it — giving time for Esther’s intervention.

3:8–9

Haman’s pitch: "There is a certain people... whose customs are different... it is not in the king’s best interest to tolerate them." The language of anti-Semitism: otherness as threat, difference as danger.

3:12–14

The decree goes out in every language to every province: destroy, kill, annihilate all Jews — young and old, women and children — on the 13th of Adar. Three verbs for one purpose. The redundancy is legal thoroughness and moral horror.

3:15

"The king and Haman sat down to drink, but the city of Susa was bewildered." The chapter’s final sentence is its most devastating. The architects of genocide drink while the capital reels.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:7

Calvin: "Haman cast the lot but God held it. The date fell on Adar — the last month — giving nearly a year for God’s counter-plan. What the wicked intend as fate, God uses as schedule."

3:15

Calvin: "They sat down to drink while a nation was sentenced to death. This is the conscience of the ungodly: it sleeps when it should scream."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:7

The NET notes pūr is attested in Akkadian texts from Nuzi and elsewhere as a divination cube or lot-casting device. The Hebrew plural pūrîm gives the festival its name.

3:9

The NET observes 10,000 talents of silver is roughly two-thirds of the annual revenue of the Persian Empire (Herodotus 3.95). Haman is either fantastically wealthy or expects to fund the payment from confiscated Jewish property.

Levenson Notes

levenson
3:8–9

Levenson analyses Haman’s speech as a classic anti-Semitic formula: (1) they are different, (2) they are everywhere, (3) they disobey, (4) it’s profitable to destroy them. The formula recurs through history.

3:13

Levenson connects "destroy, kill, annihilate" to the Deuteronomic herem warfare vocabulary — turned against Israel. The hunter becomes the hunted.

Jobes Notes

jobes
3:7

Jobes: "The pur is the theological key to the book. Haman trusts in chance; the reader learns that what looks random is directed. The festival named after this lot celebrates the God who governs what pagans call fate."

3:15

Jobes on the final verse: "The juxtaposition is deliberately nauseating. Drinking and genocide in the same sentence. The banality of evil."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Haman
Agagite; the king’s vizier; architect of genocide
Descended from Agag, king of the Amalekites. His refusal to accept one man’s defiance escalates to planned genocide. He represents the recurring biblical pattern: Amalek’s war against God’s people.
