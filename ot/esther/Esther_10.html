Esther 10

Mordecai’s Greatness

Verses 1–2 — Xerxes’ Tribute; Mordecai in the Annals
12

Hebrew Word Study

מַס mas — tribute / forced labor: King Xerxes imposed tribute (mas) on the empire. The word mas echoes Solomon’s forced labour (1 Kgs 5:13) and the Egyptian bondage (Exod 1:11). The empire’s extractive machinery continues — but within it, the Jews are now secure.

Context

The mention of imperial tribute frames the book: it opened with the king’s display of wealth (1:4) and closes with his taxation. The empire grinds on. Within it, one community’s survival story is recorded in the royal annals alongside tax records. Sacred history lives inside secular archives.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Kgs 5:13Solomon’s forced labour — the same word mas for imperial extraction.
  • Gen 41:40–44Joseph’s position in Egypt — the archetype Mordecai fulfils.

Verse 3 — Mordecai’s Greatness
3

Hebrew Word Study

דּוֹרֵשׁ dōrēš — seeking / working for: Mordecai "worked for (dōrēš) the good of his people and spoke up for the welfare of all the Jews." The verb dōrēš (seek, inquire, care for) is the Chronicler’s signature word (2 Chr 7:14). To seek the good of one’s people is the vocation of the faithful leader in exile.

Historical Context

Mordecai is described as "second in rank to King Xerxes" — the same position Joseph held in Egypt (Gen 41:40–44). The diaspora Jew at the pinnacle of foreign power is a biblical archetype: Joseph, Daniel, Mordecai. Each saves his people from the inside.

Context

The book ends not with Esther but with Mordecai, and not with the Jews but with the Persian empire. The final note is that Mordecai is "prominent among the Jews and held in high esteem by his many fellow Jews." His greatness serves his people. Power is measured by service.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1

King Xerxes imposed tribute throughout the empire. The mention of tribute is a narrative frame: the book opened with the king’s display of wealth (1:4) and closes with his taxation. The empire continues; within it, the Jews are now secure.

10:2

Mordecai’s acts are recorded "in the book of the annals of the kings of Media and Persia." The same chronicles that saved his life (6:1–2) now preserve his legacy. The archives are a recurring character in Esther.

10:3

"Second in rank... great among the Jews... working for the good of his people." The threefold description defines true greatness: political power, communal standing, active service. All three are necessary; none alone is sufficient.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
10:3

Calvin: Mordecai used his power for his people, not for himself. This is the test of all authority: does it serve others or itself? Mordecai passed where Haman failed.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
10:2

The NET notes the reference to "the book of the annals of the kings of Media and Persia" parallels the Kings formula ("the book of the chronicles of the kings of Israel/Judah"). Mordecai’s deeds are recorded alongside those of kings.

10:3

The NET observes "spoke up for the welfare of all the Jews" uses dōrēš shālōm — literally "sought the peace/wholeness" of his people. The language echoes Jer 29:7: seek the welfare of the city where I have sent you.

Levenson Notes

levenson
10:1–3

Levenson: the ending mirrors Joseph’s ending in Genesis: a Jewish leader, second to the king, preserving his people in a foreign land. The Exodus-return has not happened; the diaspora continues. But within it, God’s people survive and their leader serves. This is the book’s final theology: life in exile is possible when the hidden God governs through faithful servants.

Jobes Notes

jobes
10:3

Jobes: the book’s final word is not about God but about Mordecai’s service to his people. In a book where God is hidden, the evidence of God’s presence is a man who seeks the welfare of others. The hidden God is revealed through visible faithfulness.
