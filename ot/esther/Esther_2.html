Esther 2

Esther Becomes Queen; Mordecai Uncovers a Plot

Verses 1–11 — The Beauty Contest; Esther Conceals Her Identity
1234567891011

Hebrew Word Study

הֲדַסָּה hădassâ — myrtle: Esther’s Hebrew name is Hadassah (myrtle) — a native plant. Her Persian name Esther may derive from Ishtar (Babylonian goddess) or from the Persian stāra (star). She carries two identities: one hidden, one public. The dual naming is the book’s central metaphor.

לא הִגִּידָה lōʾ higgîḏâ — she did not reveal: Esther "had not revealed her nationality or family background, because Mordecai had forbidden her to do so." The hiddenness is strategic. In a book where God is hidden, God’s agent is also hidden. The theology of concealment operates at every level.

Historical Context

The beauty preparation period was twelve months: six months with oil of myrrh, six months with perfumes (v.12). This was standard Persian royal protocol for women entering the king’s harem. The process was not merely cosmetic but included acculturation to court manners, language, and protocol.

Context

Esther’s concealment of her Jewish identity is the narrative engine. It creates the dramatic tension of chapters 5–7: the queen is one of the people targeted for destruction, and the king doesn’t know. Every scene in the book is shaped by who knows what — and who doesn’t.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 12:11–13Abraham conceals Sarah’s identity in Egypt — the patriarchal precedent for strategic concealment.
  • Dan 1:3–7Daniel and friends given Babylonian names — the same dual-identity pattern in exile.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:5–6

Mordecai’s genealogy: son of Jair, son of Shimei, son of Kish — a Benjaminite. The lineage connects him to Saul (also son of Kish, 1 Sam 9:1). The Saul-Agag conflict will replay through Mordecai and Haman the Agagite.

2:7

Mordecai adopted Hadassah/Esther after her parents died. She is an orphan raised by a cousin. Her vulnerability makes her elevation more dramatic.

2:10

"Esther had not revealed her nationality." Mordecai’s instruction. The concealment is not deception but survival strategy in a precarious environment.

2:11

Mordecai walks near the harem court daily to learn about Esther’s welfare. Parental care expressed through surveillance. He watches over her as God watches over Israel — present but unseen.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:7

Calvin: "God often raises the lowliest to the highest positions. An orphan Jewish girl becomes queen of Persia. Providence works through unlikely instruments."

2:10

Calvin on concealment: "Mordecai did not command her to deny her faith but to conceal her nation. There is a difference between denial and discretion."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:5–6

The NET notes "carried into exile from Jerusalem by Nebuchadnezzar" grammatically refers to Kish (the ancestor), not Mordecai himself. Mordecai is a later-generation diaspora Jew.

2:7

The NET observes "Esther" may derive from Persian stāra (star) rather than from the goddess Ishtar. Both etymologies are debated.

Levenson Notes

levenson
2:5–7

Levenson notes the genealogy mirrors Moses’s introduction (Exod 2:1–10): both are raised by surrogate parents, both conceal identity, both rise to power in a foreign court. Esther is a new Moses.

2:10

Levenson: "The hidden Jew in the palace is the book’s most important symbol. When identity is dangerous, survival requires strategic revelation. The question is not whether to reveal but when."

Jobes Notes

jobes
2:5–7

Jobes traces the Benjaminite genealogy: Kish links Mordecai to Saul. Haman is called "the Agagite" (3:1) — Agag was the Amalekite king Saul failed to execute (1 Sam 15). The ancient enmity between Benjamin and Amalek resurfaces.

2:10

Jobes on the concealment: "The hidden identity is the book’s master metaphor. God is hidden. Esther is hidden. Providence is hidden. The entire theology works through concealment."

Verses 12–18 — Esther Wins Favour; Crowned Queen
12131415161718

Hebrew Word Study

חֵן ḥēn — grace / favour: Esther "won the favour (ḥēn) of everyone who saw her" (v.15). The word ḥēn is the same root as "grace." She finds grace not through manipulation but through some quality the narrator leaves unnamed. Like Joseph in Egypt, favour follows her without explanation — which is to say, it follows by providence.

Context

Esther is chosen "in the tenth month, the month of Tebeth, in the seventh year" (v.16). She enters the palace four years after Vashti’s removal (1:3 = year 3; 2:16 = year 7). The four-year gap coincides with Xerxes’ absence on the failed Greek campaign (Herodotus). The king returns defeated and chooses a new queen. Providence uses military defeat to advance its purpose.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 39:4–6Joseph found favour in Potiphar’s house — the same unexplained grace.
  • Prov 3:3–4"Let love and faithfulness never leave you... then you will find favour in the sight of God and man."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:15

"She asked for nothing other than what Hegai recommended." Esther’s restraint contrasts with the other candidates. She trusts the expert’s judgment rather than loading herself with accessories.

2:17

"The king was attracted to Esther more than to any of the other women." The narrator does not say the king loved her character — he was attracted. The relationship begins on the king’s terms, not Esther’s.

2:18

A coronation banquet — "Esther’s feast" — and a holiday for the provinces. The new queen’s celebration is the second of the book’s banquets. The pattern continues.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:17

Calvin: "God inclined the king’s heart toward Esther, just as he inclined Cyrus’s heart toward Jerusalem. The affections of kings are instruments of divine purpose."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:16

The NET dates this to January 479 BC (Tebeth, year 7), four years after Vashti’s removal, consistent with Xerxes’ absence on the Greek campaign.

Levenson Notes

levenson
2:16–17

Levenson notes the timing: Esther becomes queen in Xerxes’ seventh year, after the disastrous Greek campaign. The king who lost at Salamis and Thermopylae finds consolation in a new queen. Geopolitics serves providence.

Jobes Notes

jobes
2:15–17

Jobes: "Esther’s favour is unexplained by the narrative. The Hebrew ḥēn simply happens to her, as it happened to Joseph, as it happened to Daniel. The narrator is pointing to something beyond human charm."

Verses 19–23 — Mordecai Uncovers an Assassination Plot
1920212223

Hebrew Word Study

סֵפֶר הַדְּבָרִים sēper haddiḇrîm — book of chronicles / annals: The plot is recorded in "the book of the annals" (sēper diḇrê hayyāmîm). This record will be read to the sleepless king in chapter 6 — the pivotal "coincidence" that reverses everything. An unrewarded deed, filed and forgotten, becomes God’s timing mechanism.

Context

Mordecai saves the king’s life but receives no reward. The oversight is recorded. This Chekhov’s gun — planted in chapter 2, fired in chapter 6 — is the book’s finest structural achievement. The reader who knows the ending sees providence; the reader who doesn’t sees injustice. Both readings are correct.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 40:14—23The cupbearer forgets Joseph for two years. The same pattern: an unrewarded deed, a forgotten benefactor, a providential delay.
  • Esth 6:1–3The delayed reward — the sleepless night that changes everything.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:21–22

Two eunuchs plot to assassinate the king. Mordecai discovers the plot and reports through Esther. His loyalty to the Persian crown saves the king’s life.

2:23

"All this was recorded in the book of the annals in the presence of the king." Recorded but not rewarded. The delay is the narrative’s most important structural feature.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:23

Calvin: "God stored this deed in the annals for the appointed time. What seems like the king’s forgetfulness is God’s timing."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:21

The NET notes "Bigthana and Teresh" are Persian names. Their positions as "doorkeepers" (threshold guardians) gave them access to the king’s private quarters.

Levenson Notes

levenson
2:21–23

Levenson connects this to the Joseph narrative: "Mordecai, like Joseph, saves his master’s life and is forgotten. The delay between service and reward is the space where faith must operate."

Jobes Notes

jobes
2:19–23

Jobes: "The unrewarded deed is the fulcrum of the entire book. Without this planted detail, chapter 6 has no mechanism. The narrator places it here casually, almost as an afterthought — which is exactly how providence works."
