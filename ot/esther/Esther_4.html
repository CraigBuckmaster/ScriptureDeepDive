Esther 4

Mordecai’s Appeal; "For Such a Time as This"

Verses 1–8 — Mordecai Mourns; Sends the Decree to Esther
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

שַׂק śaq — sackcloth: Mordecai puts on sackcloth and ashes and goes into the city "wailing loudly and bitterly." The mourning is public and extreme. No one entering the king’s gate may wear sackcloth (v.2) — grief is forbidden at the threshold of power. The palace excludes the very emotion the crisis demands.

Historical Context

"In every province to which the edict and order of the king came, there was great mourning among the Jews, with fasting, weeping and wailing. Many lay in sackcloth and ashes" (v.3). The mourning is empire-wide. The Jewish community’s only weapon is grief — and in a book without God’s name, grief directed at whom?

Context

Esther learns of the decree through intermediaries. She sends clothes to Mordecai (he refuses them) and then sends Hathak to find out what is happening. The layered communication — Esther → Hathak → Mordecai → Hathak → Esther — mirrors the concealment theme: even the queen cannot communicate directly with her guardian.

Cross-Reference

  • Joel 1:13–14"Put on sackcloth and mourn, you priests" — the prophetic pattern of communal lament.
  • Dan 9:3Daniel’s response to crisis: prayer with fasting, sackcloth, and ashes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1–2

Mordecai cannot enter the palace gate in sackcloth. The palace literally excludes grief. The architecture of power has no room for the suffering of the powerless.

4:3

Empire-wide mourning: fasting, weeping, sackcloth. The book that never mentions prayer describes every element of prayer except the addressee. The reader supplies what the text withholds.

4:7–8

Mordecai sends the exact text of the decree and instructs Esther to "go into the king’s presence to beg for mercy and plead with him for her people." The moment of revelation approaches: Esther must unmask.

Verses 9–17 — "For Such a Time as This"; "If I Perish, I Perish"
91011121314151617

Hebrew Word Study

כָזאת kāzōʾt — such as this / like this: "Who knows whether you have not come to royal position for such a time as this?" (ləʿēṯ kāzōʾt). The most famous phrase in Esther. "Who knows" is deliberately agnostic — Mordecai does not say "God sent you." He says "who knows?" The hiddenness of God extends even to the moment of greatest theological clarity.

אָבַדְתִּי ʾāḇadətî — I am lost / I perish: "If I perish, I perish" (kaʾăšer ʾāḇadətî ʾāḇāḏtî). Esther accepts the possibility of death without any guarantee of deliverance. This is faith without assurance — the most radical form. She does not say "God will save me." She says "If I die, I die."

Historical Context

Persian law forbade unsummoned approach to the king under penalty of death (v.11). The only exception: the king extends his golden sceptre. Esther has not been summoned for thirty days. Her approach is genuinely life-threatening — this is not rhetorical drama but real danger.

Context

The exchange between Mordecai and Esther is the theological centre of the book. Mordecai’s argument has three parts: (1) don’t think you’ll escape — being in the palace won’t save you (v.13); (2) deliverance will arise "from another place" even if you refuse — God’s purpose cannot be thwarted (v.14a); (3) "who knows?" — perhaps this is why you’re here (v.14b). The argument moves from warning to theology to vocation. Esther’s response moves from fear to resolve: "If I perish, I perish."

Cross-Reference

  • Exod 4:10–16Moses’ reluctance and God’s insistence — the calling-and-resistance pattern.
  • Dan 3:16–18Shadrach, Meshach, Abednego: "our God is able to deliver us... but even if he does not" — the same faith-without-guarantee.
  • Luke 22:42"Not my will, but yours be done" — the ultimate expression of submission to uncertain outcomes.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:11

The death penalty for unsummoned approach. Esther has not been called for thirty days. The king’s cooling affection adds urgency to the danger.

4:13

"Do not think that because you are in the king’s house you alone of all the Jews will escape." Mordecai eliminates the option of hiding. Esther’s concealment, which protected her until now, will not protect her from this.

4:14

"Deliverance will arise from another place." The closest the book comes to naming God. "Another place" (māqōm ʾāḥēr) is the circumlocution — God is the place left unnamed. If Esther refuses, God will still deliver. But Esther and her family will perish.

4:16

"Go, gather together all the Jews in Susa, and fast for me." Esther takes command. She gives orders to Mordecai, not the other way around. The power dynamic reverses. "If I perish, I perish" — three words of absolute resolve.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:14

Calvin: "Mordecai speaks of ‘another place’ because he believed firmly that God would preserve his people. He did not know how, but he knew God would. This is the confidence of faith: certain of the outcome, uncertain of the method."

4:16

Calvin on "If I perish": "This is the highest expression of piety: to commit oneself to God’s will without any assurance of survival. Esther did not say ‘God will save me.’ She said ‘I will go, and what happens is in God’s hands.’"

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:14

The NET notes māqōm (‘place’) is used as a substitute for the divine name in later rabbinic tradition (hammāqōm = "the Place" = God). This may already be the sense here.

4:16

The NET observes the three-day fast is the maximum duration for total abstinence from food and water. Esther risks physical debilitation before approaching the king.

Levenson Notes

levenson
4:14

Levenson: "‘Another place’ (māqōm ʾāḥēr) is the book’s most famous circumlocution. Jewish tradition universally reads it as referring to God. The circumlocution is itself theological: in a book where God is hidden, even references to God are hidden."

4:16

Levenson connects Esther’s three-day fast to other biblical three-day patterns (Jonah 1:17; Hos 6:2; Jesus’ burial). Three days is the biblical interval between death and new life.

Jobes Notes

jobes
4:14

Jobes on "who knows?": "The phrase is not doubt but humility. Mordecai does not presume to know God’s plan; he only observes the possibility. This is the book’s theology in miniature: God is real, God acts, but God’s methods are hidden."

4:16

Jobes on "If I perish, I perish": "This is faith without a safety net. No angel appears. No voice from heaven. Just a decision to act on the possibility that her position has a purpose. This is what faith looks like when God is silent."

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Hathak
Eunuch; Esther’s trusted messenger
The intermediary between the hidden queen and the public mourner. His name may mean "truth" in Persian. He carries the information that makes Esther’s decision possible.
