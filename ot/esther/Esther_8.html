Esther 8

The Counter-Decree; Jewish Defence Authorised

Verses 1–8 — Mordecai Elevated; The Irrevocable Law Problem
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

טַבַּעַת ṭabbaʿat — signet ring: The king gives Mordecai his signet ring (ṭabbaʿat) — the same ring he gave Haman (3:10). Power transfers via the seal. The ring that signed genocide now signs deliverance. The instrument is the same; the hand is different.

Historical Context

Persian law could not be annulled (cf. Dan 6:8). Haman’s decree stands. The solution is not cancellation but counter-legislation: a second decree authorising the Jews to defend themselves. Two irrevocable laws in direct conflict — the outcome is decided by the people, not the paper.

Context

Esther’s second petition is as brave as her first: she falls at the king’s feet weeping, begging him to "put an end to the evil plan" (v.3). The king extends the sceptre again (v.4) — the second time. Esther is now operating in full authority, co-directing the counter-decree with Mordecai.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:1–2

Esther receives Haman’s estate. Mordecai receives the signet ring. The assets of the destroyer transfer to the targets of destruction.

8:3–4

Esther weeps before the king again. The second petition requires the same courage as the first — the death penalty for unsummoned approach still applies.

8:5–6

Esther’s careful diplomacy: "If it pleases the king... if he regards me with favour... if the thing seems right... and if I have found favour." Four conditions, each deferring to royal authority while pressing for action.

8:8

Write another decree — "no document written in the king’s name and sealed with his ring can be revoked." The legal framework that created the crisis now creates the solution.

Verses 9–17 — The Counter-Decree; Joy Among the Jews
91011121314151617

Hebrew Word Study

שָׂשׂוֹן śāśōn — joy / gladness: "For the Jews it was a time of happiness and joy (śāśōn), gladness and honour." Four words for joy. The reversal of 4:3 (mourning, fasting, weeping, wailing) is exact: four words of grief become four words of joy.

מִתְיַהֲדִים mityəhadîm — becoming Jews / converting: "Many people of other nationalities became Jews" (mityəhadîm). The verb is unique in the Hebrew Bible. Fear of the Jews drives conversion — not the most theological motivation, but the narrator records it without comment.

Historical Context

The counter-decree is written on the 23rd of Sivan (June), giving nearly nine months before the 13th of Adar (March). The decree authorises Jews to "destroy, kill and annihilate" any armed force that attacks them — the exact three verbs from Haman’s decree (3:13), now reversed.

Context

The chapter’s final verse — "many people became Jews because fear of the Jews had seized them" — is theologically provocative. The conversion is fear-based, not faith-based. The narrator neither endorses nor criticises it. In a book where God is hidden, even conversion is ambiguous.

Cross-Reference

  • Esth 3:13Haman’s three verbs — now reversed in the counter-decree.
  • Zech 8:23Ten Gentiles will take hold of one Jew — a more positive vision of the nations joining Israel.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:9–14

The counter-decree uses the same administrative machinery as Haman’s: scribes, couriers, every language, every province. The empire’s bureaucracy now serves deliverance as it served destruction.

8:11

The Jews may "destroy, kill and annihilate" any force that attacks them — including women and children, and to plunder their property. The language mirrors 3:13 exactly. The reversal is total and deliberate.

8:15

Mordecai in royal robes: blue and white, a large gold crown, a purple garment. The contrast with 4:1 (sackcloth and ashes) is visual theology.

8:17

In every province: joy, gladness, feasting, celebration. And: many people "became Jews." The fear of the Jews produces conversions. Providence works even through ambiguous motivations.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:15–17

Calvin: the reversal from mourning to joy shows that God’s timing, though painful in the waiting, produces abundance in the fulfilment. The joy exceeded the grief.

8:17

Calvin: some became Jews from fear, others perhaps from genuine admiration for a God who could produce such a reversal. God uses all motives.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:9

The NET notes the 23rd of Sivan is the longest day of the year — symbolic or coincidental, the decree goes out at the year’s peak.

8:17

The NET observes mityəhadîm (hithpael of yhd) is a hapax legomenon — the only occurrence in the Bible. It may mean formal conversion or simply identification with the Jewish community.

Levenson Notes

levenson
8:15–17

Levenson: Mordecai’s royal garments reverse the sackcloth of chapter 4 as the joy reverses the mourning. Every element has its counterpart. The book’s structure is a perfect palindrome of suffering and salvation.

8:17

Levenson reads "became Jews" as genuine conversion, noting the term mityəhadîm enters Jewish vocabulary from this verse. Whatever the motivation, the result is communal inclusion.

Jobes Notes

jobes
8:11

Jobes notes the three verbs are identical to 3:13: destroy, kill, annihilate. The reversal is word-for-word. The same language that condemned now liberates.

8:17

Jobes on the conversions: the narrator records without judging. In a book of ambiguities, even religious conversion is presented ambiguously.
