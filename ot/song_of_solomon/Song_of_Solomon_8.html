Song of Solomon 8

Love as Strong as Death

Verses 1–7 — “Set Me as a Seal upon Your Heart”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

חוֹתָם ḥōṭām — seal / signet: "Set me as a seal (ḥōṭām) upon your heart, as a seal upon your arm" (v.6). A seal was pressed into hot wax to mark ownership and identity. It was worn on a cord over the heart or as a ring on the hand. The beloved asks to be imprinted on the lover permanently — on his heart (affection) and his arm (action). Love as identity-mark.

עַזָּה כַמָּוֶת ʿazzâ kammāwet — strong as death: "Love is as strong as death (ʿazzâ kammāwet), its jealousy unyielding as the grave. It burns like blazing fire, like a mighty flame" (v.6). The poem’s theological summit. Love matches death in power. The two forces that govern human existence — love and death — are equal. Neither yields. And love’s fire is literally "the flame of Yah" (shalhebet-yâ) — the only occurrence of God’s name in the poem. Love’s fire is divine fire.

Context

The poem builds to its theological climax. The beloved wishes he were her brother so she could kiss him publicly without shame (vv.1–2) — the social constraints on public affection. She brings him to her mother’s house (v.2), the refrain repeats one final time (v.4), and then the definitive statement: "Set me as a seal... love is as strong as death... many waters cannot quench love; rivers cannot sweep it away" (vv.6–7). If a man offered all his wealth for love, it would be utterly scorned. Love is not purchasable, not earnable, not resistible. It is as strong as death and comes from God.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 8:35–39"Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? Neither death nor life..." — Paul’s declaration that love defeats even death.
  • 1 Cor 13:8"Love never fails" — the permanence the Song asserts.
  • John 15:13"Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one’s life for one’s friends" — the love stronger than death enacted at Calvary.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:4

"Do not arouse or awaken love until it so desires." The refrain’s third and final occurrence. The poem’s structural markers: 2:7, 3:5, 8:4.

8:6

"Set me as a seal upon your heart... for love is as strong as death, its jealousy unyielding as the grave. It burns like blazing fire, like a mighty flame." The book’s theological peak. Love equals death in power. The flame is shalhebet-yâ — "the flame of the LORD" — the only reference to God’s name in the entire poem.

8:7

"Many waters cannot quench love; rivers cannot sweep it away. If one were to give all the wealth of one’s house for love, it would be utterly scorned." Love is unquenchable and unpurchasable. It transcends both nature and commerce.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:6

Alter: "strong as death" — the only force in human experience as absolute as love is death. Both are unavoidable, irreversible, and total in their claim. The Song asserts parity.

8:6

Alter: shalhebet-yâ — the divine name smuggled into the poem’s climax. God has been absent from the Song’s surface but present in its fire. Love’s origin is revealed at the last possible moment.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:6

Calvin: "love is as strong as death" — Calvin: Christ’s love for the church IS stronger than death. He entered death and conquered it. The Song prophesies what Calvary accomplishes.

8:7

Calvin: love’s unquenchability is God’s own nature: "God is love" (1 John 4:8). The flame of Yah burns because God is its source. No water on earth can extinguish the divine fire.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:6

The NET: shalhebet-yâ is debated — "flame of Yah(weh)" or "mighty flame" (using the divine name as an intensifier). Either reading connects love’s fire to God.

8:7

The NET: "many waters" echoes the chaos-waters of creation (Gen 1:2). Even primordial chaos cannot drown love. Love predates and survives the forces of disorder.

Longman Notes

longman
8:6

Longman: shalhebet-yâ — "flame of Yah" — is the Song’s only mention of God by name. It comes at the climactic moment. The fire of love is divine in origin. This single word transforms the entire poem from secular love poetry into theological statement.

8:7

Longman: "if one were to give all the wealth of one’s house for love, it would be utterly scorned" — the anti-transactional theology. Love cannot be bought. This refutes every attempt to reduce love to economics, including the friends’ transactional theology in Job.

Garrett Notes

garrett
8:6–7

Garrett: the seal/death/fire/water sequence is the densest theology in the poem. Seal = permanence. Death = power. Fire = divine origin. Water = indestructibility. Four dimensions of love’s nature in four images.

8:7

Garrett: "utterly scorned" — the Hebrew is emphatic. The person who tries to buy love is not merely refused but despised. Love’s nature makes transaction obscene.

Verses 8–14 — “Make Haste, My Beloved”
891011121314

Hebrew Word Study

בְרַח bəraḥ — flee / make haste / hurry: "Make haste (bəraḥ), my beloved, and be like a gazelle or a young stag on the spice-laden mountains" (v.14). The poem’s final words are an invitation: come quickly. The poem ends not with resolution but with longing. Love is never finished; desire is never fully satisfied. The final image is the beloved running toward her, fleet as a deer on fragrant mountains. Love in motion, always arriving, never complete.

Context

The poem’s closing section addresses practical matters (vv.8–10: the little sister who has no breasts — protection of the immature), recaps the vineyard imagery (vv.11–12: Solomon had a vineyard; the beloved’s vineyard is her own to give), and ends with the lovers calling to each other (vv.13–14). "Let me hear your voice" (v.13). "Make haste, my beloved" (v.14). The poem closes as it opened: with desire, longing, and invitation. Love is not a destination but a journey, always calling, always running.

Cross-Reference

  • Rev 22:17"The Spirit and the bride say, ‘Come!’" — the cosmic invitation that echoes the Song’s closing "make haste."
  • Rev 22:20"Come, Lord Jesus" — the final prayer of Scripture, an echo of the bride’s final cry.
  • Isa 5:1–7"My beloved had a vineyard" — the vineyard as love/covenant metaphor across Scripture.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
8:8–10

"We have a little sister, and her breasts are not yet grown." Protection of the immature: if she is a wall (strong), adorn her; if she is a door (vulnerable), protect her. The community cares for those not yet ready for love.

8:11–12

"Solomon had a vineyard... my own vineyard is mine to give." The beloved asserts ownership of her own sexuality. It is hers to give, not Solomon’s to take. Agency is the final theological statement about love.

8:13–14

"Let me hear your voice." "Make haste, my beloved." The poem ends with mutual invitation. Love is never finished. The final word is not "the end" but "come."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
8:12

Alter: "my own vineyard is mine" echoes and corrects 1:6. The vineyard she failed to tend is now fully possessed. The poem’s last self-reference is ownership: she belongs to herself before she gives herself.

8:14

Alter: the gazelle and young stag on the spice-laden mountains return from 2:8–9. The poem closes where it opened: with the beloved leaping toward her. The circle is both complete and open.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
8:12

Calvin: "my own vineyard is mine to give" is the theology of grace applied to human love. What is freely given has infinite value; what is compelled or purchased has none.

8:14

Calvin: "make haste, my beloved" — the church’s cry to Christ: "Come, Lord Jesus" (Rev 22:20). The Song ends as Scripture ends: with longing for the Bridegroom’s return.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
8:12

The NET: the vineyard contrast is economic: Solomon’s vineyard generates income (1000 shekels); hers generates love. The values are incommensurable.

8:14

The NET: the poem’s final word is an imperative of desire: come! hurry! The open ending leaves the reader in the position of the beloved: waiting, longing, calling.

Longman Notes

longman
8:11–12

Longman: "my own vineyard is mine to give" recalls 1:6 where she neglected her own vineyard. The poem’s arc: from neglected vineyard to owned vineyard. She has claimed her own life, her own love, her own agency.

8:14

Longman: the poem ends with desire, not consummation. The beloved calls: come quickly. The open ending mirrors life: love is always in process, always calling, never complete until the final consummation of all things (Rev 22:17).

Garrett Notes

garrett
8:11–12

Garrett: the vineyard contrast — Solomon’s commercial vineyard vs the beloved’s personal vineyard — asserts that love transcends economics. Her vineyard is priceless because it is freely given, not leased.

8:14

Garrett: the open ending is the Song’s most sophisticated literary choice. No resolution, no tidy conclusion. Love continues. The reader is left with longing — which is love’s natural state.
