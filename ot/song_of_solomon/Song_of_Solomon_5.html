Song of Solomon 5

The Lover Knocks; The Beloved Searches

Verses 1–8 — “I Opened for My Beloved, but He Had Turned and Gone”
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

מֹר mōr — myrrh: "I rose to open for my beloved, and my hands dripped with myrrh, my fingers with flowing myrrh, on the handles of the bolt" (v.5). The lover left myrrh on the door — evidence of his presence, fragrance of his absence. He came and went while she hesitated. The myrrh-drenched bolt is the poem’s most poignant image: love was here, and you missed it.

Context

The poem’s crisis. The Lover knocks at night; the Beloved hesitates ("I have taken off my robe — must I put it on again?" v.3). She rises to open, but he has gone (v.6). She searches the streets and the watchmen find her — this time they beat her and take her cloak (v.7). The darker parallel to chapter 3’s search: love delayed is love complicated. Hesitation has consequences.

Cross-Reference

  • Rev 3:20"I stand at the door and knock" — the same image of the beloved knocking while the one inside delays.
  • Matt 25:10–12"The door was shut... ‘Lord, open the door!’" — delayed response to the bridegroom’s arrival.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:2–3

"I slept but my heart was awake. Listen! My beloved is knocking. ‘Open to me, my sister, my darling.’ I have taken off my robe — must I put it on again?" The hesitation: comfort over love. She delays opening.

5:5–6

"My hands dripped with myrrh... I opened for my beloved, but my beloved had left; he was gone. My heart sank at his departure. I looked for him but did not find him." The consequence of delay: absence. He left evidence (myrrh) but not himself.

5:7

"The watchmen found me... they beat me, they bruised me; they took away my cloak." The darker search: the watchmen who helped in 3:3 now harm. Love’s seeking can be costly.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:3

Alter: "I have taken off my robe" — the excuse is deliberately trivial. The poem exposes how small barriers can prevent great meetings.

5:5–6

Alter: the myrrh-drenched bolt and the empty doorway: the poem’s composition of presence and absence in a single image. He was here; the proof remains; he does not.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:2–6

Calvin: the allegorical reading: Christ knocks (Rev 3:20); the soul delays; when finally she opens, she finds absence. Calvin: spiritual apathy has consequences. God’s presence is not guaranteed to those who delay.

5:7

Calvin: the watchmen as hostile forces: the world attacks those who seek Christ openly. The search for love is not always safe.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:5

The NET: "flowing myrrh" on the bolt indicates the lover anointed the lock with perfume — a love-gift left even in departure. The fragrance is both evidence and reproach.

5:7

The NET: the watchmen’s violence is unexpected. They stripped her veil — an act of public shaming. The search has become dangerous.

Longman Notes

longman
5:2–6

Longman: the delay is the poem’s turning point. Love requires responsiveness. The beloved chose comfort; the lover withdrew. Not punishment but natural consequence: love does not wait forever.

5:7

Longman: the beating by the watchmen is the poem’s darkest moment. The city that was neutral (ch 3) is now hostile. Seeking love in the wrong places carries risk.

Garrett Notes

garrett
5:2–8

Garrett: this passage parallels and inverts chapter 3. Both involve night searching; both involve watchmen. But here the search fails and the seeker is harmed. The poem deepens through repetition with variation.

5:5

Garrett: the myrrh on the door handles is the most evocative image in the poem. He was here; the fragrance proves it; but he is gone. Presence becomes absence scented with what was almost touched.

Verses 9–16 — “What Is Your Beloved More Than Another?” — The Male Wasf
910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

דּוֹדִי dōdî — my beloved: "My beloved (dōdî) is radiant and ruddy, outstanding among ten thousand" (v.10). The Daughters ask: what makes your beloved special? She responds with the male wasf — the only extended male-beauty description in the Bible. Head of gold, locks like raven, eyes like doves, cheeks like spice beds, lips dripping myrrh, arms of gold, body like ivory, legs like pillars of marble, appearance like Lebanon. "He is altogether lovely" (v.16).

Context

The Daughters of Jerusalem ask: why is your beloved better than others? (v.9). Her response is the male wasf — a head-to-toe description matching the female wasf of chapter 4. Gold, beryl, ivory, sapphire, marble, Lebanon cedar — the materials are precious and architectural. He is landscape and structure: as solid as he is beautiful. "This is my beloved, this is my friend" (v.16) — the same word (rēaʿ) used for companion. He is both lover and friend.

Cross-Reference

  • Dan 10:5–6"A man dressed in linen... body like topaz, face like lightning" — the angelic-visitor description uses similar precious-material imagery.
  • Rev 1:13–16The glorified Christ described with gold, white, blazing eyes — the same genre of luminous description.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:10–11

"My beloved is radiant and ruddy, outstanding among ten thousand. His head is purest gold; his hair is wavy and black as a raven." Gold and raven — the contrast of light and dark.

5:12–15

"His eyes are like doves... his cheeks are like beds of spices... his lips like lilies dripping with myrrh... his arms are rods of gold set with topaz... his legs are pillars of marble." Precious materials and natural images combined.

5:16

"His mouth is sweetness itself; he is altogether lovely. This is my beloved, this is my friend, daughters of Jerusalem." The wasf’s conclusion: altogether lovely. And: my friend. The erotic and the companionate are one.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:10–16

Alter: the precious-material imagery (gold, beryl, ivory, sapphire, marble) makes the beloved into an artwork. He is sculpted, ornamented, architectural. The body as temple.

5:16

Alter: "altogether lovely" mirrors "altogether beautiful" (4:7). The mutual wasfs create a symmetry of praise: each sees the other as flawless.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:10–16

Calvin: the male wasf, read allegorically, describes Christ’s glories. Gold = his divine nature. Raven hair = his humanity. Marble pillars = his strength. "Altogether lovely" = the total sufficiency of Christ.

5:16

Calvin: "this is my friend" — Calvin: Christ is not only Lord but friend (John 15:15). The Song’s dual identification is incarnational: the divine beloved is also the intimate companion.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:10

The NET: "radiant and ruddy" combines brightness (health, vitality) with ruddiness (colour, warmth). The description communicates vigour.

5:16

The NET: the final identification — dōdî wə-rēʿî — places the lover in both romantic and friendship categories simultaneously.

Longman Notes

longman
5:10–16

Longman: the male wasf is remarkable for its existence. Ancient love poetry rarely described male beauty in this detail. The Song grants the woman’s gaze the same expressive power as the man’s.

5:16

Longman: "my beloved, my friend" — the two words capture the Song’s ideal: erotic love that is also genuine friendship. Neither alone is sufficient.

Garrett Notes

garrett
5:10–16

Garrett: the male wasf answers the Daughters’ question with overwhelming evidence. He is not merely better than others; he is incomparable. The catalogue overwhelms.

5:16

Garrett: the closing dual identity — beloved AND friend — is the poem’s relational ideal: passion sustained by companionship, desire grounded in knowledge.
