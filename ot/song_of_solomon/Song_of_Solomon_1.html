Song of Solomon 1

The Beloved’s Longing

Verses 1–8 — “Let Him Kiss Me”
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

דּוֹד dōd — beloved / uncle / love: The name David shares this root. Dōd is intimate love — the word for the lover throughout the poem. It carries warmth, physical desire, and committed affection.

נֵרד nērd — nard / spikenard: "My nard sends forth its fragrance" (v.12). Nard is an expensive imported perfume from the Himalayas. The same perfume Mary of Bethany pours on Jesus’ feet (John 12:3). Love and extravagance are inseparable.

Context

The poem opens with the woman’s voice — unusual in ancient literature. She desires her lover’s kisses, praises his name as poured-out perfume, and asks him to take her away. The Daughters of Jerusalem serve as a chorus. She describes herself as "dark but lovely" (v.5) — sun-darkened from vineyard labour, not pampered. Her beauty is working beauty.

Cross-Reference

  • John 12:3Mary anoints Jesus with nard — the same perfume, the same extravagance of love.
  • Eph 5:25–32Paul reads marital love as a picture of Christ and the church — the allegorical tradition rooted in Song.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

"Solomon’s Song of Songs" — the superlative: the greatest song. As "Holy of Holies" means the holiest place, "Song of Songs" means the supreme song.

1:2–4

"Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth" — the woman speaks first. Her desire is uninhibited. "Your love is more delightful than wine" — love intoxicates more than the finest vintage.

1:5–6

"Dark am I, yet lovely." Sun-darkened from vineyard work. She apologises for her appearance but asserts her beauty. Insecurity and confidence in the same breath.

1:7–8

She asks where he grazes his flock — she wants to find him. The Daughters reply: follow the tracks. Love seeks and finds.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:2

Alter: "your love is better than wine" — love and wine are the poem’s twin metaphors for intoxication. Both alter perception; both produce joy.

1:5

Alter: "dark am I, yet lovely" — the conjunction can also be translated "and," making it affirmation rather than concession: dark AND lovely.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

Calvin: the Song celebrates marriage as God’s gift. The church fathers read it allegorically (Christ and the church); Calvin affirms both the literal (marriage) and the spiritual (divine love) readings.

1:2–4

Calvin: the woman’s desire for her beloved mirrors the soul’s desire for God. "Let him kiss me" — the longing for intimate presence, not just knowledge about.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:1

The NET: "Song of Songs" is a superlative construction (like "King of Kings" or "Holy of Holies"). This is the greatest song.

1:5–6

The NET discusses the kedar/Solomon tent wordplay: "dark like the tents of Kedar, like the tent curtains of Solomon" — both are dark but the second is luxurious.

Longman Notes

longman
1:2–4

Longman: the woman’s initiative is remarkable in the ANE context. She is not passive; she pursues. The Song grants women full agency in romantic love.

1:5–6

Longman: "dark but lovely" addresses social prejudice — dark skin from outdoor labour marked lower class. The Beloved reclaims her beauty against the standard.

Garrett Notes

garrett
1:1

Garrett: the title attributes the poem to Solomon but may mean "about Solomon" or "in the Solomonic tradition." The royal setting provides the poem’s backdrop.

1:2–8

Garrett: the opening establishes the dramatic situation: the Beloved longs for the Lover, seeks him, and the Daughters of Jerusalem witness. The poem will alternate voices throughout.

Verses 9–17 — “How Beautiful You Are, My Darling”
91011121314151617

Hebrew Word Study

רַעְיָתִי raʿyātî — my darling / my companion / my friend: "How beautiful you are, my darling (raʿyātî)!" (v.15). Raʿyâ means companion, friend, intimate associate. The lover does not just desire the beloved; he befriends her. The relationship is erotic AND companionate.

Context

The Lover speaks: she is beautiful as a mare among Pharaoh’s chariots (v.9) — a striking image of decorated beauty and controlled power. Her cheeks are adorned, her neck circled. He will make her earrings of gold and silver (vv.10–11). She responds: his love is fragrant as myrrh between her breasts (v.13), as henna blossoms in En Gedi (v.14). The dialogue closes with mutual admiration: you are beautiful; our bed is verdant; our house has cedar beams (vv.15–17).

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 31:10–12"A wife of noble character... her husband has full confidence in her" — the marital partnership the Song dramatises.
  • Rev 19:7–9"The wedding of the Lamb has come" — the eschatological marriage feast that the Song foreshadows.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:9–11

"I liken you to a mare among Pharaoh’s chariot horses." Egyptian chariot horses were magnificent — adorned, powerful, bred for beauty. The comparison is of ornamented splendour.

1:12–14

"While the king was at his table, my perfume spread its fragrance. My beloved is to me a sachet of myrrh resting between my breasts." The fragrance imagery: love is sensory, bodily, aromatic.

1:15–17

"How beautiful you are, my darling! Your eyes are doves. How handsome you are, my beloved! Our bed is verdant." Mutual admiration. Both voices speak. The relationship is reciprocal.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:13–14

Alter: myrrh between the breasts and henna in En Gedi — the fragrance imagery is both erotic and geographic. En Gedi is a desert oasis: love blooms in unexpected places.

1:16–17

Alter: "our bed is verdant" — the outdoors as bedroom. Nature participates in the lovers’ joy. The Song’s ecology is not backdrop but participant.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:15

Calvin: "your eyes are doves" begins a pattern of nature-metaphors for the beloved’s body. Calvin: the comparisons honour the body as God’s creation. Physical beauty is praised, not apologised for.

1:16–17

Calvin: the shared bed under cedar beams is marriage’s legitimate intimacy. Calvin reads the Song as a defence of marital sexuality against both asceticism and licentiousness.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:9

The NET: Pharaoh’s chariot horses were legendary for their beauty and adornment — golden bridles, jewelled trappings. The comparison is to adorned magnificence.

1:13

The NET: "sachet of myrrh resting between my breasts" is intimate imagery. Myrrh was worn as a perfume pouch. The beloved carries her lover’s fragrance on her body.

Longman Notes

longman
1:9

Longman: the mare image is about decorated beauty, not speed or wildness. Pharaoh’s chariot horses were the most lavishly adorned animals in the ANE.

1:15–17

Longman: the bed-and-house imagery shifts from court to pastoral: green bed, cedar beams, fir rafters. Nature is their bedroom. The Song moves freely between urban and rural settings.

Garrett Notes

garrett
1:9–11

Garrett: the comparison to Pharaoh’s horses is a royal compliment: you are as stunning as the finest things in the most powerful kingdom on earth.

1:15–17

Garrett: the mutual dialogue establishes the Song’s pattern: she speaks, he speaks, they speak together. The voices interweave rather than alternate rigidly.
