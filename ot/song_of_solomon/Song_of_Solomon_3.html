Song of Solomon 3

Seeking and Finding; Solomon’s Procession

Verses 1–5 — “I Looked for Him but Did Not Find Him”
12345

Hebrew Word Study

בִקַּשְׁתִּי biqqashtî — I sought / I searched: "On my bed at night I searched (biqqashtî) for the one my heart loves" (v.1). The verb is intensive: thorough, persistent searching. She searched in bed, then in the streets, then among the watchmen. The seeking is both literal (nighttime search) and metaphorical (the soul’s quest for the beloved). The pattern: seek → not find → seek more → find. Love requires pursuit.

Context

The Beloved searches for her Lover at night: in bed (v.1), through the streets (v.2), past the watchmen (v.3). She finds him and will not let go until she brings him to her mother’s house (v.4). The refrain returns: do not awaken love until it desires (v.5). The passage echoes psalm-language ("I sought the LORD and he found me") and anticipates the longer search in chapter 5.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 63:1"You, God, are my God, earnestly I seek you; I thirst for you" — the same seeking language.
  • Luke 15:8–10The woman searching for her lost coin — the same persistence in seeking what is loved.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1–2

"All night long on my bed I looked for the one my heart loves; I looked but did not find him." Love lost is love desperately sought. She rises and searches the city.

3:3–4

"The watchmen found me... scarcely had I passed them when I found the one my heart loves. I held him and would not let him go." The finding is sudden, after persistent seeking. She grips and does not release.

3:5

"Do not arouse or awaken love until it so desires." The second occurrence of the refrain. The pattern: desire → seeking → finding → caution.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:1

Alter: "on my bed at night" may indicate a dream — the search happens in the liminal space between sleep and waking, where desire is most raw.

3:4

Alter: "my mother’s house" — the place of origin, safety, and legitimacy. She brings him to the space where identity was formed.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1–4

Calvin: the allegorical reading: the soul searches for Christ in seasons of spiritual darkness. The finding brings joy; the holding expresses the determination never to lose him again.

3:5

Calvin: the repeated refrain teaches that love cannot be manufactured. It must be waited for, sought, and when found, cherished.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:1

The NET: the fourfold "the one my heart loves" (shennafshî ʾahēbâ) emphasises the depth of emotional attachment.

3:4

The NET: "my mother’s house" (not "my father’s house") is unusual and may emphasise the feminine domestic space.

Longman Notes

longman
3:1–4

Longman: the night search may be a dream sequence or a literary dramatisation of the anxiety of separation. Either way, the emotional truth is real: love fears loss.

3:4

Longman: "I held him and would not let him go" — the intensity of reunion after separation. Love tightens its grip after experiencing absence.

Garrett Notes

garrett
3:1–5

Garrett: the seeking-finding pattern will repeat in 5:2–8 with a darker outcome. Here the search succeeds; later it will involve beating and humiliation. The poem deepens.

Verses 6–11 — Solomon’s Wedding Procession
67891011

Hebrew Word Study

אַפִּרְיוֹן ʾappiryōn — litter / palanquin / sedan chair: "King Solomon made himself a palanquin (ʾappiryōn) from the wood of Lebanon" (v.9). A royal sedan chair carried by bearers — the wedding procession’s centrepiece. It is made of Lebanon cedar, silver posts, gold base, purple cushions, inlaid with love by the Daughters of Jerusalem. The bridegroom arrives in state.

Context

The scene shifts to a royal wedding procession. Something approaches from the wilderness like columns of smoke, perfumed with myrrh and incense (v.6). Sixty warriors surround Solomon’s litter (v.7–8), all armed against "terrors of the night." The carriage is lavishly described (vv.9–10). The chapter closes: "Come out... and look at King Solomon wearing a crown, the crown with which his mother crowned him on the day of his wedding" (v.11). A public celebration of love consummated.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 45:8–9"Your robes are all fragrant with myrrh and aloes and cassia" — the royal wedding psalm.
  • Matt 25:1–10The ten virgins waiting for the bridegroom — the same wedding-procession imagery.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:6

"Who is this coming up from the wilderness like a column of smoke, perfumed with myrrh and incense?" The approach is visually dramatic: smoke columns rising from desert, fragrance announcing arrival.

3:7–8

"Sixty warriors around Solomon’s litter, all experienced in battle, each with his sword at his side, prepared for the terrors of the night." The wedding guard: love is protected, not casual.

3:9–10

"King Solomon made himself a carriage... its posts of silver, its base of gold, its seat of purple, its interior inlaid with love." The carriage is a work of art. The material escalates: wood, silver, gold, purple, love. Love is the finest material.

3:11

"Come out and look at King Solomon wearing a crown... on the day of his wedding, the day his heart rejoiced." The climax: the wedding day is the day of greatest joy. The public celebrates what the private cherishes.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:6

Alter: "columns of smoke" rising from the desert is a theophanic image — God’s presence in the wilderness was a pillar of cloud and fire. The bridegroom arrives with divine associations.

3:11

Alter: the mother crowns the king on his wedding day — not the political coronation but the personal one. Marriage is the truest enthronement.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:6–11

Calvin: the allegorical reading: Christ comes from the wilderness (this world) in glory, surrounded by his saints, to claim his bride (the church). The wedding procession is eschatological.

3:11

Calvin: "the day his heart rejoiced" — Calvin: if Solomon’s heart rejoiced at his wedding, how much more does Christ rejoice over the church? "As a bridegroom rejoices over his bride, so will your God rejoice over you" (Isa 62:5).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:6

The NET: "columns of smoke" (tîmərōṭ ʿāshān) recalls the wilderness pillar (Exod 13:21–22). The bridal procession has exodus overtones.

3:9–10

The NET: ʾappiryōn may be a Persian loanword for a covered litter or sedan chair. The materials — Lebanon cedar, silver, gold, purple — escalate in value.

Longman Notes

longman
3:6–11

Longman: the procession passage may describe a literal royal wedding or may use royal imagery to elevate ordinary lovers. Either way, the Song treats every wedding as royal. Love ennobles.

3:11

Longman: "the day his heart rejoiced" — the wedding day is described from the bridegroom’s perspective. His joy, not his power, is what matters.

Garrett Notes

garrett
3:6–11

Garrett: the procession is the Song’s public moment. The rest is private dialogue; this is civic celebration. Love is both intimate and communal.

3:10

Garrett: "inlaid with love" — the Hebrew is difficult but the sense is clear: love is the decorative principle. The most beautiful thing about the carriage is not gold or silver but love.
