Song of Solomon 4

The Lover’s Praise: “You Are Altogether Beautiful”

Verses 1–8 — The Wasf: Head-to-Toe Praise
12345678

Hebrew Word Study

יָפָה yāfâ — beautiful / lovely: "How beautiful (yāfâ) you are, my darling! Oh, how beautiful!" (v.1). The wasf (Arabic: descriptive praise poem) catalogues the beloved’s beauty from head down: eyes like doves, hair like goats descending Gilead, teeth like shorn sheep, lips like scarlet ribbon, temples like pomegranate, neck like the tower of David, breasts like twin fawns. Each image draws on nature and architecture — the beloved is landscape and fortress both.

Context

The wasf (descriptive praise poem) is a genre known across ANE love poetry. The Lover describes the Beloved from eyes to breasts: each feature is compared to something from nature or architecture. The images are not photographic but evocative — they communicate impression, not anatomy. "You are altogether beautiful, my darling; there is no flaw in you" (v.7) is the summary.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 5:18–19"May your fountain be blessed, and may you rejoice in the wife of your youth" — Proverbs’ marital celebration.
  • Eph 5:27"A radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish" — the church as flawless bride, echoing "no flaw in you."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:1–3

"Your eyes behind your veil are doves. Your hair is like a flock of goats descending Mount Gilead. Your teeth are like a flock of sheep just shorn." Each image communicates an impression: doves = gentleness, goats descending = flowing dark hair, shorn sheep = white evenness.

4:4

"Your neck is like the tower of David, built with courses of stone; on it hang a thousand shields." The neck as fortress: strength, adornment, pride. The beloved is not only beautiful but strong.

4:7

"You are altogether beautiful, my darling; there is no flaw in you." The wasf’s conclusion. Not individual features but the whole: altogether, completely, without defect.

4:8

"Come with me from Lebanon, my bride." The first use of "bride" (kallâ). The relationship has moved from courtship to marriage.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:1–7

Alter: the wasf images work through association, not literal resemblance. Hair like goats = dark, flowing, alive. Teeth like sheep = white, even, paired. The reader experiences impression, not description.

4:7

Alter: "altogether beautiful" — the Hebrew kullāk yāfâ is emphatic: ALL of you is beautiful. Not a checklist of parts but a declaration about the whole.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:7

Calvin: "no flaw in you" — Calvin reads this allegorically as Christ’s verdict on the church: "I have taken away your sin; in my eyes you are perfect." Justification as the lover’s declaration.

4:8

Calvin: "come from Lebanon, my bride" — the call to leave the old life and enter the new. Calvin: conversion is a wedding invitation.

Longman Notes

longman
4:1–7

Longman: the wasf uses nature imagery because nature is the poem’s language for beauty. Hair like goats is not absurd — it evokes flowing dark movement against a green hillside.

4:7

Longman: "no flaw" does not mean physical perfection but the lover’s perception: love sees wholeness. The beloved is complete in the lover’s eyes.

Garrett Notes

garrett
4:1–7

Garrett: the wasf is the poem’s most concentrated praise passage. Each image rewards meditation: goats on Gilead, sheep from washing, pomegranate temples. The density is deliberate.

4:8

Garrett: "my bride" (kallâ) marks the transition. The courtship phase is complete. What follows is the wedding night.

Verses 9–16 — “A Garden Locked, a Spring Enclosed”
910111213141516

Hebrew Word Study

גַן נָעוּל gan nāʿūl — locked garden / enclosed garden: "You are a garden locked (gan nāʿūl), my sister, my bride; you are a spring enclosed, a sealed fountain" (v.12). The locked garden is the poem’s most important image: beauty that is protected, intimacy that is exclusive, sexuality that is reserved. The garden will be opened — but only to the one who holds the key.

Context

The Lover calls the Beloved "my sister, my bride" — combining familial intimacy with erotic love. She is a locked garden, a sealed fountain (v.12) — exclusive, protected. Her garden is full of choice fruits (vv.13–14): pomegranates, henna, nard, saffron, calamus, cinnamon, myrrh, aloes. Then the Beloved responds: "Awake, north wind, and come, south wind! Blow on my garden, that its fragrance may spread. Let my beloved come into his garden" (v.16). The garden is unlocked. The invitation is given.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 2:8–9"The LORD God had planted a garden in the east" — Eden as the first garden. The Song’s garden imagery recalls paradise.
  • Rev 22:1–2"The river of the water of life... and on each side of the river stood the tree of life" — the restored garden at the end of Scripture.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
4:9–11

"You have stolen my heart, my sister, my bride... how much more pleasing is your love than wine." "Sister" adds tenderness to desire. Their love combines erotic passion with familial safety.

4:12

"A garden locked, my sister, my bride; a spring enclosed, a sealed fountain." Exclusivity: this garden opens for one person only. Sexuality is portrayed as a garden — cultivated, beautiful, and bounded.

4:13—14

The garden’s inventory: pomegranates, henna, nard, saffron, calamus, cinnamon, frankincense, myrrh, aloes. Every luxury spice of the ancient world. The beloved is a paradise of sensory delight.

4:16

"Awake, north wind... blow on my garden, that its fragrance may spread. Let my beloved come into his garden." The beloved opens the garden. The invitation is her initiative. The consummation is her choice.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
4:12

Alter: "garden locked" is the poem’s central metaphor. The garden contains everything beautiful and fragrant. Its being locked is not deprivation but anticipation.

4:16

Alter: the winds summoned to spread the garden’s fragrance transform the private garden into something the world can perceive. Love, when consummated, becomes fragrant to others.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
4:12

Calvin: the locked garden represents the exclusivity of the marriage covenant. Calvin: sexual intimacy is a walled garden — beautiful within, protected from without. The walls are not oppression but honour.

4:16

Calvin: the beloved’s invitation is consent freely given. Calvin: the Song teaches that marital intimacy requires mutual desire, not merely obligation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
4:12

The NET: "locked garden" and "sealed fountain" are parallel images of inaccessibility that protects value. Springs in the ANE were precious and often sealed to prevent contamination.

4:13–14

The NET: the spice list includes the most valuable imports of the ancient world — nard from India, saffron from Persia, cinnamon from Sri Lanka. The beloved is a garden of global treasures.

Longman Notes

longman
4:12

Longman: the locked garden is not about virginity as property but about sexuality as a gift that is given, not taken. The lock is the beloved’s agency; the key is her invitation.

4:16

Longman: "let my beloved come into his garden" — she calls it "my garden" but invites him to call it "his." Ownership becomes shared in the act of giving. This is the Song’s theology of marital sexuality: gift, not transaction.

Garrett Notes

garrett
4:12–16

Garrett: the garden sequence is the poem’s climactic movement toward consummation. Garden locked (v.12) → garden described (vv.13–15) → garden opened (v.16). The arc is from exclusivity to invitation.

4:16

Garrett: the Beloved’s initiative in opening the garden is theologically significant: mutual desire, freely expressed. Neither partner is passive.
