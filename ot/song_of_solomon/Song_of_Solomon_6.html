Song of Solomon 6

The Beloved Found; “I Am My Beloved’s”

Verses 1–3 — “Where Has Your Beloved Gone?”
123

Hebrew Word Study

גַנִּים gannîm — gardens: "My beloved has gone down to his garden (gannîm), to the beds of spices" (v.2). The garden returns. After the crisis of chapter 5, the beloved knows where her lover is: in the garden (her). "I am my beloved’s and my beloved is mine" (v.3) — the formula from 2:16 reversed. The shift from "mine/his" to "his/mine" marks growth: from claiming to belonging.

Context

The Daughters ask where the beloved has gone (v.1). She answers: he has gone to his garden. The crisis is resolved not through dramatic reunion but through quiet knowledge: she knows where he is, and she knows she belongs to him. The mutual-belonging formula evolves: "I am my beloved’s" comes first now. She leads with surrender, not possession.

Cross-Reference

  • Song 2:16"My beloved is mine and I am his" — the earlier formula. The reversal marks growth.
  • Gal 2:20"I no longer live, but Christ lives in me" — the surrender-first identity that echoes the reversed formula.

Verses 4–13 — “Beautiful as Tirzah, Lovely as Jerusalem”
45678910111213

Hebrew Word Study

תִרְצָה Tirzâ — Tirzah / beautiful: "You are as beautiful as Tirzah, my darling, lovely as Jerusalem, majestic as troops with banners" (v.4). Tirzah means "beautiful" and was the early capital of the northern kingdom. The beloved is compared to two capital cities — the beauty of both kingdoms combined. And then: "majestic as troops with banners" — her beauty has military force.

Context

The Lover praises the Beloved again: beautiful as Tirzah, lovely as Jerusalem (v.4). He asks her to turn her eyes away because they overwhelm him (v.5). The wasf elements repeat from chapter 4 (hair, teeth, temples). Then a comparison: sixty queens, eighty concubines, virgins without number — but she is unique, the only one, her mother’s favourite (vv.8–9). The Daughters see her and call her blessed (v.9). "Who is this that appears like the dawn, fair as the moon, bright as the sun, majestic as the stars?" (v.10).

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 12:4"A wife of noble character is her husband’s crown" — the same theology of marital honour.
  • Rev 21:2"The Holy City, the new Jerusalem, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed" — Jerusalem as bride, echoing the Song’s city-as-beauty imagery.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
6:4–5

"Beautiful as Tirzah, lovely as Jerusalem, majestic as troops with banners. Turn your eyes from me; they overwhelm me." Her beauty is overpowering. Even the lover needs relief from its intensity.

6:8–9

"Sixty queens, eighty concubines, and virgins beyond number. But my dove, my perfect one, is unique, the only daughter of her mother, the favourite of the one who bore her." Amidst infinite options, she is singular.

6:10

"Who is this that appears like the dawn, fair as the moon, bright as the sun?" She is described in cosmic terms: dawn, moon, sun. The beloved is not just humanly beautiful but celestially radiant.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
6:4

Alter: Tirzah and Jerusalem — two capitals, two kingdoms, two forms of beauty unified in one beloved. The poem is implicitly political: love unites what politics divided.

6:10

Alter: the celestial progression (dawn → moon → sun → stars) creates an ascending scale of luminosity. She becomes brighter with each comparison.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
6:8–9

Calvin: "unique" among queens and concubines — Calvin: the church is Christ’s unique bride. Many religions exist; only one is the true beloved.

6:10

Calvin: the dawn/moon/sun imagery — the church grows in glory as it grows in Christ. "The path of the righteous is like the morning sun, shining ever brighter" (Prov 4:18).

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
6:4

The NET: Tirzah was the northern capital before Samaria. Its name means "pleasant/beautiful" — the comparison works on both levels: city and etymology.

6:10

The NET: the celestial comparisons use three ascending luminaries: dawn (first light), moon (night’s brightest), sun (full radiance). Each is more brilliant than the last.

Longman Notes

longman
6:8–9

Longman: the comparison with queens and concubines makes exclusivity the point. Not "you are the most beautiful" (comparative) but "you are the only one" (absolute). Love singles out.

6:10

Longman: dawn/moon/sun progression moves from dim light to full brightness. The beloved’s beauty increases as the lover describes it, like the sun rising.

Garrett Notes

garrett
6:4

Garrett: Tirzah as a beauty-comparison suggests the poem predates the fall of the northern kingdom (722 BC) or uses archaic traditions. The geographical references ground the poetry in real landscape.

6:10

Garrett: "majestic as the stars in procession" — the astronomical imagery elevates the beloved beyond earthly comparison. She is cosmic.
