Song of Solomon 2

The Lily and the Apple Tree

Verses 1–7 — “I Am a Rose of Sharon”; “Do Not Arouse Love”
1234567

Hebrew Word Study

חֲבַצֶּלֶת ḥăbaṣṣeleṭ — lily / crocus / meadow flower: "I am a rose of Sharon, a lily (ḥăbaṣṣeleṭ) of the valleys" (v.1). Probably a common wildflower, not a cultivated rose. The Beloved’s self-description is modest: I am an ordinary meadow flower. The Lover counters: you are a lily among thorns (v.2). Ordinary to herself, extraordinary to him.

אַהֲבָה ʾahabâ — love: "Do not arouse or awaken love (ʾahabâ) until it so desires" (v.7). The refrain occurs three times (2:7; 3:5; 8:4). Love has its own timing. It cannot be forced or rushed. The Daughters are adjured: let love unfold naturally.

Context

She calls herself a common flower; he elevates her. He is an apple tree among forest trees; she delights in his shade and his fruit is sweet (v.3). She is brought to the banqueting house, and his banner over her is love (v.4). She asks for raisins and apples because she is faint with love (v.5). His left arm under her head, his right arm embraces her (v.6). Then the refrain: do not awaken love prematurely (v.7).

Cross-Reference

  • Hos 2:14–16"I will lead her into the wilderness and speak tenderly to her" — God’s love as courtship, the same imagery.
  • Eph 5:25"Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church" — the NT development of the marital love theology.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:1–2

"I am a rose of Sharon, a lily of the valleys." Modesty. "Like a lily among thorns is my darling among the young women." His counter: you are incomparable.

2:3–4

"Like an apple tree among the trees of the forest is my beloved among the young men." She reciprocates: he stands out. "He brought me to the banqueting house, and his banner over me was love." Love as feast, love as banner — public declaration.

2:6–7

"His left arm is under my head, and his right arm embraces me. Do not arouse or awaken love until it so desires." The embrace is tender. The refrain warns: love must not be hurried.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:3

Alter: "apple tree among the trees" — the only fruit-bearing tree in a wild forest. He nourishes where others merely shade.

2:6–7

Alter: the embrace verse followed by the warning creates a deliberate tension: intimacy described, then patience commanded. The poem models desire controlled by reverence.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:4

Calvin: "his banner over me was love" — love as public declaration, not hidden transaction. Calvin: marriage is meant to be visible, celebrated, and witnessed.

2:7

Calvin: "do not arouse love until it so desires" is the Song’s ethic of timing. Calvin: sexual love within marriage is beautiful; outside its proper time, it is destructive. The refrain teaches patience.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:1

The NET: "rose of Sharon" is probably not a rose but a crocus or meadow saffron — a common wildflower of the Sharon plain. The modesty of the image matters.

2:7

The NET: the adjuration formula "I charge you" uses a solemn oath form. The gazelles may be a euphemism for divine names (by the hosts = ṣəbāʾōṭ).

Longman Notes

longman
2:1–2

Longman: the lily/thorn contrast is the Lover elevating the Beloved’s self-assessment. She sees herself as ordinary; he sees her as singular. Love transforms self-perception.

2:7

Longman: the refrain adjures by "gazelles and does of the field" — animals associated with grace and beauty. The natural world witnesses the oath. Love’s timing is as organic as animal movement.

Garrett Notes

garrett
2:4

Garrett: "his banner over me was love" is a military image domesticated: the battle standard that marks territory. Love claims this space.

2:7

Garrett: the refrain is the poem’s structural marker, dividing major sections. Its repeated warning against premature arousal is practical: love that skips stages collapses.

Verses 8–17 — “Arise, My Darling” — Spring Has Come
891011121314151617

Hebrew Word Study

סְתָו sətāw — winter / rainy season: "See! The winter (sətāw) is past; the rains are over and gone. Flowers appear on the earth; the season of singing has come" (vv.11–12). The spring poem: love arrives with the season. Winter is death; spring is resurrection. The natural world and the emotional world are synchronised.

Context

The Beloved hears her Lover approaching — leaping across mountains, bounding over hills like a gazelle (vv.8–9). He peers through the lattice and calls her out: the winter is past, spring has come, the fig tree forms early fruit, the blossoming vines spread fragrance (vv.10–13). "Arise, my darling, my beautiful one, come with me" (v.10, 13). She responds: "catch for us the foxes, the little foxes that ruin the vineyards" (v.15). Threats to love must be caught early. The chapter closes: "My beloved is mine and I am his" (v.16) — the poem’s central declaration of mutual belonging.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 24:32"When its twigs get tender and its leaves come out, you know that summer is near" — Jesus uses the same fig-tree-as-sign imagery.
  • Rev 22:17"The Spirit and the bride say, ‘Come!’" — the cosmic invitation that echoes the Lover’s "arise, come with me."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
2:8–9

"Listen! My beloved! Look! Here he comes, leaping across the mountains, bounding over the hills." His arrival is energetic, eager, unstoppable. Love does not walk; it leaps.

2:10–13

"Arise, my darling... the winter is past; the rains are over and gone. Flowers appear... the season of singing has come." The spring poem: all creation participates in love’s renewal.

2:15

"Catch for us the foxes, the little foxes that ruin the vineyards." Small threats destroy love. The vineyard (relationship) must be protected from early, seemingly minor, intrusions.

2:16

"My beloved is mine and I am his." The mutual-belonging formula. Repeated in 6:3 and 7:10 with variations. This is the Song’s covenant statement.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
2:8–9

Alter: "leaping across the mountains" — the lover is compared to a gazelle and a young stag. Speed, grace, and eagerness. Love arrives like a wild animal.

2:16

Alter: the mutual-belonging formula is the Song’s emotional centre. All the imagery — flowers, foxes, mountains — serves this declaration: we belong to each other.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
2:10–13

Calvin: the spring poem is often read allegorically as Christ calling the church from spiritual winter to new life. Calvin allows both readings: the literal (lovers in spring) and the spiritual (Christ awakening the soul).

2:16

Calvin: "mine and his" is the language of covenant. Calvin: marriage is a covenant, not merely a contract. The Song provides the emotional content that legal formulations lack.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
2:11–12

The NET: the seasonal markers are specific to Israel’s climate: the winter rains end in March–April, flowers bloom, migrating birds return, figs form. The poem is set in late spring.

2:15

The NET: foxes (or jackals) were known to damage vineyards by digging at roots and eating low-hanging fruit. The image is of small but real threats to something cultivated.

Longman Notes

longman
2:10–13

Longman: the spring poem connects love to creation’s renewal. When love arrives, the world itself comes alive. The Song’s ecology is not merely setting but theology: love participates in creation’s flourishing.

2:15

Longman: the "little foxes" are whatever small things threaten a relationship: neglect, busyness, carelessness. They must be caught while small. Love requires vigilance.

2:16

Longman: "my beloved is mine and I am his" is the Song’s closest parallel to the covenant formula "I will be your God and you will be my people" (Jer 31:33). Mutual belonging is covenant language.

Garrett Notes

garrett
2:8–13

Garrett: the spring poem is the Song’s most lyrical passage. The movement from winter to spring mirrors the movement from longing to consummation. Love and nature synchronise.

2:16

Garrett: the mutual-belonging formula will evolve through the poem: "my beloved is mine and I am his" (2:16) → "I am my beloved’s and my beloved is mine" (6:3) → "I belong to my beloved, and his desire is for me" (7:10). The progression shifts from possession to surrender.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The Beloved
The woman; speaks most of the poem
She initiates, seeks, describes, and declares. Her voice dominates the Song. She is dark from vineyard labour, passionate, uninhibited in expressing desire, and loyal.
The Lover
The man; leaps over mountains to reach her
He praises, pursues, and invites. His speech is full of nature imagery. He sees her as singular — a lily among thorns, incomparable.
Daughters of Jerusalem
The chorus; witnesses and confidantes
They ask questions, receive the refrain’s warning, and provide the audience within the poem for the lovers’ declarations.
