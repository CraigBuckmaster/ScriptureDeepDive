Genesis 48

Jacob Blesses Ephraim and Manasseh

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–12 — The Adoption of Ephraim and Manasseh
1Some time later Joseph was told, "Your father is ill." So he took his two sons Manasseh and Ephraim along with him.2When Jacob was told, "Your son Joseph has come to you," Israel rallied his strength and sat up on the bed.3Jacob said to Joseph, "God Almighty appeared to me at Luz in the land of Canaan, and there he blessed me4and said to me, 'I am going to make you fruitful and increase your numbers. I will make you a community of peoples, and I will give this land as an everlasting possession to you and your descendants after you.'5"Now then, your two sons born to you in Egypt before I came to you here will be reckoned as mine; Ephraim and Manasseh will be mine, just as Reuben and Simeon are mine.6Any children born to you after them will be yours; in the territory they inherit they will be reckoned under the names of their brothers.7As I was returning from Paddan, to my sorrow Rachel died in the land of Canaan while we were still on the way, a little distance from Ephrath. So I buried her there beside the road to Ephrath" (that is, Bethlehem).8When Israel saw the sons of Joseph, he asked, "Who are these?"9"They are the sons God has given me here," Joseph said to his father. Then Israel said, "Bring them to me so I may bless them."10Now Israel's eyes were failing because of old age, and he could hardly see. So Joseph brought his sons close to him, and his father kissed them and embraced them.11Israel said to Joseph, "I never expected to see your face again, and now God has allowed me to see your children too."12Then Joseph removed them from Israel's knees and bowed down with his face to the ground.

Hebrew Word Study

El Shaddai El Shaddai — God Almighty: V.3: Jacob opens his deathbed blessing by recalling the Bethel appearance of El Shaddai. The covenant name grounds the blessing: what Jacob is about to confer on Ephraim and Manasseh is not personal preference but the transmission of divine promise. El Shaddai is the name associated with covenant fertility and land promises (17:1; 28:3; 35:11).

yeled yeled — son / child: Vv.5-6: Jacob's legal adoption formula -- "Ephraim and Manasseh will be mine, just as Reuben and Simeon are mine." The adoption elevates Joseph's sons to tribal status, giving Joseph a double portion (two tribes) without making Joseph himself a tribe. This fulfils Jacob's preference for Joseph while maintaining the twelve-tribe structure.

Historical Context

The blessing of Ephraim and Manasseh is one of the most legally precise scenes in Genesis. Jacob adopts Joseph's two sons as his own — placing them on the same legal level as Reuben and Simeon, his firstborn sons. This adoption elevates Joseph's portion in the inheritance: instead of one tribal portion, Joseph receives two (through Ephraim and Manasseh). The legal act is grounded in a recitation of the covenant promises — El Shaddai's appearance, the land gift, the fruitfulness promise.

Context

The first half of Genesis 48 is the legal preamble to the blessing: Jacob cites his Bethel covenant (vv.3-4), performs the formal adoption of Ephraim and Manasseh (vv.5-6), and pauses to remember Rachel (v.7) -- grounding the whole ceremony in the grief and grace of the patriarchal story. The dim eyes of v.10 prepare for the crossed-hands drama to follow: Jacob cannot see, but his blessing is prophetically precise.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 35:11-12El Shaddai at Bethel: be fruitful and increase; I will give this land to your descendants -- the promise Jacob quotes in vv.3-4. He is transmitting what he received.
  • Gen 41:50-52Manasseh and Ephraim are born to Joseph in Egypt -- the sons now being adopted were born in the land of exile, yet they inherit the covenant of the promised land.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
48:3-4

Jacob recites the El Shaddai covenant (35:9-12) as the ground for his legal action. The covenant promises — fruitfulness, community of peoples, the land — are invoked as the basis for the adoption. The adoption is not a private family arrangement but a covenant act: Jacob is distributing covenant inheritance according to his understanding of God's purposes.

48:5-6

"Ephraim and Manasseh will be mine as Reuben and Simeon are mine" — the adoption formula is precise. Joseph's sons become Jacob's sons at the same legal level as Jacob's firstborn sons. Any further children Joseph has will remain Joseph's (v.6), but the two who already exist are now Jacob's. The legal effect is to give Joseph a double portion — the firstborn's right that had been stripped from Reuben by his sin (35:22; 49:3-4).

48:11

Jacob's reflection — "I never expected to see your face again, and now God has allowed me to see your children too" — is one of the most grateful speeches in Genesis. The man who mourned Joseph as dead for twenty-two years is now blessing Joseph's sons. The providence he could not see while mourning is visible to him now from the vantage point of its completion.

48:5-6

The adoption formula — "they shall be mine" — is paralleled in ancient Near Eastern adoption texts. Sarna: "The language of adoption in Genesis 48 is the legal language of ancient adoption contracts: the adopter names the adoptee as his own son with full legal standing. Jacob is using the full weight of patriarchal legal authority to elevate Joseph's sons to the status of tribes."

Jacob recites the Bethel covenant as the theological basis for the adoption. Sarna: "The recitation is not mere reminiscence — it is legal citation. Jacob is invoking the covenant promises as the warrant for his distribution of covenant inheritance. The adoption is grounded in revelation."

Alter — Literary Reading

48:11

Jacob's expression of gratitude — "I did not expect to see your face; and here God has let me see your offspring too" — is the most personal and theologically reflective speech of his final days. Alter: "Jacob is looking at the entire Joseph cycle from the perspective of its conclusion. What seemed like irreversible catastrophe (Joseph's supposed death) has been reversed so completely that he is now blessing Joseph's sons. The divine overruling of human loss is the content of Jacob's gratitude."

The adoption places Ephraim and Manasseh at the same level as Reuben and Simeon. Alter notes: "Reuben and Simeon are the two sons who will be most diminished in the tribal blessings of chapter 49. By placing Ephraim and Manasseh at their level, Jacob is making a subtle redistribution: Joseph's sons will fill the positions that Reuben and Simeon fail to occupy."

Verses 13–22 — The Crossed Hands; Ephraim Over Manasseh; Return Promised
13And Joseph took both of them, Ephraim on his right toward Israel's left hand and Manasseh on his left toward Israel's right hand, and brought them close to him.14But Israel reached out his right hand and put it on Ephraim's head, though he was the younger, and crossing his arms, he put his left hand on Manasseh's head, even though Manasseh was the firstborn.15Then he blessed Joseph and said: "May the God before whom my fathers Abraham and Isaac walked faithfully, the God who has been my shepherd all my life to this day,16the Angel who has delivered me from all harm -- may he bless these boys. May they be called by my name and the names of my fathers Abraham and Isaac, and may they increase greatly on the earth."17When Joseph saw his father placing his right hand on Ephraim's head he was displeased; so he took hold of his father's hand to move it from Ephraim's head to Manasseh's head.18Joseph said to him, "No, my father, this one is the firstborn; put your right hand on his head."19But his father refused and said, "I know, my son, I know. He too will become a people, and he too will become great. Nevertheless, his younger brother will be greater than he, and his descendants will become a group of nations."20He blessed them that day and said, "In your name will Israel pronounce this blessing: 'May God make you like Ephraim and Manasseh.'" So he put Ephraim ahead of Manasseh.21Then Israel said to Joseph, "I am about to die, but God will be with you and take you back to the land of your fathers.22And to you I give one more ridge of land than to your brothers, the ridge I took from the Amorites with my sword and my bow."

Hebrew Word Study

śiklel siklel — crossed / guided his hands: V.14: Jacob deliberately crosses his hands -- siklel yad means to act with understanding/wisdom, to guide with deliberate intention. This is not accidental confusion by a blind old man; it is prophetic intention. The crossing of the hands is an acted oracle: the younger will be greater than the older.

haRoeh ha-Roeh — the Shepherd: V.15: Jacob describes God as "the God who has been my Shepherd (ha-Roeh) all my life." This is one of the earliest uses of the shepherd metaphor for God in Genesis. The God who has guided Jacob through Laban's deceptions, the wrestling at Jabbok, and the loss of Joseph is the same Shepherd who will guide Ephraim and Manasseh.

Historical Context

The crossed-hands scene is one of the most theologically loaded gestures in Genesis. Joseph places his sons so that Manasseh (the elder) will receive Jacob's right hand (the more honoured blessing) and Ephraim (the younger) will receive the left. Jacob crosses his hands, placing his right on Ephraim and his left on Manasseh. Joseph objects; Jacob insists: "I know, my son, I know. He too will become a people, and he too will be great. Nevertheless, his younger brother will be greater than he." The younger-over-elder pattern that has run through the entire Genesis narrative is enacted one final time.

Context

The crossed-hands blessing (vv.13-20) is the chapter's theological climax. Joseph arranges his sons correctly: Manasseh (firstborn) at Jacob's right hand, Ephraim at his left. Jacob deliberately reverses the hands. Joseph protests; Jacob refuses the correction: "I know, my son, I know." The blessing is not accidental but prophetic -- and history confirms it: Ephraim becomes the dominant northern tribe. The chapter closes with Jacob's final word to Joseph: God will take you back to the land.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 25:23The older will serve the younger -- the pattern of Esau and Jacob is repeated in Manasseh and Ephraim. God consistently reverses human primogeniture to demonstrate that election is his act, not birth's automatic product.
  • Heb 11:21By faith Jacob, when he was dying, blessed each of Joseph's sons, and worshipped as he leaned on the top of his staff -- Hebrews cites this as a paradigm of faith-blessing, acting on what cannot yet be seen.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
48:13-14

Joseph carefully positions the boys — Manasseh at Jacob's right, Ephraim at his left — following birth order. Jacob deliberately crosses his hands. The crossing is intentional: the text says "he guided his hands wisely" (sikkeil et-yadav), the verb sikkeil meaning to act with wisdom or discernment. Jacob is not confused; he is choosing. The gesture enacts the final instance of the Genesis pattern.

48:17-19

Joseph's objection and Jacob's insistence — "I know, my son, I know" — is the most gracious of all the Genesis reversals. Jacob does not fight Joseph; he acknowledges Manasseh's greatness ("he too will become great") while maintaining the crossing ("nevertheless, his younger brother will be greater"). The reversal is maintained without contempt for the passed-over elder.

48:20

The blessing formula — "in your name Israel will pronounce this blessing: May God make you like Ephraim and Manasseh" — establishes Ephraim and Manasseh as the standard of blessing for future generations. The blessing becomes proverbial in Israel: to wish someone the blessing of Ephraim and Manasseh is to wish them the fullness of covenant inheritance.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
48:14

The "crossing of hands" (sikkeil et yadav) has been read by Jewish interpreters as Jacob's inspired spiritual perception rather than a gesture of confusion. Sarna: "The verb sikkeil (from sekel = wisdom, discernment) signals that the crossing is a conscious, purposeful act — not an old man's mistake. Jacob perceives something about these two boys that Joseph does not see. The gesture is prophetic, not accidental."

48:19

Jacob's "I know, my son, I know" is the most patient correction in Genesis. Sarna: "Jacob does not dismiss Joseph's concern — he explicitly acknowledges Manasseh's future greatness. But he insists on the crossing. The double 'I know' signals complete awareness: he knows Joseph's expectation, he knows Manasseh's destiny, and he maintains the reversal nonetheless."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
48:13-14

The blocking of the scene — Joseph positions carefully; Jacob crosses — is one of Genesis's most visually precise moments. Alter: "The spatial arrangement is exact: Manasseh on Jacob's right (Joseph's left), Ephraim on Jacob's left (Joseph's right). Jacob's crossing hands reach across the expected alignment. The gesture is a physical enactment of the theology of divine election that has run through Genesis from the beginning."

48:20

Alter on the blessing formula: "Ephraim and Manasseh are set as the ideal blessing — to be like them is to receive the fullest possible covenant inheritance. The younger-over-elder reversal, which has been a scandal in every generation (Isaac over Ishmael, Jacob over Esau, Joseph over his brothers), here becomes the standard of blessing. What looks like transgression of natural order is in fact the expression of divine generosity."

Ancient Sources

Crossed hands in ancient adoption
The right hand carried the greater honour in ancient Near Eastern blessing and legal ceremonies. Jacob's deliberate crossing was an intentional reversal of birth order, not confusion.
The gesture would have been unmistakable to the original audience.
Ephraim's dominance
In the divided kingdom (930 BC onwards), Ephraim was the most powerful northern tribe and the northern kingdom was sometimes called "Ephraim" (Isa 7:2; Hos 5:3). Jacob's blessing was historically precise.
The fulfilment validates the prophetic nature of the crossing.

Reception History

Hebrews 11:21
By faith Jacob blessed Joseph's sons -- the Hebrews' faith chapter cites this as the paradigm of end-of-life faith. The dying patriarch acts on what he cannot see.
The shepherd metaphor
Jacob's "God who has been my shepherd" became the foundation of the Psalter's shepherd theology, culminating in Psalm 23 and John 10's Good Shepherd.

Textual Notes

48:14שִׂכֵּל אֶת יָדָיו — "he guided his hands wisely"
MT sikkeil et yadav — "he made his hands wise/crossed them deliberately." The verb sikkeil (Hiphil of sakal = to be wise/discerning) is the key word. LXX ἐναλλάξ — "crosswise/alternately." The LXX preserves the idea of deliberate crossing.
The use of the wisdom verb sikkeil for the crossing of hands is the narrator's signal that Jacob's act is not senile confusion but prophetic discernment. The same verb describes the action of a wise person understanding a situation (Ps 14:2; 41:1; Prov 16:20; 21:11). Jacob "wisely crossed" — the deliberateness is the theological point.

Scholarly Debates

The Double-Tribe Status of Joseph: Legal and Theological Significance
Compensation for the firstborn right
Sarna, Hamilton
Reuben forfeited the firstborn's double portion (49:3-4). By adopting Ephraim and Manasseh as equal to his own sons, Jacob effectively gives Joseph the firstborn's double portion — two tribal allotments in the land. 1 Chr 5:1-2 confirms this reading.
Fulfilment of Joseph's destiny
Wenham
Joseph was the beloved son, the heir-apparent. The double portion gives him the inheritance commensurate with his role in the family and the covenant narrative.
1 Chronicles 5:1-2 explicitly confirms the Reuben-to-Joseph firstborn transfer: "the rights of the firstborn were given to the sons of Joseph son of Israel." The adoption of Ephraim and Manasseh is the legal mechanism for this transfer. The two tribal allotments in the land of Canaan (Joshua 16-17) represent Joseph's double portion.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 48 is about the transmission of covenant inheritance against natural expectation. The adoption of Ephraim and Manasseh gives Joseph the double portion of the firstborn. The crossing of hands gives Ephraim the primary blessing over Manasseh. Both acts reverse natural expectations in favour of divine election. The younger-over-elder pattern that has structured Genesis from Abel to Joseph receives its final and most deliberate enactment.
