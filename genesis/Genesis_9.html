Genesis 9

The Noahic Covenant; Noah's Drunkenness

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration.

What prompted it

A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–7 — The Noahic Commission and the Sanctity of Blood
1Then God blessed Noah and his sons, saying to them, "Be fruitful and increase in number and fill the earth.2The fear and dread of you will fall on all the beasts of the earth, and on all the birds in the sky, on every creature that moves along the ground, and on all the fish in the sea; they are given into your hands.3Everything that lives and moves about will be food for you. Just as I gave you the green plants, I now give you everything.4"But you must not eat meat that has its lifeblood still in it.5And for your lifeblood I will surely demand an accounting. I will demand an accounting from every animal. And from each human being, too, I will demand an accounting for the life of another human being.6"Whoever sheds human blood, by humans shall their blood be shed; for in the image of God has God made mankind.7As for you, be fruitful and increase in number; multiply on the earth and increase upon it."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:1–3

The Noahic covenant (vv.1–17) is a universal covenant — not just with Noah and his family but with "every living creature" and "all generations to come" (v.12). God restores the creation mandate (be fruitful, multiply, fill the earth) and expands human dominion to include meat-eating — a concession not present in Genesis 1–2. The fear of humans now falls on animals: the Edenic harmony between humanity and creation is partially broken as a consequence of the fall, and God ratifies the new post-flood order.

9:4–6

"Whoever sheds human blood, by humans shall their blood be shed; for in the image of God has God made mankind." Capital punishment for murder is established not on Mosaic law but on creation order: human life is sacred because every human being is an image-bearer of God. To murder a person is to assault the image of God. This verse is the biblical foundation for the state's authority to execute justice — an authority grounded in the image of God, not in human convention. The Noahic covenant is the origin of civil government as a divinely-ordained institution.

9:13–16

The rainbow (qeshet — the word for a warrior's bow) is God's covenant sign: his weapon of war laid down, pointed away from the earth, toward heaven. This is the most profound image of divine peace in the Old Testament. Every rainbow is a visible renewal of the covenant promise: "Never again will the waters become a flood to destroy all life." God binds himself by his own sign. The rainbow is not primarily for humanity's reassurance but for God's own "remembrance" — it is the sign before which God commits himself to restrain judgment.

Hebrew Word Study

Berit olam — “Everlasting covenant” (v.16). The Noahic covenant is the first formally ratified covenant in Scripture, and uniquely it is made not just with Noah’s family but with “every living creature” — the only biblical covenant that includes animals and the whole earth. It is unconditional: God asks nothing of Noah in return. It rests entirely on divine commitment.


Qeshet — “Rainbow.” The Hebrew word is the same as the word for a warrior’s bow. Some scholars suggest the image is of God hanging up his war-bow in the sky after the flood — a gesture of disarmament, a declaration that the war against human sin will not be prosecuted by cosmic flood again. The bow is pointed not at earth but at heaven, as if God turns his own weapon against himself — a motif that points toward the cross.


Shofekh dam ha-adam ba-adam damo yishafekh — “Whoever sheds human blood, by humans shall their blood be shed” (v.6). The first divine institution of capital punishment, grounded explicitly in the tselem Elohim (image of God). To murder a human is to attack the image-bearer of God — an act of cosmic lese-majesté. The severity of the punishment matches the severity of the offence.


Dam — “Blood” (v.4). The prohibition on eating blood (dam nefesh — “blood of life”) is one of the oldest and most universal biblical laws — pre-Mosaic, pre-Sinai, applicable to all nations. Blood represents life itself, which belongs to God. This is why Leviticus centers so much on blood in sacrifice — life given back to God. The NT reads Christ’s blood as the ultimate fulfilment of this logic.

Historical Context — The Covenant Structure

The Noahic covenant follows the structure of ancient Near Eastern royal grants: a great king makes an unconditional promise to a vassal, confirmed by a visible sign. No obligations are placed on the recipient — the covenant is pure gift. This contrasts with the Mosaic covenant (bilateral — “if you obey, I will bless”) and resembles the Abrahamic and Davidic covenants in its unconditionality.


The permission to eat meat (v.3) represents a significant shift from the original vegetarian diet implied in Genesis 1:29–30. Post-flood human existence is harder and more provisional; God accommodates the new reality. The blood prohibition (v.4) becomes the basis for the kosher laws and is reaffirmed for Gentile believers in Acts 15:20 — one of the few Noahic laws carried into the NT explicitly.

Deep Context — A Universal Covenant

The Noahic covenant is the theological foundation for all of human civilization. It is not made with Israel (who doesn’t exist yet) but with all humanity, all animals, and the earth itself — every subsequent covenant (Abrahamic, Mosaic, Davidic, New) is built on top of this bedrock promise that the world will continue to exist. Without it, none of the others would be possible.


Genesis 9:6’s grounding of human dignity in the Imago Dei is the source of what we now call universal human rights. If all people bear the image of God, then all human lives have intrinsic, inalienable worth that cannot be violated without offense to God. This is the biblical root of the legal and moral traditions that would eventually become the Geneva Conventions, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, and the prohibition on genocide.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:20–21

Noah's drunkenness is not excused or celebrated — it is the first recorded sin of the post-flood world, a sobering reminder that the flood changed the external environment but not the human heart. Wine is not condemned (Scripture treats wine as a good gift when used appropriately) but drunkenness — the loss of self-control — is a moral failure. That the first man to plant a vineyard ends up naked and shamed in his tent is a deliberate echo of Adam and Eve in Genesis 3: the pattern of sin, shame, and exposure repeats in the new world. The flood did not produce a new humanity.

9:25–27

The curse on Canaan has been grotesquely misapplied in history as justification for racial slavery — an application the text never supports. The curse falls on Canaan, ancestor of the Canaanite peoples Israel would later dispossess, not on African peoples. The theological point is the moral trajectory of disrespect for a father, which Canaan perpetuated culturally. The blessing on Shem ("Blessed be the LORD, the God of Shem") is the key theological statement: the covenant name Yahweh is associated with Shem's line — the line through which Abraham, Israel, and ultimately Christ will come. The blessing on Japheth ("may God extend Japheth's territory") points to the future Gentile inclusion in Israel's blessings — fully realised in the New Testament.

Hebrew Word Study

Va-yar Ham et-ervat aviv — “Ham saw his father’s nakedness” (v.22). The phrase ervah (nakedness) is used in Leviticus 18 as a euphemism for sexual violation. Some scholars argue that Ham’s act was more than passive observation — that the phrase implies a sexual violation of his father. The severe curse on Canaan (not Ham himself) would be more proportionate to a serious violation than to simple embarrassment. The text is deliberately oblique. At minimum, Ham failed to cover and honour his father; at maximum, he may have done something far worse.


Arur Kena’an — “Cursed be Canaan.” Noah curses Canaan (Ham’s son), not Ham himself. This puzzles readers but may reflect: (1) Ham already being cursed implicitly through his offspring, (2) the curse functioning as prophetic narrative setting up Israel’s later conflict with Canaan, (3) Canaan being responsible alongside Ham. Critically: this curse has nothing to do with race or skin colour. It is a prophetic statement about the Canaanite nations, which Israel would later conquer (Josh 9:23 uses almost identical language about the Gibeonites).


Yaft Elohim le-Yefet — “May God extend Japheth’s territory” (v.27). A wordplay: yaft (extend/enlarge) and Yefet (Japheth). The three sons’ names are written into their blessings and curses. Noah’s prophetic poem shapes the destiny of three civilizational streams.

Historical Context — The “Curse of Ham” Misuse

This passage was grotesquely misused for centuries to justify African slavery — by falsely equating Ham with dark-skinned Africans and reading the “curse of Ham” as a divine sanction for enslavement. This interpretation is historically and textually wrong on every count: (1) the curse falls on Canaan, not Ham; (2) Canaan’s descendants are Middle Eastern, not African; (3) Ham’s other sons (Cush, Mizraim, Put) represent African and Egyptian peoples who are not under this curse; (4) no ancient interpreter before the medieval period made a racial application; (5) the passage concerns ancient Canaanite peoples, not race in any modern sense. The “curse of Ham” as a racial doctrine is a later fabrication with no exegetical basis.

Deep Context — The Second Adam Fails

Noah was introduced as the man who would bring comfort from the cursed ground (5:29). He survived the flood by righteousness. He built the first altar and received God’s covenant. And then he plants a vineyard, gets drunk, and lies uncovered — and sin re-enters human community through his tent. The pattern is devastating: Adam in a garden with forbidden fruit → Noah in a vineyard with forbidden excess. The second Adam repeats the first Adam’s failure in miniature, and the consequences again fracture family.


This is not an accident. Genesis is making the theological point that the flood did not fix the human heart (as God himself stated in 8:21). A new world needs a new humanity — not just a fresh start but a transformed nature. The Noahic covenant is real and precious, but it cannot complete the work. The story continues past Noah.

Theme Moments This Chapter
Covenant — vv.8–17

Berit olam — the first formally ratified, unconditional, universal covenant. Its scope is all living creatures, not just Israel. Its sign (rainbow) is for God to see, not humanity to perform.

Imago Dei — v.6

The image of God is explicitly affirmed post-fall as the ground of human rights: murder is wrong because it destroys a being who bears God's image. The image survives the fall intact.

Land — vv.18–27

The three sons divide humanity's territory — a proto-distribution of the earth that anticipates the Table of Nations (ch.10) and ultimately the eschatological inheritance.

Kingship — vv.25–27

Noah's blessing/curse on his sons implicitly assigns futures to their descendants — the first proto-royal allocation of dominion. Shem's blessing ('LORD, God of Shem') is covenantal, not merely territorial.

Intertextual Threading

4 canonical threads — 4 forward (passages that echo this chapter)  |  0 backward (texts this chapter echoes)

v.6 — image grounds justice → James 3:9; Gen 1:26 Echo

Gen 9:6's grounding of capital punishment in the tselem Elohim establishes that the image of God in humans is not erased by the fall. James 3:9 invokes the same principle to condemn verbal abuse: 'With the tongue we... curse human beings, who have been made in God's likeness.' The image is inviolable — it grounds both legal protection (Gen 9) and ethical obligation (James 3).

vv.8–17 — Noahic covenant → Jer 33:20–21,25–26; Isa 54:9–10 Echo

Isaiah 54:9–10 explicitly invokes the Noahic covenant: 'As I swore that the waters of Noah would never again cover the earth, so now I have sworn not to be angry with you.' God's unconditional promise to Noah becomes the template for his unconditional promise to Israel after judgment — the covenant with creation is the guarantee of the covenant with the people. Jeremiah 33:20–21 similarly ties God's faithfulness to the fixed order of day and night (the covenant of 8:22).

v.13 — rainbow → Rev 4:3; 10:1 Echo

The rainbow (qeshet) appears twice in Revelation: around God's throne in 4:3 (an emerald rainbow — the warrior's bow hung as a peace-symbol in the divine throne room) and around the head of the mighty angel in 10:1. Both occurrences signal divine presence and covenant faithfulness — the Noahic sign of 'never again' visible at the center of cosmic governance and at the threshold of final judgment.

vv.20–23 — Noah's failure → 1 Cor 10:1–12 Echo

Paul uses a similar typological logic in 1 Cor 10: those who experienced extraordinary divine rescue (the Exodus) nonetheless fell into sin and judgment. Gen 9's Noah — who experienced the ultimate rescue and immediately failed — is the narrative template. Rescue does not guarantee sustained faithfulness. Paul's conclusion ('if you think you are standing firm, be careful that you don't fall') reads as a commentary on Noah's vineyard.

People — Genesis 9

Noah
The Second Adam Who Falls — vv.20–21

Noah plants a vineyard — the first farmer after the flood, as Adam was the first gardener before it. He gets drunk, lies uncovered in his tent. The parallel to Adam is precise: both are in a garden; both are exposed in nakedness; both bring shame into their households; both result in a curse on a son. But Noah's failure is post-covenant, post-rescue, post-divine promise. This is the author's point: even the best human, saved by grace, given a fresh start, cannot solve the problem of human nature. The solution to sin is not a better environment (Eden) or a catastrophic reset (the flood) — it requires something deeper.

Ham / Canaan
The Act and Its Consequence — vv.22–25

Ham 'saw the nakedness of his father' and told his brothers. What exactly this means has been debated: (1) simple voyeurism — he saw and gossiped; (2) sexual violation — some scholars read 'uncover nakedness' as a euphemism for incest (cf. Lev 18's 'uncover the nakedness of your father'); (3) a power-grab — taking a father's concubine established succession (cf. Absalom, Reuben). The curse falls on Canaan, not Ham — which puzzled ancient readers. Most likely explanation: the Canaanites are the people Israel is about to encounter, and this narrative grounds their moral reputation theologically. The curse is not a racial statement — it is a theological characterization of a specific historical people-group at a specific moment.

Shem and Japheth
The Honourable Sons — vv.23,26–27

Shem and Japheth walk backward with a garment to cover Noah — refusing to look, acting with deliberate honor. Their blessing is structured as a poem: Shem receives the most significant blessing (YHWH is 'the God of Shem' — a covenantal designation that anticipates the Abrahamic line); Japheth receives spatial blessing (expansion, dwelling in Shem's tents — possibly indicating future association with Israel's covenant). From Shem comes the Semitic peoples, the line of Abraham, and eventually Jesus (Luke 3:36 traces the genealogy through Shem). Shem's blessing is the thread connecting Noah to Abraham.

NoteThis verse is rendered in Hebrew as a chiasm: shofekh dam ha-adam / ba-adam damo yishafekh — 'one who sheds blood of the human / by the human his blood shall be shed.' The literary mirroring enacts the principle of proportional justice. The grounding ('for God made man in his image') makes this the most theologically weighty capital punishment argument in Scripture — and simultaneously limits it: not all killing, but killing that violates the image of God.

Ancient Source Texts — Covenant Forms

Hittite Suzerainty Treaties (c. 1400–1200 BCE)
These are the words of the Sun, Mursilis, the great king... I have placed you, Duppi-Tessub, under the oath of the Sun... If you, Duppi-Tessub, do not honour the words of this tablet... may these oath gods destroy you.

Hittite treaty forms between a great king (suzerain) and a lesser king (vassal) follow a fixed structure: preamble → historical prologue → stipulations → document clause → divine witnesses → blessings and curses. Biblical scholars (Mendenhall, Kline) identified this structure throughout the OT covenant texts. Genesis 9's Noahic covenant uses a simplified version: God (suzerain) makes a unilateral promise to Noah and creation (vassal), with the rainbow as a sign (document clause). Uniquely, there are no stipulations in the Noahic covenant — it is entirely unconditional.

Egyptian Royal Grants
His Majesty... gives to him a gift consisting of fields... forever and ever, to his children's children, as a thing which is his forever.

The Noahic covenant more closely resembles royal grants than suzerainty treaties: unconditional gifts from a king to a loyal servant, guaranteed in perpetuity to his descendants. This form emphasizes the one-way nature of the commitment — God's promise is not conditioned on human obedience. The everlasting nature (olam) parallels 'forever and ever' in ancient grant language. The Abrahamic and Davidic covenants will follow this same grant pattern.

Reception History — Genesis 9

The Curse of Ham — A History of Misuse

From at least the 7th century CE, certain Islamic texts used a version of the 'curse of Ham' to explain African dark skin as divine punishment. European colonizers from the 16th century onward weaponized this interpretation to justify the transatlantic slave trade, arguing that Africans (identified with Ham/Canaan) were biblically ordained for servitude. This interpretation persisted in American pro-slavery theology through the Civil War. It is now universally rejected by biblical scholars as both textually incoherent (the curse is on Canaan, not Ham; Canaanites are not Africans) and morally catastrophic — a case study in how eisegesis (reading into the text) can produce monstrous results.

The Noahide Laws — Jewish Tradition

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 56b) derives from Genesis 9 seven universal moral laws binding on all humanity: prohibition of idolatry, blasphemy, murder, theft, sexual immorality, eating a limb torn from a living animal, and a positive command to establish legal systems. These are understood as the covenant obligations implied by God's universal covenant with Noah. Rabbinic tradition held that Gentiles who keep these laws have a share in the world to come — making Gen 9 the basis for a remarkably inclusive theology of salvation beyond Israel.

Acts 15 — The Jerusalem Council

The Jerusalem Council's decision (Acts 15:19-20) — that Gentile Christians abstain from food polluted by idols, sexual immorality, meat of strangled animals, and blood — maps closely onto the Noahide laws derived from Genesis 9. James's ruling appears to invoke the Noahic covenant as the universal moral baseline for all peoples regardless of the Mosaic law. This makes Genesis 9 not just prehistory but actively operative in the NT church's definition of Gentile ethical obligation.

Literary Structure — The Covenant Architecture

Genesis 9:1–17 is structured as a formal covenant text with deliberate repetition and chiastic balance:

ABe fruitful and multiply (v.1) — echoes Gen 1:28
BFear of you on animals (v.2)
CYou may eat animals — with blood prohibition (vv.3-4)
DBlood-for-blood principle: Imago Dei (vv.5-6)
★ COVENANT ESTABLISHED (vv.8-11) ★
D'Sign of the covenant: rainbow (v.12)
C'Rainbow in clouds — God sees it (vv.13-15)
B'God remembers his covenant (v.16)
A'Everlasting covenant for all life on earth (v.17)

The Noah narrative then pivots sharply (vv.18-29) from cosmic covenant to domestic failure — the contrast is deliberate. The greatest universal covenant in the primeval history is followed immediately by a family breakdown involving nakedness, shame, and a curse. The author refuses to let the reader settle into optimism: even covenant does not fix the human heart. The solution the reader has been waiting for since Gen 3 is still coming.
