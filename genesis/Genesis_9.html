Genesis 9

The Noahic Covenant; the Table of Ham

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–7 — The Noahic Commission and the Sanctity of Blood
1Then God blessed Noah and his sons, saying to them, "Be fruitful and increase in number and fill the earth.2The fear and dread of you will fall on all the beasts of the earth, and on all the birds in the sky, on every creature that moves along the ground, and on all the fish in the sea; they are given into your hands.3Everything that lives and moves about will be food for you. Just as I gave you the green plants, I now give you everything.4"But you must not eat meat that has its lifeblood still in it.5And for your lifeblood I will surely demand an accounting. I will demand an accounting from every animal. And from each human being, too, I will demand an accounting for the life of another human being.6"Whoever sheds human blood, by humans shall their blood be shed; for in the image of God has God made mankind.7As for you, be fruitful and increase in number; multiply on the earth and increase upon it."

Hebrew Word Study

דַּם הָאָדָם dam haAdam — blood of man: V.6: The prohibition of murder is grounded in the imago Dei — the image of God in humanity. "Whoever sheds human blood, by humans shall their blood be shed; for in the image of God has God made mankind." This is the most explicit statement of the death penalty in Scripture and simultaneously the most theologically grounded: murder attacks the image of God and therefore demands the image-God's response.

בְּשַׂר בְּנַפְשׁוֹ דָמוֹ bisar benafsho damo — flesh with its life-blood in it: V.4: The prohibition of eating blood anticipates the Levitical blood theology. Blood is the life-force (nefesh = life/soul); to eat the blood is to consume the life itself. The distinction between flesh and blood is the foundation of the dietary laws: the flesh is for food, but the blood — the life — belongs to God. This principle will be codified extensively in Leviticus (17:10-14) and Acts 15:29.

Historical Context

The Noahic covenant (vv.8-17) is the most universal covenant in Scripture — it is made with "every living creature" (vv.10,12,15,16,17), not just with Noah or Israel. It is the covenant of common grace: God's commitment to maintain the conditions of life for all creatures until the final consummation. The rainbow sign is a natural phenomenon (light refracting through water) that God appropriates as a covenant sign — the same creative power that sent the flood now signs a covenant of non-destruction.

Context

The Noahic commission (vv.1-7) is the post-flood version of the creation commission (1:28-30). The similarities are deliberate: the blessing (be fruitful and multiply), the dominion over animals, the provision of food. But significant differences appear: fear and dread now characterise the human-animal relationship (v.2), animal flesh is now permitted for food (v.3), and the sanctity of human life requires capital punishment (v.6). The new creation is similar to the first but operates under different conditions.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 1:28The mandate to “be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth” is repeated almost verbatim in 9:1 — Noah receives the Adamic commission fresh, making him a second Adam.
  • Acts 15:20The Jerusalem Council requires Gentile believers to abstain from blood — carrying the Noahic prohibition (Gen 9:4) into the new covenant era as a universal moral law.
  • Isa 54:9–10God swears his covenant faithfulness to Israel “as in the days of Noah” — the Noahic covenant is the template for understanding God’s unbreakable commitments.
  • Rev 4:3A rainbow encircles the throne of God in John’s vision — the covenant sign of Genesis 9 frames the divine throne-room in eternity.
  • Rom 13:4Paul grounds the state’s authority to bear the sword in the divine mandate of justice — rooted in the Noahic institution of Gen 9:6.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:1-3

The Noahic commission deliberately echoes 1:28 — the same blessing, the same mandate to fill the earth. But the conditions have changed: animal fear of humans is now built into creation order (v.2), and animal flesh is now permitted for food (v.3). The new creation is not a perfect restoration of the original but a re-creation adapted to the post-fall, post-flood world. God provides for human sustenance in the conditions that actually exist.

9:6

"Whoever sheds human blood, by humans shall their blood be shed; for in the image of God has God made mankind." This is the theological foundation of capital punishment and of all human justice systems: the sanctity of human life is grounded in the imago Dei, not in social utility. The image of God in every person is the basis for the absolute prohibition of murder and the basis for its ultimate sanction.

9:4

The prohibition of consuming blood is the earliest dietary restriction in Scripture, predating Sinai by centuries. Blood is the carrier of life (nefesh); to consume it is to appropriate something that belongs to God. This principle will be developed extensively in Leviticus (17:10-14) and will be one of the requirements the Jerusalem Council places on Gentile believers (Acts 15:29) — showing that it is not merely a Mosaic regulation but has universal covenantal status.

9:2

The fear and dread of humans that now characterises the animal world is a consequence of the fall and the flood — it was not present in the original creation (2:19-20, where Adam named the animals without any indication of conflict). The new creation maintains human dominion but in a context of broken relationship with the natural world. The peaceable kingdom of Isaiah 11 will be the restoration of what was lost.

9:1

Vayevarekh Elohim et Noach — "God blessed Noah." The same verb and the same structure as 1:28 (God blessed them). Sarna: "The post-flood blessing is the theological resumption of the creation blessing — the flood interrupted but did not cancel God's purposes for humanity. The new world is a new beginning within the same overall divine purpose." The blessing of 1:28 is not voided by the fall or the flood; it is re-issued after each.

9:6

Shofekh dam haadam baadam damo yishafekh — "whoever sheds the blood of man, by man shall his blood be shed." The verse is written in a chiastic structure: A (blood) B (of man) C (by man) B' (his blood) A' (shall be shed). The chiasm reinforces the lex talionis principle: the punishment mirrors the crime in structure as well as content. Sarna: "The chiasm is not merely literary decoration but the visual representation of the reciprocity principle."

9:2

The fear and dread that now characterises animal-human relations is not in the original creation narrative. Alter: "In the garden, the man named the animals; there is no hint of fear or violence. Now, after the fall and the flood, the relationship has fundamentally changed. The animals fear humanity, and human dominion over them includes the permission to kill them for food. The new creation is continuous with the first but has been reconfigured by sin and judgment."

9:6

The prohibition of murder, grounded in the image of God, is one of the most compressed and powerful theological statements in Genesis. Alter: "In four Hebrew words — ki betselem Elohim asah et haadam — the entire case for human dignity is made. Not social utility, not natural rights, not rational capacity: the image of God. Every human person carries a divine imprint that makes their life inviolable."

9:1

The renewal of the blessing and mandate after the flood shows that God's purposes for creation persist through judgment. Calvin: "God, after having purged the world by the flood, again assumed the character of a father towards Noah and his sons, and gave them the same dominion over the earth as he had granted to Adam from the beginning." The covenant is not a new beginning but the continuation of the original covenant after its interruption.

9:6

Calvin on capital punishment: "God, by this law, draws a distinction between the killing of a man and the killing of an ox. He demands an account of the life of man. The reason which he assigns is — because man was made in the image of God... Let men consider how great a crime it is to destroy the image of God, to disturb the order which he has established." The imago Dei is the basis of both the prohibition of murder and the justification for its punishment.

9:4

The NET note on "flesh with its life-blood still in it" (bisar benafsho damo) explains the equation of blood with life (nefesh). The note traces the blood theology forward through Leviticus 17:11,14 and Deuteronomy 12:23, and notes that Acts 15:29's requirement to abstain from blood shows the Jerusalem Council treating this as a pre-Mosaic, universally binding principle rather than a specifically Israelite regulation.

9:6

The NET note on the chiastic structure of v.6 observes that the lex talionis principle (like for like) is the foundation of justice in ancient legal codes. The note also addresses whether this verse establishes capital punishment as a universal divine mandate or as a specific post-flood accommodation. The note observes that the verse is descriptive of the new covenantal order rather than prescriptive of specific legal procedures, though its principle has been widely applied.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:1-7

The Noahic re-commission parallels the creation mandate of 1:28-30 but with significant modifications. Sarna: (1) Animals now fear humans — the pre-fall harmony of creation is replaced by a functional hierarchy maintained by fear. (2) Humans may now eat animals — the original vegetarian diet (1:29-30) is expanded. (3) Blood is prohibited — the life (nefesh) is in the blood (Lev 17:11), and blood must not be consumed. (4) Capital punishment is mandated — murder is an assault on the image of God.

9:6

Shofeich dam haAdam, baAdam damo yishafech — "whoever sheds the blood of man, by man shall his blood be shed." Sarna notes the poetic structure: the sentence is a chiasm in Hebrew — A-B-A-B — shed/blood/man/man/blood/shed. The form mirrors the content: murder creates a reciprocal obligation. The theological grounding — "for in the image of God has God made mankind" — makes murder an assault not just on the victim but on God himself.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:1-3

The resumption of the blessing "be fruitful and multiply" connects the post-flood world to the creation. But Alter notes the modifications: "The new world is a world of fear. The animals will fear humans; humans may eat animals. The vegetarian paradise of Genesis 1-2 is over — the world has been through the flood, and the relationship between humans and animals is different on the other side."

9:6

The capital punishment verse is given in the formal Hebrew legal style: chiastic structure, compressed phrasing, theological grounding. Alter: "The image of God as the basis for the prohibition of murder is the most radical possible grounding for the value of human life. Every human being, regardless of status or merit, bears the divine image — and therefore every human killing is an assault on the image in which God sees himself reflected."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:2-3

Calvin on the post-flood permission to eat animals: "God now permits to men the use of flesh, which he had not done before. The reason was that, since the earth had been purged by the deluge, he thought it good to allow men greater licence." Calvin does not read this as a change in the moral order but a gracious accommodation to the post-fall, post-flood world.

9:6

Capital punishment is divinely mandated here and has never been revoked. Calvin: "We see how God arms the magistrate with the sword for the avenging of slaughter, in order that the sacred image of God should not be violated with impunity. For though God had offered his own image to be trampled upon in the person of men, yet now, to prevent violence, he threatens death to all who shall dare to shed innocent blood." The image of God grounds both the prohibition of murder and the sanction for it.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:3

The NET note on the expanded diet (all moving creatures permitted for food) discusses the transition from the implied vegetarianism of 1:29-30. The note observes this is not a concession to fallen appetites but a new divine grant — God explicitly permits what was not previously permitted. The restriction on blood (v.4) is the single qualification on this permission.

9:6

The NET note on "by man shall his blood be shed" discusses whether this establishes capital punishment as a universal norm (natural law) or merely a post-flood arrangement. The note surveys the NT treatment (Rom 13:1-7; Rev 13:10) and concludes that the image-of-God grounding makes this a universal principle, not culturally relative.

Verses 8–17 — The Rainbow Covenant
8Then God said to Noah and to his sons with him:9"I now establish my covenant with you and with your descendants after you10and with every living creature that was with you—the birds, the livestock and all the wild animals, all those that came out of the ark with you—every living creature on earth.11I establish my covenant with you: Never again will all life be destroyed by the waters of a flood; never again will there be a flood to destroy the earth."12And God said, "This is the sign of the covenant I am making between me and you and every living creature with you, a covenant for all generations to come:13I have set my rainbow in the clouds, and it will be the sign of the covenant between me and the earth.14Whenever I bring clouds over the earth and the rainbow appears in the clouds,15I will remember my covenant between me and you and all living creatures of every kind. Never again will the waters become a flood to destroy all life.16Whenever the rainbow appears in the clouds, I will see it and remember the everlasting covenant between God and all living creatures of every kind on the earth."17So God said to Noah, "This is the sign of the covenant I have established between me and all life on the earth."

Hebrew Word Study

קֶשֶׁת qeshet — bow / rainbow: V.13: Qeshet is the ordinary Hebrew word for a warrior's bow — a weapon of war. God places his war-bow in the clouds as a sign that the war is over: the weapon of divine judgment is laid down. The bow pointed toward heaven (away from earth) is the sign that God has turned his destructive power away. The rainbow is not merely a pretty natural phenomenon but a decommissioned weapon of war.

בְּרִית עוֹלָם berit olam — everlasting covenant: V.16: The covenant with Noah is permanent — berit olam, an eternal covenant. It is the first of several eternal covenants in Scripture (Abrahamic, Mosaic, Davidic, New Covenant). The permanence is the theological point: the flood will never be repeated. God commits himself irrevocably to the conditions necessary for human life to continue.

Historical Context

The rainbow covenant is unique among biblical covenants in being entirely unconditional and entirely universal. There are no human obligations attached — God simply commits himself. The covenant is with "all life" (vv.10,12,15,16,17), not just with humanity. This is the covenant of common grace — the theological term for God's universal provision for all creatures, regardless of their relationship to him. It is the basis for the regular seasons of 8:22 and the conditions for all subsequent human history.

Context

The covenant sign of the rainbow is striking in its direction: it is not a sign for humanity to observe primarily but for God. "Whenever the rainbow appears in the clouds, I will see it and remember the everlasting covenant" (v.16). The covenant sign is God's own reminder to himself. This is theological condescension of the highest order: God accommodates himself to the human need for visible signs, and in the process commits himself publicly and permanently.

Cross-Reference

  • Isa 54:9-10As in the days of Noah when I swore the waters would never again cover the earth, so now I have sworn not to be angry with you — Isaiah uses the Noahic covenant as the basis for God's commitment to Israel after judgment.
  • Rev 4:3A rainbow that shone like an emerald encircled the throne — the rainbow reappears around the divine throne, its covenant significance intact.
  • Ezek 1:28The appearance of a rainbow... was the appearance of the likeness of the glory of the LORD — the rainbow as a theophanic sign.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:8-11

The Noahic covenant is the most universal covenant in Scripture — made with every living creature, not just with humanity. This is common grace in its most formal covenantal expression: God commits himself to maintaining the conditions of life for all creatures. The covenant does not address salvation; it addresses preservation. God maintains the world so that his saving purposes can be accomplished within it.

9:13

The bow (qeshet) placed in the clouds is a warrior's bow set aside. In the ANE, a warrior hanging up his bow was a gesture of the end of hostilities. God places his bow in the sky as a sign that the war against human wickedness — temporarily settled at the flood — will not be repeated in this form. The rainbow is not merely atmospheric beauty; it is the decommissioned weapon of divine judgment, pointed toward heaven as a sign of restraint.

9:15-16

The covenant sign is described as God's own reminder: "I will remember my covenant." The rainbow is not primarily for human benefit (as a reassurance) but for divine covenant memory. This is anthropomorphic language for the permanence of divine commitment: God has written his covenant in the sky so that he himself will always see it. The sign is God's self-imposed reminder of his own promise.

9:13

Qeshet — the war bow — placed in the clouds. Sarna notes the ANE background: in Mesopotamian iconography, the storm god Adad was depicted with a thunderbolt weapon; the storm theophany was regularly associated with divine warfare. By placing the bow in the cloud (the storm cloud), God is specifically disarming the storm — the instrument of the flood — as a weapon of judgment. The rainbow is the sign that the storm-judgment is permanently withheld.

9:16

Berit olam — everlasting/eternal covenant. This is the first use of olam (eternal, forever) in a covenant context. Sarna: "The Noahic covenant is the baseline of all subsequent biblical covenants — the foundation on which the Abrahamic, Sinaitic, and Davidic covenants rest. Without the Noahic guarantee that the world will continue, no subsequent covenant would be possible. The eternal covenant with creation is the presupposition of all covenant history."

9:13-15

The rainbow sign is directed toward God as much as toward humanity. Alter: "'Whenever I bring clouds over the earth and the rainbow appears in the clouds, I will remember my covenant.' The sign is not for Noah to observe but for God to observe. God binds himself to a visible reminder of his own covenant commitment. The gesture is extraordinary — the divine sovereign writing his own obligation into the sky."

9:17

The threefold repetition of "the sign of the covenant between me and all flesh" (vv.12,13,17) is the covenant's seal. Alter notes the structural parallel with the threefold "very good" of 1:31 — both are the concluding affirmations of completed acts of divine purpose. The covenant is sealed with the same certainty as the original creation's goodness.

9:13

Calvin on the rainbow: "God did not create the rainbow at this time, for it is a natural effect of the sun shining on the clouds. But God gives it a new significance: from this time forward it shall be a testimony and pledge of his favour and of the covenant which he is making." The sign is not a new creation but a new meaning attached to an existing phenomenon — the same principle as circumcision and the Sabbath.

9:16

Calvin on the everlasting covenant: "God declares that he will remember his covenant whenever he sees the rainbow. Not that God is subject to forgetfulness — but he accommodates himself to our weakness, promising that, as often as we see the bow, we may know that the sign is before his eyes as well as ours, so that he may fulfil his promise."

9:13

The NET note on qeshet discusses the "war bow" interpretation — noting that in Habakkuk 3:9-11 the divine bow appears in the context of storm and battle. The placement of the bow in the cloud could be read as God laying down his battle weapon as a sign of armistice. The note acknowledges this interpretation alongside the simpler "rainbow as natural phenomenon now given covenant significance."

9:16

The NET note on berit olam ("everlasting covenant") notes this is the first of several uses of olam in a covenant context in Genesis and the OT. The note traces the term through the Abrahamic (17:7,13,19), Mosaic (Exod 31:16), and Davidic (2 Sam 23:5) covenants, and observes that the Noahic covenant is unique in being with "all living creatures" rather than with a specific people.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:8-17

The Noahic covenant is the first universal covenant in Scripture — it encompasses Noah, his descendants, and every living creature. Sarna notes three features: (1) It is entirely unilateral — God commits without requiring anything in return (contrast the Mosaic covenant's bilateral structure). (2) It is universal — all flesh, not just Israel. (3) It is everlasting (berith olam). The rainbow (qeshet) is the covenant sign — a weapon of war hung in the sky as a sign of peace.

9:13

The rainbow as covenant sign: Sarna observes that in ancient Near Eastern imagery, the bow (qeshet) was God's weapon. The storm and the divine bow were associated — the storm god wielded the bow-weapon. By hanging the bow in the cloud, God is literally "retiring" his weapon of judgment, placing it in the sky as a sign that the war against creation is over. The rainbow is a disarmed weapon of divine peace.

9:9-11

The threefold repetition of "never again" in vv.11,15 is the structural spine of the covenant. Alter: "The covenant is an unconditional divine promise — the emphatic repetition of 'never again' reflects the gravity of what is being promised. Alter: "The rainbow is not merely for human reassurance — it is God's own reminder. Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:12-13

The rainbow as covenant sign accommodates divine condescension to human weakness. Calvin: "Although the Lord in setting his bow in the clouds, accommodates himself to the capacity of the human mind, yet there is nothing absurd in his using as signs those things which he has already created." The rainbow was already a natural phenomenon; God infuses it with covenantal meaning, turning a meteorological event into a theological sign.

9:13

The NET note on qeshet (rainbow/bow) discusses the double meaning: a bow as a weapon and a rainbow as a natural phenomenon. Verses 18–29 — Noah's Drunkenness; the Curse of Canaan
18The sons of Noah who came out of the ark were Shem, Ham and Japheth. (Ham was the father of Canaan.)19These were the three sons of Noah, and from them came the people who were scattered over the whole earth.20Noah, a man of the soil, proceeded to plant a vineyard.21When he drank some of its wine, he became drunk and lay uncovered inside his tent.22Ham, the father of Canaan, saw his father naked and told his two brothers outside.23But Shem and Japheth took a garment and laid it across their shoulders; then they walked in backward and covered their father's naked body. Their faces were turned the other way so that they would not see their father naked.24When Noah awoke from his wine and found out what his youngest son had done to him,25he said, "Cursed be Canaan! The lowest of slaves will he be to his brothers."26He also said, "Praise be to the Lord, the God of Shem! May Canaan be the slave of Shem.27May God extend Japheth's territory; may Japheth live in the tents of Shem, and may Canaan be the slave of Japheth."28After the flood Noah lived 350 years.29Noah lived a total of 950 years, and then he died.

Hebrew Word Study

וַיִּשְׁתְּ מִן הַיַּיִן wayyeshte min hayyayin — he drank of the wine: V.21: This is the first record of viticulture and drunkenness in the Bible. The man who survived the flood — the righteous preacher of 2 Pet 2:5 — becomes drunk in his tent. The fall pattern repeats: nakedness, shame, covering. The new Adam fails as the first Adam did. The narrative does not condemn Noah explicitly but the parallels to chapter 3 are unmistakable.

עֶבֶד עֲבָדִים eved avadim — servant of servants: V.25: The strongest form of the superlative in Hebrew — the most abject kind of servitude. The curse on Canaan is one of the most misused texts in the Bible's history of interpretation: it was used to justify African slavery by falsely claiming that Africans descended from Ham. The text says nothing about race or skin colour. The curse is on Canaan specifically, not on Ham, and its application is to the Canaanite peoples of the land of Canaan in relation to Israel.

Historical Context

The "curse of Ham" (actually the curse on Canaan) was notoriously used to justify African slavery from the 17th-19th centuries, based on the racist identification of Ham with dark-skinned Africans. This interpretation has no basis in the text: the curse falls on Canaan (not Ham), concerns Canaanite-Israelite relations (not African-European), and involves no reference to skin colour or race. It is one of the most egregious misreadings of Scripture in history.

Context

The drunkenness of Noah is a sobering narrative placed immediately after the greatest covenant in the primeval history. The covenant with all creation is followed by the failure of the covenant's representative. The pattern is consistent: Adam and Eve in the garden, Cain after the fall, the generation before the flood, and now Noah after the flood. Every fresh start in Genesis is followed quickly by fresh failure. The narrative insists that no human covenant partner is adequate — the covenant requires a different kind of mediator.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 3:7Their eyes were opened and they saw they were naked — the parallel between Noah's nakedness and Adam's.
  • Lev 18:6-18The Levitical laws against uncovering nakedness — the legal codification of the shame Ham violated.
  • 1 Cor 10:12Let anyone who thinks he stands take heed lest he fall — the principle Noah's failure illustrates.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
9:20-21

The drunkenness of Noah is the first failure of the post-flood world's covenant representative — and it comes almost immediately. The pattern of Genesis is consistent: after every new beginning (creation, post-flood), the human covenant partner fails. This is not incidental but theological: the narrative is building the case that no human mediator is sufficient. The new creation needs a better Noah — a last Adam who will not fail.

9:22-23

Ham's sin is not specified precisely but involves the uncovering/exposure of his father's nakedness. The severity of the response (a generational curse) suggests more than mere observation — the text may imply a deliberate violation of filial honour or something more serious. Shem and Japheth's response — walking backward to cover Noah without seeing his nakedness — is the model of honouring a parent's dignity even in shameful circumstances.

9:25-27

The three-part blessing and curse establish the framework for the Table of Nations in chapter 10: Canaan will serve Shem and Japheth; Japheth will be enlarged and dwell in Shem's tents; Shem is blessed specifically in relation to "the LORD" (v.26 — "Blessed be the LORD, the God of Shem"). Shem's line is the covenant line through which the promised seed will come: from Shem to Abraham (11:10-26), and from Abraham to the Messiah.

9:29

Noah's death at 950 years closes the primeval history's most important figure. He was born into the world of the fall, survived its catastrophic judgment, received the first formal covenant, failed as its representative — and died. The refrain of chapter 5 returns: "and he died." Even the flood survivor, even the covenant partner, dies. The mortality of the fall is not reversed by the covenant; it awaits the resurrection.

9:20-21

The mention of Noah as "a man of the soil" (ish haadamah) and his planting of a vineyard connects him to the Cain/Abel narrative: Cain was also "a worker of the ground" (4:2, oved adamah). Both lines of humanity — the Sethite line through Noah and the Cainite line — produce people defined by their relationship to the earth. And both lines produce failures. Sarna: "The post-flood world recapitulates the pre-flood world with startling speed."

9:25

The curse falls on Canaan, not on Ham. Sarna: "This is significant: the literary function of the story is etiological — to explain the subordinate status of the Canaanites in relation to Israel. The curse has nothing to do with African peoples or skin colour. It is a theological explanation of the Canaanite-Israelite relationship, and of the divine sanction for Israel's occupation of Canaan."

9:20-23

The parallel to Genesis 3 is carefully constructed. Alter: "Nakedness, shame, covering — the vocabulary of the fall reappears. In chapter 3, the man and woman's eyes were opened and they saw they were naked; they made coverings. Here, Noah's nakedness is seen by Ham and covered by his brothers. The new Adam's first major narrative after the covenant is a fall-narrative."

9:26

The blessing on Shem — "Blessed be the LORD, the God of Shem" — is unusual: God is blessed, not Shem. Alter: "The formula does not say 'Blessed be Shem' but 'Blessed be the LORD, the God of Shem.' The blessing of Shem is mediated through his relationship to YHWH. Shem's greatness is derivative of his God's greatness — the covenantal logic that will run through the entire patriarchal history."

9:20-21

Calvin on Noah's drunkenness: "Let no man be proud of his own steadfastness since this excellent man — who is called 'righteous' and 'perfect in his generations' and 'the friend of God' — through incaution fell shamefully, and brought upon himself this disgrace. This, then, is the use of the present narrative: God would have us remember our infirmity, that we may cultivate sobriety and caution."

9:25-27

Calvin on the curse of Canaan: "It may be asked, why Noah imprecates a curse upon his nephew who had not offended? I answer: because Canaan was the son of Ham, and the punishment was extended to him, partly to avenge the injury of Noah more severely, and partly to render the disgrace of Ham more conspicuous." Calvin understands the generational curse as an extension of the father's judgment, not as arbitrary discrimination against the son.

9:22

The NET note on what Ham did ("saw the nakedness of his father") discusses various interpretive options for why the punishment was so severe. The note surveys proposals: (1) mere observation was considered taboo; (2) Ham committed a sexual act (cf. Lev 18:6-18 where "uncover the nakedness" often means sexual violation); (3) Ham castrated Noah (a Talmudic tradition). The note finds the text ambiguous but notes that the severity of the curse suggests more than mere observation.

9:25

The NET note on the "curse of Canaan" explicitly addresses the history of misuse. The note states clearly that the text does not mention race or skin colour, and that the curse applies to the Canaanites in relation to Israel — not to any modern ethnic group. The note identifies this as one of the most historically misapplied texts in Scripture.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:20-29

The Noah-Ham episode is one of the most debated passages in Genesis. Sarna surveys the main interpretive options for "saw his father's nakedness": (1) literally saw Noah naked; (2) a sexual act (supported by the Leviticus usage of "uncover nakedness"); (3) castration (a rabbinic reading). Sarna notes that whatever the act, Ham's sin was publicising his father's shame rather than covering it. The contrast with Shem and Japheth — walking backward to avoid seeing — shows the proper response.

9:25-27

The curse of Canaan (not Ham) has been the most misused verse in biblical history — wrongly applied to justify African slavery for centuries. Sarna is emphatic: "The curse falls on Canaan, not on Ham, and certainly not on African peoples. The Table of Nations in chapter 10 locates Canaan in the ancient Near East. The curse is a historical prophecy about the Canaanite nations whom Israel will encounter and displace — it has no ethnic application beyond that context."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
9:21-23

The narrative economy of the Ham episode is striking. Alter: "Noah is drunk, naked, exposed — a fallen figure, diminished from the man who walked with God and built the ark. Ham sees and tells his brothers. Shem and Japheth walk backward, covering their father. The contrasting actions define two moral stances: Ham the voyeur and publiciser of shame; Shem and Japheth the preservers of dignity."

9:26-27

Shem's blessing ("Blessed be the LORD, the God of Shem") is the first instance of blessing directed at a God named in connection with a specific people. Alter: "The blessing of Shem is the theological seed of the Abrahamic narrative that follows. Shem is the ancestor of Abraham; the LORD who will be Abraham's God is here first named as 'the God of Shem.' The primeval history ends by pointing toward the patriarchal history."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
9:22-23

Calvin on Ham: "Ham, by bringing his father into derision, violated the most sacred bond of nature. He not only failed to cover the fault of his father, but wished it to be noticed by his brethren." The sin is not seeing but publicising — gossip and dishonour within the family. The covering by Shem and Japheth is the model of godly behaviour: they protect dignity rather than exploit shame.

9:25

Calvin on the curse of Canaan: the curse is a prophetic declaration about the historical role of the Canaanite peoples — their subjugation to Israel as God's judgment. Calvin explicitly rejects any racial application: "This is not a curse upon the whole posterity of Ham, but only upon the Canaanites." The misuse of this verse to justify slavery was, in Calvin's reading, a straightforward misreading of the text.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
9:22

The NET note on "saw his father's nakedness" discusses the interpretive options (accidental viewing vs. sexual act) and concludes that the ambiguity is intentional — the text is deliberately understated about what exactly Ham did. The note observes that the phrase "uncovering nakedness" in Leviticus is a euphemism for sexual sin, but that the context here does not require that reading. The sin may simply be the failure to honour a parent.

9:25-27

The NET note is explicit about the misuse of the Canaan curse: it applies to the Canaanite peoples of the second millennium BC whom Israel displaced, not to any modern ethnic group. The note provides extensive cross-references to the conquest narratives of Joshua, where the subjugation of Canaan is the historical fulfilment of this curse. The note condemns the historical use of this verse to justify racial hierarchy.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Noah
Covenant mediator of the restored world; first to fail it
The Noahic covenant establishes Noah as the mediator of the new creation — recipient of the most universal covenant in Scripture. His subsequent drunkenness and the shame it exposes follows the pattern of every biblical covenant representative: the human mediator fails, pointing forward to the need for a perfect covenant keeper. He is simultaneously the greatest figure of the primeval history and a demonstration of its limits.
Shem
Father of the Semitic line; bearer of the covenant blessing
Noah's blessing on Shem — "Blessed be the LORD, the God of Shem" — identifies the Shemite line as the covenant line. From Shem to Abraham (11:10-26) traces the genealogy of the promised seed. Shem's honour of his father contrasts with Ham's violation; his covenant line will run through the entire patriarchal history to the Messiah.
Ham and Canaan
Father of the Canaanite nations; his line under judgment
Ham's sin of dishonoring his father is visited on his son Canaan — establishing the theological basis for the Israelite occupation of Canaan. The Canaanites become the representative people of the kind of moral corruption that characterises the pre-flood world: the curse of Canaan is the theological counterpart of the judgment that preceded the flood.

Ancient Sources

Ancient Near Eastern Covenant Meals
In the ANE, covenants were typically ratified by a shared meal — the two parties eating together as a sign of mutual commitment. Noah's sacrifice and God's acceptance of the pleasing aroma (8:20-21) functions as the covenant meal of the Noahic covenant: God and his covenant partner share in the offering. The rainbow covenant formalises what the sacrifice established.
The ANE covenant-meal parallel illuminates the structure of the flood narrative: sacrifice (8:20-21) → covenant promise (8:22) → formal covenant ratification with sign (9:8-17). The pattern is followed in the Abrahamic covenant (15:9-21) and the Sinai covenant (24:9-11).
The Ugaritic Baal Cycle and the Warrior's Bow
In Canaanite mythology, the storm god Baal defeats the sea god Yam with a pair of magical clubs. The storm god's weapons are the instruments of cosmic victory. The placement of the divine bow in the clouds after the flood has been compared to the warrior god hanging up his weapons after victory.
The comparison illuminates the ANE background of qeshet (bow) in a storm-theophany context, but inverts the mythology: in the Baal Cycle, the storm god wins; in Genesis, the same God who controlled the storm now disarms himself as a sign of covenant peace. The Genesis account is not dependent on the Baal mythology but engages its imagery deliberately.

Reception History

Origin of Christian Covenant Theology
The Noahic covenant became the foundation for the Reformed doctrine of covenant theology, which reads all of Scripture through the lens of successive covenants (creation, Noahic, Abrahamic, Mosaic, Davidic, new). The Noahic covenant functions in this system as the covenant of "common grace" — God's universal provision for all humanity that creates the conditions within which the covenant of redemption can operate.
Thomas Aquinas, Summa Theologica, I-II.q.98-105 (c.1273)
Aquinas discusses the natural law basis of the Noahic covenant's prohibitions (blood-eating, murder) — arguing these are expressions of natural law knowable to all humans, codified in the Noahic covenant before the Mosaic law. The Noahic covenant is thus the basis for Aquinas's claim that moral obligations are universal, not limited to Israel.
Abraham Kuyper, Common Grace (1902-1905)
Kuyper's multi-volume work develops the theological concept of common grace rooted in the Noahic covenant. God's commitment to maintain the world for all people, regardless of their relationship to him, is the basis for human culture, science, art, and civilisation. The Noahic covenant is not merely about survival — it is the covenantal foundation for the goodness of creation order that continues to operate in a fallen world.

Textual Notes

9:4blood prohibition — dietary or symbolic?
MT Ach basar b'nafsho damo lo tochlu — "but flesh with its life, that is, its blood, you shall not eat." The life (nefesh) is in the blood. This prohibition is prior to the Mosaic law (Lev 17:11-14) and is included in the Jerusalem Council's ruling for Gentiles (Acts 15:20,29) as a universal creation ordinance. Some (Wenham) read this as symbolic: life belongs to God and must be returned to him via the altar.
The blood prohibition as a pre-Mosaic creation ordinance is significant for the continuity between the Noahic and Mosaic covenants and for the Acts 15 ruling's theological basis.
9:6in the image of God he made man
MT The image of God (tselem) reappears here as the basis for the death penalty for murder. The image is assumed to survive the fall — humanity after the flood still bears God's image, though the flood was precipitated by human wickedness. The image is damaged but not destroyed.
Critical for understanding the post-fall human condition and for legal-ethical reasoning about capital punishment, human dignity, and the indestructibility of the divine image.
9:20-27the curse of Canaan — racial application?
MT Noah curses Canaan (not Ham) after Ham's offense. The Hebrew is specific: it is Canaan who is cursed, not all Hamites. The historical application: the Canaanite nations are under this curse, providing theological justification for Israel's conquest of Canaan.
The curse of Canaan was grotesquely misapplied for centuries to justify African slavery (claiming Africans = sons of Ham = under the curse). This is exegetically unjustifiable: (1) Canaan, not Ham, is cursed; (2) even if Ham were cursed, the curse applies to his son Canaan, not all his descendants; (3) Africans are not primarily descended from Canaan.

Scholarly Debates

Ham's Sin: What Did He Do?
Observed nakedness only
Calvin, most traditional interpreters
Ham saw his father's nakedness (a shameful sight in ancient Near Eastern culture) and reported it to his brothers with evident delight rather than covering it respectfully as Shem and Japheth did. The sin is disrespect for paternal dignity.
Castration
Some rabbinic sources
Ham castrated Noah while he slept — which is why Noah says "Canaan will be the lowest of slaves" (depriving Canaan of position). The evidence: Noah's response implies something more than voyeurism.
Incest with Noah's wife
Bassett, Carmichael
"Uncovering a father's nakedness" is a euphemism for sexual relations with the father's wife (cf. Lev 18:7-8). Ham slept with Noah's wife and Canaan was the offspring.
Ritual humiliation
Sarna, Hamilton
Ham's act had ritual or cultural dimensions — mocking the father who lay naked was a formal act of disrespect with social/legal implications in ancient Near Eastern contexts.
The observational reading remains the most widely held but the least dramatically motivated. Castration or sexual humiliation readings explain the severity of the response. No consensus; the text is intentionally brief about the nature of the act.
The Curse of Canaan: Prophecy or Justification?
Prophetic
Traditional
Noah speaks as a prophet, pronouncing the future destiny of the three major ethnic divisions of humanity. The cursed servitude of Canaan will be fulfilled in Israel's conquest under Joshua.
Etiological
von Rad, Westermann, Brueggemann
The story explains (provides an etiology for) why Canaanites are subordinate to Israelites — a backward-looking justification written during or after the conquest period.
Both
Wenham, Waltke
The prophecy is historically grounded and forward-looking while also providing a theological framework for understanding Israel's later relationship with Canaan.
The etiological reading explains the Canaanite curse's placement in the narrative. The prophetic reading takes the text at face value. Most scholars accept both dimensions: real historical consequence embedded in the narrative with later theological reflection.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 9 is the covenantal capstone of the flood narrative and a theological pivot for all subsequent Scripture. The Noahic covenant establishes the universal conditions for human life; the imago Dei grounds both justice and dignity; the rainbow sign is the most visible covenant sign in creation. But the chapter ends with Noah's failure — the consistent Genesis pattern of covenant hope followed by human inadequacy. The new creation, like the first, cannot be sustained by its human representative alone.
