Covenant of Circumcision

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration.

What prompted it

A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–8 — The Covenant Renewed; Abram Becomes Abraham
1When Abram was ninety-nine years old, the Lord appeared to him and said, "I am God Almighty; walk before me faithfully and be blameless.2Then I will make my covenant between me and you and will greatly increase your numbers."3Abram fell facedown, and God said to him,4"As for me, this is my covenant with you: You will be the father of many nations.5No longer will you be called Abram; your name will be Abraham, for I have made you a father of many nations.6I will make you very fruitful; I will make nations of you, and kings will come from you.7I will establish my covenant as an everlasting covenant between me and you and your descendants after you for the generations to come, to be your God and the God of your descendants after you.8The whole land of Canaan, where you now reside as a foreigner, I will give as an everlasting possession to you and your descendants after you; and I will be their God."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
17:1

Thirteen years pass between 16:16 and 17:1 — thirteen years of silence from God. Then God appears and introduces himself as El Shaddai (God Almighty — the all-sufficient one, the one who is more than enough). The name El Shaddai is connected with the patriarchal period: it is the name by which God specifically identified himself to the patriarchs (Exod 6:3). The command "walk before me faithfully and be blameless" does not mean sinless perfection but wholehearted, undivided devotion — the same word (tamim) used of Noah in 6:9. The covenant response to God's sufficiency is total life-orientation toward him.

17:5

The name change from Abram ("exalted father") to Abraham ("father of a multitude") is a covenant act — God writes his promise into Abram's identity. Every time his new name is spoken, the promise is proclaimed. The name change is a declaration of faith: Abraham will be called "father of a multitude" for 25 years before Isaac is born, before he has a single son by Sarah. He must confess his faith every time he introduces himself. The same pattern: Simon becomes Peter (the rock) before he shows any stability; Saul becomes Paul at his conversion.

Hebrew Word Study

אֵל שַׁדַּי ʾēl šadday — God Almighty: El Shaddai — God's self-revelation to the patriarchs (Exod 6:3 says YHWH was not known by this name to Abraham). The exact meaning is debated: "God of the mountains" (shadday from shaddu = mountain), "God the Sufficient/Almighty," or possibly "God of the breast" (shad). Most likely emphasizes inexhaustible power and provision.

אַבְרָהָם ʾaḇrāhām — Abraham: The name change from Abram ("exalted father") to Abraham is given a folk etymology: "father of a multitude of nations" (ʾaḇ + hāmôn gôyîm). The extra syllable "ha" phonetically evokes the Hebrew for "multitude." The name becomes a daily declaration of faith — every time someone calls his name, they affirm the impossible promise.

הִתְהַלֵּךְ hithallek — walk before me: The Hitpael reflexive of hālak — "walk back and forth, walk continuously before me." Not a single act but a lifestyle of divine presence. The same form used of Enoch who "walked with God" (5:22). A call to sustained, integrated faithfulness.

Context

The name change from Abram to Abraham is one of the most significant acts in Genesis. In the ancient world, names declared identity and destiny. Giving someone a new name meant fundamentally redefining who they were. Abraham will hear his new name spoken thousands of times — each utterance is a repetition of the impossible promise. God essentially builds the covenant promise into Abraham's daily identity.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 4:16–18Paul quotes "father of many nations" (Gen 17:5) as the basis for Abraham being the spiritual father of both Jews and Gentiles who share his faith.
  • Exod 6:3God tells Moses he appeared to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob as El Shaddai — but his name YHWH was not yet fully known. Genesis 17 is one of those El Shaddai appearances.
Verses 9–14 — The Sign of the Covenant: Circumcision
9Then God said to Abraham, "As for you, you must keep my covenant, you and your descendants after you for the generations to come.10This is my covenant with you and your descendants after you, the covenant you are to keep: Every male among you shall be circumcised.11You are to undergo circumcision, and it will be the sign of the covenant between me and you.12For the generations to come every male among you who is eight days old must be circumcised, including those born in your household or bought with money from a foreigner—those who are not your offspring.13Whether born in your household or bought with your money, they must be circumcised. My covenant in your flesh is to be an everlasting covenant.14Any uncircumcised male, who has not been circumcised in the flesh, will be cut off from his people; he has broken my covenant."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
17:9–11

Circumcision as the covenant sign is not arbitrary. It is a sign in the organ of procreation — the physical embodiment of the promise of descendants. Every birth in the covenant line is marked: the offspring of the promise carry the sign of the promise on their bodies. It is a sign of death and new life (the cutting away of flesh, the flowing of blood) that points forward to the circumcision made without hands (Col 2:11) — the cutting away of the sinful nature through union with Christ in his death and resurrection. The sign does not save; it marks membership in the covenant community and calls the recipient to the faith the sign represents.

17:14

"Any uncircumcised male, who has not been circumcised in the flesh, will be cut off from his people; he has broken my covenant." The severity of the requirement underlines the seriousness of covenant membership. Circumcision was not an optional enhancement to one's religious life; it was the mark of belonging to the people of God. To refuse it was to declare oneself outside the covenant. The parallel in the New Testament is baptism: the sign of covenant membership, marking entrance into the community of the new covenant.

Hebrew Word Study

מִילָה mîlāh — circumcision: From the root mûl, to circumcise. The physical sign of covenant identity, performed on the male organ of generation — the very body part involved in producing the promised offspring. The sign is placed at the point of promise.

אוֹת ʾôt — sign: The covenant sign functions as a memorial and reminder — in the body itself, not on it. Other covenant signs in Genesis: rainbow (9:12–13), Sabbath (Exod 31:13). Each sign is a perpetual physical reminder of the divine promise.

נִכְרְתָה nikrĕtāh — cut off: The penalty for covenant violation uses the same root (kārat) as "cut a covenant" (15:18) — a grim wordplay. The one who refuses the covenant sign will be "cut" from the covenant people, mirroring the cutting ceremony of the covenant itself.

Historical Context

Circumcision was practiced by various ANE peoples (Egyptians, some Semitic groups) for hygienic and/or initiatory reasons. But the Genesis text radically redefines it: not a rite of passage or hygiene but a covenant sign. The distinction is ideological — Israel's circumcision carries a specific theological meaning (covenant identity) absent from neighboring practice. Herodotus (5th c. BC) notes that Phoenicians and Syrians learned circumcision from Egypt, suggesting the practice spread in the ANE context.

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 4:10–12Paul notes Abraham was justified (Gen 15:6) before he was circumcised (Gen 17) — proving circumcision is a sign of existing faith, not the basis of righteousness.
  • Gal 6:15Paul argues neither circumcision nor uncircumcision matters — what counts is new creation. The external sign has been replaced by the internal reality.
  • Col 2:11Paul calls Christian conversion a "circumcision of Christ" — the cutting away of the "body of the flesh" in spiritual circumcision.
Verses 15–27 — Sarai Becomes Sarah; Isaac Promised; All Males Circumcised
15God also said to Abraham, "As for Sarai your wife, you are no longer to call her Sarai; her name will be Sarah.16I will bless her and will surely give you a son by her. I will bless her so that she will be the mother of nations; kings of peoples will come from her."17Abraham fell facedown; he laughed and said to himself, "Will a son be born to a man a hundred years old? Will Sarah bear a child at the age of ninety?"18And Abraham said to God, "If only Ishmael might live under your blessing!"19Then God said, "Yes, but your wife Sarah will bear you a son, and you will call him Isaac. I will establish my covenant with him as an everlasting covenant for his descendants after him.20And as for Ishmael, I have heard you: I will surely bless him; I will make him fruitful and will greatly increase his numbers. He will be the father of twelve rulers, and I will make him into a great nation.21But my covenant I will establish with Isaac, whom Sarah will bear to you by this time next year."22When he had finished speaking with Abraham, God went up from him.23On that very day Abraham took his son Ishmael and all those born in his household or bought with his money, every male in his household, and circumcised them, as God told him.24Abraham was ninety-nine years old when he was circumcised,25and his son Ishmael was thirteen;26Abraham and his son Ishmael were both circumcised on that very day.27And every male in Abraham's household, including those born in his household or bought from a foreigner, was circumcised with him.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
17:15–16

Sarah's name change (from Sarai to Sarah — both mean "princess" but Sarah is the fuller, more exalted form) signals her full elevation as the covenant partner. The promise is now specific: not just a son to Abraham, but a son by Sarah specifically. All previous suggestions of alternative fulfilment (Ishmael, Eliezer) are definitively closed. The covenant line runs through Sarah. "I will bless her and she will be the mother of nations" — Sarah is not a passive vehicle for Abraham's promise but herself a recipient of the covenant blessing.

17:17–19

Abraham's laughter (wayitsechaq) at the prospect of a child from a 100-year-old man and a 90-year-old woman is the laughter of astonishment, not of disbelief — contrasted with Sarah's laughter of incredulity (18:12). Abraham's immediate response is to ask God to consider Ishmael: the father's heart for the son already born. God's answer is firm: no — the covenant will go through Isaac (the name means "he laughs"). God does not forget Ishmael (v.20) but the promise has a specific channel. Divine election does not eliminate divine care for those outside the primary covenant line.

Hebrew Word Study

שָׂרָה śārāh — Sarah: The name change from Sarai to Sarah is less etymologically distinct — both relate to "princess" or "noble lady" (śar = prince/ruler). The change may represent a formalization of her royal status in the covenant promise. She is now officially the covenant matriarch.

יִצְחָק yiṣḥāq — Isaac: Named from Abraham's laughter (ṣāḥaq, v.17) — the name means "he laughs" or "laughter." The promised child will carry his father's laughter of disbelief in his name, becoming a perpetual reminder that God's promise outlasted Abraham's doubt.

וַיִּצְחַק wayyiṣḥaq — and he laughed: Abraham laughs at the promise — which might be disbelief, joy, or (as some read) wonder. Sarah will also laugh (18:12). The child named "Laughter" (Isaac) will become the embodied answer to both laughs.

Context

Abraham's laughter in v.17 is psychologically honest — at 99 and 90 years old, a natural child is biologically impossible. Yet God insists on Isaac, not Ishmael, as the covenant heir (v.19). This distinction is not a rejection of Ishmael (v.20 gives him a sevenfold blessing) but a clarification: the covenant promise will travel a specific line. The biological impossibility is the point — Isaac will be a miracle child, making clear that the covenant's fulfillment depends entirely on God, not human biology.

Cross-Reference

  • Heb 11:11–12Sarah and Abraham's faith enabled the conception of Isaac "from one man, and him as good as dead" — pointing beyond biology to divine power.
  • Gal 4:28Paul calls Christians "children of promise" like Isaac — born not of natural processes but of the Spirit's work.
  • Rom 9:7–9Paul distinguishes physical descent from Abraham (like Ishmael) from covenant descent (like Isaac) — "children of the promise are counted as offspring."
Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Abraham (formerly Abram)
Covenant patriarch; newly named
Receives his permanent name and the covenant sign. His laughter at the promise (v.17) is an honest moment of incredulity that God meets with reaffirmation. He obeys immediately (vv.23–27), circumcising all males in his household the same day.
Sarah (formerly Sarai)
Covenant matriarch; newly named
The second name change of the chapter. For the first time, God explicitly names Sarah — not just Abram's wife — as the covenant mother. "Kings of peoples shall come from her" (v.16) — she is a covenant figure in her own right.
Ishmael
Son of Abraham; circumcised; blessed
Though not the covenant heir, Ishmael is circumcised (v.25) and given a sevenfold blessing (v.20) — "I will make him into a great nation." He is not rejected; he is positioned. The nations descended from Ishmael are a fulfilment of this promise.

Ancient Sources

Herodotus, Histories II.104
Herodotus states that Phoenicians and Syrians learned circumcision from the Egyptians — "The Egyptians and Ethiopians have practised circumcision since time immemorial." This confirms the practice existed in the ANE environment from which Abraham came, while Genesis redefines its meaning as covenant sign.
4QMMT (Dead Sea Scrolls)
The Qumran community interpreted circumcision strictly within a covenant-obedience framework, using its presence or absence as a marker of covenant faithfulness. The Genesis 17 institution was foundational for their community self-understanding.

Reception History

Paul, Romans 4:9–12
Paul's argument that Abraham was justified before circumcision (Gen 15:6 precedes Gen 17) makes circumcision a "seal of the righteousness that he had by faith while he was still uncircumcised." The sign confirms but does not create righteousness.
The Jerusalem Council, Acts 15
The earliest church debate concerned whether Gentile converts must be circumcised (i.e., become Jews) to be saved. The council's decision (no) rests on the Pauline reading of Genesis — faith, not circumcision, is the covenant entrance.

Literary Structure

IGod speaks to Abram: covenant terms, name change to Abraham (vv.1–8)
IICovenant sign: circumcision (vv.9–14)
Name change: Sarai → Sarah; Isaac promised (vv.15–19)
II'Ishmael's blessing; circumcision of entire household (vv.20–27)
The chapter is organized around three pairs: two name changes (Abraham, Sarah), two covenant parties (Abraham with his household, then the specific promise to Sarah), and two sons (Isaac promised, Ishmael blessed). The center is the explicit naming of Sarah as covenant matriarch — a theological clarification that the promise travels through her.

Hebrew-Rooted Reading

1 The LORD appeared to Abram and said, "I am God Almighty [ʾēl šadday]; walk before me and be blameless. 5 No longer shall your name be called Abram, but your name shall be Abraham [ʾaḇrāhām: father of multitudes], for I have made you the father of a multitude of nations. 10 This is my covenant [bĕrît] which you shall keep... Every male among you shall be circumcised [nimmôl]. 15 As for Sarai your wife, you shall call her name Sarah. 19 You shall call his name Isaac [yiṣḥāq: laughter]."

Intertextual Threading

Gen 15:6→Gen 17:10Echo
Faith (Gen 15:6) precedes the covenant sign (Gen 17:10) by 13 years — Paul's argument in Romans 4 that righteousness is prior to and independent of circumcision rests on this chronological gap.
Gen 17:5→Rom 4:17Quotation
Paul quotes "I have made you the father of many nations" as the basis for Abraham's spiritual fatherhood of all who share his faith — both Jews and Gentiles.
Gen 17:10–14→Gal 5:6; Col 2:11Fulfillment
Physical circumcision as covenant sign is fulfilled in "circumcision of the heart" (Deut 30:6, Rom 2:29) and the "circumcision of Christ" (Col 2:11) — the cutting away of the flesh in the cross.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 17 is the covenant's formal constitution. Three elements mark covenant membership: the name of God (El Shaddai), the sign (circumcision), and the names of the partners (Abraham, Sarah). God builds the promise into the very identity of the patriarchs — their new names are a daily profession of faith in the impossible.
