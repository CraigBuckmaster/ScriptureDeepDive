The Final Call & Loading the Ark — Verses 1–10
1 The LORD then said to Noah, “Go into the ark, you and your whole family, because I have found you righteous in this generation. 2 Take with you seven pairs of every kind of clean animal, a male and its mate, and one pair of every kind of unclean animal, a male and its mate, 3 and also seven pairs of every kind of bird, male and female, to keep their various kinds alive throughout the earth. 4 Seven days from now I will send rain on the earth for forty days and forty nights, and I will wipe from the face of the earth every living creature I have made.” 5 And Noah did all that the LORD commanded him.

6 Noah was six hundred years old when the floodwaters came on the earth. 7 And Noah and his sons and his wife and his sons’ wives entered the ark to escape the waters of the flood. 8 Pairs of clean and unclean animals, of birds and of all creatures that move along the ground, 9 male and female, came to Noah and entered the ark, as God had commanded Noah. 10 And after the seven days the floodwaters came on the earth.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1

"Go into the ark, you and your whole family, because I have found you righteous in this generation." The invitation to enter the ark before judgment falls is the pattern of all divine rescue: God calls his people out of and into safety before wrath is poured out. The seven-day waiting period (v.4) before the flood is itself a grace — one final week for any who would repent. No one comes. The door that God shuts (v.16) is shut from the outside, by God — not from the inside by Noah. The opportunity for entry had existed; now it closes.

7:2–3

Clean animals in sevens (or seven pairs), unclean in twos. The clean/unclean distinction exists before the Mosaic law — it is embedded in the creation order. The extra clean animals are for sacrifice (8:20) — Noah's post-flood worship was planned by God before the flood began. God's provision anticipates the needs of his redeemed people.

Hebrew Word Study

Tahor / tame’ — “Clean / unclean.” The distinction between clean and unclean animals appears here centuries before Leviticus 11. This implies Noah already operated within a sacrificial/priestly framework (confirmed in 8:20 when he offers burnt offerings). The Levitical categories are not an innovation of Moses but a formalisation of something already present in the primeval world.


Shiv’ah shiv’ah — “Seven pairs” (literally “seven seven”). Clean animals are taken in sevens, unclean in pairs. After the flood, when Noah sacrifices (8:20), the extra clean animals mean the species are not depleted. The divine mathematics of the command looks ahead to its purpose.


Arba’im yom ve-arba’im laylah — “Forty days and forty nights.” The number forty becomes the biblical number of testing and judgment: Moses on Sinai (Ex 24:18), Israel in the wilderness (Num 14:33), Elijah’s journey (1 Kgs 19:8), Jonah’s warning to Nineveh (Jon 3:4), Jesus in the desert (Matt 4:2). The flood establishes the pattern.

Historical Context — The Seven-Day Wait

The seven-day period before the flood (v.4, v.10) echoes the seven-day structure of creation in Genesis 1. The flood is a de-creation event — the ordered world of Genesis 1 is being unmade. The seven-day countdown mirrors the seven days of making. Ancient flood traditions also feature preparatory periods: Utnapishtim in the Gilgamesh Epic builds and loads for seven days before the storm. The shared structure likely reflects a common ancient understanding of how catastrophic floods begin — with warning signs and a period of ominous calm.

Deep Context

The repetition between Genesis 6 and 7 (animals entering the ark is described twice, in slightly different language) has long been noted by source critics as evidence of two interwoven sources (J and P). Rather than a problem, this repetition functions as literary intensification — the author is describing events of such cosmic magnitude that one telling is insufficient. The doubling creates a sense of solemn, inexorable movement. Noah “did all that the LORD commanded him” appears in 6:22 and again in 7:5 — the obedience is underscored twice because it is the pivot on which everything turns.

The Flood Unleashed — Verses 11–24
11 In the six hundredth year of Noah’s life, on the seventeenth day of the second month — on that day all the springs of the great deep burst forth, and the floodgates of the heavens were opened. 12 And rain fell on the earth forty days and forty nights.

13 On that very day Noah and his sons, Shem, Ham and Japheth, together with his wife and the wives of his three sons, entered the ark. 14 They had with them every wild animal according to its kind, all livestock according to their kinds, every creature that moves along the ground according to its kind and every bird according to its kind, everything with wings. 15 Pairs of all creatures that have the breath of life in them came to Noah and entered the ark. 16 The animals going in were male and female of every living thing, as God had commanded Noah. Then the LORD shut him in.

17 For forty days the flood kept coming on the earth, and as the waters increased they lifted the ark high above the earth. 18 The waters rose and increased greatly on the earth, and the ark floated on the surface of the water. 19 They rose greatly on the earth, and all the high mountains under the entire heavens were covered. 20 The waters rose and covered the mountains to a depth of more than fifteen cubits. 21 Every living thing that moved on land perished — birds, livestock, wild animals, all the creatures that swarm over the earth, and all mankind. 22 Everything on dry land that had the breath of life in its nostrils died. 23 Every living thing on the face of the earth was wiped out; people and animals and the creatures that move along the ground and the birds were wiped from the earth. Only Noah was left, and those with him in the ark. 24 The waters flooded the earth for a hundred and fifty days.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:11

"All the springs of the great deep burst forth, and the floodgates of the heavens were opened." The flood is not merely heavy rainfall but a reversal of creation: the waters above and below (separated on days 2 and 3) are released to reclaim the earth. This is de-creation — the undoing of the ordered world to judge the beings who have defaced the image of God. The 40 days of rain, the 150 days of prevailing waters, and the 150 days of receding are all precise chronological markers signalling a historical event, not a theological symbol.

7:23

"Only Noah was left, and those with him in the ark." The compression of history's greatest catastrophe into a single clause. Every human being outside the ark — every descendant of Adam, every bearer of the image of God — died. The magnitude of God's judgment on sin is not to be softened: the flood is the most comprehensive act of divine judgment in the Old Testament, exceeded only by the final judgment. Peter uses it as the type of the coming judgment by fire (2 Pet 3:5–7). The ark is the type of salvation: being "in Christ" is the only safety from the coming wrath.

Hebrew Word Study — De-Creation Language

Mayanot tehom rabbah — “Springs of the great deep.” Tehom is the deep waters of Genesis 1:2, over which the Spirit hovered at creation. The flood is not just rain — the tehom bursts upward from below while the heavens open from above. The boundaries established at creation (waters above, waters below, dry land between — Genesis 1:6–10) are dissolved. This is de-creation, creation running in reverse.


Arubbot hashamayim — “Floodgates of the heavens” (literally “windows of the sky”). The same “windows” that God “opens” in 2 Kings 7:2 — the firmament-windows that normally regulate rain. Here they are flung open in an act of cosmic undoing.


YHWH vayisgor ba’ado — “Then the LORD shut him in” (v.16). God himself closes the door of the ark. The most intimate detail in the chapter. Noah does not lock himself in — God seals him. The same act that ends Noah’s access to the outside world is the act of absolute divine protection.

Deep Context — The Flood as Judgment & De-Creation

Genesis 7 uses the cosmic geography of Genesis 1 as its vocabulary of destruction. Day 2 of creation separated waters above from waters below (1:6–8); the flood collapses that separation. Day 3 gathered the waters so dry land appeared (1:9–10); the flood reverses that. Day 5–6 filled sea, sky, and land with creatures (1:20–25); the flood un-fills them. The narrative is constructed as a photographic negative of creation week.


The theological message is stark: the moral fabric and the physical fabric of creation are not separate. When humanity becomes thoroughly corrupt (Hamas, violence; shachat, corruption), creation itself comes apart. This is not arbitrary punishment but consequence embedded in reality. Paul writes in Romans 8:20–22 that “the creation was subjected to frustration” — the connection between human sin and cosmic disorder is a biblical constant from Genesis 3 onward.

Cross-References

  • Gen 1:2, 6–10The creation structures that the flood reverses — the tehom, the firmament, the gathering of waters — making the flood a deliberate narrative un-creation.
  • Ps 29:10“The LORD sits enthroned over the flood” — the flood demonstrated the LORD’s sovereignty over even the most catastrophic forces of chaos.
  • Isa 54:9God swears by the flood of Noah never again to cover the earth — the flood is the benchmark of divine wrath that God pledges not to repeat.
  • 2 Pet 3:5–7Peter links the flood to the final judgment by fire — the same God who destroyed the world by water will judge it by fire at the end.

Theme Moments This Chapter
Sin & Judgment — vv.17–24

The flood executes the judgment announced in ch.6. The de-creation mirrors Gen 1 in reverse — sin has unmade the world. This is the OT's paradigm of divine judgment on cosmic scale.

Grace & Rescue — v.16

'The LORD shut him in' — God's personal action secures the rescue. The ark is not self-sufficient; it is divinely sealed and sustained. Rescue is always an act of grace, not engineering.

Priesthood — vv.2–3

The distinction of clean/unclean animals — before Leviticus — suggests the sacrificial system is embedded in creation order, not invented at Sinai. Noah's categories anticipate the priestly legislation.

Intertextual Threading

3 canonical threads — 2 forward (passages that echo this chapter)  |  1 backward (texts this chapter echoes)

v.16 — God shut the door → Matt 25:10; Luke 13:25 Echo

Jesus's parables of the shut door echo Gen 7:16 precisely: the five foolish virgins arrive to find 'the door was shut' (Matt 25:10); in Luke 13:25, the owner shuts the door and those outside cannot enter. In both parables, as in Gen 7, the door's closing is an act of divine sovereignty marking the end of the time for entry. Grace has a door — and doors close.

vv.11–12 — fountains and windows ← Gen 1:6–7 Reversal

The 'fountains of the great deep' bursting open and the 'floodgates of the heavens' opening (7:11) directly reverse God's creative acts in Gen 1:6–7, where the waters above and below were separated. The flood is the un-doing of Day 2 of creation — the firmament fails, the separation collapses. The same divine word that ordered creation now un-orders it.

vv.21–23 — all life perished → 1 Pet 3:20–21 Echo

Peter's typological reading makes the flood water a double sign: it was the instrument of destruction for the unrighteous and simultaneously the vehicle of salvation for the eight. This dual function — judgment and rescue through the same water — is the template for baptism, which enacts death to the old life and resurrection to new life. The ark is the church; the water is baptism.

People — Genesis 7

Noah's Family
Eight Survivors — v.7,13

The text carefully names all eight survivors: Noah, his wife, his three sons (Shem, Ham, Japheth), and their wives. Not one person is named outside the family. This is not incidental — the flood narrative frames Noah as a second Adam whose three sons will repopulate the entire earth (ch.10). The women are unnamed throughout, a feature consistent with much ancient narrative but increasingly noted by modern readers. Peter's reading (1 Pet 3:20) emphasizes the number eight as significant — eight people saved through water, paralleling baptism.

Noah as Second Adam
The Obedient One — v.5,9,16

Three times in ch.7 the narrator notes Noah's complete obedience: 'Noah did all that the LORD commanded him' (v.5); 'as God had commanded Noah' (v.9); 'as God had commanded' (v.16). This triple stress contrasts with Adam's single act of disobedience. Noah is the obedient man in whose day God hits the reset button on creation — but ch.9 will show the reset is incomplete. The deeper problem is not the world around humanity but the heart within it (8:21 confesses this explicitly after the flood).

Translation Comparison — Key Verses

Verse 1 — Righteous before me

VersionRendering
NIVGo into the ark, you and your whole family, because I have found you righteous in this generation
ESVGo into the ark, you and all your household, for I have seen that you are righteous before me in this generation
NASBGo into the ark, you and all your household, for you alone I have seen to be righteous before Me in this time
NoteESV adds 'before me' — translating lefanai which NIV omits. This is significant: righteousness is defined in relation to God, not merely in comparison to contemporaries. NASB 'you alone' emphasizes the singularity of Noah's distinction. The phrase 'in this generation' (bador hazeh) implies his righteousness is contextual and comparative, which some rabbinic readers used to question whether Noah would have been as distinguished in Abraham's generation.

Verse 16 — The LORD shut him in

VersionRendering
NIVThen the LORD shut him in
ESVAnd the LORD shut him in
NASBand the LORD closed it behind him
KJVand the LORD shut him in
NoteA theologically pregnant verse that all translations render nearly identically. The divine locking of the door means: (1) the time of grace has ended — no one else can enter; (2) Noah is protected by God's own action, not by the door's engineering; (3) God is personally present at the flood's threshold. Jesus alludes to this in the parable of the ten virgins (Matt 25:10) when 'the door was shut' — the same theological logic of a fixed moment of grace.

Ancient Source Texts — The Flood Narrative

Gilgamesh Epic — Loading the Ark (Tablet XI)
Whatever I had of living beings I loaded upon her, I caused to go up into the ship all my family and relations, the beasts of the field, the animals of the field, all the craftsmen I caused to go up.

Utnapishtim's ark loading parallels Noah's in remarkable detail: family, animals, craftsmen/provisions. The Gilgamesh version also specifies the ark's shape (a perfect cube — 120 cubits each side) versus Genesis's elongated boat proportions (300x50x30 cubits), which modern naval architects note are surprisingly seaworthy ratios. The Babylonian cube would be nautically absurd but cosmologically significant. Genesis's proportions suggest the author was describing a real vessel, not a mythological symbol.

Atrahasis Epic — The Door Sealed (Tablet III)
He sent his family on board. They ate and they drank. But he went in and out, could not stay still or rest on his haunches... he cut through the mooring rope and cast off the boat.

The Atrahasis flood hero cuts his own mooring rope — he controls his departure. In Genesis, God shuts the door (v.16) — Noah has no agency in the closure. This small difference encodes enormous theological meaning: in Mesopotamian tradition the flood hero is a capable agent who manages his own survival; in Genesis, Noah's survival is entirely in God's hands from beginning to end.

Reception History — Genesis 7

1 Peter 3:20–21 — Baptismal Typology

Peter's reading of the flood as a type of baptism became one of the most influential interpretations in Christian history. 'Corresponding to that, baptism now saves you — not the removal of dirt from the flesh, but an appeal to God for a good conscience — through the resurrection of Jesus Christ.' The flood water that destroyed the world simultaneously saved eight people; baptismal water enacts the same dual movement of judgment and rescue. This typological reading made Noah's ark a standard image for the church in early Christian art — over 100 ark images survive in the Roman catacombs.

Chrysostom — Homily on 'The LORD shut him in'

Chrysostom preached extensively on v.16's divine door-closing: 'Do you see how He cared for the just man? The LORD shut him in — not with a lock and key but with His own power, making the ark unassailable from without and secure within.' He used this verse to argue that divine protection is not mechanical (the ark's engineering) but personal (God's presence). The sermon became a model for preaching divine providence — human preparation is necessary but God's direct action is what ultimately saves.

Origen — The Ark as the Church

Origen systematized the typological reading: the ark is the church, outside of which there is no salvation during the flood of judgment. The eight saved correspond to those who enter through baptism (he connected eight with the eighth day — Sunday, resurrection day, the day beyond the seven-day week of this age). This 'ark = church' typology shaped the medieval theological maxim extra ecclesiam nulla salus (outside the church no salvation), though that formula carries more weight than Origen intended.

Literary Structure — The De-Creation Mirror

Genesis 7 systematically reverses Genesis 1, day by day. The flood is a literary and theological un-creation:

Gen 1:6-7Waters separated — sky above, sea below
Gen 7:11Floodgates of sky & springs of deep burst open — separation collapses
—
Gen 1:9-10Dry land appears — sea contained
Gen 7:19-20All high mountains covered — dry land disappears
—
Gen 1:20-22Birds and sea creatures fill their domains
Gen 7:21-22All creatures with breath of life perish
—
Gen 1:28Be fruitful and fill the earth
Gen 7:23Only Noah remained — and those with him in the ark

The flood narrative also uses precise chronological data (day 17 of the 2nd month; 40 days; 150 days) that is unique in the primeval history. Scholars have identified a 370-day flood chronology that mirrors a solar year — creation takes a week; un-creation and re-creation take a year. The precision is not incidental: it marks the flood as a cosmic liturgical event, not merely a natural disaster.
