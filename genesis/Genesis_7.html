Genesis 7

The Flood: Waters Cover the Earth

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–10 — The Call to Enter the Ark
1The Lord then said to Noah, "Go into the ark, you and your whole family, because I have found you righteous in this generation.2Take with you seven pairs of every kind of clean animal, a male and its mate, and one pair of every kind of unclean animal, a male and its mate,3and also seven pairs of every kind of bird, male and female, to keep their various kinds alive throughout the earth.4Seven days from now I will send rain on the earth for forty days and forty nights, and I will wipe from the face of the earth every living creature I have made."5And Noah did all that the Lord commanded him.6Noah was six hundred years old when the floodwaters came on the earth.7And Noah and his sons and his wife and his sons' wives entered the ark to escape the waters of the flood.8Pairs of clean and unclean animals, of birds and of all creatures that move along the ground,9male and female, came to Noah and entered the ark, as God had commanded Noah.10And after the seven days the floodwaters came on the earth.

Hebrew Word Study

טָהוֹר tahor — clean / pure: V.2: The distinction between clean and unclean animals — seven pairs of clean, one pair of unclean — anticipates the Mosaic dietary laws but predates them. This is the earliest occurrence of the clean/unclean distinction in Scripture, suggesting it has a pre-Mosaic basis. Noah takes more clean animals, presumably because they will be needed for sacrifice (8:20).

עוֹד שִׁבְעַת יָמִים od shivat yamim — seven more days: V.4: The seven-day waiting period before the flood echoes the seven-day creation structure of chapter 1. The flood narrative is structured as a mirror of the creation account: the waters cover the earth (returning to the tohu vavohu of 1:2), then recede, then the earth is re-created. The seven-day rhythm signals that what is about to happen is a de-creation and re-creation.

Historical Context

The two accounts of Noah entering the ark (vv.1-5 and vv.7-9) have been identified by source critics as evidence of two different sources (J and P) being combined. The differences (seven pairs vs. two pairs of clean animals; seven days vs. rain immediately) are real but may also reflect the normal ancient narrative technique of resumptive repetition (telling the same event twice with different emphasis). The theological coherence of the flood narrative as a whole argues against arbitrary combination.

Context

The command to enter the ark is accompanied by God's personal testimony: "I have found you righteous in this generation" (v.1). This is an extraordinary statement — God himself publicly affirms Noah's righteousness. The flood is not arbitrary destruction; it is the moral response of a just God who has carefully distinguished the righteous from the wicked before executing judgment. Noah enters the ark with divine certification of his standing.

Cross-Reference

  • 1 Pet 3:20Eight people were saved through water — Peter reads the flood as a type of baptism and notes the specific number of survivors.
  • 2 Pet 2:5God protected Noah, a preacher of righteousness, and brought the flood on the ungodly world — Noah as preacher, not merely survivor.
  • Luke 17:27People were eating and drinking, marrying and being given in marriage up to the day Noah entered the ark — normal life until sudden judgment.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1

God's personal statement — "I have found you righteous in this generation" — is among the most intimate divine affirmations in the OT. It is not merely a selection criteria but a testimony: God himself vouches for Noah's character before all of heaven and earth. Noah enters the ark not as a lucky survivor but as a man whose righteousness has been personally certified by the Judge of all the earth.

7:2-3

The distinction between clean and unclean animals predates the Mosaic law — it is embedded in creation's moral order, not invented at Sinai. Seven pairs of clean animals are taken (presumably because they will be needed for sacrifice and food) and one pair of unclean. God's provision is proportionate: more of what will serve the post-flood restoration.

7:4-5

The seven-day wait before the flood is an act of grace — one final week for repentance, one final week for any who might turn. The New Testament reads Noah as "a preacher of righteousness" (2 Pet 2:5) during this period. The flood does not fall until every opportunity has been given. The same God who grieves over sin (6:6) also delays judgment to give space for repentance (2 Pet 3:9).

7:1

The divine command to enter the ark is framed by the covenant language of 6:18: God summons his covenant partner to the place of safety he has prepared. Sarna notes the tenderness of the command: "Come into the ark, you and your whole family" — it is an invitation, not merely a command. The God who is about to destroy the world preserves his covenant partner and his family with intimate care.

7:2

The clean/unclean distinction appearing here before Sinai indicates it has roots in a pre-Mosaic understanding of holiness. Sarna: the distinction may have been generally understood in the ancient world as part of common religious practice — what was appropriate for sacrifice was what was ritually pure. The Mosaic law codifies and specifies a distinction that already existed in religious practice.

7:1-5

The narrative doubles back to repeat the entry into the ark — a technique Alter calls "resumptive repetition." The first account (vv.1-5) focuses on God's command and the theological framing (Noah's righteousness, the seven-day wait). The second account (vv.7-9) focuses on the execution of the command. Together they create a complete picture: the theological ground and the physical action. Neither alone is sufficient.

7:5

"And Noah did all that the LORD commanded him." The same refrain as 6:22 — "just as God commanded him." The repetition is deliberate: Noah's comprehensive obedience is the chapter's moral signature. In a world of comprehensive disobedience (6:5), comprehensive obedience is the mark of the covenant partner.

7:1

Calvin on "come into the ark": "God does not drive Noah into the ark as a prisoner, but invites him as a guest. The word 'come' implies a gentle and friendly manner of address, as if God would say, 'I have prepared a dwelling for thee, enter into it.' The relationship between God and Noah is not one of master and slave but of patron and guest — the patron has prepared the place; the guest is invited to enter.

7:4

The seven-day wait is an act of patience and long-suffering. Calvin: "God, though he had fixed the time of the deluge, yet, as if repenting of his purpose, delays it for a week. Whence it appears, how averse he is from destruction, and how ready to show mercy." Even on the threshold of judgment, God waits. The week is a final window of grace.

7:2

The NET note on clean versus unclean animals notes that the Mosaic categories are codified in Leviticus 11 and Deuteronomy 14, but the present passage assumes the distinction is already understood. The note suggests the distinction may have been based on suitability for sacrifice (clean animals could be offered to God) rather than on dietary concerns at this stage. The purpose of taking seven pairs of clean animals is confirmed by 8:20, where Noah sacrifices from every clean animal.

7:4

The NET note on "seven days" discusses the seven-day structure and its possible echoes of the creation week. The note also addresses the question of whether Noah preached repentance during this week (2 Pet 2:5 calls him "a preacher of righteousness") — the Genesis text is silent on this but the NT expands it.

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:1

YHWH said to Noah: "Come into the ark, you and your whole family, because I have found you righteous in this generation." Sarna: the divine invitation — "come" rather than "go" — implies God himself will be in the ark with Noah. The formulation "I have found you righteous" is the covenant formula of divine acceptance, parallel to the favour of 6:8. Noah does not merely survive the flood by luck or merit; he is specifically invited by divine address.

7:2-3

The distinction between clean and unclean animals (seven pairs of clean, one pair of unclean) anticipates the Mosaic dietary laws. Sarna notes this is the first occurrence of the clean/unclean distinction in the Bible. Its presence in the flood narrative, before Sinai, suggests that the categories of clean and unclean are not merely Mosaic innovations but rooted in a primordial ordering of creation that Israel's law will formalise.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:1

The boarding scene repeats and extends the commissioning of chapter 6, but with notable differences: God speaks directly ("Come into the ark"), the time frame is specified (seven days), and the clean/unclean distinction is introduced. Alter: the repetition is not redundancy but the biblical technique of resumptive repetition — the narrative re-grounds itself before moving to the flood proper. Each repetition adds a layer of specificity and immediacy.

7:9-10

They came to Noah and entered the ark as God had commanded Noah. The passive construction — "they came" — removes agency from the animals. They do not wander in; they come, as if drawn. The obedience of the animals mirrors Noah's obedience to God. Alter: "The animals' coming to Noah is a re-enactment of 2:19-20 where God brought the animals to Adam. The flood narrative echoes the creation narrative at every point — the undoing and remaking of creation."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:1

The divine command "come" reveals that God entered the ark with Noah. Calvin: "God does not command Noah to go into the ark, but to come — as if he would go first and show the way." The presence of God with Noah in the flood is the theological center of the narrative. The flood is not divine abandonment but divine accompaniment — God preserving his people through the waters of judgment, not from a distance.

7:2-3

The clean/unclean distinction shows that the Mosaic dietary laws were not arbitrary innovations but expressions of a primordial ordering. Calvin: "The distinction between clean and unclean animals has existed from the beginning of the world." Noah understood which animals were appropriate for sacrifice — the clean animals he will offer in 8:20. The sevens of clean animals anticipate their use in worship.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:2

The NET note on the clean/unclean animal distinction here vs. the "two of every kind" in 6:19-20 acknowledges an apparent contradiction. The note explains that 6:19-20 gives the general principle (pairs of all kinds) and 7:2-3 specifies it (seven pairs of clean, one pair of unclean). The contradiction is harmonised as complementary levels of detail rather than different sources.

7:4

The NET note on "seven days" observes this is the first of several seven-day periods in the flood narrative (7:4, 7:10, 8:10, 8:12) — the same structure as the creation week of chapter 1. The flood narrative mirrors creation: the seven-day structure, the waters above and below, the wind over the waters (8:1), the emergence of dry land, the blessing and commission. The flood is a de-creation followed by a new creation.

Verses 11–16 — The Flood Begins; the Lord Shuts the Door
11In the six hundredth year of Noah's life, on the seventeenth day of the second month—on that day all the springs of the great deep burst forth, and the floodgates of the heavens were opened.12And rain fell on the earth forty days and forty nights.13On that very day Noah and his sons, Shem, Ham and Japheth, together with his wife and the wives of his three sons, entered the ark.14They had with them every wild animal according to its kind, all livestock according to their kinds, every creature that moves along the ground according to its kind and every bird according to its kind, everything with wings.15Pairs of all creatures that have the breath of life in them came to Noah and entered the ark.16The animals going in were male and female of every living thing, as God had commanded Noah. Then the Lord shut him in.

Hebrew Word Study

מַעְיְנֹת תְּהוֹם רַבָּה mayenot tehom rabbah — springs of the great deep: V.11: The flood has two sources: the springs of the great deep (below) and the floodgates of the sky (above). The tehom (deep) of 1:2 — the primordial waters that God separated at creation — are released. The creation's water-barriers are opened: the firmament above (1:7) and the waters below (1:9) both give way. This is deliberate de-creation language: the boundaries established at creation are dissolved.

וַיִּסְגֹּר יְהוָה בַּעֲדוֹ wayyisgor YHWH baado — the LORD shut him in: V.16: The most tender detail of the entire flood narrative. After all the animals and Noah's family have entered, the LORD himself shuts the door. Noah does not close the ark; God closes it. The one who commanded the building is the one who seals the safety. The sovereign God who opens the springs of the deep and the windows of heaven personally secures the door of the ark.

Historical Context

The date formula of v.11 — "the seventeenth day of the second month" in the six hundredth year of Noah's life — is one of the most precise chronological notations in the primeval history. This precision serves a theological purpose: the flood is a datable, historical event in real time, not a mythological event outside history. The ANE flood traditions (Atrahasis, Gilgamesh) have no comparable chronological precision, suggesting Genesis is making a historical claim the myths do not.

Context

The entry sequence of vv.13-16 is deliberately ceremonial: "on that very day" (v.13) Noah and his family entered; the animals came "two by two" (v.15); "those that entered were male and female" (v.16). The repetitive, almost liturgical description of the entry suggests a solemn procession — the last acts of the old world before its end. And then: "the LORD shut him in" (v.16). The old world is sealed off; the new world is sealed in.

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:11

The dual sources of the flood — the springs of the great deep and the floodgates of the sky — are the undoing of creation. The separation of waters above and below that God performed on Day 2 (1:7) is reversed. The waters that God gathered into seas on Day 3 (1:9-10) return. The flood is not merely a natural catastrophe; it is a deliberate reversal of the ordering work of creation, performed by the same God who created the order.

7:16

The LORD closing the door of the ark is one of the most grace-saturated details in the Bible. Noah built the ark; God sealed the safety. The covenant partner provides the obedience; the covenant-making God provides the security. Noah could not have sealed himself in with any confidence; but when God closes the door, it is closed. The ark is safe because God made it safe, not because Noah made it.

7:11

The opening of the springs of the great deep and the windows of the sky is described in terms that explicitly reverse the creation account. Sarna: "The flood is not merely a catastrophe but a cosmic event — the undoing of creation's ordering work. The waters that God separated and bounded at creation are released from their boundaries. The flood is de-creation followed by re-creation." This reading of the flood as de-creation is now widely accepted in biblical scholarship.

7:13-16

The liturgical precision of the entry sequence — the repetition of "two by two," the insistence that "male and female came," the note "as God had commanded" — reads like a census of the saved. Sarna: "Every creature that boards the ark is counted and identified. The narrator refuses to let the mass of perishing creatures obscure the individuality of those who are saved. Each pair has a name, a kind, a place in the ark."

7:11

Alter on the de-creation language: "The springs of the great deep — tehom rabbah — recall the tehom of 1:2, the primordial deep that God's creative word held at bay. Now the tehom is released. The narrative is carefully constructed to reverse the creation account. In chapter 1, God separated the waters; here the separation is undone. The flood is not simply destruction; it is the return of chaos."

7:16

Wayyisgor YHWH baado — "and the LORD shut him in." Alter: "This brief phrase is one of the most moving in the entire Torah. After all the preparations, all the obedience, all the engineering — the LORD himself closes the door. The human actor has done everything required; the divine actor takes the final, securing step. Noah is safe not because he built well but because God sealed him in."

7:11

The opening of the windows of heaven and the fountains of the deep shows that God controls the entire created order. Calvin: "Moses says that the fountains of the great deep were broken up, and the windows of heaven were opened — meaning that the waters, which had been separated and confined by God's command, now return to their original chaos, as if God had dissolved the bonds of the world." The flood is an act of cosmic unmaking — deliberately so.

7:16

Calvin on God closing the door: "It was necessary that God should do this, lest Noah should be in danger of opening the door, or lest he should be overwhelmed with terror at the magnitude of the catastrophe. God seals him in, as a loving father seals his child in a place of safety before the storm." The gesture is both practical and theological: God personally secures what he has promised to preserve.

7:11

The NET note on "springs of the great deep" discusses the cosmological significance: the tehom (deep) is the same word used in 1:2 for the primordial waters. The note observes that the flood narrative uses creation vocabulary deliberately to signal that what is happening is a reversal of creation — the boundaries established in days 2 and 3 are dissolved. This "de-creation" reading is well-supported by the literary parallels between Genesis 1 and the flood narrative.

7:16

The NET note on "then the LORD shut him in" marks this as the only place in the flood narrative where the personal name YHWH is used rather than Elohim — a shift that scholars traditionally assigned to the J source, but which may instead signal that this moment of intimate care is specifically a YHWH-act, an act of covenant faithfulness rather than cosmic sovereignty.

7:11

The date formula — second month, seventeenth day — gives the flood the quality of a historical record. Alter notes that no other event in the primeval history is dated with such precision. The precision lends the flood a legal or administrative character — this is a precisely timed divine act, not a myth. 7:11

Verses 17–24 — The Waters Rise; All Life Perishes
17For forty days the flood kept coming on the earth, and as the waters increased they lifted the ark high above the earth.18The waters rose and increased greatly on the earth, and the ark floated on the surface of the water.19They rose greatly on the earth, and all the high mountains under the entire heavens were covered.20The waters rose and covered the mountains to a depth of more than fifteen cubits.21Every living thing that moved on land perished—birds, livestock, wild animals, all the creatures that swarm over the earth, and all mankind.22Everything on dry land that had the breath of life in its nostrils died.23Every living thing on the face of the earth was wiped out; people and animals and the creatures that move along the ground and the birds were wiped from the earth. Only Noah was left, and those with him in the ark.24The waters flooded the earth for a hundred and fifty days.

Hebrew Word Study

וַיִּמַח wayyimach — blotted out / wiped away: V.23: The verb machah means to wipe out, to erase — as ink is wiped from a page or writing from a tablet. Every living creature that was outside the ark is "blotted out" from the earth. The same verb is used in Exod 32:32-33 for being wiped from the book of life. The flood is not merely physical death but cosmic erasure — the undoing of existence itself for those outside the covenant.

רַק נֹחַ raq Noach — only Noah: V.23: Two words that carry the full weight of the flood narrative. Against the total erasure of every living creature — "all flesh that moved on the earth" — stands this: only Noah. The narrowing of all of humanity and all of creation to a single covenant partner is the most concentrated expression of divine particularity in the entire primeval history.

Historical Context

The flood's duration is precisely recorded: 40 days of rain (v.12), 150 days of water prevailing (v.24). The precision contrasts with the Gilgamesh Epic, where the flood lasts only seven days. The extended duration in Genesis makes a theological point: this is not a passing catastrophe but a sustained judgment — the waters prevail for five months. The ark is not a shelter for a week but the world for half a year.

Context

The chapter ends with a startling contrast: total destruction on one side, raq Noach ("only Noah") on the other. The narrative has moved from the universal (all flesh corrupted, all flesh destroyed) to the singular (only Noah survived) to the seed of restoration. The flood wipes out the old world; the ark preserves the seed of the new one. The logic of the entire chapter is the logic of the cross: comprehensive judgment, comprehensive grace for those inside.

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
7:17-23

The flood narrative reaches its most solemn note in vv.21-23: "Every living thing that moved on land perished — birds, livestock, wild animals, all the creatures that swarm over the earth, and all mankind. Everything on dry land that had the breath of life in its nostrils died. Every living thing on the face of the earth was wiped out." The scope is total. The vocabulary is deliberate: "everything," "all," "every" — the universality of the judgment matches the universality of the corruption.

7:23-24

The two-word summary "only Noah" (raq Noach) is the theological fulcrum of the flood narrative. Against the comprehensive destruction, a single covenant partner survives. The narrowing of all humanity to Noah is the same logic as the narrowing of all nations to Abraham (12:1), all Israel to the remnant, all the remnant to One. The entire biblical narrative moves through progressive narrowing toward the one in whom all are included.

7:21-22

The double formula — "all flesh" and "all in whose nostrils was the breath of life" — echoes the creation language of 2:7 (nishmat chayyim, the breath of life) and 1:21-25 (all living creatures). The flood narrative systematically reverses the creation account: what God made alive with the breath of life is now dead. Sarna: "The flood is the most explicit reversal of creation in the entire Bible — a return to the pre-creation condition of the earth covered with water."

7:23

Raq Noach — "only Noah." Sarna notes that this brief phrase is the pivotal statement of the entire flood narrative. After the comprehensive enumeration of destruction, two words stand: only Noah. The covenant partner is preserved; the covenant continues. The flood does not end God's purposes for creation; it purges the corruption that was destroying them.

7:17-20

The repetitive structure of the flood's rise — "the waters rose... the waters increased... the waters rose greatly" — builds with the same cumulative force as the creation account's "and God said... and it was so... and God saw." In creation, repetition conveyed the orderly emergence of a good world; here it conveys the relentless advance of judgment. The literary technique that celebrated creation is now deployed to describe its dissolution.

7:23

Alter on raq Noach: "Two words of absolute economy: only Noah. After the relentless enumeration of everything that was blotted out — birds, cattle, wild beasts, swarming creatures, every human being — the narrative stops at two words. Only Noah. The compression is devastating and beautiful: the entire surviving world is in those two words."

7:21-23

Calvin on the universality of the flood's destruction: "Let us learn from this how detestable sin is in the sight of God, since it was the cause of so great a destruction. For it is not to be thought that God wantonly or from cruelty destroyed such a multitude of people. But he was constrained to purge the earth, defiled by the contagion of sin, with this deluge." The flood's universality is the measure of sin's seriousness.

7:23-24

Calvin on "only Noah remained": "The preservation of Noah was a miracle, not only because he escaped the common destruction, but because he was sustained by faith in the midst of such horrible devastation. He saw the whole world perishing — every human face he had ever known, the entire creation — and yet he held on to the promise of God. This is faith: to hold the word when everything visible contradicts it."

7:21-22

The NET note on "the breath of life in its nostrils" (nishmat chayyim, the same phrase as 2:7) observes that the flood narrative deliberately recapitulates the language of creation to signal that what is being undone is precisely what was created. The note also observes that the flood narrative does not mention the death of fish — only creatures with the breath of life in their nostrils. The flood is selective in a way that mirrors the selectivity of creation.

7:24

The NET note on "the waters prevailed" for 150 days observes that the narrative is structured in a precise chiasm: 7 days of waiting (7:4), 40 days of rain (7:12), 150 days of flood (7:24), 150 days of receding (8:3), 40 days until Noah opens the window (8:6), 7 days between dove releases (8:10). The chiastic structure around the center point of 8:1 ("God remembered Noah") signals that the whole narrative is organised around that pivotal verse.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:17-24

The flood narrative reaches its theological climax in the repeated affirmations of total destruction: "every living thing... was wiped out... only Noah was left, and those with him in the ark." Sarna notes the structural echo with Genesis 1: as creation moved from chaos to order, the flood moves from order back to chaos. The waters "prevailed" (gabar) — the same root used for the mighty men (giborim) of 6:4. The cosmic waters overwhelm the cosmic violence, both expressions of power unchecked by covenant.

7:24

The waters "prevailed" for 150 days — five months. Sarna: the 150-day period of complete inundation is the structural center of the flood narrative. The turning point will come in 8:1 with the divine remembering of Noah. The waters prevail for exactly the period between God's shutting the door (7:16) and God's remembering (8:1). Between these two divine actions, Noah is entirely passive — preserved, but unable to act.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
7:17-20

The threefold rising of the waters is a literary expansion on the ascent: "the flood kept coming... the waters increased greatly... the waters rose greatly." The Hebrew uses different verbs and intensifiers for each stage, building toward the climactic statement: "the waters covered the mountains to a depth of more than fifteen cubits." Alter: the rising waters are described with almost epic grandeur — this is not a local flood but a cosmic event, the dissolution of the world.

7:23

Vayimmach et kol hayeqoom — "every living thing was wiped out." The verb mchh (wipe out) is the same word used for erasing writing from a tablet. The flood is God erasing what had been written — unwriting the world. Alter: "The verb of erasure is perfectly chosen for what the flood accomplishes: not mere destruction but the elimination of the record, as if humanity's story had never been told. Only the ark and its contents remain."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
7:17-20

The universal scope of the flood — mountains covered, all flesh destroyed — is not negotiable for Calvin. He argues against any merely local flood interpretation: "Moses says expressly that the waters covered the highest mountains. They who contend that the flood was only partial, are greatly deceived." The text's claim to universal destruction is the point of the narrative — a partial flood cannot do what the flood is meant to do theologically: reset the entire created order.

7:23

"Only Noah was left." Calvin: "God, who is the Father of all, when he was provoked by the extreme wickedness of men to destroy them, yet could not forget his own mercy, but reserved some seed for the restoration of the human race." The preservation of Noah is not the minimising of judgment but the expression of grace within judgment — the same grace that will find its ultimate expression in the cross, where the judgment of God and the mercy of God are simultaneously revealed.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
7:19-20

The NET note on "all the high mountains under the entire heavens were covered" addresses the question of universality vs. locality. The note acknowledges both universal-flood and local-flood interpretive traditions among evangelical scholars, notes that the Hebrew kol (all) is sometimes used in hyperbolic contexts in the OT (cf. Gen 41:57; Deut 2:25), but concludes that the text most naturally supports a universal scope. The theological claim — total reset of the created order — requires universal scope.

7:24

The NET note on the 150 days connects to the chronology of the flood narrative: 40 days of rain (7:12), then 150 days total of prevailing waters (7:24), then God remembers (8:1) and the waters begin to recede. The note produces a detailed flood chronology showing the narrative spans approximately one year — a complete solar cycle, structurally echoing the seven-day creation week expanded to yearly scale.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Noah and his family
Eight survivors of the flood; bearers of the new creation
Noah, his wife, his three sons and their wives — eight people (1 Pet 3:20) — are the entire surviving human family. The specificity of eight is theological: the creation of a new humanity requires a specific number of specific people. Noah is simultaneously the last man of the old world and the first man of the new — a second Adam. His obedience (building, entering, waiting) is the pattern of covenant faith under conditions of total surrounding unbelief.

Ancient Sources

The Epic of Gilgamesh, Tablet XI — The Flood
Utnapishtim (the Babylonian Noah) describes: warning from the god Ea, a boat of specific dimensions, animals and family aboard, a flood, birds sent to test the receding waters, a sacrifice after the flood, divine response to the sacrifice. The parallel to Genesis 7-8 is remarkably close at numerous points.
The closeness of the parallels demonstrates literary relationship — either direct borrowing, common source, or shared cultural memory of a real catastrophic flood. The theological differences are absolute: the Babylonian flood is arbitrary and the gods quarrel over it; the Genesis flood is the just response of a morally consistent God to universal corruption. The Gilgamesh account preserves the event; Genesis interprets it.
The Black Sea Hypothesis (Ryan and Pitman, 1997)
Geologists William Ryan and Walter Pitman proposed that a catastrophic flooding of the Black Sea basin (c.5600 BCE), when the Mediterranean breached the Bosphorus, may be the geological event behind the flood traditions of the ANE, including Genesis.
The hypothesis is scientifically debated (the scale and timing are disputed), but it illustrates that flood traditions across the ANE may have a common geological event as their historical basis. Genesis does not require identification with any specific geological event — it claims theological significance for a historical flood, whatever its precise mechanism.

Reception History

Augustine, The Literal Meaning of Genesis (c.401 AD)
Augustine argues for the literal historicity of the flood while acknowledging that the account has typological significance. He is the first to address systematically the question of how all species could fit in the ark, concluding that the cubit may have been longer than standard measurements and that many species could be represented by smaller individuals.
Martin Luther, Lectures on Genesis (c.1535 AD)
Luther reads the flood as the most terrifying demonstration of divine wrath in history, and simultaneously as the most comforting proof of divine faithfulness: "What could be more terrifying than the sight Noah saw from the ark — the entire world drowning? And yet God was with him. This teaches us that faith holds God's word even when all visible evidence contradicts it."
John Walton, The Lost World of the Flood (2018)
Walton argues that the flood account should be read in its ancient cognitive environment — not as a scientific account of a global geological event but as a theological claim about divine judgment and covenant preservation. He reads "all the earth" (kol haares) as potentially referring to the known world rather than the entire globe, consistent with ANE usage.

Literary Structure

Divine Invitation — 7:1"Come into the ark" — God enters with Noah. Righteous found, invited, preserved.
Clean/Unclean — 7:2-3First clean/unclean distinction in Scripture. Anticipates Mosaic law; seven pairs for sacrifice.
Fountains of the deep + floodgates of heaven = reversal of Day 2. Creation's structure dismantled.
God Shuts the Door — 7:16YHWH shut him in — divine hand seals the ark. Custody, not mere rescue.
Waters Prevail — 7:17-24Threefold rising; mountains covered; every living thing wiped out. "Only Noah was left."
Genesis 7 is the de-creation chapter. The opening of the fountains and floodgates reverses the work of Day 2; the rising waters undo the dry land of Day 3; the death of all living things undoes Days 5-6. The narrative moves from divine invitation (v.1) through divine sealing (v.16) to divine erasure (vv.17-23). Only one element remains constant: the ark, held in the hand of God, carrying the seed of a new creation through the waters of judgment.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 7 is the flood at its fullest — de-creation, comprehensive judgment, and the preservation of the covenant line. Every theme of the flood narrative reaches maximum intensity here: the reversal of creation (springs of the deep, windows of heaven), the totality of judgment (all flesh, everything with the breath of life), the intimacy of divine care (God shuts the door), and the singularity of covenant grace (only Noah). The chapter ends in silence: 150 days of waters prevailing over a drowned world, and one ark, sealed by God, floating on the face of the deep.
