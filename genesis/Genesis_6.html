The Sons of God & the Nephilim — Verses 1–4
1 When human beings began to increase in number on the earth and daughters were born to them, 2 the sons of God saw that the daughters of humans were beautiful, and they married any of them they chose. 3 Then the LORD said, “My Spirit will not contend with humans forever, for they are mortal; their days will be a hundred and twenty years.” 4 The Nephilim were on the earth in those days — and also afterward — when the sons of God went to the daughters of humans and had children by them. They were the heroes of old, men of renown.

Hebrew Word Study

Benei ha-Elohim — “Sons of God.” Three major interpretations have existed throughout church history: (1) Divine beings / angelic figures (the oldest reading, shared by the Septuagint, Dead Sea Scrolls, 1 Enoch, Jubilees, and assumed by Jude 6 and 2 Peter 2:4). (2) Dynastic rulers or kings claiming divine status — a common ANE idiom. (3) The godly line of Seth intermarrying with Cain’s line (a later patristic reading to avoid supernatural implications). The first reading is grammatically most natural; benei ha-Elohim always refers to divine beings elsewhere in the OT (Job 1:6; 2:1; 38:7; Ps 82:6).


נְפִילִים Nephilim — From naphal (to fall) or a related root; possibly “fallen ones” or “those who cause others to fall.” The LXX renders it gigantes (giants). They reappear in Numbers 13:33 when the spies report giants in Canaan. Whatever their exact nature, they represent the apex of human corruption — mighty, renowned, but products of transgressed boundaries.


120 years — Most likely the time remaining until the flood, not a new human lifespan limit (post-flood patriarchs still live centuries). God’s patience has a horizon.

Historical Context

Virtually every ancient Near Eastern culture has stories of divine-human unions producing heroic offspring. Greek mythology (Hercules, Achilles), Mesopotamian epics (Gilgamesh is two-thirds divine), and Canaanite texts all feature similar figures. Genesis 6:1–4 appears to be deliberately engaging and reframing these traditions: yes, such unions occurred, but they represent transgression, not divine favor — and they triggered the flood, not an heroic age. The “heroes of old, men of renown” are not celebrated; they are symptoms of a catastrophically corrupted world.


The Dead Sea Scrolls text 1 Enoch (chapters 6–16) expands this passage dramatically: 200 angels called Watchers descended, took wives, and taught humanity forbidden arts — metallurgy, cosmetics, sorcery, astrology. Jude 6 and 2 Peter 2:4 allude to angels who “did not keep their proper domain” and are kept in darkness, suggesting the authors knew this tradition.

Deep Context

These four verses are among the most debated in Genesis. Their function in the narrative is clear even if their precise meaning is not: they mark the absolute bottom of the pre-flood moral decline. The chapter began with humanity multiplying (1:28 was a blessing); now that multiplication has become a vehicle for corruption. The “sons of God” episode represents a blurring of the God-ordained boundaries between heaven and earth, divine and human — a cosmic-level version of Adam’s grasping in chapter 3. The result is not heroes but catastrophe.

God’s Grief & the Decision to Flood — Verses 5–12
5 The LORD saw how great the wickedness of the human race had become on the earth, and that every inclination of the thoughts of the human heart was only evil all the time. 6 The LORD regretted that he had made human beings on the earth, and his heart was deeply troubled. 7 So the LORD said, “I will wipe from the face of the earth the human race I have created — and with them the animals, the birds and the creatures that move along the ground — for I regret that I have made them.” 8 But Noah found favor in the eyes of the LORD.

9 This is the account of Noah and his family. Noah was a righteous man, blameless among the people of his time, and he walked faithfully with God. 10 Noah had three sons: Shem, Ham and Japheth.

11 Now the earth was corrupt in God’s sight and was full of violence. 12 God saw how corrupt the earth had become, for all the people on earth had corrupted their ways.

Hebrew Word Study

Yetser lev ha-adam ra’ raq kol hayyom — “Every inclination of the thoughts of the human heart was only evil all the time.” One of the most thorough statements of total depravity in Scripture. Yetser (inclination/imagination) is the inner forming-faculty of the mind — not just individual acts but the deepest shaping of desire. Raq (only) and kol hayyom (all the time) leave no exceptions. This verse recurs almost verbatim in 8:21 — after the flood — where God uses it to explain why he will never flood the earth again. The flood doesn’t fix the heart.


Vayinnachem YHWH — “The LORD regretted / was grieved.” The verb naham means to feel sorrow, to be moved with compassion, to change course. This is the language of genuine divine emotion, not ignorance corrected. God is not surprised; he is grieved. The anthropomorphic expression is deliberate — God’s response to sin is not detached judicial process but wounded love.


Hen (chen) — “Favor” (v.8). Matzah chen be’einei YHWH — “Found grace/favor in the eyes of the LORD.” The first use of chen (grace) in the Bible. Noah is not saved because he is sinless — he is saved by grace. Genesis 9:21 shows his fallenness. But he is tsaddiq (righteous) and tamim (blameless) — words of covenant faithfulness, not sinless perfection.


Hamas — “Violence” (v.11). Covers physical brutality, oppression, injustice, exploitation — the full spectrum of social wrongdoing. The earth is saturated with it. Shachat (corrupt, v.11) — literally “ruined, destroyed” — the earth has self-destructed morally; the flood mirrors externally what has already happened internally.

Deep Context — The Grief of God

Verse 6 is one of the most theologically daring verses in the Bible: “The LORD regretted that he had made human beings on the earth, and his heart was deeply troubled.” The Hebrew is visceral — God’s heart is “pained” (yit’atsev) — the same word used for Eve’s pain in childbirth (3:16) and Adam’s toil (3:17). God feels the consequences of the Fall as sharply as the creatures who caused it.


The famous “But Noah found favor” (v.8) is the pivot of the entire chapter. Set against the universal darkness of v.5, it is the smallest possible point of light — one man, one family. The salvation of the world rests on this single “but.” Structurally, this “but” (Hebrew ve-Noah) is the same narrative move as Ephesians 2:4 — “But God, who is rich in mercy…”

Cross-References

  • Jer 17:9“The heart is deceitful above all things” — the prophet echoes Genesis 6:5’s diagnosis of the human heart.
  • Eph 2:3–5“By nature deserving of wrath… but God, who is rich in mercy” — Paul’s great “but” structurally mirrors Gen 6:5–8.
  • Matt 24:37–39Jesus compares the days before his return to “the days of Noah” — life proceeding as normal until sudden judgment.
  • Heb 11:7“By faith Noah, when warned about things not yet seen, in holy fear built an ark” — his righteousness (Gen 6:9) was the fruit of faith.
The Ark — Verses 13–22
13 So God said to Noah, “I am going to put an end to all people, for the earth is filled with violence because of them. I am surely going to destroy both them and the earth. 14 So make yourself an ark of cypress wood; make rooms in it and coat it with pitch inside and out. 15 This is how you are to build it: The ark is to be three hundred cubits long, fifty cubits wide and thirty cubits high. 16 Make a roof for it, leaving below the roof an opening one cubit high all around. Put a door in the side of the ark and make lower, middle and upper decks. 17 I am going to bring floodwaters on the earth to destroy all life under the heavens, every creature that has the breath of life in it. Everything on earth will perish. 18 But I will establish my covenant with you, and you will enter the ark — you and your sons and your wife and your sons’ wives with you. 19 You are to bring into the ark two of all living creatures, male and female, to keep them alive with you. 20 Two of every kind of bird, of every kind of animal and of every kind of creature that moves along the ground will come to you to be kept alive. 21 You are to take every kind of food that is to be eaten and store it away as food for you and for them.” 22 Noah did everything just as God commanded him.

Hebrew Word Study

תֵבָה Tevah — “Ark.” This word appears only twice in the OT: here for Noah’s ark, and in Exodus 2:3 for the basket-ark that carries baby Moses on the Nile. Both are vessels of salvation carrying the hope of the world through water. The connection is deliberate — Moses is a new Noah, and the Exodus is a new flood/new creation.


Berit — “Covenant” (v.18). The first use of the word berit (covenant) in the Bible. It will be formally ratified in chapter 9. God does not destroy without promising restoration. The covenant precedes the catastrophe — grace is announced before judgment falls.


300 cubits × 50 × 30 — A cubit is roughly 18 inches (45 cm). The ark would be approximately 450 ft long × 75 ft wide × 45 ft high (~137 × 23 × 14 m). A displacement of roughly 43,000 tons — comparable to a modern ocean-going vessel. The proportions (6:1 length-to-width ratio) are those of a stable ocean barge, not a sailing ship. Naval architects note these dimensions are optimal for stability in open-sea swells.

Historical Context — Flood Narratives

The Babylonian Gilgamesh Epic (Tablet XI) contains the most detailed flood parallel: Utnapishtim is warned by the god Ea, builds a massive boat, loads animals and family, survives a catastrophic flood, releases birds to find land, and offers sacrifice after landing. The gods gather “like flies” around the sacrifice. The parallels to Genesis are undeniable — both draw on a shared memory of a catastrophic ancient flood event.


The differences are equally significant. In Gilgamesh, the flood is caused by the gods’ annoyance at human noise; salvation is arbitrary; multiple gods quarrel over the outcome. In Genesis: the flood is caused by moral corruption; salvation is covenantal and just; one God acts with grief and purpose. The Genesis account demythologizes and moralizes a common tradition.


Archaeological evidence: The Black Sea Flood hypothesis (Ryan & Pitman, 1997) proposes that around 5600 BCE, rising Mediterranean waters catastrophically broke through the Bosphorus, flooding a freshwater lake to create the Black Sea — displacing large populations throughout the ancient Near East. This event may underlie many flood traditions. Flood deposit layers at Ur (c. 2800 BCE), excavated by Leonard Woolley, once seemed to confirm a local catastrophic flood in Mesopotamia.

Cross-References

  • Ex 2:3The same word tevah for Moses’ basket-ark — linking the two great deliverances through water by the same Hebrew term.
  • 1 Pet 3:20–21Peter reads the ark and flood as a type of baptism — eight people saved through water, now fulfilled in the water of new birth.
  • 2 Pet 2:5“He protected Noah, a preacher of righteousness” — Noah’s building of the ark was itself a witness to his generation.
  • Luke 17:26–27Jesus uses Noah’s day as a type of the end times — ordinary life proceeding until sudden, total judgment.

The most comprehensive statement of human depravity in the OT: every inclination, only evil, all the time. The earth is 'filled with violence' (hamas) — the same word used for structural injustice in the prophets.

⇄ Intertextual Threading

4 canonical threads — 4 forward (passages that echo this chapter)  |  0 backward (texts this chapter echoes)

v.8 — Noah found grace → Eph 2:8–9; Rom 3:24 Echo

The first occurrence of chen (grace/favour) in Scripture is in the context of universal judgment. This establishes the theological pattern: grace appears not as a reward for virtue but as an act of unilateral divine favour in the face of deserved destruction. Paul's 'saved by grace through faith, not by works' (Eph 2:8) is the full articulation of what Gen 6:8 announces with two words.

vv.11–12 — violence filling the earth → Matt 24:37–39; Luke 17:26–27 Echo

Jesus uses the days of Noah as the paradigm for the end times: 'As it was in the days of Noah, so it will be at the coming of the Son of Man.' The flood narrative becomes the eschatological template — sudden judgment falling on a world carrying on normally, with only those prepared surviving. Gen 6's moral diagnosis ('filled with violence') is the condition Jesus identifies as the sign of the end.

v.18 — covenant first occurrence → Gen 9:9–17; 15:18; Jer 31:31 Echo

The first use of berit (covenant) in Scripture is God's promise to Noah (6:18) — before the rainbow, before Sinai, before Abraham. This establishes covenant as the fundamental framework of God's relationship with humanity. The Noahic → Abrahamic → Mosaic → Davidic → New Covenant sequence is all anticipated in this first berit.

v.5 — every inclination evil → Gen 8:21; Jer 17:9; Rom 3:10–18 Echo

God's diagnosis in 6:5 ('every inclination... only evil all the time') is repeated verbatim in 8:21 as the grounds for mercy rather than judgment — the same problem generates opposite responses. Jeremiah 17:9 ('the heart is deceitful above all things') echoes the same anthropological verdict. Paul's catena of OT quotations in Romans 3:10–18 builds the same case: universal human depravity as the precondition for universal grace.

People — Genesis 6

Noah
The Grace-Finder — v.8–9

Noah is introduced with a trio of qualities: he was righteous (tsaddiq), blameless among his contemporaries (tamim), and walked with God (hithallek et ha-Elohim) — the same phrase used of Enoch. He does not speak in ch.6; he simply does everything God commands (v.22). He found chen (grace/favour) in God's eyes — the first use of this crucial word in the Bible. Grace precedes the command, the covenant, and the rescue: Noah is saved not because he earned it but because God's favour rested on him. The NT reads him as a 'preacher of righteousness' (2 Pet 2:5) and a figure of those saved through water in baptism (1 Pet 3:20-21).

The Nephilim
The Enigmatic Giants — v.4

Nefilim — those who fell, or those who cause others to fall. They appear only twice in the OT: Genesis 6:4 and Numbers 13:33 (where the Israelite spies see them in Canaan and feel like grasshoppers). Three main interpretive positions: (1) Sons of God = fallen angels who cohabited with human women (1 Enoch, Jude 6, 2 Pet 2:4 seem to support this); (2) Sons of God = Sethite men who intermarried with Cainite women (Augustine, Calvin — avoids the angel interpretation); (3) Sons of God = divine council members or tyrannical human kings who claimed divine status (Heiser, some modern scholars). The text itself is deliberately cryptic, but the Nephilim represent the catastrophic corruption of the human-divine boundary.

Ancient Source Texts — Flood Traditions

Gilgamesh Epic — The Decision to Send the Flood (Tablet XI)
The gods were frightened by the Deluge, and, shrinking back, they ascended to the heaven of Anu. The gods cowered like dogs crouched against the outer wall. Ishtar shrieked like a woman in childbirth... 'The olden days are alas turned to clay, because I said evil things in the assembly of the gods.'

In Gilgamesh, the flood is sent because the gods are annoyed by human noise. The gods then regret it because they lose their food offerings from humans. They cower, quarrel, and mourn. Genesis replaces this divine chaos with moral seriousness: God sends the flood because of human violence and corruption, grieves with genuine pathos, and has a redemptive purpose (rescue Noah, restart humanity). The contrast in divine character is the theological core of Genesis 6.

1 Enoch — The Watchers (Chapters 6–11, c. 300–200 BCE)
It came to pass when the children of men had multiplied that in those days were born unto them beautiful and comely daughters. And the angels, the children of the heaven, saw and lusted after them, and said to one another: 'Come, let us choose us wives from among the children of men and beget us children.'

1 Enoch's expansion of Genesis 6:1-4 names 200 angel-'Watchers' who descend on Mount Hermon, marry human women, and teach humanity forbidden arts (weapons, sorcery, cosmetics). Their offspring are the giants who corrupt the earth. This tradition was enormously influential in the Second Temple period and shapes the allusions in Jude 6 and 2 Peter 2:4. Jude's citation of 1 Enoch (14-15) and Peter's 'angels who sinned' both appear to endorse the angelic interpretation of Genesis 6.

Reception History — Genesis 6

Origen — Against Celsus (c. 248 CE)

Origen accepted the angelic interpretation of 'sons of God' as historical but read it allegorically: the angels represent souls that descend from spiritual realms into material existence. He used Gen 6 to develop his controversial pre-existence of souls doctrine — souls existed before bodies and 'fell' into human flesh as a consequence of prior choices. This reading, condemned at the Council of Constantinople (553 CE), shows how Genesis 6's cryptic verses could generate radically different theological systems depending on one's hermeneutical framework.

Augustine — City of God (Book XV, Chapters 22–23)

Augustine rejected the angelic interpretation entirely, finding it physically incredible and theologically dangerous. He argued the 'sons of God' were the righteous Sethites who intermarried with the wicked Cainites, corrupting the godly line. His alternative became dominant in the Western church through the medieval period. However, Augustine acknowledged the text was difficult and that the Hebrew benei ha-Elohim could support the angelic reading — he simply found the Sethite reading more theologically coherent.

Michael Heiser — The Unseen Realm (2015)

Heiser's influential recent work argues the standard translations obscure the text's own framework: 'sons of God' is divine council language throughout the OT, never referring to humans. He situates Genesis 6 in the context of the divine council rebellion, where lesser divine beings violate the human-divine boundary, producing the Nephilim and triggering the flood as God's response to both human and supernatural rebellion. Heiser argues this 'cosmic geography' framework (developed through Deut 32:8-9 and Ps 82) is essential background for understanding the NT's language about principalities and powers.

Literary Structure — Parallel with Genesis 1

Genesis 6 deliberately mirrors Genesis 1 in reverse — creation language is systematically negated to show that the flood is an un-creation:

Gen 1God saw all he had made — very good (1:31)
Gen 6God saw how great wickedness had become (6:5)
—
Gen 1Let us make mankind in our image (1:26)
Gen 6Every inclination of thoughts of heart — only evil (6:5)
—
Gen 1God blessed them: be fruitful and fill the earth (1:28)
Gen 6The earth was corrupt and filled with violence (6:11-12)
—
Gen 1God separated the waters above from below (1:6-7)
Gen 7Floodgates above and springs below burst open (7:11)

The flood narrative is a literary chiasm at the macro level — the entire structure from 6:9 to 9:17 is built as a mirror around 8:1 (God remembered Noah). Every element of the flood’s coming has a corresponding element of its recession. The author of Genesis is not simply recording events but constructing a theological narrative with the precision of a liturgical poet.
