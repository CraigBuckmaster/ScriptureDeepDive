Genesis 34

The Defiling of Dinah

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration. What prompted it: A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–12 — The Violation of Dinah; Shechem's Request
1Now Dinah, the daughter Leah had borne to Jacob, went out to visit the women of the land.2When Shechem son of Hamor the Hivite, the ruler of that area, saw her, he took her and raped her.3His heart was drawn to Dinah daughter of Jacob; he loved the young woman and spoke tenderly to her.4And Shechem said to his father Hamor, "Get me this girl as my wife."5When Jacob heard that his daughter Dinah had been defiled, his sons were in the fields with his livestock; so he did nothing about it until they came home.6Then Shechem's father Hamor went out to talk with Jacob.7Now Jacob's sons had come in from the fields as soon as they heard what had happened. They were shocked and furious, because Shechem had done an outrageous thing in Israel by sleeping with Jacob's daughter—a thing that should not be done.8But Hamor said to them, "My son Shechem has his heart set on your daughter. Please give her to him as his wife.9Intermarry with us; give us your daughters and take our daughters for yourselves.10You can settle among us; the land is open to you. Live in it, trade in it, and acquire property in it."11Then Shechem said to Dinah's father and brothers, "Let me find favor in your eyes, and I will give you whatever you ask.12Make the price for the bride and the gift I am to bring as great as you like, and I'll pay whatever you ask me. Only give me the young woman as my wife."

Hebrew Word Study

טִמֵּא ṭimmēʾ — defiled / humiliated: The verb ṭāmēʾ (defile) carries both ritual and moral weight in the OT. Violation of a person makes them ritually impure and morally wronged simultaneously.

נְבָלָה nebālāh — outrageous thing / folly: Appears in cases of sexual violation (2 Sam 13:12) and other grave social offenses. It describes an act that violates the foundational order of society — not merely personal sin but communal rupture.

דִּבֶּר עַל לְבָהּ dibber ʿal libbāh — spoke to her heart: The tenderness of v.3 makes the assault more not less troubling — Shechem acts with violent entitlement and then romanticizes it. The same phrase is used of Boaz toward Ruth (Ruth 2:13) in a context of genuine honor.

Historical Context

Shechem was a major Bronze Age city controlling the central highland pass. The Hivites (v.2) were one of the pre-Israelite peoples of Canaan. The negotiation for intermarriage and circumcision reflects actual Bronze Age practices of diplomatic assimilation between semi-nomadic groups and settled urban populations — the Shechemites see Jacob's clan as a desirable addition to their economic sphere (v.23: "Will not their livestock, their property and all their other animals become ours?").

Context

The narrative is told with deliberate moral complexity. Dinah "went out to see the women of the land" — an unguarded exposure to Canaanite culture that parallels Lot's movement toward Sodom. Shechem's love is real (v.3) but his initial act is criminal. Jacob's silence (v.5: "held his peace") is read by the brothers as passivity; Jacob will later condemn their response (49:5–7). The text refuses to endorse any character: Dinah is a victim; Shechem is both a criminal and a sincere suitor; Jacob is passive; the brothers are righteous in their indignation but murderous in their response.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
34:1–2

The tragedy of Genesis 34 begins with Dinah going out "to see the women of the land." This is not a moral condemnation of Dinah; she is a victim. But the narrative locates the crisis in the unguarded exposure of covenant people to Canaanite culture. Jacob's settling at Shechem (33:18–19) brought his daughter within range of a Canaanite prince. The consequences of compromise with the surrounding culture are paid in the currency of the most vulnerable.

34:5,7

Jacob's silence (v.5) contrasts sharply with his sons' indignation (v.7). The sons correctly identify Shechem's act as nebālāh — "such a thing must not be done in Israel." They name the moral reality with clarity. Jacob does not. His passivity throughout this chapter — never confronting Shechem directly, never defending Dinah publicly, only complaining about the consequences for himself (v.30) — is a failure of paternal and covenant leadership that Jacob will eventually acknowledge at the end of his life (49:5–7).

Verses 13–31 — The Deception and the Massacre
13Because their sister Dinah had been defiled, Jacob's sons replied deceitfully as they spoke to Shechem and his father Hamor.14They said to them, "We can't do such a thing; we can't give our sister to a man who is not circumcised. That would be a disgrace to us.15We will enter into an agreement with you on one condition only: that you become like us by circumcising all your males.16Then we will give you our daughters and take your daughters for ourselves. We'll settle among you and become one people with you.17But if you will not agree to be circumcised, we'll take our sister and go."18Their proposal seemed good to Hamor and his son Shechem.19The young man, who was the most honored of all his father's family, lost no time in doing what they said, because he was delighted with Jacob's daughter.20So Hamor and his son Shechem went to the gate of their city to speak to the men of their city.21"These men are friendly toward us," they said. "Let them live in our land and trade in it; the land has plenty of room for them. We can marry their daughters and they can marry ours.22But the men will agree to live with us as one people only on the condition that our males be circumcised, as they themselves are.23Won't their livestock, their property and all their other animals become ours? So let us agree to their terms, and they will settle among us."24All the men who went out of the city gate agreed with Hamor and his son Shechem, and every male in the city was circumcised.25Three days later, while all of them were still in pain, two of Jacob's sons, Simeon and Levi, Dinah's brothers, took their swords and attacked the unsuspecting city, killing every male.26They put Hamor and his son Shechem to the sword and took Dinah from Shechem's house and left.27The sons of Jacob came upon the dead bodies and looted the city where their sister had been defiled.28They seized their flocks and herds and donkeys and everything else of theirs in the city and out in the fields.29They carried off all their wealth and all their women and children, taking as plunder everything in the houses.30Then Jacob said to Simeon and Levi, "You have brought trouble on me by making me obnoxious to the Canaanites and Perizzites, the people living in this land. We are few in number, and if they join forces against me and attack me, I and my household will be destroyed."31But they replied, "Should he have treated our sister like a prostitute?"

Hebrew Word Study

מִרְמָה mirmāh — deceit / treachery: The brothers "answered deceitfully" (v.13). The same root used of Jacob's deception of Isaac (27:35). The family of deception continues: Jacob deceived his father; his sons now use deception — in Jacob's name and heritage — to arrange a massacre.

בֶּטַח beṭaḥ — securely / unsuspecting: The city "felt secure" (v.25) — the exact betrayal of the covenant of circumcision. A sign meant for the covenant people was weaponized against those who received it in good faith.

Context

Simeon and Levi's response is disproportionate and treacherous: they weaponize the covenant sign of circumcision to incapacitate and then massacre an entire city. Their final question (v.31) is unanswered by Jacob in the text — but Jacob's dying blessing (49:5–7) does answer it: he condemns their anger as cruel and their violence as self-willed. They are right that Dinah was wronged; they are wrong in how they respond. The narrative holds both truths simultaneously without resolving the tension.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 49:5–7Jacob's deathbed curse on Simeon and Levi: "Cursed be their anger... I will scatter them in Israel." The consequence of this chapter echoes decades later.
  • 2 Sam 13:1–22Amnon's assault of Tamar and Absalom's revenge follows the same moral topology: sexual violation, silence from the father (David), violent revenge from a brother.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
34:25–26

The massacre at Shechem is one of the darkest acts in the patriarchal narratives. Simeon and Levi kill an entire city — men who had in good faith undergone circumcision — to avenge their sister. The act cannot be defended. It is treacherous, disproportionate, and a desecration of the covenant sign. Yet it arises from genuine love for Dinah and genuine outrage at injustice. The brothers are not monsters; they are men of passionate loyalty who have no framework for measured justice and no restraint from a passive father.

34:30–31

The confrontation between Jacob and his sons reduces to a single exchange: Jacob complains about the political consequences for himself; his sons ask about the moral consequences for their sister. Jacob's complaint is entirely self-referential: "you have made me stink," "I shall be destroyed." He does not once mention Dinah. His sons ask the right question — "should he have treated our sister like a prostitute?" — but they pursued the right question through the wrong means. Both father and sons fail Dinah, in different directions.

Ancient Sources

LXX differences
The Septuagint adds details softening the narrative: the brothers' deception is described more explicitly, and Hamor's speech is expanded.
The LXX revision shows early interpreters wrestling with the moral ambiguity of the chapter.
Jubilees 30
The book of Jubilees (2nd c. BC) strongly endorses Simeon and Levi's action as righteous — those who give a daughter to a Gentile deserve death.
Jubilees represents an interpretation that resolves the narrative's tension by unambiguously validating the revenge.

Reception History

Origen
The chapter is a type of the soul's defilement through contact with worldly pleasures and the necessary violence required to purge them — an allegorical reading that distances the discomfort.
Calvin, Commentary
Calvin refuses to endorse either Jacob's passivity or the brothers' massacre. He argues that the deception was sinful even if the grief was just, and that vengeance belongs to God.
Modern Feminist Readings
Contemporary scholarship highlights Dinah's silence and invisibility in her own story — she disappears after v.3, never asked what she wants or feels. The text represents a world where women's voices are structurally excluded from the narrative.

C' — vv.18–24The Shechemites accept the condition
B' — vv.25–29Sons act on anger; massacre and plunder

The chiasm centers on the deceitful circumcision condition — the hinge around which the Shechemites' fate turns. The final unanswered question (v.31) is deliberate: the narrative refuses to close morally.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 34 is the darkest chapter of the Jacob narrative, deliberately unresolved. It holds together genuine outrage at injustice, disproportionate response, paternal failure, and the unheard voice of the victim. The final question hangs without answer — the text insists on the reader's moral engagement.
