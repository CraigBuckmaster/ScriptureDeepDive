Genesis 11

Tower of Babel & The Line to Abram

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration.

What prompted it

A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–9 — The Tower of Babel
1Now the whole world had one language and a common speech.2As people moved eastward, they found a plain in Shinar and settled there.3They said to each other, "Come, let's make bricks and bake them thoroughly." They used brick instead of stone, and tar for mortar.4Then they said, "Come, let us build ourselves a city, with a tower that reaches to the heavens, so that we may make a name for ourselves; otherwise we will be scattered over the face of the whole earth."5But the Lord came down to see the city and the tower the people were building.6The Lord said, "If as one people speaking the same language they have begun to do this, then nothing they plan to do will be impossible for them.7Come, let us go down and confuse their language so they will not understand each other."8So the Lord scattered them from there over all the earth, and they stopped building the city.9That is why it was called Babel—because there the Lord confused the language of the whole world. From there the Lord scattered them over the face of the whole earth.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:1–4

Babel is the definitive case study in human pride. The project has four features: (1) a city — permanent, organised human power; (2) a tower reaching to the heavens — not literally to God's residence but a ziggurat, a cultic structure claiming to bridge earth and heaven on human terms; (3) a name — self-glorification, the opposite of God's command to Abraham ("I will make your name great," 12:2) where the name comes as a divine gift; (4) a purpose — "so that we may not be scattered over the face of the whole earth," in explicit defiance of the creational mandate to fill the earth (1:28; 9:1). The project is not wicked in its ambition to build but in its orientation: for human glory, not God's; for human unity against God, not in service of him.

Hebrew Word Study

שָׂפָה sāpāh — lip / language: The word for "language" is literally "lip" (śāpāh), emphasizing speech as the medium of human solidarity and rebellion.

מִגֶּדֶם miqqedem — from the east: The direction "east" in Genesis consistently marks movement away from God — Eden's east gate, Cain going east, now this.

מִגְדָּל migdāl — tower: Not simply a building but a staged temple-tower (ziggurat) meant to bridge heaven and earth — a human usurpation of divine accessibility.

שֵׁם šēm — name: The builders want to "make a name" (šēm) for themselves — an ironic contrast with God's later promise to make Abram's name great (12:2).

Historical Context

The Babel narrative reflects Mesopotamian ziggurat culture. The Etemenanki ("house of the foundation of heaven and earth") in Babylon may be the specific referent — a massive 7-stage ziggurat dedicated to Marduk. Cuneiform texts describe these structures as cosmic mountains connecting heaven and earth. The use of fired brick and bitumen (rather than stone and mortar, as in Canaan) is archaeologically accurate for the Mesopotamian plain, confirmed by excavations at Ur and Uruk.

Context

The Babel story concludes the primeval history (Gen 1–11) by explaining the origin of language diversity. It stands in ironic contrast to Pentecost (Acts 2), where the Spirit reverses the confusion. The "one language" echoes the pre-fall unity of creation; the tower project is humanity's corporate attempt at self-deification — a societal Adam-and-Eve rebellion.

Places & Geography

Shinar
Mesopotamia / Babylonia
The plain of Shinar is the Babylonian alluvial plain between the Tigris and Euphrates — the heartland of ancient Sumer and later Babylon. Settled c. 4000 BC.
Babel
Babylon, Iraq
Babel (bāḇel) is the Hebrew name for Babylon. The text provides a popular etymology: God "confused" (bālal) the language there. The historical city of Babylon became the empire that would later exile Israel.
Verses 10–26 — From Shem to Abram: The Genealogy of Promise
10This is the account of Shem's family line. Two years after the flood, when Shem was 100 years old, he became the father of Arphaxad.11And after he became the father of Arphaxad, Shem lived 500 years and had other sons and daughters.12When Arphaxad had lived 35 years, he became the father of Shelah.13And after he became the father of Shelah, Arphaxad lived 403 years and had other sons and daughters.14When Shelah had lived 30 years, he became the father of Eber.15And after he became the father of Eber, Shelah lived 403 years and had other sons and daughters.16When Eber had lived 34 years, he became the father of Peleg.17And after he became the father of Peleg, Eber lived 430 years and had other sons and daughters.18When Peleg had lived 30 years, he became the father of Reu.19And after he became the father of Reu, Peleg lived 209 years and had other sons and daughters.20When Reu had lived 32 years, he became the father of Serug.21And after he became the father of Serug, Reu lived 207 years and had other sons and daughters.22When Serug had lived 30 years, he became the father of Nahor.23And after he became the father of Nahor, Serug lived 200 years and had other sons and daughters.24When Nahor had lived 29 years, he became the father of Terah.25And after he became the father of Terah, Nahor lived 119 years and had other sons and daughters.26After Terah had lived 70 years, he became the father of Abram, Nahor and Haran.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:5

"But the LORD came down to see the city and the tower the people were building." The divine descent is deeply ironic: the tower built to reach heaven is so small that God must "come down" to even see it. The anthropomorphism is deliberate humour at human pretension. God's response (vv.6–8) is not panic but judicial action: if unchecked, human ambition will continue to escalate. The confusing of language is an act of mercy as much as judgment — it prevents the catastrophe of a unified, God-defying humanity from completing what it has started. Pentecost (Acts 2) is the beginning of Babel's reversal: where Babel divided by language, Pentecost unifies across languages, pointing to the final gathering of every tribe, language, people, and nation (Rev 7:9).

Hebrew Word Study

יָרַד yārad — came down: The irony is sharp: humanity's tower reaches toward heaven, yet God must descend to even see it — the tower is cosmically insignificant despite human pride.

הָבָה hāḇāh — Come!: The divine "Come, let us go down" (v.7) directly mocks the human "Come, let us build" (v.4) — God takes the initiative and undoes their project using their own verbal pattern.

בָּלַל bālal — confuse / mix: The root bālal gives the name "Babel" a satirical folk etymology. Babylon's own name meant "gate of god" (bāb-ilim); Genesis redefines it as "confusion."

Context

The divine "Let us go down" (v.7) echoes "Let us make man" (1:26), suggesting a divine council. God's response is not angry destruction but dispersal — the very thing the builders feared (v.4). The scattering enacts the Genesis 1 mandate to "fill the earth" which humanity was refusing. Judgment and creation-purpose converge.

  • Zeph 3:9God promises to "restore to the peoples a pure language" — eschatological reversal of Babel.
Verses 27–32 — Terah's Family; the Journey to Harran
27This is the account of Terah's family line. Terah became the father of Abram, Nahor and Haran. And Haran became the father of Lot.28While his father Terah was still alive, Haran died in Ur of the Chaldeans, in the land of his birth.29Abram and Nahor both married. The name of Abram's wife was Sarai, and the name of Nahor's wife was Milkah; she was the daughter of Haran, the father of both Milkah and Iskah.30Now Sarai was childless because she was not able to conceive.31Terah took his son Abram, his grandson Lot son of Haran, and his daughter-in-law Sarai, the wife of his son Abram, and together they set out from Ur of the Chaldeans to go to Canaan. But when they came to Harran, they settled there.32Terah lived 205 years, and he died in Harran.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
11:26–32

The genealogy from Shem to Abram narrows the redemptive focus dramatically. The lifespan figures decrease: we are moving toward normal human mortality. Terah takes Abram, Sarai, and Lot from Ur of the Chaldeans toward Canaan — but stops at Haran and dies there. The journey toward the promised land is interrupted by the death of the patriarch. Abram will resume it only when God's direct call reaches him (12:1). Stephen's speech (Acts 7:2–4) clarifies that God's call came while Abram was still in Ur — Terah's death at Haran is a delay, not the starting point. The call precedes the obedience; grace initiates before faith responds.

Hebrew Word Study

תּוֹלְדֹת tôlĕdôt — generations / account: The tôlĕdôt formula ("these are the generations of") structures all of Genesis into 10 sections. This is the 9th occurrence, bridging from the nations to Abraham.

אוּר כַּשְׂדִּים ʾûr kaśdîm — Ur of the Chaldeans: Ur was one of the great Sumerian city-states. "Of the Chaldeans" is an anachronistic clarification for later readers who knew Chaldea as the region — a feature consistent with Moses writing for Israel's understanding.

חָרָן Ḥārān — Haran: Both a personal name (Abram's deceased brother, v.26) and a city name — the same Hebrew spelling. The city Haran (modern Harran, Turkey) was a major moon-god (Sîn) worship center, like Ur.

Historical Context

Archaeological excavations at Ur (modern Tell el-Muqayyar, Iraq) by Leonard Woolley (1920s–30s) revealed a sophisticated city of 65,000+ people with advanced art, writing, and ziggurat worship of the moon-god Nanna. The patriarchal migration from Ur to Haran to Canaan follows documented trade and migration routes of the early 2nd millennium BC. Haran (Harran) served as a major node on the Fertile Crescent trade network.

Places & Geography

Ur of the Chaldeans
Southern Iraq (Tell el-Muqayyar)
One of the great Sumerian city-states, excavated by Woolley. The ziggurat of Ur-Nammu still stands. Center of moon-god Nanna worship. Abraham's departure marks a radical break with Mesopotamian religion.
Haran
Southeastern Turkey (modern Harran)
A major ancient city on the Balikh River. Also a moon-god worship center. The family stopped here — perhaps because the familiar religion made it a comfortable halfway point. God's call would push Abram the rest of the way.

Timeline

c. 2166 BC (traditional) — Abraham born in Ur during the height of the Ur III dynasty

c. 2091 BC — Departure from Haran at age 75 (Gen 12:4)

Ur III period (2112–2004 BC) — The Sumerian renaissance — high culture, ziggurats, administrative complexity

Early 2nd millennium — Migration patterns from Mesopotamia to Canaan well-attested in cuneiform records

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Terah
Patriarch; Father of Abram, Nahor, Haran
Initiated the journey from Ur but stopped short in Haran, dying there at 205. His name may connect to the Hebrew word for moon worship (yārēaḥ-related). He is the last patriarch of the old world and the first of the new.
Abram (Abraham)
Son of Terah; Patriarch of Israel
Introduced here without fanfare — just one more name in a genealogy. The abruptness sets up the explosive call of chapter 12. His wife Sarai is noted as barren, raising the question that will drive Genesis 12–25.
Sarai
Wife of Abram; Later Sarah
Introduced with her barrenness as the defining fact: "Sarai was barren; she had no child" (v.30). This is not incidental detail — it is the theological problem that chapter 12 onward will address.
Lot
Son of Haran; Nephew of Abram
Orphaned early (father Haran died in Ur), Lot will travel with Abram to Canaan and become a foil to Abraham's faith throughout Genesis 13–19.

Ancient Sources

Enuma Elish (Babylonian Creation Epic)
The Babylonian creation myth describes Marduk building Babylon and the Esagila temple as a cosmic center — precisely the kind of "name-making" city building that Genesis 11 critiques. The biblical account inverts the Babylonian theology: God, not Marduk, controls the ordering of humanity.
The parallel is deliberately polemical — Israel's scripture answers Babylon's self-aggrandizing mythology.
Jubilees 10:18–26 (2nd c. BC)
The Book of Jubilees elaborates the Babel story: Mastema (the adversary) assists the builders; the confusion of languages involves angels speaking different tongues to different groups.
Shows how Second Temple Judaism found the Babel story generative for angelology and the theology of the nations.
Josephus, Antiquities 1.4.3
Josephus identifies Nimrod (Gen 10:8–10) as the instigator of the Babel project, building the tower as a monument against another flood. He describes the tower as being made of baked brick with bitumen for mortar — matching the Genesis account precisely.
Josephus's retelling shows the story was read historically in the 1st century, not as myth.

Reception History

Augustine, City of God XIX
Augustine saw Babel as the foundational text for understanding the earthly city — humanity organized around self-love rather than love of God. Babylon becomes his paradigm for all human civilization oriented away from the divine.
John Calvin, Commentary on Genesis
Calvin emphasized God's providential use of the scattering: what humanity feared (dispersion) became the mechanism by which the earth was populated per Gen 1:28. Judgment and blessing intertwine.
Modern Scholarship
Claus Westermann and others see the Babel story as an etiology for language diversity with no necessary historical claim. Evangelical scholars like Kenneth Mathews and Bruce Waltke argue the narrative is historically grounded while carrying profound theological weight about human rebellion.

Literary Structure

A
One language, one people (vv.1–2)
B
Human project: "Come, let us build" (vv.3–4)
CENTER
God descends to see (v.5)
B'
Divine counter-project: "Come, let us go down" (vv.6–7)
A'
Many languages, scattered peoples (vv.8–9)
The Babel narrative is a perfect chiasm centered on God's descent (v.5). The structure enacts the theme: human ascent meets divine descent, and the divine wins. The repeated "Come, let us" in both human speech (v.4) and divine speech (v.7) creates deliberate irony.

Intertextual Threading

Gen 1:28→Gen 11:4Echo
The creation mandate was "fill the earth" — Babel is humanity's organized refusal. God's judgment simply enforces the original design.
Gen 11:4→Gen 12:2Echo
The builders want to "make a name" (šēm) for themselves. God immediately offers to "make Abram's name great" — the covenant answer to Babel's self-exaltation.
Gen 11:9→Acts 2:4–11Reversal
Pentecost reverses Babel: the Spirit enables mutual understanding across the language barrier. The Babel curse is undone in Christ's community.
Gen 11:28–31→Acts 7:2–4Quotation
Stephen's Pentecost-era speech rehearses God's call to Abraham from Ur — explicitly citing these verses as the start of Israel's story.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 11 pivots the entire Bible. The primeval history ends in fragmentation (Babel) and death (genealogy's repeated "and he died"). But the genealogy accelerates — shortening lifespans, narrowing to one family — until it reaches Abram and the stunning silence: Sarai is barren. Everything now hangs on whether God can make something of nothing.
