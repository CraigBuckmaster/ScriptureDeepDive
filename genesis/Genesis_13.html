Genesis 13

Abram and Lot Separate

Timeline

Genesis 13 follows naturally from the Egypt episode. Assigned to the Yahwist (J) by critical scholars, it narrates the separation of Abram and Lot with elegant economy. The contrast between Lot's choice (by sight, toward Sodom) and Abram's trust (by faith, receiving the promise again) is the theological heart.

Verses 1–9 — Abram's Generous Offer
1So Abram went up from Egypt, he and his wife and all that he had, and Lot with him, into the Negeb.5And Lot, who went with Abram, also had flocks and herds and tents,6so that the land could not support both of them dwelling together; for their possessions were so great that they could not dwell together.8Then Abram said to Lot, "Let there be no strife between you and me, and between your herdsmen and my herdsmen, for we are kinsmen.9"Is not the whole land before you? Separate yourself from me. If you take the left hand, then I will go to the right, or if you take the right hand, then I will go to the left."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:8–9

Abram's offer to Lot is remarkable: as the senior, the patriarch, the covenant-bearer, he had prior right to choose. Instead he defers, giving Lot the choice of the whole land. This is the fruit of genuine faith: the one who trusts God's promise doesn't need to grasp for the best position. Abram can afford to be generous because his inheritance comes from God, not from his own securing of the best land. The contrast with Lot's choice (lifting his eyes toward the well-watered Jordan plain) will be developed through chapters 13–19 as a study in the catastrophic cost of choosing by sight rather than by faith.

Context

Abram's generosity to Lot is striking — as the patriarch and elder, he had every right to choose first. Instead he defers, trusting God to fulfill the promise regardless of which portion of land he receives. This is a model of faith in action: holding the promise loosely, letting God provide. Lot's choice will prove catastrophically poor.

Cross-Reference

  • Prov 3:5–6Trusting in the LORD rather than leaning on one's own sight — the principle Abram embodies and Lot violates.
  • 2 Pet 2:7–8Peter calls Lot "righteous" — he was not wicked, but his choice to live near Sodom shows the danger of choosing by sight rather than faith.
Verses 10–13 — Lot Chooses the Valley
10And Lot lifted up his eyes and saw that the Jordan Valley was well watered everywhere like the garden of the LORD, like the land of Egypt, in the direction of Zoar.11So Lot chose for himself all the Jordan Valley, and Lot journeyed east, and they separated from each other.12Abram settled in the land of Canaan, while Lot settled among the cities of the valley and moved his tent as far as Sodom.13Now the men of Sodom were wicked, great sinners against the LORD.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
13:10–13

Lot "looked up and saw that the whole plain of the Jordan toward Zoar was well watered, like the garden of the LORD" — the comparison to Eden is deliberate and ominous. The Jordan plain appears as paradise but is next door to Sodom and Gomorrah, where "the people were wicked and were sinning greatly against the LORD." Lot chooses by the eyes: the land looks like Eden; it is adjacent to hell. First Peter 2:7–8 calls Lot a righteous man tormented by what he saw and heard — he was never comfortable in Sodom. But he stayed. The incremental movement toward compromise (first near the cities, then in the cities, 14:12) is the anatomy of spiritual drift.

Hebrew Word Study

יִשָּׂא yiśśāʾ — lifted up his eyes: Lot "saw" with natural eyes and chose by appearance. Throughout Genesis, those who "lift up their eyes" to choose by sight (rather than faith) make disastrous choices — cf. Eve (3:6), Potiphar's wife, etc.

גַּן־יְהוָה gan-YHWH — garden of the LORD: The Jordan Valley is described as Eden-like — but Eden was lost; this apparent paradise near Sodom will prove equally deadly. The comparison is an ironic warning the reader catches before Lot does.

רָעִים rāʿîm — wicked: The Hebrew root suggests not merely bad behavior but fundamental opposition to the good (ṭôḇ) of creation. Sodom's wickedness is announced here as a narrative warning before the disaster of chapters 18–19.

Context

Lot's choice is described in Eden-like language — the Jordan Valley "like the garden of the LORD." This echoes 2:8–9, but the paradise is illusory: Sodom lurks at the end of the road. The text tells us immediately that Sodom's men were "wicked, great sinners" — information Lot apparently did not consider. He chose by sight, Abram by faith.

Places & Geography

Jordan Valley
Rift valley, southern Israel/Jordan
The fertile depression of the Jordan River south toward the Dead Sea. In antiquity this was one of the most agriculturally productive regions in Canaan — which is why it looked so attractive to Lot.
Sodom
Possibly south end of Dead Sea
The precise location of Sodom remains debated. Some archaeologists identify it with Tall el-Hammam in Jordan (Bronze Age city destroyed violently c. 1650 BC); others place it south of the Dead Sea. Genesis treats it as a real city.
Verses 14–18 — God Renews the Promise
14The LORD said to Abram, after Lot had separated from him, "Lift up your eyes and look from the place where you are, northward and southward and eastward and westward,15"for all the land that you see I will give to you and to your offspring forever.17"Arise, walk through the length and the breadth of the land, for I will give it to you."18So Abram moved his tent and came and settled by the oaks of Mamre, which are at Hebron, and there he built an altar to the LORD.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14–17

After Lot's departure God renews the land promise — now with expanded language: "all the land that you see I will give to you and your offspring forever." The four directions (north, south, east, west) indicate totality. "Offspring like the dust of the earth" — innumerable. God's response to Abram's generosity is an enlargement of the promise. This is the pattern of kingdom ethics: those who give up what they could have grasped receive more from God than they released. Abram moves to Hebron and builds another altar — the third since entering Canaan. Wherever the promise is renewed, worship follows.

Hebrew Word Study

שָׂא עֵינֶיךָ śāʾ ʿênêkā — lift up your eyes: God now commands Abram to "lift up his eyes" — the same action Lot performed in v.10, but now divinely directed. Lot saw with natural eyes and chose Sodom; Abram sees at God's command and receives the whole land.

מַמְרֵא Mamrēʾ — Mamre: A proper name (later identified as an Amorite ally of Abram, 14:13) and a location near Hebron. Becomes the base camp for the Abraham narrative — the oaks of Mamre will be the setting for the divine visit in ch.18.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 15:18The land promise is formalized in covenant: "To your offspring I give this land, from the river of Egypt to the great river, the river Euphrates."
  • Heb 11:9Abraham "lived in the land of promise, as in a foreign land, living in tents" — the promise was real but not yet possessed, requiring faith.
  • Gal 3:16Paul identifies the "offspring" as singular — Christ — connecting this land promise to its ultimate fulfillment in the new creation.

Places & Geography

Mamre / Hebron
Southern hill country (modern Hebron)
Hebron (alt. name Kiriath-arba) becomes Abraham's home base. The cave of Machpelah nearby becomes the patriarchal burial site (ch.23). One of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world, c. 3300 BC. Sacred to Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.
Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Abram
Patriarch; model of generosity and faith
By surrendering the first choice to Lot, Abram demonstrates the counter-intuitive economics of faith: release what you can see and trust what God has promised. The promise is immediately renewed after Lot departs (v.14).
Lot
Nephew; contrast figure to Abram
Lot's choice is tragic in slow motion — he moves toward Sodom by stages: the valley (v.11), among the cities (v.12), then to Sodom itself (13:12 → 14:12 → 19:1, sitting in the gate). Each chapter he is deeper in.

Ancient Sources

Josephus, Antiquities 1.8.1–2
Josephus notes that the region of Sodom was exceptionally fertile before divine judgment, and that traces of divine fire could still be seen in his day (1st c. AD) — possibly referring to the sulfur-rich terrain around the Dead Sea.
Shows the text was read geographically/historically in the 1st century.

Reception History

Augustine, City of God XI.1
Augustine uses the Abram/Lot separation to illustrate the two cities: Abram represents the City of God (ordered by love of God), Lot drifts toward the earthly city (ordered by self-interest).
John Chrysostom
Chrysostom praised Abram's yielding to Lot as a supreme act of virtue: "He gave up the whole earth to his nephew" — despite being the patriarch with prior claim.

Literary Structure

ALot goes east (toward Sodom)
BAbram stays in Canaan
CENTER: Promise renewed to Abram (vv.14–16)
B'Abram walks the land
A'Abram settles at Mamre/Hebron; altar built
The chapter turns on the renewal of the promise in vv.14–17 — the center of a loose concentric structure. Lot's departure (A) is answered by God filling the relational gap with the divine presence (C), then Abram taking symbolic possession by walking the land (B').

Hebrew-Rooted Reading

10 And Lot lifted up his eyes and saw that the Jordan Valley was well watered everywhere like the garden [gan] of the LORD [YHWH], like the land of Egypt. 14 The LORD said to Abram after Lot had separated from him, "Lift up your eyes and look from the place where you are... 15 for all the land that you see I will give to you and to your offspring [zeraʿ] forever. 17 Arise, walk through the length and the breadth of the land, for I will give it to you." 18 So Abram settled by the oaks of Mamre, which are at Hebron, and there he built an altar to the LORD.

Intertextual Threading

Gen 3:6→Gen 13:10Echo
Eve "saw that the tree was good for food, pleasing to the eye" — Lot "saw that the Jordan Valley was well watered." Both choose by sight rather than divine word. Both choices lead toward disaster.
Gen 13:15→Gal 3:16Fulfillment
Paul reads the "offspring" (zeraʿ) of the land promise as singular, pointing to Christ as the ultimate heir of the Abrahamic land grant.
Gen 13:12→Gen 19:1Descent
Lot's trajectory toward Sodom: valley (13:11) → near cities (13:12) → inside Sodom at the gate (19:1). Gradual compromise has a destination.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 13 is a study in two ways of seeing. Lot "lifted up his eyes" and chose by natural sight — beautiful land, prosperous valley, proximity to Sodom. Abram surrendered his rights and received everything. The contrast becomes a template for the life of faith: those who grasp lose; those who release receive.
