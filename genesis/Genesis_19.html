Genesis 19

Sodom and Gomorrah Destroyed

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration.

What prompted it

A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–11 — The Angels at Sodom; Lot's Hospitality
1The two angels arrived at Sodom in the evening, and Lot was sitting in the gateway of the city. When he saw them, he got up to meet them and bowed down with his face to the ground.2"My lords," he said, "please turn aside to your servant's house. You can wash your feet and spend the night and then go on your way early in the morning." "No," they answered, "we will spend the night in the square."3But he insisted so strongly that they did go with him and entered his house. He prepared a meal for them, baking bread without yeast, and they ate.4Before they had gone to bed, all the men from every part of the city of Sodom—both young and old—surrounded the house.5They called to Lot, "Where are the men who came to you tonight? Bring them out to us so that we can have sex with them."6Lot went outside to meet them and shut the door behind him7and said, "No, my friends. Don't do this wicked thing.8Look, I have two daughters who have never slept with a man. Let me bring them out to you, and you can do what you like with them. But don't do anything to these men, for they have come under the protection of my roof."9"Get out of our way," they replied. "This fellow came here as a foreigner, and now he wants to play the judge! We'll treat you worse than them." They kept bringing pressure on Lot and moved forward to break down the door.10But the men inside reached out and pulled Lot back into the house and shut the door.11Then they struck the men who were at the door of the house, young and old, with blindness so that they could not find the door.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
19:1–3

Lot sits at the gate of Sodom — the position of civic authority, a magistrate. He has risen to a position of social prominence in the city he chose for its appearance. Yet when the angels arrive, he immediately prostrates himself and urges them strongly not to spend the night in the square. Lot knows what Sodom is. He has accommodated himself to it enough to hold civic office, but he has not become comfortable with its violence. 2 Peter 2:7–8 calls him a "righteous man" who was "tormented in his righteous soul by the lawless deeds he saw and heard day after day." Lot is the portrait of the believer who has drifted into the world without becoming of it, and who pays a catastrophic personal price for the drift.

19:9–11

The men of Sodom's demand for the visitors and their rage at Lot's resistance reveal the total moral collapse of the city. The sin of Sodom was comprehensive: Ezekiel 16:49–50 identifies arrogance, gluttony, and indifference to the poor alongside the sexual sin that Genesis 19 makes explicit. Jude 7 confirms that Sodom and Gomorrah "gave themselves up to sexual immorality and perversion." The blinding of the men of Sodom (v.11) is the physical sign of their spiritual condition: they have groped their way into darkness and can no longer see.

Hebrew Word Study

יָדַע yādaʿ — know / know sexually: The men demand to "know" the visitors — the same verb used for sexual knowledge (Adam "knew" Eve, 4:1). The context makes the sexual meaning unmistakable. Sodom's sin here is violent gang rape of strangers — a comprehensive violation of hospitality and human dignity.

שַׁעַר šaʿar — gate: Lot sitting "in the gate" indicates he has become a city elder — he has assimilated fully into Sodom's civic life. This is the end of the trajectory begun in 13:12 ("moved his tent as far as Sodom"). Position in the community doesn't mean position in God's covenant.

סַנְוֵרִים sanwērîm — blindness: The word for the divinely-inflicted blindness is extremely rare — used elsewhere only in 2 Kings 6:18 (Elisha striking the Aramean army blind). It may indicate a specific type of dazzling or disorientation rather than complete loss of sight.

Context

Ezekiel 16:49–50 provides the most detailed biblical analysis of Sodom's sin: "arrogance, overfed and unconcerned; they did not help the poor and needy. They were haughty and did detestable things before me." Homosexual gang rape is the culminating expression of a society organized around pride, comfort, and contempt for the vulnerable. The hospitality violation — attacking guests — is the specific act that triggers judgment in the narrative.

Cross-Reference

  • Ezek 16:49–50Ezekiel's summary of Sodom's sins: pride, excess, neglect of the poor — with the sexual violence as the culmination of a corrupt society.
  • Jude 7Jude describes Sodom and Gomorrah as undergoing "the punishment of eternal fire" as an example for those who pursue "sexual immorality and strange flesh."
  • Luke 10:12Jesus uses Sodom as the epitome of judgment, saying it will be "more tolerable for Sodom" than for cities that reject the gospel — making the Sodom account about reception of divine messengers.
Verses 12–22 — Lot's Family Warned; the Escape to Zoar
12The two men said to Lot, "Do you have anyone else here—sons-in-law, sons or daughters, or anyone else in the city who belongs to you? Get them out of here,13because we are going to destroy this place. The outcry to the Lord against its people is so great that he has sent us to destroy it."14So Lot went out and spoke to his sons-in-law, who were pledged to marry his daughters. He said, "Hurry and get out of this place, because the Lord is about to destroy the city!" But his sons-in-law thought he was joking.15With the coming of dawn, the angels urged Lot, saying, "Hurry! Take your wife and your two daughters who are here, or you will be swept away when the city is punished."16When he hesitated, the men grasped his hand and the hands of his wife and of his two daughters and led them safely out of the city, for the Lord was merciful to them.17As soon as they had brought them out, one of them said, "Flee for your lives! Don't look back, and don't stop anywhere in the plain! Flee to the mountains or you will be swept away!"18"No, my lords, please!" Lot said.19"Your servant has found favor in your eyes, and you have shown great kindness to me in sparing my life. But I can't flee to the mountains; this disaster will overtake me, and I'll die.20Look, here is a town near enough to run to, and it is small. Let me flee to it—it is very small, isn't it? Then my life will be spared."21He said to him, "Very well, I will grant this request too; I will not overthrow the town you speak of.22But flee there quickly, because I cannot do anything until you reach it." (That is why the town was called Zoar.)

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
19:16

"When he hesitated, the men grasped his hand and the hands of his wife and of his two daughters and led them safely out of the city, for the LORD was merciful to them." Lot hesitates at the threshold of his own rescue. He lingers in a burning city. God's mercy overrides his paralysis: the angels physically drag them out. This is grace as rescue despite reluctance. The picture of God's mercy is not a man eagerly fleeing from the city of destruction but a man being dragged to safety by divine compassion. The warning not to look back (v.17) is about the orientation of the heart: don't long for what God is destroying.

19:24–26

The destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah by burning sulphur is treated as historical fact by every subsequent biblical reference (Deut 29:23; Isa 1:9; Jer 50:40; Amos 4:11; Matt 10:15; Luke 17:28–29; 2 Pet 2:6; Jude 7; Rev 11:8). Jesus uses it as the standard comparison for eschatological judgment. Lot's wife looking back — and becoming a pillar of salt — is a judgment on the heart that had never truly left Sodom. Jesus says, "Remember Lot's wife" (Luke 17:32): those who try to save their life in the old world will lose it.

Context

Lot's rescue is explicitly credited to Abraham's intercession in v.29 — "God remembered Abraham and sent Lot out." The intercession of ch.18 had its effect not in sparing Sodom but in sparing Lot. The divine mercy operates through the covenant relationship even when the direct prayer is not answered as asked. Lot's wife's backward look is interpreted by Jesus as a warning: "Remember Lot's wife" (Luke 17:32) — clinging to what God has condemned means sharing its fate.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 17:28–32Jesus uses Sodom's destruction as a type of eschatological judgment, warning "Remember Lot's wife" — do not look back when the Son of Man comes.
  • 2 Pet 2:6–9Peter: God "condemned the cities of Sodom and Gomorrah...making them an example of what is going to happen to the ungodly; and if he rescued Lot, a righteous man...then the Lord knows how to rescue the godly from trials."

Places & Geography

Sodom & Gomorrah
Possibly Tall el-Hammam or south Dead Sea
The locations remain debated. Tall el-Hammam (Jordan) shows evidence of catastrophic destruction c. 1650 BC possibly from a cosmic airburst event — sulfur and other debris have been found in the destruction layer. The Dead Sea region is rich in bitumen, sulfur, and salt formations consistent with the biblical description.
Zoar
Small city; Lot's refuge
Zoar ("little one") was spared at Lot's request (v.22). Later mentioned in Deut 34:3 as visible from Mount Nebo, and in Isaiah 15:5 and Jeremiah 48:34 as a Moabite city — confirming its continued existence.
Verses 23–38 — Sodom Destroyed; Lot's Wife; Lot and His Daughters
23By the time Lot reached Zoar, the sun had risen over the land.24Then the Lord rained down burning sulfur on Sodom and Gomorrah—from the Lord out of the heavens.25Thus he overthrew those cities and the entire plain, destroying all those living in the cities—and also the vegetation in the land.26But Lot's wife looked back, and she became a pillar of salt.27Early the next morning Abraham got up and returned to the place where he had stood before the Lord.28He looked down toward Sodom and Gomorrah, toward all the land of the plain, and he saw dense smoke rising from the land, like smoke from a furnace.29So when God destroyed the cities of the plain, he remembered Abraham, and he brought Lot out of the catastrophe that overthrew the cities where Lot had lived.30Lot and his two daughters left Zoar and settled in the mountains, for he was afraid to stay in Zoar. He and his two daughters lived in a cave.31One day the older daughter said to the younger, "Our father is old, and there is no man around here to give us children—as is the custom all over the earth.32Let's get our father to drink wine and then sleep with him and preserve our family line through our father."33That night they got their father to drink wine, and the older daughter went in and slept with him. He was not aware of it when she lay down or when she got up.34The next day the older daughter said to the younger, "Last night I slept with my father. Let's get him to drink wine again tonight, and you go in and sleep with him so we can preserve our family line through our father."35So they got their father to drink wine that night also, and the younger daughter went in and slept with him. Again he was not aware of it when she lay down or when she got up.36So both of Lot's daughters became pregnant by their father.37The older daughter had a son, and she named him Moab; he is the father of the Moabites of today.38The younger daughter also had a son, and she named him Ben-Ammi; he is the father of the Ammonites of today.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Lot
Nephew of Abraham; "righteous" but compromised
Called "righteous" by 2 Peter 2:7–8, yet he lingered (v.16), offered his daughters to the mob (v.8), and needed to be seized by angels to escape. His righteousness is real but barely adequate — he is saved "as through fire" (1 Cor 3:15).
Lot's Wife
Unnamed; became a pillar of salt
Her backward glance (v.26) has generated enormous interpretive attention. Jesus warns: "Remember Lot's wife" (Luke 17:32). The salt pillar may be historical (the Lisan Peninsula area has salt formations) or symbolic of petrification in the moment of disobedience.
Two Angels
Messengers; executors of judgment
The same figures as two of the three Mamre visitors. They execute divine judgment with decisive efficiency — and merciful persistence in rescuing Lot despite his hesitation.

Ancient Sources

Tall el-Hammam Archaeological Evidence (2021)
A 2021 Nature Scientific Reports article by Bunch et al. described evidence of a catastrophic cosmic airburst (similar to the Tunguska event) at Tall el-Hammam c. 1650 BC — melted pottery, shocked quartz, platinum spike consistent with an extraterrestrial impact. The city showed sudden total destruction.
The findings do not prove the biblical account but provide a possible natural mechanism for a catastrophic destruction event in exactly the right geographical and chronological range.

Reception History

Throughout OT Prophecy
Sodom becomes the stock image of divine judgment: Isaiah 1:9–10 calls Jerusalem's leaders "rulers of Sodom"; Jeremiah 23:14 says Jerusalem's prophets are "like Sodom"; Amos 4:11 uses Sodom as a benchmark of God's past warnings. The city's destruction permeates prophetic imagination.
Revelation 11:8
The great city where the two witnesses are killed is called "spiritually...Sodom and Egypt, where their Lord was crucified" — apocalyptic Sodom becomes a symbol of any anti-God civilization.

Literary Structure

AAngels arrive in Sodom; Lot welcomes them (vv.1–3)
BSodom's men demand the visitors (vv.4–5)
CLot's failed protection; angels strike blind (vv.6–11)
CENTER: Escape commanded; Lot lingers; seized by mercy (vv.12–16)
C'Sodom destroyed (vv.24–25)
B'Lot's wife looks back (v.26)
A'Abraham watches; God remembered Abraham (vv.27–29)
The chapter's center is the mercy shown to the lingering Lot — "the LORD being merciful to him" (v.16). The destruction is real and total, but the structural emphasis falls on divine rescue even of the compromised and hesitant. Judgment and mercy are not opposites in Genesis 19; mercy operates within judgment.

Intertextual Threading

Gen 7:1→Gen 19:15–17Echo
The Flood pattern repeats: divine judgment on total wickedness, rescue of one family through divine intervention, subsequent new beginning. Noah/Lot; Flood/Sodom; ark/Zoar.
Gen 19:26→Luke 17:32Quotation
Jesus explicitly cites Lot's wife as a warning: "Remember Lot's wife." The eschatological judgment to come will be like Sodom — do not look back at what must be left behind.
Gen 19:29→Jas 5:16–18Echo
"God remembered Abraham" connects intercessory prayer to divine action. The pattern: a righteous person's prayer effects rescue (Abraham → Lot). James applies the same principle: Elijah's prayer, our prayer.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 19 is the biblical paradigm of divine judgment on societal wickedness. Sodom's destruction is cited throughout Scripture (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Amos, Zephaniah, Matthew, Luke, 2 Peter, Jude, Revelation) as the archetypal event of divine justice. Its theological weight is second only to the Flood in the OT judgment narratives.
