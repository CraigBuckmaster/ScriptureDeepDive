Genesis 22

The Binding of Isaac: the Akedah

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–10 — The Test: Abraham Commanded to Offer Isaac
1Some time later God tested Abraham. He said to him, "Abraham!" "Here I am," he replied.2Then God said, "Take your son, your only son, whom you love—Isaac—and go to the region of Moriah. Sacrifice him there as a burnt offering on a mountain I will show you."3Early the next morning Abraham got up and loaded his donkey. He took with him two of his servants and his son Isaac. When he had cut enough wood for the burnt offering, he set out for the place God had told him about.4On the third day Abraham looked up and saw the place in the distance.5He said to his servants, "Stay here with the donkey while I and the boy go over there. We will worship and then we will come back to you."6Abraham took the wood for the burnt offering and placed it on his son Isaac, and he himself carried the fire and the knife. As the two of them went on together,7Isaac spoke up and said to his father Abraham, "Father?" "Yes, my son?" Abraham replied. "The fire and wood are here," Isaac said, "but where is the lamb for the burnt offering?"8Abraham answered, "God himself will provide the lamb for the burnt offering, my son." And the two of them went on together.9When they reached the place God had told him about, Abraham built an altar there and arranged the wood on it. He bound his son Isaac and laid him on the altar, on top of the wood.10Then he reached out his hand and took the knife to slay his son.

Hebrew Word Study

נִסָּה nissāh — tested: The key interpretive word — the narrator tells the reader from the start that this is a "test" (nisyôn). Abraham and Isaac do not know this. The reader's knowledge creates profound dramatic irony: we know God will not ultimately require the sacrifice; they do not.

לֶךְ־לְךָ lek-lĕkā — Go!: The emphatic "go for yourself" appears only here and in 12:1 — the call to leave Ur. The Abraham narrative is bracketed by two identical commands. The final test echoes and surpasses the first: leave your country vs. sacrifice your son.

יֶחֱזוּ yeḥĕzû — the LORD will provide: Abraham's word to Isaac — "God will provide [see to] the lamb for himself" — is either an evasion or a statement of extraordinary faith. Hebrews 11:19 reads it as faith: Abraham "considered that God was able even to raise him from the dead." YHWH-jireh ("The LORD will provide/see") becomes the name of the mountain.

יַחְדָּו yaḥdāw — together: The word "together" appears three times in this passage (vv.6, 8, 19) — father and son walking in terrible unity toward the mountain. The repetition creates an aching rhythm of companionship in the face of the unimaginable.

Historical Context

None

Context

The command to sacrifice Isaac creates a perfect theological crisis: the God who promised "through Isaac shall your offspring be named" (21:12) now commands Isaac's death. How can both be true? Hebrews 11:17–19 gives the answer: Abraham "considered that God was able even to raise him from the dead, from which, figuratively speaking, he did receive him back." Abraham's faith was resurrection faith — the only logic that could reconcile the divine promise with the divine command.

Cross-Reference

  • Heb 11:17–19The fullest NT interpretation: Abraham offered Isaac believing God could raise him — "figuratively speaking, he did receive him back." The Akedah is understood as a resurrection type.
  • Rom 8:32Paul's language mirrors Genesis 22: "He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for all of us" — the Akedah is the OT backdrop for the atonement.
  • John 3:16"God so loved the world that he gave his only Son" — echoes "your son, your only son, whom you love" (v.2). The divine Father re-enacts Abraham's role at Calvary without staying his hand.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:1–2

"Take your son, your only son, whom you love — Isaac — and go to the region of Moriah. Sacrifice him there as a burnt offering." The command is the supreme test of faith in the Old Testament. God gives four identifying phrases — "your son," "your only son," "whom you love," "Isaac" — each one deepening the cost. The last 25 years of Abraham's life have been oriented around this child, through whom every promise runs. Now he is asked to return the son to God. The word "test" (v.1: nissah) tells the reader what Abraham does not know at the moment: God is not actually requiring the death of Isaac. But Abraham does not have v.1 to read as he walks up the mountain.

22:5,8

"We will worship and then we will come back to you." Abraham says "we" — both of them will return. Hebrews 11:17–19 explains: "Abraham reasoned that God could even raise the dead." Abraham's faith has reached the point where he has integrated the command with the promise: God swore that the covenant line runs through Isaac; therefore if Isaac dies, God will raise him. "God himself will provide the lamb" (v.8) — Abraham's word to Isaac is unwittingly prophetic. On this mountain, God will provide the lamb. On this mountain (Moriah = Jerusalem) God will one day provide the final Lamb.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:1-2

The binding of Isaac (the Akedah) opens with the most devastating command in the Abraham narrative: "Take your son, your only son, whom you love — Isaac — and go to the region of Moriah." Sarna: "The fourfold identification — 'son', 'only son', 'whom you love', 'Isaac' — delays the command to its most specific and personal form. Each qualifier intensifies: not just any son, but the only son, and not just the only son but the loved son, and not just the loved son but Isaac — the son of the promise."

22:2

Moriah: Sarna identifies the region with Jerusalem — the mountain on which Solomon's temple will be built (2 Chr 3:1). The connection is not incidental: the place of the Akedah becomes the place of the temple sacrifices, which become the place of the final sacrifice. The whole sacrificial theology of Israel is geographically rooted in Abraham's act on Moriah.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:3

Abraham rises early in the morning. Alter: "The immediate action is the first and most significant detail in the narrative. There is no recorded deliberation, no dialogue with Sarah, no prayer, no argument. The man who argued boldly with God about Sodom in chapter 18 says nothing here. He simply rises early and goes."

22:7-8

The boy asks, "where is the lamb?" — and Abraham answers, "God himself will provide the lamb." Alter: "The exchange is the most agonizing in the chapter. The question is entirely innocent; the questioner does not know how perfectly the question exposes the situation. And Abraham's answer — God will provide the lamb — is at once a statement of faith and a refusal to deceive. The reader cannot tell whether Abraham believes it or is simply unable to say the truth."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:1-3

The test of Abraham is the supreme test of faith precisely because it requires Abraham to surrender what God gave him. Calvin: "God requires at his hands the sacrifice of his son, by whose death the covenant should be ratified. Therefore the faith of Abraham was put to the severest trial — he must sacrifice the very promise on the altar." The paradox of the test is total: God commands the destruction of the means of God's own promise.

22:8

Calvin on "God will provide the lamb": "Abraham's answer is ambiguous — he either spoke prophetically, being moved by the Spirit to say more than he knew, or he suppressed the truth to spare his son's feelings. In either case, his answer was literally true: God did provide the lamb." The ambiguity is preserved because both readings are theologically significant.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:2

The NET note on Moriah discusses the identification with Jerusalem. The note observes that 2 Chronicles 3:1 explicitly identifies Mount Moriah as the site of Solomon's temple. The note notes that while the identification is traditional and has ancient support, the geography of Genesis 22 (a three-day journey from Beersheba) is broadly consistent with the Jerusalem location.

22:1

The NET note on "God tested Abraham" discusses whether the test was cruel given God's foreknowledge of the outcome. The note observes that the test was real — Abraham did not know the outcome — and that its purpose was the demonstration of faith to Abraham himself and to subsequent generations, not to provide information to God.

Verses 11–19 — The Ram; the Oath of Blessing
11But the angel of the Lord called out to him from heaven, "Abraham! Abraham!" "Here I am," he replied.12"Do not lay a hand on the boy," he said. "Do not do anything to him. Now I know that you fear God, because you have not withheld from me your son, your only son."13Abraham looked up and there in a thicket he saw a ram caught by its horns. He went over and took the ram and sacrificed it as a burnt offering instead of his son.14So Abraham called that place The Lord Will Provide. And to this day it is said, "On the mountain of the Lord it will be provided."15The angel of the Lord called to Abraham from heaven a second time16and said, "I swear by myself, declares the Lord, that because you have done this and have not withheld your son, your only son,17I will surely bless you and make your descendants as numerous as the stars in the sky and as the sand on the seashore. Your descendants will take possession of the cities of their enemies,18and through your offspring all nations on earth will be blessed, because you have obeyed me."19Then Abraham returned to his servants, and they set off together for Beersheba. And Abraham stayed in Beersheba.

Hebrew Word Study

אַיִל ʾayil — ram: The ram "caught in a thicket" is the divine provision at the moment of maximum crisis. The substitution — ram for son — is the theological center of the chapter. The ram dies in Isaac's place. This substitutionary logic becomes the grammar of atonement throughout the OT.

יְהוָה יִרְאֶה YHWH-yirʾeh — The LORD will provide/see: The place-name Abraham gives the mountain: "YHWH sees/provides." The same root as "El Roi" (God of Seeing) that Hagar used in ch.16. The divine provision is an act of divine seeing — God sees the crisis and provides the substitute.

בְּהַר יְהוָה יֵרָאֶה bĕhar YHWH yērāʾeh — On the mount of the LORD it shall be provided: The proverb attached to the place: future tense. What happened here once will happen again. Ultimately: on this mountain, God will provide the final substitute — his own Son.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
22:11–13

The angel of the LORD stops Abraham at the moment of maximum obedience — the knife raised, the will fully surrendered. The provision of the ram "caught by its horns in a thicket" is the divine response to Abraham's faith: substitutionary provision. The ram dies in the place of Isaac. This is the explicit structure of substitutionary atonement: an innocent substitute provided by God, dying in the place of the condemned. The name Abraham gives the place — Yahweh-Yireh, "the LORD will provide" — becomes a proverb: "On the mountain of the LORD it will be provided." Calvary is the mountain where the proverb is finally and fully kept.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:11-14

The ram caught in the thicket — God's provision — gives the site its name: YHWH Yireh ("the LORD will see/provide"). Sarna: "The name plays on the earlier narrative: God saw Abraham's fear (v.12), Abraham called the place 'the LORD sees', and the proverb arises 'on the mountain of the LORD it will be seen/provided.' The provision is also a seeing — God sees the need and provides."

22:15-18

The oath that follows the Akedah is the most solemn statement of the Abrahamic covenant in the entire narrative. Sarna: "The divine oath — 'I swear by myself' — is unique in the Abraham narrative. God swears by his own being because there is nothing greater to swear by (Heb 6:13). The oath is a response to Abraham's obedience: the covenant terms are now confirmed with the highest possible divine commitment."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
22:13

The ram caught by its horns in the thicket. Alter: "The ram appears with magnificent narrative economy: no explanation of how it came to be there, no divine announcement, no fanfare. 'Abraham looked up and there in a thicket he saw a ram.' The provision comes without explanation, just as the command came without explanation. The narrative trusts the reader to register the theological weight of the provision."

22:16-18

The divine oath is the narrative climax of the entire Abraham story. Alter: "The oath is sworn by God's own being — the highest possible guarantee. The covenant promises that have been stated and restated through the story are now confirmed by an oath that cannot be revoked. Abraham's obedience has not earned the promises; it has confirmed the covenant's reliability. The oath is the divine seal on what was already promised."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
22:12

The angel's declaration — "now I know that you fear God" — is not a discovery for God but a public demonstration. Calvin: "God says 'now I know' not as if he had been previously ignorant, but because the thing which he had proved in the hidden recesses of Abraham's heart is now made manifest. The test brought to light what was already real."

22:16-18

The divine oath on the basis of Abraham's obedience does not make the covenant conditional on human merit. Calvin: "God swears by himself, not by Abraham's merit, but because Abraham's obedience is the occasion — not the cause — of the oath. The cause is in God's own gracious will. The obedience provided the occasion for the oath to be given in its most final and irrevocable form."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
22:14

The NET note on YHWH Yireh discusses whether yireh means "will see" or "will provide." The note observes that the Hebrew root raah (to see) develops the meaning "to provide" through the connection between seeing a need and meeting it. The note translates "the LORD will provide" while noting the visual dimension of the original. The proverb — "on the mountain of the LORD it will be provided" — anticipates the temple theology of Israel.

The oath God swears after the Akedah is the most solemn in Genesis: "I swear by myself" — God has nothing greater by which to swear (Heb 6:13–14). Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Abraham
Patriarch; model of ultimate faith
At the apex of his faith journey. He "considers" God able to raise Isaac (Heb 11:19), walks the three-day journey, raises the knife. His faith is declared righteous not in the easy moments but at the hardest point. The NT consistently returns to this moment as the definition of covenant faithfulness.
Isaac
Son; willing participant
Old enough to carry the wood (v.6), strong enough to be bound (v.9). Patristic tradition emphasizes Isaac's willing submission as a type of Christ's willing sacrifice. The text's silence about Isaac's inner response is filled by centuries of interpretation.
The RAM
Substitute; type of Christ
The anonymous ram provides the substitutionary sacrifice. In the economy of the Akedah, the ram's death enables Isaac's life. This is the logic of atonement: one dies that another might live. The ram points forward to the Lamb of God.

Ancient Sources

Aqedah in Jewish Tradition
The Akedah became central to Jewish theology of merit and intercession. In the Midrash, God's mercy toward Israel is repeatedly grounded in "the merit of the Akedah" — Abraham's willingness to sacrifice Isaac creates a permanent fund of divine favor. The shofar (ram's horn) blown at Rosh Hashanah recalls the ram of Genesis 22.
Dead Sea Scrolls 4Q225 (Pseudo-Jubilees)
A fragmentary Qumran text dramatically expands the Akedah, introducing Mastema (the adversary figure) as the one who urged God to test Abraham — paralleling Job 1 and anticipating Satan's role in the NT.

Reception History

Kierkegaard, Fear and Trembling (1843)
Kierkegaard's masterpiece uses the Akedah to explore the "teleological suspension of the ethical" — Abraham's obedience transcends ordinary moral categories. His "Knight of Faith" is defined by the ability to hold the paradox: command and promise simultaneously. The book is one of the most influential philosophical treatments of biblical faith.
Rashi (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, 1040–1105)
Rashi's influential commentary emphasizes that God "tested" Abraham to demonstrate his righteousness to the world — God knew the outcome. He also notes that the angel stopped Abraham before the sacrifice (not the LORD himself) as a sign that God never truly intended Isaac's death.
Christian tradition: Typology
The Fathers uniformly read the Akedah typologically: Isaac carrying the wood = Christ carrying the cross; the three-day journey = resurrection on the third day; the ram caught in thorns = Christ crowned with thorns; Mount Moriah = Calvary. The typological reading is one of the most elaborate in all of biblical interpretation.

Literary Structure

The Command — 22:1-2Fourfold identification: son / only son / whom you love / Isaac. Command to sacrifice the promise itself.
Abraham rises early, says nothing. "God will provide the lamb" — prophetic ambiguity. Isaac asks; father answers.
Divine Provision — 22:11-14Angel stops Abraham. Ram caught in thicket. YHWH Yireh — the LORD sees and provides. Moriah = future temple site.
The Oath — 22:15-18God swears by himself — highest possible guarantee. Covenant confirmed irrevocably as response to the Akedah.
Nahor Genealogy — 22:20-24Bridge to Isaac narrative. Rebekah introduced. Providence preparing the covenant continuation.
Genesis 22, the Akedah, is the theological summit of the Abraham narrative and one of the most carefully crafted literary texts in all of Scripture. Its architecture moves from devastating command to silent obedience to divine provision to irrevocable oath. The test requires Abraham to surrender the means of the promise; the provision demonstrates that God himself is the provider. YHWH Yireh — the LORD sees and provides — becomes the theological formula for all subsequent sacrifice and ultimately for the sacrifice at Calvary.

Hebrew-Rooted Reading

2 "Take your son, your only son Isaac, whom you love, and go [lek-lĕkā] to the land of Moriah, and offer him there as a burnt offering [ʿôlāh]." 8 "God will provide [yirʾeh] for himself the lamb for a burnt offering, my son." 12 "For now I know that you fear [yārēʾ] God." 14 Abraham called the name of that place "The LORD Will Provide" [YHWH-yirʾeh].

Intertextual Threading

Gen 12:1→Gen 22:2Bracket
The two "go for yourself" (lek-lĕkā) commands bracket the entire Abraham narrative. The first calls him from his homeland; the final calls him to sacrifice his son. Each requires radical release of what is most dear.
Gen 22:2→Rom 8:32Typology
"He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for all of us" (Rom 8:32) — Paul's language deliberately echoes "you have not withheld your son, your only son" (v.16). The Father's act at Calvary re-enacts Abraham's act at Moriah, but without the angel's intervention.
Gen 22:8→John 1:29Fulfillment
"God will provide for himself the lamb" is fulfilled in John the Baptist's declaration: "Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world." The ram of Moriah points forward 2,000 years to the ultimate divine provision.
Gen 22:14→2 Chr 3:1Fulfillment
Mount Moriah, where Abraham offered Isaac, is identified as the site where Solomon built the temple. The place of substitutionary sacrifice becomes the permanent home of sacrificial worship — and ultimately the site of the crucifixion.

Textual Notes

22:1וְהָאֱלֹהִים נִסָּה — "God tested Abraham"
MT nassah — tested/tried (same verb as Exod 16:4; 20:20; Deut 8:2,16 for wilderness testing). The narrator tells the reader this is a test before Abraham knows it; the reader experiences the narrative without Abraham's knowledge.
The narrator's disclosure in v.1 ("God tested Abraham") is a theological frame that transforms the entire reading. Without it, the story would be a near-murder. With it, it is a test of faith. The reader's knowledge that Abraham lacks changes the moral and emotional register completely.
22:14יְהוָה יִרְאֶה — "the LORD will provide/see"
MT YHWH Yireh — "the LORD will see/provide." The mountain is named "On the mount of the LORD it will be provided/seen." The wordplay is deliberate: ra'ah (see) in both the name and the proverb.
The name encodes the theology of the chapter: what God sees, God provides. The ram caught in the thicket (v.13) is what God had "seen" from eternity. The same mountain (Moriah, v.2 = Jerusalem, 2 Chr 3:1) is where Solomon's temple will stand. The Akedah and the temple are theologically linked.

Scholarly Debates

The Akedah as Polemic Against Child Sacrifice
Anti-sacrifice polemic
von Rad, Westermann, many moderns
The story's primary purpose is to establish that YHWH, unlike Molech, does not require child sacrifice. The near-sacrifice is the test; the ram-substitution is the theological conclusion: God provides a substitute.
Test of faith
Calvin, Wenham, Hamilton, NT witness
Hebrews 11:17-19 reads the Akedah as a test of resurrection faith: Abraham believed God could raise Isaac. The anti-sacrifice polemic may be a secondary function; the primary meaning is the testing and vindicating of Abrahamic faith.
Typological/messianic
Church Fathers, Irenaeus, Origen
The ram as substitute, the father-son relationship, the wood, the mountain, the three-day journey — all point forward to the cross. The Akedah is the most developed Christological type in Genesis.
All three readings have textual support and are not mutually exclusive. The anti-sacrifice polemic is present but not primary — the text never frames it that way explicitly. The Hebrews reading carries apostolic authority. The typological reading is the most christologically rich and has the strongest cumulative support from the narrative details.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 22 is the theological summit of the OT. Every theme of Genesis converges here: the test of faith, the covenant promise, divine provision, substitutionary sacrifice, and the seed through whom all nations will be blessed. Every score is at 5 — this chapter does not merely touch the great themes; it is their definitive expression in narrative form.
