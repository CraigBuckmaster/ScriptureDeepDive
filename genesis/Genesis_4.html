Cain & Abel — Verses 1–8
1 Adam made love to his wife Eve, and she became pregnant and gave birth to Cain. She said, “With the help of the LORD I have brought forth a man.” 2 Later she gave birth to his brother Abel. Now Abel kept flocks, and Cain worked the soil.

3 In the course of time Cain brought some of the fruits of the soil as an offering to the LORD. 4 And Abel also brought an offering — fat portions from some of the firstborn of his flock. The LORD looked with favor on Abel and his offering, 5 but on Cain and his offering he did not look with favor. So Cain was very angry, and his face was downcast.

6 Then the LORD said to Cain, “Why are you angry? Why is your face downcast? 7 If you do what is right, will you not be accepted? But if you do not do what is right, sin is crouching at the door; it desires to have you, but you must rule over it.”

8 Now Cain said to his brother Abel, “Let’s go out to the field.” While they were in the field, Cain attacked his brother Abel and killed him.

Hebrew Word Study

קַיִן Qayin — “Cain.” Eve’s exclamation (qaniti — “I have acquired/created”) suggests the name means “acquired/gotten.” Some ancient interpreters read “with the help of the LORD” as Eve believing Cain might be the promised seed of 3:15 who would crush the serpent’s head. The irony is devastating.


הֶבֶל Hevel — “Abel.” The Hebrew word for “breath” or “vapour” — the exact word used for emptiness in Ecclesiastes (hevel havalim, “vanity of vanities”). His very name signals brevity and transience. He is the first person in the Bible to die, and his name means the shortest thing in creation.


Robets — “Crouching” (v.7). The same word used for an animal crouching ready to spring (cf. Gen 49:9). Sin is personified as a predator at the door of Cain’s heart. This is one of the Bible’s earliest pictures of sin as a power that seeks to master its host — the language of addiction, compulsion, and spiritual bondage.


Teshuqah — “Desires” (v.7). The same word used in 3:16 for the woman’s desire for her husband. Sin’s desire for Cain mirrors the distorted relational dynamic of the Fall — a hunger to master and possess.

Historical Context

The Abel/Cain conflict maps onto one of the oldest tensions in human civilization: nomadic herders vs. settled farmers. This conflict was violent and recurring throughout the ancient Near East. Mesopotamian texts show the same tension — the Sumerian “Debate between Sheep and Grain” even features a divine adjudication between the two ways of life. The text doesn’t endorse one vocation over the other — both offerings are legitimate. The issue is the heart behind the offering.


Why was Abel’s offering accepted and Cain’s rejected? The text gives two clues: (1) Abel brought “fat portions from the firstborn” — the best, the priority offering. Cain brought “some of the fruits” — no indication of first or best. (2) Hebrews 11:4 says Abel’s offering was made “by faith.” The text is less about the type of offering (animal vs. vegetable) and more about the posture of heart behind it.

Deep Context

God’s warning to Cain (vv.6–7) is one of the most remarkable moments in Genesis. Before the murder, God intervenes pastorally — asking questions, warning, offering a path. This is the same God who asked “Where are you?” in chapter 3. Cain is not ambushed by the murder — he chooses it in the face of a direct divine appeal. The murder is premeditated in the shadow of grace.


The phrase “Let’s go out to the field” (v.8) appears to be a pretextual invitation in the MT (many manuscripts include a full speech here, which the Septuagint, Samaritan Pentateuch, and Dead Sea Scrolls preserve as: “Let us go out to the field”). The field is where Cain has authority — it is his domain, away from community, away from witnesses. The first murder is also the first premeditated deception.

Cross-References

  • Heb 11:4Abel offered his sacrifice by faith — and through his faith he still speaks, even though he is dead. Abel is the first member of the great cloud of witnesses.
  • 1 John 3:12“Do not be like Cain, who belonged to the evil one and murdered his brother. And why did he murder him? Because his own actions were evil and his brother’s were righteous.”
  • Matt 23:35Jesus names Abel as the first righteous martyr — “from the blood of righteous Abel to the blood of Zechariah” — framing redemptive history as the story of innocent blood shed.
  • Heb 12:24The blood of Jesus “speaks a better word than the blood of Abel” — Abel’s blood cried for justice; Christ’s blood speaks mercy.
The Interrogation of Cain — Verses 9–16
9 Then the LORD said to Cain, “Where is your brother Abel?” “I don’t know,” he replied. “Am I my brother’s keeper?”

10 The LORD said, “What have you done? Listen! Your brother’s blood cries out to me from the ground. 11 Now you are under a curse and driven from the ground, which opened its mouth to receive your brother’s blood from your hand. 12 When you work the ground, it will no longer yield its crops for you. You will be a restless wanderer on the earth.”

13 Cain said to the LORD, “My punishment is more than I can bear. 14 Today you are driving me from the land, and I will be hidden from your presence; I will be a restless wanderer on the earth, and whoever finds me will kill me.”

15 But the LORD said to him, “Not so; anyone who kills Cain will suffer vengeance seven times over.” Then the LORD put a mark on Cain so that no one who found him would kill him. 16 So Cain went out from the LORD’s presence and lived in the land of Nod, east of Eden.

Hebrew Word Study

“Am I my brother’s keeper?” — Cain’s question becomes one of the defining ethical questions of Scripture. The word shomer (keeper/guardian) is the same used for keeping the Torah, guarding the sanctuary, and the LORD himself being Israel’s keeper (Ps 121:5 — YHWH shomrekha). Cain’s answer is implicitly “no” — but the entire arc of Scripture answers “yes.”


Demei ahikha — “Your brother’s blood.” The word is plural: “bloods.” Ancient rabbis read this as meaning not just Abel but all his potential descendants — every person who might have descended from Abel was destroyed by the murder. The Mishnah (Sanhedrin 4:5) states: “Whoever destroys a single soul, Scripture accounts it as if he had destroyed an entire world.”


Nod — “Wandering.” The land of Nod (eretz Nod) is a wordplay on nad (wanderer). Cain’s fate is to inhabit his own condition — he lives in Wandering. “East of Eden” echoes the direction of exile from the garden (3:24).

Deep Context — The Mark of Cain

The “mark of Cain” (v.15) is one of the most misread passages in history. For centuries it was used to justify racial slavery — a profoundly false and evil interpretation. The text says the mark is a protection, not a curse. God marks Cain to prevent his being killed. The mark is an act of mercy — even for the first murderer, God maintains his role as guardian of life. The curse is the wandering; the mark is grace within the curse.


Cain’s complaint in v.13–14 is surprisingly sympathetic. “My punishment is more than I can bear” — the word is also “my iniquity/guilt.” Whether this is sorrow over consequences or genuine remorse is ambiguous. His concern that “whoever finds me will kill me” raises the famous question: who else is out there? The narrative assumes a wider human population than it has explicitly described — a reminder that Genesis is not a comprehensive account of all human origins but a theological account of covenant beginnings.

Cross-References

  • Gen 9:5–6After the flood God formally institutes the death penalty for murder, grounding it in the Imago Dei — what Cain violated by killing Abel.
  • Ps 121:5“The LORD watches over you” — YHWH as shomer (keeper), answering Cain’s rhetorical question with divine responsibility.
  • Heb 12:24Abel’s blood cried for vengeance; Christ’s blood speaks mercy. The contrast between the two brothers becomes a contrast between two covenants.
  • Jude 11“Woe to them! They have taken the way of Cain” — Cain becomes a type of godless religious externalism without genuine faith or love.
The Line of Cain & Seth — Verses 17–26
17 Cain made love to his wife, and she became pregnant and gave birth to Enoch. Cain was then building a city, and he named it after his son Enoch. 18 To Enoch was born Irad, and Irad was the father of Mehujael, and Mehujael was the father of Methushael, and Methushael was the father of Lamech.

19 Lamech married two women, one named Adah and the other Zillah. 20 Adah gave birth to Jabal; he was the father of those who live in tents and raise livestock. 21 His brother’s name was Jubal; he was the father of all who play stringed instruments and pipes. 22 Zillah also had a son, Tubal-Cain, who forged all kinds of tools out of bronze and iron. Tubal-Cain’s sister was Naamah.

23 Lamech said to his wives,
  “Adah and Zillah, listen to me;
    wives of Lamech, hear my words.
  I have killed a man for wounding me,
    a young man for injuring me.
24 If Cain is avenged seven times,
    then Lamech seventy-seven times.”

25 Adam made love to his wife again, and she gave birth to a son and named him Seth, saying, “God has granted me another child in place of Abel, since Cain killed him.” 26 Seth also had a son, and he named him Enosh. At that time people began to call on the name of the LORD.

Hebrew Word Study

Lamech’s Song (vv.23–24) — The second poem in Genesis after Adam’s love poem (2:23) is a war boast. The contrast is brutal. Humanity’s first poem celebrated union and shared essence; the second celebrates disproportionate vengeance and self-glorification. The seven-fold protection God gave Cain (v.15) is weaponised by Lamech into seventy-seven-fold personal revenge. Culture advances (cities, music, metalwork); moral character regresses.


שֵת Shet — “Seth.” From shat (appointed/placed). Eve’s naming of Seth signals the beginning of hope — God has “appointed” another seed in place of Abel. The line through which the promise of 3:15 will travel shifts from Cain (who builds a godless city) to Seth (whose line calls on YHWH).


Az huhal liqro beshem YHWH — “At that time people began to call on the name of the LORD.” The first recorded corporate worship. The verb qara beshem (call on the name) becomes the defining activity of covenant relationship throughout the Psalms and prophets. Abraham does it at every altar (Gen 12:8; 13:4); it is what Joel says will mark the Day of the LORD (Joel 2:32 / Acts 2:21).

Deep Context — Two Lines, Two Cities

Genesis 4 presents two parallel genealogies establishing two trajectories for humanity after the Fall. The line of Cain produces civilisation — cities (’ir), animal husbandry (Jabal), music (Jubal), metallurgy (Tubal-Cain). These are genuine gifts and cultural achievements. But the culmination of Cain’s line is Lamech’s revenge song — culture without covenant, power without accountability.


The line of Seth produces worship — calling on the name of YHWH. Augustine’s City of God reads this as the foundation of his “two cities” theology: the City of Man (built on pride and self-love to the contempt of God) and the City of God (built on love of God to the contempt of self). The tension between the two cities will structure the rest of Genesis and ultimately all of history.


Lamech’s seventy-seven-fold vengeance becomes the foil for Jesus’ teaching in Matthew 18:22: when Peter asks how many times to forgive — “up to seven times?” — Jesus answers “seventy-seven times” (or “seventy times seven”). Lamech’s vengeance arithmetic is directly reversed.

Cross-References

  • Matt 18:22Jesus overturns Lamech’s seventy-seven-fold vengeance with seventy-seven-fold (or seventy times seven) forgiveness — a direct reversal of Gen 4:24.
  • Joel 2:32“Everyone who calls on the name of the LORD will be saved” — the covenantal prayer that began with Seth’s generation becomes the promise of the new covenant age.
  • Acts 2:21Peter quotes Joel 2:32 on Pentecost — the “calling on the name” inaugurated in Genesis 4:26 reaches its fullest expression at the birth of the church.
  • Luke 3:38Luke’s genealogy of Jesus traces through Seth all the way to Adam — confirming the Seth line as the one through which the promised seed travels.
Supplementary Scholarship — Genesis 4

People — Genesis 4

Cain / Qayin
First Murderer — vv.1–16,25

Eve names Cain with the exclamation qaniti (I have acquired/created), possibly believing he is the promised seed of 3:15 who will crush the serpent — tragic irony. Cain is a farmer ('oved adamah — worker of ground), continuing the cursed vocation of 3:17. His offering is rejected not because it is vegetable (the text never says this is the problem) but apparently because it lacks the firstfruits quality Abel's has. God's pastoral warning to him before the murder (4:6-7) makes Cain's act fully premeditated defiance. In the NT he is a type of godless religion (Jude 11), self-righteous externalism, and the first shedder of innocent blood (Matt 23:35; 1 John 3:12).

Abel / Hevel
The First Martyr — vv.2–8

Abel's name means breath, vapour, vanity — the same word (hevel) Ecclesiastes uses for the transience of all things. He is the first person in the Bible to die, and his name means the shortest-lived thing in creation. He is never given dialogue — he is entirely passive even in his death. Yet Hebrews 11:4 says he still speaks through his faith. His blood cries from the ground (v.10) — establishing the principle that innocent blood has a voice before God that cannot be silenced by murder. Jesus names him the first righteous martyr (Matt 23:35) and Hebrews 12:24 says his blood called for vengeance, whereas Christ's blood speaks mercy.

Lamech (Cain's line)
First Polygamist and Boaster — vv.19–24

The seventh generation from Adam through Cain, Lamech is the culmination of the Cainite genealogy's moral descent. He introduces polygamy (two wives), boasts of disproportionate violence (killing a man for a wound — seventy-seven-fold revenge), and weaponizes the protective logic of Cain's mark. He is the dark mirror of the Seth-line Lamech (ch.5) who lives 777 years in covenant faithfulness. His 'Sword Song' (vv.23-24) is the oldest war boast in Scripture — and the foil Jesus directly reverses in Matt 18:22 with seventy-seven-fold forgiveness.

Verse 7 — Sin crouching at the door

VersionRendering
NIVsin is crouching at the door; it desires to have you, but you must rule over it
ESVsin is crouching at the door. Its desire is contrary to you, but you must rule over it
NASBsin is lurking at the door; and its desire is for you, but you must master it
KJVsin lieth at the door. And unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him
NoteESV 'contrary to you' is a notable interpretive choice — reading teshuqah (desire) negatively, as hostile desire to dominate. NIV and NASB read it as simple desire/longing. The same word in 3:16 describes the woman's desire for her husband. KJV's masculine 'him' reflects an older reading of sin as a personified male figure. The Akkadian word rabisu (a crouching demon at doorways) may underlie the imagery.

Verse 15 — The mark of Cain

VersionRendering
NIVthe LORD put a mark on Cain so that no one who found him would kill him
ESVthe LORD put a mark on Cain, lest any who found him should attack him
NASBthe LORD appointed a sign for Cain, so that no one finding him would kill him
NoteNo translation specifies what the mark was — the Hebrew ot means sign or mark but gives no physical description. Ancient interpreters guessed: a letter on his forehead (Rashi), a horn (Targum), a dog companion, a trembling body. The text's silence is deliberate. The mark's function — protection, not punishment — is unambiguous in all versions.

Ancient Source Texts — The Farmer and the Shepherd

Sumerian Debate Poem — 'The Dispute between Sheep and Grain'
Sheep and Grain disputed with each other... Sheep said: 'I am foremost...' Grain answered: 'I am more excellent than you...' Enlil [the chief god] said: 'Both of you are equal — you are sisters.'

The Sumerian tradition features formal debates between shepherding and farming as rival livelihoods, with divine adjudication. In another version, the farmer/shepherd rivalry has the shepherd win divine favor. Genesis 4 appears to engage this tradition: two brothers, two vocations, a divine evaluation of their offerings. But Genesis does not frame it as vocation-rivalry (both are acceptable) but as heart-rivalry — and uniquely, the shepherd wins God's favor not because shepherding is better but because Abel's faith is genuine.

The Story of Adapa — On Protective Marks
Ea set a guard over Adapa... he was marked so that the gatekeeper would recognize him.

The concept of a divine mark of protection — a sign applied to a person to prevent others from harming them — appears in Mesopotamian literature as a recognizable motif. God's mark on Cain fits into this tradition of divine protective marking, but with the striking twist that it protects the guilty rather than the innocent.

Reception History — Genesis 4

Philo of Alexandria — On the Birth of Abel and Cain

Philo read Cain and Abel as allegorical representations of self-love vs. love of God. Cain (self-love) naturally brings an offering from himself — his own produce — while Abel (piety) brings the firstborn and best, recognizing God as the source of all. This allegorical reading made the Cain-Abel story a template for two types of religious psychology: the person who performs religion for self-advancement versus the person who genuinely seeks God. Philo's influence on Christian allegorical interpretation was enormous, especially through Origen and the Alexandrian school.

John Chrysostom — Homilies on Genesis (c. 390 CE)

Chrysostom focused on God's pastoral gentleness toward Cain before the murder: 'See how merciful God is, how He doesn't abandon even this man, doesn't lose patience with him, but calls him to repentance.' Chrysostom used Cain's story to argue against despair — if God sought out even the first murderer with pastoral concern, no sinner is beyond divine reach. This reading made Gen 4:6-7 a model of pastoral prevenient grace that precedes even the worst human acts.

Augustine — City of God (Book XV)

Augustine read the Cain-Abel story as the founding moment of his 'two cities' — the City of Man (Cain, the murderer who then builds a city, Babel's ancestor) and the City of God (Abel, the martyr whose blood cries to heaven; Seth, whose line calls on YHWH). These two cities are not geographic but spiritual communities running through all of history, intertwined until the final judgment separates them. This framework structures Books XV-XVII of City of God and has shaped Western political theology ever since.

Literary Structure — Two Genealogies, Two Trajectories

Genesis 4 presents a deliberate literary contrast between two genealogical lines and their cultural products:

CAIN’S LINE (vv.17-24)
➤Enoch — city built and named after him
➤Jabal — livestock and tent-dwelling
➤Jubal — music (lyre and pipe)
➤Tubal-Cain — bronze and iron tools
➤Lamech — polygamy + war boast poem
— — — — — — —
SETH’S LINE (vv.25-26)
➤Seth — appointed in place of Abel
➤Enosh — 'people began to call on YHWH'

The contrast is stark and intentional: Cain’s line produces civilization (cities, music, metallurgy) but culminates in Lamech’s boastful violence — culture without covenant. Seth’s line produces only one thing: worship. The genealogy of chapter 5 will extend this line through Enoch (who walked with God) to Noah. The entire trajectory of redemptive history runs through Seth’s two-verse genealogy, not Cain’s elaborate seven-generation cultural achievement.
