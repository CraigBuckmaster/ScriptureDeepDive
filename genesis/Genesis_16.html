Genesis 16

Hagar and Ishmael; the God Who Sees

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–6 — Sarai's Plan; Hagar and Abram
1Now Sarai, Abram's wife, had borne him no children. But she had an Egyptian slave named Hagar;2so she said to Abram, "The Lord has kept me from having children. Go, sleep with my slave; perhaps I can build a family through her." Abram agreed to what Sarai said.3So after Abram had been living in Canaan ten years, Sarai his wife took her Egyptian slave Hagar and gave her to her husband to be his wife.4He slept with Hagar, and she conceived. When she knew she was pregnant, she began to despise her mistress.5Then Sarai said to Abram, "You are responsible for the wrong I am suffering. I put my slave in your arms, and now that she knows she is pregnant, she despises me. May the Lord judge between you and me."6"Your slave is in your hands," Abram said. "Do with her whatever you think best." Then Sarai mistreated Hagar; so she fled from her.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
16:1-3

MacArthur: Sarai's proposal to give her maidservant to Abram follows the accepted custom of the ancient world for barren wives. What is culturally conventional is not necessarily divinely approved. The plan is a human solution to a divine promise -- attempting to fulfil God's word through human means, the pattern that produces Ishmael rather than Isaac.

16:6

MacArthur: Abraham's response -- 'do with her whatever you think best' -- abdicates responsibility. He is the head of the household and the covenant recipient; the welfare of both Sarai and Hagar is his charge. Passive abdication of responsibility in a domestic conflict is the patriarch's failure here, not Sarai's harshness alone.

Historical Context

The practice of using a servant as a surrogate mother for a barren wife is well-documented in ANE law codes. The Nuzi tablets (15th c. BC) contain a marriage contract stipulating that if a wife is barren, she must provide her husband with a servant-wife. The Code of Hammurabi also addresses the legal status of children born to servant-wives. Sarai's suggestion, though morally problematic from a later biblical perspective, was standard ANE legal procedure.

Context

Sarai's plan is a "help" that creates catastrophic complications — a human attempt to fulfill the divine promise through human wisdom. The parallel with Eden (3:6) is strong: both involve a human shortcut that bypasses trust in God. Yet Genesis does not simply condemn Sarai — her anguish is understandable, the cultural context makes her solution reasonable. The narrative is compassionate to all parties while showing the cost of bypassing divine timing.

Cross-Reference

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    16:1-3

    Sarai's proposal — that Abram take her Egyptian slave Hagar as a surrogate — was legally normative in the ancient Near East. Sarna: "Several ancient Near Eastern legal codes (Hammurabi, Nuzi tablets) provide for exactly this arrangement: a barren wife could give her slave to her husband, and any child born would be considered the wife's child. Sarai is not acting immorally by the standards of her world — she is following established legal practice."

    16:5-6

    Sarai's accusation — "you are responsible for the wrong I am suffering" — begins a pattern of blame that will recur in the Abraham story. Sarna: "Sarai holds Abram responsible for the consequences of her own plan. Abram's response — 'your slave is in your hands' — withdraws from responsibility entirely. Both responses reveal the relational damage created by the surrogate arrangement."

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    16:3

    The irony of "after Abram had been living in Canaan ten years" is not accidental. Alter: "Ten years have passed since the promise. The divine word has not been fulfilled. Sarai's plan is the human solution to the divine delay — taking the promise into her own hands rather than waiting for God's timing. The narrative presents the plan without endorsing it; the consequences will show its inadequacy."

    16:6

    Vate'anneha Sarai — "and Sarai mistreated her." The verb anah (afflict, humble) is the same word used for Israel's oppression in Egypt (Exod 1:11-12). Alter: "The Egyptian slave Hagar is afflicted by the matriarch Sarah — and she flees to Egypt via the desert. The narrative quietly echoes the Exodus pattern: affliction, flight, divine rescue. Hagar's story is a mini-Exodus, and she becomes the first person in the Bible to receive a divine promise for her child."

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    16:2

    Sarai's plan reflects the natural human tendency to help God keep his promises. Calvin: "We perceive how greatly the faith of Sarai was shaken, so that instead of depending on God, she laid hold of an unlawful remedy. Thus unbelief is always in a hurry, and cannot wait for God." The surrogate plan is not an act of bold faith but of impatient unbelief — an attempt to provide for God what God has not yet provided.

    16:5-6

    Calvin on the blame exchange: "We see how great is the perversity of human nature. Sarai could not endure that her maid should be treated with more consideration than herself. And Abram, in order to escape contention, gave up his wife to her caprice." The domestic conflict reveals the fruit of departing from God's plan — the household is damaged by the plan Sarai initiated.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    16:1-3

    The NET note on the surrogate arrangement confirms the legal background from ancient Near Eastern sources. The note cites the Nuzi tablets specifically, which document similar practices. The note observes that the legal normalcy of the practice does not mean it was God's plan — the narrative will show the arrangement creating conflict that persists through the entire Abraham story and beyond.

    16:4-6

    The NET note on Hagar's contempt discusses the social dynamics: in ancient Near Eastern surrogate arrangements, bearing a child elevated the surrogate's status, which could destabilise the household hierarchy. Hagar's contempt is the natural result of a custom that elevated the child-bearer.

    Places

    Shur road / Beer Lahai Roi
    Desert between Canaan and Egypt
    The road to Shur connects Canaan to Egypt — Hagar is fleeing toward her homeland. The angel finds her at a spring on this road. Beer Lahai Roi ("well of the Living One who sees me") becomes a named covenant location: Hagar's encounter gives a place its theological name.
    Beer Lahai Roi
    Between Kadesh and Bered in the Negev
    This well is mentioned again in 24:62 and 25:11 as Isaac's dwelling place. The son of the promise lives at the well named by the Egyptian slave-woman's encounter with God. The geography of covenant grace has no simple boundaries.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
    16:7-9

    MacArthur: The Angel of the LORD finds Hagar at a spring in the wilderness -- the first of many divine encounters in the wilderness that anticipate Israel's wilderness experience. The Angel's questions ('Where have you come from, and where are you going?') are not requests for information but pastoral interventions calling Hagar to self-awareness.

    16:13

    MacArthur: Hagar named God El Roi -- 'the God who sees me' -- the only place in Scripture where a human gives God a new name. A foreign slave woman, fleeing and alone, becomes the recipient of divine encounter. God's covenant care extends beyond the covenant family to all who encounter him in their need.

    Context

    The Hagar theophany is one of the Bible's most moving passages. A pregnant runaway slave encounters the Angel of the LORD, who calls her by name, acknowledges her pain, and gives her son a promise rivaling Abraham's own. She is the first person in Scripture to receive a divine messenger, the first to give God a name, and the first to receive a birth announcement. None of this is incidental — Genesis is making a theological statement about divine care for the marginalized.

    Cross-Reference

    • Gen 21:17–19In ch.21 Hagar is again in the wilderness, again heard by God, again given a promise for Ishmael. The pattern repeats: human rejection, divine encounter, divine provision.
    • Gal 4:21–31Paul uses Hagar and Sarah allegorically: Hagar represents the Sinai covenant (law-based slavery), Sarah the new covenant (promise-based freedom). The allegory has been controversial in its effect on Hagar's legacy.
    • Luke 1:31The birth announcement to Hagar ("you shall call his name…") uses exactly the same formula as Gabriel's announcement to Mary: "You shall call his name Jesus." Hagar's encounter is a prototype of the annunciation.

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    16:7-14

    The angel of the LORD finds Hagar at a spring in the wilderness — the first theophany to a woman in the Bible, and to an Egyptian slave-woman at that. Sarna: "Hagar's encounter with the divine is the most remarkable in the Abraham narrative for its recipients: she is female, a foreigner, a slave, and at the lowest point of her life. God's care reaches precisely those whom the social hierarchy has rendered invisible."

    16:13

    Hagar names God: El Roi — "the God who sees me." Sarna: "She is the only person in the Bible who names God. The name expresses her astonishment: 'Have I really seen God and remained alive after seeing him?' The God who is visible to the powerful (Abraham, who receives elaborate covenant ceremonies) is also visible to the powerless slave-woman in the wilderness."

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    16:7-12

    The angel's questions to Hagar — "where have you come from, and where are you going?" — echo God's questions in the garden narrative (3:9) but with different weight. Alter: "Hagar is not interrogated as a transgressor but as a lost person. 'Where have you come from, and where are you going?' — both questions are open: she has fled from affliction, and her destination is unknown. The angel meets her in the in-between space of her flight."

    16:13

    Hagar's naming of God is extraordinary. Alter: "In the patriarchal narratives, it is always God who renames — Abram to Abraham, Jacob to Israel. Here, a slave woman names God. The reversal is deliberate: the one who has no status names the one who has all status. El Roi — the God who sees — receives his name from the one no one else sees."

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    16:9-10

    The angel commands Hagar to return — to submit to Sarai. Calvin notes the severity of this: "God does not here approve of the cruelty of Sarai; but he teaches Hagar that she must not seek a remedy for her evils by unlawful means. She is commanded to return, not because her affliction was just, but because the due order of the household could not be maintained in any other way."

    16:13

    The naming of God by Hagar shows the universality of divine concern. Calvin: "God does not confine his care to the elect only. The fact that the angel appeared to Hagar, who was not among the elect, demonstrates that God's providence extends over all creatures, even those outside the covenant. He who sees all things was moved to mercy by the distress of this foreign slave-woman."

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    16:7

    The NET note on "the angel of the LORD" discusses the long-debated question of whether the angel is a separate being or a manifestation of God himself. The note observes that in this and other passages, the angel speaks in the first person as God (v.10: "I will increase your descendants so much..."), is addressed as God by Hagar (v.13), and yet is distinguished from God. The note identifies this as a theophanic angel — a manifestation of the divine presence in angelomorphic form.

    16:11

    The NET note on Ishmael ("God hears") observes that the name is given before the birth — a divine naming, not a parental one. The promise about Ishmael's character — "a wild donkey of a man; his hand will be against everyone and everyone's hand against him" — is discussed as a characterisation of the Bedouin lifestyle of his descendants, not a moral condemnation.

    Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    Hagar
    Egyptian slave; Mother of Ishmael
    Receives the most personal divine encounter in the patriarchal narratives — addressed by name, asked about her journey, given a son's name and a promise. First person in the Bible to receive a birth announcement, first to name God.
    Sarai (Sarah)
    Wife of Abram; initiates the surrogate plan
    Her solution is culturally reasonable and humanly understandable — but bypasses trust in God's timing. The text is sympathetic to her anguish while showing the cost of the decision.
    Ishmael
    Son of Abraham and Hagar; ancestor of Arab peoples
    Given a promise of nationhood parallel to Isaac's, though distinct. Genesis 17:20 reaffirms God's blessing on Ishmael — he is not abandoned, only repositioned in the covenant narrative.

    Ancient Sources

    Nuzi Marriage Contract (HSS V, 67)
    A contract stipulating: "If Gilimninnu [the wife] does not bear children, Gilimninnu shall take a woman of N/Lullu-land [i.e., a slave woman] as a wife for Shennima [the husband]" — the precise legal arrangement of Gen 16.
    The parallel shows Sarai's action was legally normative in the patriarchal world, not a moral failure in its cultural context.

    Reception History

    Phyllis Trible, Texts of Terror (1984)
    Trible includes Hagar among the "texts of terror" — women who suffer in the Bible. She reads the text as exposing the violence of patriarchal structures against the marginalized.
    Delores Williams, Sisters in the Wilderness (1993)
    Womanist theologian Williams reads Hagar as the paradigmatic figure for Black women in America — suffering, dismissed, yet encountered by God in the wilderness. Her reading has been enormously influential in liberationist theology.
    John Calvin, Commentary on Genesis
    Calvin emphasized the Angel of the LORD as a Christophany and Hagar's name for God (El Roi) as evidence that true knowledge of God was not limited to Israel but available to those outside the covenant community when God chose to reveal himself.

    Literary Structure

    Hagar's Encounter: The God Who Sees16:13Hagar names God El Roi -- the God who sees me. A foreign slave becomes the first person in Scripture to give God a personal name. No one is outside God's sight, not even the covenant's margin.
    Ishmael Promised: Grace Beyond the Covenant Line16:10-12The angel's promise to Hagar uses the covenant multiplication formula. God gives to the covenant's outside what he gives to the inside -- the blessing's scope exceeds its immediate vehicle.

    Textual Notes

    Scholarly Debates

    Hagar and Ishmael: The Status of the Rejected Line
    Outside the covenant
    Traditional Reformed reading
    The Abrahamic covenant runs through Isaac (17:21; 21:12). Ishmael is blessed (17:20) but is not the covenant heir. Paul's allegory (Gal 4:21-31) uses Hagar/Ishmael as the figure of the law-covenant vs. the promise-covenant.
    Within divine care
    Sarna, Trible, Alter
    The Genesis narrative gives Hagar and Ishmael extensive divine attention: two angelic appearances (16:7-12; 21:17-19), a personal divine name (El Roi), a promise for Ishmael (17:20; 21:18), and God hearing his cry (21:17). The rejected line is not abandoned.
    Both readings are textually grounded. Paul's allegory uses Hagar typologically for a specific theological argument; it does not determine how we read the Genesis narrative in its own terms. The text presents the rejected line as genuinely cared for by God even while the covenant runs through Isaac. Divine election and divine compassion for the non-elect coexist.
