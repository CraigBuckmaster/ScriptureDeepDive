Hagar and Ishmael

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration.

What prompted it

A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–6 — Sarai's Plan; Hagar and Abram
1Now Sarai, Abram's wife, had borne him no children. But she had an Egyptian slave named Hagar;2so she said to Abram, "The Lord has kept me from having children. Go, sleep with my slave; perhaps I can build a family through her." Abram agreed to what Sarai said.3So after Abram had been living in Canaan ten years, Sarai his wife took her Egyptian slave Hagar and gave her to her husband to be his wife.4He slept with Hagar, and she conceived. When she knew she was pregnant, she began to despise her mistress.5Then Sarai said to Abram, "You are responsible for the wrong I am suffering. I put my slave in your arms, and now that she knows she is pregnant, she despises me. May the Lord judge between you and me."6"Your slave is in your hands," Abram said. "Do with her whatever you think best." Then Sarai mistreated Hagar; so she fled from her.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:1–2

Sarai's proposal to give Hagar to Abram as a surrogate wife was legally standard in the ancient Near East — the Nuzi tablets and Code of Hammurabi both provide for this practice. That it was culturally acceptable does not make it right. The text's silence on divine approval is deafening. Ten years have passed since the promise (12:4; 16:3) — ten years of waiting without visible fulfilment. Sarai's plan is the attempt to fulfil God's promise by human engineering. It is the perennial temptation of faith: trusting God's word but taking matters into your own hands when the timeline disappoints. The result is conflict, suffering, and division that echoes through the centuries to this day.

16:4–6

When Hagar conceives, she despises Sarai — pride in fertility against the barren mistress. Sarai blames Abram; Abram defers entirely to Sarai; Sarai treats Hagar harshly. Every relationship in the triangle is fractured. This is the harvest of the scheme: what was meant to produce blessing produces strife. There are no villains in a simple sense — each character is acting from understandable motivations — but the fruit of acting outside God's revealed will is never peace.

Hebrew Word Study

שִׁפְחָה šipḥāh — female servant / slave: Hagar's social status — property, not person in legal terms. Yet the text gives her a name, a divine encounter, and a personal promise. The narrative dignifies whom the social system marginalizes.

שָׁמַע בְּקוֹל šāmaʿ bĕqôl — listened to the voice of: The same phrase used for Adam "listening to the voice of" his wife (3:17) — both times a man heeds a human voice over divine direction, with disastrous results. The echo is deliberate.

תֵּקַל tēqal — was contemptible / looked with contempt: Hagar's contempt for Sarai once pregnant is a predictable human reaction — her only point of power in a powerless situation. The social dynamics of the household become explosive.

Historical Context

The practice of using a servant as a surrogate mother for a barren wife is well-documented in ANE law codes. The Nuzi tablets (15th c. BC) contain a marriage contract stipulating that if a wife is barren, she must provide her husband with a servant-wife. The Code of Hammurabi also addresses the legal status of children born to servant-wives. Sarai's suggestion, though morally problematic from a later biblical perspective, was standard ANE legal procedure.

Context

Sarai's plan is a "help" that creates catastrophic complications — a human attempt to fulfill the divine promise through human wisdom. The parallel with Eden (3:6) is strong: both involve a human shortcut that bypasses trust in God. Yet Genesis does not simply condemn Sarai — her anguish is understandable, the cultural context makes her solution reasonable. The narrative is compassionate to all parties while showing the cost of bypassing divine timing.

Verses 7–16 — The Angel Finds Hagar; Ishmael Promised
7The angel of the Lord found Hagar near a spring in the desert; it was the spring that is beside the road to Shur.8And he said, "Hagar, slave of Sarai, where have you come from, and where are you going?" "I'm running away from my mistress Sarai," she answered.9Then the angel of the Lord told her, "Go back to your mistress and submit to her."10The angel added, "I will increase your descendants so much that they will be too numerous to count."11The angel of the Lord also said to her: "You are now pregnant and you will give birth to a son. You shall name him Ishmael, for the Lord has heard of your misery.12He will be a wild donkey of a man; his hand will be against everyone and everyone's hand against him, and he will live in hostility toward all his brothers."13She gave this name to the Lord who spoke to her: "You are the God who sees me," for she said, "I have now seen the One who sees me."14That is why the well was called Beer Lahai Roi; it is still there, between Kadesh and Bered.15So Hagar bore Abram a son, and Abram gave the name Ishmael to the son she had borne.16Abram was eighty-six years old when Hagar bore him Ishmael.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
16:7–9

The Angel of the LORD finds Hagar at a spring in the desert. The Angel of the LORD in the Old Testament is consistently identified with God himself (cf. 16:13; Exod 3:2–6; Judg 6:11–24) — this is most likely a pre-incarnate appearance of the Son of God. The one whom Sarai dismissed, whom Abram allowed to be mistreated, is found and addressed by God. The divine attention to the outcast, the slave, the expelled — this is the character of the God of Genesis. He sees her (El-Roi, v.13). No one else saw Hagar; God did.

16:11–13

"You are now pregnant and you will give birth to a son. You shall name him Ishmael, for the LORD has heard of your misery." Ishmael means "God hears." The description of Ishmael — "a wild donkey of a man; his hand will be against everyone and everyone's hand against him" — is an accurate prophetic characterisation of the Arab peoples who would descend from him: independent, fierce, unconquerable. The prophecy is descriptive, not derogatory. Hagar names God El-Roi ("the God who sees me") — the only person in Scripture to give God a new name. The slave-woman outcast is the first person to see God and name him.

Hebrew Word Study

מַלְאַךְ יְהוָה malʾak YHWH — Angel of the LORD: This figure speaks as God in the first person (v.10: "I will multiply") yet is described as the "angel of the LORD." Throughout the OT this figure is distinct from yet identified with YHWH — leading many to see a pre-incarnate appearance of the Son of God.

יִשְׁמָעֵאל yišmāʿēl — Ishmael: The name means "God hears" (šāmaʿ + ʾēl). The child of a slave's suffering is named for the divine act of hearing — a stunning reversal of Hagar's social invisibility. God heard what society ignored.

אֵל רֳאִי ʾēl rōʾî — God of Seeing: Hagar's name for God — the only time in the Bible a human gives God a new name. "You are a God who sees me." A slave woman, the most marginal of the marginal, names God. The well is called Beer-lahai-roi, "well of the Living One who sees me."

Context

The Hagar theophany is one of the Bible's most moving passages. A pregnant runaway slave encounters the Angel of the LORD, who calls her by name, acknowledges her pain, and gives her son a promise rivaling Abraham's own. She is the first person in Scripture to receive a divine messenger, the first to give God a name, and the first to receive a birth announcement. None of this is incidental — Genesis is making a theological statement about divine care for the marginalized.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 21:17–19In ch.21 Hagar is again in the wilderness, again heard by God, again given a promise for Ishmael. The pattern repeats: human rejection, divine encounter, divine provision.
  • Gal 4:21–31Paul uses Hagar and Sarah allegorically: Hagar represents the Sinai covenant (law-based slavery), Sarah the new covenant (promise-based freedom). The allegory has been controversial in its effect on Hagar's legacy.
  • Luke 1:31The birth announcement to Hagar ("you shall call his name…") uses exactly the same formula as Gabriel's announcement to Mary: "You shall call his name Jesus." Hagar's encounter is a prototype of the annunciation.
Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Hagar
Egyptian slave; Mother of Ishmael
Receives the most personal divine encounter in the patriarchal narratives — addressed by name, asked about her journey, given a son's name and a promise. First person in the Bible to receive a birth announcement, first to name God.
Sarai (Sarah)
Wife of Abram; initiates the surrogate plan
Her solution is culturally reasonable and humanly understandable — but bypasses trust in God's timing. The text is sympathetic to her anguish while showing the cost of the decision.
Ishmael
Son of Abraham and Hagar; ancestor of Arab peoples
Given a promise of nationhood parallel to Isaac's, though distinct. Genesis 17:20 reaffirms God's blessing on Ishmael — he is not abandoned, only repositioned in the covenant narrative.

Ancient Sources

Nuzi Marriage Contract (HSS V, 67)
A contract stipulating: "If Gilimninnu [the wife] does not bear children, Gilimninnu shall take a woman of N/Lullu-land [i.e., a slave woman] as a wife for Shennima [the husband]" — the precise legal arrangement of Gen 16.
The parallel shows Sarai's action was legally normative in the patriarchal world, not a moral failure in its cultural context.

Reception History

Phyllis Trible, Texts of Terror (1984)
Trible includes Hagar among the "texts of terror" — women who suffer in the Bible. She reads the text as exposing the violence of patriarchal structures against the marginalized.
Delores Williams, Sisters in the Wilderness (1993)
Womanist theologian Williams reads Hagar as the paradigmatic figure for Black women in America — suffering, dismissed, yet encountered by God in the wilderness. Her reading has been enormously influential in liberationist theology.
John Calvin, Commentary on Genesis
Calvin emphasized the Angel of the LORD as a Christophany and Hagar's name for God (El Roi) as evidence that true knowledge of God was not limited to Israel but available to those outside the covenant community when God chose to reveal himself.

Literary Structure

ISarai's problem and plan (vv.1–3)
IIConflict: Hagar's contempt, Sarai's harshness (vv.4–6)
IIIFlight: Hagar in the wilderness (v.7)
Divine encounter at the well (vv.8–12)
III'Hagar names God; well named (v.13–14)
II'Resolution: Hagar returns (v.9 fulfilled)
I'Ishmael born; ages noted (vv.15–16)
The divine encounter at the well is the structural center of the chapter. The narrative moves from human conflict (I–II) to wilderness isolation (III) to divine encounter (CENTER) to theological naming (III') and resolution (I'). The structure enacts the theme: human cruelty drives Hagar to the wilderness where God meets her.

Hebrew-Rooted Reading

You shall call his name Ishmael [yišmāʿēl: God hears], because the LORD has listened [šāmaʿ] to your affliction [ʿonyî]. 13 So she called the name of the LORD who spoke to her, 'God of Seeing [ʾēl rōʾî],' for she said, 'Truly here I have seen him who looks after me.'

Intertextual Threading

Gen 3:17→Gen 16:2Echo
"Adam listened to the voice of his wife" (3:17) → "Abram listened to the voice of Sarai" (16:2). The same phrase marks human compliance with a human plan that bypasses divine direction.
Gen 16:11→Luke 1:31Typology
The divine birth announcement formula — "you shall bear a son, you shall call his name" — is repeated virtually verbatim by Gabriel to Mary. Hagar's encounter at the well is the structural prototype of the Annunciation.
Gen 16:13→Ps 139:7–12Echo
Hagar's "God who sees" is the personal dimension of the omniscience celebrated in Psalm 139: "Where can I flee from your presence?" God sees the slave in the wilderness and the psalmist in the depths.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 16 is a story about divine sight and human blindness. Society cannot see Hagar — she is property, a means to an end. But God sees. "El Roi" — the God Who Sees — is Hagar's name for the divine encounter. Every subsequent reader of Scripture is invited to see the world through divine eyes: to notice who is invisible, to hear who is unheard.
