Genesis 15

The Covenant of Faith

Genesis 15 is one of the most theologically dense chapters in the entire Bible. Critical scholars assign it to both J and E sources, noting the two divine speeches (vv.1–6; vv.7–21) and the unique covenant ceremony. The chapter contains the first explicit "covenant" (bĕrît) with Abraham, the foundational statement of justification by faith (v.6), and a dramatic covenant ratification ceremony drawn from ANE treaty customs.

Verses 1–6 — Faith Counted as Righteousness
1After these things the word of the LORD came to Abram in a vision: "Fear not, Abram, I am your shield; your reward shall be very great."2But Abram said, "O Lord GOD, what will you give me, for I continue childless, and the heir of my house is Eliezer of Damascus?"4And behold, the word of the LORD came to him: "This man shall not be your heir; your very own son shall be your heir."5And he brought him outside and said, "Look toward heaven, and number the stars, if you are able to number them." Then he said to him, "So shall your offspring be."6And he believed the LORD, and he counted it to him as righteousness.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
15:1

"Do not be afraid, Abram. I am your shield, your very great reward." The vision comes after Abram has refused Sodom's wealth (14:23) — God immediately reassures him that the reward he declined from Sodom, God himself will be. "I am your shield" — the same divine self-designation that underlies Ps 3, 18, 28, 91. God as personal protector is the experiential reality that makes the refusal of worldly security possible.

15:6

"Abram believed the LORD, and he credited it to him as righteousness." The most important verse in Genesis after 3:15. Paul quotes it three times (Rom 4:3, 9, 22; Gal 3:6) as the foundational proof that justification is by faith alone, apart from works, and that this was always so — even before circumcision, before the law. Abraham is the father of all who believe (Rom 4:11). The righteousness credited to him is not his own — it is reckoned, imputed, counted. Faith is the instrument; God's righteousness is the content; the crediting is the act. This verse is the seed of the entire Pauline doctrine of justification.

Hebrew Word Study

הֶאֱמִן heʾĕmîn — believed / trusted: The Hiphil of ʾāman — the root of "amen." Abram "amened" God, gave his full trust to the divine word. This is not intellectual assent but whole-person reliance. The same root gives us "faithful" (neʾĕmān) throughout the OT.

צְדָקָה ṣĕdāqāh — righteousness: Not "righteousness" in the sense of moral perfection, but right-standing in a covenant relationship. God declared Abram righteous — a forensic act of reckoning, not a moral achievement. Paul's entire Romans 4 argument hangs on this verse.

חָשַׁב ḥāšaḇ — counted / reckoned: The accounting term: God "reckoned" or "credited" righteousness to Abram. Same word used for counting money or recording transactions. Paul uses this accounting metaphor extensively in Romans 4.

Context

Genesis 15:6 is arguably the most theologically significant verse in the entire Old Testament. Paul quotes it three times (Rom 4:3, 9, 22; Gal 3:6) to establish that justification has always been by faith, not works. This is not a NT innovation — it is the foundational principle of how God has always related to humanity. Abram's faith is the pattern; his righteousness is the prototype of what Paul calls "the righteousness of God."

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 4:1–25Paul's full exposition: Abraham was justified before circumcision (ch.15 precedes ch.17), proving that faith, not law or circumcision, is the basis of righteousness.
  • Gal 3:6–9The Abrahamic faith-righteousness is the basis of Gentile justification: "those who are of faith are blessed along with Abraham, the man of faith."
  • Heb 11:11–12Abraham's faith in the impossible promise is the model for faith as "the assurance of things hoped for."
  • Jas 2:21–23James (using the same verse!) argues that Abraham's faith was "completed" by his works — not contradicting Paul but emphasizing genuine faith produces action.
Verses 7–21 — The Covenant of Pieces
7"And he said to him, "I am the LORD who brought you out from Ur of the Chaldeans to give you this land to possess."9He said to him, "Bring me a heifer three years old, a female goat three years old, a ram three years old, a turtledove, and a young pigeon."12As the sun was going down, a deep sleep fell on Abram. And behold, dreadful and great darkness fell upon him.17When the sun had gone down and it was dark, behold, a smoking fire pot and a flaming torch passed between these pieces.18On that day the LORD made a covenant with Abram, saying, "To your offspring I give this land, from the river of Egypt to the great river, the river Euphrates."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
15:12–13

The deep sleep (tardemah) that falls on Abram is the same word used of Adam's sleep in 2:21 — both are moments of divine covenant-making while the human recipient is unconscious. The darkness and dread Abram experiences represent the weight of what God is revealing: 400 years of slavery for his descendants before the promised land is entered. God does not hide the suffering from Abram; he names it. The covenant carries the promise of both suffering and redemption — the cross before the crown.

15:17–18

God alone passes through the divided animals as a smoking firepot and a blazing torch. In ancient Near Eastern covenant ceremonies, both parties passed between the pieces, binding both to the oath under threat of the same fate as the animals. Here only God passes through. This is a unilateral covenant — God binds himself, not Abram. The fulfilment of the land promise depends entirely on God's faithfulness, not on Abram's performance. This is grace in its purest covenantal form: God obligates himself to keep his promise regardless of the recipient's failures. The land boundaries given in vv.18–21 have never yet been fully possessed — their complete fulfilment awaits the Messianic kingdom.

Hebrew Word Study

כָּרַת בְּרִית kārat bĕrît — cut a covenant: The idiom "cut a covenant" comes from the practice of cutting animals in half, then walking between the pieces — the one who passes through takes on the curse of the animals if the covenant is broken. Here, only God passes through.

תַּנּוּר עָשָׁן tannûr ʿāšān — smoking fire pot: The theophanic symbols of fire and smoke represent the divine presence throughout the OT: the burning bush (Exod 3), the pillar of fire and cloud (Exod 13), Sinai ablaze (Exod 19). God himself passes between the pieces.

תַּרְדֵּמָה tardēmāh — deep sleep: The same word used for Adam's sleep when Eve is created (2:21). Both are divine-induced states preceding a covenant act. The connection suggests the Abraham covenant is as foundational as creation.

Historical Context

The covenant ceremony of Genesis 15 has direct parallels in ANE suzerainty treaties. In Mari texts and Hittite treaty documents, parties to a covenant would walk between the halves of slain animals, invoking the curse: "May what happened to these animals happen to the one who breaks this covenant." The stunning innovation in Genesis 15 is that GOD alone passes through — taking the covenant curse upon himself, making it an unconditional divine commitment.

Context

The one-sided nature of this covenant is theologically explosive. Abram is put into a "deep sleep" — he cannot pass through the pieces. Only the divine presence (fire pot and torch) passes. This means the covenant's fulfillment depends entirely on God, not on Abram's faithfulness. It is an unconditional promise, a suzerain's self-imposed oath. Theologians see this as the basis for the "new covenant" of Hebrews, where God's own Son bears the covenant curse on the cross.

Cross-Reference

  • Jer 34:18–20When Israelites "cut a covenant" and then broke it by re-enslaving freed servants, God invokes the curse of walking between the pieces — exactly the Genesis 15 ceremony.
  • Gal 3:15–17Paul argues the Mosaic law cannot annul the Abraham covenant, which was confirmed 430 years earlier — an unconditional promise cannot be made conditional later.
  • Heb 6:13–18God swore by himself because there was no greater (v.13) — the basis of "two unchangeable things" (promise + oath) giving "strong encouragement" to those who flee to Christ.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Abram
Patriarch; First justified-by-faith believer
His faith in the face of an impossible promise — childless at advanced age — becomes the paradigmatic act of faith in the Bible. Paul calls him "the father of all who believe" (Rom 4:11).
Eliezer of Damascus
Abram's servant; default heir
Mentioned only here in the Bible. As Abram's chief servant and potential heir under the custom of adoption-for-inheritance (attested in Nuzi texts), his existence represents the human "solution" that God overrules.

Ancient Sources

Nuzi Tablets (15th c. BC)
Cuneiform tablets from Nuzi (northeastern Iraq) document the custom of adopting a servant as heir when a man had no son — exactly Abram's situation with Eliezer. The tablets show that a subsequently born natural son would supersede the adopted heir.
Provides direct cultural background for Abram's anxiety about Eliezer and the legal framework God's promise overrides.
LXX (Septuagint) Genesis 15:6
The Greek translation renders "counted/reckoned" (ḥāšaḇ) as elogisthē — the same word Paul uses throughout Romans 4. This linguistic bridge directly enabled Paul's theological argument about imputed righteousness.
The LXX translation choice becomes a key link in the NT's theological use of this verse.

Reception History

Martin Luther, Lectures on Genesis (1535–45)
Luther called Genesis 15:6 "the foremost passage in the entire Old Testament" and the foundation of his reformation doctrine of justification sola fide. He argued it proved that righteousness before God has always been by faith alone, not merit.
N.T. Wright, Paul and the Faithfulness of God
Wright nuances the traditional reading: Abraham's faithfulness (as well as God's) is in view — the verse is about covenant loyalty, not merely individual forensic justification. His reading sparks the "New Perspective on Paul" debate, one of the most significant theological controversies of the late 20th century.

Literary Structure

IVision & Promise: seed like stars (vv.1–6)
Culminates in faith-righteousness (v.6)
IICovenant ceremony (vv.7–21)
a. Command to prepare animals (vv.9–11)
b. Deep sleep + vision of darkness (vv.12–16)
c. Divine fire passes through (v.17)
d. Covenant declared + land defined (vv.18–21)
The chapter has two parallel movements, each with promise → question → divine answer → confirmation. In the first, Abram asks about an heir; God promises countless offspring and reckons righteousness. In the second, Abram asks about the land; God performs a unilateral covenant ceremony sealing the promise with a divine oath.

Intertextual Threading

Gen 15:6→Rom 4:3,9,22; Gal 3:6Quotation
Paul quotes Gen 15:6 three times as the foundational text for justification by faith — calling Abraham "father of all who believe." The most-cited OT verse in Paul's letters.
Gen 15:13–16→Exod 1–15Fulfillment
God predicts 400 years of sojourn and slavery in a foreign land, followed by exodus with great possessions. This is the entire Exodus story announced to Abraham centuries in advance.
Gen 15:17→Heb 6:13–18Fulfillment
God's self-oath in the covenant ceremony becomes the basis of the "two unchangeable things" (promise + oath) of Heb 6 — the anchor of Christian hope.
Gen 15:12→Gen 2:21Echo
The "deep sleep" (tardēmāh) placed on Abram echoes Adam's sleep during Eve's creation. Both precede a foundational covenant act; both involve the human party being passive while God works.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 15 is the doctrinal center of the Abrahamic narrative and, arguably, of the entire OT. Verse 6 establishes the principle of faith-righteousness that Paul will mine in Romans and Galatians. The covenant ceremony establishes that God's promises rest on divine oath, not human performance. The 400-year prophecy (vv.13–16) demonstrates God's sovereign foreknowledge of history centuries in advance.
