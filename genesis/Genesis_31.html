Genesis 31

Jacob's Flight from Laban

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration. What prompted it: A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–16 — Jacob Resolves to Leave; God Commands It
1Jacob heard that Laban's sons were saying, "Jacob has taken everything our father owned and has gained all this wealth from what belonged to our father."2And Jacob noticed that Laban's attitude toward him was not what it had been.3Then the Lord said to Jacob, "Go back to the land of your fathers and to your relatives, and I will be with you."4So Jacob sent word to Rachel and Leah to come out to the fields where his flocks were.5He said to them, "I see that your father's attitude toward me is not what it was before, but the God of my father has been with me.6You know that I've worked for your father with all my strength,7yet your father has cheated me by changing my wages ten times. However, God has not allowed him to harm me.8If he said, 'The speckled ones will be your wages,' then all the flocks gave birth to speckled young; and if he said, 'The streaked ones will be your wages,' then all the flocks bore streaked young.9So God has taken away your father's livestock and has given them to me.10"In breeding season I once had a dream in which I looked up and saw that the male goats mating with the flock were streaked, speckled or spotted.11The angel of God said to me in the dream, 'Jacob.' I answered, 'Here I am.'12And he said, 'Look up and see that all the male goats mating with the flock are streaked, speckled or spotted, for I have seen all that Laban has been doing to you.13I am the God of Bethel, where you anointed a pillar and where you made a vow to me. Now leave this land at once and go back to your native land.'"14Then Rachel and Leah replied, "Do we still have any share in the inheritance of our father's estate?15Does he not regard us as foreigners? Not only has he sold us, but he has used up what was paid for us.16Surely all the wealth that God took away from our father belongs to us and our children. So do whatever God has told you."

Hebrew Word Study

תְּרָפִים terāpîm — household gods / teraphim: Small figurines used for divination and domestic cult. Their exact function is debated; they appear in Judges 17–18, 1 Samuel 19:13, and Ezekiel 21:21 as objects of illicit religious practice.

גָּנַב gānab — steal: The same root used for the 8th commandment. Rachel's theft echoes Jacob's own history of taking what belonged to another; the family pattern continues.

שָׁמַר šāmar — watch / guard: Laban accuses Jacob of guarding his heart in secret (v.20 lit. "stole the heart of Laban"). The idiom means to deceive — again the family lexicon of deception.

Context

Jacob's departure is divinely commanded (v.3) and confirmed by the Bethel angel (v.13) — he is not fleeing in fear but obeying God. The twenty-year arc is complete: God kept his Bethel promise (28:15: "I will be with you and will watch over you wherever you go, and I will bring you back"). Rachel's theft of the teraphim introduces a subplot of unresolved idolatry that Jacob will address in 35:2–4 when he buries all the foreign gods under the oak at Shechem.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 28:15God's Bethel promise: "I will bring you back" — now fulfilled.
  • Gen 35:2–4Jacob commands his household to put away foreign gods; the teraphim are finally buried.
  • Deut 26:5The liturgical confession: "My father was a wandering Aramean" — Jacob's Paddan-aram sojourn remembered.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:3

God's direct command to return brackets the entire Laban episode: God sent Jacob here (28:15) and now God calls him home. The twenty years of service are over not because Jacob decided but because God directed. The pattern of the patriarchal life is always God calling, God directing, God returning. The initiative is entirely divine; the human role is to hear and obey.

31:19

Rachel's theft of the household gods is unexplained in the text — we are not told why she took them. Whatever her motive (nostalgia, inheritance claim, superstitious security), the act plants a dangerous problem in Jacob's household. His unwitting curse in verse 32 ("the one with whom you find your gods shall not live") will hang over Rachel through the rest of her short life. Jacob does not know he has cursed his beloved wife.

Verses 17–35 — The Flight; Rachel Steals the Household Gods
17Then Jacob put his children and his wives on camels,18and he drove all his livestock ahead of him, along with all the goods he had accumulated in Paddan Aram, to go to his father Isaac in the land of Canaan.19When Laban had gone to shear his sheep, Rachel stole her father's household gods.20Moreover, Jacob deceived Laban the Aramean by not telling him he was running away.21So he fled with all he had, crossed the Euphrates River, and headed for the hill country of Gilead.22On the third day Laban was told that Jacob had fled.23Taking his relatives with him, he pursued Jacob for seven days and caught up with him in the hill country of Gilead.24Then God came to Laban the Aramean in a dream at night and said to him, "Be careful not to say anything to Jacob, either good or bad."25Jacob had pitched his tent in the hill country of Gilead when Laban overtook him, and Laban and his relatives camped there too.26"What have you done?" Laban said to Jacob. "You've deceived me, and you've carried off my daughters like captives in war.27Why did you run off secretly and deceive me? Why didn't you tell me, so I could send you away with joy and singing to the music of timbrels and harps?28You didn't even let me kiss my grandchildren and my daughters goodbye. You have done a foolish thing.29I have the power to harm you; but last night the God of your father said to me, 'Be careful not to say anything to Jacob, either good or bad.'30Now you have gone off because you longed to return to your father's household. But why did you steal my gods?"31Jacob answered Laban, "I was afraid, because I thought you would take your daughters away from me by force.32But if you find anyone who has your gods, that person shall not live. In the presence of our relatives, see for yourself whether there is anything of yours here with me; and if so, take it." Now Jacob did not know that Rachel had stolen the gods.33So Laban went into Jacob's tent and into Leah's tent and into the tent of the two female servants, but he found nothing. After he came out of Leah's tent, he entered Rachel's tent.34Now Rachel had taken the household gods and put them inside her camel's saddle and was sitting on them. Laban searched through everything in the tent but found nothing.35Rachel said to her father, "Don't be angry, my lord, that I cannot stand up in your presence; I'm having my period." So he searched but could not find the household gods.

Hebrew Word Study

פַּחַד יִצְחָק paḥad yitsḥāq — Fear of Isaac: A divine name found only in v.42 and v.53. Not fear that Isaac experienced, but the Fearsome One whom Isaac worshipped — an archaic epithet for God as the Awesome Sovereign.

רֵיקָם rêqām — empty-handed: The opposite of "blessing." Laban sent nothing; God provided everything. The word contrasts with the abundant flocks Jacob takes — all of it, Jacob insists, is God's provision.

Context

God's warning to Laban in the dream (v.24) is not protection of Jacob's deception but intervention to prevent Laban from harming Jacob — God restrains the powerful uncle from doing violence to the patriarch. Jacob's speech (vv.36–42) is the first time in Genesis he speaks with full moral clarity: he lists twenty years of faithful service and directly attributes his prosperity to God. The man who once grabbed his brother's heel now stands before his accuser and invokes "the Fear of Isaac."

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 20:3God warns Abimelech in a dream — same pattern of divine protection via nocturnal warning.
  • Gen 28:20–22Jacob's Bethel vow: "if God gives me bread to eat and clothing to wear" — v.41 shows God kept exactly this promise.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:24

God warning Laban is a remarkable act of sovereign mercy. Laban is a pagan, an exploiter, a man who changed Jacob's wages ten times (v.41). Yet God speaks to him. This is the same God who spoke to Abimelech (20:3) and who will one day speak to Balaam. The Lord of covenant is also Lord of all nations; he governs even the pagans who intersect with his covenant people. The warning protects Jacob not because Jacob has been perfect but because God has made a promise.

31:42

Jacob's invocation of "the Fear of Isaac" is his most theologically mature speech to this point in the narrative. He has learned to attribute his prosperity entirely to God. The twenty years of service that began with self-interested scheming end with a doxology. God used two decades of Laban's exploitation to transform a schemer into a man who knows that everything he has comes from God.

Verses 36–55 — Jacob's Accusation; the Covenant at Galeed
36Jacob was angry and took Laban to task. "What is my crime?" he asked Laban. "How have I wronged you that you hunt me down?37Now that you have searched through all my goods, what have you found that belongs to your household? Put it here in front of your relatives and mine, and let them judge between the two of us.38"I have been with you for twenty years now. Your sheep and goats have not miscarried, nor have I eaten rams from your flocks.39I did not bring you animals torn by wild beasts; I bore the loss myself. And you demanded payment from me for whatever was stolen by day or night.40This was my situation: The heat consumed me in the daytime and the cold at night, and sleep fled from my eyes.41It was like this for the twenty years I was in your household. I worked for you fourteen years for your two daughters and six years for your flocks, and you changed my wages ten times.42If the God of my father, the God of Abraham and the Fear of Isaac, had not been with me, you would surely have sent me away empty-handed. But God has seen my hardship and the toil of my hands, and last night he rebuked you."43Laban answered Jacob, "The women are my daughters, the children are my children, and the flocks are my flocks. All you see is mine. Yet what can I do today about these daughters of mine, or about the children they have borne?44Come now, let's make a covenant, you and I, and let it serve as a witness between us."45So Jacob took a stone and set it up as a pillar.46He said to his relatives, "Gather some stones." So they took stones and piled them in a heap, and they ate there by the heap.47Laban called it Jegar Sahadutha, and Jacob called it Galeed.48Laban said, "This heap is a witness between you and me today." That is why it was called Galeed.49It was also called Mizpah, because he said, "May the Lord keep watch between you and me when we are away from each other.50If you mistreat my daughters or if you take any wives besides my daughters, even though no one is with us, remember that God is a witness between you and me."51Laban also said to Jacob, "Here is this heap, and here is this pillar I have set up between you and me.52This heap is a witness, and this pillar is a witness, that I will not go past this heap to your side to harm you and that you will not go past this heap and pillar to my side to harm me.53May the God of Abraham and the God of Nahor, the God of their father, judge between us." So Jacob took an oath in the name of the Fear of his father Isaac.54He offered a sacrifice there in the hill country and invited his relatives to a meal. After they had eaten, they spent the night there.55Early the next morning Laban kissed his grandchildren and his daughters and blessed them. Then he left and returned home.

Hebrew Word Study

מִצְפָּה miṣpāh — Mizpah / Watchtower: From the root ṣāpāh, "to watch." The name means "watchtower" — here a place where God watches over the boundary between two parties. Later Mizpah is an important religious site in Judges and Samuel.

גַּלְעֵד gal'êd — Galeed: Means "heap of witness" in Hebrew. Laban also calls it Jegar-sahadutha in Aramaic (v.47) — the bilingualism reflects the boundary between Israelite and Aramean spheres.

בְּרִית berît — covenant: The treaty between Jacob and Laban follows recognizable ancient Near Eastern treaty forms: monument, witnesses (v.50: God as witness), oath, meal (v.54).

Historical Context

The Mizpah covenant employs standard ancient Near Eastern treaty elements: a boundary stone as monument, divine witnesses, an oath formula, and a covenant meal. The Aramaic/Hebrew bilingualism of the two names for the stone heap (Jegar-sahadutha / Galeed) reflects the linguistic and ethnic boundary being established. Excavations in Transjordan have uncovered boundary stelae from the 2nd millennium BC marking territorial agreements between polities.

Context

The Mizpah benediction ("The LORD watch between you and me") is often quoted as a blessing between friends. In context it is a warning: "since we distrust each other, may God watch to ensure neither of us crosses this line." The heap marks the end of the Jacob-Laban cycle. From this point Jacob moves toward Esau and toward Canaan — the land of promise finally in view. Laban disappears from the narrative. The blessing he speaks over his daughters (v.55) is his only recorded act of genuine fatherly love.

Places & Geography

The covenant site whose exact location is uncertain but in the Gilead highlands east of Jordan. Several OT Mizpahs exist; this one marks the Jacob-Laban boundary.
Galeed / Jegar-sahadutha
Gilead ridge
The bilingual name for the boundary heap — Hebrew and Aramaic meaning the same thing: "heap of witness." A permanent territorial marker.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
31:49

The Mizpah "blessing" is actually a covenant curse: may God enforce this boundary since we cannot trust each other. It is a remarkable irony that this sentence — invoked at parting ceremonies and embroidered on pillows — is originally a declaration of mutual distrust. The God who watches at Mizpah is a judge, not merely a guardian. He will hold both parties accountable to the treaty terms.

31:55

Laban's final act — kissing his grandchildren and daughters — is the only moment in the entire Jacob-Laban narrative where Laban is unambiguously tender. Twenty years of exploitation, deception, and manipulation end with a kiss. The complicated figure of Laban departs the story as he entered it: as a man whose best impulses are real but consistently overridden by self-interest.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Jacob
Patriarch, son of Isaac
Standing at the threshold of his return to Canaan after 20 years in Haran. His speech in vv.36–42 marks a new spiritual maturity — attributing all prosperity to God.
Laban
Aramean patriarch, Jacob's uncle
The great antagonist of Jacob's Haran years. An exploiter who changed Jacob's wages ten times yet whose prosperity derived from God's blessing on Jacob.
Rachel
Jacob's beloved wife, Laban's daughter
Her theft of the teraphim introduces a spiritual danger that Jacob unknowingly curses; her premature death (35:16–20) haunts the rest of the Jacob narrative.
Leah
Jacob's first wife
Though less loved, Leah loyally follows Jacob. Her sons — Reuben, Simeon, Levi, Judah — will carry the weight of the tribal narrative.

Ancient Sources

LXX (Septuagint)
The Greek translates teraphim (v.19) as ta eidōla, "the idols" — making explicit the religious dimension that the Hebrew term leaves partly ambiguous.
The LXX interpretation influenced early Christian readings of Rachel's theft as a type of the church taking spiritual treasure from paganism — an allegory found in Origen.
Josephus, Antiquities 1.19
Josephus emphasizes Rachel's intelligence in hiding the teraphim under a camel saddle and inventing a menstrual excuse — he finds her deception admirable where the text is neutral.
Josephus consistently humanizes patriarchal figures by filling in psychological motivation.

Reception History

Augustine, City of God XVIII
The Mizpah covenant functions as a type of the partition between the city of God and the city of man — two peoples dwelling side by side under divine surveillance.
Calvin, Commentary on Genesis
Calvin notes that Jacob's twenty-year inventory of faithful service is not boasting but legitimate witness against a false accusation. The righteous may defend themselves with facts.
Modern Missions Movement
The Mizpah benediction was adopted by 19th-century missionaries sending partners out as a prayer for mutual accountability and protection across distance.

Chiastic structure centers on God's warning dream to Laban — the hinge around which Jacob's vindication pivots. The search for the teraphim (C′) mirrors the flight (C) but reverses it: what was taken in secret is sought in the open and not found.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 31 concludes the Haran cycle with a demonstration of divine sovereignty over human scheming. God directed Jacob's departure, warned Laban, and vindicated twenty years of faithful labor. The chapter closes one arc (Jacob and Laban) and opens another (Jacob and Esau; Jacob and Canaan).
