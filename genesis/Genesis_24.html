Genesis 24

Isaac and Rebekah

Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration. What prompted it: A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–9 — Abraham's Commission: A Wife for Isaac
1Abraham was now very old, and the Lord had blessed him in every way.2He said to the senior servant in his household, the one in charge of all that he had, "Put your hand under my thigh.3I want you to swear by the Lord, the God of heaven and the God of earth, that you will not get a wife for my son from the daughters of the Canaanites, among whom I am living,4but will go to my country and my own relatives and get a wife for my son Isaac."5The servant asked him, "What if the woman is unwilling to come back with me to this land? Shall I then take your son back to the country you came from?"6"Make sure that you do not take my son back there," Abraham said.7"The Lord, the God of heaven, who brought me out of my father's household and my native land and who spoke to me and promised me on oath, saying, 'To your offspring I will give this land'—he will send his angel before you so that you can get a wife for my son from there.8If the woman is unwilling to come back with you, then you will be released from this oath of mine. Only do not take my son back there."9So the servant put his hand under the thigh of his master Abraham and swore an oath to him concerning this matter.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
24:1–4

The final major act of Abraham's life is securing a wife for Isaac from his own people — not a Canaanite woman. The concern is not racial but covenantal: intermarriage with Canaanites would mean intermarriage with Canaanite religion, corrupting the covenant line. The servant's oath (sworn on the most solemn oath formula of the ancient Near East — hand under the thigh, near the organ of procreation and covenant promise) binds him to the task with the weight of the promise itself. Abraham's confidence that God will send his angel before the servant (v.7) is the confidence of a man who has learned over a lifetime that God keeps his word.

Context

Abraham's insistence that Isaac not marry a Canaanite woman is the first explicit concern for covenant continuity in the next generation. The oath sworn on the patriarch's thigh (an ancient oath gesture, possibly relating to the "seed" — descendants) is the most solemn form of commitment in the ANE. The concern for endogamy (marriage within the kinship group) preserves the covenant line and echoes forward to the Mosaic prohibition of intermarriage with Canaanites (Deut 7:3).

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 26:34–35; 28:1–2Isaac's sons will face the same issue: Esau's Canaanite wives "make life bitter" for Isaac and Rebekah; Jacob is specifically sent to Paddan-aram to find a wife from the family line.
  • 2 Cor 6:14Paul's "do not be unequally yoked with unbelievers" echoes the same theological principle — covenant integrity requires covenant partnership.
Verses 10–27 — The Servant Prays; Rebekah Appears
10Then the servant left, taking with him ten of his master's camels loaded with all kinds of good things from his master. He set out for Aram Naharaim and made his way to the town of Nahor.11He had the camels kneel down near the well outside the town; it was toward evening, the time the women go out to draw water.12Then he prayed, "Lord, God of my master Abraham, make me successful today, and show kindness to my master Abraham.13See, I am standing beside this spring, and the daughters of the townspeople are coming out to draw water.14May it be that when I say to a young woman, 'Please let down your jar that I may have a drink,' and she says, 'Drink, and I'll water your camels too'—let her be the one you have chosen for your servant Isaac. By this I will know that you have shown kindness to my master."15Before he had finished praying, Rebekah came out with her jar on her shoulder. She was the daughter of Bethuel son of Milkah, who was the wife of Abraham's brother Nahor.16The woman was very beautiful, a virgin; no man had ever slept with her. She went down to the spring, filled her jar and came up again.17The servant hurried to meet her and said, "Please give me a little water from your jar."18"Drink, my lord," she said, and quickly lowered the jar to her hands and gave him a drink.19After she had given him a drink, she said, "I'll draw water for your camels too, until they have had enough to drink."20So she quickly emptied her jar into the trough, ran back to the well to draw more water, and drew enough for all his camels.21Without saying a word, the man watched her closely to learn whether or not the Lord had made his journey successful.22When the camels had finished drinking, the man took out a gold nose ring weighing a beka and two gold bracelets weighing ten shekels.23Then he asked, "Whose daughter are you? Please tell me, is there room in your father's house for us to spend the night?"24She answered him, "I am the daughter of Bethuel, the son that Milkah bore to Nahor."25And she added, "We have plenty of straw and fodder, as well as room for you to spend the night."26Then the man bowed down and worshiped the Lord,27saying, "Praise be to the Lord, the God of my master Abraham, who has not abandoned his kindness and faithfulness to my master. As for me, the Lord has led me on the journey to the house of my master's relatives."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
24:12–14

The servant's prayer at the well is a model of biblical faith: specific ("give me success today"), expectant (he sets a precise sign as a test), and God-oriented ("show kindness to my master Abraham"). The sign he asks for is not arbitrary — it tests for the character quality of generous initiative in hospitality, precisely what Rebekah's role will require. Before he finishes praying (v.15: "before he had finished praying"), Rebekah arrives. Answered prayer before the petition is completed — the same reality Paul describes as God doing "immeasurably more than all we ask or imagine" (Eph 3:20).

24:27

"Praise be to the LORD, the God of my master Abraham, who has not abandoned his kindness and faithfulness to my master." The servant's response to answered prayer is immediate doxology. Hesed (kindness, covenant love) and emet (faithfulness, truth) — the two covenant attributes of God that Moses will see in Exodus 34:6 — are the theological categories with which the servant interprets the providential leading he has experienced. Providence is hesed and emet made visible in events.

Hebrew Word Study

חֶסֶד ḥesed — steadfast love / covenant loyalty: The theological keyword of the chapter, appearing three times. Ḥesed is God's covenant love — loyal, persistent, dependable. The servant prays for ḥesed (v.12), recognizes it in Rebekah's appearance (v.27), and declares it fulfilled. The entire chapter is a meditation on how ḥesed operates in ordinary events.

הִצְלִיחַ hiṣlîaḥ — granted success: The servant prays for success (ṣlḥ) and recognizes it immediately when Rebekah appears. The word also means "to prosper, to make succeed." Providence in this chapter is quiet — no burning bushes or angelic appearances — just a servant's prayer answered by the first young woman who appears.

רִבְקָה riḇqāh — Rebekah: The name may mean "tied" or possibly related to "fatted young cow." More importantly, her actions reveal her character before her identity — she draws water for a stranger and then voluntarily for all his camels (10 camels would need hundreds of gallons). This extraordinary generosity identifies her.

Context

The literary technique of Genesis 24 is deliberate repetition. The servant's journey is described (vv.10–21), then narrated again in his speech to Laban (vv.34–49) with small but significant variations. This double narration slows the reader, creates suspense, and confirms the providential interpretation. In the servant's retelling, what appeared coincidental in the first account now appears as divinely orchestrated. Providence is often only visible in retrospect.

Places & Geography

Nahor / Paddan-Aram / Haran
Southeastern Turkey / northwestern Syria
The "city of Nahor" is associated with the region of Haran — the family's original stopping point. "Paddan-aram" (the field of Aram) appears in later chapters. Abraham's family maintained connections there for generations — this is where Jacob will later go to find his wives.
Mesopotamia
Tigris-Euphrates region
The servant's journey from Canaan to Mesopotamia (modern Iraq/Syria) covers hundreds of miles. The mission's seriousness is underscored by the ten-camel caravan loaded with gifts — a considerable diplomatic expedition.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
24:58

"Will you go with this man?" "I will go." Rebekah's one-word answer (in Hebrew: elech, "I will go") is the decisive act of faith that echoes Abraham's original lech-lecha ("go, you") from 12:1. She leaves country, people, and father's household — the same three things Abraham was called to leave. The parallel is unmistakeable: the wife who must come to the covenant son mirrors the call of the covenant father. Faith responds to the divine word with immediate, unhesitating obedience, even when it requires leaving everything known.

Context

Rebekah's two-word answer — "I will go" — parallels Abraham's departure from Ur in its radical willingness to leave everything and go to an unknown land to a man she has never seen. Her family blesses her with words that echo the Abrahamic promise: "May you increase to thousands upon thousands; may your offspring possess the gate of their enemies" (v.60). She enters the story already carrying the covenant blessing.

Cross-Reference

  • Ruth 1:16–17Ruth's "where you go I will go" parallels Rebekah's "I will go" — both are covenant declarations of loyalty from a woman leaving her homeland to join the covenant people.
  • Gen 12:1–4Rebekah's "I will go" echoes Abraham's obedience to "go" — the covenant consistently calls people to radical, immediate departure toward the promise.
Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The Servant (likely Eliezer)
Abraham's chief servant; model of faithful stewardship
Prays before acting, credits God immediately, refuses to eat before completing his report, does not deviate from his mission. A model of servant leadership — completely self-effacing, entirely focused on his master's purpose. His name is never given in this chapter.
Rebekah
Daughter of Bethuel; future matriarch
Identified by extraordinary generosity before her identity is known. Her "I will go" is one of the most consequential two-word decisions in the entire Bible. She becomes the mother of Jacob and Esau, the next generation of covenant carriers.
Isaac
Son of Abraham; bridegroom
Largely passive in this chapter — the subject of everyone's efforts. His role is to receive. He is "comforted after his mother's death" — the grief of ch.23 finds healing in ch.24. Marriage becomes a vehicle of divine comfort.

Ancient Sources

ANE Marriage Customs
Marriage negotiation in the ancient world involved family representatives, bride price, and parental consent. Genesis 24 follows this pattern while uniquely emphasizing the bride's own consent (v.58 — "Will you go?"). This element of Rebekah's choice is notable in an ancient context.

Reception History

The Providence Theme
Commentators from Augustine to Chrysostom to Calvin have used Genesis 24 as the primary biblical text on divine providence in ordinary life. No angel appears, no miracle occurs — just a prayer answered by the first young woman at a well. God's guidance operates through natural means and human character.
Type of the Church
Patristic interpreters read the chapter allegorically: Abraham = God the Father; Isaac = Christ; the servant = the Holy Spirit; Rebekah = the Church called from the nations to be Christ's bride. The allegorical reading, while not controlling, captures genuine theological resonances.

Literary Structure

AAbraham commissions servant; oath sworn (vv.1–9)
BJourney to Mesopotamia (vv.10–11)
CServant's prayer at the well (vv.12–14)
CENTER: Rebekah appears; sign fulfilled (vv.15–21)
C'Servant worships; declares ḥesed (vv.22–27)
B'Journey narrated again in servant's speech (vv.34–49)
A'Rebekah's consent; journey home; marriage (vv.50–67)
The double narrative (journey described, then re-narrated) is the literary signature of the chapter. The repetition is not redundancy but a theological statement: what looked like coincidence in the first telling is revealed as providence in the retelling. Genesis 24 teaches how to read your own life in retrospect — as divinely guided.

Intertextual Threading

Gen 24:58→Ruth 1:16–17Echo
"I will go" — Rebekah's instant decision mirrors Ruth's "where you go I will go." Both are women of extraordinary covenant loyalty, leaving home for an unknown man in faith.
Gen 12:1→Gen 24:58Echo
Rebekah's "I will go" echoes Abraham's departure — she, too, answers the call to leave homeland for the covenant promise. The covenant character is replicated in each new generation.
Gen 24:12→1 Thess 5:17Echo
The servant "prays before acting" — the model of prayer-before-mission that Paul commends: "pray without ceasing." Providence responds to persistent, specific, expectant prayer.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 24 is the Bible's great chapter on providence. God is never named as the direct actor — no angel descends, no thunder speaks. Yet every reader senses the divine hand in the servant's answered prayer, Rebekah's extraordinary generosity, and the timing of Isaac's evening walk. Providence works through ordinary human faithfulness, prayer, and character.
