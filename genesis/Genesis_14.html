Genesis 14

War of Kings & Melchizedek

Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration. What prompted it: A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–12 — The War of the Kings; Lot Captured
1At the time of Amraphel king of Shinar, Arioch king of Ellasar, Kedorlaomer king of Elam and Tidal king of Goyim,2these kings went to war against Bera king of Sodom, Birsha king of Gomorrah, Shinab king of Admah, Shemeber king of Zeboyim, and the king of Bela (that is, Zoar).3All these latter kings joined forces in the Valley of Siddim (that is, the Dead Sea Valley).4For twelve years they had been subject to Kedorlaomer, but in the thirteenth year they rebelled.5In the fourteenth year, Kedorlaomer and the kings allied with him went out and defeated the Rephaites in Ashteroth Karnaim, the Zuzites in Ham, the Emites in Shaveh Kiriathaim6and the Horites in the hill country of Seir, as far as El Paran near the desert.7Then they turned back and went to En Mishpat (that is, Kadesh), and they conquered the whole territory of the Amalekites, as well as the Amorites who were living in Hazezon Tamar.8Then the king of Sodom, the king of Gomorrah, the king of Admah, the king of Zeboyim and the king of Bela (that is, Zoar) marched out and drew up their battle lines in the Valley of Siddim9against Kedorlaomer king of Elam, Tidal king of Goyim, Amraphel king of Shinar and Arioch king of Ellasar—four kings against five.10Now the Valley of Siddim was full of tar pits, and when the kings of Sodom and Gomorrah fled, some of the men fell into them and the rest fled to the hills.11The four kings seized all the goods of Sodom and Gomorrah and all their food; then they went away.12They also carried off Abram's nephew Lot and his possessions, since he was living in Sodom.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:1–12

The war of four kings against five is a real historical event with verifiable geographical details. The eastern coalition's campaign down the Transjordan and into the Negev corresponds to known ancient trade and military routes. Lot's capture is the direct consequence of his choice in ch.13: he moved his tent "near Sodom" (13:12), then lived in Sodom (14:12). The gravitational pull of the world's cities draws him in until he is taken with them in their defeat. His uncle Abram — the wandering nomad with the altars — must rescue him. The one who seemed to have the inferior position (the wanderer in the hills) has the superior standing (the covenant with God).

Historical Context

The four-against-five-kings war is set in an era of Mesopotamian hegemony over Canaan, consistent with historical patterns in the early 2nd millennium BC. Chedorlaomer of Elam is particularly notable — Elamite power extended westward during the Isin-Larsa period. The cities of the plain (Sodom, Gomorrah, Admah, Zeboiim, Zoar) are also listed in Deuteronomy 29:23, confirming an established tradition.

Places & Geography

Home of Chedorlaomer, the dominant king. Elamite power in Mesopotamia and Canaan is historically attested in the early 2nd millennium BC.

Verses 13–16 — Abram Rescues Lot
13A man who had escaped came and reported this to Abram the Hebrew. Now Abram was living near the great trees of Mamre the Amorite, a brother of Eshkol and Aner, all of whom were allied with Abram.14When Abram heard that his relative had been taken captive, he called out the 318 trained men born in his household and went in pursuit as far as Dan.15During the night Abram divided his men to attack them and he routed them, pursuing them as far as Hobah, north of Damascus.16He recovered all the goods and brought back his relative Lot and his possessions, together with the women and the other people.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:14–16

Abram's response to the news of Lot's capture is immediate and decisive: he arms 318 trained men of his household and pursues. This is not inconsistent with faith — the God of Abraham is the Lord of armies, and Abram acts as a legitimate protector of family and the innocent. The rescue is complete: Lot, his possessions, all the people, are recovered. This episode establishes Abram as a significant military and political figure in Canaan, which gives weight to both the Melchizedek encounter and the subsequent refusal of Sodom's king. Abram's power is real; his refusal of Sodom's reward (v.23) is therefore a genuinely costly act.

Context

Abram's military rescue of Lot reveals a man of action and loyalty, not merely a passive recipient of promises. His 318 trained men ("born in his house") indicate he is a significant chieftain, not merely a wandering shepherd. Yet after the victory, he refuses any personal profit — setting up the Melchizedek scene as the proper response to military success.

Cross-Reference

  • Heb 7:1–2The author of Hebrews builds his entire Melchizedek theology on this single episode.
  • Ps 110:4"You are a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek" — the royal psalm applied to David's Lord, cited by Jesus (Matt 22:44) and Hebrews.
Verses 17–24 — Melchizedek Blesses Abram
17After Abram returned from defeating Kedorlaomer and the kings allied with him, the king of Sodom came out to meet him in the Valley of Shaveh (that is, the King's Valley).18Then Melchizedek king of Salem brought out bread and wine. He was priest of God Most High,19and he blessed Abram, saying, "Blessed be Abram by God Most High, Creator of heaven and earth.20And praise be to God Most High, who delivered your enemies into your hand." Then Abram gave him a tenth of everything.21The king of Sodom said to Abram, "Give me the people and keep the goods for yourself."22But Abram said to the king of Sodom, "With raised hand I have sworn an oath to the Lord, God Most High, Creator of heaven and earth,23that I will accept nothing belonging to you, not even a thread or the strap of a sandal, so that you will never be able to say, 'I made Abram rich.'24I will accept nothing but what my men have eaten and the share that belongs to the men who went with me—to Aner, Eshkol and Mamre. Let them have their share."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
14:18–20

Melchizedek is one of the most mysterious and theologically significant figures in the Old Testament. He is king of Salem (Jerusalem) and priest of El Elyon (God Most High) — combining the royal and priestly roles that in Israel were always kept strictly separate. He has no genealogy, no record of birth or death — not because he is an angel or a pre-incarnate Christ, but because the text deliberately omits these details to make him a type. Hebrews 7 develops the Melchizedekian priesthood at length: Jesus is a priest not after the Levitical order but after the order of Melchizedek — eternal, non-hereditary, royal-priestly. Abram's tithe to Melchizedek (before the Levitical law) establishes the superiority of the Melchizedekian order over the Aaronic. The bread and wine Melchizedek brings are not incidentally suggestive of the Lord's Supper.

Hebrew Word Study

מַלְכִּי־צֶדֶק malkî-ṣedeq — Melchizedek: The name means "my king is righteousness" or "king of righteousness" — a throne name rather than a personal name, common in ANE royal titulary. Hebrews 7:2 exploits the etymological meaning.

שָׁלֵם šālēm — Salem: Identified with Jerusalem in Psalm 76:2 and by Josephus. The name means "peace" (šālôm) — "king of Salem" = "king of peace" (Heb 7:2).

אֵל עֶלְיוֹן ʾēl ʿelyôn — God Most High: A divine title combining the generic Semitic word for God (ʾēl) with the superlative ʿelyôn (Most High). Abram immediately identifies this with YHWH (v.22), showing he recognizes Melchizedek's God as his own.

מַעֲשֵׂר maʿăśēr — tenth / tithe: Abram spontaneously gives a tenth to the priest-king before any tithing law existed. This establishes the spiritual priority of the Melchizedekian order over the later Levitical priesthood (the argument of Heb 7:4–10).

Context

Melchizedek appears from nowhere, has no genealogy (a significant omission in the genealogy-obsessed world of Genesis), pronounces blessing, receives a tithe, and disappears. He is simultaneously king of a city and priest of God Most High — a combination later forbidden in Israel (Uzziah was struck with leprosy for attempting it, 2 Chr 26:16–21). His appearance anticipates the royal-priestly office that Israel's system cannot sustain but that Christ embodies.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 110:4God swears to David's Lord: "You are a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek" — the Davidic king as eternal priest.
  • Heb 5:6–10; 7:1–28The most extensive NT treatment: Melchizedek's priesthood is superior to Aaron's because Abraham (ancestor of Levi) paid tithes to him. Christ is the fulfillment of this superior priesthood.
  • Heb 7:3Melchizedek is described as "without father or mother or genealogy, having neither beginning of days nor end of life" — not that he was supernatural, but that Scripture records none of these, making him a type of Christ's eternal priesthood.

Places & Geography

Salem / Jerusalem
Site of later Jerusalem
The earliest reference to the city that becomes Jerusalem. Melchizedek's priesthood at Salem anticipates the Davidic covenant, the temple, and ultimately the heavenly Jerusalem of Revelation 21.
Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Melchizedek
King of Salem; Priest of God Most High
The most theologically significant figure in the chapter. He appears without introduction, blesses Abram, receives a tithe, and vanishes — his lack of genealogy (unusual for Genesis) becomes the basis for Hebrews' typological argument about Christ's eternal priesthood.
Abram
Patriarch; Warrior; Man of integrity
Revealed here as a military leader with 318 trained men. His refusal of the king of Sodom's offer demonstrates that his wealth will come from God alone — an act of faith that Hebrews reads as foundational.
King of Sodom
Unnamed; Morally corrupt city's ruler
Offers Abram the spoils of war. His offer, rejected by Abram, is a test: will the patriarch allow a wicked king to claim credit for his prosperity? Abram's refusal protects the theological purity of his witness.

Ancient Sources

Dead Sea Scrolls — 11QMelchizedek
A fragmentary Qumran text casts Melchizedek as a heavenly being who will execute judgment on the Day of Atonement. He is identified with the "elohim" of Psalm 82 and given a quasi-angelic status.
Shows how Second Temple Judaism elevated Melchizedek far beyond the Genesis text, making the Hebrews author's careful typological reading a measured response to existing speculation.
Josephus, Antiquities 1.10.2
Josephus identifies Salem with Jerusalem and Melchizedek as the first to build a temple there. He translates Melchizedek as "righteous king" and notes his priestly role.
Josephus' geographical identification with Jerusalem became standard in Jewish and Christian tradition.

Reception History

Letter to the Hebrews, chapters 5–7
The NT's most extensive use of the OT interprets Melchizedek as the type whose antitype is Christ. Key moves: (1) his name = "king of righteousness"; (2) Salem = Jerusalem = "king of peace"; (3) no recorded genealogy = eternal priesthood; (4) Levi paid tithes through Abraham = Melchizedekian order superior to Levitical.
Psalm 110 and David
Psalm 110:4 ("a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek") is the most-cited OT verse in the NT. Jesus uses it to stump the Pharisees (Matt 22:41–46); Hebrews builds a theology of Christ's priesthood on it.

Literary Structure

BLot captured

CENTER: Melchizedek blesses Abram (vv.18–20)
C'King of Sodom offers reward
B'Abram refuses to be enriched by Sodom

The Melchizedek episode stands at the exact center of the chapter — a literary signal of its theological priority. The war provides context, Lot's rescue provides occasion, but the central point is the encounter with the priest-king who blesses in God's name and receives the patriarch's tithe.

Intertextual Threading

Gen 14:18→Ps 110:4Typology
The priest-king of Salem anticipates the Davidic king who is also priest "after the order of Melchizedek" — Jesus identifies himself as the fulfillment of Psalm 110.
Gen 14:18–20→Heb 7:1–10Fulfillment
Hebrews builds its entire high-priestly Christology on this 3-verse passage: Christ is the eternal Melchizedekian priest whose ministry surpasses the Levitical order.
Gen 14:22→Gen 12:2–3Echo
Abram refuses enrichment from Sodom's king because his wealth comes from YHWH alone — directly living out the Abrahamic covenant, where God provides the blessing.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 14 contains the Bible's most mysterious figure: Melchizedek. His appearance without genealogy, his dual office of king and priest, his receipt of tithe from the patriarch, and his blessing — all point forward to Christ's eternal priesthood. The chapter is foundational for the Letter to the Hebrews' entire argument.
