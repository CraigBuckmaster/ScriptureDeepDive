Genesis 37

Joseph's Dreams; Sold into Egypt

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–11 — Joseph's Dreams; His Brothers' Hatred
1Jacob lived in the land where his father had stayed, the land of Canaan.2This is the account of Jacob's family line. Joseph, a young man of seventeen, was tending the flocks with his brothers, the sons of Bilhah and the sons of Zilpah, his father's wives, and he brought their father a bad report about them.3Now Israel loved Joseph more than any of his other sons, because he had been born to him in his old age; and he made an ornate robe for him.4When his brothers saw that their father loved him more than any of them, they hated him and could not speak a kind word to him.5Joseph had a dream, and when he told it to his brothers, they hated him all the more.6He said to them, "Listen to this dream I had:7We were binding sheaves of grain out in the field when suddenly my sheaf rose and stood upright, while your sheaves gathered around mine and bowed down to it."8His brothers said to him, "Do you intend to reign over us? Will you actually rule us?" And they hated him all the more because of his dream and what he had said.9Then he had another dream, and he told it to his brothers. "Listen," he said, "I had another dream, and this time the sun and moon and eleven stars were bowing down to me."10When he told his father as well as his brothers, his father rebuked him and said, "What is this dream you had? Will your mother and I and your brothers actually come and bow down to the ground before you?"11His brothers were jealous of him, but his father kept the matter in mind.

Hebrew Word Study

כְּתֹנֶת פַּסִּים ketōnet passîm — robe of many colors / long robe: The exact meaning of passîm is debated — it may mean "long-sleeved" or "ornamented." The same phrase appears only in 2 Sam 13:18 for the garment of royal daughters. This is a garment marking status and favoritism — and it will become the instrument of Joseph's apparent death (v.31).

חָלַם ḥālam — dream: Dreams are the primary vehicle of divine revelation in the Joseph story. The same root is used for Pharaoh's dreams (ch.41) — God speaks through Joseph and through Pharaoh using the same medium.

קָנָא qānāʾ — jealous / zealous: The brothers' jealousy (qin'āh) is the same root as God's own jealousy for his covenant people (Exod 20:5). Misdirected passion for honor drives them to sell their own brother.

Historical Context

Joseph's coat (v.3) has generated extensive debate: the Hebrew ketonet passim means either "a coat of many colours" (KJV) or "a coat with long sleeves" or "an ornamented tunic" (other translations). The same phrase appears in 2 Samuel 13:18 for the garment worn by royal daughters. Whatever its precise form, the coat is a status symbol — Jacob marks Joseph as the heir-designate, bypassing the older sons. This is a repetition of the pattern of the younger over the older that has run through Genesis.

Context

The Joseph narrative opens with the same family dynamics that structured the Jacob narrative: parental favoritism (Isaac preferred Esau; Jacob prefers Joseph), sibling rivalry, and the younger son's elevation. But where Jacob stole his position through deception, Joseph's elevation comes through divine dreams and providential suffering. The two dreams (sheaves bowing, heavenly bodies bowing) frame the entire narrative — they will not be fulfilled until 42:6 and 43:26 when the brothers bow before Joseph in Egypt.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 42:6; 43:26The dream fulfillments: brothers bow before Joseph the governor.
  • Psalm 105:17–19The Psalm retrospectively identifies Joseph's suffering as God's testing: "He had sent a man before them — Joseph, sold as a slave... until what he foretold came to pass."
  • Acts 7:9–10Stephen's sermon: "The patriarchs, jealous of Joseph, sold him into Egypt. But God was with him."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
37:2-4

The toledoth of Jacob — the structural marker for the final section of Genesis — opens with Joseph at 17, tending flocks with his brothers and bringing Jacob a bad report about them. The bad report and Jacob's special love for Joseph together set up the conflict immediately. Jacob's love for Joseph is tied to his love for Rachel — Joseph is Rachel's firstborn, born in Jacob's old age. The differential love Jacob received (and perpetuated) is being passed to the next generation.

37:5-8

Joseph's first dream — sheaves bowing to his sheaf — is presented to the brothers without apparent awareness of its provocative content. Either Joseph is naive or he is deliberately asserting the dream's claim. The brothers' interpretation is immediate and correct: "Do you intend to reign over us?" The dream means exactly what they say. Joseph's later exaltation in Egypt will confirm that their interpretation was right — and that their hatred achieved the opposite of what they intended.

37:9-11

The second dream — sun, moon, and eleven stars bowing to Joseph — escalates the claim. Now not just the brothers but the parents are included. Jacob rebukes Joseph for the second dream but "kept the matter in mind." The father who has arranged the family dynamics that provoked the brothers' jealousy is also the first to recognise that the dreams may be genuine. He rebukes publicly and ponders privately.

37:11

The brothers are jealous; Jacob keeps the matter in mind. Alter's observation: "The brothers seize on the dreams as further evidence of Joseph's presumption; Jacob sees in them something more. The father who gave Joseph the coat now registers the dreams with the caution of a man who has himself encountered divine dreams." Jacob is the link between the dream traditions of the patriarchs and the dream traditions of the Joseph narrative.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:3

Ketonet passim — the problematic phrase for the coat. Sarna reviews the options: (1) "coat of many colours" (based on LXX and Vulgate); (2) "long-sleeved coat" (based on cognate evidence); (3) "ornamented coat" (based on archaeological parallels). The second occurrence (2 Sam 13:18) describes royal daughters' attire, supporting a high-status reading. The specific colour/form matters less than its function: a visible marker of paternal favour and heir-designation.

37:8

The brothers' question — "Will you really reign over us? Will you actually rule us?" — uses the words maloch timloch and mashal timshol, two different verbs for ruling in emphatic construction. Sarna notes the double emphasis: the brothers are not merely asking a question but expressing outrage at the implied claim. The dreams are not private visions but public assertions of status, and the brothers hear them as such.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:3-4

The sequence is clinically precise: Jacob loved Joseph more (cause) → made him the coat (visible expression) → brothers saw that their father loved him more than them (perception) → they hated him (effect). Alter: "The narrative does not comment on whether Jacob's differential love was wise or just; it simply presents the family system that will generate the crisis. The coat is the visible emblem of a love that the family cannot absorb without fracturing."

37:10-11

Jacob's response to the second dream — "What is this dream you had? Will your mother and I and your brothers actually come and bow down to the ground before you?" — is followed immediately by the note that "his brothers were jealous of him, but his father kept the matter in mind." Alter: the contrast between the brothers' jealousy and Jacob's pondering is the key to the chapter. The one who has himself received divine dreams (28:12; 31:10) knows to weigh rather than dismiss them.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
37:3-4

Calvin on Jacob's partiality: "Although Jacob is not to be altogether excused for this error, yet the cause of greater blame rests with the brothers who were carried away by envy to fratricidal hatred." Calvin distributes the moral responsibility: Jacob's preference is a parental failure; the brothers' hatred is a more serious sin. The coat is Jacob's mistake; the desire to kill is the brothers' sin.

37:9-11

Calvin on Joseph's dreams: "There is no doubt that God had chosen this method of announcing his purpose to Joseph... God by this means declared that Joseph was to be the preserver of the family. Moses did not record these dreams by accident; the whole narrative of Joseph is an illustration of the doctrine that God watches over his own people, however apparently desperate their condition."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
37:3

The NET note on ketonet passim surveys the textual and archaeological evidence. The note notes that the LXX (chiton poikilon = variegated coat) and the traditional "many colours" reading are based on the LXX, not the Hebrew. The Hebrew passim may come from a root meaning flat of hand or sole of foot (i.e. a long coat reaching the extremities), consistent with the royal garment of 2 Sam 13:18. The note concludes the coat was a long ornamented tunic marking high status.

37:11

The NET note on "kept the matter in mind" (shamar et hadavar) observes this is the same expression used for Mary in Luke 2:51 (pondered these things in her heart). The note draws the connection deliberately: in both cases, a parent receives a divine communication about their child's extraordinary destiny and holds it internally rather than acting on it immediately. Jacob's pondering is the model for all who receive divine intimation before the time of fulfilment.

Verses 12–28 — The Plot; Joseph Sold into Slavery
12Now his brothers had gone to graze their father's flocks near Shechem,13and Israel said to Joseph, "As you know, your brothers are grazing the flocks near Shechem. Come, I am going to send you to them." "Very well," he replied.14So he said to him, "Go and see if all is well with your brothers and with the flocks, and bring word back to me." Then he sent him off from the Valley of Hebron. When Joseph arrived at Shechem,15a man found him wandering around in the fields and asked him, "What are you looking for?"16He replied, "I'm looking for my brothers. Can you tell me where they are grazing their flocks?"17"They have moved on from here," the man answered. "I heard them say, 'Let's go to Dothan.'" So Joseph went after his brothers and found them near Dothan.18But they saw him in the distance, and before he reached them, they plotted to kill him.19"Here comes that dreamer!" they said to each other.20"Come now, let's kill him and throw him into one of these cisterns and say that a ferocious animal devoured him. Then we'll see what comes of his dreams."21When Reuben heard this, he tried to rescue him from their hands. "Let's not take his life," he said.22"Don't shed any blood. Throw him into this cistern here in the wilderness, but don't lay a hand on him." Reuben said this to rescue him from them and take him back to his father.23So when Joseph came to his brothers, they stripped him of his robe—the ornate robe he was wearing—24and they took him and threw him into the cistern. The cistern was empty; there was no water in it.25As they sat down to eat their meal, they looked up and saw a caravan of Ishmaelites coming from Gilead. Their camels were loaded with spices, balm and myrrh, and they were on their way to take them down to Egypt.26Judah said to his brothers, "What will we gain if we kill our brother and cover up his blood?27Come, let's sell him to the Ishmaelites and not lay our hands on him; after all, he is our brother, our own flesh and blood." His brothers agreed.28So when the Midianite merchants came by, his brothers pulled Joseph up out of the cistern and sold him for twenty shekels of silver to the Ishmaelites, who took him to Egypt.

Hebrew Word Study

בֹּר bōr — pit / cistern: The same word used in Psalms for Sheol/the grave (Ps 28:1; 30:3). Joseph's descent into the pit is a death-and-resurrection pattern: he is removed from the land of the living, then raised up to rule. The motif recurs in the prison (another pit, matṣōr).

עֶשְׂרִים כֶּסֶף ʿesrîm kesep — twenty pieces of silver: The price of a slave aged 5–20 according to Leviticus 27:5. Joseph was seventeen — the brothers received the legal minimum for a young male slave. This price is later echoed in Zechariah's thirty pieces (Zech 11:12–13) and Judas's betrayal.

Historical Context

The sale of Joseph to the Ishmaelites/Midianite traders is historically plausible: the spice trade route from Gilead to Egypt passed through the region of Dothan, and Egyptian records from the Middle Kingdom period (roughly the period of the patriarchs) document the presence of Asiatic slaves in Egypt. The price of twenty shekels of silver is consistent with the slave price for a young man in the ancient Near East (the Mosaic law of Lev 27:5 sets the redemption price for a male between 5-20 years at twenty shekels).

Context

The pit scene is layered with irony: the dreamer is thrown into an empty pit — the same Hebrew word (bōr) used for the grave. The brothers sit down to eat while Joseph is in the pit (v.25) — an echo of Esau's selling his birthright for food. Reuben's plan to rescue Joseph (v.21) is frustrated by his absence (v.29) — he bears the guilt of failing to act decisively. Judah's compromise (sell him rather than kill him, v.26) is presented as the "better" option, but it still amounts to betrayal. No brother emerges with moral credit.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 50:20Joseph's definitive theological interpretation: "You intended to harm me, but God intended it for good."
  • Psalm 105:17God "sent a man before them — Joseph, sold as a slave." The Psalm reads the sale as divine sending.
  • Matt 27:3–10Judas's thirty pieces of silver echo Joseph's twenty — both are betrayals of the innocent for money.
  • Acts 7:9Stephen cites the brothers' jealousy as the mechanism of God's providential plan for Israel's salvation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
37:18-20

The plot to kill Joseph begins while he is still at a distance — "they conspired against him to kill him." The premeditation is complete before he arrives. But the conspiracy immediately fractures: Reuben proposes to throw him in a cistern rather than kill him (secretly planning to rescue him), and then Judah proposes to sell him rather than kill him. The plot against Joseph is mitigated by its own internal disagreements.

37:24-27

The brothers strip Joseph of his coat — the emblem of his favoured status — and throw him into the dry cistern. While he is in the pit, they sit down to eat. Their indifference to his distress as they eat is morally horrifying. Reuben is absent when the Midianites pass. Judah proposes the sale with an argument that is partly merciful ("don't let us harm him, for he is our brother and our own flesh") and partly commercial ("what will we gain?"). Both motives are present simultaneously.

37:26-27

Judah's suggestion to sell rather than kill Joseph is the pivot of the narrative. The same Judah who will, decades later, offer himself as a substitute for Benjamin (44:33) here initiates the sale of Joseph. The contrast between Genesis 37 and Genesis 44 is the measure of Judah's transformation. The man who sold his brother becomes the man who volunteers to die for his brother.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:20

The brothers' plan — "throw him into one of these cisterns... then we'll say that a ferocious animal devoured him" — already contains its own self-indictment. They are planning both the act and the lie simultaneously. Sarna: "The brothers who hated Joseph's dreams now become the unwitting instruments of their fulfilment. By selling Joseph into slavery they send him to Egypt; by going to Egypt he saves them from famine; by saving them he fulfils the dreams of the sheaves and stars."

37:28

The Ishmaelites/Midianites confusion (v.25 says Ishmaelites; v.28 says Midianites; 39:1 says Potiphar bought him from the Ishmaelites) is one of the source-critical touchstones of the Pentateuch. Sarna argues for the non-critical reading: the terms may have been used interchangeably for related semi-nomadic trading peoples (the judges era attests this interchange, cf. Judg 8:22-24).

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:23-24

Joseph is stripped of his coat and thrown into the cistern. Alter notes the coat's trajectory through the chapter: given by Jacob (v.3), seen by the brothers (v.4), reported worn as he arrives (v.23), stripped off (v.23), dipped in blood and shown to Jacob (v.31). The coat is the chapter's material through-line, carrying the story's emotional content from paternal favour through violent rejection to the deception of the father.

37:25

The brothers sit down to eat after throwing Joseph in the pit. Alter: "Their composure in the face of their brother's cries — and we hear those cries retrospectively from Joseph himself in 42:21 — is one of the most chilling details in the narrative. The meal continues; the caravan arrives; Joseph is sold. The brothers' hardness of heart is documented in the act of eating.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
37:20-22

Calvin reads Reuben's intervention charitably: "Reuben, although the eldest, had not sufficient authority to liberate Joseph from his brothers' hands; yet it was something that he restrained their ferocity so far as to substitute a milder evil." Calvin does not praise Reuben's indirection (throwing into a pit rather than confronting the sin directly) but acknowledges that Reuben's partial courage saved Joseph's life — and was part of God's providential management of the situation.

37:26-28

Calvin on Judah's proposal: "The cruelty of the brothers was extreme, in that they could sell their own flesh and blood; yet, in comparison with the preceding design of murder, they seemed to be humane... God, however, drew good out of this evil: for he not only preserved the life of Joseph, but opened up a way for the accomplishment of the dreams which they had tried to frustrate."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
37:25

The NET note on "Ishmaelites" and "Midianites" being used interchangeably acknowledges the tension and surveys three main explanations: (1) different sources (J and E) combined by a redactor; (2) the terms are used interchangeably for related tribes (supported by Judges 8:22-24); (3) the Midianites sold Joseph to the Ishmaelites who then sold him to Potiphar. The note acknowledges the difficulty and presents all options without forcing a conclusion.

37:28

The NET note on the sale price of twenty shekels notes this is the standard value for a young male slave in ancient Near Eastern law (Lev 27:5 sets the same price for a boy 5-20 years old). The note observes the ironic precision: the covenant heir sold at market price. The same note later connects this to Matthew 26:15, where Judas accepts thirty pieces of silver for Jesus — a slave price for the one who came to serve.

Verses 29–36 — Reuben's Distress; Jacob's Grief
29When Reuben returned to the cistern and saw that Joseph was not there, he tore his clothes.30He went back to his brothers and said, "The boy isn't there! Where can I turn now?"31Then they got Joseph's robe, slaughtered a goat and dipped the robe in the blood.32They took the ornate robe back to their father and said, "We found this. Examine it to see whether it is your son's robe."33He recognized it and said, "It is my son's robe! Some ferocious animal has devoured him. Joseph has surely been torn to pieces."34Then Jacob tore his clothes, put on sackcloth and mourned for his son many days.35All his sons and daughters came to comfort him, but he refused to be comforted. "No," he said, "I will continue to mourn until I join my son in the grave." So his father wept for him.36Meanwhile, the Midianites sold Joseph in Egypt to Potiphar, one of Pharaoh's officials, the captain of the guard.

Hebrew Word Study

בֹּר bōr — pit / cistern: The same word used in Psalms for Sheol/the grave (Ps 28:1; 30:3). Joseph's descent into the pit is a death-and-resurrection pattern: he is removed from the land of the living, then raised up to rule. The motif recurs in the prison (another pit, matṣōr).

Historical Context

The use of Joseph's coat dipped in goat blood is a precise reversal: Jacob used a goat skin to deceive Isaac (27:16); his sons use a goat's blood to deceive Jacob. The deceiver is deceived by the same animal and the same device. The narrative's poetic justice is exact. Jacob's grief — "I will go to my grave mourning my son" — is the longest expression of grief in the patriarchal narratives, and it goes unrelieved. The consolation offered by his children (v.35) only increases his pain.

Context

The pit scene is layered with irony: the dreamer is thrown into an empty pit — the same Hebrew word (bōr) used for the grave. The brothers sit down to eat while Joseph is in the pit (v.25) — an echo of Esau's selling his birthright for food. MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
37:29-30

Reuben's return to the cistern and his discovery that Joseph is gone is a study in private grief. He tears his garments — the ancient sign of extreme distress — and returns to his brothers with the cry "where can I turn now?" The question is not rhetorical; it expresses genuine moral anguish. Reuben failed to protect Joseph (he was absent at the crucial moment) and now bears the weight of that failure.

37:31-33

The brothers' deception of Jacob — presenting the blood-dipped coat and asking Jacob to "examine it" — is constructed to produce the father's own false conclusion. They do not say "Joseph is dead"; they make Jacob say it. "It is my son's robe! Some ferocious animal has devoured him. Joseph has surely been torn to pieces." The mechanics of the deception echo the original deception of Isaac: the father made to draw his own wrong conclusion from false evidence.

37:35

Jacob's refusal to be consoled — "I will continue to mourn until I join my son in the grave" — is the family's punishment for the brothers' lie. Jacob will live in unrelieved grief for approximately twenty years until the Egyptian reunion. The grief is real; the consolation offered by the very people who caused it is impossible to receive. The brothers will carry the weight of their father's grief as long as the lie stands.

37:36

The final verse pivots sharply: "Meanwhile, the Midianites sold Joseph in Egypt to Potiphar." The narrative has been following Jacob's grief; now it turns to Egypt. Joseph is not dead. He has been sold to a high official of Pharaoh, captain of the guard. The chapter that began with dreams of sheaves bowing ends with the dreamer in Egypt — in the exact position from which the dreams will be fulfilled.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:31-33

The brothers' use of the goat and the coat is a precise echo of Jacob's use of goat skins in 27:16. Sarna: "The instrument of Jacob's deception of his father becomes the instrument of his sons' deception of him. The same animal, the same family dynamic — the younger deceiving the older — the same devastating emotional consequence. The narrative's poetic justice is not accidental; it is structural." The sons do to Jacob exactly what Jacob did to Isaac.

37:36

The final verse — Potiphar, "a high official of Pharaoh and captain of the guard" — is both ending and beginning. Sarna notes the Egyptian setting is established with precise vocabulary. Potiphar's title (sar hatabbachim = chief of the slaughterers/guards) is the Hebrew rendering of an authentic Egyptian title. The chapter closes on the slave market of Egypt; the next chapter (38) will interrupt the Joseph story with the Judah-Tamar episode before returning to Egypt in chapter 39.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
37:34-35

Jacob's mourning is expressed with four verbs: tore his garments, put on sackcloth, mourned, refused to be comforted. Alter notes the escalation: each verb is a deeper expression of grief. The refusal to be comforted by his sons and daughters — "all his sons and daughters came to comfort him" — carries an extra layer of irony. The very people who caused his grief are trying to console him for it. The consolation offered by the guilty to the deceived is necessarily futile.

37:36

The chapter ends on a cold commercial note: "The Midianites had sold him in Egypt." Alter: "The grammar and diction strip the transaction of all sentiment. The dreamer of royal destiny is a commodity. The chapter's movement — from the coat of favour to the slave market — is the narrative arc of the entire Joseph cycle in miniature. Everything that follows is the reversal of this ending."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
37:34-35

Calvin on Jacob's inconsolable grief: "Jacob had been, from his youth, exercised in manifold troubles; yet here he is so overcome with grief, that he is almost absorbed by it... But we must observe, that he is not so deeply affected by his loss as to forget himself entirely, or to give loose reins to his grief; for he acknowledges that he is mortal, and confesses that he shall follow his son." Even in extreme grief Calvin notes Jacob's theological orientation: he knows he will die; he frames his grief within his own mortality.

37:36

Calvin reads the final verse as the beginning of Providence's work: "Here we see how Providence provides for the safety of Joseph. Although the holy man was thrown into extreme misery, God was the director of the whole business, and turned everything to a useful end. For the same Potiphar who had the power of life and death over Joseph, was providentially assigned to be his master."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
37:33

The NET note on "Joseph has surely been torn to pieces" (tarof toraf Yosef) observes this is the emphatic Hebrew construction (infinitive absolute + finite verb), expressing certainty. Jacob draws the intended conclusion with full grammatical weight. The note draws the parallel to Isaac's drawn conclusion in ch.27 — in both cases, a father is presented with fabricated evidence and draws the conclusion his deceivers intend.

37:36

The NET note on Potiphar's title (sar hatabbachim) explains that tabbach literally means "slaughterer" (one who kills animals for food), and came to mean "guard/executioner" by extension. The title corresponds to an Egyptian title documented in extra-biblical sources. The note observes this is the first authentic Egyptian administrative term in the Joseph narrative — a marker that the Egyptian setting is drawn from genuine historical knowledge.

Ancient Sources

Egyptian Slave Records
Egyptian administrative papyri record Semitic slaves (ʿāpiru/Habiru) bought and sold in Egypt from c. 1800 BC. The price of twenty shekels matches Middle Bronze Age slave prices documented in Mesopotamian tablets.
The economic details of the Joseph sale are archaeologically consistent with the period.
Potiphar's Title
The title "captain of the guard" (śar haṭṭabbāḥîm, lit. "chief of the slaughterers/executioners") appears in Egyptian records as a high court official. The title marks Potiphar as a significant figure in Pharaoh's household.
The specificity of Joseph's owner's title is consistent with authentic Egyptian administrative terminology.

Reception History

Augustine, City of God XVI
Joseph is the clearest type of Christ in the OT: the beloved son, betrayed by his own brothers for silver, apparently dead, raised to rule and save. Augustine develops the typology extensively.
Thomas Mann, Joseph and His Brothers (1943)
Mann's four-volume novel is the great literary reception of the Joseph narrative — an exploration of myth, psychology, and the way archetypal patterns repeat through history.

Literary Structure

Jacob loves Joseph most → coat → brothers see and hate. Family system that generates the crisis established in four verses.
Two Dreams — 37:5-11Dream 1: sheaves. Dream 2: sun/moon/stars. Brothers' jealousy and Jacob's pondering are opposite responses to the same information.
The Pit — 37:12-24Brothers plot. Reuben deflects to pit. Joseph arrives wearing the coat — stripped of it immediately.
The Sale — 37:25-28Judah's commercial proposal. Caravan. Twenty shekels. The dreamer sold as a slave.
The Lie — 37:29-35Goat blood on coat. Jacob's conclusion: ferocious animal. Inconsolable grief. Sons console the father they deceived.
Egypt — 37:36Single-verse pivot: Midianites sold him to Potiphar. The chapter that opened with dreams ends with a slave market.
Genesis 37 opens the Joseph novella — the longest and most literary of the Genesis narratives. The chapter is built around the coat: given (v.3), seen and resented (v.4), arriving at Dothan (v.23), stripped (v.23), dipped in blood (v.31), shown to Jacob (v.33). The coat is the material sign of the family's disorder and will drive the deception that sends the family into twenty years of grief. Against this human catastrophe the two dreams stand as God's announcement of his own purpose — a purpose the brothers will spend the next twenty years trying to prevent.

Hebrew-Rooted Reading

37:3 כְּתֹנֶת פַּסִּים ketonet passim — the coat (passim = long-sleeved or ornamented; same phrase 2 Sam 13:18 for royal daughter's garment; marker of heir-status)

37:28 עֶשְׂרִים כָּסֶף esrim kasef — twenty pieces of silver (standard slave price for a young male, Lev 27:5; cf. thirty pieces of silver for Jesus, Matt 26:15)

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 37 is the beginning of the Bible's greatest story of reversal. The dreamer sold into slavery becomes the ruler of Egypt; the brothers who bow to make the dreams impossible become the brothers who bow to fulfil them. The theological engine of the Joseph narrative is stated here in the dreams: God has announced an outcome; every human effort to prevent it becomes its instrument. The coat that caused the hatred is replaced by Egyptian robes; the pit is replaced by a palace.
