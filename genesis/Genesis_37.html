Genesis 37

Joseph's Dreams; Sold into Egypt

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline
Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration.

What prompted it

A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–11 — Joseph's Dreams; His Brothers' Hatred
1Jacob lived in the land where his father had stayed, the land of Canaan.2This is the account of Jacob's family line. Joseph, a young man of seventeen, was tending the flocks with his brothers, the sons of Bilhah and the sons of Zilpah, his father's wives, and he brought their father a bad report about them.3Now Israel loved Joseph more than any of his other sons, because he had been born to him in his old age; and he made an ornate robe for him.4When his brothers saw that their father loved him more than any of them, they hated him and could not speak a kind word to him.5Joseph had a dream, and when he told it to his brothers, they hated him all the more.6He said to them, "Listen to this dream I had:7We were binding sheaves of grain out in the field when suddenly my sheaf rose and stood upright, while your sheaves gathered around mine and bowed down to it."8His brothers said to him, "Do you intend to reign over us? Will you actually rule us?" And they hated him all the more because of his dream and what he had said.9Then he had another dream, and he told it to his brothers. "Listen," he said, "I had another dream, and this time the sun and moon and eleven stars were bowing down to me."10When he told his father as well as his brothers, his father rebuked him and said, "What is this dream you had? Will your mother and I and your brothers actually come and bow down to the ground before you?"11His brothers were jealous of him, but his father kept the matter in mind.

Hebrew Word Study

כְּתֹנֶת פַּסִּים ketōnet passîm — robe of many colors / long robe: The exact meaning of passîm is debated — it may mean "long-sleeved" or "ornamented." The same phrase appears only in 2 Sam 13:18 for the garment of royal daughters. This is a garment marking status and favoritism — and it will become the instrument of Joseph's apparent death (v.31).

חָלַם ḥālam — dream: Dreams are the primary vehicle of divine revelation in the Joseph story. The same root is used for Pharaoh's dreams (ch.41) — God speaks through Joseph and through Pharaoh using the same medium.

קָנָא qānāʾ — jealous / zealous: The brothers' jealousy (qin'āh) is the same root as God's own jealousy for his covenant people (Exod 20:5). Misdirected passion for honor drives them to sell their own brother.

Historical Context

Elaborate multicolored garments are documented in Egyptian tomb paintings (e.g., Beni Hasan, c. 1900 BC) showing Semitic traders wearing decorated robes. Dreams as divine communication were taken seriously in the ancient Near East — the Mesopotamian Dream Book catalogued dream types and their meanings. Joseph's dreams follow the pattern of double-confirmation (two dreams = certainty) that appears in Egyptian and Mesopotamian literature.

Context

The Joseph narrative opens with the same family dynamics that structured the Jacob narrative: parental favoritism (Isaac preferred Esau; Jacob prefers Joseph), sibling rivalry, and the younger son's elevation. But where Jacob stole his position through deception, Joseph's elevation comes through divine dreams and providential suffering. The two dreams (sheaves bowing, heavenly bodies bowing) frame the entire narrative — they will not be fulfilled until 42:6 and 43:26 when the brothers bow before Joseph in Egypt.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 42:6; 43:26The dream fulfillments: brothers bow before Joseph the governor.
  • Psalm 105:17–19The Psalm retrospectively identifies Joseph's suffering as God's testing: "He had sent a man before them — Joseph, sold as a slave... until what he foretold came to pass."
  • Acts 7:9–10Stephen's sermon: "The patriarchs, jealous of Joseph, sold him into Egypt. But God was with him."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
37:3–4

The robe of many colors is the visible symbol of favoritism — Jacob repeats his own parents' mistake. Isaac preferred Esau; Jacob prefers Joseph. In both cases, the preferential treatment produces dangerous family fracture. Jacob has learned many things through his years of trial, but he has not learned to avoid the partiality that poisoned his own childhood. The consequences fall on the next generation.

37:5–11

The two dreams are not merely predictive — they are prophetic commissions. God is telling Joseph what he will do with him before Joseph or anyone else understands it. Jacob "kept the saying in mind" (v.11) — he does not understand it, but he does not dismiss it. The dreams will seem most unlikely at the moment Joseph is at the bottom of the pit (v.24) and most forgotten during his years in prison. But God does not forget his own revelations.

Verses 12–28 — The Plot; Joseph Sold into Slavery
12Now his brothers had gone to graze their father's flocks near Shechem,13and Israel said to Joseph, "As you know, your brothers are grazing the flocks near Shechem. Come, I am going to send you to them." "Very well," he replied.14So he said to him, "Go and see if all is well with your brothers and with the flocks, and bring word back to me." Then he sent him off from the Valley of Hebron. When Joseph arrived at Shechem,15a man found him wandering around in the fields and asked him, "What are you looking for?"16He replied, "I'm looking for my brothers. Can you tell me where they are grazing their flocks?"17"They have moved on from here," the man answered. "I heard them say, 'Let's go to Dothan.'" So Joseph went after his brothers and found them near Dothan.18But they saw him in the distance, and before he reached them, they plotted to kill him.19"Here comes that dreamer!" they said to each other.20"Come now, let's kill him and throw him into one of these cisterns and say that a ferocious animal devoured him. Then we'll see what comes of his dreams."21When Reuben heard this, he tried to rescue him from their hands. "Let's not take his life," he said.22"Don't shed any blood. Throw him into this cistern here in the wilderness, but don't lay a hand on him." Reuben said this to rescue him from them and take him back to his father.23So when Joseph came to his brothers, they stripped him of his robe—the ornate robe he was wearing—24and they took him and threw him into the cistern. The cistern was empty; there was no water in it.25As they sat down to eat their meal, they looked up and saw a caravan of Ishmaelites coming from Gilead. Their camels were loaded with spices, balm and myrrh, and they were on their way to take them down to Egypt.26Judah said to his brothers, "What will we gain if we kill our brother and cover up his blood?27Come, let's sell him to the Ishmaelites and not lay our hands on him; after all, he is our brother, our own flesh and blood." His brothers agreed.28So when the Midianite merchants came by, his brothers pulled Joseph up out of the cistern and sold him for twenty shekels of silver to the Ishmaelites, who took him to Egypt.

Hebrew Word Study

בֹּר bōr — pit / cistern: The same word used in Psalms for Sheol/the grave (Ps 28:1; 30:3). Joseph's descent into the pit is a death-and-resurrection pattern: he is removed from the land of the living, then raised up to rule. The motif recurs in the prison (another pit, matṣōr).

עֶשְׂרִים כֶּסֶף ʿesrîm kesep — twenty pieces of silver: The price of a slave aged 5–20 according to Leviticus 27:5. Joseph was seventeen — the brothers received the legal minimum for a young male slave. This price is later echoed in Zechariah's thirty pieces (Zech 11:12–13) and Judas's betrayal.

Context

The pit scene is layered with irony: the dreamer is thrown into an empty pit — the same Hebrew word (bōr) used for the grave. The brothers sit down to eat while Joseph is in the pit (v.25) — an echo of Esau's selling his birthright for food. Reuben's plan to rescue Joseph (v.21) is frustrated by his absence (v.29) — he bears the guilt of failing to act decisively. Judah's compromise (sell him rather than kill him, v.26) is presented as the "better" option, but it still amounts to betrayal. No brother emerges with moral credit.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 50:20Joseph's definitive theological interpretation: "You intended to harm me, but God intended it for good."
  • Psalm 105:17God "sent a man before them — Joseph, sold as a slave." The Psalm reads the sale as divine sending.
  • Matt 27:3–10Judas's thirty pieces of silver echo Joseph's twenty — both are betrayals of the innocent for money.
  • Acts 7:9Stephen cites the brothers' jealousy as the mechanism of God's providential plan for Israel's salvation.

Places & Geography

Dothan
Northern Canaan highlands
Where the brothers were grazing flocks when Joseph arrived (v.17). Located near major trade routes connecting Canaan to Egypt — which is why Ishmaelite traders passed by.
Egypt
Northeastern Africa
The destination of Joseph's slavery and the setting for the next thirteen years of his life. Egypt (Mizraim) becomes the crucible of providence.

Timeline

c. 1898 BC — Joseph sold into Egypt — traditional date in the patriarchal chronology. He is seventeen years old (v.2).

c. 1885 BC — Joseph will stand before Pharaoh at age thirty (41:46) — thirteen years of slavery and imprisonment.

c. 1876 BC — Jacob's family descends to Egypt (46:6) — approximately twenty-two years after Joseph's sale.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
37:20

The brothers' taunt — "we will see what will become of his dreams" — is the sharpest irony in the Joseph narrative. They intend it mockingly; God intends it literally. The dreams will come true precisely because of what the brothers do to try to prevent them. Every action they take to bury the dreams advances the dreams' fulfillment. This is providence operating through human opposition.

37:28

The sale of Joseph for twenty pieces of silver is a betrayal — and it is also the beginning of Israel's salvation. Joseph must go to Egypt so that Israel can be saved from famine. He must be sold so that he can be raised to rule. The price paid for his betrayal is the exact price God uses to position his savior. The brothers mean it for evil; this phrase — which will appear verbatim on Joseph's lips in 50:20 — is the theological summary of the entire Joseph narrative.

Verses 29–36 — Reuben's Distress; Jacob's Grief

"No," he said, "I will continue to mourn until I join my son in the grave." Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Joseph
Jacob's eleventh son, Rachel's firstborn
Seventeen, favored, a dreamer. His story dominates the final fourteen chapters of Genesis.
Judah
Jacob's fourth son by Leah
Proposes selling rather than killing Joseph (v.26) — a morally compromised "mercy." His story will develop significantly in ch.38 and ch.44.
Reuben
Jacob's firstborn by Leah
Plans to rescue Joseph but is absent at the critical moment (v.29). His failure compounds his earlier sin (35:22).

Ancient Sources

Egyptian Slave Records
Egyptian administrative papyri record Semitic slaves (ʿāpiru/Habiru) bought and sold in Egypt from c. 1800 BC. The price of twenty shekels matches Middle Bronze Age slave prices documented in Mesopotamian tablets.
The economic details of the Joseph sale are archaeologically consistent with the period.
Potiphar's Title
The title "captain of the guard" (śar haṭṭabbāḥîm, lit. "chief of the slaughterers/executioners") appears in Egyptian records as a high court official. The title marks Potiphar as a significant figure in Pharaoh's household.
The specificity of Joseph's owner's title is consistent with authentic Egyptian administrative terminology.

Reception History

Augustine, City of God XVI
Joseph is the clearest type of Christ in the OT: the beloved son, betrayed by his own brothers for silver, apparently dead, raised to rule and save. Augustine develops the typology extensively.
Thomas Mann, Joseph and His Brothers (1943)
Mann's four-volume novel is the great literary reception of the Joseph narrative — an exploration of myth, psychology, and the way archetypal patterns repeat through history.

Intertextual Threading

37:7,9→42:6; 43:26Fulfillment
Joseph's two dreams — brothers bowing, heavenly bodies bowing — are fulfilled when the brothers bow before the governor of Egypt (42:6) and again when all eleven brothers bow (43:26).
37:28→Matt 26:15Echo
Twenty pieces of silver for Joseph; thirty pieces for Jesus. Both are beloved sons betrayed by their own people for money. Both betrayals become the mechanism of salvation.

"We will see what will become of his dreams" / "You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good." The brothers' taunt becomes Joseph's doxology.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 37 launches the Joseph narrative with its central theological question: how does God's sovereign purpose work through human evil? The answer unfolds over fourteen chapters, but the question is fully stated here: the dreamer is in the pit, the dreams seem impossible, and God has not yet spoken.
