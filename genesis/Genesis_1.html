Genesis 1

The Creation of the Heavens and the Earth

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–5 — The First Day: Light
1In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth.2Now the earth was formless and empty, darkness was over the surface of the deep, and the Spirit of God was hovering over the waters.3And God said, "Let there be light," and there was light.4God saw that the light was good, and he separated the light from the darkness.5God called the light "day," and the darkness he called "night." And there was evening, and there was morning—the first day.

Hebrew Word Study

בְּרֵאשִׁית bĕrēʾšît — In the beginning: The opening word of Scripture — not "from the beginning" (implying a pre-existing time) but "in the beginning," marking the absolute start of time itself. The LXX renders it ἐν ἀρχῇ (en archē), echoed in John 1:1.

בָּרָא bārāʾ — created: Used exclusively with God as subject — never humans. Implies bringing into existence something genuinely new. While it does not always mean ex nihilo in every OT use, combined with v.1's absolute framing, the text affirms creation from nothing.

אֱלֹהִים ʾĕlōhîm — God: Plural form with singular verb — a grammatical tension the NT resolves Trinitarianly. In ANE context it is an "intensive plural" conveying fullness of divine majesty. The chapter names God 35 times.

תֹהוּ וָבֹהוּ tōhû wāḇōhû — formless and empty: A rhyming pair unique to this verse (cf. Jer 4:23). Not a pre-existent chaos God must overcome (contra Babylonian Enuma Elish) but the unformed, unfilled state of the just-created earth — the canvas before the Artist begins.

מְרַחֶפֶת mĕraḥepet — hovering: The piel participle of rāḥap — used elsewhere only of an eagle hovering over its young (Deut 32:11). The Spirit's presence over the waters is protective, maternal, preparatory. Creation is a Trinitarian act.

Historical Context

The Babylonian Enuma Elish (c. 1100 BCE in written form, possibly older orally) opens with primeval waters — Apsu (fresh water) and Tiamat (salt water) — whose mingling produces the gods. Creation emerges from divine conflict: Marduk slays Tiamat and forms the heavens and earth from her body. Genesis 1 is a deliberate counter-narrative: no divine conflict, no theogony, no chaos-monster. God speaks and it is done. The Sumerian King List opens with "When kingship descended from heaven" — Genesis opens earlier: before kingship, before humanity, before the earth itself, God creates. The Egyptian Memphite Theology (c. 700 BCE) has Ptah creating by divine speech — the closest ANE parallel to the "God said" formula of Genesis 1.

Context

Verse 1 functions as a theological prologue, not merely the first step of a sequence. "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth" is the thesis statement of all Scripture — before anything else existed, God acted. Verse 2 then describes the earth's initial state before the six days of forming and filling begin. The structure of days 1–6 follows a pattern of formation (days 1–3) and filling (days 4–6): day 1 creates light / day 4 creates light-bearers; day 2 separates waters / day 5 fills the waters and sky; day 3 creates land and plants / day 6 fills the land with animals and humans.

Cross-Reference

  • John 1:1–3In the beginning was the Word — John's opening deliberately echoes Genesis 1:1, identifying Jesus as the agent of creation. "All things were made through him."
  • Col 1:16All things created through Christ and for Christ — the Trinitarian nature of creation made explicit.
  • Heb 11:3By faith we understand that the universe was formed at God's command, so that what is seen was not made out of what was visible — the clearest NT statement of creation ex nihilo.
  • Ps 33:6,9By the word of the LORD the heavens were made — the creation-by-speech motif confirmed in the Psalms.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

This verse is the foundational statement of all biblical revelation. It affirms that the universe had a definite beginning — contra the ancient view of an eternal universe and contra modern steady-state cosmology. "God" (Elohim) is a plural noun with a singular verb, hinting at the plurality within the Godhead that the NT will fully reveal as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. The verb "created" (bara) describes an act that belongs to God alone — no human is ever the subject of this verb. The phrase "heavens and earth" is a merism (a figure of speech using two opposites to mean the whole), encompassing the totality of the universe. This verse rules out atheism (there is a God), polytheism (there is one God), pantheism (God is distinct from his creation), materialism (matter is not eternal), and dualism (God alone is the ultimate reality). The universe did not create itself, did not emerge from pre-existing matter, and was not made by lesser divine beings — God alone, from nothing, in the beginning, created everything.

1:2

The earth's initial state — "formless and empty" (tohu wabohu) — describes not a chaotic disorder but an unformed, unfilled condition awaiting God's creative work. Some have proposed a "gap theory" inserting a long period and a Satanic fall between verses 1 and 2, but the Hebrew construction offers no grammatical support for this. The verse simply describes the starting condition of the earth God has just created. "Darkness over the surface of the deep" is not a threatening chaos-power (contra Babylonian myth) but the simple absence of light before God speaks it into existence. The "hovering" of the Spirit (merachephet) is the same word used of an eagle hovering over her nest — the Spirit of God was present, active, and about to begin his transformative work over the yet-unformed creation.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:1

The Hebrew bereshit does not require a definite article — both the absolute reading ("In the beginning God created") and the temporal clause reading ("When God began to create") are defensible. The critical point: the formula establishes temporal priority. Whatever follows is not eternal. The Babylonian cosmogonies lacked this; their gods emerged from pre-existing primordial matter. Genesis makes a claim no surrounding culture made: absolute divine priority over all that exists.

1:2

Tohu wabohu is a rhyming pair unique to Hebrew, conveying utter formlessness — not chaos as an opposing power but an unordered condition awaiting divine speech. The ruach Elohim hovering (merachefet) uses a verb found elsewhere only of an eagle tending its nest — a nurturing, life-generating presence. The darkness and deep (tehom) are not personified adversaries as in Mesopotamian myth; they are simply the unformed material of creation.

1:3-5

The divine speech formula "and God said" appears ten times in the chapter, structuring creation as a series of royal decrees. God names the light "day" and the dark "night" — in the ancient world, naming constituted ownership and function. The formula "evening and morning" reflects the ancient reckoning of a day beginning at sunset, still preserved in Jewish practice. Three features distinguish Israelite cosmogony: God acts alone, creates by word, and declares the result good.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:1-2

The opening of Genesis is among the most discussed sentences in world literature. The syntactic ambiguity is real and productive. The text begins in medias res — darkness, deep, formless void already present. The narrative does not explain these; it begins with God acting on them. The economy of the prose is itself a theological statement: no theogony, no genealogy of the gods, no conflict narrative. Creation is effortless. The only instrument is language.

1:3

The first words spoken in the Bible are creative speech — a profound contrast with Mesopotamian creation myths where creation involves divine combat and physical effort. The entire cosmos is a speech act. This has implications the text does not elaborate: language is the medium of creation, and humanity — the image-bearer — is the only creature that uses language as God does.

1:4-5

The naming of light and dark is an act of royal possession and ordering. God names what he creates; humanity will be commissioned to name the animals (2:19-20). The rhythm of "evening and morning" has a liturgical quality — the chapter is structured as a poem of ordered time, each day a verse, each refrain a confirmation of the poem's rightness.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:1

Moses begins with the creation of the world, not with the internal nature of God — a mercy to human weakness. We could not comprehend the infinite essence of God; he condescends to us by showing us first what he has made. The phrase "in the beginning" sets creation within time, and time itself is a creature. Augustine was right: the question "what was God doing before creation" is unanswerable — there was no "before." Let us not inquire beyond what God has revealed.

1:2

The Spirit of God hovering over the waters shows that the world could not subsist for a moment without divine energy. God did not merely create and depart; he sustains what he has made by the same Spirit that moved over the original formlessness. Calvin resists the Platonic reading that matter is eternal: the formless void was itself created, though Moses does not describe its creation.

1:3-5

Let there be light — and there was light. The simplicity of divine command and its immediate execution shows that God needs no instrument, no process, no effort. Light exists before the sun, created on day four. This teaches us that the sun is not the source of light but merely its vehicle: God can produce and sustain light without any creaturely means.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:1

The NET renders this as an independent clause: "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth," reflecting the majority view that v.1 is a complete sentence asserting absolute creation. The note acknowledges the minority reading (Rashi, Speiser) where the Hebrew construct chain makes v.1 a temporal clause ("When God began to create..."), but argues the absence of the definite article on reshit does not require a construct reading. The NET opts for the theologically significant absolute reading while transparently flagging the ambiguity.

1:2

The NET translates ruach elohim as "the Spirit of God" (not "a wind from God"), citing the consistent use of ruach as the divine Spirit in contexts where God is acting creatively or redemptively. The note documents the alternative rendering ("a mighty wind") favoured by some source-critical scholars, but argues the personal-divine reading fits both the immediate context and the broader biblical use of ruach in creation contexts (Ps 104:30; Job 33:4). The participle merachefet ("hovering/brooding") is noted as a Piel form appearing elsewhere only in Deut 32:11.

1:3-5

The NET notes that "And God said" (wayyomer elohim) introduces each creative act as a divine speech event — a deliberate rhetorical pattern. The verb bara in v.1 is distinguished from asah (made) used elsewhere in the chapter: bara appears exclusively with God as subject and may carry the nuance of effortless, sovereign origination. The note on "good" (tov) observes that in ANE contexts the word connotes functional suitability — what is good is what works as intended — not merely aesthetic approval, though both senses are present.

Verses 6–13 — Days Two and Three: Sky, Sea, and Land
6And God said, "Let there be a vault between the waters to separate water from water."7So God made the vault and separated the water under the vault from the water above it. And it was so.8God called the vault "sky." And there was evening, and there was morning—the second day.9And God said, "Let the water under the sky be gathered to one place, and let dry ground appear." And it was so.10God called the dry ground "land," and the gathered waters he called "seas." And God saw that it was good.11Then God said, "Let the land produce vegetation: seed-bearing plants and trees on the land that bear fruit with seed in it, according to their various kinds." And it was so.12The land produced vegetation: plants bearing seed according to their kinds and trees bearing fruit with seed in it according to their kinds. And God saw that it was good.13And there was evening, and there was morning—the third day.

Hebrew Word Study

וַיֹּאמֶר אֱלֹהִים wayyōmer ʾĕlōhîm — And God said: The formula occurs ten times in the chapter — creation by divine speech. The spoken word of God is immediately effective: "he spoke, and it came to be" (Ps 33:9). This establishes language and communication as fundamental to the nature of God and reality.

יְהִי אוֹר yĕhî ʾôr — Let there be light: The simplest and most majestic creative utterance in literature. Light is created on day 1; its sources (sun, moon) on day 4. Light is therefore not identical to sunlight — it may refer to primordial electromagnetic energy, or more broadly to the distinction of ordered existence from formless void.

כִּי טוֹב kî ṭôḇ — that it was good: The refrain appears seven times (days 1, 2 lacking it, then 3, 4, 5, 6×2). "Good" (ṭôḇ) means fit, functional, beautiful, and morally right — the creation perfectly accomplishes God's intention. The sevenfold repetition signals completeness and Sabbath-anticipation.

לְמִינֵהוּ lĕmînêhû — according to its kind: The created-kinds principle — plants and animals reproduce after their kind (vv.11, 12, 21, 24, 25). This does not require biological species fixity but affirms that God created discrete reproductive categories. The boundaries are real even if the precise scope of "kind" is debated.

Historical Context

The "firmament" or "vault" (raqîaʿ, v.6) has been misread as a solid dome based on later Jewish cosmography, but the word means an expanse or spread-out space — the atmosphere. The Akkadian cognate (raqu) can mean to beat or spread thin, as a metalworker spreads metal, but the biblical usage consistently refers simply to the sky/atmosphere (cf. Ps 19:1; Ezek 1:22–26). The separation of waters above and below the firmament describes the hydrological cycle: atmospheric moisture above, seas below. Ancient scientific models should not be read into the text as binding cosmology — Genesis 1 describes what God made, not the physics of how he made it.

Context

Days 1–3 are days of forming (separation and naming): day 1 separates light from darkness; day 2 separates upper waters from lower; day 3 separates land from sea and produces vegetation. Days 4–6 will fill what was formed. The naming act in v.5 ("God called the light 'day'") is a sovereignty claim — in the ancient world, naming established authority over the named. God names the basic constituents of reality, asserting his lordship over time, space, and matter. The evening-morning sequence of each day (evening first) reflects the Hebrew reckoning of a day from sunset — the pattern observed in Sabbath and festival practice.

Cross-Reference

  • 2 Cor 4:6God who said "Let light shine out of darkness" has shone in our hearts — Paul reads the creation of light as the type of spiritual illumination in salvation.
  • John 8:12"I am the light of the world" — Jesus claims the role of the primordial light, the source of all true illumination.
  • Ps 104:2The LORD wraps himself in light as with a garment — the creation of light associated with the divine glory.
  • Rev 21:23The new Jerusalem has no need of sun or moon, for the glory of God gives it light — the primordial light of day 1 restored in the new creation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:3

"God said" — the creation of the universe by divine speech is the foundational act establishing that reality is personal and communicative at its core. God does not labor, struggle, or work through pre-existing materials. He speaks and it is immediately so. The NT identifies the spoken word with the eternal Word (Logos) of John 1 — creation itself is a Christological act. The light created here is real, physical light, but it precedes the sun and moon (created day 4). This is not a problem for chronological reading — God can sustain light independently of its primary current source.

1:5

The phrase "evening and morning, one day" is the Hebrew way of marking a complete calendar day. The use of a cardinal number with "day" (yom echad, one day) and the evening-morning formula is the strongest possible grammatical indicator of a literal 24-hour day. The same formula is used in every subsequent day. There is no grammatical or contextual signal in this chapter that "day" means anything other than a normal day. Attempts to read vast ages into these days require importing assumptions from outside the text. The Hebrew word yom combined with a numeral always elsewhere in the OT refers to a literal day.

1:6–8

The "expanse" or "firmament" (raqia) is the atmosphere — the space between the waters on the earth's surface and the waters in the clouds above. This is not a solid dome (a misreading based on post-biblical cosmography) but the breathable sky. Day 2 is the only day without the "it was good" refrain — possibly because the work of separation is not complete until the waters are also gathered on day 3.

1:11–13

Vegetation is created on day 3 — before the sun is named on day 4. This sequence confirms that the text does not present the sun as the ultimate source of life and growth but as one instrument among many that God uses. The "kinds" (min) established at creation represent the God-ordained boundaries of reproduction. Within kinds, enormous variation is possible — but the kinds themselves are not interchangeable or derivable from one another.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
1:6-8

The raqia (firmament/expanse) reflects ancient Israelite cosmology in which a solid dome separated waters above from those below. This is descriptive phenomenology — how creation appears — not scientific cosmography. The point is the act of separation: God distinguishes, orders, names. The naming of "heavens" (shamayim) on day 2 parallels the naming of "day" and "night" on day 1. Order emerges through acts of distinction.

1:9-10

The gathering of the lower waters continues the separation pattern. The seas, which in Canaanite religion were the domain of the adversarial god Yam, are here simply part of God's ordered creation — named and bounded without resistance. The polemic against sea-deity religion is built into the structure of the text without a word of argumentation.

1:11-13

Vegetation is created on day 3 before the sun exists — a deliberate theological statement. Plants do not depend on the sun; they depend on God's word. This prevents the sun from being understood as the source of life. The threefold "according to its kind" establishes biological fixity — species reproduce consistently, a foundational observation for agricultural civilisation.

1:6-8

The word raqia — expanse, firmament — comes from the root meaning to beat flat. It suggests a beaten-metal canopy, the vault of the sky as ancient people experienced it. Genesis 1 does not correct the phenomenological cosmology of its audience; it works within it. The theological point is not cosmographic precision but the act of separation: God divides, distinguishes, names. Order emerges from formlessness through acts of distinction.

1:9-13

The gathering of the lower waters is described with terse elegance: "Let the water under the sky be gathered to one place, and let dry ground appear." The dry land simply emerges — no geological process, no gradual uplift. The narrative time of creation is instantaneous divine will. The detail "according to its kind" affirms the structured, reproducible order of the natural world.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
1:6-8

Moses here accommodates himself to common understanding. When he speaks of waters above the firmament, he speaks as an unlearned person speaks — describing what appears to the eye, not what the astronomer calculates. God did not give us a scientific treatise but a description suited to our capacity. The theological substance is the act of separation: God brings order from formlessness, and the order he brings is good.

1:9-13

The gathering of the waters shows God's sovereign government of the natural world. The sea does not overflow its bounds by accident but is held back by divine decree. The fertility of the land that immediately follows is a sign of divine provision: the earth is not a barren rock but a garden prepared for its inhabitants. God does not make a world and abandon it; he makes it habitable and provides for all he has made.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
1:6

The NET renders raqia as "expanse" (not "firmament" or "vault"), reflecting the translators' judgment that the term describes the visible sky rather than a solid dome. The note candidly acknowledges that ancient Israelite cosmology did envision a solid canopy separating upper and lower waters, and that "expanse" is a somewhat interpretive choice that modernises the cosmological picture. The translational decision is documented rather than hidden.

1:11

The threefold "according to its kind" (leminehu / leminah) is noted as a phrase that recurs throughout the creation account for plants (v.11-12), sea creatures (v.21), and land animals (v.24-25). The NET note observes that the phrase establishes the reproducible categories of creation without specifying the mechanism or extent of variation within kinds. It does not resolve the debate between different creationist positions but accurately notes what the text does and does not say.

1:12

The NET notes the shift from imperfect jussive ("let the land produce") in v.11 to perfect consecutive ("the land produced") in v.12 — the grammar marks the execution of the divine command. This pattern of command-fulfilment, repeated across each day, is a deliberate rhetorical structure the NET translators were careful to preserve in English where possible.

Verses 14–19 — Day Four: Sun, Moon, and Stars
14And God said, "Let there be lights in the vault of the sky to separate the day from the night, and let them serve as signs to mark sacred times, and days and years,15and let them be lights in the vault of the sky to give light on the earth." And it was so.16God made two great lights—the greater light to govern the day and the lesser light to govern the night. He also made the stars.17God set them in the vault of the sky to give light on the earth,18to govern the day and the night, and to separate light from darkness. And God saw that it was good.19And there was evening, and there was morning—the fourth day.

Hebrew Word Study

מְאֹרֹת mĕʾōrōt — lights / luminaries: The word is deliberately chosen over the more common ʾôr (light) — these are light-bearers, light-holders, not the light itself. The sun is "the greater luminary"; it is named neither sun (šemeš) nor moon (yārēaḥ) — both of which were names of Canaanite deities. The deliberate avoidance is a theological polemic: the objects of pagan worship are merely functional servants of the one God.

לְאֹתֹת lĕʾōṯōṯ — for signs: Signs (ʾôṯōṯ) — the same word used for covenant signs (rainbow, circumcision) and miraculous signs (plagues). The luminaries are sign-bearers for "sacred times" (môʿădîm — appointed seasons, including Sabbaths and feasts). The calendar itself is built into creation.

Historical Context

The sun god Shamash was the chief deity of Babylonian justice; the moon god Sin was worshipped throughout Mesopotamia; the Venus-star Ishtar was the goddess of love and war. Egyptian religion had Ra (sun) and Thoth (moon) among its highest deities. Genesis 1:16's deliberate refusal to name sun or moon — calling them simply "the greater light" and "the lesser light" — is a stunning theological polemic: the gods your neighbors worship are not gods at all. They are lamps God hung in the sky. "He also made the stars" — thrown in almost as an aside, the entire star-field that populated ancient religion with deities is dismissed in five Hebrew words.

Context

Day 4 fills what day 1 formed. Day 1 created light and separated it from darkness; day 4 creates the light-bearers to sustain that distinction. The luminaries have three functions: to separate day from night (v.14), to mark sacred times (môʿădîm — the same word for Israel's appointed feasts in Lev 23), and to give light on the earth (v.15). The calendar — Sabbaths, new moons, annual festivals — is therefore not a human invention but written into the structure of creation itself. Time is sacred because God made it so from the very beginning.

Cross-Reference

  • Ps 19:1–4The heavens declare the glory of God — the luminaries as testimony to the Creator, not themselves divine.
  • Deut 4:19Warning not to worship sun, moon, and stars — precisely the idolatry Genesis 1 preemptively counters by subordinating them to the one Creator.
  • Rev 21:23–24In the new creation there is no sun or moon — God's own glory replaces the temporary luminaries, completing the typology begun in v.3.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:14

The luminaries are created "for signs and for seasons, and for days and years." The word "seasons" (moedim) is the same Hebrew word used for Israel's appointed feasts (Lev 23:2, 4). The sacred calendar — Passover, Pentecost, Tabernacles, Sabbath — is therefore embedded in creation order itself. God did not later impose a calendar on an unstructured universe; he built temporal rhythm into the fabric of reality from the beginning. The luminaries also serve as signs — not for astrology (which the OT consistently condemns) but as markers of God's ordering of time for worship and human life.

1:16

The text calls the sun and moon "the greater light" and "the lesser light" — deliberately avoiding their proper Hebrew names (shemesh and yareach), both of which were names of pagan deities in Canaan and Mesopotamia. Genesis demythologizes the heavenly bodies: they are not gods, not divine beings, not worthy of worship. They are instruments God made and placed. "He also made the stars" — in five Hebrew words, the entire starfield that filled the ancient world with competing deities is reduced to a sub-clause. The theological statement is breathtaking in its brevity: the stars are God's handiwork, nothing more.

Verses 20–25 — Days Five and Six: Creatures of Sea, Air, and Land
20And God said, "Let the water teem with living creatures, and let birds fly above the earth across the vault of the sky."21So God created the great creatures of the sea and every living thing with which the water teems and that moves about in it, according to their kinds, and every winged bird according to its kind. And God saw that it was good.22God blessed them and said, "Be fruitful and increase in number and fill the water in the seas, and let the birds increase on the earth."23And there was evening, and there was morning—the fifth day.24And God said, "Let the land produce living creatures according to their kinds: the livestock, the creatures that move along the ground, and the wild animals, each according to its kind." And it was so.25God made the wild animals according to their kinds, the livestock according to their kinds, and all the creatures that move along the ground according to their kinds. And God saw that it was good.

Hebrew Word Study

תַּנִּינִם tannînîm — great sea creatures: The great tannînim — sea monsters. The same word used elsewhere for the dragon/serpent of chaos (Ps 74:13; Isa 51:9). In Canaanite and Babylonian religion, the sea monsters were primordial deities or chaos-powers (Lotan/Leviathan, Tiamat). Genesis 1:21 dismisses them in a subordinate clause: God created them. They are creatures, not gods. The demythologization is deliberate and complete.

נֶפֶשׁ חַיָּה nepeš ḥayyāh — living creature / living soul: The same phrase applied to both animals (here) and Adam (2:7). Both are "living souls" in the Hebrew sense — animated, breathing beings. This does not make humans and animals equivalent; the image of God differentiates humanity decisively. But it does mean animals have genuine creaturely life, not mere mechanical existence.

פְּרוּ וּרְבוּ pĕrû ûrĕḇû — be fruitful and multiply: The first divine blessing in Scripture — spoken to animals (v.22) before humans (v.28). Biological reproduction is a divine gift, not a concession to human weakness. Fruitfulness is built into creation as God's intention from the beginning.

Historical Context

The Enuma Elish depicts sea monsters (Tiamat and her brood) as primordial enemies that Marduk must defeat to create the world. Ugaritic texts feature Baal's battle with Yam (Sea) and Lotan (the twisting serpent). Genesis 1:21 responds to this entire tradition: tannînim are not chaos powers requiring divine combat. They are animals God made on a Tuesday. The ANE combat-creation myth is comprehensively denied — creation comes not from conflict but from sovereign speech.

Context

Days 5–6 fill what days 2–3 formed. Day 5 fills the waters and sky (formed day 2) with fish and birds. Day 6 fills the land (formed day 3) with animals and humans. The blessing of fruitfulness (v.22) is God's first spoken word to his creatures — before the fall, before commands, before restriction. The original posture of God toward his creation is blessing and fruitfulness. The created kinds repeat throughout vv.24–25 (three times in two verses) — the repetition is emphatic. Biological boundaries are part of God's good design.

  • Job 40:15–41:34The Behemoth and Leviathan speeches — God's answer from the whirlwind, reminding Job that the creatures he finds most terrifying are God's creatures. They are not chaos powers; they are exhibits in God's zoo.

Sexuality and reproduction are not concessions to human weakness but reflections of God's intentional design.

1:24–25

Verses 26–31 — The Sixth Day: Humanity in the Image of God
26Then God said, "Let us make mankind in our image, in our likeness, so that they may rule over the fish in the sea and the birds in the sky, over the livestock and all the wild animals, and over all the creatures that move along the ground."27So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them.28God blessed them and said to them, "Be fruitful and increase in number; fill the earth and subdue it. Rule over the fish in the sea and the birds in the sky and over every living creature that moves on the ground."29Then God said, "I give you every seed-bearing plant on the face of the whole earth and every tree that has fruit with seed in it. They will be yours for food.30And to all the beasts of the earth and all the birds in the sky and all the creatures that move along the ground—everything that has the breath of life in it—I give every green plant for food." And it was so.31God saw all that he had made, and it was very good. And there was evening, and there was morning—the sixth day.

Hebrew Word Study

נַעֲשֶׂה אָדָם naʿăśeh ʾādām — Let us make mankind: The plural "let us" has generated three major interpretations: (1) the Trinity (Christian reading — consistent with John 1:3 and Col 1:16); (2) God addressing his heavenly court/angels (but angels did not participate in creation); (3) a "royal we" or "plural of deliberation." The Trinitarian reading is the most coherent with the full canon. The deliberate shift to plural before the creation of humanity signals something unique about this act.

צֶלֶם ṣelem — image: The physical/representative sense — a ṣelem was a statue or representation erected in a territory to represent a king's presence and authority. Humanity is God's "statue" erected in creation, representing his rule. This grounds human dignity and the mandate for stewardship.

דְּמוּת dĕmût — likeness: The relational/qualitative sense — humanity resembles God in rationality, morality, creativity, relationality, and language. The two words together (image + likeness) represent the full spectrum of what it means to bear God's imprint.

זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה zāḵār ûnĕqēḇāh — male and female: The binary of human sexuality is created — not evolved, not culturally constructed, not chosen. Both sexes equally bear the image of God (v.27). The distinction is fundamental to human identity, to the dominion mandate, and to the marriage structure of Genesis 2.

Historical Context

The Egyptian concept of the king as the "image of god" (selem in Akkadian, twt in Egyptian) placed the image-of-god status in a single royal individual. Genesis 1:26–27 is a radical democratization: every human being — not just the pharaoh — is made in God's image. Every person has royal dignity. This would have been a staggering claim in the ancient world. The Atrahasis Epic (Babylonian flood story) creates humans from the blood of a slain rebel god to be laborers for the gods — no image, no dignity, just servants. Genesis creates humanity in God's image to be stewards, not slaves.

Context

The creation of humanity is the apex and goal of the six days. The shift from "let there be" to "let us make" marks a unique act. The dominion mandate (v.28) is not a license for exploitation but a royal commission for stewardship — humanity rules on behalf of the King. The image of God is the foundation of: human dignity (all people deserve respect regardless of utility), gender (male and female are equally image-bearers and equally differentiated), work (stewardship is a pre-fall vocation), and ethics (harming an image-bearer is an assault on the God whose image they bear, Gen 9:6).

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
1:26

"Let us make man" — the plural deliberation before the creation of humanity has no parallel in the creation of anything else. This is not a syntactical accident. The Christian reading — that the plural reflects the intra-Trinitarian counsel of Father, Son, and Spirit — is the most exegetically satisfying, especially in light of John 1:3 (all things made through the Word) and the Spirit's presence in 1:2. "In our image, in our likeness" — two Hebrew words (tselem, demut) that together describe the totality of humanity's God-resemblance. Tselem (image) carries the idea of representation — a king's statue in a province represented his authority there. Humanity is God's representative presence in creation. Demut (likeness) describes similarity — humans are like God in rationality, morality, will, creativity, language, and relational capacity. The image is not located in one human faculty but in humanity as a whole.

1:27

The threefold use of "created" (bara) in this verse — the only place in the chapter where bara is used three times in one verse — underlines the uniqueness and dignity of this act. The creation of humanity as "male and female" is part of the image of God statement: both sexes together image God in a way neither does alone. This is the biblical foundation for the complementarity of the sexes, for marriage as a man-woman union, and for the equal dignity of men and women before God. The binary of male and female is not a cultural construction but a creational given.

1:28

The "cultural mandate" or "dominion mandate" — the commission to be fruitful, fill the earth, subdue it, and rule over creatures — is the original charter of human existence. "Subdue" (kabash) means to bring under control, to harness for productive use — not to exploit or destroy. "Rule" (radah) means to govern as a regent, not as a tyrant. Humanity is God's vice-regent, exercising delegated authority over creation on behalf of the true King. This mandate grounds all legitimate human cultural activity: science, agriculture, city-building, art, and technology are all extensions of the original dominion commission.

1:31

"Very good" (tov meod) — the only occurrence of "very good" in the chapter. All previous days received "good"; only after the completion of humanity and the full creation does God declare it "very good." This is the creation in its intended state: no death, no suffering, no futility, no sin. The fall of Genesis 3 represents a catastrophic departure from this intended state — which is precisely why redemption aims at restoration. The new creation of Revelation 21–22 is not something fundamentally different from Genesis 1–2 but the same creation recovered, perfected, and made permanent.

The shift from imperative ("let there be") to deliberative ("let us make") is one of the most discussed moments in Genesis. Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

God (Elohim)
Creator and sovereign Lord
The plural name Elohim (used 35 times in ch.1) takes singular verbs throughout — a grammatical tension the OT does not resolve but the NT reads Trinitarianly (John 1:1-3; Col 1:16). God in ch.1 is radically transcendent: he does not struggle, tire, or negotiate with chaos. He speaks, and it is so. Creation is effortless sovereign act.
The Spirit of God
The hovering creative presence (v.2)
The ruach Elohim merachefet — "hovering" or "brooding" over the waters. The verb merachefet (Piel participle) occurs elsewhere only in Deut 32:11 of an eagle hovering over its young. The Spirit is present before the first word is spoken, as the energy of creative imminence. This verse becomes the NT's basis for linking the Spirit to new creation (Luke 1:35; John 3:5-6).
Humanity (Adam / Imago Dei)
The crown of creation; image-bearers (vv.26-28)
Created last, with the most elaborate divine deliberation ("let us make"), and bearing the unique designations tselem (image) and demut (likeness). They are the only creatures directly addressed by God (v.28) and commissioned to rule as vice-regents. The image is simultaneously structural (they are like God), functional (they rule as God's representatives), and relational (they are made for communion with God). The fall (ch.3) mars but does not destroy the image (Gen 9:6; James 3:9).

Ancient Sources

Enuma Elish (Babylon, c.12th c. BCE)
The Babylonian creation epic: Marduk defeats the sea-dragon Tiamat and creates the world from her corpse. The waters of chaos (Tiamat; cf. tehom, "the deep," Gen 1:2) must be conquered before creation can proceed.
Genesis 1 uses the same vocabulary (tehom, tohu wabohu) but radically inverts the theology: the deep is not a hostile deity but raw material under divine sovereignty. God does not battle; he speaks. The Israelite audience would have heard this as a polemic against Babylonian cosmogony.
Memphite Theology (Egypt, c.25th c. BCE)
The Egyptian god Ptah creates all things through the word of his tongue and the thought of his heart: "He made all things and words... the heart and tongue rule over all the limbs." Creation by divine speech.
This is the closest ANE parallel to Genesis 1's creation-by-word. The parallel shows that creation-by-speech was a recognisable theological category; Genesis 1 claims that it is Israel's God, not Ptah, whose word actually creates. The parallel strengthens rather than undermines the unique theology of Genesis 1.
Atrahasis Epic (Babylon, c.17th c. BCE)
Before creating humans, the gods deliberate: "Let us create a human being, that he bear the yoke, that he bear the yoke assigned by Enlil." Humanity is created as slave labour to relieve the gods of work.
The divine deliberation ("let us make," Gen 1:26) has a structural parallel in Atrahasis, but with a complete theological inversion: in Genesis, the deliberation creates humans not as slaves but as image-bearers and rulers. The same literary form carries the opposite theological content.
Philo of Alexandria, De Opificio Mundi (c.20 BCE)
Philo reads Genesis 1 as the creation of an ideal/intelligible world (the Logos/patterns) before the material world. The "six days" describe logical, not temporal, order — the sequence of an architect's plan rather than a builder's timetable.
Philo's Logos-reading of Genesis 1, deeply influenced by Platonism, was enormously formative for early Christian interpretation. John 1:1-3 is in direct dialogue with this tradition. Philo shows that the Logos-creation connection was already established in Hellenistic Jewish thought before the NT was written.

Reception History

Bereishit Rabbah (Rabbinic, c.5th c. CE)
The rabbis read Gen 1:1 as presupposing the Torah: "For the sake of the Torah God created the world" (Torah = "beginning" in Prov 8:22). Extensive debate followed on which day angels were created, whether darkness was created or pre-existent, and what the tohu wabohu describes. The Midrash treats Genesis 1 as inexhaustible — every word contains multiple legitimate meanings.
Augustine, De Genesi ad Litteram (c.401-415 CE)
Augustine argued that the "days" are not temporal sequences but simultaneous acts described as logical categories for human comprehension. God created everything instantaneously (citing Sir 18:1); the six days describe the order of angelic cognition of creation. Augustine is the foundational voice for non-literal readings of Genesis 1 within orthodox Christianity.
Calvin, Commentary on Genesis (1554)
Calvin introduced the "accommodation" principle: God describes creation in terms suited to ordinary human observation, not scientific precision. The "firmament" is not cosmological doctrine but a description visible to any observer. This hermeneutical tool became the dominant Protestant framework for relating Genesis to natural science.
John H. Walton, The Lost World of Genesis One (2009)
Walton argues that Genesis 1 is not an account of material origins but of functional origins — God assigning functions (not creating substances) in a cosmos already materially existing, understood in ancient Near Eastern terms as the inauguration of a cosmic temple. The "days" mark the seven-day temple dedication pattern. The most influential scholarly reading of Genesis 1 in the early 21st century.
Modern Creation Debate
Four main positions: (1) Young Earth Creationism — literal 24-hour days, c.6000 BC (Whitcomb, Morris, AiG). (2) Day-Age theory — days as geological epochs (Hugh Ross, RTB). (3) Framework Hypothesis — days as literary/topical framework, not sequence (Meredith Kline, Henri Blocher). (4) Functional Creation — days as cosmic temple inauguration, not material creation (Walton). All four have serious scholarly defenders; none has achieved consensus.

Literary Structure

Day 1 — vv.3-5Light / darkness separated — time begins; the framework for all that follows
Day 2 — vv.6-8Waters separated — sky above, sea below; vertical space created
Day 3 — vv.9-13Land separated from sea; vegetation — horizontal space and life-sustaining food
Day 4 — vv.14-19Sun, moon, stars FILL Day 1's light/dark — timekeepers for signs and seasons
Day 5 — vv.20-23Birds and fish FILL Day 2's sky and sea — the spaces are now inhabited
Day 6 — vv.24-31Animals and humanity FILL Day 3's land — rulers placed in the ruled space
Day 7 — ch.2:1-3Sabbath — God rests; the crown and goal; creation's culmination is rest, not work
The forming/filling structure (Kline, Blocher) is the key literary discovery: Days 1-3 create spaces; Days 4-6 fill them in exact correspondence. The chapter does not build to humanity but to the Sabbath — the seventh day is the only one without an "evening and morning" closing formula, suggesting it is still ongoing. The sevenfold repetition of "it was good" and the single "very good" at Day 6 create a liturgical rhythm — this is structured prose, not narrative accident.

"God who said 'Let light shine out of darkness' has shone in our hearts." Paul reads the new creation as the true light-event, the Gospel as the second "let there be light."
Gen 1:26→Col 1:15Fulfilment
"The image of the invisible God, the firstborn over all creation." Christ is the true and perfect image (tselem) that Adam was designed to be. The image marred in the fall is restored in the last Adam.
Gen 1:26-28→Gen 9:1-2Echo
"Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth" — the creation mandate of 1:28 is repeated verbatim after the flood. Noah is a new Adam; the flood is a new creation.
Gen 1:31→Rom 8:20-22Echo
"Very good" — creation at its zenith. Paul's groaning creation (Rom 8) presupposes the "very good" of Gen 1:31; the fall's corruption of creation and its eschatological redemption are both premised on creation's original goodness.
Gen 1:1→Rev 21:1Fulfilment
"A new heaven and a new earth, for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away." The new creation of Revelation completes and exceeds the first. The Bible's first verse and its penultimate vision form the creation bracket of all of Scripture.
Gen 1:2→Gen 8:1Echo
"God sent a wind (ruach) over the earth, and the waters receded." The ruach Elohim hovering over the waters in 1:2 reappears as the agent of the new-creation drying after the flood — an intentional literary echo linking both creation events.

Textual Notes

1:1Independent clause or temporal subordinate clause?
MT "In the beginning God created" (absolute clause — creation ex nihilo) vs JPS/Rashi "When God began to create" (temporal clause — implies pre-existing matter). The MT syntax is genuinely ambiguous; the LXX (ἐν ἀρχῇ) supports the absolute reading.
Theological weight is enormous: the absolute reading underpins creation ex nihilo, a doctrine first made explicit in 2 Macc 7:28 and 2 Bar 21:4, and foundational to Christian and Jewish theology. The temporal reading is a minority position but has serious defenders (Rashi, Speiser).
1:2ruach Elohim: Spirit of God or wind of God?
MT ruach Elohim — ambiguous: spirit/breath/wind. LXX pneuma theou — "Spirit of God" (unambiguously personal). NIV/ESV/NASB "Spirit of God"; JPS "wind from God."
The LXX's pneuma theou shaped all subsequent Christian reading of this verse as trinitarian. If ruach = wind, v.2 is cosmological scene-setting; if ruach = Spirit, the third Person of the Trinity is present at creation. The NT's consistent use of pneuma for the Spirit supports the personal reading.
1:26"Let us make" — the plural referent
MT naasseh adam — cohortative plural, unambiguous in form. LXX poiesomen — plural preserved. All ancient versions preserve the plural; the difficulty is the referent.
Four interpretive options have serious defenders: (1) Divine council/heavenly assembly (Ps 82, Job 1) — most OT scholars; (2) Plural of majesty — classical interpretation; (3) Trinitarian anticipation — NT authors and Church Fathers; (4) God addressing the earth. The NT (Col 1:16; John 1:3) reads the "us" as inclusive of the Son.

Scholarly Debates

The Nature of the "Days" (yom)
Literal 24-hour days (Young Earth)
Whitcomb, Morris (The Genesis Flood, 1961); AiG; ICR
Yom with an ordinal number and "evening and morning" formula consistently means a solar day elsewhere in the OT. The straightforward reading of the text requires literal days. A global young earth coheres with the flood geology of Genesis 6-8.
Day-Age / Progressive Creation
Hugh Ross (Reasons to Believe); B.B. Warfield; W.H. Green
Yom can mean an indefinite period (Gen 2:4 — "in the day the LORD made..."; Ps 90:4). The geological record shows an ancient earth; the sequence of Genesis 1 broadly parallels the fossil record. God created progressively over vast epochs.
Framework Hypothesis
Meredith Kline; Henri Blocher (In the Beginning, 1984); C. John Collins
The "days" are a literary/topical framework: Days 1-3 (forming) parallel Days 4-6 (filling). The framework is a theological portrait of creation's order, not a chronological account. The genre is elevated prose-poetry, not historical narrative.
Functional Creation / Cosmic Temple
John H. Walton (The Lost World of Genesis One, 2009)
In ancient Near Eastern thinking, something "exists" when it has a function and a name. Genesis 1 is not about material origins but the inauguration of the cosmic temple — God assigning functions to cosmos and installing his image (humanity) as the temple's priests.
No position has achieved scholarly consensus. The framework hypothesis and functional creation represent the most sophisticated recent readings. The debate is ultimately about genre: is Genesis 1 historical narrative, elevated prose-poetry, or ancient cosmological theology? Most evangelical scholars hold that the text is compatible with an ancient earth while insisting on the theological non-negotiables: God created, creation is good, humanity bears the image.
The Image of God (Imago Dei) — tselem and demut
Structural / Substantive
Aquinas; Augustine; most classical theology
The image is a quality inhering in human nature — reason, moral capacity, free will, spiritual nature. Humanity is like God in its rational and moral structure. The image makes humans qualitatively different from animals.
Functional / Royal
Walton; J. Richard Middleton (The Liberating Image, 2005); Wenham
In ancient Near Eastern usage, a king's "image" (tselem) was a statue placed in conquered territory to represent his presence and authority. Humanity is placed in creation as God's vice-regent, ruling on his behalf. The image is a role and commission, not a quality.
Relational
Karl Barth (Church Dogmatics III/1)
The image is the capacity for relationship — "male and female he created them" (v.27) is the key clause. Humanity images God's relational nature (Father/Son) in the male/female complementarity. The image is constituted in the I-Thou encounter.
The three positions are not mutually exclusive; the text likely supports all three dimensions. The functional reading has the strongest ANE support and best explains the immediate context (vv.26-28). The structural reading best accounts for the NT's use of the image motif (Col 3:10; Eph 4:24). The relational reading captures the social dimension of v.27. Most recent scholars (Middleton, Wenham, McDowell) argue for a multi-dimensional understanding.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 1 is not just the first chapter of the Bible — it is the theological foundation on which every subsequent doctrine is built. The nature of God (sovereign, transcendent, speaking), the nature of humanity (image-bearers, commissioned rulers, made for rest), the goodness of creation (affirmed seven times, "very good" once), and the rhythm of work and Sabbath are all established here. Every NT claim about new creation (2 Cor 5:17; Rev 21:1) presupposes and exceeds what is stated here. To read Genesis 1 well is to hold the lens through which everything else in Scripture comes into focus.
