Genesis 1

NIV · Scripture Study · Hebrew · Historical · Places · Timelines

📜 Traditional View — Moses

Jewish and Christian tradition holds that Moses wrote all five books of the Torah, likely during Israel’s wilderness wandering, c. 1446–1406 BCE. Moses was leading a slave nation freshly liberated from Egypt and surrounded by Egyptian polytheism — Genesis answers who their God was and where they came from.

“If you believed Moses, you would believe me, for he wrote about me.” — Jesus (John 5:46)

🔍 Modern Scholarship — JEDP Theory

Scholars identified four literary strands woven through the Torah. Genesis 1 is assigned to the Priestly (P) source — formal, structured, liturgical, using Elohim throughout. P is obsessed with order, ritual, and genealogy. The other sources (J, E, D) contribute different passages throughout Genesis.

🚨 The Crisis Behind Genesis 1

The P source dates to the Babylonian exile (586–538 BCE). Nebuchadnezzar II destroyed Solomon’s Temple, burned Jerusalem, and deported Israel’s priests to Babylon. In ancient thinking, when a nation was conquered, its god had been defeated. The exiles faced a shattering question: Has our God lost? Is Marduk more powerful?

“By the rivers of Babylon we sat and wept when we remembered Zion.” — Psalm 137:1

💡 Genesis 1 as Theological Response

Genesis 1 is the Priestly community’s answer — written as captives in Babylon, surrounded by the Enuma Elish (recited publicly each New Year), the Esagila temple of Marduk, and daily pressure to assimilate. Their response: Our God created everything — including what you worship. The sun and moon are just lamps He made. And we — not your king — bear His image.

Written by a defeated, exiled people — it reads like a declaration of cosmic victory.

🇮🇶 Life in Babylonian Exile

The exiles weren’t imprisoned — they were settled in communities along Babylonian canals (Ezekiel writes from “Tel Aviv” on the Chebar River). But they were foreigners with no Temple, no king, no homeland. Babylon was the greatest city on earth: the Ishtar Gate, the Esagila ziggurat, the Hanging Gardens. The exiles lived in its shadow every day.

In the Beginning — Verses 1–2
1 In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth. 2 Now the earth was formless and empty, darkness was over the surface of the deep, and the Spirit of God was hovering over the waters.

Hebrew Word Study

בּרֵאשִית Bere’shit — “In the beginning.” The Hebrew name of the entire book. Construct form pointing forward: “at the beginning of God’s creating.” Time itself begins here — not merely the first event within pre-existing time.


אֱלֹהִים Elohim — Plural noun, singular verb bara’. Divine majesty, or an early hint of plurality within the Godhead. Used 35 times in Genesis 1 — never YHWH (the personal covenant name), signalling this as a universal cosmic narrative rather than a covenantal one.


בָּרָא Bara’ — “Created.” Used exclusively with God as subject in the Hebrew Bible. Later tradition formalised this as creation ex nihilo — from nothing.


Tohu wabohu — “Formless and empty.” A rhyming pair. Unordered, uninhabitable void — not evil, just pre-creation. Appears again in Jer 4:23 as a description of divine judgment that mirrors un-creation.


Ruah Elohim — “Spirit/Wind/Breath of God.” The participle merahepet (hovering) appears elsewhere only in Deut 32:11 — an eagle hovering protectively over its young. Anticipatory, nurturing, about to act.

Historical Context

Genesis 1 stands against the Babylonian Enuma Elish (c. 1100 BCE), where creation results from divine warfare — Marduk defeats the sea-monster Tiamat and forms the world from her corpse. In Genesis: no conflict, no rivals, no violence. God speaks and reality obeys. The primordial sea (tehom, v.2 — cognate with Tiamat) is not a goddess to be battled but unordered matter waiting for God’s word. This de-mythologisation of chaos was theologically radical in its world.

Deep Context

The seven-day structure mirrors ancient temple dedication texts — the cosmos is being inaugurated as God’s cosmic temple (Isa 66:1; Ps 78:69). Day 7’s divine “rest” uses shabbat, which in temple contexts means “taking up residence.” Creation is complete not when things are made, but when God rests in their midst — moving in.


“The heavens and the earth” is a merism — two polar opposites expressing totality. The ancient Hebrew way of saying “the entire universe.” Nothing exists outside this creation.

Cross-References

  • John 1:1–3John deliberately echoes Gen 1:1 — the Logos as agent of all creation. “Through him all things were made.”
  • Col 1:16“All things were created through him and for him” — Christ as the for-whom behind the bara’.
  • Heb 11:3The universe formed at God’s command — NT affirmation of ex nihilo creation by faith.
  • Ps 33:6“By the word of the LORD the heavens were made” — creation by divine speech as ongoing doctrine.
  • Rev 4:11“You created all things, and by your will they were created” — worship grounded in creation.

📍 Key Places — The World of Genesis

🏛
Babylon
Modern Al-Hillah, Iraq

Capital of the Neo-Babylonian Empire. Nebuchadnezzar II brought Israel’s priests here in 586 BCE after destroying Jerusalem. The Enuma Elish creation myth was recited publicly every New Year festival at the Esagila temple. Genesis 1 was almost certainly written here — the Priestly community’s direct theological response, composed in the empire’s own backyard.

✡️
Jerusalem
Modern Jerusalem, Israel

Capital of the Israelite kingdom and site of Solomon’s Temple — destroyed by Babylon in 586 BCE, triggering the exile. The Temple was modelled on cosmic temple imagery connecting creation and sacred space. Losing it raised the desperate question: where is God now?

⭐
Ur of the Chaldeans
Modern Tell el-Mukayyar, Iraq

Abraham’s hometown (Gen 11:28–31). A major Sumerian city with the Great Ziggurat of Ur dedicated to the moon god Nanna (Sin). The patriarch of faith began his journey from a city saturated with lunar worship — his call out of Ur was a call out of polytheism itself.

☀️
Heliopolis (On)
Modern Cairo suburbs, Egypt

“City of the Sun.” Primary cult centre of Ra/Atum, Egypt’s solar creator god. Joseph’s father-in-law was its chief priest (Gen 41:45). Hebrew slaves knew this city well. The Pyramid Texts — the oldest religious writings on earth — originated from solar theology here.

📜
Ugarit
Modern Ras Shamra, Syria

Canaanite city whose tablet library was discovered in 1929. The tablets revealed that “shemesh” (sun) and “yareach” (moon) were divine names in Canaanite religion — the exact words Genesis 1 pointedly refuses to use, calling them only “the greater light” and “the lesser light.” The omission was deliberate.

🏺
Nineveh
Modern Mosul area, Iraq

Capital of the Assyrian Empire. The library of King Ashurbanipal preserved cuneiform copies of the Enuma Elish and the Gilgamesh Epic (which contains a flood narrative strikingly similar to Genesis 6–9). God sent Jonah here (Jon 1:2). These texts illuminate the exact religious world Genesis was written into.

🌙
Harran
Modern Şanlıurfa region, Turkey

Abraham’s family stopped here after leaving Ur (Gen 11:31–32). A major centre of moon-god (Sin) worship. The city remained openly pagan and devoted to lunar religion for centuries — one of the last pagan communities in the ancient Near East, still practising into the medieval period.

🔺
Giza
Egypt

The Great Pyramids are solar monuments — vehicles for the dead pharaoh to join the solar barque of Ra. The Sphinx faces the rising sun. All Egyptian monumental architecture expressed solar theology. Israel spent 400+ years in Egypt surrounded by this worldview before the Exodus.

These locations define the cultural and theological world Genesis 1 was written into and directly addresses.

📅 Timeline — From Oral Tradition to Final Text

Genesis 1’s final form emerges from the Babylonian exile — Israel’s darkest hour — as a declaration that the God who made everything had not been defeated.

Day One — Light · Verses 3–5
3 And God said, “Let there be light,” and there was light. 4 God saw that the light was good, and he separated the light from the darkness. 5 God called the light “day,” and the darkness he called “night.” And there was evening, and there was morning — the first day.

Hebrew Word Study

Wayyomer Elohim — “And God said.” Repeats 10 times in Genesis 1 as a liturgical refrain. Creation by divine speech — God doesn’t labour or struggle; he speaks and reality obeys. Foundation of John 1’s Logos doctrine.


טוב Tov — “Good.” Beautiful, fitting, complete, suited to purpose. Not a moral verdict but a functional one. On Day 6 the whole is tov me’od — “very good” — the only escalation in the entire refrain.


’Erev waboqer — “Evening and morning.” The Hebrew day begins at sundown — still observed in the Jewish Sabbath. A liturgical refrain marking each creative period with rhythm and order.

Deep Context — Light Before the Sun

Light is created on Day 1; the sun and moon not until Day 4. This was deliberate de-deification. In Egypt (Ra), Babylon (Shamash), and Canaan (Shemesh), the sun was the supreme deity. By creating light independently of the sun, Genesis asserts that light, time, and day/night rhythms belong to God — the sun is merely a lamp he installed later.


The “days” have been interpreted four main ways: (1) literal 24-hour periods, (2) geological ages, (3) the Framework Hypothesis — the days form a literary structure, not a chronology — and (4) analogical days. The Hebrew yom supports multiple readings.

Cross-References

  • 2 Cor 4:6Paul applies Gen 1:3 to spiritual new creation — God makes his light shine in human hearts.
  • John 1:4–5The Logos is the light of all people — the darkness has not overcome it.
  • Rev 22:5In new creation, no sun is needed — God himself is the light. Day 1 finds ultimate fulfilment here.
  • Isa 45:7“I form the light and create darkness” — God’s sovereign ownership of the Day 1 realities.

📅 The Literary Architecture of the 7 Days

Days 1–3 create the realms (forming); Days 4–6 populate them (filling). Genesis 1 is a carefully architected liturgical poem.

Day Two — Sky & Waters · Day Three — Land & Plants · Verses 6–13
6 And God said, “Let there be a vault between the waters to separate water from water.” 7 So God made the vault and separated the water under the vault from the water above it. And it was so. 8 God called the vault “sky.” And there was evening, and there was morning — the second day.

9 And God said, “Let the water under the sky be gathered to one place, and let dry ground appear.” And it was so. 10 God called the dry ground “land,” and the gathered waters he called “seas.” And God saw that it was good.

11 Then God said, “Let the land produce vegetation: seed-bearing plants and trees on the land that bear fruit with seed in it, according to their various kinds.” And it was so. 12 The land produced vegetation: plants bearing seed according to their kinds and trees bearing fruit with seed in it according to their kinds. And God saw that it was good. 13 And there was evening, and there was morning — the third day.

Hebrew Word Study

רָקִיע Raqia’ — “Vault” (NIV), “firmament” (KJV), “expanse.” Root means to hammer out metal thin — like beating bronze into a dome. Ancients envisioned the sky as a solid dome holding back celestial waters. The inspired author uses this shared cosmological picture to carry theological truth without requiring scientific precision.


Leminehu — “According to its kind.” Appears 10 times in Genesis 1. Emphasises order and the self-replicating power built into creation — life reproduces life. Creation has generative capacity from the very beginning.

Historical Context

The three-tier cosmos (waters above the dome / sky dome / earth and seas below) appears across Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and Canaanite cosmologies — it was how ancient peoples pictured the world. Day 2 uniquely has no “it was good” declaration. Ancient rabbis noted this: the Talmud (Bereshit Rabbah) suggests it’s because division/separation is an incomplete act — the waters aren’t properly bounded until Day 3 when the seas are gathered and named.

Day Four — Sun, Moon & Stars · Verses 14–19
14 And God said, “Let there be lights in the vault of the sky to separate the day from the night, and let them serve as signs to mark sacred times, and days and years, 15 and let them be lights in the vault of the sky to give light on the earth.” And it was so. 16 God made two great lights — the greater light to govern the day and the lesser light to govern the night. He also made the stars. 17 God set them in the vault of the sky to give light on the earth, 18 to govern the day and the night, and to separate light from darkness. And God saw that it was good. 19 And there was evening, and there was morning — the fourth day.

Hebrew Word Study

The text never names the sun (shemesh) or moon (yareach) — calling them only “the greater light” and “the lesser light.” These were names of Canaanite sun and moon deities. By refusing their names, Genesis strips them of divine status: they are functional objects, not gods.


מְאֹרֹת Me’orot — “Luminaries / light-bearers.” Fixtures in God’s cosmic temple. They “govern” (memshelah) time rhythms but possess no inherent deity or power over fate.

Historical Context

Solar worship was the most widespread religion in the ancient world: Ra/Atum in Egypt, Shamash in Babylon and Assyria, Shemesh in Canaan, Helios in Greece. Ezekiel 8:16 records Israelites bowing to the sun inside the Jerusalem Temple itself. The Israelite exile authors of Genesis 1 lived in Babylon — the very city whose Shamash temple at Sippar (the E-babbar, “Shining House”) was one of the most magnificent structures of the age.

📍 Places — Sun & Moon Worship Sites Surrounding Israel

☀️
Heliopolis — Ra/Atum
Modern Cairo suburbs, Egypt

Egypt’s supreme solar deity Ra/Atum was worshipped here as the creator who spoke the world into being. The Pyramid Texts (c. 2400 BCE) are the oldest religious writings on earth, centred on solar theology. Genesis 1’s creation-by-speech parallels this narrative — but replaces a solar god with the invisible God of Israel.

🔺
Giza — Solar Monuments
Egypt

The Great Pyramids are precisely oriented to the sun and were vehicles for the dead pharaoh to join Ra’s solar barque. The Sphinx faces the rising sun. The Israelite slaves who built for Pharaoh (Ex 1:11) lived in the shadow of this solar theology for four centuries before the Exodus.

🌞
Babylon/Sippar — Shamash
Modern Iraq

Shamash was the Babylonian sun god of justice and law. The Code of Hammurabi (c. 1750 BCE) shows Hammurabi receiving law directly from Shamash. The E-babbar (“Shining House”) temple at Sippar was one of Babylon’s greatest shrines. The exiled Israelites who wrote Genesis 1 passed it regularly.

🌙
Ur — Sin/Nanna
Southern Iraq

Sin (Akkadian) or Nanna (Sumerian) was the moon god of Ur — father of the sun god Shamash and the goddess Ishtar. The Great Ziggurat of Ur was dedicated to Nanna and still partially stands. Abraham’s call out of Ur was a call away from this lunar religion. Genesis 1 declares the moon a mere lamp.

🌙
Harran — Sin Worship
Modern Turkey

Another major moon-god (Sin) centre where Abraham’s family settled after leaving Ur (Gen 11:31). Harran remained pagan and devoted to lunar religion centuries after surrounding regions changed — one of history’s most persistent pagan holdouts, still practising moon worship into the 10th century CE.

⚡
Ugarit — Shapshu & Yarikh
Modern Ras Shamra, Syria

Canaanite tablets from Ugarit name the sun goddess Shapshu and moon god Yarikh as major deities. This proves that “shemesh” (sun) and “yareach” (moon) were active divine names in the world bordering Israel. Genesis 1’s refusal to use these names was a pointed, culturally understood theological statement.

⚠️
Jerusalem Temple — Sun Worship!
Jerusalem, Israel

Ezekiel 8:16 records twenty-five men in the Temple courtyard “with their backs toward the temple of the LORD, bowing down to the sun.” Solar worship had penetrated Israel’s holiest site. Genesis 1’s refusal to name the sun wasn’t only a polemic against foreigners — it was urgently needed within Israel itself.

Genesis 1 was written into a world where the sun and moon were gods. Its refusal to even name them was a radical, politically charged theological statement — one every reader in the ancient world would have understood immediately.

Day Five — Sea & Sky Creatures · Day Six — Land Animals & Humanity · Verses 20–31
20 And God said, “Let the water teem with living creatures, and let birds fly above the earth across the vault of the sky.” 21 So God created the great creatures of the sea and every living thing with which the water teems and that moves about in it, according to their kinds, and every winged bird according to its kind. And God saw that it was good. 22 God blessed them and said, “Be fruitful and increase in number and fill the water in the seas, and let the birds increase on the earth.” 23 And there was evening, and there was morning — the fifth day.

24 And God said, “Let the land produce living creatures according to their kinds: the livestock, the creatures that move along the ground, and the wild animals, each according to its kind.” And it was so. 25 God made the wild animals according to their kinds, the livestock according to their kinds, and all the creatures that move along the ground according to their kinds. And God saw that it was good.

26 Then God said, “Let us make mankind in our image, in our likeness, so that they may rule over the fish in the sea and the birds in the sky, over the livestock and all the wild animals, and over all the creatures that move along the ground.”

27 So God created mankind in his own image,
    in the image of God he created them;
    male and female he created them.

28 God blessed them and said to them, “Be fruitful and increase in number; fill the earth and subdue it. Rule over the fish in the sea and the birds in the sky and over every living creature that moves on the ground.”

29 Then God said, “I give you every seed-bearing plant on the face of the whole earth and every tree that has fruit with seed in it. They will be yours for food. 30 And to all the beasts of the earth and all the birds in the sky and all the creatures that move along the ground — everything that has the breath of life in it — I give every green plant for food.” And it was so.

31 God saw all that he had made, and it was very good. And there was evening, and there was morning — the sixth day.

Hebrew Word Study — Imago Dei

Adam — “Mankind/human being,” from adamah (ground/soil). The earth-creature elevated to divine image-bearing. Not a personal name until Genesis 2–3.


Tselem (image) — Used elsewhere for a physical statue or idol. In ancient Near Eastern royal ideology, the king alone was the “image” of the god — his representative on earth. Genesis extends this royal status to all of humanity. Every person is a royal representative of God — a revolutionary democratic claim in an age of absolute monarchy.


Demut (likeness) — Real resemblance without identity. Together with tselem, it asserts a genuine but non-identical correspondence between humans and God.


The plural “Let us” — divine council? Majestic plural? The Trinity? Rabbinic tradition: God consulting the angels. Most Christian theology: the Trinity in intra-divine deliberation before the most significant creative act.

Deep Context — Three Views of Imago Dei

Structural view: Humans resemble God in rationality, morality, and spiritual capacity — dominant in Western theology from Augustine through Calvin. The image is what we are.


Functional view: The image is about royal role — humans as God’s vice-regents over creation. Strongly supported by v.28 (“rule over…”) and ancient Near Eastern royal ideology. The majority scholarly view today. The image is what we do.


Relational view: The image is constituted in relationship — “male and female he created them” (v.27). Emphasised by Karl Barth. The image is how we relate.


The image grounds: the prohibition of murder (Gen 9:6), the condemnation of cursing others (Jas 3:9), the entire theology of human dignity, and ultimately the logic of the Incarnation.

Cross-References — Imago Dei

  • Gen 9:6Murder prohibited because humans bear God’s image — grounds law even after the Fall.
  • Ps 8:4–6Humans “crowned with glory and honour” — poetic meditation on the dignity given in Genesis 1.
  • Col 1:15Christ is “the image of the invisible God” — the perfect imago Dei, restoring what Adam distorted.
  • Rom 8:29Believers conformed to the image of his Son — redemption understood as image-restoration.
  • Jas 3:9Cursing others condemned because they bear God’s likeness — the image grounds everyday ethics.
  • 2 Cor 3:18“Transformed into his image with ever-increasing glory” — progressive renewal of the imago throughout the Christian life.

📍 Places — The Eden Region (Genesis 2:10–14)

🌿
Traditional Eden Location
Basra region, southern Iraq

Where the Tigris and Euphrates converge at the head of the Persian Gulf — the most widely accepted scholarly location for Eden. Archaeologist Juris Zarins (1987) proposed this entire area was the Garden, flooded as sea levels rose after the last Ice Age (~8000 BCE). The ancient shoreline sat 60+ miles further inland than it does today.

🏔️
Tigris Headwaters
Eastern Turkey

The Tigris (Hebrew: Hiddekel) rises near Lake Hazar in southeastern Turkey. Genesis 2:14 says it “flows east of Ashur” — the most securely identified of Eden’s four rivers. Flows ~1,900 km south through Iraq, passing Nineveh and Assur, before joining the Euphrates.

🏔️
Euphrates Headwaters
Eastern Turkey

The Euphrates (Hebrew: Perat) also rises in eastern Anatolia. At ~2,800 km, it’s one of history’s most significant rivers — Babylon was built on its banks. Both the Tigris and Euphrates originate in Turkey, leading the “Armenia theory” to locate Eden at their headwaters.

🏛
Babylon
Modern Al-Hillah, Iraq

Built on the Euphrates and linked to the Tower of Babel (Gen 11). More significantly, it’s the city where the Priestly authors wrote the final form of Genesis 1–2 during the exile. The scribes describing the Garden of Eden were captives in the world’s most powerful empire — a profound irony woven into the text’s formation.

⭐
Ur of the Chaldeans
Southern Iraq

Abraham’s hometown, near the ancient Persian Gulf shoreline. The Great Ziggurat of Ur still partially stands. Eden and Abraham’s homeland occupy the same general region — connecting creation and covenant narratively. The man God called to begin redemptive history came from Eden’s neighbourhood.

💧
Pishon River (proposed)
Saudi Arabia / Kuwait — Wadi al-Batin

Genesis 2:11 says the Pishon “winds through the entire land of Havilah, where there is gold.” Satellite imagery has revealed an ancient dried riverbed — the Wadi al-Batin — running through Saudi Arabia into Kuwait. This may be the ancient Pishon, now dry since the climate shift following the last Ice Age.

💧
Gihon River (proposed)
Southwest Iran — Karun River

Genesis 2:13 says the Gihon “winds through the entire land of Cush.” One proposal identifies Gihon with the Karun River in southwestern Iran (ancient Elam), which flows into the Shatt al-Arab near the Tigris–Euphrates confluence. If correct, all four rivers once converged near the northern Persian Gulf.

🌊
Northern Persian Gulf
Off the coast of Kuwait & Iraq

During the last Ice Age, sea levels were ~120 metres lower, exposing a large landmass in the Persian Gulf. As glaciers melted and seas rose (~10,000–6,000 BCE), this fertile river valley was submerged. Some scholars believe this was the Garden — a lush, well-watered plain that now sits on the ocean floor.

The geography of Eden remains genuinely uncertain. The most plausible proposals all centre on the Tigris–Euphrates basin in modern Iraq — the same land where the exile authors of Genesis were living when they wrote it all down.

📅 Imago Dei Through Theological History

The meaning of Imago Dei has been debated for over two millennia. Current scholarly consensus leans functional — all humans as God’s royal representatives — while incorporating structural and relational dimensions.

⇄ Intertextual Threading

6 canonical threads — 6 forward (passages that echo this chapter)  |  0 backward (texts this chapter echoes)

vv.1–2 → John 1:1–3 Echo

John opens with En archē — 'In the beginning' — the exact LXX rendering of bereshit. He then identifies the pre-existent Word (Logos) as the agent of all creation: 'through him all things were made.' John's prologue is a deliberate new-Genesis, announcing that Jesus is the fulfillment of what Gen 1 began.

v.2 — ruah over waters → Matt 3:16; Luke 3:22 Echo

At Jesus's baptism, the Spirit of God descends like a dove over the water — recalling Gen 1:2's ruah Elohim merahefet (Spirit hovering over waters). Both scenes mark the beginning of a new creative act: Gen 1 = original creation; the baptism = new creation inaugurated through Christ.

v.3 — Let there be light → 2 Cor 4:6 Echo

Paul explicitly draws the parallel: 'God, who said, "Let light shine out of darkness," made his light shine in our hearts to give us the light of the knowledge of God's glory displayed in the face of Christ.' The new creation in Christ is Paul's direct theological extension of Gen 1:3.

v.26 — Let us make → Col 1:15–17 Fulfillment

Paul identifies Christ as 'the image of the invisible God' and 'the firstborn over all creation,' through whom all things were created. The tselem Elohim (image of God) that humans bear imperfectly (Gen 1:26) finds its perfect expression in Christ — he is the true Image. The 'us' of Gen 1:26 receives its fullest meaning in Trinitarian theology.

v.28 — Fill and subdue → Rev 21–22 Fulfillment

Revelation's new creation restores and completes the Gen 1 mandate. The curse (Gen 3) is explicitly reversed (Rev 22:3: 'No longer will there be any curse'). The fruitful city-garden of Rev 21–22 is Eden restored and escalated — the original mandate of Gen 1:28 finally fulfilled in the new heavens and earth.

vv.1–31 — Creation pattern → Exod 39–40 Echo

The construction of the tabernacle in Exodus 39–40 uses the identical sevenfold structure of Gen 1: seven divine speech acts, the work is 'completed' (same word: vayekhal), Moses 'sees' and approves (same verb as Gen 1's vayar), and blesses the people. The tabernacle is a portable Eden — a micro-cosmos. The OT authors understood creation and worship as structurally identical.

People — Genesis 1

God / Elohim
Divine Actor — Sole Subject of Ch.1

Elohim is a plural form used with singular verbs throughout ch.1 — the 'plural of majesty' or possibly a hint of inner-divine complexity. He speaks, evaluates, names, blesses. He never appears in physical form. Genesis 1's God is radically unlike ANE deities: he does not struggle against chaos, is not born, has no consort, needs nothing from creation. He creates by speech alone — va-yomer Elohim (and God said) occurs 10 times, matching the 10 commandments and the 10 plagues. The name Elohim is used exclusively in ch.1; YHWH Elohim begins only in ch.2.

The Spirit / Ruah
Divine Presence over the Waters — v.2

The ruah Elohim (Spirit/Wind of God) hovering over the waters in v.2 is the first hint of inner-divine action beyond the singular speaking God. Merahefet (hovering/brooding) is used only one other time in the OT — Deut 32:11, where God hovers over Israel like an eagle over its nest. The Spirit is the creative, animating presence of God active within the created order. The NT identifies this presence with the Holy Spirit (Matt 3:16; John 1:32).

Ancient Source Texts — Enuma Elish

Enuma Elish — Babylonian Creation Epic (c. 1100 BCE)
When on high the heaven had not been named, firm ground below had not been called by name — naught but primordial Apsu, their begetter, and Mummu-Tiamat, she who bore them all, their waters commingling as a single body... Then were created the gods in the midst of heaven.

The Babylonian creation epic begins with watery chaos (Apsu = fresh water; Tiamat = salt water) before anything is named — parallel to Gen 1:2's formless deep. Key differences: in Enuma Elish, the world is made from Tiamat's corpse after Marduk kills her in battle. In Genesis, creation is effortless speech. There is no combat, no rival power, no divine birth. The Hebrew author appears to be deliberately reframing Babylonian cosmology: the 'deep' (tehom / Tiamat) is not a god to be defeated but simply water over which God's spirit moves.

Enuma Elish — The Seventh Tablet
Let the seventh day be exalted... it is the day of rest from toil. Let this day be called Shabbattum [a day of rest / appeasement].

The Babylonian seventh-day rest appears in ANE tradition independently of Genesis. The Hebrew Shabbat and the Babylonian Shabbattum share the same linguistic root. Genesis recasts this not as appeasement of anxious gods but as divine satisfaction in completed, ordered creation — a fundamentally different theology wearing similar vocabulary.

Egyptian Memphite Theology (c. 2300 BCE)
Ptah... who made all things and created the gods... his Enneads are before him as teeth and lips... He spoke and they came into being.

The Egyptian creator god Ptah creates by speaking — the closest ANE parallel to Genesis 1's creation-by-word theology. This text, carved on the Shabaka Stone and claiming to copy a worm-eaten ancient papyrus, predates most standard dating of Genesis and shows that 'word-creation' theology was not unique to Israel — but Genesis radicalizes it: one God, no pantheon, no magical speech, no resistance.

Reception History — Genesis 1

Basil of Caesarea — Hexaemeron (c. 370 CE)

Basil's nine sermons on Genesis 1 became the definitive patristic treatment. He insisted on a literal six-day reading: 'If it has been said once, that the day is one, it is because it was the type and measure of other days.' He used the text to argue against Greek philosophical ideas of eternal matter, insisting creation ex nihilo — from nothing — was essential to Christian theology. His sermons were enormously influential on both Eastern Orthodox and Western traditions.

Augustine of Hippo — De Genesi ad Litteram (c. 415 CE)

Augustine argued surprisingly that the 'days' of Genesis 1 need not be literal 24-hour periods, since the sun isn't created until day 4. He proposed that God created everything simultaneously in seminal principles (rationes seminales) — seeds of future development planted in creation from the beginning. This reading, often called proto-evolutionary, gave Augustine the flexibility to accommodate scientific observations without sacrificing the theological core. His approach has been influential in the Catholic tradition's engagement with evolutionary science.

Rashi (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, 1040–1105)

Rashi's opening comment on Genesis 1:1 remains famous: the Torah didn't need to begin with creation at all — it should have begun with Exodus 12 (the first commandment). Creation is included to establish Israel's right to the land: God who created the world gives it to whom he chooses, and he chose to give Canaan to Israel. This reading makes Genesis 1 not primarily cosmological but land-theological — a statement about divine sovereignty over all earthly territory.

Modern Scholarship — John Walton (Lost World of Genesis One, 2009)

Walton's influential reading argues Genesis 1 describes a 'functional' rather than 'material' creation — God is not making physical matter but assigning functions and roles to things that already materially exist. He argues that in ANE thought, things exist when they have a function, not just when they have matter. On this reading, Genesis 1 is about the cosmos becoming God's temple (a 'cosmic temple inauguration'), not a scientific account of material origins. This approach has significantly influenced evangelical engagement with science.

Literary Structure — The Seven-Day Chiasm

Genesis 1 is constructed as a precise literary architecture. Days 1–3 create domains; Days 4–6 fill them with inhabitants. The symmetry is exact:

D1Light / Darkness separated
D2Sky / Waters separated
D3Land / Seas + Vegetation
— — — — — — —
D4Sun / Moon fill Day 1’s domains
D5Birds + Sea creatures fill Days 2’s domains
D6Land animals + Humanity fill Day 3’s domain
— — — — — — —
D7Sabbath rest — the climax and goal

The chapter also contains exactly 10 divine speech acts (va-yomer Elohim), echoing the 10 commandments and the 10 plagues. The word “good” (tov) appears 7 times. The word “God” (Elohim) appears exactly 35 times (7×5). The entire chapter is saturated with seven-based numerics — a literary signature of completeness and covenant. This is not accidental; it is the deliberate craft of a highly skilled author composing a liturgical text intended for recitation and memorization.
