Genesis 10

The Table of Nations

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration. What prompted it: A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–5 — The Table of Nations: Sons of Japheth

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:1–5

The Table of Nations is unique in ancient literature — no other ancient culture produced a systematic account of the origin of all known nations from a common ancestor, treating all peoples as members of one family. This is the biblical foundation for human racial unity: one blood (Acts 17:26), one origin, one family, descended from Noah's three sons. The 70 nations listed (a number of completeness in Hebrew thought — 70 descendants of Jacob go to Egypt in Gen 46; 70 elders in Exodus 24) represent the totality of the known world. Genesis 10 plants the seed of the Great Commission: all nations are known to God, descended from his creation, and the objects of his redemptive purpose. Revelation 7:9 shows the harvest: "every nation, tribe, people, and language" before the throne.

Hebrew Word Study

Toledot — The familiar structural formula: “This is the account of…” The Table of Nations is the 6th toledot section in Genesis. It is unique in the ancient world — no comparable document from any other ancient Near Eastern culture attempts a comprehensive mapping of known humanity as descended from a common ancestor.


Goyim — “Nations / peoples.” Each entry in the table represents a goy (nation) — a people group, language community, and territory. The 70 nations listed here (by traditional count) become the background against which Abraham’s call in chapter 12 operates: “all peoples on earth will be blessed through you” (12:3). The 70 nations of chapter 10 are the 70 peoples to be blessed.

Historical Context — Identifying Japheth’s Sons

Japheth’s descendants correspond broadly to Indo-European peoples north and west of Israel. Gomer = Cimmerians (Black Sea region); Magog = Scythians (central Asia); Madai = Medes (western Iran); Javan = Ionians / Greeks; Tubal and Meshek = peoples of Anatolia (central Turkey); Tiras = possibly Thracians or Etruscans; Ashkenaz = a people of the Caucasus (later Jewish tradition applied this name to Germanic peoples, hence “Ashkenazi Jews”); Kittites = Cyprus (Kition); Tarshish = possibly Sardinia or southern Spain — the western horizon of the known world. The Japheth list represents the most geographically dispersed family — maritime peoples spread across the Mediterranean and Black Sea.

Deep Context — The Table of Nations as Theological Statement

Genesis 10 is one of the most extraordinary documents in ancient literature. It presents all known humanity —70 nations, spread across three continents — as a single family descended from Noah’s three sons. No other ancient culture made this claim. Egypt traced its origins to the gods. Greece had city-specific founding myths. Mesopotamia had local creation stories. Only Israel’s Scripture asserts the radical unity of all humanity — that every human being, regardless of language, culture, or geography, shares a common ancestor and therefore a common dignity.


This is the foundation for Paul’s statement in Acts 17:26: “From one man he made all the nations, that they should inhabit the whole earth.” The Table of Nations is the biblical basis for human equality and the mission of the gospel to “all nations” (Matt 28:19).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:8–12

Nimrod is introduced as "a mighty warrior on the earth" and "a mighty hunter before the LORD" — a phrase that can mean either "with the LORD's blessing" or "in defiance of the LORD." Given that Nimrod's kingdom includes Babylon, Erech, and Akkad (Shinar) — the location of Babel in ch.11 — and Nineveh (later the capital of Assyria, Israel's great oppressor), the context tilts negative. Nimrod represents the archetype of human empire: power concentrated, cities built, dominion exercised for human glory rather than God's. The great empires that will oppress God's people all trace back to Ham through Nimrod.

Hebrew Word Study — Nimrod

נִמְרֹד Nimrod — Possibly from marad (“to rebel”), implying “We will rebel.” He is described as a gibbor (mighty man) — the same word used for the offspring of the sons of God in 6:4. He is a tsayid (hunter) — a word that in context may mean more than animal hunting; he hunts men, dominates peoples, builds empires.


Lifnei YHWH — “Before the LORD” (v.9). The phrase is ambiguous: it can mean “in the presence of / with the blessing of the LORD” (a positive reading), or it can be ironic — Nimrod’s prowess is displayed before the LORD who watches it with displeasure. Given that Nimrod founds Babylon (the great symbol of anti-God empire) and Nineveh (Israel’s great enemy), most read the repetition of “before the LORD” as pointed irony.

Historical Context — Nimrod & Empire

Nimrod is the first empire-builder in the Bible. His cities — Babylon (Babel), Uruk, Akkad, Nineveh — are the great power-cities of the ancient Near East. Uruk (biblical Erech) was one of the world’s first cities, home of Gilgamesh. Babylon would become the Babylonian Empire that destroyed Jerusalem in 586 BCE. Nineveh became the Assyrian capital that destroyed the Northern Kingdom in 722 BCE. Nimrod’s genealogy is the origin story of every empire that will oppress Israel.


Ham’s family also includes Egypt (Mizraim — the land of slavery), the Philistines (Israel’s persistent coastal enemies), the Hittites (a major ANE empire), Amorites, Jebusites (original inhabitants of Jerusalem), and Canaanites (the peoples Israel was called to displace). The narrative geography of Genesis 10 is the backstory to nearly every conflict in the rest of the OT.

Places — Cities of Nimrod

Babylon (Babel)
Central Iraq, near modern Hillah

The first and greatest of Nimrod’s cities; center of the Babylonian Empire. Excavated by Robert Koldewey (1899–1917), revealing the Ishtar Gate, massive ziggurats, and palaces. Babylon destroyed Jerusalem in 586 BCE and exiled the Jews — the defining trauma of OT history. In Revelation it becomes the symbolic name for the final anti-God empire.

Uruk (Erech)
Southern Iraq, near modern Warka

One of the world’s first cities (c. 4000 BCE), home of the legendary king Gilgamesh. The Epic of Gilgamesh, containing the most detailed flood parallel to Genesis, originated here. Uruk may have had a population of 80,000 at its peak — enormous for the ancient world. Excavations have uncovered the world’s earliest writing systems here.

Nineveh
Northern Iraq, near modern Mosul

Capital of the Assyrian Empire at its height (c. 700 BCE). Sennacherib built a “palace without rival” here. The Assyrians destroyed Israel’s Northern Kingdom in 722 BCE and besieged Jerusalem under Hezekiah (Isa 36–37). Jonah was sent to preach here. Nahum prophesied its destruction, which came in 612 BCE when the Babylonians sacked it.

Sidon
Modern Lebanon, coastal city

Firstborn of Canaan (v.15) — the great Phoenician port city. The Phoenicians (Canaanites) were the ancient world’s master sailors and traders, spreading their alphabet (ancestor of ours) across the Mediterranean. Sidon appears throughout the prophets as a symbol of coastal wealth and pride. Jesus visited its region (Matt 15:21) and cited it more favourably than Israel’s own cities (Luke 10:13–14).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
10:21–31

The line of Shem receives the final position — the place of honour in a Hebrew toledot sequence. From Shem's line the text will trace the narrowing path to Abraham (11:10–26). The blessing of Shem (9:26: "Blessed be the LORD, the God of Shem") is not merely ethnic pride but theological: the covenant name Yahweh is associated with Shem's line. God is the God of Shem's descendants in a special way that will become explicit in the Abrahamic covenant. The genealogy is not merely demographic information but the tracking of God's redemptive purpose as it narrows through history toward one family, one nation, and ultimately one Person.

Hebrew Word Study

Benei Ever — “Sons of Eber” (v.21). Shem is identified as the “ancestor of all the sons of Eber” — a remarkable designation that places Eber above Shem’s other descendants in significance. Ever is widely considered the root of the word Ivri — Hebrew. The Israelites are “the Hebrews” — literally the “Eber-ites,” the sons of Eber. The Table of Nations encodes the origin of Israel’s ethnic identity four generations before Abraham.


Peleg — “Division” (v.25). Ki be-yamav niflegah ha-aretz — “because in his time the earth was divided.” This almost certainly refers to the Babel dispersion of chapter 11, placed here as a proleptic note. The name Peleg dates the Babel event — it occurred in the generation of Eber’s sons.


Elam, Ashur, Aram — These become major players in OT history: Elam (southwestern Iran), Ashur (Assyria), Aram (Syria — the Arameans, whose language became the international language of the ancient world and the language Jesus spoke). The table is not merely historical but prophetic — it names the nations that will shape Israel’s entire future.

Historical Context — Shem’s Descendants

The Semitic family (Shem’s sons) covers the ancient Near East’s Semitic-language-speaking peoples: Elam (Elamites of southwestern Iran); Ashur (Assyrians); Arphaxad (Chaldeans / ancestors of Abraham); Lud (Lydians of western Anatolia); Aram (Arameans of Syria). Ophir (v.29) was a legendary source of gold (1 Kgs 9:28) — location disputed but possibly Yemen, Somalia, or India. Sheba (v.28) is the kingdom of the Queen of Sheba (1 Kgs 10) in southern Arabia or Ethiopia.


The Shem list is the most theologically significant because the covenant line runs through it: Shem → Arphaxad → Shelah → Eber → Peleg → (Genesis 11) → Terah → Abraham. The entire table is a funnel narrowing toward one man and one promise.

Deep Context — 70 Nations

Traditional Jewish counting gives 70 nations in the Table of Nations (Shem: 26; Ham: 30; Japheth: 14). The number 70 is not accidental — it recurs throughout Scripture as a symbol of completeness: 70 elders of Israel (Ex 24:9), 70 years of exile (Jer 25:11), 70 weeks of Daniel (Dan 9:24), Jesus sends out 70 (or 72) disciples (Luke 10:1). The Septuagint version of Deuteronomy 32:8 explicitly links the 70 nations to the 70 sons of Israel who went to Egypt (Gen 46:27) — God apportioned the nations according to the number of Israel. The Table of Nations is the universal canvas on which the particular story of Israel is painted.

Cross-References

  • Acts 17:26“From one man he made all the nations, that they should inhabit the whole earth” — Paul’s Areopagus sermon draws directly on the theology of Genesis 10.
  • Gen 12:3“All peoples on earth will be blessed through you” — Abraham’s call is the answer to the 70 nations of chapter 10; the particular answers the universal.
  • Rev 7:9“A great multitude that no one could count, from every nation, tribe, people and language” — the 70 nations of Genesis 10 gathered before the throne in the new creation.
  • Luke 10:1Jesus sends out 70 (or 72) disciples — a mission to the 70 nations of the Table of Nations, inaugurating the universal blessing of Abraham.

Timeline — From Noah to Abraham

The covenant line runs Shem → Arphaxad → Shelah → Eber → Peleg → Reu → Serug → Nahor → Terah → Abraham (Gen 11:10–26). The Table of Nations is the universal context for this narrowing thread of promise.

Land — vv.1–32

The Table of Nations is the most explicit land theology in Genesis: 70 nations, each with territory, language, clan. Land is divinely ordered, not merely conquered. Deut 32:8 (LXX) calls this God's own act.

Kingship — vv.8–12

Nimrod is the first human king named in Scripture — and his empire is built on the cities that will become Israel's greatest oppressors (Babylon, Nineveh). Kingship-as-domination is the problem; kingship-as-service (David) is the solution.

Seed/Messiah — vv.21–25

Shem's line begins its narrowing toward Abraham. Eber (v.24) generates the name 'Hebrew.' The seed of 3:15 is being focused: from all humanity → through Shem → through Eber → toward one man.

Intertextual Threading

4 canonical threads — 4 forward (passages that echo this chapter)  |  0 backward (texts this chapter echoes)

vv.1–32 — 70 nations → Deut 32:8–9 (LXX); Acts 17:26; Rev 5:9; 7:9 Fulfillment

The 70 nations of Gen 10 become the theological framework for the entire Bible's view of the nations. Deut 32:8 (LXX) says God set the boundaries of the nations according to the number of the 'sons of God' (divine council members), with Israel as his own portion. Paul in Acts 17:26 reads God's ordering of national boundaries as designed so that 'each nation might seek him.' Revelation's vision of 'every tribe, language, people and nation' (5:9; 7:9) is the eschatological gathering of all 70 — the Table fulfilled.

vv.8–12 — Nimrod's empire → Gen 11:1–9; Dan 2–7; Rev 17–18 Echo

Nimrod's city-building (Babel, Nineveh) is the beginning of the Bible's sustained critique of human empire. The tower of Babel (ch.11) continues Nimrod's logic. Daniel's vision of successive world empires (chs. 2, 7) traces the same Babylonian-imperial trajectory. Revelation's 'Babylon the Great' (chs. 17–18) is the final expression of Nimrod's impulse — empire organized around human glory rather than divine glory. Nimrod is the prototype; Babylon is the archetype; Revelation's Babylon is the eschatological culmination.

v.21 — sons of Eber → Gen 14:13; Exod 1:15–16; John 4:9 Echo

The term 'Hebrew' (Ivri) — derived from Eber in Gen 10:21 — is the ethnic identifier that will define God's covenant people through the entire OT. Abraham is first called 'the Hebrew' in Gen 14:13. The Hebrew midwives in Exod 1:15 resist Pharaoh's genocide. Jesus is identified to the Samaritan woman as a 'Jew' (literally, a descendant of Judah, within the Hebrew line). The single name Eber generates the identity 'Hebrew' that threads through every covenant moment in Scripture.

vv.2–5 — Japheth's coastlands → Isa 66:18–19; Rom 15:16–24 Fulfillment

Isaiah 66:18–19 specifically lists 'Tarshish, the Libyans and Lydians... Tubal and Greece' — Japhethite nations from Gen 10 — as the recipients of God's glory in the eschatological mission. Paul frames his own missionary strategy in these terms: he has 'proclaimed the gospel from Jerusalem all the way around to Illyricum' (Rom 15:19) and plans to go to Spain — moving through precisely the Japhethite coastlands of Gen 10:5. The Table of Nations is Paul's missionary map.

People — Genesis 10

Nimrod
First Empire-Builder — vv.8–12

Nimrod — possibly from marad (to rebel). He is the first person called a 'mighty warrior' (gibbor) on earth — the same word used of the Nephilim's offspring (6:4). He is described as a mighty hunter 'before YHWH' (lifnei YHWH) — a phrase that can mean 'in YHWH's presence' (approved) or 'in defiance of YHWH' (opposed). His cities: Babylon, Erech (Uruk), Akkad, Nineveh — every one of them becomes an oppressor of Israel in later biblical history. He is one of only two people in the table of nations given extended narrative description, which signals his theological importance as the proto-type of human imperial ambition.

Eber / Ever
Father of the Hebrews — v.24-25

Eber is the eponymous ancestor of the Hebrews (Ivri — one who crosses over). The name Ever may relate to 'avar (to cross) — the Hebrews as those who crossed the Euphrates (cf. Josh 24:2-3). From Eber come two sons: Peleg (in whose days 'the earth was divided' — possibly a reference to Babel in ch.11) and Joktan (ancestor of Arabian tribes). Eber sits at the hinge between the table of nations (universal scope) and the narrowing to Abraham's line that begins in ch.11. The name 'Hebrew' — which will define a people through all of Scripture — begins here.

Shem
Ancestor of the Covenant Line — vv.21–31

Shem is listed last in ch.10 (though first in birth order) — a narrative technique of delayed focus that prepares for ch.11's genealogy which zooms in on his line to Abraham. He is introduced with an unusual honorific: 'the father of all the sons of Eber, the older brother of Japheth' (v.21) — the double identification signals he is the theologically primary son. His genealogy in ch.10 is then repeated and extended in 11:10-26, narrowing from 26 descendants to one: Abram. The table of nations radiates outward from Noah; the genealogy of Shem radiates inward toward one man.

Ancient Source Texts — The Known World

Sumerian City Lists and Eponymous Ancestors
In the Sumerian tradition, cities and peoples were personified as descendants of gods or legendary heroes. Uruk (biblical Erech) was the city of Gilgamesh and sacred to the goddess Inanna. Akkad was the capital of Sargon's empire. Nineveh was sacred to Ishtar.

The Table of Nations uses the same ancient literary device of eponymous ancestry — nations named as 'descendants' of a founding person — that appears throughout Mesopotamian literature. This was the standard ancient way of mapping ethnic and political relationships. The 70 nations of Genesis 10 correspond to the 70 elders of Israel (Exod 24:9), the 70 members of Jacob's family (Gen 46:27), and the 70 disciples sent out by Jesus (Luke 10:1) — the number carries the meaning 'all the nations, fully represented.'

Egyptian Execration Texts (c. 1900 BCE)
The rulers of Canaan, all the Retenu, all the Nubians, all the Asiatics who may rebel... their confederates, their allies, all who may rebel — they are accursed and doomed.

Egypt's execration texts curse all known foreign peoples by name — an ancient form of geo-political mapping through ritual. These texts, combined with Assyrian annals, Hittite records, and the Amarna letters, allow archaeologists to verify the geographical reality of many Table of Nations peoples. Most of the 70 nations in Genesis 10 can be identified with known ANE peoples. The Table is not mythology — it is the most accurate ancient ethnographic survey of the known world of its time.

Reception History — Genesis 10

Augustine — City of God (Book XVI)

Augustine read the Table of Nations as establishing the theological framework for all of human political history. He identified Nimrod's Babylon as the prototype of the City of Man — human empire organized around self-glorification and dominion. He traced the two cities (City of God / City of Man) from Cain and Abel through Babel and on to Rome. His use of Genesis 10-11 to ground his theology of history made this obscure genealogical chapter one of the most theologically generative texts in the Western tradition.

Paul — Acts 17:26

Paul's sermon on the Areopagus quotes the logic of Genesis 10 directly: 'From one man he made all the nations, that they should inhabit the whole earth; and he marked out their appointed times in history and the boundaries of their lands.' This is Genesis 10 read as universal providence — the table of 70 nations is not ethnic determinism but divine ordering of history toward the moment when each nation might seek God. Paul uses the Table of Nations to ground both human unity (one source) and divine purpose in human diversity.

Pentecost — Acts 2 as Reversal of Babel

The early church read Acts 2's Pentecost event as the deliberate reversal of Genesis 11's Babel confusion. At Babel, one language becomes many, scattering the nations. At Pentecost, many languages are supernaturally understood as one, gathering the nations. The 'devout Jews from every nation under heaven' (Acts 2:5) who hear the gospel echo the 70 nations of Genesis 10. Luke's list of nations in Acts 2:9-11 is structured similarly to the Table of Nations. The Spirit does not erase ethnic diversity (everyone hears in their own language) but redeems it — fulfilling the purpose of Genesis 10 rather than abolishing it.

Literary Structure — The 70 Nations Architecture

Genesis 10 is structured with deliberate numerical and narrative architecture:

JAPHETH’S LINE (vv.2-5) — 14 nations
➤Indo-European peoples: Gomer, Magog, Madai, Javan, Tubal, Meshech, Tiras
➤Coastlands of the nations — maritime, northern peoples
HAM’S LINE (vv.6-20) — 30 nations
➤Africa and ANE: Cush, Egypt, Put, Canaan
➤Nimrod narrative inserted (vv.8-12) — empire as theological problem
➤Canaanite tribes named — Israel’s future adversaries
SHEM’S LINE (vv.21-31) — 26 nations
➤Semitic peoples: Elam, Ashur, Arphaxad, Lud, Aram
➤Narrowing toward Eber → Peleg → Abram’s line

Total: 14 + 30 + 26 = 70 nations. The number 70 in Scripture always signifies completeness of the nations (Deut 32:8 LXX; Luke 10:1; Rev 5:9). The table moves from periphery to center: Japheth (distant), Ham (ANE empires), Shem (covenant line). Each section ends with the same formula: “by their clans and languages, in their territories and nations” — emphasizing that human diversity is the divinely-ordered result of the Noahic covenant, not a disorder to be corrected. Babel (ch.11) is not the creation of diversity but its corruption into confusion.
