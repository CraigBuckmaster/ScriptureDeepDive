The Table of Nations — Introduction · Verses 1–5
1 This is the account of Shem, Ham and Japheth, Noah’s sons, who themselves had sons after the flood.

2 The sons of Japheth: Gomer, Magog, Madai, Javan, Tubal, Meshek and Tiras. 3 The sons of Gomer: Ashkenaz, Riphath and Togarmah. 4 The sons of Javan: Elishah, Tarshish, the Kittites and the Rodanites. 5 (From these the maritime peoples spread out into their territories by their clans within their nations, each with its own language.)

Hebrew Word Study

Toledot — The familiar structural formula: “This is the account of…” The Table of Nations is the 6th toledot section in Genesis. It is unique in the ancient world — no comparable document from any other ancient Near Eastern culture attempts a comprehensive mapping of known humanity as descended from a common ancestor.


Goyim — “Nations / peoples.” Each entry in the table represents a goy (nation) — a people group, language community, and territory. The 70 nations listed here (by traditional count) become the background against which Abraham’s call in chapter 12 operates: “all peoples on earth will be blessed through you” (12:3). The 70 nations of chapter 10 are the 70 peoples to be blessed.

Historical Context — Identifying Japheth’s Sons

Japheth’s descendants correspond broadly to Indo-European peoples north and west of Israel. Gomer = Cimmerians (Black Sea region); Magog = Scythians (central Asia); Madai = Medes (western Iran); Javan = Ionians / Greeks; Tubal and Meshek = peoples of Anatolia (central Turkey); Tiras = possibly Thracians or Etruscans; Ashkenaz = a people of the Caucasus (later Jewish tradition applied this name to Germanic peoples, hence “Ashkenazi Jews”); Kittites = Cyprus (Kition); Tarshish = possibly Sardinia or southern Spain — the western horizon of the known world. The Japheth list represents the most geographically dispersed family — maritime peoples spread across the Mediterranean and Black Sea.

Deep Context — The Table of Nations as Theological Statement

Genesis 10 is one of the most extraordinary documents in ancient literature. It presents all known humanity —70 nations, spread across three continents — as a single family descended from Noah’s three sons. No other ancient culture made this claim. Egypt traced its origins to the gods. Greece had city-specific founding myths. Mesopotamia had local creation stories. Only Israel’s Scripture asserts the radical unity of all humanity — that every human being, regardless of language, culture, or geography, shares a common ancestor and therefore a common dignity.


This is the foundation for Paul’s statement in Acts 17:26: “From one man he made all the nations, that they should inhabit the whole earth.” The Table of Nations is the biblical basis for human equality and the mission of the gospel to “all nations” (Matt 28:19).

The Line of Ham — Verses 6–20
6 The sons of Ham: Cush, Egypt, Put and Canaan. 7 The sons of Cush: Seba, Havilah, Sabtah, Raamah and Sabteka. The sons of Raamah: Sheba and Dedan. 8 Cush was the father of Nimrod, who became a mighty warrior on the earth. 9 He was a mighty hunter before the LORD; that is why it is said, “Like Nimrod, a mighty hunter before the LORD.” 10 The first centers of his kingdom were Babylon, Uruk, Akkad and Kalneh, in Shinar. 11 From that land he went to Assyria, where he built Nineveh, Rehoboth Ir, Calah 12 and Resen, which is between Nineveh and Calah — which is the great city.

13 Egypt was the father of the Ludites, Anamites, Lehabites, Naphtuhites, 14 Pathrusites, Kasluhites (from whom the Philistines came) and Caphtorites.

15 Canaan was the father of Sidon his firstborn, and of the Hittites, 16 Jebusites, Amorites, Girgashites, 17 Hivites, Arkites, Sinites, 18 Arvadites, Zemarites and Hamathites. Later the Canaanite clans scattered 19 and the borders of Canaan reached from Sidon toward Gerar as far as Gaza, and then toward Sodom, Gomorrah, Admah and Zeboyim, as far as Lasha. 20 These are the sons of Ham by their clans and languages, in their territories and nations.

Hebrew Word Study — Nimrod

נִמְרֹד Nimrod — Possibly from marad (“to rebel”), implying “We will rebel.” He is described as a gibbor (mighty man) — the same word used for the offspring of the sons of God in 6:4. He is a tsayid (hunter) — a word that in context may mean more than animal hunting; he hunts men, dominates peoples, builds empires.


Lifnei YHWH — “Before the LORD” (v.9). The phrase is ambiguous: it can mean “in the presence of / with the blessing of the LORD” (a positive reading), or it can be ironic — Nimrod’s prowess is displayed before the LORD who watches it with displeasure. Given that Nimrod founds Babylon (the great symbol of anti-God empire) and Nineveh (Israel’s great enemy), most read the repetition of “before the LORD” as pointed irony.

Historical Context — Nimrod & Empire

Nimrod is the first empire-builder in the Bible. His cities — Babylon (Babel), Uruk, Akkad, Nineveh — are the great power-cities of the ancient Near East. Uruk (biblical Erech) was one of the world’s first cities, home of Gilgamesh. Babylon would become the Babylonian Empire that destroyed Jerusalem in 586 BCE. Nineveh became the Assyrian capital that destroyed the Northern Kingdom in 722 BCE. Nimrod’s genealogy is the origin story of every empire that will oppress Israel.


Ham’s family also includes Egypt (Mizraim — the land of slavery), the Philistines (Israel’s persistent coastal enemies), the Hittites (a major ANE empire), Amorites, Jebusites (original inhabitants of Jerusalem), and Canaanites (the peoples Israel was called to displace). The narrative geography of Genesis 10 is the backstory to nearly every conflict in the rest of the OT.

📍 Places — Cities of Nimrod

🏛️
Babylon (Babel)
Central Iraq, near modern Hillah

The first and greatest of Nimrod’s cities; center of the Babylonian Empire. Excavated by Robert Koldewey (1899–1917), revealing the Ishtar Gate, massive ziggurats, and palaces. Babylon destroyed Jerusalem in 586 BCE and exiled the Jews — the defining trauma of OT history. In Revelation it becomes the symbolic name for the final anti-God empire.

🏛️
Uruk (Erech)
Southern Iraq, near modern Warka

One of the world’s first cities (c. 4000 BCE), home of the legendary king Gilgamesh. The Epic of Gilgamesh, containing the most detailed flood parallel to Genesis, originated here. Uruk may have had a population of 80,000 at its peak — enormous for the ancient world. Excavations have uncovered the world’s earliest writing systems here.

🏛️
Nineveh
Northern Iraq, near modern Mosul

Capital of the Assyrian Empire at its height (c. 700 BCE). Sennacherib built a “palace without rival” here. The Assyrians destroyed Israel’s Northern Kingdom in 722 BCE and besieged Jerusalem under Hezekiah (Isa 36–37). Jonah was sent to preach here. Nahum prophesied its destruction, which came in 612 BCE when the Babylonians sacked it.

🌊
Sidon
Modern Lebanon, coastal city

Firstborn of Canaan (v.15) — the great Phoenician port city. The Phoenicians (Canaanites) were the ancient world’s master sailors and traders, spreading their alphabet (ancestor of ours) across the Mediterranean. Sidon appears throughout the prophets as a symbol of coastal wealth and pride. Jesus visited its region (Matt 15:21) and cited it more favourably than Israel’s own cities (Luke 10:13–14).

The Line of Shem — Verses 21–32
21 Sons were also born to Shem, whose older brother was Japheth; Shem was the ancestor of all the sons of Eber.

22 The sons of Shem: Elam, Ashur, Arphaxad, Lud and Aram. 23 The sons of Aram: Uz, Hul, Gether and Meshek. 24 Arphaxad was the father of Shelah, and Shelah the father of Eber. 25 Two sons were born to Eber: One was named Peleg, because in his time the earth was divided; his brother was named Joktan. 26 Joktan was the father of Almodad, Sheleph, Hazarmaveth, Jerah, 27 Hadoram, Uzal, Diklah, 28 Obal, Abimael, Sheba, 29 Ophir, Havilah and Jobab. All these were sons of Joktan. 30 The region where they lived stretched from Mesha toward Sephar, in the eastern hill country. 31 These are the sons of Shem by their clans and languages, in their territories and nations.

32 These are the clans of Noah’s sons, according to their lines of descent, within their nations. From these the nations spread out over the earth after the flood.

Hebrew Word Study

Benei Ever — “Sons of Eber” (v.21). Shem is identified as the “ancestor of all the sons of Eber” — a remarkable designation that places Eber above Shem’s other descendants in significance. Ever is widely considered the root of the word Ivri — Hebrew. The Israelites are “the Hebrews” — literally the “Eber-ites,” the sons of Eber. The Table of Nations encodes the origin of Israel’s ethnic identity four generations before Abraham.


Peleg — “Division” (v.25). Ki be-yamav niflegah ha-aretz — “because in his time the earth was divided.” This almost certainly refers to the Babel dispersion of chapter 11, placed here as a proleptic note. The name Peleg dates the Babel event — it occurred in the generation of Eber’s sons.


Elam, Ashur, Aram — These become major players in OT history: Elam (southwestern Iran), Ashur (Assyria), Aram (Syria — the Arameans, whose language became the international language of the ancient world and the language Jesus spoke). The table is not merely historical but prophetic — it names the nations that will shape Israel’s entire future.

Historical Context — Shem’s Descendants

The Semitic family (Shem’s sons) covers the ancient Near East’s Semitic-language-speaking peoples: Elam (Elamites of southwestern Iran); Ashur (Assyrians); Arphaxad (Chaldeans / ancestors of Abraham); Lud (Lydians of western Anatolia); Aram (Arameans of Syria). Ophir (v.29) was a legendary source of gold (1 Kgs 9:28) — location disputed but possibly Yemen, Somalia, or India. Sheba (v.28) is the kingdom of the Queen of Sheba (1 Kgs 10) in southern Arabia or Ethiopia.


The Shem list is the most theologically significant because the covenant line runs through it: Shem → Arphaxad → Shelah → Eber → Peleg → (Genesis 11) → Terah → Abraham. The entire table is a funnel narrowing toward one man and one promise.

Deep Context — 70 Nations

Traditional Jewish counting gives 70 nations in the Table of Nations (Shem: 26; Ham: 30; Japheth: 14). The number 70 is not accidental — it recurs throughout Scripture as a symbol of completeness: 70 elders of Israel (Ex 24:9), 70 years of exile (Jer 25:11), 70 weeks of Daniel (Dan 9:24), Jesus sends out 70 (or 72) disciples (Luke 10:1). The Septuagint version of Deuteronomy 32:8 explicitly links the 70 nations to the 70 sons of Israel who went to Egypt (Gen 46:27) — God apportioned the nations according to the number of Israel. The Table of Nations is the universal canvas on which the particular story of Israel is painted.

Timeline — From Noah to Abraham

The covenant line runs Shem → Arphaxad → Shelah → Eber → Peleg → Reu → Serug → Nahor → Terah → Abraham (Gen 11:10–26). The Table of Nations is the universal context for this narrowing thread of promise.
