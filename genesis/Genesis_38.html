Genesis 38

Judah and Tamar

Hebrew

Timeline

Genesis 38 interrupts the Joseph narrative with the story of Judah and Tamar — a chapter whose placement is deliberate, not accidental. While Joseph is being established in Potiphar's house (the end of ch.37, continued in ch.39), Judah's story unfolds in Canaan. The contrast is pointed: Judah sinks into moral compromise among the Canaanites while Joseph maintains integrity in Egypt. Tamar's role produces Perez, an ancestor of David and of the Messiah (Matt 1:3).

Verses 1–11 — Judah's Sons; Tamar Left Widowed
1At that time, Judah left his brothers and went down to stay with a man of Adullam named Hirah.2There Judah met the daughter of a Canaanite man named Shua. He married her and made love to her;3she became pregnant and gave birth to a son, who was named Er.4She conceived again and gave birth to a son and named him Onan.5She gave birth to still another son and named him Shelah. It was at Kezib that she gave birth to him.6Judah got a wife for Er, his firstborn, and her name was Tamar.7But Er, Judah's firstborn, was wicked in the Lord's sight; so the Lord put him to death.8Then Judah said to Onan, "Sleep with your brother's wife and fulfill your duty to her as a brother-in-law to raise up offspring for your brother."9But Onan knew that the child would not be his; so whenever he slept with his brother's wife, he spilled his semen on the ground to keep from providing offspring for his brother.10What he did was wicked in the Lord's sight; so the Lord put him to death also.11Judah then said to his daughter-in-law Tamar, "Live as a widow in your father's household until my son Shelah grows up." For he thought, "He may die too, just like his brothers." So Tamar went to live in her father's household.

Hebrew Word Study

לֵוִירָת lēwîrāt — levirate duty: The institution of levirate marriage (from Latin levir, "brother-in-law") requires a man to marry his deceased brother's widow to produce offspring for the dead brother's name. Onan's refusal to fulfill this duty is his sin — self-interest over covenantal obligation.

רַע raʿ — wicked / evil: Er was "wicked (raʿ) in the sight of the LORD" — the same word used of the pre-flood generation (6:5). His specific sin is not named; his character is enough. The LORD's direct judgment (the same verb used in 6:7) shows this is not natural death.

Historical Context

Levirate marriage is attested in the Code of Hammurabi (§193) and Hittite law — it was a widespread ancient Near Eastern institution protecting widows and preserving property lines. The later Mosaic law (Deut 25:5–10) codifies a practice already present in the patriarchal period. Judah's marriage to a Canaanite woman reflects the danger of cultural assimilation that the Joseph narrative is partly designed to resolve — Joseph in Egypt maintains covenant identity; Judah in Canaan loses it.

Context

Chapter 38 is placed precisely here to provide contrast with Joseph (ch.39). Judah descends (v.1: "went down") from his brothers and from covenant integrity simultaneously. His marriage to a Canaanite, his failure to protect Tamar, his hypocrisy in condemning her — all mark his moral nadir. But the chapter is also a story of Judah's exposure and the beginning of his reformation, which will climax in his great speech of ch.44. Tamar is the active agent of covenant faithfulness — she ensures the line continues when Judah will not.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
38:7,10

The LORD puts Er and Onan to death — two direct divine judgments within three verses. This is unusual in Genesis and underlines the seriousness of the covenant obligation being violated. Er's unnamed wickedness and Onan's named sin (deliberately preventing conception to avoid levirate duty) both represent failures of covenant obligation serious enough to warrant divine execution. The LORD is actively involved in the continuity of the covenant line.

38:1

Judah's "going down" is both geographical and moral. He leaves his brothers, marries outside the covenant, and progressively loses his moral bearings. The man who proposed selling Joseph (37:26) rather than killing him — a compromise that seemed relatively merciful — now embarks on a trajectory of compromise that will eventually require Tamar to expose him publicly before he can recover.

Verses 12–30 — Tamar's Deception; the Birth of Perez and Zerah
12After a long time Judah's wife, the daughter of Shua, died. When Judah had recovered from his grief, he went up to Timnah, to the men who were shearing his sheep, and his friend Hirah the Adullamite went with him.13When Tamar was told, "Your father-in-law is on his way to Timnah to shear his sheep,"14she took off her widow's clothes, covered herself with a veil to disguise herself, and then sat down at the entrance to Enaim, which is on the road to Timnah. For she saw that, though Shelah had now grown up, she had not been given to him as his wife.15When Judah saw her, he thought she was a prostitute, for she had covered her face.16Not realizing that she was his daughter-in-law, he went over to her by the roadside and said, "Come now, let me sleep with you." "And what will you give me to sleep with you?" she asked.17"I'll send you a young goat from my flock," he said. "Will you give me something as a pledge until you send it?" she asked.18He said, "What pledge should I give you?" "Your seal and its cord, and the staff in your hand," she answered. So he gave them to her and slept with her, and she became pregnant by him.19After she left, she took off her veil and put on her widow's clothes again.20Meanwhile Judah sent the young goat by his friend the Adullamite in order to get his pledge back from the woman, but he did not find her.21He asked the men who lived there, "Where is the shrine prostitute who was beside the road at Enaim?" "There hasn't been any shrine prostitute here," they said.22So he went back to Judah and said, "I didn't find her. Besides, the men who lived there said, 'There hasn't been any shrine prostitute here.'"23Then Judah said, "Let her keep what she has, or we will become a laughingstock. After all, I did send her this young goat, but you didn't find her."24About three months later Judah was told, "Your daughter-in-law Tamar is guilty of prostitution, and as a result she is now pregnant." Judah said, "Bring her out and have her burned to death!"25As she was being brought out, she sent a message to her father-in-law. "I am pregnant by the man who owns these," she said. And she added, "See if you recognize whose seal and cord and staff these are."26Judah recognized them and said, "She is more righteous than I, since I wouldn't give her to my son Shelah." And he did not sleep with her again.27When the time came for her to give birth, there were twin boys in her womb.28As she was giving birth, one of them put out his hand; so the midwife took a scarlet thread and tied it on his wrist and said, "This one came out first."29But when he drew back his hand, his brother came out, and she said, "So this is how you have broken out!" And he was named Perez.30Then his brother, who had the scarlet thread on his wrist, came out. And he was named Zerah.

Hebrew Word Study

צָדְקָה מִמֶּנִּי ṣādĕqāh mimmennî — she is more righteous than I: Judah's confession is one of the most remarkable statements in Genesis. He acknowledges not just his sin but that Tamar acted with greater covenant faithfulness than he did. The comparative (mimmennî, "than I") makes it absolute.

פֶּרֶץ pereṣ — Perez: Means "breaking out" — Perez pushes through first in the twin birth despite his brother's hand appearing first. He becomes an ancestor of Boaz (Ruth 4:18–22), David, and the Messiah (Matt 1:3).

Context

Tamar is the moral hero of ch.38. She is the victim of Judah's failure to give her to Shelah; she acts to ensure the covenant line continues when the authorized person will not. Her method is morally complex (she disguises herself as a prostitute), but her motive is covenant faithfulness — she is doing what the levirate institution requires, even if Judah will not. Judah's judgment on her ("let her be burned") is immediately exposed as rank hypocrisy when the pledges are produced. His confession ("she is more righteous than I") is the moral turning point of Judah's character arc.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 1:3Tamar appears in the Messianic genealogy — one of four women mentioned by Matthew, each associated with unexpected grace and irregular situations.
  • Ruth 4:12The people of Bethlehem bless Boaz and Ruth: "may your house be like the house of Perez, whom Tamar bore to Judah." Tamar's legacy as a founding mother is celebrated.
  • Gen 44:18–34Judah's great intercession for Benjamin — the fruit of the transformation that begins with his confession in v.26.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
38:24–26

Judah's hypocrisy is perfectly exposed: the man who proposes burning Tamar for immorality is the father of her child. When the pledges are produced, he has only one response: "She is more righteous than I." This is the moment Judah's redemption begins. The man who callously sold his brother into slavery, who failed Tamar repeatedly, who pronounced death on a woman he himself had wronged — this man, at this moment, tells the truth about himself. That confession is the seed of the Judah who will offer himself as a slave in Benjamin's place in chapter 44.

38:29

Perez — "breaking out" — becomes the messianic line. The irregular, scandalous, unexpected birth of Perez is woven directly into the genealogy of the Messiah (Matt 1:3). God's purposes do not require clean human situations. He works through Tamar's desperate covenant faithfulness, Judah's confessed sin, and a twin birth with a tangled scarlet thread to produce the lineage of the King of kings.

Ancient Sources

Ruth 4:12,18–22
The genealogy of Boaz traces directly through Perez — "the family of Perez, whom Tamar bore to Judah." Tamar's legacy is honored in the book of Ruth.
Matthew's genealogy (Matt 1:3) includes Tamar by name — one of five women in the list, each representing unexpected grace.
Jubilees 41
Jubilees treats Judah's sin severely and explains the deaths of Er and Onan more explicitly as punishment for specific sins against Tamar.
Jubilees reflects a tradition that fills in the narrative gaps of Genesis 38 with interpretive material.

Reception History

Matthew 1:3
Tamar's inclusion in the Messianic genealogy by Matthew signals that God's grace operates through the unexpected, the marginalized, and the scandalous — the genealogy is a catalog of divine faithfulness in difficult human situations.
Calvin, Commentary
Calvin reads Tamar as an example of extraordinary faith — she trusted that God's covenant purposes would be fulfilled through Judah's line and acted to ensure it, even at great personal risk.

Literary Structure

Descent — vv.1–5Judah goes down; Canaanite marriage; three sons
Crisis — vv.6–11Er dies; Onan dies; Shelah withheld from Tamar
Exposure — vv.12–23Tamar acts; Judah unwittingly fathers her child
Confession — vv.24–26Judah's hypocrisy exposed; "she is more righteous than I"
Resolution — vv.27–30Twin birth; Perez breaks out — the messianic line secured
The chapter traces a complete arc: Judah's descent, a crisis of covenant obligation, Tamar's faithful action, Judah's exposure and confession, and the birth of the messianic ancestor. The placement between chs.37 and 39 creates a deliberate contrast: Judah compromises with Canaanite women; Joseph resists an Egyptian woman.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 38 is the dark backdrop against which Joseph's integrity shines brighter. But it is also the story of God's sovereign grace working through scandal, confession, and an unlikely birth to secure the Messianic line. Tamar's covenant faithfulness — however irregular its expression — produces Perez, ancestor of the King of kings.
