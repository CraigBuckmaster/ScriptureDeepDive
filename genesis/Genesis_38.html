Genesis 38

Judah and Tamar

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–11 — Judah's Sons; Tamar Left Widowed
1At that time, Judah left his brothers and went down to stay with a man of Adullam named Hirah.2There Judah met the daughter of a Canaanite man named Shua. He married her and made love to her;3she became pregnant and gave birth to a son, who was named Er.4She conceived again and gave birth to a son and named him Onan.5She gave birth to still another son and named him Shelah. It was at Kezib that she gave birth to him.6Judah got a wife for Er, his firstborn, and her name was Tamar.7But Er, Judah's firstborn, was wicked in the Lord's sight; so the Lord put him to death.8Then Judah said to Onan, "Sleep with your brother's wife and fulfill your duty to her as a brother-in-law to raise up offspring for your brother."9But Onan knew that the child would not be his; so whenever he slept with his brother's wife, he spilled his semen on the ground to keep from providing offspring for his brother.10What he did was wicked in the Lord's sight; so the Lord put him to death also.11Judah then said to his daughter-in-law Tamar, "Live as a widow in your father's household until my son Shelah grows up." For he thought, "He may die too, just like his brothers." So Tamar went to live in her father's household.

Hebrew Word Study

לֵוִירָת lēwîrāt — levirate duty: The institution of levirate marriage (from Latin levir, "brother-in-law") requires a man to marry his deceased brother's widow to produce offspring for the dead brother's name. Onan's refusal to fulfill this duty is his sin — self-interest over covenantal obligation.

רַע raʿ — wicked / evil: Er was "wicked (raʿ) in the sight of the LORD" — the same word used of the pre-flood generation (6:5). His specific sin is not named; his character is enough. The LORD's direct judgment (the same verb used in 6:7) shows this is not natural death.

Historical Context

Levirate marriage — the obligation of a man to marry his deceased brother's widow to produce an heir for the dead brother — is formalised in Deuteronomy 25:5-10 but is clearly a pre-Mosaic custom here. The institution preserves the name and inheritance of the dead man in his family. Er and Onan's deaths are both attributed directly to divine judgment ("wicked in the LORD's sight"), which is unusual in Genesis. Onan's sin is not primarily contraception in general but the specific refusal to fulfil the levirate duty toward his dead brother.

Context

Chapter 38 is placed precisely here to provide contrast with Joseph (ch.39). Judah descends (v.1: "went down") from his brothers and from covenant integrity simultaneously. His marriage to a Canaanite, his failure to protect Tamar, his hypocrisy in condemning her — all mark his moral nadir. But the chapter is also a story of Judah's exposure and the beginning of his reformation, which will climax in his great speech of ch.44. Tamar is the active agent of covenant faithfulness — she ensures the line continues when Judah will not.

Cross-Reference

  • Deut 25:5-10The Mosaic levirate law — formalising the custom Tamar invokes.
  • Ruth 4:1-10Boaz as kinsman-redeemer — the same duty Onan refused, Boaz fulfils.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
38:1-5

Judah's separation from his brothers and his marriage to a Canaanite woman is the first step of an assimilation that chapter 38 will interrupt and reverse. The man who sold Joseph into Egypt now goes down among the Canaanites, takes a Canaanite wife, and has three sons with Canaanite names. If the trajectory continues, Judah will be absorbed into Canaan. Tamar will prevent this.

38:7

Er's wickedness is unnamed — the text says simply "he was wicked in the LORD's sight, so the LORD put him to death." The spare brevity is the narrative's own judgment: nothing else needs to be said. The same formula will apply to Onan (v.10). Two of Judah's three sons receive direct divine judgment. The family of the man who sold his brother is not flourishing.

38:9-10

Onan's sin is specifically the refusal of levirate duty — he takes the marriage (and the conjugal rights) but withholds the heir. He "spilled his seed" not as a general method of contraception but specifically to deprive his dead brother of an heir. The wickedness the LORD sees is the exploitation of the widow's sexual availability while denying her the social protection and inheritance she is owed. Onan uses Tamar while refusing to serve her.

38:11

Judah's dismissal of Tamar to her father's house, promising Shelah when he comes of age, is a stall that becomes a deception. Judah has no intention of giving Shelah to Tamar — he fears Shelah will die too (v.11). The widow is left without a husband, without a child, and without a credible prospect of either. Tamar will take matters into her own hands.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
38:1

The chapter interruption of the Joseph narrative by the Judah-Tamar episode is deliberate, not accidental. Sarna: "The placement of chapter 38 between the sale of Joseph (ch.37) and Joseph's arrival at Potiphar's house (ch.39) is a literary stratagem. The time-gap implied in the Judah story corresponds to the years Joseph spends in Potiphar's house and in prison. When the narrative returns to Joseph in ch.39, considerable time has passed — time accounted for by the Judah episode."

38:9

Onan's act — spilling on the ground so as not to provide offspring for his brother — is precisely characterised by Sarna as violation of the levirate principle: "The offense was not the act itself but the fraudulent use of Tamar, enjoying the privileges of marriage while refusing the obligations. He deprived Tamar of her legal rights and insulted the memory of his dead brother."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
38:1

The interruption of the Joseph narrative is one of the most discussed structural decisions in Genesis. Alter: "The Judah-Tamar story is not a digression from the Joseph story; it is its counterpoint. Joseph in Egypt is resisting sexual temptation (ch.39) while Judah in Canaan is succumbing to it (ch.38). The two stories run simultaneously and comment on each other. What Joseph is, Judah is not — yet."

38:11

Judah sends Tamar back to her father's house "until my son Shelah grows up." Alter notes the narrative gap opened here: we know Judah has no intention of giving Shelah to Tamar. The reader waits for the inevitable collision between Judah's self-protective lie and Tamar's legitimate claim. The chapter builds tension from the moment Judah speaks.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
38:7

Calvin on divine judgment of Er and Onan: "Moses does not mention what was the character of their wickedness, but simply states that they died for their sin. For God sometimes cuts off reprobates in the flower of their age, before they have had the time to commit many crimes, as an example to others." The unnamed wickedness is sufficient for Calvin: God's judgment makes the point without requiring details.

38:9-10

Calvin on Onan: "He not only defrauded his brother of the right due to him, but he also despised the command of God... It is a horrible thing to pour out seed besides the intercourse of man and woman... and doubly horrible when the deed is accompanied by fraud in defrauding the brother of his right." Calvin's condemnation is strong; the specific gravity is the levirate fraud, not contraception as such.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
38:9

The NET note on Onan carefully distinguishes between contraception generally and the specific violation here. The note explains levirate marriage (from Latin levir = husband's brother) and Onan's breach of its terms. The note observes that the sin for which God judges Onan is specifically the refusal to raise up offspring for his brother, not contraceptive technique in general. The note flags that the passage has been widely misapplied in history to condemn all non-procreative sexual acts.

38:11

The NET note on Judah's dismissal of Tamar notes the legal ambiguity: Judah has technically fulfilled his duty by promising Shelah, but his internal motivation (fear Shelah will die too, v.11b) reveals he has no intention of fulfilling the promise. Tamar is legally bound to the family but practically abandoned. The note observes that her subsequent action (vv.14-18) is a response to real legal injustice, not mere transgression.

Verses 12–30 — Tamar's Deception; the Birth of Perez and Zerah
12After a long time Judah's wife, the daughter of Shua, died. When Judah had recovered from his grief, he went up to Timnah, to the men who were shearing his sheep, and his friend Hirah the Adullamite went with him.13When Tamar was told, "Your father-in-law is on his way to Timnah to shear his sheep,"14she took off her widow's clothes, covered herself with a veil to disguise herself, and then sat down at the entrance to Enaim, which is on the road to Timnah. For she saw that, though Shelah had now grown up, she had not been given to him as his wife.15When Judah saw her, he thought she was a prostitute, for she had covered her face.16Not realizing that she was his daughter-in-law, he went over to her by the roadside and said, "Come now, let me sleep with you." "And what will you give me to sleep with you?" she asked.17"I'll send you a young goat from my flock," he said. "Will you give me something as a pledge until you send it?" she asked.18He said, "What pledge should I give you?" "Your seal and its cord, and the staff in your hand," she answered. So he gave them to her and slept with her, and she became pregnant by him.19After she left, she took off her veil and put on her widow's clothes again.20Meanwhile Judah sent the young goat by his friend the Adullamite in order to get his pledge back from the woman, but he did not find her.21He asked the men who lived there, "Where is the shrine prostitute who was beside the road at Enaim?" "There hasn't been any shrine prostitute here," they said.22So he went back to Judah and said, "I didn't find her. Besides, the men who lived there said, 'There hasn't been any shrine prostitute here.'"23Then Judah said, "Let her keep what she has, or we will become a laughingstock. After all, I did send her this young goat, but you didn't find her."24About three months later Judah was told, "Your daughter-in-law Tamar is guilty of prostitution, and as a result she is now pregnant." Judah said, "Bring her out and have her burned to death!"25As she was being brought out, she sent a message to her father-in-law. "I am pregnant by the man who owns these," she said. And she added, "See if you recognize whose seal and cord and staff these are."26Judah recognized them and said, "She is more righteous than I, since I wouldn't give her to my son Shelah." And he did not sleep with her again.27When the time came for her to give birth, there were twin boys in her womb.28As she was giving birth, one of them put out his hand; so the midwife took a scarlet thread and tied it on his wrist and said, "This one came out first."29But when he drew back his hand, his brother came out, and she said, "So this is how you have broken out!" And he was named Perez.30Then his brother, who had the scarlet thread on his wrist, came out. And he was named Zerah.

Hebrew Word Study

צָדְקָה מִמֶּנִּי ṣādĕqāh mimmennî — she is more righteous than I: Judah's confession is one of the most remarkable statements in Genesis. He acknowledges not just his sin but that Tamar acted with greater covenant faithfulness than he did. The comparative (mimmennî, "than I") makes it absolute.

פֶּרֶץ pereṣ — Perez: Means "breaking out" — Perez pushes through first in the twin birth despite his brother's hand appearing first. He becomes an ancestor of Boaz (Ruth 4:18–22), David, and the Messiah (Matt 1:3).

Historical Context

Tamar's disguise as a prostitute (qedeshah — a cult prostitute or simply a prostitute at a crossroads shrine) and Judah's use of her services represent a morally complex situation the narrative evaluates carefully. Judah's verdict — "she is more righteous than I" — is one of the most remarkable moral pronouncements in Genesis. He acknowledges that Tamar's deception was justified by his failure to give her Shelah. Her righteousness is relative (she acted to secure what was legally hers) and comparative (Judah had wronged her first).

Context

Tamar is the moral hero of ch.38. She is the victim of Judah's failure to give her to Shelah; she acts to ensure the covenant line continues when the authorized person will not. Her method is morally complex (she disguises herself as a prostitute), but her motive is covenant faithfulness — she is doing what the levirate institution requires, even if Judah will not. Judah's judgment on her ("let her be burned") is immediately exposed as rank hypocrisy when the pledges are produced. His confession ("she is more righteous than I") is the moral turning point of Judah's character arc.

Cross-Reference

  • Matt 1:3Tamar appears in the Messianic genealogy — one of four women mentioned by Matthew, each associated with unexpected grace and irregular situations.
  • Ruth 4:12The people of Bethlehem bless Boaz and Ruth: "may your house be like the house of Perez, whom Tamar bore to Judah." Tamar's legacy as a founding mother is celebrated.
  • Gen 44:18–34Judah's great intercession for Benjamin — the fruit of the transformation that begins with his confession in v.26.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
38:14-15

Tamar's disguise is calculated and effective. She covers her face (as a prostitute would) and positions herself at the entrance to Enaim on the road Judah will travel. The plan requires courage and desperation in equal measure — she is risking her life (as v.24 will show: Judah orders her burned when he hears she is pregnant). The narrative does not approve her deception but it explains it: she had no other recourse within the legal system.

38:24-26

The scene of Judah ordering Tamar burned and then receiving his own pledge items — "she is more righteous than I" — is one of the great reversals in the Bible. The man who said "bring her out and have her burned" is the man who made her pregnant. His acknowledgment of guilt is immediate and complete: "she is more righteous than I, since I withheld my son Shelah from her." Judah, who was unrecognised while he sinned, now recognises himself in his own seal and staff.

38:27-30

The birth of Perez and Zerah — with the reversal of who emerges first, echoing the Jacob-Esau birth — brings the chapter to a close. Perez (meaning "breaking through") is in the line of David and of Jesus Christ (Matt 1:3). The illegitimate son of Judah and Tamar, born through deception, becomes an ancestor of the Messiah. Providence is not fastidious about the lineage it uses.

38:26

Judah's final statement — "and he did not sleep with her again" — closes the episode with clean clarity. The relationship is not romanticised or continued; it was the specific means by which Tamar secured her legal right. Having secured it, the episode ends. The chapter is not about adultery or prostitution; it is about a woman fighting for the legal standing she was owed.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
38:26

Tsadeqah mimmenni — "she is more righteous than I." Sarna calls this "one of the most remarkably honest self-judgments in the Bible." The Hebrew tsadeqah is the covenant vocabulary of righteousness — used for legal standing, for doing what is right within the covenant relationship. Tamar is more righteous because she was acting to fulfil the legal obligation Judah had evaded. Judah's acknowledgment is not merely personal honesty but a theological verdict: covenant obligations matter.

38:29

The birth of Perez precedes Zerah despite Zerah's hand appearing first. Sarna notes the echo of the Jacob-Esau birth reversal: again the "firstborn" status belongs to the one who breaks through at the last moment. Perez (paretz = breach, breaking through) is named for the act that makes him first. In Matthew 1:3, Perez is in the direct lineage to Jesus. The ancestral line of the Messiah runs through a birth as contested and reversed as its greatest patriarch's.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
38:14-18

The negotiation between Judah and the disguised Tamar is handled with cool efficiency. Alter notes the irony: Judah haggles over the pledge, accepts her terms, and leaves his seal, cord, and staff — the most personally identifying objects a man possessed in the ancient world. He has, unknowingly, given Tamar everything she needs to prove his paternity. The pledge that was meant to be temporary becomes the evidence in a trial he will not be able to escape.

38:25-26

The scene of recognition — Tamar sending the pledge items to Judah with the message "the man to whom these belong made me pregnant" — is handled with restraint. Alter: "She does not name him; she lets the objects speak. Judah recognises them; the recognition triggers the moral verdict. The same seal and staff that identified him to the prostitute now identifies him to the court." The objects are the chapter's moral instrument, as the coat was in ch.37.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
38:14-15

Calvin defends Tamar's action with characteristic nuance: "I do not excuse her dissimulation; but because the design of Tamar was not to gratify her lust, but to revive the seed of her deceased husband, she is acquitted... She did not act from lust, but from a pious desire to raise up seed to her husband, which was a holy intention, though the means were illicit." Calvin distinguishes motive (legitimate) from method (illicit). Providence used both.

38:26

Calvin on Judah's verdict: "Judah is not to be commended for having been the author of this incest, but because he honestly declares, though to his own shame, that Tamar had done nothing more than reclaim her right. This candour, nevertheless, deserves praise; that when the cause was perceived, he was not ashamed to pass sentence against himself." Honesty before God and others, even when it costs, is always a virtue in Calvin's reading.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
38:25

The NET note on "identify whose seal and cord and staff these are" explains the legal force of Tamar's presentation. The seal (hotam) was a cylinder or signet used to authenticate documents — the most personally identifying object in the ancient world. The note observes that Tamar's demand for these objects as a pledge was strategically perfect: she asked for the one thing that could not be denied or duplicated.

38:29

The NET note on Perez and Zerah discusses the birth-order reversal and its literary echo of Jacob and Esau. The note observes that Perez appears in both Davidic genealogies (Ruth 4:18-22) and the Matthean genealogy of Jesus (Matt 1:3), making this chapter — despite its moral complexity — essential to the messianic lineage. The note observes that Matthew includes four women in his genealogy (Tamar, Rahab, Ruth, Bathsheba), all of whom are involved in morally complex situations.

Ancient Sources

Ruth 4:12,18–22
The genealogy of Boaz traces directly through Perez — "the family of Perez, whom Tamar bore to Judah." Tamar's legacy is honored in the book of Ruth.
Matthew's genealogy (Matt 1:3) includes Tamar by name — one of five women in the list, each representing unexpected grace.
Jubilees 41
Jubilees treats Judah's sin severely and explains the deaths of Er and Onan more explicitly as punishment for specific sins against Tamar.
Jubilees reflects a tradition that fills in the narrative gaps of Genesis 38 with interpretive material.

Reception History

Matthew 1:3
Tamar's inclusion in the Messianic genealogy by Matthew signals that God's grace operates through the unexpected, the marginalized, and the scandalous — the genealogy is a catalog of divine faithfulness in difficult human situations.
Calvin, Commentary
Calvin reads Tamar as an example of extraordinary faith — she trusted that God's covenant purposes would be fulfilled through Judah's line and acted to ensure it, even at great personal risk.

Literary Structure

Judah's Descent — 38:1-11Judah separates, marries Canaanite, loses two sons to divine judgment. Tamar widowed twice, dismissed.
Judah's wife dies. Tamar disguises as prostitute. Judah takes the pledge items that will identify him.
Recognition — 38:24-26"She is more righteous than I." Judah's self-judgment. The seal convicts its owner.
Perez and Zerah — 38:27-30Birth reversal echoes Jacob/Esau. Perez breaks through — ancestor of David and of Christ.
Genesis 38 is the counterpoint to Joseph's story: while Joseph resists temptation in Egypt, Judah falls into it in Canaan. But the chapter's deeper logic is legal justice: Tamar is a woman fighting within a system that has failed her. Her deception is met with Judah's honest self-judgment — "she is more righteous than I." Through this morally complex lineage, the messianic line runs: Perez, ancestor of David, ancestor of Jesus.

Hebrew-Rooted Reading

38:26 צָדְקָה מִמֶּנִּי tsadeqah mimmenni — she is more righteous than I (tsadeqah = covenant righteousness; Judah's most honest self-judgment; the first sign of his transformation)

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 38 interrupts the Joseph narrative to show what is happening to the covenant line in Judah's branch: assimilation, judgment, deception, and ultimately honest self-reckoning. Judah's "she is more righteous than I" is the first step in the moral transformation that will be complete in Genesis 44. The chapter also establishes the messianic lineage through Perez — Providence using the most morally complex circumstances to advance its most important purpose.
