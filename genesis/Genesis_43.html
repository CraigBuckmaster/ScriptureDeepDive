Genesis 43

Judah's Pledge; the Brothers Return to Egypt

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–15 — Judah's Pledge; Jacob Releases Benjamin
1Now the famine was still severe in the land.2So when they had eaten all the grain they had brought from Egypt, their father said to them, "Go back and buy us a little more food."3But Judah said to him, "The man warned us solemnly, 'You will not see my face again unless your brother is with you.'4If you will send our brother along with us, we will go down and buy food for you.5But if you will not send him, we will not go down, because the man said to us, 'You will not see my face again unless your brother is with you.'"6Israel asked, "Why did you bring this trouble on me by telling the man you had another brother?"7They replied, "The man questioned us closely about ourselves and our family. 'Is your father still living?' he asked us. 'Do you have another brother?' We simply answered his questions. How were we to know he would say, 'Bring your brother down here'?"8Then Judah said to Israel his father, "Send the boy along with me and we will go at once, so that we and you and our children may live and not die.9I myself will guarantee his safety; you can hold me personally responsible for him. If I do not bring him back to you and set him here before you, I will bear the blame before you all my life.10As it is, if we had not delayed, we could have gone and returned twice."11Then their father Israel said to them, "If it must be, then do this: Put some of the best products of the land in your bags and take them down to the man as a gift—a little balm and a little honey, some spices and myrrh, some pistachio nuts and almonds.12Take double the amount of silver with you, for you must return the silver that was put back in the mouths of your sacks. Perhaps it was a mistake.13Take your brother also and go back to the man at once.14And may God Almighty grant you mercy before the man so that he will let your other brother and Benjamin come back with you. As for me, if I am bereaved, I am bereaved."15So the men took the gifts and double the amount of silver, and Benjamin also. They hurried down to Egypt and presented themselves to Joseph.

Hebrew Word Study

אֶעֶרְבֶנּוּ ʾeʿerebennû — I will be a pledge / surety: Judah offers personal covenant-surety for Benjamin. The same word undergirds redemptive-obligation language in Ruth 4. This is the beginning of Judah's transformation into the substitute-redeemer who will offer himself in ch.44.

אֵל שַׁדַּי ʾēl šadday — God Almighty: Jacob invokes the covenant name from Bethel (35:11) and Abraham (17:1). In his moment of surrender, he falls back on the only sufficient ground.

שָׁכַלְתִּי šākalti — bereaved: Jacob's resignation — "if I am bereaved, I am bereaved" — is the language of Naomi and Job: profound submission to sovereignty expressed in acceptance of the worst.

Historical Context

The resumption of the Egypt mission, months after the first (the famine is severe; they have eaten the grain brought back), is delayed by Jacob's refusal to release Benjamin. The stalemate is broken by Judah's offer to be personally responsible for Benjamin — a surety arrangement that is legally specific in the ancient world. Judah's offer in chapter 43 anticipates his fuller offer in chapter 44 where he proposes himself as a substitute slave. The transformation of Judah from the man who proposed selling Joseph (37:26-27) to the man who offers to die for Benjamin is the most significant character development in the Joseph cycle.

Context

The shift from Reuben's offer (42:37: "put both my sons to death") to Judah's offer here is the moral development the narrative requires. Reuben's pledge was excessive and self-focused; Judah's is personal and covenantal. Jacob's "if I am bereaved, I am bereaved" is his Peniel-trained surrender — the patriarch who wrestled with God can now release his last connection to Rachel into God's hands.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 42:37Reuben's offer: take my sons. Judah's offer: take me. The moral distance between the two reveals genuine growth.
  • Gen 44:33Judah's pledge here is redeemed in ch.44: he offers himself as slave in Benjamin's place — the surety becomes the substitute.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
43:1-5

The famine forces what Jacob's grief could not avoid: Benjamin must go to Egypt. The stalemate between Jacob's protective fear and the family's survival need is broken by the famine itself. Providence uses necessity to accomplish what persuasion could not. Jacob's resistance is real and loving; but the family cannot eat on the strength of his love for Benjamin. Reality is God's tool as much as dreams and angels.

43:8-10

Judah's offer is the chapter's pivot — and the Joseph cycle's central moral development. The man who said "what will we gain if we kill our brother? Let's sell him instead" (37:26) now says "I will be his surety; if I do not bring him back to you safely, let me bear the blame before you all my life." The contrast is total: Judah moves from calculation to sacrifice, from self-interest to self-substitution. The transformation is not sudden; it has been growing since 38:26.

43:11-14

Jacob's response — sending gifts, double the silver, and Benjamin — is accompanied by the most theologically honest prayer in the Jacob narrative: "May God Almighty grant you mercy before the man so that he will let your other brother and Benjamin come back with you. As for me, if I am bereaved, I am bereaved." The final clause — "if I am bereaved, I am bereaved" — is the language of faith that holds grief and trust simultaneously.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
43:8-10

Judah's surety offer — ani e'ervennah miyaday tevaqshennuh, "I will be personally responsible for him; you can hold me accountable" — uses the legal vocabulary of a personal guarantee. Sarna: "The word erev (surety/guarantee) appears in commercial and legal contexts (Prov 11:15; 17:18; 22:26). Judah is invoking a formal legal obligation — not merely a personal promise but a bindingly enforceable commitment. He stakes his entire future on Benjamin's safe return."

43:14

El Shaddai yitten lachem rachamim — "May God Almighty grant you mercy." Sarna notes Jacob invokes El Shaddai — the covenant name of Genesis 17 and 35:11. The same divine name used for the covenant promises is now invoked in a moment of anguished surrender. Jacob knows he is releasing Benjamin into an uncertain situation; his prayer is not confident but it is theologically oriented. "If I am bereaved, I am bereaved" is not resignation but trust.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
43:8-10

The contrast between Reuben's surety offer (42:37: "put both my sons to death if I do not bring him back") and Judah's (43:9: "I myself will be surety for him; from my hand you shall require him") reveals the difference in moral weight. Alter: "Reuben's offer sacrifices his sons; Judah's sacrifices himself. Reuben is still thinking in terms of the family economy — he offers what costs others; Judah offers what costs him."

43:14

"And if I am bereaved of my children, I am bereaved." Alter notes this is Jacob's most theologically mature statement in the entire Genesis narrative. The man who has spent twenty-two years in inconsolable grief (37:35) now utters a sentence that combines honest fear with acceptance of God's sovereignty. The verb bereaved is sharp; the acceptance is real. Something has shifted in Jacob — perhaps the wrestling at Peniel is bearing fruit this late.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
43:9-10

Calvin on Judah's surety: "Judah offers himself as security; for he not only promises, but also puts himself in the place of Benjamin, and is willing to be judged and condemned if he shall return without his brother. This was the part of a brave and faithful man... We see from this example how much the Spirit of God avails for exciting the virtue of men at the right time."

43:14

Calvin on Jacob's "if I am bereaved, I am bereaved": "This is the language of one who, though oppressed with grief, yet ceases not to hope in God. He does not give himself up to despair; he acknowledges that whatever happens will be of God... Let us learn from this example, that even when God seems to be against us, we should still hope in his mercy." Calvin reads the clause as trust-within-grief, not despair.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
43:9

The NET note on "I myself will be surety for him" discusses the legal vocabulary of aravon (surety/guarantee) in the ancient Near Eastern context. The note cites Proverbs 22:26 and notes that surety arrangements were formal legal instruments. Judah is not offering a casual promise but a legally binding personal guarantee — his own freedom and future as collateral for Benjamin's safety.

43:14

The NET note on "if I am bereaved, I am bereaved" (va'asher shakholti shakholti) discusses the doubled verb construction as emphatic — the same device used for Lamech's vengeance (4:24) and the burning bush (Exod 3:14). The note reads the statement as theological acceptance: "whatever happens is in God's hands." The note connects to Esther 4:16 ("if I perish, I perish") as a parallel expression of committed surrender in a dangerous situation.

Verses 16–34 — Joseph Hosts His Brothers; Benjamin's Five Portions
16When Joseph saw Benjamin with them, he said to the steward of his house, "Take these men to my house, slaughter an animal and prepare a meal; they are to eat with me at noon."17The man did as Joseph told him and took the men to Joseph's house.18Now the men were frightened when they were taken to his house. They thought, "We were brought here because of the silver that was put back in our sacks the first time. He wants to attack us and overpower us and seize us as slaves and take our donkeys."19So they went up to Joseph's steward and spoke to him at the entrance to the house.20"We beg your pardon, our lord," they said, "we came down here the first time to buy food.21But at the place where we stopped for the night we opened our sacks and each of us found his silver—the exact weight—in the mouth of his sack. So we have brought it back with us.22We have also brought additional silver with us to buy food. We don't know who put our silver in our sacks."23"It's all right," he said. "Don't be afraid. Your God, the God of your father, has given you treasure in your sacks; I received your silver." Then he brought Simeon out to them.24The steward took the men into Joseph's house, gave them water to wash their feet and provided fodder for their donkeys.25They prepared their gifts for Joseph's arrival at noon, because they had heard that they were to eat there.26When Joseph came home, they presented to him the gifts they had brought into the house, and they bowed down before him to the ground.27He asked them how they were, and then he said, "How is your aged father you told me about? Is he still living?"28They replied, "Your servant our father is still alive and well." And they bowed down, prostrating themselves before him.29As he looked about and saw his brother Benjamin, his own mother's son, he asked, "Is this your youngest brother, the one you told me about?" And he said, "God be gracious to you, my son."30Deeply moved at the sight of his brother, Joseph hurried out and looked for a place to weep. He went into his private room and wept there.31After he had washed his face, he came out and, controlling himself, said, "Serve the food."32They served him by himself, the brothers by themselves, and the Egyptians who ate with him by themselves, because Egyptians could not eat with Hebrews, for that is detestable to Egyptians.33The men had been seated before him in the order of their ages, from the firstborn to the youngest; and they looked at each other in astonishment.34When portions were served to them from Joseph's table, Benjamin's portion was five times as much as anyone else's. So they feasted and drank freely with him.

Hebrew Word Study

כִּי נִכְמְרוּ רַחֲמָיו kî nikmerû raḥamāyw — his compassion grew warm: Literally "his bowels/womb-feelings were hot" — raḥamîm from reḥem (womb). The most visceral, maternal tenderness. Joseph's love for Benjamin is overwhelming and physical.

וַיִּתְאַפַּק wayyitʾappaq — controlling himself: He restrains himself with enormous effort. The disclosure is not yet time; the test must continue. The internal cost of the restraint is visible in the text.

Historical Context

Joseph's host at his own house is an extraordinary act of hospitality in the ancient world: a royal official hosting foreign visitors at his personal residence. The brothers' fear — that they are being set up for punishment because of the returned silver — reflects the legitimate anxiety of foreigners in a foreign legal system. The steward's reassurance ("your God and the God of your father has given you treasure in your sacks") is theologically sophisticated for an Egyptian steward, and has been read as evidence of Joseph's influence on his household.

Context

All eleven brothers bow together (v.26) — the second dream partially fulfilled. Benjamin receives five times the portion, a deliberate test: will the brothers resent another favoured son of Rachel? They eat and are merry. The old jealousy does not surface. Genuine change. One more test (ch.44) will confirm it definitively.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 37:9Second dream approaching fulfilment — all eleven brothers before Joseph.
  • Gen 45:2Joseph's controlled weeping here anticipates the full release at disclosure — restrained emotion makes the eventual flood more powerful.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
43:26-28

When the brothers bow before Joseph and present their gifts, Joseph asks about their father: "is he still living?" (v.27). His first question is for the father. The dreamer sold by his brothers is now the lord before whom they bow — the dream fulfilled — but his concern is for Jacob. The human heart of the narrative is in this moment: the powerful man asking about the old man who mourned him for twenty-two years.

43:30

Joseph hurries out of the room and weeps — the second of five weeping scenes. He can barely maintain the concealment. Seeing Benjamin, the full brother of his mother, he is overwhelmed. The testing is not cruelty; it is the work of a man fighting his own desire to reveal himself in order to complete the moral work the brothers need to do. Concealment costs Joseph as much as it costs them.

43:33-34

The seating of the brothers in birth order — eldest to youngest — astonishes them ("they looked at each other in astonishment"). An Egyptian official has somehow known their birth order. The supernatural element of the seating protocol unsettles them; they do not yet understand that the man before them has known them since birth. Benjamin receives five times the portion of anyone else — the same disproportionate favour that Joseph's coat once signalled, now extended to the other Rachel-son.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
43:30

Vatimale rachamav el achiv vayevaqesh livkot — "his compassion for his brother was overwhelming and he was about to weep." Sarna notes the word rachamim (compassion) shares a root with rechem (womb) — it is the deep compassion of a shared origin, a mother-bond. Joseph and Benjamin share Rachel as their mother; the rachamim Joseph feels for Benjamin is literally the compassion of a shared womb. He weeps because he cannot not weep.

43:33

The brothers seated in birth order — without Joseph having been told — is the chapter's most uncanny moment. Sarna: "The seating protocol that the brothers cannot explain is one of the narrative's deliberately supernatural elements. Joseph knows things that an Egyptian official should not know. The accumulation of inexplicable knowledge will eventually compel the recognition: this man knows us from before Egypt."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
43:27-28

Joseph's question about the father and the brothers' answer — "our father is still alive and well" — is followed immediately by Joseph bowing. Alter: "The bow is ambiguous: is Joseph bowing as a social gesture? Is he overwhelmed and bowing to hide his face? Is the narrator gesturing at the reversal of the ch.37 bowing? The narrative leaves the gesture without explanation, allowing the reader to layer all three meanings simultaneously."

43:34

Benjamin receives five portions — a five-fold abundance. Alter notes five is the number of Joseph's generosity throughout the reunion narrative: Benjamin gets five portions here, five changes of clothes in 45:22. The number is not incidental; it is Joseph's signature excess, the overflow of love for his full brother. The favourite who once had one coat now gives the next favourite five.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
43:26-28

Calvin on Joseph's question about the father: "Joseph, perceiving that his father is in his thoughts, inquires concerning him, not as a stranger, but tenderly, as a son... There is here a beautiful specimen of the most excellent of the virtues — Joseph inquires about his father before he speaks of himself." The priority of the question reveals the priority of the heart: the reunion with his brothers matters; the news of his father matters more.

43:30

Calvin on the weeping: "The tears which Joseph shed were not those of weakness, but of holy affection. He had manfully restrained himself while he was among his brethren; but now, in the absence of witnesses, he gives full vent to his feelings. Such self-government in the presence of others, and such free expression in private, are the marks of a soul that is both strong and tender."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
43:30

The NET note on "his compassion for his brother" discusses rachamim as a plural of intensity (literally "deep compassions/mercies"). The note observes this is the same root used for God's mercy (rachamim) throughout the OT, and for the divine compassion Rachel invokes in 30:22 (God "remembered" Rachel — the text uses a similar emotional register). The note observes Joseph's mercy for Benjamin anticipates the divine mercy that the entire story has been demonstrating.

43:33

The NET note on "they were seated before him in the order of their birth" discusses the three explanatory options offered by ancient and modern readers: Joseph told his steward, Joseph himself arranged it, or it was perceived as supernatural. The note favours the third: from the brothers' perspective, it was inexplicable — an Egyptian official knowing eleven brothers' birth order is genuinely uncanny. The supernatural quality of the scene is part of the narrative's building pressure toward recognition.

Ancient Sources

Egyptian Seating Customs
Egyptian tomb paintings show Egyptians and foreigners dining separately — consistent with v.32 ("Egyptians could not eat with Hebrews, for that is an abomination to the Egyptians").
The cultural detail is authentic.
Five-Fold Portion
Egyptian administrative texts document special portions for honoured guests at official banquets.
Joseph's gesture has cultural intelligibility within Egyptian court protocol.

Reception History

Chrysostom
Joseph's private weeping is the supreme example of love restrained by wisdom — he loves fully but waits strategically for the moment when full disclosure will accomplish the most good.
Calvin
The meal scene is the foretaste of final reconciliation — grace extended in stages before full restoration. God often operates this way.

Literary Structure

Famine forces the decision. Reuben vs. Judah: sacrifice others vs. sacrifice self. Judah's surety — the moral turning point.
Jacob's Prayer — 43:11-15Double silver, gifts, Benjamin released. "If I am bereaved, I am bereaved." The most theologically honest sentence Jacob speaks.
Joseph's House — 43:16-25Brothers fear the silver — "he wants to seize us as slaves." Steward's reassurance: your God has given you treasure. Simeon freed.
The Meal — 43:26-34Brothers bow. Joseph asks of father, weeps in private. Seated in birth order — astonishment. Benjamin gets five portions.
Genesis 43 is the chapter of Judah's transformation. The man who sold Joseph now pledges himself for Benjamin. The surety offer is not heroic overnight conversion but the harvest of a moral journey that began with "she is more righteous than I" (38:26). The chapter ends with the brothers eating at Joseph's table — not yet knowing who he is — and Benjamin receiving five portions: the excess of love that Joseph cannot yet express directly.

Hebrew-Rooted Reading

43:9 אָנֹכִי אֶעֶרְבֶנּוּ anochi e'ervennuh — I myself will be his surety (legal guarantee formula; Judah stakes his own life; contrast with 37:26-27 where he calculated profit from Joseph's sale)

43:14 וַאֲנִי כַּאֲשֶׁר שָׁכֹלְתִּי שָׁכָלְתִּי — if I am bereaved, I am bereaved (emphatic doubled verb; Jacob's surrender; cf. Esther's "if I perish, I perish")

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 43 advances the moral testing of the brothers by placing them at Joseph's table, with Benjamin receiving disproportionate favour. The question the narrative is building toward: if Benjamin were threatened, would the brothers abandon him as they abandoned Joseph? Judah's surety offer (v.9) is the first answer. Chapter 44 will provide the definitive test.
