Genesis 43

Judah's Pledge; the Brothers Return to Egypt

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–15 — Judah's Pledge; Jacob Releases Benjamin
1Now the famine was still severe in the land.2So when they had eaten all the grain they had brought from Egypt, their father said to them, "Go back and buy us a little more food."3But Judah said to him, "The man warned us solemnly, 'You will not see my face again unless your brother is with you.'4If you will send our brother along with us, we will go down and buy food for you.5But if you will not send him, we will not go down, because the man said to us, 'You will not see my face again unless your brother is with you.'"6Israel asked, "Why did you bring this trouble on me by telling the man you had another brother?"7They replied, "The man questioned us closely about ourselves and our family. 'Is your father still living?' he asked us. 'Do you have another brother?' We simply answered his questions. How were we to know he would say, 'Bring your brother down here'?"8Then Judah said to Israel his father, "Send the boy along with me and we will go at once, so that we and you and our children may live and not die.9I myself will guarantee his safety; you can hold me personally responsible for him. If I do not bring him back to you and set him here before you, I will bear the blame before you all my life.10As it is, if we had not delayed, we could have gone and returned twice."11Then their father Israel said to them, "If it must be, then do this: Put some of the best products of the land in your bags and take them down to the man as a gift—a little balm and a little honey, some spices and myrrh, some pistachio nuts and almonds.12Take double the amount of silver with you, for you must return the silver that was put back in the mouths of your sacks. Perhaps it was a mistake.13Take your brother also and go back to the man at once.14And may God Almighty grant you mercy before the man so that he will let your other brother and Benjamin come back with you. As for me, if I am bereaved, I am bereaved."15So the men took the gifts and double the amount of silver, and Benjamin also. They hurried down to Egypt and presented themselves to Joseph.

Hebrew Word Study

אֶעֶרְבֶנּוּ ʾeʿerebennû — I will be a pledge / surety: Judah offers personal covenant-surety for Benjamin. The same word undergirds redemptive-obligation language in Ruth 4. This is the beginning of Judah's transformation into the substitute-redeemer who will offer himself in ch.44.

אֵל שַׁדַּי ʾēl šadday — God Almighty: Jacob invokes the covenant name from Bethel (35:11) and Abraham (17:1). In his moment of surrender, he falls back on the only sufficient ground.

שָׁכַלְתִּי šākalti — bereaved: Jacob's resignation — "if I am bereaved, I am bereaved" — is the language of Naomi and Job: profound submission to sovereignty expressed in acceptance of the worst.

Historical Context

The famine's severity forces Jacob to relent on Benjamin. Judah's offer of surety — "I myself will guarantee Benjamin's safety; if I do not bring him back, I will bear the blame before you all my life" — is the first sign of the transformed Judah who will make his great speech in chapter 44. The same Judah who proposed selling Joseph for profit now volunteers personal liability for Benjamin's safety. Jacob releases Benjamin with gifts and prayer — the most theologically explicit prayer of the Jacob narrative.

Context

The shift from Reuben's offer (42:37: "put both my sons to death") to Judah's offer here is the moral development the narrative requires. Reuben's pledge was excessive and self-focused; Judah's is personal and covenantal. Jacob's "if I am bereaved, I am bereaved" is his Peniel-trained surrender — the patriarch who wrestled with God can now release his last connection to Rachel into God's hands.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 42:37Reuben's offer: take my sons. Judah's offer: take me. The moral distance between the two reveals genuine growth.
  • Gen 44:33Judah's pledge here is redeemed in ch.44: he offers himself as slave in Benjamin's place — the surety becomes the substitute.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
43:8-9

Judah's guarantee — "I myself will guarantee his safety; you can hold me personally responsible" — is the verbal form of the surety he will enact physically in 44:18-34. The word arev (guarantee/surety) is a legal term for personal liability. Judah is placing himself under the debt of his brother's safe return. The same man who said "what will we gain if we kill our brother?" (37:26) now says "hold me personally accountable."

43:11-14

Jacob's response is a model of resigned faith: send gifts (practical wisdom), return the silver (honour), take Benjamin (the unavoidable decision), and — the theological pivot — "may God Almighty grant you mercy before the man so that he will let your other brother and Benjamin come back with you. As for me, if I am bereaved, I am bereaved." The final phrase is not despair but submission: whatever God ordains, I accept.

43:14

"If I am bereaved, I am bereaved" (va'ani ka'asher shakolti shakolti) is one of the great expressions of resigned faith in the OT. It echoes Esther's "if I perish, I perish" (Esth 4:16) and Job's "the LORD gave and the LORD has taken away" (Job 1:21). Jacob cannot control the outcome; he releases the outcome to God.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
43:9

Judah's surety speech creates a legal obligation (arev = surety) that he will fulfil completely in chapter 44. Sarna: "The legal vocabulary is precise: Judah is not merely promising to do his best — he is placing himself under a binding legal obligation. If Benjamin does not return safely, Judah bears the liability personally and permanently (lit. 'I will sin against you all my days')." The legal commitment is the moral baseline from which Judah's 44:18-34 speech will rise.

43:14

The phrase "if I am bereaved, I am bereaved" (sha-khol sha-khalti) uses a rare verb (shakal = to be bereaved of children) reduplicated for emphasis. Sarna: "The construction is the grammar of resignation — Jacob is saying: the worst can happen and I am prepared to receive it. This is a fundamentally different Jacob from the man who refused comfort at Joseph's supposed death (37:35). Something has shifted. He is learning to hold the future loosely."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
43:8-10

Judah's argument to Jacob — "send the boy with me; we will go so that we may live and not die, both we and you and our little ones" — is urgent but measured. Alter: "Judah does not minimise the danger; he places the danger in its proper context. The risk of going to Egypt with Benjamin is real; the certainty of starvation without going is absolute. He is making the only rational choice, and he is willing to bear personal liability for it."

43:14

Jacob's final word — "if I am bereaved, I am bereaved" — Alter identifies as the narrative's most resigned theological statement. "Jacob has been fighting the loss of Joseph for twenty years. He finally names the worst-case scenario — continued bereavement — and accepts it. The acceptance is not hopeless; El Shaddai has been invoked (v.14). But the outcome is released. For a man whose entire narrative has been about grasping and holding, this release is a spiritual climax."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
43:11-14

Calvin on Jacob's prayer and resignation: "The old man, though groaning under the weight of his grief, corrects his too ardent desire by the fear of God... His submission is not the despair of a man who has given up — it is the faith of a man who has learned that the outcomes are not his to manage. 'If I am bereaved, I am bereaved.' This is the language of faith that has passed through grief."

43:9

Calvin on Judah's surety: "Judah offers himself as surety for his brother — this is a great and honourable act, especially given that he had previously been the prime mover in selling Joseph. The same man who calculated profit from his brother's sale now pledges permanent personal liability for his brother's safety. The transformation is the work of God in Judah's conscience."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
43:9

The NET note on arev (guarantee/surety) explains the legal institution: a surety is a third party who assumes the liability of the primary debtor. In this case Judah assumes Benjamin's liability before Jacob. The note compares Proverbs 17:18 ("a person who shakes hands in pledge becomes a surety for his neighbour") and observes that Judah is entering a binding legal arrangement, not merely making a promise.

43:14

The NET note on "if I am bereaved, I am bereaved" discusses the Hebrew construction (shakal repeated) as an expression of resigned acceptance. The note compares Esther 4:16 ("if I perish, I perish") as a structural parallel — both statements accept the worst possible outcome rather than refuse to act until the outcome is guaranteed. The note identifies this as the grammar of faith in the face of irreducible uncertainty.

Verses 16–34 — Joseph Hosts His Brothers; Benjamin's Five Portions
16When Joseph saw Benjamin with them, he said to the steward of his house, "Take these men to my house, slaughter an animal and prepare a meal; they are to eat with me at noon."17The man did as Joseph told him and took the men to Joseph's house.18Now the men were frightened when they were taken to his house. They thought, "We were brought here because of the silver that was put back in our sacks the first time. He wants to attack us and overpower us and seize us as slaves and take our donkeys."19So they went up to Joseph's steward and spoke to him at the entrance to the house.20"We beg your pardon, our lord," they said, "we came down here the first time to buy food.21But at the place where we stopped for the night we opened our sacks and each of us found his silver—the exact weight—in the mouth of his sack. So we have brought it back with us.22We have also brought additional silver with us to buy food. We don't know who put our silver in our sacks."23"It's all right," he said. "Don't be afraid. Your God, the God of your father, has given you treasure in your sacks; I received your silver." Then he brought Simeon out to them.24The steward took the men into Joseph's house, gave them water to wash their feet and provided fodder for their donkeys.25They prepared their gifts for Joseph's arrival at noon, because they had heard that they were to eat there.26When Joseph came home, they presented to him the gifts they had brought into the house, and they bowed down before him to the ground.27He asked them how they were, and then he said, "How is your aged father you told me about? Is he still living?"28They replied, "Your servant our father is still alive and well." And they bowed down, prostrating themselves before him.29As he looked about and saw his brother Benjamin, his own mother's son, he asked, "Is this your youngest brother, the one you told me about?" And he said, "God be gracious to you, my son."30Deeply moved at the sight of his brother, Joseph hurried out and looked for a place to weep. He went into his private room and wept there.31After he had washed his face, he came out and, controlling himself, said, "Serve the food."32They served him by himself, the brothers by themselves, and the Egyptians who ate with him by themselves, because Egyptians could not eat with Hebrews, for that is detestable to Egyptians.33The men had been seated before him in the order of their ages, from the firstborn to the youngest; and they looked at each other in astonishment.34When portions were served to them from Joseph's table, Benjamin's portion was five times as much as anyone else's. So they feasted and drank freely with him.

Hebrew Word Study

כִּי נִכְמְרוּ רַחֲמָיו kî nikmerû raḥamāyw — his compassion grew warm: Literally "his bowels/womb-feelings were hot" — raḥamîm from reḥem (womb). The most visceral, maternal tenderness. Joseph's love for Benjamin is overwhelming and physical.

וַיִּתְאַפַּק wayyitʾappaq — controlling himself: He restrains himself with enormous effort. The disclosure is not yet time; the test must continue. The internal cost of the restraint is visible in the text.

Historical Context

The feast at Joseph's house is one of the most dramatically ironic scenes in the Joseph cycle. The brothers are terrified — they assume the silver found in their sacks is a pretext for enslavement. Joseph's steward reassures them with a phrase that is theologically loaded: "Your God and the God of your father has given you treasure in your sacks." A pagan steward attributes the silver to the God of the patriarchs. Joseph weeps privately when he sees Benjamin for the first time. At the banquet table the brothers are seated in exact birth order — a detail that terrifies them further. And Benjamin receives five times as much food as his brothers.

Context

All eleven brothers bow together (v.26) — the second dream partially fulfilled. Benjamin receives five times the portion, a deliberate test: will the brothers resent another favoured son of Rachel? They eat and are merry. The old jealousy does not surface. Genuine change. One more test (ch.44) will confirm it definitively.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 37:9Second dream approaching fulfilment — all eleven brothers before Joseph.
  • Gen 45:2Joseph's controlled weeping here anticipates the full release at disclosure — restrained emotion makes the eventual flood more powerful.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
43:23

The steward's response — "your God, the God of your father, has given you treasure in your sacks; I received your silver" — is one of the most surprising theological statements in the chapter. A pagan Egyptian household official is attributing the unexplained silver to the God of the patriarchs. Joseph has presumably spoken to his household about his God. The silver that the brothers received as grace (Joseph put it there) is correctly identified as divine gift by the Egyptian steward.

43:30-31

Joseph's private weeping when he sees Benjamin is the second of his five weeping scenes. The sight of his full brother — the only son of Rachel, his mother — overcomes him. He hurries to a private room and weeps. Then he washes his face and returns to maintain the official persona. The discipline required to contain the emotion and continue the test is one of the Joseph cycle's most moving elements.

43:34

Benjamin receives five times as much food as his brothers. The five-fold portion is both a test and a foreshadowing: will the brothers resent Benjamin's favoured treatment as they once resented Joseph's? The brothers eat and drink freely — apparently no resentment surfaces. The test of chapter 44 (the silver cup in Benjamin's sack) will be the decisive one.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
43:23

The steward's theological attribution is remarkable. Sarna: "That an Egyptian official speaks of 'your God, the God of your father' indicates that the Israelite God has become known in Joseph's household. Joseph's faithful witness to his God — evident throughout chapters 39-41 — has penetrated his household staff. The pagan steward has become a vehicle of divine reassurance."

43:32

The separate seating of Egyptians and Hebrews (and of Joseph separately as well) reflects authentic Egyptian social customs: Egyptians considered eating with foreigners ritually defiling. Sarna: "The detail is historically accurate and creates one of the scene's ironies: the Egyptian administrator eating separately is the Hebrew they are trying to reach."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
43:33

The brothers are seated in exact birth order — "the firstborn in the place of the firstborn and the youngest in the place of the youngest." Alter: "The brothers stare at each other in astonishment. The probability of chance arrangement in exact birth order is astronomically low. They know something is happening that transcends coincidence, but they cannot identify what. The scene generates maximum dramatic irony: the reader knows what the brothers do not."

43:34

The five-fold portion to Benjamin is a deliberate repetition of the original sin's conditions: a younger brother receiving markedly more than the others from their father's hand. Alter: "Joseph is recreating the conditions of his own favouritism to see if his brothers have changed. Will they resent Benjamin's five-fold portion as they resented Joseph's coat? The brothers drink freely — the test is not yet concluded, but the early signs are promising."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
43:23

Calvin on the steward's words: "The steward, although a Gentile, yet ascribes this good to God... Thus God, even in the minds of the profane, engraves some sense of his grace, so that they are constrained to acknowledge it, though they may not know him rightly. The steward does not know the God of Abraham perfectly; but he uses the name correctly."

43:30-31

Calvin on Joseph's weeping: "When he saw Benjamin his bowels yearned — the sight of his only full brother, born of his mother Rachel, moves him more deeply than the sight of his ten half-brothers. He restrains himself by extraordinary effort and goes to weep in private. We see here a man of complete emotional honesty who is nevertheless exercising remarkable self-control in the service of a purpose that requires the concealment."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
43:23

The NET note on the steward's "your God and the God of your father" discusses what this reveals about Joseph's household. The note: "The steward's use of the patriarchal divine formula is not accidental — someone in the household has spoken of this God. The most likely source is Joseph himself. The steward has absorbed enough of Joseph's faith to deploy its vocabulary in a moment of reassurance."

43:33

The NET note on the exact birth-order seating observes that this detail is one of the Joseph cycle's most theatrically precise ironies. The note: "The narrator records the brothers' astonishment without explaining how it happened. The reader understands: Joseph knows their birth order because he grew up with them. The knowledge he possesses and they lack is the source of the theatre."

Ancient Sources

Egyptian Seating Customs
Egyptian tomb paintings show Egyptians and foreigners dining separately — consistent with v.32 ("Egyptians could not eat with Hebrews, for that is an abomination to the Egyptians").
The cultural detail is authentic.
Five-Fold Portion
Egyptian administrative texts document special portions for honoured guests at official banquets.
Joseph's gesture has cultural intelligibility within Egyptian court protocol.

Reception History

Chrysostom
Joseph's private weeping is the supreme example of love restrained by wisdom — he loves fully but waits strategically for the moment when full disclosure will accomplish the most good.
Calvin
The meal scene is the foretaste of final reconciliation — grace extended in stages before full restoration. God often operates this way.

Literary Structure

Famine's Force — 43:1-7Famine worsens. Jacob resists. Brothers explain the condition: without Benjamin, we cannot buy grain.
Judah offers personal liability: hold me accountable. The legal surety that foreshadows his speech in ch.44.
Jacob sends gifts, silver, Benjamin. El Shaddai invoked. "If I am bereaved, I am bereaved." Resigned faith.
Feast at Joseph's — 43:15-25Arrival. Terror at the house. Steward's reassurance: your God gave you treasure. Simeon released. Joseph sees Benjamin.
Five Portions — 43:26-34Second weeping, private. Exact birth-order seating terrifies brothers. Benjamin five portions. They eat and drink.
Genesis 43 deepens the moral development of the story. Judah's surety transforms the legal and moral framework of the Benjamin trip. Jacob's resigned faith releases the outcome he cannot control. And at the feast, Joseph re-creates the conditions of the original favouritism — but the brothers' response is different. The test is almost complete.

Hebrew-Rooted Reading

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 43 is the chapter of transformed faith — Judah's surety and Jacob's resigned acceptance. Judah, who proposed profit from Joseph's sale, now volunteers permanent personal liability for Benjamin. Jacob, who refused comfort and refused to release Benjamin, now releases both with prayer. The transformation of the brothers' moral character is the real subject of Genesis 42-44, and chapter 43 is its middle stage.
