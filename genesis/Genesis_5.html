Genesis 5

The Genealogy from Adam to Noah

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–5 — The Book of Adam's Descendants
1This is the written account of Adam's family line. When God created mankind, he made them in the likeness of God.2He created them male and female and blessed them. And he named them "Mankind" when they were created.3When Adam had lived 130 years, he had a son in his own likeness, in his own image; and he named him Seth.4After Seth was born, Adam lived 800 years and had other sons and daughters.5Altogether, Adam lived a total of 930 years, and then he died.

Hebrew Word Study

סֵפֶר תּוֹלְדֹת sefer toledoth — book of the generations: V.1: This is the third use of the toledoth formula in Genesis (cf. 2:4; after the heavens/earth and the garden narrative). Here it explicitly refers back to the creation of Adam in the image of God — the genealogy is not merely a record of births but a theological statement: the image of God is carried forward through the generations. Each birth is a transmission of the divine image.

בִּדְמוּת אֱלֹהִים bidmut Elohim — in the likeness of God: V.1: The creation language of 1:26-27 is deliberately recalled. The genealogy opens by re-anchoring humanity in the image of God before tracing its transmission. Seth is born "in the likeness and image" of Adam (v.3) — the same vocabulary. The image passes from God to Adam to Seth, with the terminology carefully preserved.

Historical Context

The ten-generation structure of chapter 5 (Adam to Noah) parallels the post-flood genealogy of chapter 11 (Shem to Abraham), also ten generations. Both genealogies use the same formula and both culminate in a figure of decisive redemptive significance (Noah; Abraham). The extremely long lifespans (ranging from Enoch's 365 years to Methuselah's 969) have no parallel in Israel's wider ANE context except the Sumerian King List, which records pre-flood kings reigning for tens of thousands of years. Genesis's lifespans, though long, are dramatically shorter.

Context

The genealogy of chapter 5 performs two functions simultaneously: it bridges the narrative from Adam's expulsion to Noah's flood across centuries of time, and it carries the theological assertion that the image of God — however damaged by the fall — continues to be transmitted in the human line. The repeated refrain "and he died" is the most solemn phrase in the chapter: every name ends the same way. Death is the universal consequence of the fall. Enoch alone breaks the pattern.

Cross-Reference

  • Luke 3:36-38Luke's genealogy of Jesus traces back through Noah and the patriarchs to Adam, son of God — connecting the lineage of Gen 5 to the incarnation.
  • 1 Chr 1:1-4Chronicles opens with this same genealogy — the OT's confirmation that Gen 5 is the foundational human lineage.
  • Jude 14Enoch, the seventh from Adam, prophesied — Jude quotes an Enochic tradition, treating the Gen 5 figure as a prophet.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1

The phrase "in the day that God created mankind, he made them in the likeness of God" is a deliberate flashback to 1:26-27. The genealogy does not begin with a birth record but with a theological statement: the image of God is the starting point of the entire human family. Every subsequent name in the list is a bearer of that image — however marred by the fall.

5:3

Adam fathered Seth "in his own likeness and image" — the same language used of God creating Adam. The image of God passes from creator to creature, from parent to child. The transmission of the image is the theological content of the genealogy. This is not merely a biological record but a spiritual one: the lineage through which God's purposes will advance.

5:1-5

The formula "and he died" that closes every entry (except Enoch) is the chapter's theological refrain. It underscores the universal consequence of the fall announced in 2:17 and 3:19. Even the longest-lived patriarchs — even Methuselah at 969 years — die. Death is not an accident; it is the structural reality of the post-fall world. Enoch's exception is therefore all the more striking.

5:4-5

Adam's 930 years and his many sons and daughters establish the density of the pre-flood human population. By Noah's time, the earth is filled with humanity (6:1) — the fulfillment of the blessing to "be fruitful and multiply" (1:28), but in a world now filled also with violence (6:11). The blessing has been fulfilled; the corruption has followed it.

5:1

Sefer toledoth haAdam — "the book/record of the generations of Adam." Sarna notes that sefer (book/document) suggests a formal written record, not merely an oral tradition. The choice of sefer underscores the deliberate, archival character of the genealogy — it is presented as a document, not a story. This is the Bible's first explicit reference to a written record within the narrative.

5:3

Adam's son Seth is born "in his likeness and image" — the same vocabulary used of God creating Adam in 1:26. Sarna: "The image of God is not destroyed by the fall but continues to be transmitted through the human line. The genealogy is the record of that transmission." The image, however marred, persists — which is the basis for the prohibition of murder in 9:6: the murderer attacks the image of God.

5:1-2

The opening verses recall the creation account with deliberate economy: "When God created mankind, he made them in the likeness of God; male and female he created them, and he blessed them and named them 'mankind.'..." Alter notes this is a compressed summary of 1:26-28 inserted as a header for the genealogy. The effect is to anchor the long list of names in the creation theology of chapter 1 — these are not merely mortal statistics but image-bearers.

5:5

The phrase "and he died" (wayyamot) repeats like a drumbeat through the chapter. Alter: "The tolling of this phrase at the end of each entry is one of the most powerful uses of repetition in the entire Bible. The extravagant lifespans make the death notice all the more poignant — this man lived for nine centuries, and then he died. Even 969 years is not enough."

5:1

Moses repeats the statement about the image of God at the opening of the genealogy to remind us that even in the corrupted state of fallen humanity, the dignity of the divine image is not entirely lost. Calvin: "Although Adam had lost the image of God, yet some residue of it still remained." The genealogy is therefore not merely a record of mortality but a record of the persistence of divine dignity in fallen creatures.

5:3

That Seth is born "in Adam's likeness" rather than specifically in God's likeness has led some to read a diminishment — the image passed on is now Adam's fallen image, not God's original. Calvin addresses this: the image is transmitted both in its damaged state and in its remaining dignity. The corruption does not eliminate what was created; it distorts it.

5:1

The NET note on "likeness of God" observes the chapter consciously parallels 1:26-28 using the same vocabulary (demut, tselem) to establish continuity between creation and genealogy. The note also discusses the structural parallel between this ten-generation genealogy (Adam to Noah) and the post-flood genealogy of 11:10-26 (Shem to Abram) — both ten generations, both culminating in a pivotal covenant figure.

5:3

The NET note on "in his own likeness, in his own image" observes that the prepositions are reversed from 1:26 (where "image" comes first). The note discusses whether this implies Seth bears Adam's fallen image rather than the divine image directly, and concludes that 9:6 (which prohibits murder on the grounds that humans still bear God's image) suggests the divine image persists through the transmission.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:1

The toledoth formula here ("This is the written account of Adam's line") echoes both 2:4 and the broader structural device of Genesis. Sarna: the phrase sefer toledoth Adam — "the book of the generations of Adam" — deliberately mirrors the formula of 2:4 for the heavens and earth. The human story is given the same formal introduction as the cosmos itself. Humanity's history is as significant as cosmic history.

5:3

Wayyoled bademuto ketsalmo — "and he fathered a son in his own likeness, after his image." The identical language of 1:26-27 (God's image and likeness) is now applied to Adam's son Seth. The image of God passes through the generations, but so does the fallen nature. Seth is Adam's image as Adam is God's image — a chain of likeness running through human history, fallen but not erased.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
5:1

The genealogy of chapter 5 is the Bible's first formal genealogical list, and Alter notes its structural elegance: each entry follows the same pattern (age at first son's birth, remaining years, death notice) with one exception — Enoch. The repetition creates a drumbeat of mortality: "and he died... and he died... and he died." Against this rhythm, Enoch's translation is a sudden, dissonant break.

5:3-5

Adam lived 930 years and then he died. The death notice, so formulaic it seems almost bureaucratic, is in fact the fulfilment of God's warning in 2:17: "in the day you eat of it you shall surely die." The deaths come — slowly, at great age, but they come. The genealogy is a long ledger of mortality, the toll of the fall measured out in centuries.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
5:1-2

Moses returns to the creation narrative (1:26-28) at the opening of the genealogy to remind us of human dignity before tracing human mortality. Calvin: "Moses, in order to show that the image of God was not entirely extinguished, recalls to our memory what had been before stated — that God created man in his own image. And then he immediately adds, that Adam begat a son in his own image; meaning that the celestial image, though vitiated and almost obliterated, was yet transmitted from the father to the son."

5:5

Adam lived 930 years, "and then he died." Calvin on the longevity: the long lives of the antediluvians were given by divine providence so that the world might be populated, that experience and knowledge could accumulate across generations, and that the promise of the seed (3:15) might be preserved through the line until its fulfilment. Long life is not reward for virtue but provision for redemptive history.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
5:1

The NET note on "written account" (sefer) observes this is the only place in Genesis where the toledoth formula is qualified as a sefer — a written document. This may suggest that an actual written source underlies this section, consistent with the ancient tradition of genealogical record-keeping. The note on "image of God" observes that the resumption of 1:26-27 language at the opening of the genealogy is a deliberate theological framing device.

5:3

The NET note on Seth being born "in his own likeness, after his image" carefully distinguishes this from the image-of-God language: Adam bore God's image; Seth bears Adam's image. The note asks whether this implies the image of God is transmitted through procreation (Reformed tradition) or whether the reference is to physical resemblance. The NET favours the view that both physical and spiritual likeness are intended.

Verses 6–20 — The Generations: Seth to Jared
6When Seth had lived 105 years, he became the father of Enosh.7After he became the father of Enosh, Seth lived 807 years and had other sons and daughters.8Altogether, Seth lived a total of 912 years, and then he died.9When Enosh had lived 90 years, he became the father of Kenan.10After he became the father of Kenan, Enosh lived 815 years and had other sons and daughters.11Altogether, Enosh lived a total of 905 years, and then he died.12When Kenan had lived 70 years, he became the father of Mahalalel.13After he became the father of Mahalalel, Kenan lived 840 years and had other sons and daughters.14Altogether, Kenan lived a total of 910 years, and then he died.15When Mahalalel had lived 65 years, he became the father of Jared.16After he became the father of Jared, Mahalalel lived 830 years and had other sons and daughters.17Altogether, Mahalalel lived a total of 895 years, and then he died.18When Jared had lived 162 years, he became the father of Enoch.19After he became the father of Enoch, Jared lived 800 years and had other sons and daughters.20Altogether, Jared lived a total of 962 years, and then he died.

Hebrew Word Study

וַיּוֹלֶד wayyoled — fathered / begot: The repeated verb wayyoled drives the rhythmic structure of the genealogy. It is the hiphil form of yalad (to bear/give birth), here used causatively of fathers. The repetition creates a hypnotic forward motion: name, fathered, years, sons and daughters, died. The pattern is unbroken through fifteen verses, broken only at Enoch.

Historical Context

The section covers Seth through Jared — six of the ten pre-flood patriarchs. The lifespans range from Kenan's 910 years to Jared's 962 years. The function of these extreme lifespans in the narrative is disputed: they may reflect a genuine pre-flood biological reality, a schematic literary convention (as in Sumerian king lists), symbolic significance of specific numbers, or compressed chronology. What is indisputable is their narrative function: they establish that enormous spans of time passed between creation and the flood, time filled with the multiplication of humanity.

Context

The middle section of the genealogy is structurally unremarkable — it is designed to be unremarkable. The repetitive formula does its work precisely by being monotonous. The reader is meant to feel the weight of generation after generation passing: each person born, each person fathering children, each person dying. The monotony of mortality is itself the theological point. Against this relentless backdrop, Enoch's exception will be all the more arresting.

Cross-Reference

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
    5:6-20

    The repetitive structure of the genealogy — name, years before fathering, sons and daughters, total years, died — is not literary tedium but theological architecture. Each entry makes the same three claims: this person existed (life), this person propagated the image of God (begetting), and this person died (mortality). The genealogy is a sustained meditation on what fallen human existence means: we live, we pass on life, we die. All three are equally important.

    5:6-20

    The structure of each genealogical entry — years before named son, "other sons and daughters," total lifespan, death — is a fixed formula that Sarna identifies as characteristic of ancient genealogical records. The mention of unnamed "other sons and daughters" after each named heir establishes that the genealogy is selective, not exhaustive: it traces the covenant line, not every branch of the family tree.

    5:6-20

    The genealogy achieves its effect through cumulative repetition. Alter compares it to the creation account: both use repetition to create a sense of structured reality, a world operating according to stable, predictable patterns. In chapter 1, the pattern is creation; here it is generation and death. Both patterns point toward something beyond themselves: creation toward the Sabbath, generation toward the exception of Enoch.

    5:6-20

    The long lives of the patriarchs served God's providential purposes: they allowed the knowledge of creation, the fall, and God's promises to be transmitted across many generations by living memory before writing was established. Calvin: "The long life of the patriarchs was of great use for the preservation of true doctrine; for, since the world is inclined to forgetfulness and negligence in divine things, it was necessary that the knowledge of God should be maintained by those living records."

    5:6-20

    The NET note discusses the interpretive options for the extreme lifespans: (1) literal years as we count them; (2) shorter year-units (months, seasons); (3) dynasty lengths rather than individual lifespans; (4) literary convention following ANE patterns. The note observes that option (2) creates its own problems (some patriarchs would be fathering children at ages 5-11) and that no clear solution resolves all difficulties. The note recommends holding the question with appropriate uncertainty.

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    5:6-20

    The seven names from Seth to Enoch follow the formulaic pattern with no deviation. Sarna notes that the numerical values of the pre-flood lifespans have been the subject of extensive speculation: some see astronomical or calendrical significance, others read them as expressions of primordial dignity before the flood shortened human life. The Septuagint and Samaritan Pentateuch give different numbers for most entries — the three textual traditions diverge significantly here, suggesting the numbers were subject to revision in transmission.

The reader is lulled by the repetition, conditioned to expect the death notice, so that when Enoch's entry breaks the pattern ("and he was not, for God took him"), the disruption registers with full force.     NET Bible Notes

    Verses 21–27 — Enoch Walks with God; Methuselah
    21When Enoch had lived 65 years, he became the father of Methuselah.22After he became the father of Methuselah, Enoch walked faithfully with God 300 years and had other sons and daughters.23Altogether, Enoch lived a total of 365 years.24Enoch walked faithfully with God; then he was no more, because God took him away.25When Methuselah had lived 187 years, he became the father of Lamech.26After he became the father of Lamech, Methuselah lived 782 years and had other sons and daughters.27Altogether, Methuselah lived a total of 969 years, and then he died.

    Hebrew Word Study

    וַיִּתְהַלֵּךְ אֶת הָאֱלֹהִים wayyithhallek et haElohim — walked with God: V.22,24: The Hithpael of halak (to walk) with et (with, alongside) — an intimate, sustained, reciprocal relationship. The same phrase is used of Noah (6:9). "Walking with God" is the biblical idiom for a life of consistent fellowship, obedience, and communion with God. It is the pre-fall condition (God walking in the garden, 3:8) restored in an individual life.

    אֵינֶנּוּ einennu — he was no more / he was not: V.24: The same word used when Jacob mourns that Joseph "is no more" (37:30) and when the children mourn that their children "are no more" (Rachel, Jer 31:15). Here it is not grief but mystery: "God took him." The brief phrase stands against the heavy "and he died" of every other entry. Enoch did not die — God took him. The passive verb leaves everything unexplained: only the fact, and the implication of a different kind of departure.

    Historical Context

    Enoch's "365 years" is often noted as equal to the solar year. Whether this is significant is disputed. What is undeniable is that his years are the shortest of any patriarch in the chapter — suggesting his "taking" was not delayed departure but an early translation at the peak of his walk with God. The post-biblical tradition of Enoch became enormously developed: 1 Enoch (quoted in Jude 14-15) presents him as a prophet and heavenly traveler. The Second Temple period produced extensive Enoch literature.

    Context

    Enoch's entry breaks the monotonous pattern of the genealogy like a sudden bright note in a sustained minor key. The formula changes, the refrain "and he died" is absent, and the explanation is as brief as it is extraordinary: "God took him." The pattern of the chapter has trained the reader to expect death; the absence of death in Enoch's entry is therefore maximally effective. He becomes the great biblical type of those who pass from life to God's presence without death.

    Cross-Reference

    • Heb 11:5“By faith Enoch was taken from this life, so that he did not experience death.” The NT reads Enoch’s translation as an act of faith — he pleased God.
    • Jude 14–15Jude quotes Enoch’s prophecy about the coming judgment of the Lord — drawing on the tradition of Enoch as a prophet (from 1 Enoch 1:9).
    • 2 Kgs 2:11Elijah’s translation in a whirlwind parallels Enoch — the two humans in Scripture who bypass death. Both appear at the Transfiguration (Matt 17:3).
    • 1 Thess 4:17“We who are still alive will be caught up” — the eschatological translation of believers echoes the Enoch pattern.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
    5:21-24

    Enoch is the great exception in the genealogy of death. He walked with God for 300 years after fathering Methuselah — the longest recorded walk with God in the chapter — and then God took him. He did not die. This is the first translation in Scripture: a living person removed from earth to God's presence. The New Testament reads it as a proof of the reality of resurrection and eternal life (Heb 11:5), and as a type of the rapture — believers taken without dying at Christ's return.

    5:23-24

    That Enoch's years (365) are the fewest of all the patriarchs is not incidental: he was taken at what would have been the midpoint of a normal patriarchal lifespan. The walk with God was complete before the years were complete. God takes those who walk with him when the walk is what matters, not the duration. His translation is a divine commentary on the genealogy: the one who walked most intimately with God did not die like the rest.

    5:22,24

    The phrase wayyithhallek Chanoch et haElohim — "Enoch walked with God" — appears twice, framing the account of his fathering Methuselah. Sarna notes the deliberate contrast with the genealogy's standard formula: instead of the mortal pattern (lived, fathered, sons/daughters, died), Enoch's entry is interrupted by the repeated characterisation of his walk. The walk is the content of his life; the taking is its culmination. The brevity of the description matches the ineffability of the event.

    5:24

    Einennu ki laqach oto Elohim — "he was not, for God took him." The LXX translates "God pleased himself with Enoch and he was not found" — a reading that Hebrews 11:5 follows. The Hebrew laqach (to take) is used of God taking a soul at death (Ps 49:16) and of God's intimate taking of beloved servants. The vocabulary does not specify the nature of the translation but points clearly to divine initiative: God acted; Enoch did not die; the rest is mystery.

    5:21-24

    Alter on the Enoch entry: "The sudden deviation from the formula — the substitution of 'walked with God' for the sequence of birth and death, the replacement of 'and he died' with 'and he was no more, for God took him' — is one of the most affecting moments in the entire primeval history. The reader who has been lulled by the repetitive formula is suddenly arrested. Something different has happened here. The genealogy of death has produced a man who did not die."

    5:24

    Calvin on Enoch's translation: "The Lord, by a unique miracle, freed Enoch from death, so that this example might set before men's eyes what the future resurrection of the body would be like." Enoch's translation is a sign pointing forward to resurrection — not the only way of passing from life to God, but a proleptic demonstration that physical death does not end the relationship between God and those who walk with him.

    5:24

    The NET note on "God took him" discusses the parallel with Elijah (2 Kgs 2:1,11) and notes that both cases use different vocabulary but describe the same reality: a divinely initiated removal from earthly life. The note addresses Hebrews 11:5's reading ("he did not experience death; he could not be found, because God had taken him away") as a legitimate expansion of the Hebrew brevity, consistent with the LXX. The note observes that the text is deliberately silent about the nature of the translation.

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    5:22-24

    Vayithalech Chanoch et haElohim — "Enoch walked with God." The phrase hithalech et haElohim appears only twice in Genesis: here (5:22,24) and of Noah in 6:9. In both cases it describes a person of exceptional covenant intimacy. Sarna: "To walk with God" is the Hebrew idiom for continuous, habitual fellowship with the divine — not a single encounter but a lifetime orientation. The contrast with the surrounding genealogy is total: others are described by what they did (begat, died); Enoch is described by who he was with.

    5:27

    Methuselah's lifespan of 969 years is the longest recorded in the Bible. Sarna notes the name Methuselah may mean "man of the javelin/weapon" or possibly "when he dies, it shall come" — the latter giving rise to the tradition (not in the text itself) that the flood came in the year of Methuselah's death. By the MT chronology, Methuselah dies in the year of the flood, though this is a calculation from the numbers rather than a statement in the text.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    5:21-24

    Enoch walked with God for three hundred years, "and then he was not, for God took him." Alter: the three Hebrew words — ve'einennu ki laqach oto Elohim — are among the most compressed and resonant in the entire chapter. "He was not" — a statement of absence rather than death. "For God took him" — the subject switches from the routine fathering-dying pattern to a direct divine action. The passive "took" (laqach) appears in later biblical texts of prophetic rapture (2 Kgs 2:9-10 of Elijah). The genealogy's one exception becomes a window into the possibility of life beyond the death-toll.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    5:21-24

    Enoch's translation is a sign and pledge of the resurrection. Calvin: "The Lord intended by this translation of Enoch to give a proof of immortality to his ancient people, and to console them under the hard necessity of dying, by showing that something better was prepared for them." In the midst of the long roll-call of death, God lifts one man bodily out of mortality — not as an escape from the fallen world but as a prophetic sign that the Creator is not done with his creation.

    5:27

    Methuselah's extraordinary lifespan is not merely numerical trivia. Calvin: the Lord lengthened the lives of the antediluvians so that the knowledge of creation, the fall, and the promise might be transmitted with greater certainty across the generations before the flood wiped the slate. The patriarchs as living chains of testimony.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    5:22-24

    The NET note on "walked with God" (hithalech et haElohim) observes this is the Hithpael stem — a reflexive or reciprocal action, suggesting habitual or ongoing relationship rather than a single event. The note surveys the translation options for "God took him": the LXX reads euērestēsen ("pleased God" — cf. Heb 11:5 which quotes this), suggesting a theological reading that God took him because he pleased God. The NET note acknowledges that Hebrews 11:5 explicitly says Enoch did not experience death.

    Verses 28–32 — Lamech and the Birth of Noah
    28When Lamech had lived 182 years, he had a son.29He named him Noah and said, "He will comfort us in the labor and painful toil of our hands caused by the ground the Lord has cursed."30After Noah was born, Lamech lived 595 years and had other sons and daughters.31Altogether, Lamech lived a total of 777 years, and then he died.32After Noah was 500 years old, he became the father of Shem, Ham and Japheth.

    Hebrew Word Study

    נֹחַ Noach — Noah / rest: V.29: Lamech names his son Noah (Noach) with a wordplay: "He will comfort us (yenahhamenu) in the labor and painful toil of our hands." The wordplay involves the root NHM (comfort) rather than NCH (rest) — the names are not etymologically identical but sound similar. Lamech is pointing forward: this child will bring relief from the curse of Genesis 3:17-19. The expectation is messianic in structure, if not yet in fulfilment.

    Historical Context

    Noah is born in a world already described as filled with violence (the context of chapter 6 will confirm this). Lamech's prophetic naming of Noah — connecting him to the relief of the curse — is the first explicit messianic hope since the protoevangelium of 3:15. The structure of the chapter has been pointing toward Noah: ten generations from Adam, the line of Seth reaching its climax in the one who will survive the judgment and carry the human family through to a new beginning.

    Context

    The genealogy ends not with death but with birth and hope. Noah's birth, accompanied by Lamech's prophetic naming, reorients the chapter's solemn refrain. The genealogy of mortality (every entry ending "and he died") culminates in the birth of the one through whom life will continue. The chapter is bookended by image-bearing (v.1-3) and covenant hope (v.28-32): the image of God and the promise of relief frame the long record of human mortality.

    Cross-Reference

    • Gen 6:9“Noah was a righteous man, blameless among the people of his time, and he walked faithfully with God” — the same language used for Enoch, echoing Gen 5’s highest commendation.
    • Heb 11:7“By faith Noah, when warned about things not yet seen, in holy fear built an ark” — Noah joins the great cloud of witnesses traced back to Abel and Enoch in Gen 5.
    • Luke 3:36–38Luke’s genealogy of Jesus passes through Shem, Noah, Methuselah, Enoch, Seth, and Adam — anchoring the incarnation in the Genesis 5 genealogy.
    • 1 Pet 3:20Eight people were saved through water in the ark — Peter reads Noah’s survival as a type of baptism and salvation.

    MacArthur Study Notes

    MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
    5:28-29

    Lamech's naming of Noah is a prophetic act. "He will comfort us in the labor and painful toil of our hands caused by the ground the LORD has cursed." The explicit connection to the curse of 3:17-19 makes clear that Lamech expected Noah to be the one who would reverse or relieve the effects of the fall. In a limited sense, Noah does this: through the flood and the new covenant (ch.9), the relationship between humanity and the earth is partially restored. But the full relief awaits the last Adam.

    5:32

    The birth of Shem, Ham, and Japheth at the close of the genealogy sets the stage for the flood narrative. The three sons represent the three branches of the post-flood human family (ch.10). The genealogy of chapter 5 thus serves as the bridge between the primeval world (Adam to the fall) and the flood narrative — ten generations of faithful transmission of the human line, culminating in the family that will carry it through the judgment.

    5:29

    The name Noah (Noach) and the comfort (yenahhamenu) are a folk etymology — the sounds connect even if the roots differ. Sarna: "Lamech's words are prophetic in their import: the age of Noah marks a new epoch in human history. The relief comes not through the removal of the curse itself but through the covenant that follows the flood — a new framework for human existence on a cursed earth." Noah does not end the curse; he introduces the covenant context within which humanity can endure it.

    5:28-32

    The genealogy's last entry is the longest and most narrative. Unlike the clipped formula entries for Seth through Jared, Noah's entry includes a name explanation, a quotation from Lamech, and the naming of three sons. Alter: "The expansion signals that Noah is not merely another link in the genealogical chain but a turning point. The genealogy has been building toward him. What follows — the flood narrative — is what his birth makes possible."

    5:29

    Lamech's prophecy shows that even in a corrupted world, the knowledge of the original curse and the hope for its relief was alive. Calvin: "The holy fathers were not so dull as to be ignorant of the cause of human miseries; they knew that the earth was cursed on account of sin, and therefore they sighed for deliverance." The hope for relief from the curse is the thread that connects the genealogy of death to the covenant of grace that follows the flood.

    5:29

    The NET note on the Noah/comfort wordplay explains the Hebrew: Noah (Noach) comes from nuach (to rest); comfort (yenahhamenu) comes from naham (to comfort/relieve). The sounds are similar but the roots distinct — a sound-play, not a strict etymology. The note observes this is consistent with ancient naming practices where the significance of a name was more important than strict etymological accuracy. The prophecy anticipates both the rest of the ark and the rest of the new covenant.

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    5:28-29

    Lamech's naming speech for Noah — "he will comfort us in the labour and painful toil of our hands" — is the first naming speech in the genealogy. The name Noah (Noach) is connected to the root nacham (to comfort, to relieve), though the etymology is not straightforward. Sarna notes the naming speech points forward to Noah's role after the flood: the planting of the vineyard (9:20) and the covenant of rest. Lamech's hope for a deliverer from the curse of 3:17-19 is partially fulfilled in Noah.

    5:32

    The three sons named at the end of Noah's entry — Shem, Ham, and Japheth — are the structural pivot of the primeval history. Their names will carry the narrative from the flood (chs.6-9) through the Table of Nations (ch.10) and into the beginning of the Abrahamic story. The genealogy of chapter 5 ends by opening the door to the flood narrative.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    5:29

    Lamech's speech is the only extended prose in the otherwise formulaic genealogy. Alter notes this breaks the pattern at exactly the right moment — the last entry before Noah, the pivot from genealogy to narrative. The naming speech introduces Noah as a figure of hope and relief, setting up the flood narrative with a note of expectation: this is the man through whom something decisive will happen.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    5:28-30

    Lamech's naming of Noah expresses the eschatological hope of the antediluvian saints: that God will provide relief from the curse of 3:17-19. Calvin: "Lamech predicts that his son would be an occasion of joy and comfort, and that through him the condition of men would be somewhat improved. He does not speak of his own family only, but of the whole human race." Noah does not fulfil the full hope — that awaits the last Adam — but he is the instrument of preservation and partial relief.

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    5:29

    The NET note on the Noah etymology observes the wordplay is actually imprecise: Noah (Noach) does not derive from nacham (comfort) etymologically — they share similar sounds but different roots. The note calls this a popular etymology — a naming explanation that plays on sound rather than strict derivation, a common biblical convention. The theological point (Noah as one who brings relief) is made through the sound connection regardless of strict etymology.

    Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    Enoch
    Seventh-generation patriarch; translated without death
    Enoch stands against the genealogy's relentless "and he died" as the single exception. He walked with God — the sustained, intimate relationship that was humanity's pre-fall condition — and God took him without death. He becomes the pre-flood type of resurrection hope, the proof that death is not the necessary end of a life walked with God. Second Temple Judaism developed his character extensively; the NT treats him as a genuine prophet (Jude 14) and man of faith (Heb 11:5).
    Methuselah
    Longest-lived human in recorded history; 969 years
    Son of Enoch, grandfather of Noah. His extraordinary lifespan (969 years — the longest recorded in Scripture) means he died in the year of the flood, by most calculations. Whether he died just before the flood or in it is not specified. His name, sometimes interpreted as "when he dies, it shall come" (in some Semitic readings), may contain a prophetic significance — though this is uncertain. He is remembered primarily as the human whose life spanned the greatest portion of pre-flood history.
    Noah
    Son of Lamech; bearer of the covenant line
    Born into a world already described as filled with violence, named with prophetic hope by his father. Noah is introduced at the close of the genealogy as the culmination of ten generations of faithful lineage. He will carry the image of God, the memory of creation and the fall, and the hope of the protoevangelium through the flood to a new beginning. The NT treats him as the paradigm of faith-based obedience (Heb 11:7) and as the type of final judgment and salvation (Luke 17:26-27; 1 Pet 3:20).

    Ancient Sources

    The Sumerian King List — Pre-Flood Kings
    The Sumerian King List records eight (or ten in some versions) pre-flood kings with reigns ranging from 18,600 to 43,200 years. The seventh king, Enmeduranki of Sippar, was the favourite of the sun-god and moon-god and was taught divination — a possible parallel to Enoch, the seventh from Adam, who walks with God and has prophetic knowledge.
    The parallel is structural (pre-flood list, culminating in a flood) and numerical (seventh figure with special divine relationship), but the theological content is opposite. The Sumerian kings are heroes of culture and civilisation; the Genesis patriarchs are defined by their relationship to God and the transmission of the image. The lifespans in Genesis, though extreme, are dramatically shorter than the Sumerian figures.
    1 Enoch and the Enoch Literature (3rd c. BCE onwards)
    The Book of 1 Enoch (found extensively at Qumran) presents Enoch as a heavenly traveler who receives visions of the divine throne, the fallen angels, the flood, and the final judgment. It was enormously influential in Second Temple Judaism and is quoted by Jude (14-15) in the NT.
    The Enoch literature shows how a brief biblical mention (Genesis 5:24) generated an entire theology. What Genesis leaves to mystery, later tradition filled with detail. The NT's use of 1 Enoch (Jude) indicates that some of this tradition was considered authoritative, while the silence of the Gospels and Paul about Enoch suggests the Genesis text itself is the canonical limit.

    Reception History

    Origen, On First Principles (c.230 AD)
    Origen reads Enoch's translation as evidence for the soul's capacity for direct union with God — a translation not just of body but of the entire person. Enoch became a key figure in early Christian discussions of deification (theosis): what happened to Enoch is the prototype of what happens to all the redeemed.
    John Calvin, Institutes and Commentaries
    Calvin uses Enoch as his primary proof text that the pre-Mosaic patriarchs possessed genuine hope of resurrection and eternal life. "God gave a signal proof of the immortality of man's soul, when He assumed Enoch to Himself, and this example was sufficient to maintain the faith of the pious in a future life." Against those who read pre-Mosaic religion as purely earthly, Calvin points to Enoch: the patriarchs knew they were going somewhere beyond death.
    Josephus, Jewish Antiquities (c.93 AD)
    Josephus interprets Enoch's disappearance as follows: "He returned to the divinity, whence it is written about him that he disappeared, not having died, God giving him this privilege because of his extraordinary piety." Josephus represents the widespread Jewish tradition that Enoch's translation was a reward for righteousness — an early form of what will become the theology of bodily assumption.

    Literary Structure

    Toledoth Formula — 5:1Sefer toledoth Adam — the only toledoth called a "book/written account." Resumption of 1:26-27 as theological framing.
    Pattern — 5:3-27Formulaic structure: begat at age X, lived Y more years, begat sons/daughters, died. Seven entries before Enoch.
    "He walked with God... and he was not, for God took him." Single deviation from the death formula — prepared by seven repetitions.
    Lamech's Hope — 5:29First naming speech in the genealogy. Sets Noah apart as a figure of relief and hope. Pivot to the flood narrative.
    Genesis 5 is the bridge between the primeval drama of chapters 3-4 and the flood narrative of chapters 6-9. Its literary structure — relentless repetition of the death formula — makes Enoch's exception all the more striking. The genealogy is a theology of mortality interrupted by a single sign of life beyond death. It ends with Noah, who will carry the image of God through the waters of judgment.

    Hebrew-Rooted Reading

    5:1 בִּדְמוּת אֱלֹהִים bidmut Elohim — in the likeness of God (echoes 1:26 — the genealogy opens with creation theology)  |  וַיּוֹלֶד wayyoled — fathered (hiphil, repeated as the rhythmic verb of the genealogy)

    5:24 וַיִּתְהַלֵּךְ wayyithhallek — walked (hithpael = sustained reciprocal walk)  |  אֵינֶנּוּ einennu — he was not/no more (the absence that breaks the death-refrain)  |  לָקַח laqach — took (God as the active subject of Enoch's departure)

    Textual Notes

    5:1book of the generations of Adam
    MT sefer toledoth Adam = the book/scroll of the generations of Adam. The word sefer (book, scroll, written document) suggests a written record, not merely an oral genealogy. The Wiseman hypothesis (all Genesis toledoth are clay-tablet records) sees this as the heading of a written document.
    Whether Genesis preserves ancient written sources or was composed in its entirety in the Mosaic or post-Mosaic period is one of the central questions of Pentateuchal criticism.
    5:24Enoch walked with God — translation or death?
    MT "Enoch walked with God; then he was no more, because God took him." The phrase "was no more" (einenu) is used of death elsewhere (Gen 42:13,36). But the absence of the death formula ("and he died") is conspicuous. LXX: "pleased God and was not found, because God translated him." Heb 11:5 confirms the translation reading. Some argue the text is deliberately ambiguous.
    Whether Enoch was translated bodily to heaven without dying (like Elijah, 2 Kgs 2) or simply died is both a textual and theological question. Heb 11:5 settles the canonical reading but the OT text itself is carefully indirect.
    5:1-32The Antediluvian Genealogy — Extraordinary Ages
    MT Lifespans range from 777 years (Lamech) to 969 years (Methuselah). LXX readings differ substantially for some entries. Samaritan Pentateuch offers a third set of figures. The MT numbers are lower than the LXX's for some patriarchs.
    The textual tradition is complex and the original numbers uncertain. Whether the numbers are literal, symbolic (multiples of sacred numbers), or a different counting system (months? decades?) remains debated.

    Scholarly Debates

    The Antediluvian Lifespans: Literal, Symbolic, or Alternative Counting?
    Literal
    Whitcomb, Morris, Sarfati
    The numbers are straightforward. Pre-flood conditions (different atmosphere, different diet, different genetic integrity) permitted much longer lifespans. The gradual decline after the flood (traceable through the post-flood genealogy) is consistent with deteriorating conditions.
    Symbolic/Theological
    Cassuto, Westermann, Sarna
    Ancient Near Eastern king-lists also include extraordinary reigns (tens of thousands of years). The antediluvian patriarchs are presented with dignity and longevity commensurate with their status as the founders of civilization. The numbers communicate theological significance, not biography.
    Alternative counting
    Humphreys, Young
    Some proposed that the numbers reflect months rather than years (making Methuselah's 969 months = 80 years), but this creates problems with the ages at fatherhood being implausibly young.
    The literal reading requires pre-flood conditions distinctly different from today. The symbolic reading fits the ANE context but requires explaining why the text presents numbers as biographical data. The debate remains open; most critical scholars favour a symbolic or conventional reading; most evangelical scholars favour the literal.
    The Sons of God in Chapter 6 — Anticipation
    Divine beings
    The dominant ancient reading (1 Enoch, LXX, Philo, Josephus, early Church Fathers)
    The "sons of God" (bene elohim) in 6:2 are divine beings/fallen angels who intermarried with human women — a transgression of the Creator-creature boundary precipitating the flood.
    Sethite line
    Augustine, Calvin, Luther, most Reformed interpreters
    The sons of God are the godly line of Seth (ch.5); the daughters of men are the ungodly line of Cain (ch.4). The sin is intermarriage between the covenant and non-covenant lines.
    Rulers/Tyrants
    Cassuto, Wenham (tentatively)
    The sons of God are human rulers (a title used for kings in the ancient Near East) who took multiple wives by force — an abuse of political power.
    The divine-beings reading has the strongest lexical support (bene elohim elsewhere always refers to heavenly beings: Job 1:6; 2:1; 38:7; Ps 29:1) and is reflected in Jude 6-7 and 2 Pet 2:4. The Sethite reading requires importing assumptions not in the text. The ruler reading has some ANE support.

    Theological Themes Tracker

    Genesis 5 is a chapter of profound paradox: a genealogy of death that is simultaneously a record of the transmission of the divine image. Every entry demonstrates mortality (and he died); the chapter as a whole demonstrates the persistence of God's purposes through mortal humanity. Enoch is the key that unlocks the chapter's meaning: the life walked with God transcends the death that ends all other lives. Noah is the hope that ends the chapter: the image-bearing line will survive the coming judgment.
