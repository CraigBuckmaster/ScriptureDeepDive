From Adam to Noah — Introduction & Adam · Verses 1–5
1 This is the written account of Adam’s family line. When God created mankind, he made them in the likeness of God. 2 He created them male and female and blessed them. And he named them “Mankind” when they were created.

3 When Adam had lived 130 years, he had a son in his own likeness, in his own image; and he named him Seth. 4 After Seth was born, Adam lived 800 years and had other sons and daughters. 5 Altogether, Adam lived a total of 930 years, and then he died.

Hebrew Word Study

Sefer toledot Adam — “The written account of Adam’s family line.” Toledot (generations/account) is the structural formula appearing 10 times throughout Genesis. Sefer (scroll/book) implies a written source — an archival record, not just oral tradition. Genesis presents itself as drawing on documents.


Bidmut Elohim — “In the likeness of God.” The creation account of chapter 1 is explicitly recalled here, anchoring the genealogy in the Imago Dei. Then v.3 echoes it: Seth is born “in the likeness, in the image” of Adam. The Imago Dei is transmitted through human generation — but so is fallen humanness. Seth is Adam’s image-bearer as Adam is God’s.


“And then he died” — The sevenfold refrain of Genesis 5 that punctuates every life. After the sevenfold “it was good” of chapter 1 and the Fall’s death sentence in chapter 3, chapter 5 is the ledger: “and he died… and he died… and he died.” The wages of sin, compounding through generations. Each entry in the genealogy is a small death notice.

Historical Context — Ancient Genealogies & Long Lifespans

The extraordinary lifespans of Genesis 5 (Seth: 912 years; Methuselah: 969 years) have generated enormous discussion. The Sumerian King List (c. 2100 BCE) records pre-flood kings reigning for tens of thousands of years — making Genesis 5’s numbers relatively modest by ancient standards. The numbers may be: (1) literal (a different pre-flood biology or antediluvian environment), (2) symbolic (using numbers to confer honour and status), (3) using a different year-counting unit (some scholars suggest “years” here meant shorter periods), or (4) following a genealogical convention where numbers indicate prominence rather than strict chronology. The debate remains live among scholars.


Ancient genealogies served different purposes than modern ones — they established legal lineage, covenant continuity, and theological status. Not every genealogy is complete; “begat” often means “was the ancestor of” rather than strictly “fathered.” Matthew’s genealogy of Jesus (Matt 1) deliberately omits several generations for structural reasons. The Genesis 5 genealogy establishes an unbroken line from Adam to Noah before the flood.

Deep Context

The repetitive structure of chapter 5 is often treated as tedious genealogical filler. It is anything but. The chapter is a meditation on mortality. Each entry follows the same form: X lived N years and had a son Y. After Y was born, X lived M more years and had other sons and daughters. Altogether X lived N+M years, and then he died. The relentless “and then he died” is the chapter’s theological point. The curse of chapter 3 is real, active, and without exception — until Enoch.

Seth to Jared — Verses 6–20
6 When Seth had lived 105 years, he became the father of Enosh. 7 After he became the father of Enosh, Seth lived 807 years and had other sons and daughters. 8 Altogether, Seth lived a total of 912 years, and then he died.

9 When Enosh had lived 90 years, he became the father of Kenan. 10 After he became the father of Kenan, Enosh lived 815 years and had other sons and daughters. 11 Altogether, Enosh lived a total of 905 years, and then he died.

12 When Kenan had lived 70 years, he became the father of Mahalalel. 13 After he became the father of Mahalalel, Kenan lived 840 years and had other sons and daughters. 14 Altogether, Kenan lived a total of 910 years, and then he died.

15 When Mahalalel had lived 65 years, he became the father of Jared. 16 After he became the father of Jared, Mahalalel lived 830 years and had other sons and daughters. 17 Altogether, Mahalalel lived a total of 895 years, and then he died.

18 When Jared had lived 162 years, he became the father of Enoch. 19 After he became the father of Enoch, Jared lived 800 years and had other sons and daughters. 20 Altogether, Jared lived a total of 962 years, and then he died.

The Names & Their Meanings

Each name in the Seth genealogy carries meaning that, read together, forms a theological message. Various scholars have noted that the names — Adam (man), Seth (appointed), Enosh (mortal), Kenan (sorrow), Mahalalel (the blessed God), Jared (shall come down), Enoch (teaching/dedicated), Methuselah (his death shall bring), Lamech (despairing), Noah (rest/comfort) — form a sequence that reads: “Man appointed mortal sorrow. The blessed God shall come down, teaching. His death shall bring the despairing rest.” While this reading requires some caution about etymology, it has struck many as an extraordinary embedded gospel in the genealogy itself.

Enoch — The Exception · Verses 21–27
21 When Enoch had lived 65 years, he became the father of Methuselah. 22 After he became the father of Methuselah, Enoch walked faithfully with God 300 years and had other sons and daughters. 23 Altogether, Enoch lived a total of 365 years. 24 Enoch walked faithfully with God; then he was no more, because God took him.

25 When Methuselah had lived 187 years, he became the father of Lamech. 26 After he became the father of Lamech, Methuselah lived 782 years and had other sons and daughters. 27 Altogether, Methuselah lived a total of 969 years, and then he died.

Hebrew Word Study

Hithallek et ha–Elohim — “Walked faithfully with God.” The verb halak in the Hithpael (reflexive) form means an ongoing, habitual, intimate walk — a lifestyle of communion. The same phrase is used for Noah (6:9). Only these two figures in primeval history receive this description. It stands in stark contrast to the surrounding “and then he died.”


Ve–einennu ki laqah oto Elohim — “Then he was no more, because God took him.” The most remarkable sentence in a chapter of death notices. No death. No funeral. No “and then he died.” The verb laqah (took) is used elsewhere for divine translation: Elijah is “taken up” (2 Kgs 2:3, 5, 9, 10) with the same verb. Enoch is the first rapture — the first human to bypass death.


365 years — Enoch’s lifespan is notably the shortest in the chapter and corresponds exactly to the solar year. Whether intentional or coincidental, ancient interpreters seized on this. Second Temple texts (1 Enoch, Jubilees) made Enoch a major apocalyptic figure — the recipient of heavenly secrets, including knowledge of the solar calendar. Jude quotes 1 Enoch 1:9 as Scripture (Jude 14–15).

Deep Context — Enoch as Fissure in the Refrain

The genius of Genesis 5’s structure is that the monotonous death-refrain sets up Enoch’s entry as a rupture. Ten generations of “and then he died” — and then, midway through: silence where death should be. Enoch walked with God. God took him. The genealogy of death contains a seed of deathlessness, planted exactly in the middle of the pre-flood world.


This is the first hint in Scripture that death is not the last word. It will develop through Elijah’s translation (2 Kgs 2), the resurrection imagery of Ezekiel 37, and reach fulfilment in the resurrection of Christ and the promise of bodily resurrection for believers (1 Cor 15). Enoch’s “not dying” is the early signal that the death sentence of chapter 3 has not entirely closed off the possibility of life.


Methuselah (v.25–27) holds the record at 969 years — the oldest human in Scripture. His name possibly means “his death shall bring” or “man of the dart/weapon.” Tradition notes that Methuselah died in the year of the flood, possibly the very same year — as if God delayed the flood during his lifetime.

Cross-References

  • Heb 11:5“By faith Enoch was taken from this life, so that he did not experience death.” The NT reads Enoch’s translation as an act of faith — he pleased God.
  • Jude 14–15Jude quotes Enoch’s prophecy about the coming judgment of the Lord — drawing on the tradition of Enoch as a prophet (from 1 Enoch 1:9).
  • 2 Kgs 2:11Elijah’s translation in a whirlwind parallels Enoch — the two humans in Scripture who bypass death. Both appear at the Transfiguration (Matt 17:3).
  • 1 Thess 4:17“We who are still alive will be caught up” — the eschatological translation of believers echoes the Enoch pattern.
Lamech & Noah — Verses 28–32
28 When Lamech had lived 182 years, he had a son. 29 He named him Noah and said, “He will comfort us in the labor and painful toil of our hands caused by the ground the LORD has cursed.” 30 After Noah was born, Lamech lived 595 years and had other sons and daughters. 31 Altogether, Lamech lived a total of 777 years, and then he died.

32 After Noah was 500 years old, he became the father of Shem, Ham and Japheth.

Hebrew Word Study

נֹח Noah — From nuah (to rest) or naham (to comfort). Lamech’s naming speech in v.29 is a prophecy: yenah’amenu — “he will comfort us.” After generations under the curse of the ground, Lamech senses that something is about to change. Noah is named as the one who will bring relief from the cursed ground — a hope that Genesis 8 partially fulfils when Noah plants a vineyard and Genesis 9 when God lifts the ground-curse implicitly through the covenant.


777 years — Lamech’s lifespan. The number 7 is the number of completion and covenant throughout Scripture. Lamech (the Seth-line Lamech, not the Cain-line one) lives 777 years — triple-seven, a number of divine completeness — and dies seven years before the flood. The Cain-line Lamech boasted of 77-fold vengeance; the Seth-line Lamech lives 777 years. The numerical contrast is intentional.

Deep Context — The Thread to Noah

The genealogy of chapter 5 is doing more than recording names and ages. It is tracing the covenant thread of the “seed of the woman” (3:15) from Adam through Seth to Noah — the one who will survive the flood and become the vehicle for humanity’s continuation. Every generation in this list is a link in the chain of promise.


The contrast with the Cain genealogy of chapter 4 is now complete. Chapter 4: city-building, music, metallurgy, escalating violence, Lamech’s revenge song. Chapter 5: walking with God, the anomaly of Enoch, the hope of Noah. Both lines have the same origin (Adam); both are fully human; both bear the consequences of the Fall. But one walks with God and the other builds without him.


Noah is introduced as a bridge figure — the last entry in the primeval genealogy and the first figure of the flood narrative. His three sons (Shem, Ham, Japheth) will become the three branches of post-flood humanity in the Table of Nations (Genesis 10). Everything that follows in Genesis 6–9 turns on this final verse of chapter 5.

Cross-References

  • Gen 6:9“Noah was a righteous man, blameless among the people of his time, and he walked faithfully with God” — the same language used for Enoch, echoing Gen 5’s highest commendation.
  • Heb 11:7“By faith Noah, when warned about things not yet seen, in holy fear built an ark” — Noah joins the great cloud of witnesses traced back to Abel and Enoch in Gen 5.
  • Luke 3:36–38Luke’s genealogy of Jesus passes through Shem, Noah, Methuselah, Enoch, Seth, and Adam — anchoring the incarnation in the Genesis 5 genealogy.
  • 1 Pet 3:20Eight people were saved through water in the ark — Peter reads Noah’s survival as a type of baptism and salvation.

Theme Moments This Chapter
Seed/Messiah — vv.3–32

The genealogy threads the image-bearing line from Adam through Seth to Noah — keeping alive the seed of 3:15 through the death refrain. Enoch's translation is the first rupture of death's dominion.

Sin & Judgment — vv.5–31

'And he died' — nine times. The judgment of Gen 3:19 is executed in biography after biography. Death is not metaphorical or spiritual only; it is the concrete end of every human life.

Grace & Rescue — v.24

Enoch — the single exception to the death refrain. God 'takes' him without death. Grace ruptures the pattern of judgment in a single life, pointing forward to resurrection hope.

Intertextual Threading

4 canonical threads — 3 forward (passages that echo this chapter)  |  1 backward (texts this chapter echoes)

v.24 — Enoch translated → Heb 11:5; Jude 14 Fulfillment

Hebrews 11:5 confirms Enoch's translation as faith-based: 'by faith Enoch was taken from this life, so that he did not experience death.' Jude 14 quotes his prophecy (from 1 Enoch 1:9) about the Lord coming with thousands of holy ones — making Enoch the earliest prophet in the canonical sequence. His 'taking' prefigures the resurrection hope: death is not the final word for those who walk with God.

vv.1–32 — genealogy Adam to Noah → Luke 3:36–38 Echo

Luke's genealogy of Jesus traces backward through Noah and Shem all the way to 'Adam, the son of God' — incorporating the entire Genesis 5 genealogy into the lineage of the Messiah. This makes Jesus not merely the son of Abraham (Matt 1) but the son of Adam — the representative of all humanity, not just Israel. Gen 5's death-register becomes the genealogical prelude to the one who defeats death.

v.29 — Noah's comfort → Gen 8:21; Matt 11:28–30 Echo

Lamech names Noah 'comfort' from the cursed ground (v.29). Noah partially fulfills this — after the flood, God promises never to curse the ground again (8:21). But the deeper fulfillment comes in Jesus, who echoes Noah's name in his invitation: 'Come to me, all who are weary... and I will give you rest.' Jesus is the true Noah — the rest-bringer from the curse.

v.3 — image passed down ← Gen 1:26–27 Echo

Gen 5:3 describes Adam's son Seth as born 'in his own likeness, in his own image' — the same language as God creating Adam (tselem, demut). This is theologically significant: image-bearing is transmitted to all of Adam's descendants, but now it passes through a fallen mediator. Every human still bears God's image (Gen 9:6 confirms this post-fall) but also bears Adam's fallen nature.

People — Genesis 5

Enoch
The Exception — v.24

Hanok — devoted, initiated. Seventh from Adam through Seth (mirroring Lamech's seventh place in Cain's line). While every other patriarch has the death refrain 'and he died,' Enoch's entry ends with ve-einennu ki laqah oto Elohim — 'he was not, for God took him.' This is the single rupture of the genealogy's drumbeat of death. He walked with God 365 years (matching the solar year — completeness) then was translated without dying. In the intertestamental period he became the subject of enormous literary expansion (1 Enoch, 2 Enoch, 3 Enoch). The NT cites him in Heb 11:5 as a model of faith and Jude 14 quotes his prophecy from 1 Enoch.

Methuselah
Longest Life — v.27

969 years — the oldest person in Scripture. His name is often read as metu shelah — 'his death shall bring it' — with 'it' being the flood. If the genealogical numbers are taken straightforwardly, Methuselah died the year the flood began (adding his age at Lamech's birth + Lamech's age at Noah's birth + Noah's age at the flood = 1656 years from creation; Methuselah's birth + 969 = exactly 1656). Whether the name etymology is ancient folk-memory or later scribal calculation, the convergence is striking. He is never given dialogue or deeds — his significance is entirely in his longevity and his death-timing.

Noah
The Bridge — v.29

Noah enters the genealogy last, named by his father Lamech with the hope: 'He will comfort us from our work and the painful toil of our hands caused by the ground YHWH has cursed.' The name Noah sounds like nacham (comfort) and nuach (rest). He is the hinge of the primeval history — everything from Adam to Noah (chs 1-5) leads to him; chs 6-9 are his story; ch.10 radiates from him. He is the second Adam: humanity gets a second start through him, with a renewed mandate (9:1) and a new covenant. His failure in ch.9 (the vineyard) underlines that the problem runs deeper than one man.

Ancient Source Texts — The Sumerian King List

The Sumerian King List (c. 2100 BCE)
When kingship was lowered from heaven, kingship was first in Eridu. In Eridu, Alulim became king; he ruled for 28,800 years. Alalgar ruled for 36,000 years... Then the flood swept over. After the flood had swept over, when kingship was lowered from heaven again, kingship was first in Kish.

The Sumerian King List records 8 or 10 pre-flood kings ruling for impossibly long periods (up to 72,000 years each), followed by a flood, after which lifespans drop dramatically. The structural parallel to Genesis 5 is exact: long-lived pre-flood patriarchs → flood → post-flood lifespans declining toward normal. The numbers in both lists are schematic, not biographical. In the Sumerian system the numbers are multiples of 60 and 600 (the Babylonian sexagesimal system); in Genesis the numbers cluster around 7s and patterns meaningful in Hebrew numerology.

Berossus — Babyloniaca (c. 290 BCE)
In the time before the flood there were ten kings who reigned for very many years... The years of all their reigns were 432,000 years. Then came the flood.

Berossus, a Babylonian priest writing in Greek, lists exactly 10 pre-flood kings (matching Genesis's 10 patriarchs from Adam to Noah) reigning for a total of 432,000 years. The convergence on the number 10 is striking — it suggests both traditions draw on a shared ancient conviction that the pre-flood era had exactly 10 great figures. Genesis demythologizes: not divine kings but ordinary humans who walked (or failed to walk) with God.

Reception History — Genesis 5

The Book of Jubilees (c. 160 BCE)

Jubilees, a Second Temple Jewish text, retells Genesis 5 with expanded detail. It synchronizes the patriarchal lifespans with a 49-year jubilee calendar, argues the long ages prove the patriarchs saw the entire pre-flood period personally, and expands Enoch's role enormously: he becomes the first human to write, the inventor of astronomy, and the heavenly scribe who records human sin. Jubilees' Enoch is the source of the vast Enochic literary tradition (1 Enoch, 2 Enoch) that would deeply influence Jewish apocalypticism and early Christianity.

Jude and 2 Peter — Enoch as Prophetic Witness

Jude 14-15 quotes 1 Enoch 1:9 directly — the only NT citation of a pseudepigraphical text as prophetic Scripture. This has generated enormous scholarly debate about the canon. The citation validates Enoch as a historical figure and prophet while not necessarily endorsing all of 1 Enoch as canonical. 2 Peter 2:4-5 places Enoch's tradition (fallen angels, pre-flood judgment) in the same theological sequence as Noah, treating both as paradigms of divine judgment and rescue. The NT thus treats Genesis 5-6 as historically anchored, not merely allegorical.

Early Church — Allegorical Name Meanings

A tradition preserved in early Christian preaching (found in various forms from Irenaeus onward) reads the name meanings of the Genesis 5 patriarchs as a hidden gospel message: Adam (man) Seth (appointed) Enosh (mortal) Kenan (sorrow) Mahalalel (the blessed God) Jared (shall come down) Enoch (teaching) Methuselah (his death shall bring) Lamech (the despairing) Noah (comfort/rest). Reading sequentially: 'Man appointed mortal sorrow; the blessed God shall come down teaching, his death shall bring the despairing comfort/rest.' Whether historically original or not, this reading became popular in evangelical preaching as a demonstration of embedded Messianic prophecy.

Literary Structure — The Death Refrain

Genesis 5 is built on a rigidly repeated literary formula that functions like a drumbeat — nine times the same pattern, then one rupture:

1When X had lived N years, he became the father of Y
2After that, X lived M years and had sons and daughters
3X lived a total of N+M years
AND HE DIED
— repeated 8 times — then —
✦Enoch: walked with God... AND HE WAS NOT

The refrain “and he died” (va-yamot) falls at the end of each entry like a hammer blow — the consequence of Genesis 3 made concrete in biography after biography. Enoch’s missing death notice is one of the most arresting literary ruptures in all of Scripture. The reader who has heard the refrain eight times is stunned by its absence. The chapter is also numerically structured: 10 patriarchs (Adam to Noah), matching the 10 generations of the Sumerian King List and foreshadowing the 10 generations of Shem to Abraham in ch.11.
