Genesis 35

Return to Bethel; Deaths of Rachel and Isaac

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–15 — Return to Bethel; God Renews the Covenant
1Then God said to Jacob, "Go up to Bethel and settle there, and build an altar there to God, who appeared to you when you were fleeing from your brother Esau."2So Jacob said to his household and to all who were with him, "Get rid of the foreign gods you have with you, and purify yourselves and change your clothes.3Then come, let us go up to Bethel, where I will build an altar to God, who answered me in the day of my distress and who has been with me wherever I have gone."4So they gave Jacob all the foreign gods they had and the rings in their ears, and Jacob buried them under the oak at Shechem.5Then they set out, and the terror of God fell on the towns all around them so that no one pursued them.6Jacob and all the people with him came to Luz (that is, Bethel) in the land of Canaan.7There he built an altar, and he called the place El Bethel, because it was there that God revealed himself to him when he was fleeing from his brother.8Now Deborah, Rebekah's nurse, died and was buried under the oak outside Bethel. So it was named Allon Bakuth.9After Jacob returned from Paddan Aram, God appeared to him again and blessed him.10God said to him, "Your name is Jacob, but you will no longer be called Jacob; your name will be Israel." So he named him Israel.11And God said to him, "I am God Almighty; be fruitful and increase in number. A nation and a community of nations will come from you, and kings will be among your descendants.12The land I gave to Abraham and Isaac I also give to you, and I will give this land to your descendants after you."13Then God went up from him at the place where he had talked with him.14Jacob set up a stone pillar at the place where God had talked with him, and he poured out a drink offering on it; he also poured oil on it.15Jacob called the place where God had talked with him Bethel.

Hebrew Word Study

אֱלֹהֵי הַנֵּכָר ʾĕlōhê hannēkār — foreign gods: The gods Jacob commands be put away include Rachel's stolen teraphim (31:19) and whatever other objects his household had accumulated. The purification is comprehensive: gods, earrings (associated with idolatry in Exod 32:2), and garments.

טָהֲרוּ ṭāharū — purify yourselves: Ritual purification is typically associated with the law (Mosaic), but here precedes it. The command to purify before encountering God at a sacred site reflects creational moral order independent of any specific legal code.

אֵימַת אֱלֹהִים ʾêmat ʾĕlōhîm — terror of God: The divine panic that falls on surrounding peoples (v.5) is the same supernatural fear that appears in Exod 23:27 and Josh 2:9. God protects his covenant people through psychological warfare on their enemies.

Historical Context

The return to Bethel is commanded by God as a direct response to the Shechem massacre (ch.34): Jacob must return to the place where he first encountered God and fulfil the vow he made there (28:20-22). The purification of the household — putting away foreign gods, changing garments, purifying themselves — is the first ritual purification scene in the patriarchal narratives. The earrings buried at Shechem are likely the same teraphim-related objects Rachel stole (31:19), now finally disposed of.

Context

God's command to go to Bethel (v.1) immediately follows the Shechem massacre of ch.34 — the spiritual crisis of that event is answered by a call to worship. The burial of foreign gods at Shechem (v.4) resolves the teraphim subplot begun in 31:19. The full purification before ascending to Bethel establishes the pattern of moral and ritual preparation that will characterize Israel's later approach to the sanctuary. The divine terror protecting Jacob on the journey (v.5) is God's providence actively clearing the path — the same hands that wounded Jacob at Peniel now protect him on the road.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 28:20–22Jacob's vow: "If God will be with me... then I will return here." God now commands the fulfillment of the vow twenty years later.
  • Josh 24:23At Shechem, Joshua commands Israel to "put away the foreign gods that are among you" — the same command Jacob gave, at the same location. History rhymes.
  • Exod 23:27God promises Moses: "I will send my terror ahead of you" — the same divine protection operative in v.5.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
35:1

God's command to return to Bethel arrives immediately after the Shechem disaster. The connection is not made explicit but it is implied: Jacob has been living at Shechem rather than moving toward Bethel as commanded (28:21-22). The massacre has made his position untenable. God uses the consequences of human failure to redirect his servant toward the place of covenant renewal.

35:2-4

The household purification — foreign gods surrendered, garments changed, selves purified — is the most explicitly religious act of preparation in the patriarchal narratives. Foreign gods are in the household because of the plunder of Shechem (34:29) and possibly Rachel's teraphim. Jacob acts with patriarchal authority: he commands and the household obeys. The earrings buried under the oak at Shechem represent a complete break with the past.

35:9-12

The covenant renewal at Bethel repeats and consolidates every element of the Abrahamic covenant: the name Israel confirmed, the command to be fruitful and multiply, the promise of a nation of kings, the gift of the land. This is the third major covenant confirmation in Jacob's life (after 28:13-15 and 31:3) and the most comprehensive. God marks it with a pillar and a drink offering — Jacob's most formal act of worship.

35:14-15

The pillar Jacob erects is the same type of monument he erected at the first Bethel visit (28:18). But now it receives a drink offering as well as oil — a progression in the formality of worship. The double naming of Bethel (already named Bethel at 28:19, named again here) has puzzled interpreters; Sarna reads it as confirming the sacred character of the place across two generations.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
35:2

The command to put away foreign gods echoes Joshua 24:14-15, where Joshua summons Israel to "put away the gods your ancestors worshiped beyond the Euphrates." Sarna: "Jacob's household purification at Shechem is the patriarchal precedent for Israel's covenant renewal. The pattern — journey to the sacred site, ritual purification, surrender of foreign objects, covenant encounter — is the template for all subsequent covenant renewals in Israelite history."

35:10

The confirmation of the name Israel at Bethel is significant: the name was given at the Jabbok (32:28) in a private nocturnal encounter; now it is publicly confirmed at the covenant sanctuary. Sarna observes that the public confirmation at Bethel makes the name official in the covenant community, not merely in Jacob's own experience. The people of Israel are named at Bethel, the founding shrine of the covenant.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
35:4

The earrings are buried under the oak — a detail of precision that Alter notes creates a sense of ritual completeness. The objects associated with foreign religion are not thrown away or burned but buried, placed in the earth, removed from the realm of the living. The oak at Shechem will appear again in Joshua 24 as a covenant site. Alter: "The geography of the patriarchal narratives has a long memory. Places of significance recur."

35:11-12

The covenant formula — "I am God Almighty; be fruitful and increase in number; a nation and a community of nations will come from you; kings will be among your descendants" — is the fullest statement of the covenant promise in the Jacob narrative. Alter notes it deliberately echoes both the Abrahamic covenant (17:1-8) and the creation blessing (1:28). Jacob's covenant is a recapitulation of the entire Edenic-Abrahamic trajectory.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
35:1

Calvin on God's command to return to Bethel: "God, like a careful physician, applies the remedy to the disease. Jacob, through excessive security, has fallen into many sins; God arouses him and recalls him to himself." The Shechem disaster is, in Calvin's reading, a providential correction: God uses the consequences of sin to drive his servant back to the place of covenant renewal. Divine discipline and divine guidance are the same act.

35:9-12

Calvin on the covenant renewal: "God had formerly appeared to Jacob at Bethel; but now he more clearly confirms the covenant which he had made with Abraham... He speaks of things not yet existing as though they already had existence." The covenant promise — kings, nations, the land — is spoken as present fact about a future reality. Calvin's reading anticipates the Davidic monarchy and the messianic kingdom as the content of this promise.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
35:2

The NET note on "foreign gods" (elohey hannechar) identifies these as images or symbols of non-Israelite deities, distinct from the God of Jacob. The note observes the specific mention of earrings alongside the idols — suggesting they were amuletic jewelry associated with pagan religion. The note cross-references the Exodus golden-calf episode (32:2-3), where earrings are similarly surrendered for a religious purpose, though with opposite effect.

35:11

The NET note on "God Almighty" (El Shaddai) surveys the three main etymological proposals: (1) "God of the mountain" (Akkadian cognate shadu); (2) "the All-Sufficient One" (traditional rendering); (3) "God of the breasts" (shad = breast). The note favours (1) or uncertainty, observing that El Shaddai is the patriarchal divine title par excellence (appearing in 17:1; 28:3; 35:11; 43:14; 48:3) and is distinguished from the Mosaic name YHWH (Exod 6:3).

Verses 16–29 — Rachel Dies; Reuben's Sin; Isaac Dies
16Then they moved on from Bethel. While they were still some distance from Ephrath, Rachel began to give birth and had great difficulty.17And as she was having great difficulty in childbirth, the midwife said to her, "Don't despair, for you have another son."18As she breathed her last—for she was dying—she named her son Ben-Oni. But his father named him Benjamin.19So Rachel died and was buried on the way to Ephrath (that is, Bethlehem).20Over her tomb Jacob set up a pillar, and to this day that pillar marks Rachel's tomb.21Israel moved on again and pitched his tent beyond Migdal Eder.22While Israel was living in that region, Reuben went in and slept with his father's concubine Bilhah, and Israel heard of it. Jacob had twelve sons:23The sons of Leah: Reuben the firstborn of Jacob, Simeon, Levi, Judah, Issachar and Zebulun.24The sons of Rachel: Joseph and Benjamin.25The sons of Rachel's servant Bilhah: Dan and Naphtali.26The sons of Leah's servant Zilpah: Gad and Asher. These were the sons of Jacob, who were born to him in Paddan Aram.27Jacob came home to his father Isaac in Mamre, near Kiriath Arba (that is, Hebron), where Abraham and Isaac had stayed.28Isaac lived a hundred and eighty years.29Then he breathed his last and died and was gathered to his people, old and full of years. And his sons Esau and Jacob buried him.

Hebrew Word Study

אֵל שַׁדַּי ʾēl šadday — God Almighty: The name God used with Abraham (17:1) and Isaac. Its use here links Jacob explicitly into the patriarchal covenant line. El Shaddai is the name particularly associated with the covenantal promise of land, descendants, and blessing.

מַצֵּבָה maṣṣēbāh — pillar / stele: A standing stone used as a memorial to divine encounter. Jacob set one up at Bethel in 28:18; he sets up another here (v.14), along with a drink offering and oil — the first mention of a libation in Genesis.

Historical Context

The deaths in this section — Deborah (Rebekah's nurse), Rachel (in childbirth), and Isaac — create a dirge-like quality for the end of Genesis 35. Rachel's death in childbirth near Bethlehem, naming her son Ben-Oni ("son of my sorrow") while Jacob renames him Benjamin ("son of my right hand"), is one of the most pathos-filled moments in the patriarchal narratives. The pillar Jacob erects over her tomb marks the first formal grave-monument in the Bible.

Context

The Bethel theophany is the third major covenant encounter in Jacob's life (first: Gen 28; second: Peniel, Gen 32; third: here). Each encounter marks a stage of Jacob's transformation. The name change confirmed here (v.10) was first given at Peniel — the double confirmation at two sacred sites seals the new identity. God's speech in vv.11–12 recapitulates the Abrahamic covenant in its fullness: El Shaddai (the name), fruitfulness (the commission), nations and kings (the scope), and the land (the specific promise).

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 17:1El Shaddai appears to Abraham with identical covenant language: be fruitful, multiply, nations and kings.
  • Gen 28:18–19Jacob's first Bethel pillar. Now a second pillar is set up at the same site — the vow fulfilled, the site consecrated again.
  • Gen 48:3–4On his deathbed Jacob recalls this exact Bethel theophany — it remains his most definitive encounter with God.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
35:16-18

Rachel dies giving birth to Benjamin — the child she had longed for (30:24: "may the LORD add to me another son") costs her life. Her dying name for him — Ben-Oni, "son of my sorrow" — is the name of a woman who knows she is dying. Jacob's renaming to Benjamin is a father's act of redemption: "son of my right hand" — the son of honour, the favoured position. Both names are true; Jacob chooses which truth will define the child.

35:19-20

The pillar erected over Rachel's tomb is the first grave monument in the Bible. Jacob marks her death with the same stone-pillar ritual he used at Bethel for divine encounters. The tomb at Bethlehem will be remembered by Jeremiah (31:15) as the place from which Rachel weeps for her children going into exile. Her tomb becomes a site of national mourning.

35:22

Reuben's sin — lying with Bilhah, his father's concubine — is reported in a single verse with no narrative elaboration. The enormity of the sin is in its brevity: no explanation, no response from Jacob at the time, no immediate consequence. The reckoning comes at Jacob's deathbed (49:4): "you will no longer excel, for you went up onto your father's bed." The sin is remembered for a generation.

35:27-29

Isaac's death at 180 years is reported with the same formulaic dignity as Abraham's (25:7-10): he died at a good old age, old and full of years. Esau and Jacob together bury him, as Isaac and Ishmael buried Abraham. Even in death, the estranged brothers find a moment of common act. The patriarchal generation closes with the same formula and the same fraternal reconciliation that marked its beginning.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
35:18

The two names for the same child — Ben-Oni (Rachel's) and Benjamin (Jacob's) — constitute one of the most poignant name-pairs in Genesis. Sarna: "Rachel names from her death-experience; Jacob names from his hope for the child's future. Both names are authentic; both reflect real dimensions of the birth. The mother's sorrow and the father's hope are equally true. Jacob's renaming does not erase Rachel's; it holds both in tension."

35:20

The pillar over Rachel's tomb — mattsevat qevurat Rachel — is the first grave marker in the Bible. Sarna notes the irony: Jacob has erected pillars at Bethel (divine encounters) and at Mizpah (covenant boundary); now he erects one over a human grave. The same stone-setting ritual that marks divine presence and covenant boundary now marks human loss. The monuments of faith and the monuments of grief use the same material and the same act.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
35:16-18

The Rachel death scene is narrated with the same compression that has characterised her throughout. Alter: "Rachel's life in the narrative has been a series of competing desires — children, husband, status — always in tension. Her death, like her life, is in the midst of what she most wanted (the second son) being given at the cost of what she could not afford to give (her life). Ben-Oni is the perfect name for a woman whose every gain came wrapped in loss."

35:22

The report of Reuben's sin is syntactically cut short in the Hebrew: "And Reuben went and lay with Bilhah his father's concubine; and Israel heard." The next clause is a genealogical formula, with no response from Jacob recorded. Alter: "The truncated syntax enacts Jacob's (non-)response. The narrative stops — Israel heard — and moves on. The response will come 25 years later at the deathbed. Some crimes require a long delay before judgment."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
35:18

Calvin on Rachel's dying and Benjamin's naming: "Jacob corrects the inauspicious name given by his wife, not refusing to acknowledge the grief which Rachel had expressed by calling her son Ben-Oni, but correcting the omen... He who had prevailed over the angel in prayer was not going to be overcome by sorrow at his wife's death." The renaming is an act of faith — Jacob refuses to let grief define the child born of that grief.

35:22

Calvin on Reuben's sin: "This was a detestable wickedness. And it is possible that Reuben, elated by the hope of the chief authority among his brethren — since he was the first-born — wickedly endeavoured to seize that honour, which in kingdoms frequently comes to him who has possession of the king's wives." Reuben's act may have been a claim to household authority as well as a violation — in ancient contexts, possession of the king's concubines was a claim to succession.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
35:18

The NET note on Ben-Oni discusses the two proposed meanings: "son of my sorrow/wickedness" (from awen = wickedness/sorrow) or "son of my strength" (from on = strength/vigour, as in Deut 21:17). The note observes the text's explanation — "as she was dying she named him" — makes the sorrow reading more contextually likely. Benjamin (Binyamin = "son of the right hand") is explained as the favoured position, associated with strength and honour.

35:29

The NET note on Isaac's death formula — "he breathed his last and died, and was gathered to his people, old and full of years" — compares the identical formula for Abraham (25:8). The note observes "gathered to his people" is the biblical idiom for death understood as joining the community of deceased ancestors. It is not an afterlife statement per se but an expression of communal continuity beyond death.

Ancient Sources

LXX, Hippodromon
The Septuagint places Rachel's burial "at the hippodrome" — a geographic identifier that has not been located but reflects the translator's attempt to clarify the site.
The exact location of "Rachel's Tomb" has been disputed since antiquity; the traditional site near Bethlehem has been venerated since at least the 4th century AD.
Jeremiah 31:15
Jeremiah's use of Rachel's tomb as a symbol of national grief (mothers weeping for exiled children) shows the tomb's continued cultural significance 1,300 years after her death.
Matthew's quotation of Jeremiah at the massacre of the innocents (Matt 2:18) gives Rachel's geography three layers of theological meaning: original death, exile, Messiah.

Reception History

Jerome, Letter 108
Jerome describes pilgrims visiting Rachel's Tomb in his time (4th c. AD) as a major site of veneration — the site of maternal grief becoming a site of prayer for mothers.
Calvin, Commentary
Calvin notes the irony of Rachel's prayer being answered fatally — a meditation on divine sovereignty and the cost of answered prayer. God gives what is asked; he does not always protect the asker from the consequences.

Literary Structure

Return to Bethel — 35:1-8God commands return. Household purification. Foreign gods buried. Terror of God protects. Deborah's death (Rebekah's nurse) — end of a generation.
El Shaddai appears. Name Israel confirmed. Full covenant formula: fruitful, nations, kings, the land. Pillar and drink offering.
Benjamin born. Rachel dies naming him Ben-Oni. Jacob renames: Benjamin. Pillar over her tomb — first grave monument.
Reuben's Sin — 35:22One-verse report. Reuben lies with Bilhah. Israel heard. Silence. Judgment deferred to Gen 49.
Isaac Dies — 35:27-29Jacob returns to Isaac at Mamre. Isaac dies at 180. Esau and Jacob bury him — the estranged brothers united one final time.
Genesis 35 is the chapter of renewal and ending. The covenant is renewed at Bethel with the fullest formula yet. But the covenant renewal is surrounded by death: Deborah dies, Rachel dies, Isaac dies. The generation that received the promise is passing; the generation that will carry it into Egypt is being completed. The pillar over Rachel's tomb stands between Bethlehem and the covenant — between human loss and divine faithfulness.

Textual Notes

35:22רְאוּבֵן שָׁכַב — the truncated report
MT "Reuben went and lay with Bilhah... and Israel heard." The Hebrew then begins immediately: "Now the sons of Jacob were twelve." No response, no consequence is recorded at the time. The brevity is syntactically striking.
The truncated syntax (action → "Israel heard" → topic change) is a narrative technique that withholds both Jacob's immediate response and the moral evaluation. The reckoning comes 25 years later (49:3-4). Some scholars read the syntax as a scribal marker of a lacuna (missing text); most read it as deliberate restraint that heightens the weight of the unreported response.

Scholarly Debates

Rachel's Death and Tomb: Historical Memory
Near Bethlehem
MT and Jer 31:15
Gen 35:19 locates the tomb "on the road to Ephrath (that is, Bethlehem)." Jeremiah 31:15 places Rachel weeping at Ramah for her children. The traditional site near Bethlehem has ancient attestation.
Near Ramah (Benjamin's territory)
1 Sam 10:2; Jer 31:15
1 Samuel 10:2 places Rachel's tomb "in the territory of Benjamin" — north of Jerusalem, near Ramah. Jeremiah 31:15 is consistent with this location. Some scholars argue the Bethlehem identification is secondary.
The two locations are about 10 miles apart. The discrepancy may reflect two different traditions about Rachel's burial site. The theological point — Rachel weeping for her children in exile (Jer 31:15; Matt 2:18) — applies regardless of the precise location. The pillar Jacob erects (v.20) marks a real site of patriarchal memory.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 35 is structured around the paradox of covenant renewal and death. God reappears, confirms the name Israel, restates the full covenant promise — and yet the chapter is framed by three deaths. The covenant promises are being given to a people in the process of losing those who received them first. The faithfulness of God and the mortality of his servants are placed side by side without resolution.
