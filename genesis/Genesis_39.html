Genesis 39

Joseph in Potiphar's House

Hebrew

Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration. What prompted it: A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–10 — Joseph in Potiphar's House; the Lord with Joseph
1Now Joseph had been taken down to Egypt. Potiphar, an Egyptian who was one of Pharaoh's officials, the captain of the guard, bought him from the Ishmaelites who had taken him there.2The Lord was with Joseph so that he prospered, and he lived in the house of his Egyptian master.3When his master saw that the Lord was with him and that the Lord gave him success in everything he did,4Joseph found favor in his eyes and became his attendant. Potiphar put him in charge of his household, and he entrusted to his care everything he owned.5From the time he put him in charge of his household and of all that he owned, the Lord blessed the household of the Egyptian because of Joseph. The blessing of the Lord was on everything Potiphar had, both in the house and in the field.6So Potiphar left everything he had in Joseph's care; with Joseph in charge, he did not concern himself with anything except the food he ate. Now Joseph was well-built and handsome,7and after a while his master's wife took notice of Joseph and said, "Come to bed with me!"8But he refused. "With me in charge," he told her, "my master does not concern himself with anything in the house; everything he owns he has entrusted to my care.9No one is greater in this house than I am. My master has withheld nothing from me except you, because you are his wife. How then could I do such a wicked thing and sin against God?"10And though she spoke to Joseph day after day, he refused to go to bed with her or even be with her.

Hebrew Word Study

יְהוָה הָיָה עִם YHWH hāyāh ʿim — the LORD was with: The phrase "the LORD was with Joseph" is the chapter's theological anchor (vv.2,3,21,23). The same formula appeared at Bethel (28:15: "I will be with you") and at Jacob's departure (31:3). It is the covenantal presence formula — God's personal accompaniment of the chosen servant.

מַצְלִיחַ maṣlîaḥ — successful / prosperous: From the root ṣālaḥ, "to advance, prosper, succeed." Prosperity in the Joseph narrative is always attributed to divine presence, not human skill — though Joseph's skill is real and visible.

מְשָׁרְתֵהוּ mĕšārĕtēhû — attended him / served him: The verb šārat (serve, minister) is used later for priestly service (Exod 28:35). Joseph's service of Potiphar is rendered with the full care and competence that would later characterize priestly ministry — he serves a pagan master as unto the LORD.

Context

The fourfold "LORD was with Joseph" in this chapter is the interpretive key to the entire Joseph narrative. Joseph does not explain his success through intelligence or initiative — the text consistently attributes it to divine presence. This presence is not conditional on circumstances: it operates in the house of a pagan, in the prison of a corrupt official, and eventually in the court of Pharaoh. The phrase links back to God's promise to Jacob at Bethel (28:15) and forward to the Exodus commission to Moses (Exod 3:12: "I will be with you").

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 28:15God's promise: "I will be with you." Fulfilled first in Jacob's life, now operating through Joseph.
  • Acts 7:9–10Stephen: "God was with him" — the NT directly quotes Genesis 39 as the key to understanding Joseph's story.
  • Matt 1:23"God with us" (Emmanuel) is the terminal fulfillment of the "God was with him" formula — from Joseph's Potiphar's-house presence to the incarnation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
39:2–3

The remarkable detail of v.3 is that Potiphar, a pagan Egyptian, could see that the LORD was with Joseph. Divine blessing is visible to unbelievers when it operates through a person of genuine integrity. Potiphar does not become a worshipper of YHWH, but he recognizes a category of divine favor that his own gods don't explain. The blessing of God on Joseph overflows to bless the entire Egyptian household (v.5) — the Abrahamic promise ("through you all the families of the earth will be blessed") is already operating in Potiphar's house.

Verses 11–23 — False Accusation; Joseph in Prison; God with Him

But while Joseph was there in the prison,21the Lord was with him; he showed him kindness and granted him favor in the eyes of the prison warden.22So the warden put Joseph in charge of all those held in the prison, and he was made responsible for all that was done there.23The warden paid no attention to anything under Joseph's care, because the Lord was with Joseph and gave him success in whatever he did.

Hebrew Word Study

חֶסֶד ḥesed — steadfast love / lovingkindness: In v.21 the LORD shows Joseph ḥesed — the covenantal loyalty that undergirds all of God's dealings with his people. This is the first use of ḥesed in connection with Joseph — it will sustain him through prison.

הָרָעָה הַגְּדֹלָה hārāʿāh haggĕdōlāh — this great wickedness: Joseph's refusal frames the issue as theological: "how can I sin against God?" He does not primarily appeal to loyalty to Potiphar (though he mentions that too) — the ultimate ground of his refusal is the fear of God.

בִּגְדוֹ bigdô — his garment: The garment motif continues: Joseph's robe of many colors was used to deceive Jacob (ch.37); now his garment is used to falsely accuse him. The garment is again both evidence and instrument of his apparent undoing.

Historical Context

The motif of a false accusation of rape against a virtuous man by a spurned woman appears in Egyptian literature — notably the "Tale of Two Brothers" (c. 1200 BC), where Anubis's wife makes the same accusation against his brother Bata. This does not mean Joseph's story is derived from the Egyptian tale; rather, both reflect a known social dynamic in the ancient Near East. The parallel shows the realism of the Genesis account within its cultural context.

Context

Joseph's refusal is framed in explicitly theological terms: "How can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" (v.9). He does not primarily say "it would betray Potiphar" — though that is mentioned — he says "it would be sin against God." This is the theology of integrity: behavior is governed by the vertical relationship, not only the horizontal. The prison (v.20) is Joseph's reward for righteousness — and God's "steadfast love" (ḥesed, v.21) meets him there. The pattern: integrity leads to suffering leads to elevation, repeated twice (house → prison → court).

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
39:9

Joseph's response to Potiphar's wife is the moral centerpiece of the chapter. He does not appeal primarily to self-interest, to loyalty to Potiphar, or to the practical risks. He appeals to God: "How then can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" This is the fear-of-God ethic — behavior governed by awareness of divine judgment rather than merely social consequences. Joseph in Egypt, stripped of every external support (family, homeland, freedom), has nothing left but God. That is precisely why he cannot sin against God: God is all he has.

39:20–21

Joseph is imprisoned for doing the right thing. His integrity costs him his position and his freedom. Then: "the LORD was with Joseph." The formula that explained his success in Potiphar's house (vv.2,3) now explains his survival in prison (vv.21,23). God's presence is not tied to Joseph's circumstances. It follows him into the pit, into the house, into the prison — wherever Joseph is, God is. This is the covenant promise of Bethel operative in every context.

Ancient Sources

Tale of Two Brothers (Egypt, c.1200 BC)
An Egyptian story where a wife falsely accuses her husband's brother of attempted rape after he rejects her advances — strikingly parallel to Genesis 39.
The parallel demonstrates that the social dynamic of Genesis 39 was recognized in Egyptian culture; it does not imply literary dependence but cultural realism.
Egyptian Prison Records
Papyri from the Middle Kingdom (c. 2000–1700 BC) document royal prison facilities where high-status prisoners were held — consistent with the "place where the king's prisoners were confined" (v.20).
The administrative detail of v.20 is consistent with Egyptian records.

Reception History

Chrysostom, Homilies on Genesis
Joseph's flight from temptation is the model of Christian chastity — the refusal to remain in the presence of temptation, the literal fleeing. "He left his garment and fled."
Calvin, Commentary
The fourfold "LORD was with Joseph" is for Calvin the chapter's sermon: divine presence is the explanation for both prosperity and survival in adversity. The believer is never alone.
Joseph as type of Christ
Patristic and Reformed interpreters note the parallel: the beloved son, falsely accused, imprisoned, raised to rule and save. Joseph's integrity in the face of false accusation prefigures Christ before Pilate.

The chapter mirrors itself around the central flight scene (v.12). The "LORD was with Joseph" formula at beginning and end (vv.2–3 / vv.21,23) shows that prison is not a departure from God's blessing — it is a continuation of it in a different context. The garment is the symbolic thread connecting prosperity and false accusation.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 39 is the theological heart of the early Joseph narrative. The fourfold "LORD was with Joseph" declares that divine presence is not contingent on favorable circumstances — it accompanies the faithful servant into slavery, into temptation, and into prison. Joseph's integrity is not self-generated; it is the fruit of knowing that sin is primarily against God.
