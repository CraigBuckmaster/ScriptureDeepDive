Genesis 40

The Dreams of the Cupbearer and Baker

Hebrew
Historical
Context
Cross-Ref
Places
Timeline

Author: Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120. When written: Composed during the wilderness period, c.1446–1406 BC, most likely during the 40 years of wandering following the Exodus. The material in Genesis was either received through revelation or drawn from existing genealogical and narrative traditions that Moses compiled and shaped under divine inspiration. What prompted it: A generation born in the wilderness needed to know who they were and where they came from. Genesis answers three foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? Why are we here? It grounds Israel's identity in the creation of the world, the calling of Abraham, and the covenant promises that explain why a nation of former slaves is about to inherit Canaan. Without Genesis, the Exodus has no context; with it, the Exodus is the fulfilment of promises made four centuries earlier to the patriarchs.

Verses 1–8 — The Cupbearer and Baker in Prison; Their Dreams
1Some time later, the cupbearer and the baker of the king of Egypt offended their master, the king of Egypt.2Pharaoh was angry with his two officials, the chief cupbearer and the chief baker,3and put them in custody in the house of the captain of the guard, in the same prison where Joseph was confined.4The captain of the guard assigned them to Joseph, and he attended them. After they had been in custody for some time,5each of the two men—the cupbearer and the baker of the king of Egypt, who were being held in prison—had a dream the same night, and each dream had a meaning of its own.6When Joseph came to them the next morning, he saw that they were dejected.7So he asked Pharaoh's officials who were in custody with him in his master's house, "Why do you look so sad today?"8"We both had dreams," they answered, "but there is no one to interpret them." Then Joseph said to them, "Do not interpretations belong to God? Tell me your dreams."

Hebrew Word Study

פִּתְרֹן pitrôn — interpretation: A word of Egyptian origin appearing only in the Joseph story. Its use grounds the narrative in authentic Egyptian cultural context — dream interpretation was a specialized priestly function in Egypt.

הֲלוֹא לֵאלֹהִים פִּתְרֹנִים hălôʾ lēʾlōhîm pitrōnîm — do not interpretations belong to God?: Joseph's response is theologically crucial: he does not claim personal skill but attributes the capacity for dream interpretation entirely to God. He is a conduit, not a practitioner. This is the same self-understanding Joseph maintains before Pharaoh (41:16).

Historical Context

Egyptian dream interpretation was a sophisticated priestly discipline. The Chester Beatty Papyrus III (c. 1279–1213 BC) contains a Dream Book cataloguing over 200 dream types and their interpretations. Oneiromancy (dream divination) was practiced across the ancient Near East. The cupbearer (chief of butlers) and chief baker were high court officials — their presence in a royal prison reflects the political volatility of Egyptian court life.

Context

Chapter 40 is structurally a hinge. It connects Joseph's prison period to his court period, and it prepares Pharaoh's need for an interpreter (ch.41). The dreams of the cupbearer and baker mirror each other in form but diverge in outcome — the same motifs (three) yield opposite results (life/death). Joseph's pastoral attention to troubled men (v.6-7: "he saw that they were troubled... why are your faces downcast?") shows a quality of care that will characterize his leadership throughout.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
40:8

Joseph's response — "Do not interpretations belong to God? Please tell them to me" — contains both humility and boldness. He deflects credit to God and then immediately offers to be God's instrument. This is the proper posture of the spiritually gifted person: the gift is not mine, but I will steward it for your benefit. Joseph has spent years in the pit and the prison; he has not become bitter or self-protective. He reaches toward these troubled men with genuine pastoral concern.

40:14–15

Joseph's request to the cupbearer — "remember me when it is well with you... mention me to Pharaoh, and so get me out of this place" — is not faithless; it is prudent. Joseph uses every legitimate means available. The cupbearer's forgetting (v.23) is the last delay before the appointed moment. Two more years will pass. God's timing is not our timing, and the waiting period is not wasted — it is the precise calibration that will position Joseph before Pharaoh at the exact moment of Pharaoh's need.

Verses 9–23 — Joseph Interprets the Dreams; the Forgotten Promise
9So the chief cupbearer told Joseph his dream. He said to him, "In my dream I saw a vine in front of me,10and on the vine were three branches. As soon as it budded, it blossomed, and its clusters ripened into grapes.11Pharaoh's cup was in my hand, and I took the grapes, squeezed them into Pharaoh's cup and put the cup in his hand."12"This is what it means," Joseph said to him. "The three branches are three days.13Within three days Pharaoh will lift up your head and restore you to your position, and you will put Pharaoh's cup in his hand, just as you used to do when you were his cupbearer.14But when all goes well with you, remember me and show me kindness; mention me to Pharaoh and get me out of this prison.15I was forcibly carried off from the land of the Hebrews, and even here I have done nothing to deserve being put in a dungeon."16When the chief baker saw that Joseph had given a favorable interpretation, he said to Joseph, "I too had a dream: On my head were three baskets of bread.17In the top basket were all kinds of baked goods for Pharaoh, but the birds were eating them out of the basket on my head."18"This is what it means," Joseph said. "The three baskets are three days.19Within three days Pharaoh will lift off your head and impale your body on a pole. And the birds will eat away your flesh."20Now the third day was Pharaoh's birthday, and he gave a feast for all his officials. He lifted up the heads of the chief cupbearer and the chief baker in the presence of his officials:21He restored the chief cupbearer to his position, so that he once again put the cup into Pharaoh's hand—22but he impaled the chief baker, just as Joseph had said to them in his interpretation.23The chief cupbearer, however, did not remember Joseph; he forgot him.

Hebrew Word Study

נָשָׂא רֹאשׁ nāśāʾ rōʾš — lift up the head: The idiom means to restore someone to favor (v.13) — and also to execute someone (v.19). The exact same phrase carries opposite meanings for the two men, a dark pun that only becomes clear in v.20.

יוֹם הֻלֶּדֶת yôm hulledet — birthday / day of birth: Pharaoh's birthday feast is the only birthday celebration mentioned in the Torah. Birthdays as royal celebrations are well attested in Egyptian papyri.

שָׁכַח šākaḥ — forgot: The cupbearer "forgot" (šākaḥ) Joseph. The word echoes through the Joseph narrative as a contrast to God's remembering (41:1: "after two whole years"). Human forgetting sets the stage for divine remembering.

Context

The parallel dreams (three branches/three baskets) with opposite outcomes demonstrate that the same divine gift of interpretation serves both good and difficult news. Joseph does not soften the baker's interpretation — he delivers the death sentence clearly. This integrity in hard words is consistent with Joseph's character throughout. The final verse — "the chief cupbearer did not remember Joseph, but forgot him" — is the low point of the chapter and the setup for ch.41. Two more years will pass before the moment arrives. The delay is part of the plan.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
40:20–22

The fulfillment of both dreams within three days confirms that Joseph's interpretive gift is genuine — the dreams are accurate, the interpretation is accurate, the timing is exact. This is God validating his instrument before the great test with Pharaoh. The baker is executed; the cupbearer is restored — exactly as Joseph said. The chapter demonstrates that divine interpretation through Joseph is reliable. This matters enormously: when Joseph stands before Pharaoh in ch.41, the reader already knows his interpretations can be trusted.

40:23

The cupbearer's forgetting is the final human failure in Joseph's story. His brothers sold him, Potiphar imprisoned him, and now the cupbearer forgets him. From every human direction, Joseph's situation is hopeless. Yet the text moves directly from the cupbearer's forgetting to God's remembering: chapter 41 opens "after two whole years, Pharaoh dreamed." The human forgetting and the divine timing are not in competition — God uses the two-year delay to position Joseph before Pharaoh at the exact moment it will be most effective.

Ancient Sources

Chester Beatty Papyrus III
The Egyptian Dream Book (c. 1279–1213 BC) catalogs dream symbols and their meanings — vines, birds, bread all appear as interpretive categories.
The papyrus confirms that Joseph's context (professional dream interpretation, specific symbols like the vine and baskets) reflects authentic Egyptian dream culture.
Egyptian Birthday Feasts
Papyri document royal birthday celebrations as significant court events with pardons and appointments — consistent with v.20's use of Pharaoh's birthday as the occasion for the cupbearer's restoration.
The historical detail of v.20 is culturally accurate.

Reception History

Calvin, Commentary
The cupbearer's forgetting is not a setback to God's plan — it is part of it. Calvin argues that divine timing is always better than the timing we would choose for ourselves. Joseph's waiting produces the exact positioning needed for Pharaoh's moment.
Luther, Lectures
The chapter demonstrates that God uses even human ingratitude and forgetfulness as instruments of his purpose. The cupbearer's failure to remember Joseph leads, two years later, to the greatest possible outcome.

Literary Structure

Setup — vv.1–4Two officials imprisoned; Joseph assigned to them
The Question — vv.5–8Dreams without interpreters; Joseph's theological response
Cupbearer's Dream — vv.9–13Three branches → three days → restoration
Baker's Dream — vv.16–19Three baskets → three days → execution
Fulfillment — vv.20–22Third day: exactly as Joseph said
The Delay — v.23Cupbearer forgets — two years will pass
The chapter's structure is a controlled parallel: two dreams, same structure (three + three days), opposite outcomes. The interpretive gift is demonstrated twice before the Pharaoh encounter. The final verse closes with delay — and the reader has learned enough to trust that God's timing is intentional.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 40 is the chapter of delay — human forgetting, divine timing, and the prison as God's waiting room. Joseph does not waste the delay; he exercises his gift faithfully with two prisoners who have nothing to offer him. The chapter demonstrates that faithfulness in small things precedes elevation to great ones.
