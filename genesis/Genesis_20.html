Genesis 20

Abraham and Abimelech; the Second Deception

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–7 — Abraham and Abimelek; Sarah Taken Again
1Now Abraham moved on from there into the region of the Negev and lived between Kadesh and Shur. For a while he stayed in Gerar,2and there Abraham said of his wife Sarah, "She is my sister." Then Abimelek king of Gerar sent for Sarah and took her.3But God came to Abimelek in a dream one night and said to him, "You are as good as dead because of the woman you have taken; she is a married woman."4Now Abimelek had not gone near her, so he said, "Lord, will you destroy an innocent nation?5Did he not say to me, 'She is my sister,' and didn't she also say, 'He is my brother'? I have done this with a clear conscience and clean hands."6Then God said to him in the dream, "Yes, I know you did this with a clear conscience, and so I have kept you from sinning against me. That is why I did not let you touch her.7Now return the man's wife, for he is a prophet, and he will pray for you and you will live. But if you do not return her, you may be sure that you and all who belong to you will die."

Places

Negev / between Kadesh and Shur
Southern boundary region
Abraham moves to the region between Kadesh (major Negev oasis) and Shur (the Egyptian border). This liminal zone between Canaan and Egypt is where the wife-sister deception recurs. Abimelech king of Gerar is a Philistine — the pre-Israelite inhabitants of the coastal plain.
Gerar
Philistine territory; modern Tell Haror
Gerar is the Philistine capital where both Abraham (ch.20) and Isaac (ch.26) will use the wife-sister deception. The same place tests covenant integrity twice across generations.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
20:2-3

MacArthur: Abraham repeats the wife-sister deception he used in Egypt (ch.12) -- the same sin in a different location. God intervenes again, this time through a dream warning to Abimelech. The pattern reveals both the depth of Abraham's fear-driven instinct and the depth of God's sovereign protection of the covenant promise in spite of it.

20:7

MacArthur: God calls Abraham a prophet in his instruction to Abimelech -- the first use of the word 'prophet' (nabi) in Scripture. The context is intercessory: Abraham's prayer will heal Abimelech's household. The prophet's role is not only proclamation but intercession. The covenant man's primary responsibility to the watching world is prayer, not deception.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

The chapter's most striking theological move is God's protection of the covenant promise through a pagan king's integrity rather than Abraham's faithfulness. Abimelech acts with more integrity than the patriarch. God reveals the situation to Abimelech in a dream and tells him Abraham is a prophet — an astonishing commendation for a man who has just lied. The covenant promise is protected not by Abraham's virtue but by divine sovereignty working through unexpected channels.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 12:10–20The parallel episode in Egypt — same deception, same divine intervention, same expulsion with wealth. The repetition raises questions about Abraham's character and God's patience.
  • Gen 26:1–11Isaac repeats the same deception with the same Abimelech — the pattern runs across generations, raising questions about learned behavior within the covenant family.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:1-7

The second wife-sister episode repeats the pattern of chapter 12 but with significant differences. Sarna: "This time God intervenes directly before any actual wrong occurs (v.6: 'I have kept you from sinning against me'). The divine protection of Sarah — and therefore of the covenant promise — is not dependent on Abraham's integrity but on God's own initiative. Abimelech is kept innocent by divine restraint, not by his own virtue."

20:7

Abraham is called a prophet (navi) for the first time — the only patriarch to receive this title. Sarna: "The designation of Abraham as a prophet in the context of his intercessory role is significant. Intercession is a prophetic function. The verse also explains why Abimelech must seek Abraham's prayer specifically: the covenant mediator's prayer has a power that others' prayers do not."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
20:3

God appears to Abimelech in a dream — the first divine appearance to a pagan king in Genesis. Alter: "The divine address to Abimelech is remarkable: a pagan receives a direct divine communication. God's concern for covenantal integrity overrides the ethnic or religious boundaries the reader might expect. The Judge of all the earth acts justly even toward the pagan king."

20:6-7

God's declaration that he kept Abimelech from touching Sarah reveals hidden divine governance of the apparent accident. Alter notes the theology: the deception that threatened Sarah's purity was secretly overruled by God before any human action. Providence works at a level below the narrative's visible events.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
20:3-7

Calvin on divine protection of the covenant: "God watches over his people, not because of their virtues, but because of his covenant. Abraham had sinned again; yet God prevents the violation of Sarah's honour, not to spare Abraham's unworthy cowardice, but to preserve the purity of the covenant line." Grace operates around human failure.

20:6-7

Calvin: God suffered not a hair of Sarah's head to be touched — he watched over his covenant promise even when his servant had abandoned the watch. God's faithfulness is not contingent on the faithfulness of his instruments.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
20:7

The NET note on Abraham called a "prophet" (navi) observes this is the first occurrence of the term in the Bible. The note discusses the meaning of nabi — one who speaks on behalf of God, including the intercessory function. The note observes that the intercessory role of the prophet (praying for others before God) is the dominant function in this passage.

20:3-6

The NET note on God appearing to Abimelech in a dream observes this is the only case in Genesis where God speaks to a pagan ruler in a dream. God holds even non-covenant people accountable for unwitting moral violation.

Verses 8–18 — Abimelek Confronts Abraham; Restoration
8Early the next morning Abimelek summoned all his officials, and when he told them all that had happened, they were very much afraid.9Then Abimelek called Abraham in and said, "What have you done to us? How have I wronged you that you have brought such great guilt upon me and my kingdom? You have done things to me that should never be done."10And Abimelek asked Abraham, "What was your reason for doing this?"11Abraham replied, "I said to myself, 'There is surely no fear of God in this place, and they will kill me because of my wife.'12Besides, she really is my sister, the daughter of my father though not of my mother; and she became my wife.13And when God had me wander from my father's household, I said to her, 'This is how you can show your love to me: Everywhere we go, say of me, "He is my brother."')14Then Abimelek brought sheep and cattle and male and female slaves and gave them to Abraham, and he returned Sarah his wife to him.15And Abimelek said, "My land is before you; live wherever you like."16To Sarah he said, "I am giving your brother a thousand shekels of silver. This is to cover the offense against you before all who are with you; you are completely vindicated."17Then Abraham prayed to God, and God healed Abimelek, his wife and his female slaves so they could have children again,18for the Lord had kept all the women in Abimelek's household from conceiving because of Abraham's wife Sarah.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible -- Faithful Paraphrase
20:11

MacArthur: Abraham's explanation -- 'there is surely no fear of God in this place' -- is proved wrong by the narrative. Abimelech, the pagan king, responds to God's warning with immediate obedience and moral indignation at Abraham's deception. The covenant patriarch misjudged the pagan king; Abimelech's moral sensitivity exceeds Abraham's in this episode.

20:17-18

MacArthur: Abraham prayed to God, and God healed Abimelech, his wife, and his female slaves so they could have children. The covenant man who had failed to protect his wife is restored to the intercessory role -- praying for the very family he had endangered. God's restoration of Abraham to usefulness despite his failure is an act of covenant grace.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

Abraham's explanation (v.11–13) is psychologically honest and morally uncomfortable: he lied because he was afraid, and he has been doing this systematically since leaving home. Yet God still calls him a prophet and uses his prayer to heal Abimelech. Grace operates in the midst of ongoing human failure — this is not a comfortable message but a realistic one about how God works with flawed people.

Cross-Reference

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase

    Abraham's explanation — "I said to myself, there is surely no fear of God in this place" — is both revealing and ironic. Alter: "Abraham assumed that Abimelech had no moral compass.     Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    Abraham
    Patriarch; prophet; moral failure
    Called a prophet by God (v.7) immediately after his most systematic moral failure. The juxtaposition is theologically jarring. God's gifts and callings are without revocation (Rom 11:29) — they rest on grace, not performance.
    Abimelech
    King of Gerar; acts with more integrity than Abraham
    Receives a divine dream, acts immediately on it, rebukes Abraham justly, compensates Sarah generously. A pagan king models the "fear of God" that Abraham claimed was absent in Gerar (v.11) — but appears to be absent in Abraham himself.
    Sarah
    Wife; taken into Abimelech's household
    Her silence throughout the chapter is notable. She has no recorded speech. She is the most affected party — taken into a foreign king's household — yet her response is unrecorded. The text leaves her inner life poignantly hidden.

    Ancient Sources

    Ugaritic texts on divine dreams
    Dreams as divine communication are extensively attested in ANE literature (Ugarit, Mari, Mesopotamia). The Abimelech dream follows the standard ANE pattern: divine appearance at night with a direct message.
    Confirms the cultural plausibility of dream-revelation in the patriarchal world.

    Reception History

    Origen, On First Principles
    Origen allegorized the "sister" as the soul, and Abimelech's failure to "know" her as the world's inability to penetrate spiritual reality. His reading was influential in the mystical tradition despite being far from the literal sense.
    Modern ethical reading
    Contemporary scholarship raises ethical questions about the text's apparent comfort with Abraham's deception. Some see the narrative as critiquing Abraham through Abimelech's words; others note the text never explicitly condemns him. The ambiguity is intentional — the reader must engage with the moral complexity.

    Literary Structure

    God Protects the Covenant Despite Covenant Failure20:6God tells Abimelech: I kept you from sinning against me. The divine protection of Sarah operates below the level of human action. The covenant's security is God's, not Abraham's.
    The Pagan King Rebukes the Covenant Patriarch20:9-10The pattern recurs: the pagan king asks questions Abraham cannot answer without shame. The outside world often sees the covenant community's failures more clearly than the community sees them itself.

    Textual Notes

    Scholarly Debates

    The Wife-Sister Narratives (12, 20, 26): One Event or Three?
    Three separate events
    Traditional, Sarna, Wenham
    Three separate incidents with different details (Egypt/Gerar; Abraham twice; Isaac once) involving different pharaohs/kings. Repetition of the pattern reflects the real danger of the patriarchal world for covenant women.
    Three versions of one event
    Source-critical (J, E, J)
    Classic source criticism assigns the three to different documents (J=ch.12, E=ch.20, J=ch.26). They are variants of a single event preserved in different traditions.
    Literary-theological repetition
    Alter, Fokkelman
    The repetition is deliberate literary technique: the same narrative type recurs three times to establish a pattern (God protects the covenant line through human cowardice) and to show character development (or its absence).
    The literary-canonical reading is most compelling: the repetition is intentional and has theological significance. Each iteration asks: will the patriarch protect his wife? Each time God intervenes. The pattern exposes the gap between Abrahamic faith and Abrahamic courage.
