The Seventh Day — Verses 1–3
1 Thus the heavens and the earth were completed in all their vast array. 2 By the seventh day God had finished the work he had been doing; so on the seventh day he rested from all his work. 3 Then God blessed the seventh day and made it holy, because on it he rested from all the work of creating that he had done.

Hebrew Word Study

שַבַת Shabbat — “Rested / ceased.” From the root shavat, to stop, to cease from work. Not a rest of exhaustion — God is not tired. In ancient temple inauguration texts, the deity’s “rest” inside the completed temple means taking up residence, ruling from within. The cosmos is complete; God moves in.


וַיְקַדֵש Wayyeqaddesh — “Made it holy / set it apart.” The first use of qadosh (holy) in the Bible is applied not to a place or person, but to time. Holiness enters the world as a rhythm before it ever attaches to a location.


Tseva’am — “Vast array / host.” The word used for armies. The heavens and earth are God’s ordered army, arrayed and at the ready. Used again in the title YHWH Tseva’ot — LORD of Hosts — the most common divine title in the prophets.

Deep Context — Sabbath as Cosmic Architecture

Genesis 2:1–3 is the climax of the entire creation narrative — not the making of humans (Day 6), but the establishment of sacred time. The seven-day week has no astronomical basis; it is purely theological, embedded in creation’s structure. Ancient Near Eastern temple texts show the same pattern: when a king built a temple, he celebrated for seven days, then the deity “rested” inside it. Genesis 1–2 presents the cosmos itself as God’s temple.


The Sabbath commandment in Exodus 20:11 explicitly roots Israel’s weekly rest in these verses — humans are invited to mirror the cosmic rhythm. In Exodus 31:17 it becomes a sign of the covenant itself. The author of Hebrews reads it as a type of the believer’s eschatological rest still to come (Heb 4:1–11).

Cross-References

  • Ex 20:8–11The Sabbath commandment grounds Israel’s weekly rest directly in Genesis 2:2–3 — “for in six days the LORD made the heavens and the earth.”
  • Heb 4:1–11The author of Hebrews reads Sabbath rest as pointing forward to an eschatological rest that still remains for God’s people.
  • John 5:17Jesus claims “My Father is always at his work to this very day, and I too am working” — a provocative reinterpretation of the divine Sabbath rest.
  • Rev 21:3“God’s dwelling place is now among the people” — the cosmic temple imagery of Genesis 2 reaches its fulfilment in the new creation.
The Second Account Begins — Verses 4–7
4 This is the account of the heavens and the earth when they were created, when the LORD God made the earth and the heavens. 5 Now no shrub had yet appeared on the earth and no plant had yet sprung up, for the LORD God had not sent rain on the earth and there was no one to work the ground, 6 but streams came up from the earth and watered the whole surface of the ground. 7 Then the LORD God formed a man from the dust of the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living being.

Hebrew Word Study

יְהוָה YHWH — The covenant name of God appears here for the first time, always paired with Elohim in this chapter (YHWH Elohim). This double name signals that the cosmic creator of chapter 1 is the same as the personal covenant God of Israel — a deliberate theological stitching together of the two accounts.


יִצֶר Yatsar — “Formed.” The word for a potter shaping clay. Chapter 1 used bara’ (create from nothing); chapter 2 uses yatsar — hands in the clay, intimate craft. The same verb describes God forming the prophet Jeremiah in the womb (Jer 1:5) and Israel as a nation (Isa 43:1).


Nishmat hayyim — “Breath of life.” Neshamah is the most intimate Hebrew word for breath — more personal than ruah (wind/spirit). God doesn’t just speak humanity into existence; he exhales into a human face. No other creature in Genesis receives this direct divine breath.


Nefesh hayyah — “Living being / living soul.” The same phrase used for animals in Genesis 1:20, 24. The human is a nefesh — not a soul imprisoned in a body, but an animated body, a body-soul unity. The Greek/Platonic dualism of body vs. soul is foreign to this text.

Historical Context

Verse 4 introduces a second, distinct creation account beginning with “This is the account (toledot) of…” — a formula that structures the entire book of Genesis (it appears 10 times). The two accounts have different names for God, different sequences, different emphases. Scholars assign Genesis 1:1–2:3 to the Priestly (P) source and 2:4–25 to the older Yahwist (J) source. Rather than contradictions, they are complementary perspectives — one cosmic and liturgical, one intimate and agricultural.


The Mesopotamian Atrahasis Epic (c. 1700 BCE) similarly tells of humans formed from clay mixed with divine blood to do the work the gods were tired of. Genesis 2 reframes this: humans aren’t created to serve the gods’ needs — God creates them out of intimate love and gives them a garden, not a labour camp.

Deep Context

The shift from Chapter 1’s cosmic panorama to Chapter 2’s intimate close-up is jarring — and intentional. Chapter 1 asks: What is the universe? Chapter 2 asks: Who are we, and what is our relationship to God, to each other, and to the ground? The word adamah (ground/soil) appears throughout — Adam is literally the “ground creature,” made of earth and animated by heaven. This tension between earthy origin and divine breath defines the human condition throughout Scripture.

The Garden of Eden — Verses 8–17
8 Now the LORD God had planted a garden in the east, in Eden; and there he put the man he had formed. 9 The LORD God made all kinds of trees grow out of the ground — trees that were pleasing to the eye and good for food. In the middle of the garden were the tree of life and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil.

10 A river watering the garden flowed from Eden; from there it was separated into four headwaters. 11 The name of the first is the Pishon; it winds through the entire land of Havilah, where there is gold. 12 (The gold of that land is good; aromatic resin and onyx are also there.) 13 The name of the second river is the Gihon; it winds through the entire land of Cush. 14 The name of the third river is the Tigris; it runs along the east side of Ashur. And the fourth river is the Euphrates.

15 The LORD God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it. 16 And the LORD God commanded the man, “You are free to eat from any tree in the garden; 17 but you must not eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, for when you eat from it you will certainly die.”

Hebrew Word Study

עֵדֶן Eden — Likely from a Sumerian word meaning “plain” or from Hebrew ’eden meaning “delight/luxury.” Not merely a geographic place but a state of being — unbroken communion between God, humanity, and creation.


Etz hahayyim — “Tree of life.” The life-giving tree appears across ancient Near Eastern iconography — Assyrian palace reliefs show kings flanking a sacred tree. In Genesis it is real, accessible, and its fruit would sustain immortality. Access to it is what humanity loses in chapter 3; regaining it is what Revelation 22 promises.


Da’at tov wara’ — “Knowledge of good and evil.” Da’at in Hebrew is experiential knowledge, not just intellectual knowledge. “Good and evil” is a merism (like “heavens and earth” in chapter 1) meaning the full range of moral experience. To “know” good and evil means to be the one who determines what is good and evil — to be morally autonomous, to be like God.


Mot tamut — “You will certainly die.” The Hebrew infinitive absolute construction for absolute certainty: literally “dying you will die.” Death enters the narrative as a divine warning before it becomes a human reality.

Historical Context

The four rivers of Eden (Pishon, Gihon, Tigris, Euphrates) were the ancient world’s most significant waterways. Two are easily identified — the Tigris and Euphrates. The Pishon and Gihon remain debated. Satellite imagery has revealed a dried ancient riverbed (Wadi al-Batin) running through Saudi Arabia that may be the Pishon; the Gihon may be the Karun River of southwestern Iran. If correct, all four rivers once met near the northern Persian Gulf.


Sacred garden temples were common in the ancient Near East. Mesopotamian kings built elaborate garden sanctuaries attached to their temples. The Garden of Eden is presented as both garden and sanctuary — the place where God “walked” (Gen 3:8), where humanity had priestly functions (“to work it and take care of it” uses temple service language).

Deep Context — Two Trees, One Choice

The two trees set up the entire drama of Scripture. The tree of life represents what God freely offers — sustained existence in his presence. The tree of the knowledge of good and evil represents the one boundary — the refusal to be morally self-determining. The prohibition is not arbitrary; it is the structure of creaturely existence. To be a creature is to receive the definition of good from the Creator. The temptation of chapter 3 is to collapse that distinction.


The command in v.15 — “to work it and take care of it” — uses ’avad (serve/work, used for priestly temple service) and shamar (guard/keep, used for keeping the covenant and guarding the sanctuary). Adam is the first priest, and Eden is the first temple. His failure is not just moral but priestly.

Places — The Four Rivers of Eden

💧
Tigris (Hiddekel)
Turkey → Iraq → Persian Gulf

Identified with certainty — the great river running from eastern Turkey through Iraq. Genesis says it runs “east of Ashur” (Assyria), which it does. At 1,900 km, it was the eastern border of the Fertile Crescent. Nineveh, Assur, and Ctesiphon all stood on its banks. Still flows today.

💧
Euphrates (Perat)
Turkey → Syria → Iraq → Persian Gulf

Also certain — the longest river of western Asia at 2,800 km. Babylon, Ur, Mari, and Carchemish all sat on its banks. So central to biblical geography that in Hebrew it is simply called “the River” (ha-nahar). Still flows today, though diminished by damming.

💧
Pishon (proposed)
Wadi al-Batin, Saudi Arabia / Kuwait

Genesis says it winds through Havilah “where there is gold” — and the Arabian Peninsula is indeed gold-bearing. Satellite imagery reveals the ancient Wadi al-Batin, a dried riverbed running from the Hejaz mountains into Kuwait. Now dry due to the climate shift after the last Ice Age (~6000 BCE).

💧
Gihon (proposed)
Karun River, southwestern Iran

Genesis says it winds through “the entire land of Cush” — not Ethiopian Cush, but a Mesopotamian Cush (Cosseans / Kassites of western Iran). The Karun River in Khuzestan province, which flows into the Shatt al-Arab near the Tigris-Euphrates confluence, is the strongest candidate. Still flows today.

🌊
Northern Persian Gulf
Off coast of Kuwait & Iraq

If all four rivers are identified correctly, they all converge near the head of the Persian Gulf. During the Ice Age, sea levels were 120 m lower — this entire area was dry land. As seas rose (10,000–6,000 BCE), the “garden at the head of the rivers” was submerged. Some geologists call this region “the Gulf Oasis.”

⛏️
Havilah
Arabian Peninsula

Genesis describes Havilah as rich in gold, bdellium (aromatic resin), and onyx. Arabia has historically been a source of all three. The ancient gold mines of Mahd adh Dhahab (“Cradle of Gold”) in modern Saudi Arabia may be what Genesis refers to. Havilah appears in Genesis 10:7, 29 as a region descended from both Ham and Shem.

The Tigris and Euphrates are certain. The Pishon and Gihon remain the subject of serious scholarly investigation. The most coherent theory places Eden’s headwaters near what is now the northern Persian Gulf.

The First Woman — Verses 18–25
18 The LORD God said, “It is not good for the man to be alone. I will make a helper suitable for him.”

19 Now the LORD God had formed out of the ground all the wild animals and all the birds in the sky. He brought them to the man to see what he would name them; and whatever the man called each living creature, that was its name. 20 So the man gave names to all the livestock, the birds in the sky and all the wild animals. But for Adam no suitable helper was found.

21 So the LORD God caused the man to fall into a deep sleep; and while he was sleeping, he took one of the man’s ribs and then closed up the place with flesh. 22 Then the LORD God made a woman from the rib he had taken out of the man, and he brought her to the man.

23 The man said,
  “This is now bone of my bones
    and flesh of my flesh;
  she shall be called ‘woman,’
    for she was taken out of man.”

24 That is why a man leaves his father and mother and is united to his wife, and they become one flesh.

25 Adam and his wife were both naked, and they felt no shame.

Hebrew Word Study

Lo’ tov — “Not good.” The first “not good” in creation, after the sevenfold “good” of chapter 1. Human solitude — the absence of genuine relationship — is the first thing in creation declared incomplete.


’Ezer kenegdo — “Helper suitable for him.” ’Ezer (helper) is used 21 times in the OT; 16 of those refer to God as Israel’s helper (Ps 121:2). It carries no connotation of inferiority — a helper is one who supplies what the other lacks. Kenegdo means “corresponding to him,” a mirror-match, equal and opposite. Together: a powerful ally who is the man’s complement.


Tsela’ — “Rib” (NIV), but more literally “side” — the same word used for the sides of the ark of the covenant and the side chambers of the Temple. Some scholars read this as a full “side” of Adam, implying an original androgynous human divided into two. The woman is built from the man’s innermost structure.


’Ish / ’Ishah — Man / Woman. The first poem in Scripture (v.23) is a wordplay: “she shall be called ’ishah because she was taken from ’ish.” The man’s first words in the Bible are a love poem recognising shared essence.

Deep Context

The naming of the animals (vv.19–20) is often misread as a simple taxonomy exercise. In the ancient world, naming was an act of authority and relationship — to name something was to understand its nature. Adam’s naming of every creature reveals that none is his kenegdo — his counterpart. The exercise is designed to demonstrate the gap that only the woman can fill.


Verse 24 is one of the most cited verses in the NT. Jesus quotes it in Matthew 19:5 and Mark 10:7 to argue against divorce: the “one flesh” union is the original design. Paul quotes it in Ephesians 5:31 and reads the marriage relationship as an analogy of Christ’s union with the church. The “leaving and cleaving” pattern establishes that the marriage covenant supersedes even the family of origin.


The nakedness without shame (v.25) is the chapter’s final word — a state of total transparency and vulnerability with no fear of exploitation. It sets up the contrast with chapter 3’s aftermath, when shame enters and the first human instinct is to hide and cover.

Cross-References

  • Matt 19:4–6Jesus quotes Gen 2:24 directly in his teaching on marriage and divorce, grounding the argument in the creation order.
  • Eph 5:31–32Paul quotes Gen 2:24 and calls it a “profound mystery” pointing to Christ and the church — marriage as theological symbol.
  • 1 Cor 11:8–9Paul draws on the sequence of creation (man first, then woman) in his discussion of order in worship.
  • Ps 121:2“My help (’ezer) comes from the LORD” — the same word used for the woman, showing its non-diminutive force.
  • Rev 22:2The tree of life reappears in the new Jerusalem — what was lost in Eden is restored in the eschaton.

Theme Moments This Chapter
Priesthood — v.15

Adam is placed in Eden le'avdah ule'shomrah — to serve and guard it. These are the precise terms for Levitical temple service (Num 3:7–8). The garden is a temple; Adam is its first priest.

Imago Dei — v.18–23

The 'ezer kenegdo (corresponding ally) completes the image — humanity in its fullness is relational, not individual. Barth: the Imago Dei is the I-Thou structure of human community.

Land — vv.10–14

Eden with its four rivers is the first sacred geography — the prototype of the promised land. The land theme begins as a place of divine-human fellowship, which exile (ch.3) will break and covenant will progressively restore.

Intertextual Threading

5 canonical threads — 5 forward (passages that echo this chapter)  |  0 backward (texts this chapter echoes)

v.7 — dust + breath → 1 Cor 15:45–49 Fulfillment

Paul explicitly contrasts the 'first Adam' (from the earth, a living being — nefesh hayyah, Gen 2:7) with the 'last Adam' (from heaven, a life-giving spirit — pneuma zōopoioun). The resurrection body inverts the Genesis formula: instead of dust animated by breath, it is spiritual life that transforms physicality. Gen 2:7 is Paul's baseline for understanding what the resurrection restores and transcends.

v.15 — work and keep → Num 3:7–8; 18:5–6 Echo

The identical Hebrew verbs 'abad (work/serve) and shamar (keep/guard) are used in Numbers for the Levitical priests' duties in the tabernacle. Adam is thus the first priest of the cosmic temple — Eden. His failure to guard the boundary (the forbidden tree) is priestly dereliction. The Levitical system is the institutional recovery of the vocation Adam abandoned.

v.18 — not good alone → Eph 5:25–32 Fulfillment

Paul reads the man-woman union of Gen 2 as a 'mystery' pointing to Christ and the church. Just as Eve is drawn from Adam's side (his tsela'), the church is born from Christ's wounded side (John 19:34). The 'leaving and cleaving' of 2:24 is fulfilled in Christ's incarnation (leaving the Father) and union with his bride (the church).

v.9 — tree of life → Rev 2:7; 22:2,14 Fulfillment

The tree of life, inaccessible after Gen 3:24, reappears at the end of the Bible: in the new Jerusalem it grows by the river of life, bearing fruit every month. Access is restored to those who 'overcome' (Rev 2:7) — what Eden's flaming sword denied, Christ's death unlocks. The tree of life is the Bible's first and last symbol of unobstructed divine life.

v.24 — leave and cleave → Mark 10:6–9; Matt 19:4–6 Quotation

Jesus quotes Gen 2:24 verbatim in his teaching on divorce, grounding the permanence of marriage in the original creation order — before Moses, before the law, before the fall. He then adds his own interpretive gloss ('what God has joined together, let no one separate') as a dominical commentary on the creation text. Gen 2:24 becomes the normative definition of marriage in the NT.

People — Genesis 2

Adam / Ha-Adam
The First Human — vv.7,15,19,22,23

Not a proper name in ch.2 but the title ha-adam (the human / the earthling). He becomes 'Adam' as a proper name only in 4:25. In ch.2 he is defined by his origin (adamah — ground), his vocation (priest-gardener), and his incompleteness without 'ishah. His first recorded words are a love poem (v.23). His canonical arc: image-bearer → caretaker → naming-authority → husband → fall → exile → father of Seth. The NT identifies him as a 'type of the one to come' (Rom 5:14), with Christ as the last Adam who succeeds where the first failed (1 Cor 15:45).

Eve / Hawwah
Mother of All Living — v.22 (named in 3:20)

Created from Adam's tsela' (rib/side) — the only creature not formed from the ground. Her name (Hawwah) is given in 3:20 after the fall, meaning 'life-giver' or 'living one.' In ch.2 she is introduced as 'ezer kenegdo — a powerful ally who is Adam's mirror-complement. She is the first to speak to the serpent, the first to evaluate the tree's multiple appeals, and the first to eat. In the NT she is referenced by Paul in 1 Cor 11 and 1 Tim 2, and her deception is used as a warning (2 Cor 11:3). In typology she is read as a figure of the church, born from Christ's wounded side (John 19:34).

YHWH Elohim
The Covenant Creator — throughout ch.2

The double name YHWH Elohim appears 11 times in Gen 2–3 and almost nowhere else in the Pentateuch in this combination. It is the literary seam stitching the cosmic God of ch.1 (Elohim) to the personal covenant God of Israel (YHWH). In ch.2 he is portrayed with striking intimacy: forming Adam from clay with hands, breathing into his face, planting a garden, performing surgery, and making clothes. The transcendent creator of ch.1 is the same as the intimate companion of ch.2.

Ancient Source Texts — Creation of Humanity

Atrahasis Epic — Babylonian (c. 1700 BCE)
Let one god be slaughtered, and the gods be purified in the immersion... From his flesh and blood let Nintu mix clay, that god and man may be thoroughly mixed in the clay... We shall hear the drum for the rest of time.

Humanity is created from clay mixed with a slaughtered god's blood — to relieve the lesser gods of agricultural labor. The humans are workers created to serve divine needs. Genesis 2 directly reframes this: Adam is formed from dust and divine breath (not blood), placed in a garden of delight (not a labor camp), given vocation as partnership with God (not servitude to gods). The clay-creation motif is shared; the theological meaning is opposite.

Enki and Ninhursag — Sumerian Paradise Myth
The land Dilmun is pure, the land Dilmun is clean... In Dilmun the raven utters no cries, the lion kills not, the wolf snatches not the lamb... the sick-eyed says not 'I am sick-eyed.'

The Sumerian paradise Dilmun (likely Bahrain) has remarkable parallels to Eden: a pure, painless land without predation, sickness, or death. The myth involves Enki eating forbidden plants, then suffering curses in each body part — a rib is created to heal his rib specifically (Ninti means both 'lady of the rib' and 'lady who makes live'). The Eden narrative appears to engage this tradition deliberately, with the rib and the forbidden eating as shared motifs reframed theologically.

Reception History — Genesis 2

Philo of Alexandria (c. 20 BCE – 50 CE)

Philo distinguished between the 'heavenly man' of Gen 1:26-27 (an ideal, incorporeal archetype) and the 'earthly man' of Gen 2:7 (the physical, embodied human). This Platonized reading — influenced by Greek philosophy — created a two-Adam framework that Paul may be consciously engaging in 1 Cor 15:45-49 ('the first man was of the dust of the earth; the second man is of heaven'). Philo's allegorical approach dominated Jewish Alexandria and influenced early Christian interpretation significantly.

Augustine — De Genesi ad Litteram on the Rib

Augustine wrestled extensively with the rib narrative, refusing both purely allegorical and naively literal readings. He insisted on a real event but allowed that the 'deep sleep' involved a visionary or prophetic dimension — Adam experiencing in the sleep what God was doing to his body. Augustine was also the first major theologian to read the male-female relationship of ch.2 as an image of the soul's relationship to God, and marriage as a sacramental sign.

Karl Barth — Church Dogmatics III/1 (1945)

Barth argued that Gen 2's account reveals the inner meaning of Gen 1: the Imago Dei is fundamentally relational — to be human is to exist in the I-Thou relationship of male and female. Humans are not images of God as isolated individuals but as community. Barth read the woman's creation not as an afterthought but as the telos (goal) of ch.2 — the chapter builds to genuine human community as the condition of possibility for covenant relationship with God.

Modern Scholarship — John H. Walton on Ancient Vocation

Walton argues that Adam's role in the garden ('to work it and keep it,' v.15) uses the precise Hebrew terms for priestly temple service ('abad and shamar). This makes Adam the first priest of the cosmic temple — not a farmer tending a garden but a priest maintaining a sacred space. The fall is then not just moral failure but priestly dereliction: Adam abandons his role as guardian of the sacred boundary (symbolized by the tree).

vv.21-22Woman created — need met
v.23Adam’s love poem — climax
v.24Editorial comment: leave and cleave
v.25Naked and unashamed — setup for ch.3

The inclusio of “naked” ('arummim) in v.25 and the serpent’s “craftiness” ('arum) in 3:1 is a deliberate sound-bridge between chapters. The garden narrative is also a problem → solution structure: the “not good” of v.18 is the only problem in an otherwise good creation, and the woman is its resolution. The entire chapter thus builds toward human community as the crown of the second account.
