Genesis 27

Genesis 27

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–17 — Rebekah's Plan; Jacob Prepares the Deception
1When Isaac was old and his eyes were so weak that he could no longer see, he called for Esau his older son and said to him, "My son." "Here I am," he answered.2Isaac said, "I am now an old man and don't know the day of my death.3Now then, get your equipment—your quiver and bow—and go out to the open country to hunt some wild game for me.4Prepare me the kind of tasty food I like and bring it to me to eat, so that I may give you my blessing before I die."5Now Rebekah was listening as Isaac spoke to his son Esau. When Esau left for the open country to hunt game and bring it back,6Rebekah said to her son Jacob, "Look, I overheard your father say to your brother Esau,7"Bring me some game and prepare me some tasty food to eat, so that I may give you my blessing in the presence of the Lord before I die.'8Now, my son, listen carefully and do what I tell you:9Go out to the flock and bring me two choice young goats, so I can prepare some tasty food for your father, just the way he likes it.10Then take it to your father to eat, so that he may give you his blessing before he dies."11Jacob said to Rebekah his mother, "But my brother Esau is a hairy man while I have smooth skin.12What if my father touches me? I would appear to be tricking him and would bring down a curse on myself rather than a blessing."13His mother said to him, "My son, let the curse fall on me. Just do what I say; go and get them for me."14So he went and got them and brought them to his mother, and she prepared some tasty food, just the way his father liked it.15Then Rebekah took the best clothes of Esau her older son, which she had in the house, and put them on her younger son Jacob.16She also covered his hands and the smooth part of his neck with the goatskins.17Then she handed to her son Jacob the tasty food and the bread she had made.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

Isaac's plan to bless Esau despite the oracle of 25:23 ("the older shall serve the younger") raises a deep question: is he acting in ignorance or willful defiance of divine revelation? Rebekah knows the oracle; Isaac presumably does too. The text does not explain Isaac's motivation — his attachment to Esau's game (v.4) may suggest that appetite, like Esau's own, is at the root. The patriarchal blessing is a legally binding act in the ancient world — once spoken, it cannot be recalled.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 25:23The prenatal oracle: "the older shall serve the younger." Isaac's plan to bless Esau appears to conflict with this divine word — setting up the providential irony that God's purpose will be accomplished despite, not through, human plans.
  • Heb 11:20Hebrews credits Isaac with faith: "by faith Isaac invoked future blessings on Jacob and Esau" — even the attempted Esau blessing is read as an act of faith, though misdirected.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:1-17

Rebekah's plan to divert Isaac's blessing from Esau to Jacob raises one of the most difficult moral questions in the patriarchal narratives. Sarna: "The conspiracy is clearly deceptive and morally problematic. Yet the divine oracle of 25:23 had already declared that the elder would serve the younger. Rebekah knows this; she acts to implement what God had said. The question is whether deception is justified to achieve a divinely ordained end — and the narrative does not resolve it."

27:6-13

Did God need human deception to fulfil his declared intention? Sarna notes the narrative records the deception without endorsing it, and subsequent events show it was unnecessary — God would have achieved his purpose without it.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
27:1-4

Isaac's blindness is both physical and spiritual. Alter: "The blind patriarch who cannot tell his sons apart is a figure of dramatic irony — he intends to give what divine providence has already arranged differently. The failure of Isaac's perception (both physical and theological) is the occasion for the plot. He knows the oracle; he proceeds as if he doesn't."

27:15-17

Rebekah dresses Jacob in Esau's clothes. Alter: "The disguise is crude — skins on smooth arms to simulate hairiness, Esau's clothes to carry his smell. The plan seems almost comically obvious. Yet it works — not because it is clever but because Isaac's senses are failing. The disguise succeeds where it should fail, which is the narrator's way of signalling that something beyond natural causation is at work."

27:6-10

Rebekah's plan is detailed and specific. Alter notes the specificity reveals a woman who has thought carefully about every detail and counterargument. Whatever the moral evaluation, her intelligence and decisiveness are unmistakably characterised.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
27:1-10

Calvin on the deception: "I do not excuse the fraud of Rebekah, nor the ready compliance of Jacob, but simply note that God, who turned the counsel of Ahithophel into foolishness and used the treachery of Judas as an instrument of redemption, so also overruled this sinful scheme to accomplish his will. The wickedness of the means does not justify the act; but God's sovereignty is not defeated by human sin."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
27:9

The NET note on the disguise plan observes that Rebekah is the driving force — Jacob is initially reluctant (v.11-12). The note discusses whether Rebekah's action is presented as commendable (fulfilling the oracle) or condemned (using sinful means). The note concludes that the narrative presents the moral ambiguity without resolving it explicitly, allowing the consequences to serve as implicit commentary.

27:1-4

The NET note on the deathbed blessing discusses its legal force in the ancient Near East: it was irrevocable once given. Isaac's intention to bless Esau would finalise what God had declared would be Jacob's.

Verses 18–29 — Jacob Receives Isaac's Blessing
18He went to his father and said, "My father." "Yes, my son," he answered. "Who is it?"19Jacob said to his father, "I am Esau your firstborn. I have done as you told me. Please sit up and eat some of my game, so that you may give me your blessing."20Isaac asked his son, "How did you find it so quickly, my son?" "The Lord your God gave me success," he replied.21Then Isaac said to Jacob, "Come near so I can touch you, my son, to know whether you really are my son Esau or not."22Jacob went close to his father Isaac, who touched him and said, "The voice is the voice of Jacob, but the hands are the hands of Esau."23He did not recognize him, for his hands were hairy like those of his brother Esau; so he proceeded to bless him.24"Are you really my son Esau?" he asked. "I am," he replied.25Then he said, "My son, bring me some of your game to eat, so that I may give you my blessing." Jacob brought it to him and he ate; and he brought some wine and he drank.26Then his father Isaac said to him, "Come here, my son, and kiss me."27So he went to him and kissed him. When Isaac caught the smell of his clothes, he blessed him and said, "Ah, the smell of my son is like the smell of a field that the Lord has blessed.28May God give you heaven's dew and earth's richness—an abundance of grain and new wine.29May nations serve you and peoples bow down to you. Be lord over your brothers, and may the sons of your mother bow down to you. May those who curse you be cursed and those who bless you be blessed."

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

None

Context

Rebekah's complicity in the deception is ethically complex. She is trying to fulfill the divine oracle she received (25:23) — but she is using deception to accomplish what God has already promised. The theological question the narrative poses: did God need her help? The irony is that Jacob receives the blessing, but Rebekah loses her son — she tells Jacob to flee to Laban "for a few days" (v.44), and she never sees him again. The scheme works and costs her everything.

Cross-Reference

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    27:27-29

    The blessing Isaac gives Jacob contains the three elements of the Abrahamic covenant: agricultural abundance, lordship over nations (including Esau), and the blessing-curse formula (those who bless you will be blessed). Sarna: "Isaac unwittingly passes on the Abrahamic covenant to the son God had chosen. The irony is complete: the patriarch who tries to circumvent the divine oracle ends up executing it."

    27:28-29

    Sarna notes the blessing has three dimensions: material prosperity (dew and grain), political authority ('lord it over your brothers'), and covenant transmission ('those who curse you will be cursed' — echoing 12:3). The Abrahamic blessing is transmitted intact.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    27:22-23

    Isaac said, "The voice is the voice of Jacob, but the hands are the hands of Esau." Alter: "This is one of the most famous lines in the entire Bible — a perception of discrepancy that leads to a wrong conclusion. Isaac senses the truth (the voice) but overrides it with the false evidence (the hands). The sentence is a definition of self-deception."

    27:26-27

    Isaac's request to kiss Jacob before blessing him is the narrative's point of maximum dramatic tension. Alter notes the smelling of the clothes — 'the smell of my son is like a field the LORD has blessed' — is the narrative's most poignant irony: the sensory verification that convinces Isaac is the thing staged to deceive him.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    27:28-29

    Calvin on the blessing: "Isaac blessed Jacob with the same solemn and irrevocable words that Abraham had used (12:3). Even though Isaac was deceived about the recipient, the blessing was truly given and truly received. The Epistle to the Hebrews tells us (11:20) that Isaac blessed both his sons by faith — even this confused blessing was an act of faith in the God whose covenant he was transmitting."

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    27:27

    The NET note on Isaac smelling Esau's clothes on Jacob observes that scent is the sense that clinches Isaac's decision — and it is the one most easily deceived. The note observes the sensory progression: Isaac touches Jacob (confused), hears his voice (suspicious), touches him again (reassured by the skins), then smells him (convinced). Each sense is engaged and most are deceived.

    27:29

    The NET note on 'those who curse you will be cursed and those who bless you will be blessed' identifies this as the formula from 12:3 — the Abrahamic blessing verbatim, transmitted to Jacob. Whatever the means of transmission, the content is unambiguously the Abrahamic covenant.

    Hebrew Word Study

    Historical Context

    None

    Context

    Isaac's blessing contains the full Abrahamic covenant formula: land abundance, dominion over brothers, and the curse/bless formula of Gen 12:3. The blessing is irrevocable — once spoken, it cannot be recalled even when the fraud is discovered (v.33). This reflects the legal reality of ancient oral contracts: a spoken blessing has the force of a deed. God honors it, not because of Jacob's deception, but because the content aligns with the divine oracle already given.

    Cross-Reference

    • Gen 12:2–3The Abrahamic blessing formula — now formally transmitted to Jacob despite the fraud. The covenant content is preserved even when the covenant carrier is morally compromised.
    • Num 23:8Balaam: "How can I curse whom God has not cursed?" — the irrevocability of divine blessing echoes the Isaac episode.

    Sarna — JPS Commentary

    Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    27:34-38

    Esau's cry is one of the most emotionally powerful passages in Genesis. Sarna: "Esau cried bitterly and asked for a blessing. The scene is almost unbearable: the father who has no more blessings to give, the son crying out for something that has already been given to his brother. Yet even in Esau's anguish, the narrator does not excuse him — he sold the birthright and laughed off its value in 25:34. The grief is real; its cause was his own earlier contempt."

    27:36-38

    Esau's cry — 'Is he not rightly named Jacob? He has deceived me these two times' — is an ironic misidentification. Sarna notes the name Jacob doesn't derive from 'deceive' but Esau hears it that way. The wordplay is theologically significant: the name for his birth becomes the name for his character in Esau's mouth.

    Alter — Literary Reading

    Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
    27:36

    Esau cried out: "He has deceived me these two times — he took my birthright and now he has taken my blessing." Alter: "Esau names both betrayals — the birthright (which he sold) and the blessing (which was taken). He presents himself as the victim; the narrator has shown him as partly the agent of his own dispossession. The irony is characteristic: Esau's bitterness is genuine, and yet he bears more responsibility than his cry acknowledges."

    27:41-45

    Esau's resolution to kill Jacob echoes Cain's murder of Abel — the older brother's murderous resentment of the younger who received the preferred blessing. Alter notes Genesis is tracking a pattern: Cain/Abel, Esau/Jacob, Joseph's brothers. Sibling rivalry threatens the covenant line from within the family.

    Calvin’s Commentary

    John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
    27:38

    Esau weeps and asks for a blessing. Calvin: "Even wicked people may grieve over the loss of spiritual privileges. Esau weeps — but not with the tears of genuine repentance. He weeps over the loss of the blessing, not over his own profanity. Hebrews 12:17 tells us he found no place for repentance, though he sought the blessing with tears — the difference between grief over consequences and grief over sin."

    NET Bible Notes

    NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
    27:38

    The NET note on Esau's weeping connects to Hebrews 12:17 — he "found no place of repentance, though he sought it diligently with tears." The note discusses whether the Greek metanoia in Hebrews refers to Isaac's repentance (changing his mind about the blessing) or Esau's (genuine spiritual repentance). The note favours the former: Isaac could not change his mind; the blessing was irrevocable.

    27:38-40

    The NET note discusses whether 'when you grow restless, you will break his yoke' is a prediction or a conditional. The note traces Edom's periodic rebellions against Israelite dominance (2 Sam 8:14; 1 Kgs 11:14; 2 Kgs 8:20) as the activation of this conditional.

    Chapter-Level Scholarship

    People of the Chapter

    Isaac
    Aged patriarch; appetite-driven; subverted
    His plan to bless Esau against the oracle may reflect willful ignorance or partiality. He trembles when he realizes God's reversal has occurred — the trembling is recognition, not mere surprise. He ends by confirming the blessing: "Yes, and he shall be blessed."
    Rebekah
    Schemer; self-sacrificing; ultimately bereft
    She is the oracle's guardian (25:23) and the scheme's architect. Her "Let your curse be on me" is the chapter's most striking line. She wins her goal and loses her son — Jacob flees and she never sees him again.
    Jacob
    Deceiver; covenant heir; about to be formed by suffering
    He receives the blessing by fraud and will spend the next twenty years being defrauded by Laban in turn. The deceiver will be deceived. The name "Jacob" (heel-grasper) is vindicated by Esau's bitter pun (v.36): "Is he not rightly named Jacob?"
    Esau
    Victim; bitter; genuinely grieved
    His weeping (v.38) is one of the most humanly authentic moments in Genesis. He despised his birthright in a moment of appetite; now he discovers the full cost. He is not wicked — he is tragic. Genesis does not hate Esau; it traces the consequences of his priorities.

    Ancient Sources

    Nuzi Tablet on Oral Deathbed Blessings
    Nuzi tablets (15th c. BC) document cases where oral deathbed declarations had full legal force — one tablet records a court ruling that an oral gift-declaration could not be revoked even when the circumstances were disputed. The irrevocability of Isaac's blessing reflects this legal reality precisely.

    Reception History

    Origen, Homilies on Genesis
    Origen read Jacob's deception allegorically: the two kids represent the two peoples (Jew and Gentile); the "smooth" skin represents the Gentiles receiving what was due the "hairy" firstborn Israel. His allegorical move was influential in the early church's typological reading of election.
    Luther, Lectures on Genesis
    Luther defended the deception as Rebekah and Jacob acting on faith in the oracle — they were "stealing what was already theirs by divine promise." Calvin disagreed strongly, insisting the fraud was sinful even if the outcome was providential. The debate continues.

    Scholarly Debates

    The Deception in Genesis 27: Moral Evaluation
    Providence through sin
    Calvin, Wenham
    God's purpose (the oracle of 25:23) would have been fulfilled eventually without deception. The deception was sinful; God worked through it despite, not because of, Jacob's sin. Providence uses flawed instruments.
    Rebekah's prophetic insight
    Some traditional readings
    Rebekah knew from the oracle (25:23) that Jacob was to receive the blessing. Her deception was a misguided attempt to implement divine will. The end was right; the means were wrong.
    Ironic reversal
    Alter, Sarna
    The chapter is structured as a conscious reversal of Genesis 25: Esau cooking (27:5,17), a father deceived by sense rather than sight. The literary irony points to a narrative judgment without explicit authorial condemnation.
    The narrative does not condemn or commend the deception explicitly — a characteristic biblical restraint. The consequences (Jacob must flee, Rebekah never sees her beloved son again) function as the narrative's implicit moral judgment. Calvin's reading — God works through sinful human choices without endorsing them — is the most theologically robust.
