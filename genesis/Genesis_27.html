Genesis 27

The Stolen Blessing

Hebrew
Historical

Cross-Ref

Timeline

Genesis 27 is one of the most dramatically tense chapters in the Bible — a story of deception, stolen blessing, and irreversible consequence. Assigned to the Yahwist (J), it raises acute ethical questions: how can God honor a blessing obtained by fraud? The chapter is ruthlessly honest about the moral complexity of the covenant's human carriers.

Verses 1–5 — Isaac's Plan for Esau
1When Isaac was old and his eyes were dim so that he could not see, he called Esau his older son and said to him, "My son"; and he answered, "Here I am."2"Behold, I am old; I do not know the day of my death.3"Now then, take your weapons, your quiver and your bow, and go out to the field and hunt game for me,4"and prepare for me delicious food, such as I love, and bring it to me so that I may eat, that my soul may bless you before I die."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:1–4

Isaac's plan to bless Esau contradicts the divine oracle delivered to Rebekah before the twins were born: "the older will serve the younger" (25:23). Isaac knows the oracle — he was presumably told. Yet he proceeds to bless Esau, the older, on the basis of personal preference ("Isaac loved Esau... because he ate of his game," 25:28) and physical appetite ("make me the kind of tasty food I love," v.4). The blessing Isaac attempts to give is not his to manipulate — it belongs to God's purpose. Yet God's purposes will not be frustrated: what Isaac attempts to route around the oracle, God will accomplish through it, even through the deeply flawed means of Jacob's deception.

Context

Isaac's plan to bless Esau despite the oracle of 25:23 ("the older shall serve the younger") raises a deep question: is he acting in ignorance or willful defiance of divine revelation? Rebekah knows the oracle; Isaac presumably does too. The text does not explain Isaac's motivation — his attachment to Esau's game (v.4) may suggest that appetite, like Esau's own, is at the root. The patriarchal blessing is a legally binding act in the ancient world — once spoken, it cannot be recalled.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 25:23The prenatal oracle: "the older shall serve the younger." Isaac's plan to bless Esau appears to conflict with this divine word — setting up the providential irony that God's purpose will be accomplished despite, not through, human plans.
  • Heb 11:20Hebrews credits Isaac with faith: "by faith Isaac invoked future blessings on Jacob and Esau" — even the attempted Esau blessing is read as an act of faith, though misdirected.
Verses 6–17 — Rebekah's Scheme
6Rebekah said to her son Jacob, "I heard your father speak to your brother Esau,8"Now therefore, my son, obey my voice as I command you.11"But Jacob said to Rebekah his mother, 'Behold, Esau my brother is a hairy man, and I am a smooth man.12"Perhaps my father will feel me, and I shall seem to be mocking him and bring a curse upon myself and not a blessing."13His mother said to him, "Let your curse be on me, my son; only obey my voice, and go, bring them to me."

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
26:8–13

Rebekah's scheme is motivated by genuine faith in the divine oracle (25:23) but executed through deception. She takes full responsibility ("my son, let the curse fall on me," v.13) and engineers every detail. Her faith in the promise is real; her method is sinful. God does not need our deception to accomplish his purposes. The tragedy of Jacob's flight (v.43–45: Rebekah plans for a "few days" that will become 20 years; she never sees her son again) is the harvest of the scheme. The family is shattered by the very act meant to secure the blessing.

Hebrew Word Study

עֲלַי קִלְלָתְךָ ʿălay qillāṯĕkā — Let your curse be on me: Rebekah's words echo the atonement formula — she places herself under the potential curse to free Jacob to act. This is not merely a tactical reassurance; it is a mother's self-substitutionary love. The echo of later atonement theology is unmistakable, even if unconscious.

חָלָק ḥālāq — smooth: Jacob's smooth skin (ḥālāq) contrasts with Esau's hairy (śāʿîr) skin. The same word ḥālāq means "slippery, deceptive" in other OT contexts — the narrator may be exploiting the double meaning: Jacob is smooth in both senses.

Context

Rebekah's complicity in the deception is ethically complex. She is trying to fulfill the divine oracle she received (25:23) — but she is using deception to accomplish what God has already promised. The theological question the narrative poses: did God need her help? The irony is that Jacob receives the blessing, but Rebekah loses her son — she tells Jacob to flee to Laban "for a few days" (v.44), and she never sees him again. The scheme works and costs her everything.

Verses 18–29 — Jacob Receives the Blessing
18So he went in to his father and said, "My father." And he said, "Here I am. Who are you, my son?"19Jacob said to his father, "I am Esau your firstborn. I have done as you told me; now sit up and eat of my game, that your soul may bless me."22So Jacob went near to Isaac his father, who felt him and said, "The voice is Jacob's voice, but the hands are the hands of Esau."27So he came near and kissed him. And Isaac smelled the smell of his garments and blessed him and said, "See, the smell of my son is as the smell of a field that the LORD has blessed!"28"May God give you of the dew of heaven and of the fatness of the earth and plenty of grain and wine."29"Let peoples serve you, and nations bow down to you. Be lord over your brothers, and may your mother's sons bow down to you. Cursed be everyone who curses you, and blessed be everyone who blesses you!"

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:27–29

The blessing Isaac pronounces on Jacob (thinking he is Esau) is the Abrahamic covenant blessing: agricultural abundance, lordship over brothers, cursing of those who curse him, blessing of those who bless him — the language of 12:3; 27:29. Once spoken, the blessing cannot be revoked (v.33: "I blessed him — and indeed he will be blessed"). This is not because of any magical properties of the spoken word but because the blessing corresponds to the divine election: Isaac spoke more truly than he knew. The blessing he intended for Esau belongs to Jacob by God's sovereign choice, and so once given to Jacob it stands.

Hebrew Word Study

טַל הַשָּׁמַיִם ṭal haššāmayim — dew of heaven: The dew blessing invokes the life-giving moisture of the Levant — in a land without reliable rain, dew was essential for crops. The blessing is for agricultural abundance in the promised land itself.

אָרוּר אֹרְרֶיךָ ʾārûr ʾōrĕrêkā — Cursed be everyone who curses you: The precise language of the Abrahamic blessing (12:3) — "him who dishonors you I will curse" — now formally passed to Jacob. The covenant formula has been transmitted, and despite the means, it is binding.

Context

Isaac's blessing contains the full Abrahamic covenant formula: land abundance, dominion over brothers, and the curse/bless formula of Gen 12:3. The blessing is irrevocable — once spoken, it cannot be recalled even when the fraud is discovered (v.33). This reflects the legal reality of ancient oral contracts: a spoken blessing has the force of a deed. God honors it, not because of Jacob's deception, but because the content aligns with the divine oracle already given.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 12:2–3The Abrahamic blessing formula — now formally transmitted to Jacob despite the fraud. The covenant content is preserved even when the covenant carrier is morally compromised.
  • Num 23:8Balaam: "How can I curse whom God has not cursed?" — the irrevocability of divine blessing echoes the Isaac episode.
Verses 30–46 — Esau's Discovery and Grief
33Then Isaac trembled very violently and said, "Who was it then that hunted game and brought it to me, and I ate it all before you came, and I have blessed him? Yes, and he shall be blessed."35"Your brother came deceitfully, and he has taken away your blessing."36Esau said, "Is he not rightly named Jacob? For he has cheated me these two times. He took away my birthright, and behold, now he has taken away my blessing."38"Have you but one blessing, my father? Bless me, even me also, O my father." And Esau lifted up his voice and wept.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
27:36

"Esau said, 'Isn't he rightly named Jacob? This is the second time he has taken advantage of me: He took my birthright, and now he's taken my blessing!'" Esau's bitterness interprets Jacob's name correctly: Jacob (yaaqov) means "he grasps the heel" or "he deceives." The word for taking advantage (wayaqbeni) is a pun on the name. Yet Esau's complaint omits his own culpability: he despised the birthright (25:34) and sold it willingly. He frames himself as the victim of Jacob's treachery while ignoring his own earlier contempt for the covenant blessing. The failure to own responsibility for one's own choices is the perennial human response to consequential loss.

27:38–40

"Esau lifted up his voice and wept" — genuine grief, genuine loss. But Hebrews 12:17 says "he could bring about no change of mind, though he sought the blessing with tears." The tears are real; what is absent is repentance. He weeps for the lost blessing, not for the contempt that led to its loss. This is the difference between godly sorrow and worldly sorrow (2 Cor 7:10): one leads to repentance; the other remains grief without transformation.

Context

Isaac's trembling (v.33) is one of the most affecting moments in Genesis. He knows immediately that a divine reversal has occurred — "Yes, and he shall be blessed" is not resignation but recognition. The narrative's most devastating line is Esau's plea: "Have you but one blessing, my father? Bless me, even me also." His weeping is the emotional cost of the birthright's earlier contempt. He sold it for stew; now the full weight of what he traded away breaks over him.

Cross-Reference

  • Heb 12:17Esau "desired to inherit the blessing...but he was rejected, for he found no chance to repent, though he sought it with tears." Hebrews uses this as the paradigm of irreversible spiritual loss.
  • Rom 9:13"Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated" — Paul quotes Malachi 1:2–3 which looks back to this chapter as the basis of the two nations' relationship with God.
Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Isaac
Aged patriarch; appetite-driven; subverted
His plan to bless Esau against the oracle may reflect willful ignorance or partiality. He trembles when he realizes God's reversal has occurred — the trembling is recognition, not mere surprise. He ends by confirming the blessing: "Yes, and he shall be blessed."
Rebekah
Schemer; self-sacrificing; ultimately bereft
She is the oracle's guardian (25:23) and the scheme's architect. Her "Let your curse be on me" is the chapter's most striking line. She wins her goal and loses her son — Jacob flees and she never sees him again.
Jacob
Deceiver; covenant heir; about to be formed by suffering
He receives the blessing by fraud and will spend the next twenty years being defrauded by Laban in turn. The deceiver will be deceived. The name "Jacob" (heel-grasper) is vindicated by Esau's bitter pun (v.36): "Is he not rightly named Jacob?"
Esau
Victim; bitter; genuinely grieved
His weeping (v.38) is one of the most humanly authentic moments in Genesis. He despised his birthright in a moment of appetite; now he discovers the full cost. He is not wicked — he is tragic. Genesis does not hate Esau; it traces the consequences of his priorities.

Ancient Sources

Nuzi Tablet on Oral Deathbed Blessings
Nuzi tablets (15th c. BC) document cases where oral deathbed declarations had full legal force — one tablet records a court ruling that an oral gift-declaration could not be revoked even when the circumstances were disputed. The irrevocability of Isaac's blessing reflects this legal reality precisely.

Reception History

Origen, Homilies on Genesis
Origen read Jacob's deception allegorically: the two kids represent the two peoples (Jew and Gentile); the "smooth" skin represents the Gentiles receiving what was due the "hairy" firstborn Israel. His allegorical move was influential in the early church's typological reading of election.
Luther, Lectures on Genesis
Luther defended the deception as Rebekah and Jacob acting on faith in the oracle — they were "stealing what was already theirs by divine promise." Calvin disagreed strongly, insisting the fraud was sinful even if the outcome was providential. The debate continues.

Literary Structure

AIsaac's plan: bless Esau (vv.1–4)
BRebekah's counter-plan (vv.5–13)
CJacob disguises himself (vv.14–17)
CENTER: The blessing spoken (vv.27–29)
C'Esau arrives; deception discovered (vv.30–35)
B'Esau's grief; Esau's partial blessing (vv.36–40)
A'Esau's hatred; Jacob sent away (vv.41–46)
The chapter is a tight chiasm around the moment of blessing. Every element before the blessing is reversed after: Isaac's plan → frustrated; Rebekah's scheme → succeeded; disguise → discovered; blessing → confirmed irrevocable. The structural center holds: the covenant blessing was spoken, and it shall stand.

Intertextual Threading

Gen 25:23→Gen 27:29Fulfillment
The prenatal oracle — "the older shall serve the younger" — is accomplished in the blessing: "be lord over your brothers." The fraudulent means do not invalidate the divinely intended end.
Gen 27:36→Gen 32:28Echo
Esau's bitter pun: "Is he not rightly named Jacob [supplanter]?" — answered twenty years later when God renames Jacob "Israel" (32:28). The character flaw encoded in his birth-name is transformed, not excused.
Gen 27:38→Heb 12:17Warning
Esau's tears seeking what he could not recover become the NT's paradigm of spiritual irreversibility — Hebrews uses his grief to warn against trading the eternal for the immediate.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 27 poses the sharpest ethical question in the patriarchal narrative: how does God honor a blessing stolen by fraud? The answer the text gives is indirect but unmistakable — God's sovereign purpose (the oracle of 25:23) is accomplished despite, not because of, human scheming. The covenant is transmitted; the human cost is immense. Neither Rebekah's nor Jacob's character is excused — but the covenant purpose is inexorable.
