Genesis 3

The Fall: Temptation, Disobedience, and Judgment

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–7 — The Temptation and the Fall
1Now the serpent was more crafty than any of the wild animals the Lord God had made. He said to the woman, "Did God really say, 'You must not eat from any tree in the garden'?"2The woman said to the serpent, "We may eat fruit from the trees in the garden,3but God did say, 'You must not eat fruit from the tree that is in the middle of the garden, and you must not touch it, or you will die.'"4"You will not certainly die," the serpent said to the woman.5"For God knows that when you eat from it your eyes will be opened, and you will be like God, knowing good and evil."6When the woman saw that the fruit of the tree was good for food and pleasing to the eye, and also desirable for gaining wisdom, she took some and ate it. She also gave some to her husband, who was with her, and he ate it.7Then the eyes of both of them were opened, and they realized they were naked; so they sewed fig leaves together and made coverings for themselves.

Hebrew Word Study

נָחָשׁ nachash — serpent: V.1: The word means serpent and is connected to the root for "divination" or "enchantment." The serpent is described as the most arum (crafty/subtle) of all the creatures God made — a quality that is morally neutral in itself but is here turned to destructive use. The NT identifies the serpent with Satan (Rev 12:9; 20:2), though Genesis itself presents the creature without the full developed theology of later revelation.

עָרוּם arum — crafty / shrewd: V.1: The same root as the arom (naked) of 2:25 — a deliberate wordplay. The naked (arom) innocence of 2:25 gives way immediately to the crafty (arum) assault of the serpent in 3:1. The connection alerts the reader that what follows is an attack on the very innocence just described.

תַּאֲוָה taavah — desire / craving: V.6: The woman saw that the tree was good for food (sensory appeal), pleasing to the eye (aesthetic appeal), and desirable for gaining wisdom (intellectual/spiritual appeal). Three dimensions of temptation, corresponding to 1 John 2:16's "lust of the flesh, lust of the eyes, and pride of life." The triple structure of the temptation mirrors the triple structure of the prohibition.

Historical Context

The serpent's strategy — questioning God's word, then denying its consequence, then appealing to the desire for autonomy — is the paradigm of temptation throughout Scripture. The offer of being "like God, knowing good and evil" (v.5) appeals to the image-bearing dignity already possessed by humanity (1:26) but promises a counterfeit version: self-determination rather than God-given authority. What is offered is not something new but a distorted version of something already given.

Context

The temptation narrative is a masterpiece of compressed dramatic irony. The woman already possesses the image of God; the serpent offers a counterfeit. She already has everything in the garden; the one prohibited thing becomes the focal point of desire. The structure of the temptation — seeing, desiring, taking — becomes the paradigm for the progression of sin throughout Scripture (cf. Achan, Josh 7:21; David, 2 Sam 11:2-4).

Cross-Reference

  • Rom 5:12–19Paul’s foundational theology of original sin: through one man sin entered the world. Adam’s act is the “trespass” that brought condemnation on all; Christ’s act reverses it.
  • 1 Cor 15:22“In Adam all die, so in Christ all will be made alive” — the entire Adam/Christ typology rests on Genesis 3.
  • Matt 4:1–11Jesus’ wilderness temptation mirrors the three appeals of Gen 3:6 — bread/food, spectacle/aesthetics, kingdoms/pride — and reverses each with Scripture.
  • 2 Cor 11:3Paul warns the Corinthians against being deceived as Eve was — the serpent’s cunning as an ongoing spiritual danger.
  • Rev 12:9“That ancient serpent called the devil” — Revelation identifies the Genesis serpent with Satan explicitly.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1

The serpent is introduced as "more crafty than any wild animal the LORD God had made." This is the first indication that a malevolent spiritual agent is operating in creation. The NT makes explicit what Genesis implies: the serpent is Satan (Rev 12:9; 20:2), the adversary who opposes God and deceives humanity. In Genesis, the figure is deliberately presented with some ambiguity — as a creature of God, yet one operating against God's purposes.

3:6

The progression of the fall — saw, desired, took, ate, gave — is the anatomy of sin: perception becomes desire, desire becomes action, action becomes complicity. The woman acts first; Adam takes what she offers without recorded protest. Paul notes that Adam was "not the one deceived" (1 Tim 2:14) — Adam's sin was not ignorance but deliberate disobedience. Both are culpable, in different ways.

3:1

The serpent's opening gambit — "Did God really say...?" — is the original hermeneutic of doubt. It does not deny God's word outright; it questions it, misquotes it (adding "any" to "every tree"), and thereby reframes the entire relationship between humanity and God as one of suspicion and restriction rather than abundance and love. This is the paradigm of all subsequent attacks on Scripture's authority.

3:4-5

"You will not surely die... you will be like God." The serpent's two claims: denial of consequence (death is not real) and promise of ascent (become like God). Both are half-truths in the worst sense — containing enough truth to be credible, enough falsehood to be lethal. The irony: humanity already was "like God" in the image sense (1:26). What is offered is not elevation but a counterfeit of what they already possessed.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:1

The serpent's opening question — "Did God indeed say, 'You shall not eat from any tree of the garden'?" — is a deliberate misrepresentation. God said "from every tree... you may eat freely; from the tree of knowledge... you shall not eat." The serpent's version collapses the abundance into the restriction, making the prohibition sound total when it is in fact singular. The woman corrects the misquotation but adds her own: "you must not touch it" — an addition not in the original command (2:17). Both distortions set up the fall.

3:6

Vatere ha'isha ki tov ha'etz lemachol — "the woman saw that the tree was good for eating." The verb raa (saw) echoes God's seeing in chapter 1 — "God saw that it was good." Human vision here mimics divine assessment but in a fallen register: what she sees as good is what God has forbidden. The perversion of the image is enacted in the very vocabulary of the narrative.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:1

The serpent is characterised as arum (shrewd/crafty). Alter notes this is the only psychological characterisation in the entire creation account — all other creatures are described by kind or function, not character. The serpent alone has an inner quality, a disposition. This subtlety is part of what makes the temptation dangerous: it does not assault directly but insinuates.

3:6-7

Vatikach mipiryoh vatochal vatitten gam le'ishah imah — "she took of its fruit and ate, and she also gave to her husband with her, and he ate." Alter: the narrative economy is devastating. No deliberation, no extended struggle, no dramatic pause — just a sequence of verbs. Then the immediate consequence: "and the eyes of both of them were opened." The opened eyes echo the serpent's promise (v.5) but with bitter irony: what they see is their own nakedness and shame, not the divine wisdom they sought.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:1

The serpent uses a question, not a command: "Did God really say?" Calvin: "Satan does not at first openly assail the faith of the woman in God's word, but in an indirect and insidious manner insinuates a doubt." The strategy is to undermine rather than to contradict — to make the woman question what God said before she doubts that he said it. This is the pattern of unbelief: it begins with uncertainty about God's word, not denial of it.

3:6

Calvin against those who excuse the fall as understandable: "The enormity of the sin must be estimated, not merely from one part of it, but from all its parts taken together. When the woman, corrupted by the fallacious suggestion of the devil, distrusted God, and then, on the authority of the devil, gave credit to a lie, she fell from her integrity." The sin was not ignorance but pride — the desire to be autonomous, to need no external authority.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:1

The NET note on arum (crafty) observes this is a positive word in Proverbs (shrewd, prudent) but here is used negatively. The note also addresses the serpent's identity: Genesis itself presents a talking serpent without theological commentary; the full identification with Satan comes from later revelation (Rev 12:9). The NET translation "shrewd" (over "crafty") attempts to preserve the moral ambiguity of the original Hebrew.

3:6

The NET note on "desirable for gaining wisdom" (nechmed haetz lehaskil) notes the Hebrew root sekhel means practical wisdom or discernment. The note observes that the three dimensions of the appeal — physical, aesthetic, intellectual — correspond to different aspects of human desire. The net effect is a comprehensive appeal to the whole person, making the temptation seem rational rather than merely impulsive.

Verses 8–13 — God Confronts Adam and Eve
8Then the man and his wife heard the sound of the Lord God as he was walking in the garden in the cool of the day, and they hid from the Lord God among the trees of the garden.9But the Lord God called to the man, "Where are you?"10He answered, "I heard you in the garden, and I was afraid because I was naked; so I hid."11And he said, "Who told you that you were naked? Have you eaten from the tree that I commanded you not to eat from?"12The man said, "The woman you put here with me—she gave me some fruit from the tree, and I ate it."13Then the Lord God said to the woman, "What is this you have done?" The woman said, "The serpent deceived me, and I ate."

Hebrew Word Study

אַיֶּכָּה ayyekkah — where are you: V.9: God's first recorded words after the fall. The question is not geographical — God is omniscient. It is relational: the divine call to a creature who has hidden himself, the invitation to come out of concealment and stand in the presence of God. The same root appears in Isaiah's call (Isa 6) and the prophets' summons — God's "where are you?" is always a call to account and to restoration.

יָרֵא yare — afraid: V.10: The first human emotion named in the Bible is fear — specifically, fear of God. Before the fall there was no fear, only open communion. The fear that drives hiding is the beginning of the broken relationship between humanity and God. All subsequent religion's false versions of God — the tyrant who must be appeased — trace back to this moment of shame-driven fear.

Historical Context

The divine interrogation in vv.9-13 follows a pattern familiar from ancient Near Eastern covenant litigation: the suzerain summons the vassal to account for breach of covenant. God does not confront the couple with accusation but with questions — giving each party an opportunity to acknowledge, explain, or confess. The pattern of displacement — "the woman you gave me" (Adam), "the serpent deceived me" (Eve) — is the first recorded instance of blame-shifting, and sets the pattern for all subsequent human avoidance of moral accountability.

Context

The interrogation scene reveals the spiritual anatomy of the post-fall condition: hiding (shame before God), fear (broken communion), blame-shifting (refusal of responsibility). Each element is the direct inversion of the pre-fall condition: open communion, no shame, relational harmony. The fall does not merely add sin to an otherwise intact creation; it inverts the entire structure of human existence.

Cross-Reference

  • Rev 6:15-16Kings and great men hide in caves — the echo of Adam's hiding in Gen 3:8, now at cosmic scale.
  • John 3:20Everyone who does evil hates the light and will not come into the light — Jesus reads Gen 3 as the paradigm of the rejection of divine presence.
  • Rom 3:10-12No one seeks God — Paul reads the pattern of evasion established in Gen 3 as universal human nature.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:9

God's question — "Where are you?" — is not an information-gathering exercise but a judicial summons and a grace-filled invitation. God knew where Adam was. The question forces Adam to confront his own condition. In asking "where are you?" God is asking "what have you become? what have you done to the relationship?" The question contains both accountability and the offer of restored communion.

3:12–13

Adam's "the woman you gave me" is the first recorded blame-shift in human history. Note the double displacement: blame to the woman, and implicitly to God himself ("you gave me"). Eve's "the serpent deceived me" is at least more accurate — she was deceived (1 Tim 2:14 confirms this) — but it is still evasion of personal responsibility. Both responses reveal the post-fall human refusal to own sin.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:9

Ayyekkah — "Where are you?" The divine call is the first divine speech directed specifically to an individual after the fall. Sarna notes the pathos of the question: God who fills heaven and earth (Jer 23:24) asks where his creature is. The question is not about location but about relationship. Hiding from God is itself the symptom of the broken relationship — the attempt to live outside the divine presence that was once the joy of creaturely existence.

3:12

Ha'isha asher natattah imadi hi natan li min haetz — "the woman whom you gave to be with me, she gave me of the tree." Adam's syntax is revealing: "the woman whom you gave me" — the gift of 2:22 is now presented as the source of his downfall. Sarna: "What was originally said in ecstasy (2:23) is now, in the hour of crisis, turned into recrimination." The fall does not merely break the human-divine relationship; it breaks the human-human relationship.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:8

Vayishme'u et qol YHWH Elohim mithallek bagan — "they heard the voice/sound of the LORD God moving about in the garden." The word qol means both "voice" and "sound." The theophany is auditory — the sound of the deity moving through the garden in the evening breeze. Alter notes the domestic intimacy of the image: God habitually walking in the garden in the cool of the day. This was the normal condition of human-divine fellowship; the hiding is the abnormality.

3:11

Hamin haetz asher tsivviticha levilti achol mimenu achalt — "Have you eaten from the tree of which I commanded you not to eat?" The question already contains the answer. God does not ask in ignorance but in the mode of the prosecuting judge who presents the charge as a question. The human must now either confess or evade. The narrative documents both responses — evasion — with devastating economy.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:8

Adam and Eve hid from the presence of God. Calvin: "Nothing is more irrational than that a miserable man, destitute of all help, should proceed to aggravate his own ruin by fleeing from God. But so it must be, since the person who has been alienated from God by sin cannot but dread his approach. It is therefore no wonder that Adam, conscience-stricken and confounded, was overwhelmed with fear." Hiding is the logic of guilt; exposure is the logic of grace.

3:12

Calvin is merciless on Adam's blame-shift: "Adam not only refuses to make a voluntary confession of his guilt, but transfers the blame to God himself. For in accusing the woman, he indirectly arraigns God as the author of the sin. ‘The woman,’ says he, ‘whom thou gavest me.’ He means that God had not dealt prudently or well in giving him such an associate." The fall reveals not only human disobedience but human ingratitude.

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:9

"Where are you?" — The NET note on ayyekkah observes this is a compound of ay (where?) and -kah (you, masculine singular). It is directed specifically to Adam, not to both. The individual accountability of the male head of the household is addressed first — which becomes the basis for Paul's reading in Romans 5 of Adam (not Eve) as the one through whom sin entered the world. The personal address underlines covenantal representative responsibility.

3:12

The NET note on "the woman you gave me" observes that Adam's response syntactically foregrounds the woman before mentioning his own action. "She gave me some fruit from the tree, and I ate it" — the grammatical structure minimises his own agency. The note draws the connection to the pattern of blame-shifting throughout Genesis, particularly Jacob, Saul, and Aaron.

Verses 14–19 — The Curse on Serpent, Woman, and Man
14So the Lord God said to the serpent, "Because you have done this, cursed are you above all livestock and all wild animals! You will crawl on your belly and you will eat dust all the days of your life.15And I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and hers; he will crush your head, and you will strike his heel."16To the woman he said, "I will make your pains in childbearing very severe; with painful labor you will give birth to children. Your desire will be for your husband, and he will rule over you."17To Adam he said, "Because you listened to your wife and ate fruit from the tree about which I commanded you, 'You must not eat from it,' cursed is the ground because of you; through painful toil you will eat food from it all the days of your life.18It will produce thorns and thistles for you, and you will eat the plants of the field.19By the sweat of your brow you will eat your food until you return to the ground, since from it you were taken; for dust you are and to dust you will return."

Hebrew Word Study

אֵיבָה evah — enmity / hostility: V.15: The first word of the protoevangelium. God places hostility between the serpent and the woman — between evil and humanity. This is not the creation of evil but the creation of resistance to evil. The enmity is divinely instituted, which means the human impulse to resist evil is itself God's gift.

יְשׁוּפְךָ yeshufecha — he will crush / strike you: V.15: The same verb (shuf) is used for both the crushing of the serpent's head and the striking of the heel — the translation "crush" and "strike" attempts to capture the asymmetry, but the Hebrew uses the same word for both. The decisive blow (to the head) belongs to the seed of the woman; the serpent's blow (to the heel) is painful but not fatal. This asymmetry is the basis of the messianic reading.

עִצָּבוֹן itsabon — pain / toil: V.16-17: The word appears in both the woman's curse (v.16, "pain in childbearing") and the man's (v.17, "painful toil"). The same word applies to both: both will toil with pain in the fundamental activities of their respective vocations — bearing life and sustaining it. The fall affects not the activities themselves (they were given before the fall) but the conditions under which they are performed.

Historical Context

The three curses in Genesis 3 follow the order of the temptation narrative in reverse: serpent (who initiated), woman (who acted second), man (who acted last). The asymmetry of the judgments is significant: the serpent is cursed directly; the ground is cursed for the man's sake; the woman is not cursed but receives consequences. The protoevangelium of v.15 — the first gospel announcement — is embedded within the curse on the serpent, transforming judgment into the first promise of redemption.

Context

The curses are not arbitrary punishments but the natural consequences of the fallen condition, described in terms of the three relationships the fall has broken: humanity's relationship with the spiritual realm (serpent/enmity), woman's relationship with her body and her husband (pain in childbirth, relationship distortion), and man's relationship with the ground (the adamah he was formed from now resists him). Everything that was gift becomes struggle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:15

This is the protoevangelium — the first gospel announcement in Scripture. God promises enmity between the serpent and the woman, and between their respective offspring. "He will crush your head, and you will strike his heel" — the seed of the woman will deliver the decisive blow to the serpent, though at cost to himself. Every subsequent messianic promise in the OT is an elaboration of this first prophecy. The cross is the heel-striking; the resurrection is the head-crushing.

3:16–19

The curses address the two fundamental human vocations established in chapter 2: bearing life (the woman) and sustaining life from the ground (the man). Neither vocation is removed — God does not withdraw the commission to be fruitful and to till the ground. What changes is the conditions: pain will accompany what was meant to be joyful; struggle and resistance will accompany what was meant to be flourishing partnership. The fall does not cancel creation; it corrupts it.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:14-15

The curse on the serpent is the most severe of the three judgments — it alone is explicitly cursed. Sarna notes the irony: the creature whose craftiness (arum) initiated the fall is reduced to the lowest form of creaturely existence, crawling on its belly and eating dust. The enmity God places between the serpent's seed and the woman's seed transforms the personal act of judgment into a cosmic and ongoing struggle — the history of the world is the history of this enmity.

3:16

Beetsev teldi banim — "in pain you will give birth to children." The word etsev (pain) is the same root as itsabon in v.17 for the man's toil. Sarna observes that the parallel structure of the two judgments is deliberate: the woman's pain is in childbearing (bringing forth life), the man's pain is in tilling (sustaining life). Together they define the fallen human condition: life is given and sustained, but only through suffering.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:14-15

The divine speech shifts register from interrogation (vv.9-13) to formal decree. Alter notes the three-part structure: curse on the serpent (most severe), consequences for the woman, consequences for the man. The literary symmetry — three agents tempted/judged in reverse order of their temptation — gives the judgment section the quality of a formal legal proceeding. The poetic quality of v.15 (the protoevangelium) stands out within the prose — elevated language for the most theologically significant statement in the passage.

3:19

El afar tashuv — "to dust you shall return." The ending of the man's curse returns to the dust of 2:7 — dust you were, to dust you shall return. The chiasm is complete: formed from earth, animated by divine breath, and now sentenced to return to earth. Alter: the wordplay between adam, adamah, and afar (dust) that runs through chapters 2-3 finds its darkest expression here. Human identity is bound up with the ground, and the ground now claims its own.

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:15

The seed of the woman is not merely humanity in general but the singular seed who will bruise the serpent's head. Calvin reads the messianic interpretation as the primary sense: "I interpret this simply as a prediction of perpetual discord between the human race and serpents, which yet has a deeper meaning. The victory was gained not by Adam or Eve but by their descendants; and specifically, by the one Seed in whom all the nations of the earth are blessed.

3:16-19

The curses reveal that sin does not merely add a new problem to creation; it corrupts the existing good. Pain in childbirth is the corruption of the blessing "be fruitful and multiply." Thorns in the field are the corruption of the garden's abundance. The relationship distortion in marriage is the corruption of the "one flesh" union. Calvin: "God is not the author of these corruptions, but the avenger of them." The fall's consequences are the natural result of broken relationship with the source of all good.

3:15

The NET note on yeshufecha (crush/strike) discusses the long history of interpretation. The note acknowledges the messianic reading as historically dominant in Jewish and Christian interpretation, while noting the Hebrew verb shuf is used for both actions (head-crushing and heel-striking), suggesting ongoing conflict rather than a single decisive blow. Verses 20–24 — Clothing and Expulsion from Eden
20Adam named his wife Eve, because she would become the mother of all the living.21The Lord God made garments of skin for Adam and his wife and clothed them.22And the Lord God said, "The man has now become like one of us, knowing good and evil. He must not be allowed to reach out his hand and take also from the tree of life and eat, and live forever."23So the Lord God banished him from the Garden of Eden to work the ground from which he had been taken.24After he drove the man out, he placed on the east side of the Garden of Eden cherubim and a flaming sword flashing back and forth to guard the way to the tree of life.

Hebrew Word Study

כָּתְנוֹת עוֹר kotnot or — garments of skin: V.21: The divine provision of clothing is an act of grace within judgment. God could simply have expelled the couple; instead he clothes them first. The skins require the death of an animal — the first death in Scripture — to provide covering for human shame. The theological trajectory from fig leaves (human self-covering) to animal skins (divine provision through death) points toward the sacrificial system and ultimately the cross.

אַחַד מִמֶּנּוּ echad mimmennu — one of us: V.22: The divine deliberation echoes 1:26 ("let us make"). "The man has now become like one of us, knowing good and evil." The tragic irony: the serpent's promise ("you will be like God, knowing good and evil") has come true in the worst possible way — not divine wisdom but the experiential knowledge of sin and its consequences. The plural ("us") of the divine council underscores that the human has transgressed into divine territory.

Historical Context

The cherubim stationed at the entrance to the garden (v.24) appear throughout the ancient Near East as guardian figures of sacred spaces — winged creatures flanking the entrance to temples and the approach to deities. In Israel, cherubim guard the ark of the covenant (Exod 25:18-20) and appear in Ezekiel's visions. Their placement at Eden's gate signals that the garden remains a holy place — but one from which the unholy have been expelled. The flaming sword turning every direction is a self-operating barrier, not requiring a guardian to wield it.

Context

The expulsion is the most severe consequence of the fall, but it contains within it the seeds of hope. God provides clothing before expulsion (grace precedes judgment). The tree of life is guarded rather than destroyed (redemption remains possible). The couple leaves together (the relationship survives, however damaged). And the protoevangelium of 3:15 has already been spoken. The expulsion is not abandonment but the beginning of the long covenant history by which God will bring his creature back to the garden — and beyond it.

Places

East of Eden
Eastern edge of the garden
The expulsion is directed eastward — the direction of exile throughout Genesis. Cain will go further east after his judgment (4:16); Babel is in the east (11:2). The theological direction of exile is east, away from the divine presence.
Cherubim / Flaming Sword
Entrance to the garden, eastern side
The guardian is placed at the garden's eastern entrance — the same direction of expulsion. The way back to Eden is not destroyed but guarded. The flaming sword that turns in every direction suggests no approach from any angle is possible; Hebrews 4:16 and Revelation 22 narrate the reopening of this access.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
3:21

The garments of skin are among the most theologically significant details in the entire creation narrative. God himself makes clothing for his fallen creatures — the first act of grace after the fall. The skins require the death of an animal: life forfeited to cover human shame. This is the first sacrifice, the first blood shed for human sin, the prototype of the entire sacrificial system that will culminate in the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world.

3:22–24

The expulsion from Eden is both judgment and mercy. Judgment: they cannot stay in the garden in their fallen state. Mercy: they cannot eat of the tree of life and live forever in their fallen condition — which would be the worst possible fate. The expulsion preserves the possibility of redemption. If they ate the tree of life in their fallen state, the restoration would be impossible; death opens the door to resurrection. God guards what is holy and preserves what is redemptive.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:21

Kotnot or — "garments of skin." The provision of clothing by God himself is an act of divine care within the context of judgment. Sarna notes the contrast with the fig leaves of v.7: the self-made coverings are replaced by divine provision. The repeated occurrence of or (skin) in this narrative (the serpent's craftiness in v.1 is arum, from the same root as or/skin in some readings) creates a verbal texture that ties the temptation to its consequences.

3:24

The cherubim (keruvim) are not merely ornamental figures. They appear in the tabernacle and temple as guardians of the divine presence (Exod 25:18-22; 1 Kgs 6:23-28). Their placement at Eden's entrance signals that Eden remains a sacred space — the original sanctuary — but one from which the unholy must be excluded. Sarna: "The expulsion from the Garden is not merely a punishment but a protection — the holy and the profane cannot coexist."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
3:21

Vaya'as YHWH Elohim le'adam uleshto kotnot or — "and the LORD God made for Adam and his wife garments of skin." Alter notes the grammatical tenderness of the detail: God makes (not gives, not provides, but actively makes) clothing for his creatures. After the interrogation, the judgment, the cursing — God bends to a domestic act of care. The scene has the quality of a parent clothing a child who has done something shameful. The relationship is broken; the care persists.

3:24

Vayegaresh et ha'adam — "he drove out the man." The verb garash is a strong expulsion word — used of divorcing a wife (Lev 21:7), of driving out enemies (Exod 23:28). The expulsion is not gentle. And yet, Alter notes, the couple is expelled together — "the man and his wife" (v.24 implies both). The one-flesh union of 2:24 survives the fall. Broken in its conditions, it is not dissolved. They leave together.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
3:21

The garments of skin show that God did not utterly forsake his fallen creatures. Calvin: "God by this act of kindness invites Adam, already lost and given over to despair, to seek the hope of salvation." The clothing is not merely practical but theological — God covers what human shame cannot cover. The skin of a slain animal covering human nakedness is the first enacted sacrifice, the first atonement.

3:22-24

The expulsion is an act of mercy as much as judgment. Calvin: "God deprives Adam of life, not because he wishes to deprive him of it, but because he sees that his life would be his greatest torment. For what would it have profited Adam to live forever as he then was?" The tree of life in a fallen state would be the perpetuation of misery, not the gift of blessing. God guards the tree so that the restoration, when it comes, will be a true restoration to life — not merely survival.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
3:21

The NET note on kotnot or ("garments of skin") discusses whether "skin" here means animal skin or simply the type of material (tough outer covering). The note finds no reason to depart from "animal skin" and notes the implication of animal death — a theological foreshadowing the NET notes are careful to acknowledge without over-pressing. The note also addresses the contrast with the fig-leaf self-covering: divine provision replaces human inadequacy.

3:24

The NET note on the cherubim explains these are composite divine beings (cf. Ezekiel 1,10) who serve as guardians of the divine presence. The "flaming sword flashing back and forth" is a self-operating barrier — the verb connotes rotation or turning in every direction, suggesting there is no angle of approach. The note connects to the tabernacle and temple cherubim, reading Eden's architecture as the prototype of Israel's sanctuary.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

The Serpent
Deceiver; agent of the fall
The craftiest of all creatures (arum), the serpent uses a question to insinuate doubt about God's word. Its identity as the adversary (Satan) is made explicit in later revelation (Rev 12:9; 20:2). In Genesis itself, the creature is presented as a member of God's creation who has turned against God's purposes. Its judgment — belly-crawling, dust-eating, enmity with humanity — is the most severe of the three judgments in the chapter.
Eve / The Woman
First to be deceived; bearer of the seed
Eve's sin was deception (1 Tim 2:14) — she genuinely believed the serpent's claim. Her action nonetheless was disobedience. Her judgment addresses the two fundamental dimensions of womanhood: the pain of bearing life and the distortion of the marriage relationship. Within the judgment comes the first promise: the seed of the woman will crush the serpent's head (3:15) — the protoevangelium points forward to a birth that will reverse what her birth began.
Adam
Covenant head; first sinner
Adam was not deceived (1 Tim 2:14) — he sinned knowingly. His blame-shift to the woman (and implicitly to God) is the paradigm of fallen human moral evasion. His judgment addresses the ground he was formed from: the adamah now resists the adam. Paul reads Adam as the representative head of humanity, through whom sin and death entered the world (Rom 5:12-21), whose role is fulfilled and reversed by the last Adam, Jesus Christ.

Ancient Sources

Enuma Elish — The Combat Myth
In Babylonian cosmology, the great serpent/dragon Tiamat represents the chaos powers that must be defeated before creation can be ordered. Marduk defeats Tiamat and creates the world from her body. The combat myth pattern — a god defeating a serpent-like chaos power — pervades ancient Near Eastern literature.
Genesis 3 inverts the combat myth: the serpent is not a power equal to God (Tiamat vs Marduk) but a creature who entered God's creation and was judged by God. The "crushing of the head" in 3:15 echoes the combat language of the ANE traditions but places it entirely within history and covenant, not primordial cosmogony. God does not battle the serpent; he judges it and promises its eventual defeat by a human descendant.
The Adapa Myth (Babylon, c.14th c. BCE)
The Babylonian myth of Adapa describes a wise man who is offered the bread and water of eternal life by the gods but refuses them on false advice and thus loses immortality. The parallel to Genesis 3 — a human at a threshold between mortal and immortal existence — is notable.
The parallel highlights the differences: Adapa loses immortality through ignorance and bad advice; Adam loses it through deliberate disobedience. In Adapa, immortality is withheld by the gods' strategy; in Genesis, it is withheld as a consequence of human sin. The theological weight of moral agency is absent from the Babylonian version.

Reception History

Irenaeus, Against Heresies (c.AD 180)
Irenaeus developed the concept of recapitulation: Christ as the second Adam who recapitulates (re-heads, sums up) the entire human story, reversing where the first Adam failed. "He took up man into Himself, the invisible becoming visible, the incomprehensible being made comprehensible... recapitulating in Himself all nations dispersed from Adam." The entire economy of salvation is the reversal of Genesis 3 in and through the incarnate Son.
Augustine, Confessions and City of God (c.398-426 AD)
Augustine's reading of the fall shaped Western Christianity for over a millennium. His doctrine of original sin: Adam's sin was transmitted to all his descendants, so that all humans are born in a state of sin and under condemnation. The fall was a fall in Adam — not merely an example we follow but a condition we inherit. Augustine's exegesis of Gen 3 and Rom 5 became the basis for the Reformation doctrine of total depravity.
C.S. Lewis, The Problem of Pain (1940)
Lewis argues for a "historical fall" that need not be identical to the Genesis narrative in every detail, but insists on its essential truth: there was a first sin, a definitive moment of human self-assertion against God, from which all subsequent human misery flows. Lewis: "I have the deepest respect even for Pagan myths, still more for myths in Holy Scripture. I therefore do not doubt that something which may be called The Fall occurred."

Literary Structure

Temptation — 3:1-6Serpent questions → denies → promises. Woman sees, desires, takes. Man takes without recorded deliberation.
Consequence — 3:7"Their eyes were opened" — immediate shame, self-covering. The serpent's promise fulfilled in the worst possible way.
Divine Confrontation — 3:8-13God walks, calls, questions. Adam hides, blames woman/God. Eve blames serpent. Neither confesses.
Three curses in reverse order: serpent (most severe) → woman → man. Protoevangelium embedded in serpent's curse.
Grace in Judgment — 3:20-24God clothes the couple (first sacrifice). Expulsion is mercy (prevents immortal fallenness). Protoevangelium stands.
Genesis 3 is the pivot of all Scripture. Everything before it (chapters 1-2) describes what was; everything after it describes what must be undone. The narrative is structured as a perfect literary whole: the craft of the serpent (arum, 3:1) answers the nakedness of the couple (arummim, 2:25); the opening eyes bring not wisdom but shame; the promised god-likeness arrives as knowledge of their own ruin. The chapter ends with expulsion but also with the protoevangelium — the first promise of the seed who will crush what the serpent has done.

Textual Notes

3:1nachash — serpent or divine being?
MT nachash = serpent (the common word). LXX: ophis. The serpent is identified as "more crafty than any wild animal the LORD God had made" — clearly a created being. NT identifies the serpent with Satan/the devil (Rev 12:9; 20:2). The Hebrew term does not explicitly mean a supernatural being; the theological identification is supplied by later revelation.
Whether the fall narrative presents the tempter as merely a clever animal or a supernatural adversarial being affects the doctrine of Satan and the nature of the original temptation.
3:15enmity between seed — individual or collective? masculine or neuter?
MT hu' yeshufkha rosh = "he will crush your head." The Hebrew pronoun hu' is masculine singular. LXX: autos = masculine singular (same). Vulgate (Jerome): ipsa (feminine — "she"), enabling the Marian interpretation. Modern translations follow MT masculine. The referent is debated: the seed of the woman collectively (Israel, then Christ) or a single messianic individual.
The Protevangelium (Gen 3:15) is the first messianic prophecy. The masculine pronoun supports a singular ultimate referent; the collective reading also has strong support from the use of "seed" (zera) as collective.
3:22behold, the man has become like one of us
MT The plural "us" recurs from 1:26. Options: divine council, Trinity, or rhetorical plural. The statement is ambiguous as to whether it is ironic ("like us — but now mortal") or straightforwardly descriptive ("knowing good and evil as we do").
The divine assessment of the fallen human condition: whether "like us" is an achievement or a tragic irony shapes the theology of the fall.

Scholarly Debates

The Nature of the Fall: Historical Event or Theological Narrative?
Historical fall
Wenham, Collins, Mathews
Gen 3 describes a historical event: real people, a real garden, a real act of disobedience with cosmic consequences. The NT's use of Adam (Rom 5:12-21; 1 Cor 15:21-22) requires a historical fall as the basis for both universal sin and the redemptive-historical parallel with Christ.
Theological narrative/myth
von Rad, Brueggemann, Enns
Gen 3 is a theological reflection on the universal human condition — why humans experience guilt, shame, suffering, and death — using narrative form. Historical factuality is beside the point; the theological truth about humanity's alienation from God is the point.
Mythohistory
Sarna, Goldingay
The narrative uses the idiom of ancient storytelling to convey real theological and historical content. The categories of 'history' and 'myth' are anachronistic; the text is ancient literature that conveys truth at multiple registers simultaneously.
The historical reading remains essential for the Pauline anthropology of Romans 5. The theological narrative reading has dominated critical scholarship since the 19th century. The debate turns partly on one's view of genre and partly on how seriously one takes Paul's Adam-Christ typology.
The Identity of the Serpent
Mere animal
Driver, Westermann
The serpent is a crafty animal — no more. The later identification with Satan is a secondary theological development not present in the text itself. Gen 3 is a etiological story about why snakes crawl.
Supernatural adversary
Hamilton, Waltke, Collins
The serpent's speech and knowledge exceed natural animal capacity. Rev 12:9 and 20:2 explicitly identify the serpent as the devil. The literary presentation — it speaks, it reasons, it deceives — requires a supernatural agent behind or identical with the snake.
Mediating position
Wenham, Longman
The serpent in the text is an animal; behind the serpent is a supernatural adversary. The narrative works on both levels simultaneously.
The supernatural identification is required by the canonical context (Rev 12:9) and by the NT's doctrine of Satan. The "mere animal" reading ignores the plain import of later canonical revelation.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 3 is the theological hinge of the entire Bible. It explains why the world is as it is — broken, painful, death-bound — and simultaneously announces the first promise of its repair. The protoevangelium (3:15) is the seed from which all subsequent messianic expectation grows. The themes of fall, redemption, typology, and providence are all at maximum intensity in this chapter. Every book of the Bible from here to Revelation is either the outworking of the fall or the outworking of the promise.
