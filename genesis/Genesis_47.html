Genesis 47

Genesis 47

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–12 — Jacob Before Pharaoh; Settlement in Goshen
1Joseph went and told Pharaoh, "My father and brothers, with their flocks and herds and everything they own, have come from the land of Canaan and are now in Goshen."2He chose five of his brothers and presented them before Pharaoh.3Pharaoh asked the brothers, "What is your occupation?" "Your servants are shepherds," they replied to Pharaoh, "just as our fathers were."4They also said to him, "We have come to live here for a while, because the famine is severe in Canaan and your servants' flocks have no pasture. So now, please let your servants settle in Goshen."5Pharaoh said to Joseph, "Your father and your brothers have come to you,6and the land of Egypt is before you; settle your father and your brothers in the best part of the land. Let them live in Goshen. And if you know of any among them with special ability, put them in charge of my own livestock."7Then Joseph brought his father Jacob in and presented him before Pharaoh. After Jacob blessed Pharaoh,8Pharaoh asked him, "How old are you?"9And Jacob said to Pharaoh, "The years of my pilgrimage are a hundred and thirty. My years have been few and difficult, and they do not equal the years of the pilgrimage of my fathers."10Then Jacob blessed Pharaoh and went out from his presence.11So Joseph settled his father and his brothers in Egypt and gave them property in the best part of the land, the district of Rameses, as Pharaoh directed.12Joseph also provided his father and his brothers and all his father's household with food, according to the number of their children.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

Jacob's audience before Pharaoh is the most formal scene of the patriarchal narrative. An elderly Canaanite shepherd stands before the ruler of the ancient world and blesses him — twice. The blessing of the lesser by the greater is expected; but here the greater is blessed by the lesser, which the author of Hebrews will note (Heb 7:7). Jacob's answer to Pharaoh's question about his age — "my years have been few and difficult" — is the lament of a man who has known more grief than joy, spoken at the moment of his greatest earthly consolation.

Context

Jacob blessing Pharaoh (vv.7,10) is a remarkable reversal of power dynamics: the nomadic patriarch blesses the most powerful ruler on earth twice. The text twice notes it (vv.7,10), underscoring its significance. This is the Abrahamic promise in action — "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" includes the family of Pharaoh. Jacob's burial request (vv.29–31) is the chapter's final theological statement: Egypt is not home; Canaan is. The covenant land must receive the patriarch's bones.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
47:7

The double blessing of Pharaoh by Jacob is highlighted by Sarna as the fulfilment of the Abrahamic covenant's promise that through Abraham's descendants all peoples would be blessed (12:3). Sarna: "The patriarch who has been the passive recipient of God's covenant promises now becomes the active agent of blessing to the nations. The covenant is not merely received — it is transmitted, even to the most powerful rulers of the earth."

47:9

Sarna on Jacob's self-description: "'Few and difficult' — the Hebrew me'at vera'im captures Jacob's honest self-assessment. The 'few' is comparative: his life has been shorter than his forebears. The 'difficult' is experiential: the content of his years has been harder. Jacob is not complaining before Pharaoh; he is answering a direct question with characteristic honesty. The same man who was capable of deception is also capable of this kind of painful candour."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
47:9

Jacob's answer to Pharaoh is one of the most memorable speeches in Genesis. Alter: "The response has the quality of a lament spoken at the wrong moment — a moment of maximal earthly consolation (Joseph found, family in Egypt, Pharaoh's favour). Yet Jacob names his life 'few and difficult.' This is not ingratitude but accuracy: Jacob's life has been defined by grief, deception, and loss. The consolation of the final years does not erase the pain of the preceding ones."

47:7,10

The double blessing frames the encounter. Alter notes the structural symmetry: Jacob blesses Pharaoh when he enters (v.7) and again when he departs (v.10). The encounter is bracketed by blessing. The patriarch's covenant function — to be a blessing — is enacted in the most public and politically significant arena of his life.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
47:9

Calvin on Jacob's self-description: "Jacob does not say that his life has been wretched — only that it has been difficult. He knows that God has been faithful; he also knows that the content of his years has been marked by suffering. Both things are true. The man of faith does not pretend that suffering does not exist; he carries it honestly while still holding to the covenant."

47:7

Calvin on the blessing of Pharaoh: "Jacob, though he was a stranger and inferior in earthly standing, blessed Pharaoh — not as a mere social courtesy but as the covenant patriarch who carried the blessing of God for the nations. The greater, in terms of earthly power, received blessing from the lesser in earthly terms but the greater in terms of covenant standing."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
47:9

The NET note on "few and difficult" discusses the Hebrew me'at (few, small) and ra'im (difficult, evil, hard). The note observes that Jacob is 130 — not an especially short life — yet he calls it "few" in comparison to his fathers (Abraham: 175; Isaac: 180). The note: "Jacob's statement is not false modesty; it is a reflection of his experiential sense of his life's quality, not merely its length."

47:7

The NET note on Jacob blessing Pharaoh cross-references Hebrews 7:7 ("without doubt the lesser is blessed by the greater") and observes that the author of Hebrews uses this scene to illustrate the superiority of the Melchizedekian priesthood. The covenant blessing carried by Jacob is, in this encounter, treated as superior to the political power carried by Pharaoh.

Verses 13–26 — Joseph's Famine Administration
13There was no food, however, in the whole region because the famine was severe; both Egypt and Canaan wasted away because of the famine.14Joseph collected all the money that was to be found in Egypt and Canaan in payment for the grain they were buying, and he brought it to Pharaoh's palace.15When the money of the people of Egypt and Canaan was gone, all Egypt came to Joseph and said, "Give us food. Why should we die before your eyes? Our money is all gone."16"Then bring your livestock," said Joseph. "I will sell you food in exchange for your livestock, since your money is gone."17So they brought their livestock to Joseph, and he gave them food in exchange for their horses, their sheep and goats, their cattle and donkeys. And he brought them through that year with food in exchange for all their livestock.18When that year was over, they came to him the following year and said, "We cannot hide from our lord the fact that since our money is gone and our livestock belongs to you, there is nothing left for our lord except our bodies and our land.19Why should we perish before your eyes—we and our land as well? Buy us and our land in exchange for food, and we with our land will be in bondage to Pharaoh. Give us seed so that we may live and not die, and that the land may not become desolate."20So Joseph bought all the land in Egypt for Pharaoh. The Egyptians, one and all, sold their fields, because the famine was too severe for them. The land became Pharaoh's,21and Joseph reduced the people to servitude, from one end of Egypt to the other.22However, he did not buy the land of the priests, because they received a regular allotment from Pharaoh and had food enough from the allotment Pharaoh gave them. That is why they did not sell their land.23Joseph said to the people, "Now that I have bought you and your land today for Pharaoh, here is seed for you so you can plant the ground.24But when the crop comes in, give a fifth of it to Pharaoh. The other four-fifths you may keep as seed for the fields and as food for yourselves and your households and your children."25"You have saved our lives," they said. "May we find favor in the eyes of our lord; we will be in bondage to Pharaoh."26So Joseph established it as a law concerning land in Egypt—still in force today—that a fifth of the produce belongs to Pharaoh. It was only the land of the priests that did not become Pharaoh's.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

Joseph's famine administration is the most economically detailed narrative in Genesis. Over the course of the seven-year famine, Joseph purchases all of Egypt's money, livestock, land, and ultimately the persons of the Egyptians for Pharaoh — creating a feudal system in which the crown owns all the land, the people are Pharaoh's tenants, and one-fifth of all production is paid as tax. Only the temple lands (priestly estates) are exempt. The policy has been debated for centuries: is it exploitation or salvation?

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
47:20-21

The acquisition of Egypt's land and people by Pharaoh through Joseph's administration is historically attested. Sarna: "Egyptian records from the Second Intermediate Period and New Kingdom confirm that a significant proportion of Egyptian land was owned by the crown and the temples, with the population working as tenant farmers. The biblical narrative's description of Joseph's policy corresponds to the land-tenure system that characterised ancient Egypt."

47:22

The exemption of the priestly estates from Joseph's land purchase is a significant detail. Sarna: "The priests received regular rations from Pharaoh and did not need to sell their land. The exemption confirms Joseph's political sophistication: he did not antagonise the priestly establishment, which was the most powerful non-royal institution in Egypt."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
47:13-26

The economic transformation sequence — money, then livestock, then land, then persons — has a relentless economic logic. Alter: "Each stage follows from the previous with the logic of a famine in which survival trumps ownership. The people who accepted the transaction understood what they were doing: 'you have saved our lives' (v.25). Joseph is not exploiting the vulnerable — he is providing survival at the cost of permanent economic restructuring."

47:26

The statute "one-fifth to Pharaoh" as "a law in Egypt to this day" is the narrative's acknowledgment that Joseph's policy had lasting historical consequences. Alter: "The narrator is recording a real historical institution and attributing its origin to Joseph. The famine administration is not merely a story detail but a claim about the origins of Egyptian land tenure."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
47:20-23

Calvin on the land purchase: "Joseph conducted himself wisely and uprightly in this business. He did not enrich Pharaoh by rapine or extortion; the people themselves freely offered their land for food. He made a fair exchange. The system he created was a feudal arrangement that the people accepted voluntarily as the price of their lives."

47:26

Calvin on the lasting statute: "This shows that Joseph acted with political wisdom — he established the arrangement as a law, not merely an emergency measure. The institutionalisation of the tithing arrangement gave it permanence and prevented future arbitrariness. Joseph thought beyond the immediate crisis to the long-term ordering of Egyptian society."

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
47:21

The NET note on "he reduced the people to servitude" discusses the two main textual traditions: the MT has "he moved them to cities" (he'evir otam la'arim); the LXX and Samaritan Pentateuch have "he made them slaves" (la'avadim). The note: "The LXX reading corresponds better to the context (people offering themselves as slaves, v.19) and may reflect the original text."

47:24

The NET note on the twenty percent tax (one-fifth) observes this is consistent with ancient Near Eastern tithing rates for royal estates. The note: "A twenty-percent agricultural tax — to be paid from every harvest — was within the range of ancient land-tenure systems, neither unusually exploitative nor unusually generous. The people's response ('you have saved our lives') suggests they regarded it as reasonable."

Verses 27–31 — Israel Prospers; Jacob's Final Request
27Now the Israelites settled in Egypt in the region of Goshen. They acquired property there and were fruitful and increased greatly in number.28Jacob lived in Egypt seventeen years, and the years of his life were a hundred and forty-seven.29When the time drew near for Israel to die, he called for his son Joseph and said to him, "If I have found favor in your eyes, put your hand under my thigh and promise that you will show me kindness and faithfulness. Do not bury me in Egypt,30but when I rest with my fathers, carry me out of Egypt and bury me where they are buried." "I will do as you say," he said.31"Swear to me," he said. Then Joseph swore to him, and Israel worshiped as he leaned on the top of his staff.

Hebrew Word Study

Historical Context

Israel prospers in Goshen while Jacob approaches death. Jacob's request that Joseph not bury him in Egypt but carry him back to Canaan — the oath sworn with hand under the thigh — is both a personal wish and a theological statement: Jacob belongs to the land of promise, not to Egypt. The oath is the most solemn form available. "Do not bury me in Egypt" is the dying patriarch's confession that Egypt is not home, however prosperous.

Context

Jacob blessing Pharaoh (vv.7,10) is a remarkable reversal of power dynamics: the nomadic patriarch blesses the most powerful ruler on earth twice. Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
47:27

The flourishing of Israel in Goshen — "they acquired property, were fruitful, and increased greatly in number" — uses three verbs that echo the creation mandate (1:28) and the Abrahamic promise. Sarna: "The covenant promises are being fulfilled in Egypt, not in Canaan. The geographical displacement that seemed to threaten the covenant is in fact the mechanism of its fulfilment. The great nation promised to Abraham is growing in Egypt."

47:31

Jacob worshipping at the head of his bed after receiving Joseph's oath is one of the last gestures of the patriarch. Sarna: "The posture of worship — bowing at the head of the bed — is an act of thanksgiving for the oath received. Jacob, who has been led by God through deception, grief, exile, and reunion, ends with an act of worship. The man who wrestled with God at Peniel now bows in gratitude."

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
47:29-31

The deathbed oath is the patriarchal tradition's formal mechanism for binding the next generation to the covenant geography. Alter: "Jacob does not merely ask to be buried in Canaan — he requires an oath sworn by the most solemn available means. He knows Joseph is an Egyptian official with an Egyptian life. The oath is necessary to ensure that the commitment outlasts the convenience of the moment."

47:29-31

Jacob's requirement to be buried in Canaan claims the promised land by the act of burial — the same way Abraham claimed it in ch.23. Alter notes both acts treat the promise as already real: Canaan is where the covenant people belong, even when history temporarily takes them elsewhere.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
47:31

Calvin on Jacob's worship after the oath: "He worshipped at the head of his bed — the holy man, already in the weakness of approaching death, gathered his strength for an act of gratitude. He had waited for this assurance his whole life. Now it is given: he will rest with his fathers. His faith triumphs over his weakness."

47:31

Calvin: Jacob sealed the oath with worship, giving thanks that he had been preserved so long and would now at last rest in the promised land. The posture of worship at the deathbed is the ultimate testimony of faith.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
47:31

The NET note on "Israel bowed down at the head of his bed" discusses the LXX's translation "he worshipped, leaning on the top of his staff" (rabb'a as staff rather than mittah as bed). Hebrews 11:21 follows the LXX. The note: "The Hebrew mateh can mean either bed or staff — the difference depends on the vowel pointing, which was added later. Both traditions have good support. The theological point — Jacob's act of worship — is not affected."

47:29

The NET note on the "hand under the thigh" oath explains it as the most solemn form of personal oath in ancient Israelite tradition — used in Genesis only for commitments of ultimate importance (24:2; 47:29). The note suggests the thigh represents the seat of procreation and family continuity; the oath binds the swearer to the sworn obligation as a covenant of family identity.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Jacob
Blesses Pharaoh; makes burial request
The patriarch's two great acts in this chapter: blessing the most powerful man on earth, and insisting on burial in Canaan — covenant identity maintained to the end.
Joseph
Administrator of Egypt
His grain policy (vv.13–26) saves Egypt but raises ethical questions about the concentration of power. His management is effective; its long-term consequences are complex.
Pharaoh
Receiver of blessing
Blessed by the Semitic patriarch twice — the Abrahamic promise operating at the highest levels of human power.

Ancient Sources

Egyptian Famine Administration
Administrative papyri document the centralisation of grain distribution in times of famine. Joseph's policy (vv.13–26) — acquiring land for Pharaoh in exchange for grain — is consistent with known Egyptian administrative practice.
The economic details of ch.47 are culturally plausible.
Burial Customs
Repatriation of bones to ancestral land is attested in the ancient Near East. The patriarchal insistence on Canaan burial reflects covenant-territorial identity.
Jacob's burial request is culturally intelligible within ANE practice.

Reception History

Hebrews 11:13
The epistle uses the patriarchal "sojourning" language to define Christian identity: "strangers and exiles on the earth." Jacob's self-description before Pharaoh becomes the NT paradigm for all covenant people.
Calvin, Commentary
Jacob blessing Pharaoh twice is for Calvin the demonstration that covenant blessing flows outward — the weakest (in human terms) blesses the strongest, because the covenant blessing belongs to the one who carries it, not to the one with the most power.

Scholarly Debates

Joseph's Famine Policy: Economic Wisdom or Oppression?
Oppression / exploitation
Some modern readings
Joseph systematically strips the Egyptian population of money, livestock, land, and personal freedom in exchange for food during a crisis. This is a forced transfer of wealth from the people to the crown, exploiting the famine.
Salvation and reasonable reorganisation
Sarna, Kitchen, Wenham
The people voluntarily make the exchanges; they explicitly say "you have saved our lives" (v.25) and accept the 20% tax as fair. The policy created a stable agricultural economy that outlasted the crisis. No one died of famine in Egypt under Joseph's administration.
The narrative's own verdict — "you have saved our lives" — should be taken seriously. The policy was not imposed by force; it was accepted by a population facing starvation. The 20% agricultural tax is within the range of ancient Near Eastern land-tenure arrangements. Joseph's policy is best understood as emergency crisis management that became a permanent administrative framework — economically pragmatic, not exploitative in intent.
