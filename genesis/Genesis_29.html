Genesis 29

Jacob at the Well; Two Wives; Leah's Sons

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Hebrew Word Study

וַיִּשַּׁק wayyishaq — kissed: V.13: Laban's kiss and embrace upon hearing Jacob is his nephew echoes the family reunion of 24:29-32 when Laban ran to Abraham's servant. The same generous hospitality appears — but the subsequent narrative will reveal Laban's generosity to be calculated self-interest. The physical warmth of the reception masks the relational complexity ahead.

גַּל gal — heap / stone pile: V.3: The stone covering the well mouth required collective effort to move — reinforcing that the well was a communal resource requiring cooperation. Jacob rolling it away alone (v.10) for Rachel demonstrates his exceptional strength, his impassioned response to her beauty, and the first hint that something exceptional has begun.

Historical Context

The well-meeting is a biblical type-scene: a man travels to a foreign land, meets a woman at a well, she runs to tell her family, a betrothal follows (cf. Isaac and Rebekah, ch.24; Moses and Zipporah, Exod 2:15-21). The type-scene signals to attentive readers what is coming. But each instance subverts or complicates the pattern; Genesis 29 will produce two marriages, not one, and the expected resolution will be twisted by Laban's deception.

Context

The well-meeting scene is a literary type-scene — a recognized genre in the ancient world where a future spouse or significant figure is encountered at a well, water is drawn, and a journey to the family follows. Abraham's servant met Rebekah at a well (ch.24); Moses will meet Zipporah at a well (Exod 2:15–21). The form signals to the ancient reader: this is a significant encounter with providential dimensions. Jacob rolling the large stone alone (usually requiring multiple shepherds, v.8) is an act of love-fueled strength.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 24:10–27Rebekah at the well — the preceding type-scene that Genesis 29 echoes. The pattern: journey → well → watering → running to family → hospitality.
  • Exod 2:15–21Moses meets Zipporah at a well in Midian — the pattern continues in the next generation of covenant history.
  • John 4:1–26Jesus at the well with the Samaritan woman — the NT fulfillment of the well-meeting type-scene, now carrying full theological weight about living water and true worship.

Places

Paddan Aram / Laban's household
Upper Mesopotamia; Harran region
Jacob arrives at a well in "the land of the people of the east" — Paddan Aram. The well where he meets Rachel mirrors the well where Abraham's servant met Rebekah. Both Rebekah and Rachel are first encountered at wells outside the family's Mesopotamian home city.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
29:1

The opening — Jacob "lifted his feet" and journeyed — is an idiom for eager, purposeful movement. After the gravity of Bethel (ch.28), Jacob moves forward with renewed energy. The direction is eastward — into the region of Haran, the land of Nahor, the ancestral homeland. The journey is both flight from Esau and pilgrimage toward the promised wife.

29:6-10

When Jacob sees Rachel, he immediately acts. He rolls the stone off the well single-handed — a feat requiring exceptional strength. The action is the ancient equivalent of a grand gesture: observable, impressive, done specifically in her presence. The narrator slows the pace: Jacob kisses Rachel, weeps, and then identifies himself. The emotional response is immediate and powerful.

29:13-14

Laban's declaration — "you are truly my bone and flesh" — echoes Adam's words about Eve (2:23). Family recognition uses the same language as the first human bond. But the irony is sharp: the man who will exploit Jacob for 20 years begins with the words of the deepest familial solidarity. Laban is adept at the language of relationship; his actions will consistently betray it.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:1

The well scene condenses several significant elements: the presence of multiple shepherds (vv.2-3), the stone covering, the waiting for all flocks before uncovering — all establish the well as a communal institution. Sarna notes this signals both the importance of the well as social meeting-point in the ancient world and the significance of Jacob's solo feat in v.10. The public nature of the well means his action before Rachel is a public declaration.

29:9-10

Rachel is introduced as a shepherdess — unusual in the ancient world where herding was primarily male work. Sarna notes this may indicate a family without grown sons available (Laban's sons appear in 31:1 but not here), or Rachel's exceptional capability. Her role as shepherdess echoes Moses (Exod 3:1) and David (1 Sam 16:11), associating her with other significant figures whose early life involved tending flocks.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:1-3

The opening of Genesis 29 deploys the well type-scene with deliberate formulaic quality. Alter identifies the standard elements: journey to foreign land, female water-drawer, identification of family connection, invitation to the family home. But the scene is immediately complicated: Jacob does not meet one woman but arrives at a well surrounded by men who are waiting for collective action. The normal dynamic is disrupted before it can begin.

29:11-12

Wayyishaq Yaakov leRachel — "Jacob kissed Rachel." The spontaneity of the action — he weeps as he kisses her — contrasts with the careful, mediated courtship of Isaac and Rebekah. No servant as intermediary, no negotiated terms, no gifts. Jacob's arrival is direct, physical, emotional. Alter notes the unusual sequence: action (kiss) before identification (v.12). The feeling precedes the information.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
29:1-3

Calvin reads Jacob's journey as divinely guided: "Jacob went forward as if God had given wings to his feet." The Providence that has been promised at Bethel is already working: the first people he encounters are from Haran, the specific region he seeks, and within moments he encounters his kinswoman Rachel. The guidance is not spectacular but sequentially perfect — each encounter leads naturally to the next.

29:13-14

Laban's welcome is lavish; Calvin is cautious about it. "Laban, though a wicked and perfidious man, was constrained by a sense of common humanity to embrace his nephew with signs of kindness." The hospitality of the ancient world created obligations that even the unscrupulous honoured in public while violating in private. Jacob will discover this over twenty years of service.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
29:2-3

The NET note on the stone explains it was a large stone used to prevent sand contamination and unauthorised access. The fact that all shepherds waited for each other before removing it indicates a communal water-rights arrangement — no single flock could be favoured. Jacob's act of removing it alone overrides the communal protocol — a statement of strength that also claims priority for Rachel's flock.

29:14

The NET note on "bone and flesh" (etsem uvasar) explains this is an idiom for close kinship, appearing in Judges 9:2 and 2 Samuel 5:1 in covenantal contexts. The phrase acknowledges a blood relationship that creates mutual obligation. The irony is that Laban will use this language of covenantal kinship as cover for serial exploitation.

Hebrew Word Study

עֵינַיִם רַכּוֹת einayim rakkot — weak/tender eyes: V.17: Leah's "weak eyes" vs. Rachel's beauty and figure is one of the Bible's most compressed character contrasts. Rakkot means soft, tender, delicate — not necessarily a defect but a lack of the striking, vivid quality Rachel possesses. Some ancient interpreters read the phrase as praise (gentle eyes are beautiful); most read it as comparative disadvantage. Either way, the text stages a competition Jacob has already resolved.

רַמָּה rammah — deceived: V.25: The narrative irony of Laban's substitution of Leah for Rachel is multiple. The same root (r-m-h) was used for Jacob's deception of Isaac (27:35). The deceiver is deceived, and by his own uncle, using his own techniques: darkness, disguise, and the exploitation of expectation. The verb appears twice — "he deceived me" (v.25) — and Jacob cannot miss the echo.

Context

Jacob's seven years of labor "seemed but a few days because of the love he had for her" — the most romantic line in the OT. It also sets up the chapter's devastating irony: those seven years will be doubled. The man who deceived his father about the identity of his son will be deceived about the identity of his bride. Laban is Jacob's mirror, and the reflection is uncomfortable.

Cross-Reference

  • Gal 6:7Do not be deceived: God cannot be mocked. A man reaps what he sows — Paul articulates the principle enacted in Laban's deception of Jacob.
  • Hos 12:12Jacob fled to the land of Aram; Israel served to get a wife — Hosea recalls Jacob's Haran servitude as a figure of Israel's humiliation.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
29:18

Jacob's willingness to serve seven years for Rachel is one of the most celebrated love statements in the ancient world. The seven years "seemed like only a few days to him because of his love for her" — desire transforms duration, subjective experience overcomes objective time. This is not merely romantic but theological: the quality of one's devotion determines the weight of one's sacrifice.

29:22-25

Laban substitutes Leah on the wedding night — a deception possible only under the conditions of an ancient veiled wedding ceremony, with a feast and wine preceding the bridal chamber. The morning revelation — "there was Leah!" — is the chapter's dramatic punchline. Jacob, who used a disguise to deceive his blind father, is now deceived by darkness and disguise. The precise mechanism of poetic justice is striking.

29:26-27

Laban's explanation — "it is not our custom to give the younger daughter before the older" — is breathtaking in its irony. The custom he invokes against Jacob is the exact birthright principle Jacob violated against Esau. The firstborn/younger dynamic that Jacob had exploited now disciplines him. God's justice works through human cunning.

29:30

Jacob's love for Rachel over Leah will drive the family dynamics for the next several chapters. The narrator does not morally condemn Jacob's preference; he notes it as a fact and then immediately shows God compensating Leah. The differential love in Jacob's household mirrors the differential blessing between Jacob and Esau — the younger is preferred, the older is compensated by God himself.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:17

Sarna discusses the description of Rachel as "beautiful in form and appearance" (yefat to'ar viyfat mareh) — the identical phrase used for Joseph in 39:6. The physical beauty that Rachel and Joseph share is not merely aesthetics but narrative significance: both will be at the centre of stories of desire, competition, and divine overruling of human plans. Beauty in Genesis creates vulnerability as well as favour.

29:25

The deception scene is constructed with maximum irony. Sarna notes that Jacob's accusation — "why have you deceived me?" (v.25) — uses the same verb (r-m-h) used for his own deception of Isaac (27:35). The reader who remembers the earlier chapter hears this echo immediately. The narrative does not need to comment explicitly; the vocabulary does the theological work.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:17

The literary comparison of the two sisters — Leah with her "tender eyes," Rachel "beautiful in form and appearance" — is built into the structure of the scene. Alter notes the technique: the narrator presents the objective facts without editorial comment, allowing the contrast to work on the reader emotionally. We feel Jacob's preference before it is stated, because the narrative has already staged it visually.

29:25

Wayyehi baboqer vehineh hi Leah — "and it was in the morning, and behold, she was Leah!" The single word hineh (behold!) captures the shock of the moment. Alter notes the scene has a dreamlike quality: the darkness of the wedding night, the veiled bride, the wine of the feast, and then morning revelation. Jacob's night of joy becomes a morning of realisation — a structural echo of his dream at Bethel (28:16-17), where morning also brought a new situation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
29:18-20

Calvin on Jacob's love for Rachel: "The seven years passed away pleasantly to Jacob because of his hope. In this there is nothing to be reprehended, provided the moderation of a holy affection be maintained." Calvin does not idealise romantic love but neither does he dismiss it. The extended service of seven years demonstrates the depth of commitment that love properly ordered will sustain.

29:26

Laban's appeal to custom — "it is not done in our place to give the younger before the firstborn" — provokes Calvin to observe: "The cunning man had concealed this custom until he had gained his point." The custom may have been real; Laban's selective invocation of it at the strategic moment is what marks him as duplicitous. He uses the language of tradition and propriety to cover exploitation.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
29:17

The NET note on einayim rakkot ("weak/tender eyes") surveys the interpretive options: (1) poor eyesight as a physical defect; (2) soft, gentle eyes as a different but not inferior beauty; (3) a euphemism for a less attractive appearance generally. The note favours understanding the phrase as a comparative diminishment — Leah was pretty but not as striking as Rachel. The contrast sets up the tragic competition of the following chapters.

29:27

The NET note on "complete the week" (malle shebu'at zot) explains this refers to the full seven-day wedding feast, not another seven years of work first. Jacob would complete Leah's bridal week, then receive Rachel and then serve another seven years. The arrangement is not generous — Jacob receives Rachel almost immediately — but the debt is real: fourteen years of labour for two wives.

Verses 31–35 — Leah Bears Sons
31When the Lord saw that Leah was not loved, he enabled her to conceive, but Rachel remained childless.32Leah became pregnant and gave birth to a son. She named him Reuben, for she said, "It is because the Lord has seen my misery. Surely my husband will love me now."33She conceived again, and when she gave birth to a son she said, "Because the Lord heard that I am not loved, he gave me this one too." So she named him Simeon.34Again she conceived, and when she gave birth to a son she said, "Now at last my husband will become attached to me, because I have borne him three sons." So he was named Levi.35She conceived again, and when she gave birth to a son she said, "This time I will praise the Lord." So she named him Judah. Then she stopped having children.

Hebrew Word Study

שָׂנוּאָה senuah — hated / unloved: V.31: The Hebrew senuah (hated) is a comparative term meaning "less loved" — the contrast with Jacob's love for Rachel makes Leah the one set aside rather than actively hated. This is the normal sense in contexts of comparative affection (cf. Deut 21:15-17, "two wives, one loved and one unloved"). But the word carries real pain: to be the unchosen, the one passed over, is a real form of rejection.

רָאָה raah — saw / looked upon: V.31: "The LORD saw that Leah was hated/unloved" — the divine sight that precedes divine action. God sees what Jacob does not see: the full weight of Leah's situation. The same divine sight will be extended to Hagar (16:13, "You are the God who sees me"), to the Israelites in Egypt (Exod 3:7), and to the afflicted throughout the OT. God's sight is the theological engine of his rescue.

Historical Context

The naming of each son encodes Leah's emotional state at the time of his birth — a running commentary on her experience of the marriage. Reuben ("See, a son!" or "See my affliction"), Simeon ("He heard"), Levi ("He will be attached"), Judah ("I will praise the LORD"). Each name documents both a moment of hope (God noticed, God heard) and a desire (now Jacob will love me). The fourth son breaks the pattern: with Judah the plea for Jacob's love is replaced by praise of God. Something shifts.

Context

Laban's deception is a masterpiece of poetic justice. Jacob deceived his blind father by covering himself with his older brother's identity; Laban deceives Jacob in the darkness of the wedding night by substituting the older sister for the younger. The mechanism is almost identical. Laban's justification — "it is not so done in our country, to give the younger before the firstborn" — is a knife in Jacob's ribs: he who violated the firstborn's right now has it quoted at him. He will never be free of this irony.

Cross-Reference

  • Gen 27:1–4Isaac's dim eyes could not distinguish Jacob from Esau in daylight; Jacob cannot distinguish Leah from Rachel in the darkness. The structural parallel is deliberate — Genesis is a book of mirrors.
  • Gal 6:7"Whatever a man sows, that he will also reap" — the Jacob/Laban deception cycle is the OT's most extended illustration of this principle.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
29:31-32

The divine opening of Leah's womb is the first of many such acts in Genesis (Sarah, 21:1-2; Rebekah, 25:21; Rachel, 30:22). The pattern is consistent: the barren or unloved woman receives divine attention that compensates for human inattention. God sees the competition, the rejection, the pain — and intervenes on behalf of the one society and family have marginalised.

29:33-34

Simeon and Levi are named with decreasing hope for Jacob's love but increasing confidence in God's hearing. "Because the LORD heard that I am not loved, he gave me this one too." The theology of Leah's naming is significant: she is not praying directly (no recorded prayer) but God is responding to her situation without a formal petition. Divine attentiveness does not require perfect expression of need.

29:35

With Judah the naming achieves something new: "This time I will praise the LORD." The maternal theology of the names has moved from petition (see my affliction, he heard) to praise. Leah stops asking for Jacob's recognition and turns to the LORD's. From Judah will come David, the Psalms, and ultimately Jesus — the praise of YHWH made incarnate.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:31

Vatiftach et rechmah — "and he opened her womb." The verb pathach (to open) is used for the opening of the womb as a divine act, contrasted with the act of "closing" (atsur, as in 16:2 for Sarah). Sarna notes the physiological metaphor reflects the ancient understanding of fertility as a gateway under divine control. The opening of Leah's womb is a theological statement: God is compensating the unloved with the most tangible form of blessing in the ancient world.

29:35

The fourth name — Judah (yehudah), "I will praise (odeh) YHWH" — is the only name in the sequence that does not include a reference to Leah's relationship with Jacob. Sarna: "Leah's horizon has shifted from the horizontal (Jacob's affection) to the vertical (YHWH's blessing)." The fourth son becomes the ancestor of David and of the messianic line — the praise of God embodied in a name becomes the lineage of the one who fulfils all praise.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
29:32-34

The etiology of the names is a running interior monologue by Leah. Alter notes the literary technique: the naming speeches give us direct access to Leah's emotional experience that the narrative otherwise does not supply. She is the least described character physically, the least beloved relationally — but the naming speeches make her the most psychologically present character in these chapters. Her pain, hope, and gradual reorientation are tracked with precision.

29:35

The shift at Judah's naming is structurally significant. The previous three names were explanatory (because of / now he will); Judah's name is doxological (I will praise). Alter: Leah's movement from lament to praise is not resolution — Jacob still prefers Rachel — but it is a spiritual turning. The unloved wife who learns to praise God despite her circumstances is the unlikely matrix of Israel's royal and messianic line.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
29:31-32

That God opened Leah's womb to compensate for Jacob's indifference is Calvin's text for the doctrine of divine providence attending to the marginalised: "God, who is the defender of the afflicted, determined to avenge the injury done to Leah." The language is stronger than we might expect: God is not merely consoling Leah but acting against the injustice of her situation. Providence is not neutral; it responds to human pain.

29:35

On the name Judah: Calvin sees a spiritual progression in Leah that the narrative records without editorialising: "Leah, having been a long time disquieted, and having passed through many conflicts, at length puts a bridle upon her unruly passions, and begins to celebrate with praise the grace of God." The movement from petition to praise is a model of mature faith: not the absence of difficulty but the redirecting of attention from the source of pain to the source of blessing.

Chapter-Level Scholarship

People of the Chapter

Rachel
Beloved wife; barren until ch.30
The object of Jacob's seven-year labor and fourteen-year service. Beautiful, loved, but barren — the pattern of the significant woman in Genesis (Sarah, Rebekah, now Rachel). Her barrenness will become the occasion for the servant-wife pregnancies of ch.30.
Leah
Unloved wife; fruitful; Judah's mother
The chapter's most theologically significant character despite her subordinate social position. Her name theology traces a spiritual journey from pleading for human love to pure divine praise. Her son Judah carries the Messianic line.
Laban
Jacob's uncle; master deceiver
He will deceive Jacob repeatedly over twenty years (wages changed ten times, 31:7). His deception of the wedding night is poetic justice for Jacob's deception of Isaac — and his cultural rationale ("it is not done to give the younger before the firstborn") is Genesis's sharpest irony.

Ancient Sources

ANE Well-Meeting Type-Scene (Robert Alter)
Literary scholar Robert Alter identified the "well-meeting" as a recognized type-scene in biblical narrative — a conventional literary form where future spouses (or significant figures) meet at a well, water is provided, and the scene ends with the stranger being taken home. Its conventional elements signal significance to the ancient reader. Alter's analysis in The Art of Biblical Narrative (1981) transformed biblical literary criticism.

Reception History

The Judah Trajectory
The rabbis noted that Leah's four sons provide the Messianic line (Judah → David → Jesus) while Rachel's most famous son (Joseph) provides the savior-type. Both women's sons are essential to the covenant story — but Leah's despised child carries the royal line. Christian commentators see in this another instance of divine preference for the overlooked.
Luther on Jacob's Tears
Luther found Jacob's weeping at the sight of Rachel (v.11) authentically moving — a rare moment of transparent emotion from the calculating patriarch. He used it to argue that genuine love has nothing in common with calculation, and that Jacob's love for Rachel was one of the OT's clearest pictures of covenant love.

And in the midst of the marital rivalry, the unloved wife's naming speeches trace a spiritual journey from grief to praise that will generate the lineage of David and of Christ.

Textual Notes

29:17עֵינֵי לֵאָה רַכּוֹת — "Leah's eyes were weak/tender"
MT einei Leah rakkot — "Leah's eyes were tender/delicate." Rakkot can mean weak, gentle, or simply soft — not necessarily a defect. LXX ἀσθενεῖς — "weak." Most translations: "weak eyes" (contrasted with Rachel's beauty).
The contrast is deliberate narrative irony: Leah's eyes are "weak" but her womb is strong; Rachel's appearance is beautiful but her womb is closed. The reversal of expected fruitfulness structures the entire Leah-Rachel rivalry in ch.29-30.

Scholarly Debates

Jacob's Marriages: Polygamy and the OT Narrative
Descriptive, not prescriptive
Wenham, Hamilton, most evangelicals
The OT records polygamy without endorsing it. The narrative consequences — rivalry, jealousy, suffering — constitute an implicit moral judgment. Genesis 2:24 ("one flesh") establishes the normative pattern.
Regulated permission
Some OT scholars
Mosaic law regulates but does not prohibit polygamy (Exod 21:10; Deut 21:15-17), suggesting it was a tolerated accommodation to fallen human conditions rather than an ideal.
The consensus evangelical position is that the OT records polygamy as a sociological reality while the creation ideal (2:24; Matt 19:4-6) establishes monogamy as the normative pattern. The narrative's consistent portrayal of polygamy's dysfunction (rivalry, favouritism, suffering) serves as an implicit argument against the practice.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 29 turns on the axis of divine providence compensating human injustice. The deceiver Jacob is deceived; the unloved Leah is given sons; the loved Rachel is given time to wait. Each injustice is met with a divine response that is neither simple reversal nor obvious vindication but the slow working of a purpose larger than any single character's desire.
