Genesis 36

The Descendants of Esau; the Chiefs and Kings of Edom

Author

Moses (Moshe ben Amram), c.1526–1406 BC. Born a Hebrew slave in Egypt, adopted into Pharaoh's household, educated in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22). Fled to Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer; spent 40 years as a shepherd under Jethro. Called at the burning bush (Exod 3) to lead the Exodus at age 80. Spent 40 years leading Israel through the wilderness; died on Mount Nebo in sight of Canaan, age 120.

When written

c.1445–1405 BC during the wilderness period, following the Exodus from Egypt. Moses drew on earlier patriarchal records, oral tradition, and direct divine revelation. The Pentateuch as a whole shows strong literary unity and was treated as Mosaic by both the OT (Josh 8:31; 1 Kgs 2:3; Ezra 6:18) and the NT (Mark 12:26; Luke 24:27; John 5:46).

What prompted it

To record God's creation of the world and his covenant relationship with the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — culminating in Israel's formation as a people chosen to carry the promise of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). Genesis answers the foundational questions: Who is God? Who are we? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Verses 1–19 — Esau's Descendants; the Chiefs of Edom
1This is the account of the family line of Esau (that is, Edom).2Esau took his wives from the women of Canaan: Adah daughter of Elon the Hittite, and Oholibamah daughter of Anah and granddaughter of Zibeon the Hivite—3also Basemath daughter of Ishmael and sister of Nebaioth.4Adah bore Eliphaz to Esau, Basemath bore Reuel,5and Oholibamah bore Jeush, Jalam and Korah. These were the sons of Esau, who were born to him in Canaan.6Esau took his wives and sons and daughters and all the members of his household, as well as his livestock and all his other animals and all the goods he had acquired in Canaan, and moved to a land some distance from his brother Jacob.7Their possessions were too great for them to remain together; the land where they were staying could not support them both because of their livestock.8So Esau (that is, Edom) settled in the hill country of Seir.9This is the account of the family line of Esau the father of the Edomites in the hill country of Seir.10These are the names of Esau's sons: Eliphaz, the son of Esau's wife Adah, and Reuel, the son of Esau's wife Basemath.11The sons of Eliphaz: Teman, Omar, Zepho, Gatam and Kenaz.12Esau's son Eliphaz also had a concubine named Timna, who bore him Amalek. These were grandsons of Esau's wife Adah.13The sons of Reuel: Nahath, Zerah, Shammah and Mizzah. These were grandsons of Esau's wife Basemath.14The sons of Esau's wife Oholibamah daughter of Anah and granddaughter of Zibeon, whom she bore to Esau: Jeush, Jalam and Korah.15These were the chiefs among Esau's descendants: The sons of Eliphaz the firstborn of Esau: Chiefs Teman, Omar, Zepho, Kenaz,16Korah, Gatam and Amalek. These were the chiefs descended from Eliphaz in Edom; they were grandsons of Adah.17The sons of Esau's son Reuel: Chiefs Nahath, Zerah, Shammah and Mizzah. These were the chiefs descended from Reuel in Edom; they were grandsons of Esau's wife Basemath.18The sons of Esau's wife Oholibamah: Chiefs Jeush, Jalam and Korah. These were the chiefs descended from Esau's wife Oholibamah daughter of Anah.19These were the sons of Esau (that is, Edom), and these were their chiefs.

Hebrew Word Study

אֱדוֹם ʾĕdôm — Edom / Red: Related to ʾādôm (red) and the red stew of 25:30. Edom became the name of Esau's descendants and their territory east and south of the Dead Sea.

תּוֹלְדֹת tôlĕdōt — generations / account: The toledot formula organizes all of Genesis. Ch.36's toledot of Esau is the rejected line's account — necessary to show God's sovereignty operates even outside the chosen line.

Historical Context

The Edomite genealogy of Genesis 36 is the most extensive non-Israelite genealogy in the Torah, demonstrating that God's promise to Abraham included Ishmael and Esau as well as Isaac and Jacob. Esau's settlement of Seir (the region of modern southern Jordan/Negev highlands) fulfils the oracle of 25:23 ("the older will serve the younger") — not through Esau's subjugation but through his separation from the covenant land. Edom and Israel will be competing nations throughout the OT.

Context

The toledot of Esau stands between the end of the Jacob narrative (ch.35) and the beginning of the Joseph narrative (ch.37). It functions as a bracket — Esau's line is established and then set aside, exactly as Ishmael's line was established (ch.25) before returning to Isaac. The pattern: rejected line receives a complete genealogy then exits the main story, while the chosen line carries the covenant forward. Esau's departure from Canaan echoes Lot's separation from Abraham (ch.13) — both involve too many possessions to coexist, both result in the chosen man remaining in the land of promise.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
36:1-2

The toledoth of Esau opens with the same structural formula as the toledoth of Jacob (37:2). The narrative gives Esau's genealogy full attention before transitioning to the Joseph story — honouring the breadth of the Abrahamic promise. God told Abraham "I will make you into a great nation," and the promise reached both lines. Esau's descendants become a great nation (Edom) even as Jacob's become the covenant people.

36:6-8

Esau's departure from Canaan — "because of their livestock was too great for them to remain together" — echoes Abraham and Lot's separation in 13:5-12. In both cases, abundance forces separation; the one who departs allows the covenant heir to have the land. Esau voluntarily cedes Canaan to Jacob, settling in the hill country of Seir. The departure is not exile; it is the providential clearing of the land.

36:19

The identification of Esau as Edom — "the father of the Edomites in the hill country of Seir" — anchors this genealogy to a historical people known throughout Israel's history. Edom will be the perpetual rival of Israel, blocked at the Exodus (Num 20:14-21), conquered by David (2 Sam 8:14), rebelling under Solomon and Ahab, and receiving extended prophetic judgment (Obad, Amos 1:11-12, Isa 34).

36:15-16

The chiefs (alluphim) of Esau are listed — these are clan leaders, not kings. The title alluph appears only here and in the subsequent king list (vv.31-43). The careful distinction between the pre-monarchic clan structure (chiefs) and the later monarchic structure (kings of Edom) reflects genuine historical knowledge of Edomite political development. The list preserves authentic tradition.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
36:1

The toledoth of Esau appears twice — vv.1 and v.9 — creating a double introduction. Sarna explains: the first toledoth (v.1) introduces Esau's personal descendants (vv.1-8); the second (v.9) introduces his descendants as the nation of Edom (vv.9-43). The double structure reflects the dual identity of the chapter: Esau as an individual, and Edom as a nation. The genealogy moves from person to people.

36:8

Vayeshev Esav behar Seir Esav hu Edom — "Esau settled in the hill country of Seir; Esau is Edom." Sarna notes the tautological identification: the narrative does not assume the reader knows that Esau = Edom. The explicit equation — the only such direct identification in Genesis — serves to anchor subsequent references to Edom in the Esau story. Every reference to Edom in the OT carries the genealogical memory of the brother who was sent away.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
36:1-5

The opening genealogy of Esau's Canaanite wives and their sons is notably different in tone from the preceding narrative chapters. Alter notes the shift from story to list: names accumulate without narrative development. The genealogical material is not designed to be read like narrative but to be consulted, referenced, preserved. The change of register signals the closing of the Jacob story and the archiving of the Esau material before the Joseph narrative begins.

36:9-14

The second genealogical list introduces further complexity: wives, sons, grandsons, and finally the categorisation into "chiefs." Alter notes the extraordinary density of names — over 70 in the chapter — which creates an impression of Edomite abundance. The genealogy is making a point: Esau's line is vast, vigorous, and politically organised. The "older will serve the younger" oracle has not produced Esau's erasure; it has produced his separation and his own great nation.

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
36:1

Calvin on the purpose of the Edomite genealogy: "Moses first disposes of the race of Esau, in order that, when he comes to speak of the holy race, he may pursue his account of it without interruption. For there was good reason why the history of the covenant people should be kept pure and unmixed." The genealogy is a clearing operation — it accounts for Esau completely so that the Joseph narrative can focus on the covenant line without distraction.

36:6-8

Calvin reads Esau's departure generously: "There is no doubt but that God directed the counsel of Esau, so that he voluntarily departed, and left the possession of the land to his brother Jacob." Providence works through Esau's practical decision. The man who was passed over for the covenant blessing is guided by the same God to clear the land for the covenant family — without knowing he is doing so.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
36:1

The NET note on the double toledoth formula explains the literary structure: the first introduction (v.1) is personal (these are Esau's descendants); the second (v.9) is national (these are the descendants of Esau, the father of the Edomites). The repetition is not accidental duplication but a literary seam marking two different registers of genealogical information.

36:8

The NET note on "Esau is Edom" (hu Edom) explains this is one of the Bible's explicit equations between a patriarch and a nation, parallel to "Jacob is Israel" (35:10). The equation carries theological weight: the rivalry of Jacob and Esau in the womb (25:22-23) is the prototype of the rivalry of Israel and Edom throughout the OT. The nations are named after brothers.

Historical Context

The list of Horite clans (vv.20-30) and the list of Edomite kings (vv.31-39) are among the most historically significant passages in Genesis for reconstructing the political history of ancient Edom. The king list — "before any Israelite king reigned" — is remarkable: it records a succession of kings in Edom before Israel had a king. The phrase anticipates the Israelite monarchy, suggesting the author is writing at or after the time of Saul. The kings are from different cities, suggesting Edom had an elective rather than a dynastic monarchy.

Context

The toledot of Esau stands between the end of the Jacob narrative (ch.35) and the beginning of the Joseph narrative (ch.37). It functions as a bracket — Esau's line is established and then set aside, exactly as Ishmael's line was established (ch.25) before returning to Isaac. The pattern: rejected line receives a complete genealogy then exits the main story, while the chosen line carries the covenant forward. Esau's departure from Canaan echoes Lot's separation from Abraham (ch.13) — both involve too many possessions to coexist, both result in the chosen man remaining in the land of promise.

MacArthur Study Notes

MacArthur Study Bible — Faithful Paraphrase
36:31

The notation "before any Israelite king reigned" is one of the most debated phrases in the Pentateuch. Its most natural reading implies a time when Israelite kings already existed (the author looks back). Some read it as anticipatory ("before an Israelite king would reign"). Either way, it situates the Edomite king list in historical time relative to Israel's political development.

36:32-39

The eight Edomite kings are not dynastic — each succeeds from a different city, with no stated genealogical connection to his predecessor. Edom apparently had an elective monarchy with rotating capital cities. The political sophistication of Edom before Israel is significant: the nation of the "older brother" Esau had kings before the "younger brother" Jacob's descendants. The oracle of 25:23 did not mean immediate Esau inferiority; it pointed to ultimate covenant priority for Jacob.

36:40-43

The final list of Esau's chiefs by their clans and regions is a geographical as well as genealogical record: Edomite territory is being mapped through the names of its leaders. "Each with its own property and place." Esau's people have taken root, established themselves, and organised their territory. The chapter closes the Esau account completely before the Joseph story begins.

Sarna — JPS Commentary

Nahum Sarna, JPS Torah Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
36:20-30

The Horite genealogy is embedded within the Edomite genealogy because the Horites were the original inhabitants of Seir before Esau's descendants displaced them (Deut 2:12). Sarna notes the historical layering: Esau's descendants did not move into empty land but took over a region already occupied by the Horites. The inclusion of the Horite genealogy preserves the memory of the pre-Edomite population. Biblical genealogy is also historical documentation.

36:31

The phrase "before any Israelite king reigned" is what modern critics call a "post-Mosaic gloss" — an explanatory note added by a later editor. Sarna takes a more conservative view: "Moses was aware of the divine promise that kings would come from the patriarchs (17:6; 35:11) and saw the Edomite monarchy as a foretaste of what would eventually happen in Israel." The phrase is anticipatory, not anachronistic.

Alter — Literary Reading

Robert Alter, The Hebrew Bible: A Translation with Commentary — Scholarly Paraphrase
36:31-39

The Edomite king list is one of the most unadorned passages in Genesis — pure record, no narrative, no character development, no theological comment. Alter notes the genre shift is itself the literary point: the Edomite material is being archived, not narrated. It is history as register rather than history as story. The contrast with the immediately following Joseph narrative — the most novelistic text in Genesis — is deliberate and stark.

36:40-43

The final verse — "these were the chiefs of Esau, each with its own property and place — that is, Edom" — is a closing formula that seals the Edomite archive. Alter: "The word Edom in the final position is the chapter's last word before the Joseph story begins. The nation named for the red stew, the older brother who despised his birthright, has been fully accounted for. The narrative can now turn to the covenant line without the Esau material still pending."

Calvin’s Commentary

John Calvin, Commentaries — Faithful Paraphrase
36:20-30

Calvin reads the Horite genealogy as testimony to the providential displacement of one people by another: "God permitted the Horites to be expelled from their native soil, that the children of Esau might be their successors. This was done without any right on their part; but God, the arbiter of nations, puts down the one and raises up the other as he pleases." The same sovereignty that gave Canaan to Jacob's descendants gave Seir to Esau's.

36:31

On the king list: "Moses, after having briefly enumerated the chiefs of Edom, now also describes their royal government; for it was a sign of singular honour that they had kings, whom at that time God had not yet granted to the chosen people... let us be content with the condition which the Lord assigns to us, not envying others for those gifts which he has denied to us." Calvin reads the Edomite monarchy as a reminder that God's timing for his own people is not to be compared with his provision for others.

NET Bible Notes

NET Bible Full Notes Edition — Biblical Studies Press
36:31

The NET note on "before any Israelite king reigned" is detailed: it surveys the critical view (Mosaic authorship impossible if this anticipates Saul/David) and the conservative responses (anticipatory parenthetical; Moses knew of the promise of kings in 17:6; 35:11; Deut 17:14-20). The note acknowledges the interpretive difficulty and presents both positions without forcing a conclusion. It observes that either reading is grammatically possible.

36:40-43

The NET note on the final chief list explains that these territorial divisions (by their clans and regions) suggest the Edomite chiefs functioned as territorial governors, each ruling a named district. The note connects this political structure to the later Edomite kings (who come from different cities) as consistent evidence of a non-dynastic, distributed political structure in Edom — different from the centralised monarchy that Israel will eventually develop.

Reception History

Calvin, Commentary
Calvin reads ch.36 as a demonstration of God's common grace — the rejected line flourishes materially while the chosen line carries the spiritual weight of promise. God is generous even to those outside the covenant.
Origen
The Edomite genealogy typifies the "earthly church" — organized, powerful, but outside the true covenant. The spiritual Israel is the chosen remnant, not the historically larger group.

Theological Themes Tracker

Genesis 36 teaches by its very existence: the God of Israel is the God of all nations. Esau's genealogy is preserved with as much care as Jacob's. The Edomite kings who precede Israel's monarchy are not an embarrassment but a testimony to the fulfilment of the promise to Abraham that kings would come from his line — even from the line that was not chosen for the covenant. Providence operates as broadly as creation.
